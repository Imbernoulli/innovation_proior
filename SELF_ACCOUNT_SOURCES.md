# Author Self-Account Sources

A curated, **growing** catalog of places where the people who invented a method recorded their
**own** account of how they discovered it — the highest-value grounding for this project, because
`reasoning.md` is trying to reconstruct exactly that path. The `paper-to-reasoning` skill (§1.2b)
tells every generation agent to consult this file for their method **and to add to it** whatever new
self-accounts they find. This list is a seed, **not** exhaustive — keep hunting.

## What counts as a "self-account"

**The thing we want is the *reasoning the paper leaves out*, not a dramatic anecdote.** A self-account
is any first-person record in which the author supplies the motivating thinking that the finished,
theorem-first paper edited away: why this route over the alternative, the dead ends tried and dropped,
what a definition/constant/design choice was really for, "the way I was thinking about it then." A
source that is all story and no reasoning is the *least* useful kind; a technical retrospective with no
story but rich omitted-reasoning is the *most* useful. Anecdotes are welcome when real, but incidental.

Kinds (any can carry the omitted reasoning):
1. **Technical retrospectives that re-explain the *why*** — author's later survey/textbook/lecture
   notes/commentary that restores the motivating reasoning (award-lecture technical sections, *Selected
   Works* commentary, Lamport's *My Writings* per-paper notes, Pearl's *Personal Journey*, 't Hooft's
   *Glorious Days of Physics*). Often anecdote-free and the highest value.
2. **Working manuscripts / notebooks / archives** — the rawest, author thinking on paper before
   editing. (Dijkstra's EWDs, lab notebooks, Grothendieck's manuscripts.)
3. **Real-time records** — diaries/blogs written *while* the work was happening; live collaborative
   threads where dead ends are visible. (Villani's diary; Polymath threads; Ellenberg's cap-set posts.)
4. **Retrospective "how I came to this"** — award lectures, memoirs, anniversary essays.
5. **Interviews / oral histories** — the author walked through the genesis (reasoning *and* story).

## General repositories (search these for ANY method)

- **Nobel lectures** — physics/chemistry/economics laureates frequently narrate the discovery path.
  https://www.nobelprize.org/  (lectures are free full text; e.g. Feynman 1965 on the path integral:
  https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/physics/1965/feynman/lecture/ )
- **Turing Award lectures** (ACM) — CS laureates' "how/why" lectures. https://amturing.acm.org/
- **Abel Prize** interviews & "Abel lectures" (mathematics). https://abelprize.no/
- **Fields Medalists' work & interviews** — often via ICM proceedings, Quanta, AMS Notices.
- **AMS Notices** — historical & "how this came about" articles, frequently by the author.
  https://www.ams.org/notices/
- **Oral histories** — Computer History Museum (https://computerhistory.org/collections/oral-histories/),
  AIP Niels Bohr Library (physics, https://www.aip.org/history-programs/niels-bohr-library/oral-histories).
- **Polymath project** — real-time collaborative proofs with dead ends recorded; entry via Gowers's
  blog (https://gowers.wordpress.com/) and Terence Tao's blog (https://terrytao.wordpress.com/).

## How agents should use this

1. Before writing `reasoning.md`, check this table + the general repositories for **your** method's
   inventor(s). Search: `<author> Nobel/Turing/Abel lecture`, `<author> "how I" / retrospective /
   interview`, `<method> discovery story`, `<author> notebook/archive`.
2. If you find one, **download it to `refs/`** and make the author's own narrated path the backbone.
3. **Append your find to the table above** (source, field, what it records, link) so the next method
   benefits. Keep entries terse.
4. If no self-account exists for your method, note that explicitly in `notes/` and proceed from the
   primary source + antecedents.

While he was trapped there… an idea occurred to him: **Why not apply the notion of state variables to the Wiener filtering problem?**" He then "read through Loève's book on probability theory and **equated expectation with projection**… With the additional assumption of **finite dimensionality**, he was able to derive the Wiener filter as what we now call the Kalman filter."
