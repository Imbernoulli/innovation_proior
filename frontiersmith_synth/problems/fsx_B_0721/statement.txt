# One Broken Mat: Auspicious Tatami Court Layout

## Problem

You are laying tatami mats in a ceremonial court: an `H x W` grid (`H`, `W`
both odd) with some cells permanently blocked by pillars. You have unlimited
`1x2` mats (placed horizontally or vertically) and, by tradition, **exactly
one** `1x1` mat -- the single "broken" mat that keeps the room's total mat
count from forcing an all-domino tiling. You do not have to cover every free
cell: place as many non-overlapping mats as you like, in any arrangement,
subject to the feasibility rules below, and your layout is scored by how
auspicious it looks.

**Tatami rule (feasibility, not a soft bonus):** at any interior grid point
whose four surrounding cells are all covered by mats, those four cells must
not belong to four *different* mats. Two or three different mats meeting at
a point is fine (that's a normal seam or a T-shaped joint); four meeting at
a single point is forbidden.

## Input

```
H W
```
then `H` lines of `W` characters: `.` (free) or `#` (pillar). Both `H` and
`W` are odd; the grid's exact centre cell, row `(H-1)/2`, column `(W-1)/2`,
is always free.

## Output

```
M
```
then `M` lines, each `T r c` (0-indexed):
* `H r c` -- a mat covering `(r,c)` and `(r,c+1)`.
* `V r c` -- a mat covering `(r,c)` and `(r+1,c)`.
* `S r c` -- the required `1x1` mat, covering `(r,c)` alone.

## Feasibility

Every covered cell must be free and covered by at most one mat; `S` must
appear **exactly once**; and the tatami rule above must hold everywhere.
Any violation scores `0`.

## Objective

Let `covered` be the number of free cells your mats cover and `free` the
total number of free cells. Four quantities are combined:

* `coverage = covered / free`.
* `entropy`: slide every `4x4` window of cells over the grid. In a window
  containing at least one `H` or `V` mat's anchor cell (`H`'s `(r,c)`, `V`'s
  `(r,c)`), compute the Shannon entropy (base 2) of the H-vs-V mix in that
  window (max `1.0` at a perfect 50/50 mix). `entropy` is the mean over all
  such non-empty windows (windows with no mat anchor are skipped).
* `motif`: among interior points whose four surrounding cells are ALL
  covered, the fraction where exactly three distinct mats meet (a
  T-junction) rather than two (a straight seam).
* `symmetry`: reflect the grid 180 degrees through its centre,
  `(r,c) -> (H-1-r, W-1-c)`. Among edges between orthogonally adjacent
  cells whose own two cells AND both of their mirror-image cells are all
  covered, the fraction where "is there a seam here?" (the two cells belong
  to different mats) agrees with the same question at the mirrored edge.

```
F = 1000 * (0.18*coverage + 0.37*entropy + 0.10*motif + 0.35*symmetry)
```

Your score for a test is `F` compared against a fixed internal reference
layout `B` (a plain even-rows-only tiling): `ratio = min(1000, 100*F/B) /
1000`. Per-test ratios are averaged. The reference scores `0.1`; a genuine
improvement scores higher, capped at `1.0`.

## Constraints

* `15 <= H, W <= 151` (both odd); at most a few percent of cells are
  pillars, always placed in point-symmetric pairs (`(r,c)` blocked implies
  `(H-1-r, W-1-c)` is too), and the exact centre cell is never a pillar.
* Time limit: 5 s. Memory limit: 512 MB.

## Example

A small `7x7` court, no pillars. Every row tiled with horizontal dominoes at
the same column alignment gives zero orientation entropy, and at every
column boundary shared by two rows, four different mats meet -- infeasible.
Offsetting alternate rows by one column (a "running-bond" brick pattern)
fixes feasibility but stays all-horizontal (`entropy = 0`) and drops its
`1x1` mat wherever the row scan runs out of pairs, rarely the centre, so
`symmetry` stays near chance (~`0.5`). Placing the `1x1` mat exactly on the
centre and mirroring the rest through it (mixing horizontal and vertical
mats so the whole pattern reflects onto itself) raises both `entropy` and
`symmetry` well above the running-bond layout, at some coverage cost from
the care mixed orientations need to stay tatami-legal.
