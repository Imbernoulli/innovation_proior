# Guild Forecast Tournament: Liquidity Allocation Under the Market-Scoring Rule

## Problem
The Cartographers' Guild runs a forecast tournament on N=100 binary questions. For
each question q the Guild opens a logarithmic-market-scoring-rule (LMSR) stall with
liquidity beta_q>0 that YOU choose, subject to a shared subsidy budget. A fixed,
printed roster of traders then visits the stall **in order** and trades against the
price your beta_q implies; you never see or control what any trader does beyond
setting beta_q up front.

Every question starts at price p=0.5 (logit x=0). The LMSR cost function with
liquidity beta is C(q_yes,q_no) = beta * ln( e^{q_yes/beta} + e^{q_no/beta} ), and the
current price of YES is p = e^{q_yes/beta} / (e^{q_yes/beta}+e^{q_no/beta}).

Trader i (belief b_i in (0,1), budget w_i>0) trades toward their belief: if b_i
implies a price above the current price they buy YES shares (q_yes increases, q_no
unchanged); if below, they buy NO shares (q_no increases, q_yes unchanged). Each
purchase costs C(after)-C(before). A trader keeps buying, spending real money, until
EITHER the price exactly reaches their belief OR their entire budget w_i runs out --
whichever binds first. If beta_q=0 the stall never opens: price stays 0.5, nobody
trades.

After the last trader, let p_q be the final price and q_yes,q_no the final
outstanding shares of question q. When the true outcome resolves, the Guild pays $1
per outstanding share of the winning side, and keeps the revenue C(final)-C(0,0) =
C(final) - beta_q*ln2 collected from traders along the way. Define the REALIZED
SUBSIDY of question q as subsidy_q = payout_q - revenue_q (payout_q = final q_yes if
outcome=1, else final q_no) -- money the Guild had to add out of pocket to run that
stall (it can be negative: a stall the Guild happened to profit from).

## Input
Line 1: `N B lambda` -- N=100 questions, total liquidity budget B (integer), subsidy
weight lambda (decimal), identical for every question.
Then for each question q=1..N:
  Line: `M_q outcome_q` (M_q traders follow; outcome_q in {0,1}, 1 means YES resolved).
  M_q lines: `belief_i budget_i` -- belief_i is an integer percent 1..99 (true belief
  = belief_i/100), budget_i a positive integer -- printed IN TRADING ORDER.

## Output
N lines, one real number beta_q each: the liquidity you allocate to question q.

## Feasibility
- Each beta_q must be exactly 0, or lie in [0.01, B].
- sum_q beta_q <= B (tolerance 1e-6*B).
- Every value must be finite (no nan/inf).
Any violation scores 0.

## Objective (maximize)
F = sum_q [ log(p_q)-log(0.5) if outcome_q=1 else log(1-p_q)-log(0.5) ]
    - lambda * sum_q subsidy_q
i.e. total forecasting-accuracy gain over an uninformative 0.5 prior, minus lambda
times the total money spent subsidizing the stalls. Replaying a question's printed
trader list under a chosen beta is exact and fully deterministic, so for any
candidate beta_q the value that question would contribute is a function you can
compute and re-evaluate as many times as you like before deciding how to spend B.

## Scoring
Your F is compared against an internal reference construction; the ratio is reported
to 6 decimals, `Ratio: <float>`. Spreading B uniformly, or in proportion to how many
traders show up at each stall, are both known to be poor recipes: a question where
the crowd already agrees converges to nearly the same price at almost any beta_q and
wastes subsidy sitting there, while a question that swings between confident,
well-funded, disagreeing factions only pays off at a carefully sized beta_q -- too
small and the final price is dictated by whichever faction happened to trade last,
too large and no faction can afford to move the price away from 0.5 at all.

## Constraints
N=100. 2<=M_q<=50. 1<=belief_i<=99. 1<=budget_i<=9000. B=10000, lambda=0.005000 in
every test. Time limit: 4s. Memory limit: 512MB.

## Example
Two questions, B=100, lambda=0.01.
```
2 100 0.010000
2 1
90 1000
60 50
2 0
80 30
20 30
```
Q1 (outcome=1): trader1 (belief 0.90, budget 1000) can easily afford to push the price
all the way to 0.90; trader2 (belief 0.60, budget 50) then pulls it back down toward
0.60. With beta_1=60 the final price is p_1=0.6000, contributing log(0.6)-log(0.5) =
0.1823, at a realized subsidy of 10.94.
Q2 (outcome=0): symmetric traders push the price to 0.80 then back to about 0.36; with
beta_2=40 the final price is p_2=0.3608, contributing log(1-0.3608)-log(0.5)=0.2456,
at a realized subsidy of 9.82.
F = 0.1823+0.2456 - 0.01*(10.94+9.82) = 0.2203. A uniform split (beta=50 each)
instead gives F=0.1838 here -- worse, because it under-funds Q1 (where the second,
smaller-budget trader needed more room to pull the price back) and over-funds Q2.
