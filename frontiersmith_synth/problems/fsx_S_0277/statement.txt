# Draining the Deluge: Reservoir Junction Interdiction

## Problem

The regional **reservoir dam network** delivers water from a mountain **reservoir**
(node `s`) down to a downstream **city intake** (node `t`) through a mesh of pipes that
meet at *pumping junctions*. Every pipe has a positive transit time. A hostile actor
wants to slow the delivery as much as possible by **decommissioning pumping junctions**:
shutting a junction down instantly severs *all* pipes that meet at it.

Shutting a junction down is not free — each junction `i` has a decommissioning cost
`c_i`, and the total cost of the junctions you shut may not exceed a budget `K`. The
reservoir `s` and the city intake `t` are dam terminals and can never be shut. Finally,
if the city intake is ever completely cut off from the reservoir the water authority
declares an emergency and every shutdown is reversed — so `s` and `t` must stay
connected.

Choose which junctions to decommission so that the *fastest surviving route* from the
reservoir to the city intake is as **long** as possible.

Formally: you are given an undirected weighted graph with a source `s` and sink `t`.
Each node `i` has a positive removal cost `c_i`. Choose a set `S` of nodes with
`s, t not in S` and `sum_{i in S} c_i <= K`, such that `s` and `t` remain connected in
the graph induced by deleting `S` (a deleted node takes all of its incident edges with
it), maximizing the length of the shortest `s`-`t` path in that induced graph.

This is a **node-deletion shortest-path interdiction** problem: it is NP-hard, the
optimum is unknown, and many heuristics (cost-greedy, best-improvement local search,
randomized restarts) are viable and diverge.

## Input

The first line contains five integers:

```
n m s t K
```

- `n` — number of junctions (nodes), numbered `1..n`.
- `m` — number of pipes (edges).
- `s` — the reservoir (source node).
- `t` — the city intake (sink node), `s != t`.
- `K` — the total decommissioning budget.

The second line contains `n` integers `c_1 c_2 ... c_n`, where `c_i >= 1` is the cost of
decommissioning junction `i`. The costs `c_s` and `c_t` are present but irrelevant (you
may never shut `s` or `t`).

Each of the next `m` lines describes one pipe:

```
u v w
```

meaning a two-way pipe between junctions `u` and `v` with transit time `w` (`1 <= w`).
The graph is connected, so `s` can reach `t`.

## Output

On the first line print `r` — the number of junctions you decommission (`0 <= r <= n`).
On each of the next `r` lines print one junction index (an integer in `1..n`). All
indices must be distinct, and none may equal `s` or `t`.

Printing `0` on the first line (and nothing else) is always valid.

## Feasibility

An output is feasible iff:

- `0 <= r <= n`;
- every printed index is in `1..n`, all are distinct, and none equals `s` or `t`;
- the total cost `sum_{i in S} c_i` of the chosen junctions is at most `K`;
- after deleting the chosen junctions (and all their incident pipes), `s` and `t` are
  still connected.

Any infeasible output scores `0`.

## Objective

Let `F` be the shortest-path transit time from `s` to `t` after your chosen junctions
are deleted. You are **maximizing** `F`.

## Scoring

Let `B` be the shortest-path transit time from `s` to `t` in the original network (no
junctions shut). The score for a single test is

```
ratio = min(1.0, 0.1 * F / B)
```

A do-nothing submission (`F = B`) scores `0.1`; reaching a ten-fold delay (`F = 10*B`)
caps the score at `1.0`. The overall score is the mean ratio across all tests.

## Constraints

- `2 <= n <= 1600`, `1 <= m <= 6000`.
- `1 <= w <= 100`.
- `1 <= c_i <= 50`.
- `0 <= K <= 400`.
- Time limit: 5 s. Memory limit: 512 MB.

## Example

Input:

```
5 6 1 5 3
9 2 3 4 9
1 2 1
2 5 1
1 3 4
3 4 4
4 5 4
2 4 10
```

The reservoir is `s = 1`, the city intake is `t = 5`, and the budget is `K = 3`.
The original fastest route is `1 -> 2 -> 5` with length `2`, so `B = 2`.

Junction `2` has removal cost `c_2 = 2 <= 3`. Shutting it deletes the cheap corridor;
the fastest surviving route becomes `1 -> 3 -> 4 -> 5` with length `4 + 4 + 4 = 12`, so
`F = 12` (and `s`, `t` stay connected).

Score for this test: `min(1.0, 0.1 * 12 / 2) = min(1.0, 0.6) = 0.6`.

A valid worked output:

```
1
2
```
