# Accordion Silhouette Scheduling

## Problem

A strip of `n` unit panels numbered `1..n` is joined by `n-1` creases; crease `i`
sits between panel `i` and panel `i+1`. You fold the strip flat into a stack by
**committing creases one at a time**. A commit is irrevocable, and it is what
clears access to the strip's interior: crease `i` is **exposed** (fold-able) at a
given moment iff `i` is an end crease (`i = 1` or `i = n-1`), or at least one of
its neighboring creases (`i-1` or `i+1`) has **already been committed**.
Initially only the two end creases are exposed; committing a crease exposes its
still-unfolded neighbor. Interior creases stay buried under uncommitted material
until a neighboring fold clears the way to them.

Each crease `i` also carries a required direction `req_i` (Mountain or Valley)
printed in the input — the direction the target silhouette wants there. You may
commit crease `i` in **either** direction; matching `req_i` earns full credit,
mismatching earns half (see Scoring).

The final stack's outermost, most visible layers are exactly the **last**-committed
folds. If you commit `k` creases total (any subset, `0 <= k <= n-1`, chosen by
you — folding everything is not required), the `t`-th crease you commit (1-indexed
by print order) gets **prominence multiplier `t`**: waiting longer to fold a crease
makes it more prominent and worth more, but you cannot fold it before it is exposed,
and once you start committing a chain of buried creases you cannot reorder that
chain's internal sequence — it must proceed strictly inward.

A flat-foldable strip also needs a global Mountain/Valley balance. The input gives
an integer target `T` (a Maekawa/Kawasaki-style parity target). Let `Mc, Vc` be the
number of committed creases folded Mountain / Valley (`Mc + Vc = k`). If
`Mc - Vc == T` exactly, your **entire** silhouette earns full credit; otherwise the
strip does not lie flat and your **entire** silhouette earns only half credit.
Flipping a crease's direction only changes `Mc - Vc` by 2, and dropping one crease
changes `k`'s parity — sometimes the only way to reach `T` is to leave one crease
unfolded, sacrificing that fold's own contribution to save the multiplier on
everything else.

## Input
```
n T
req_1 req_2 ... req_{n-1}      (each token "M" or "V")
w_1 w_2 ... w_{n-1}             (positive integers, silhouette weight of crease i)
```

## Output
```
k
c_1 d_1
...
c_k d_k
```
`c_j` (distinct integers in `[1, n-1]`) is the crease committed at step `j` — this
order **is** the commit order; `d_j` is `"M"` or `"V"`. `0 <= k <= n-1`.

## Feasibility
`k` in `[0, n-1]`; all `c_j` distinct, in `[1, n-1]`; each `d_j` exactly `"M"` or
`"V"`. At step `j`, crease `c_j` must be exposed given only `c_1..c_{j-1}` already
committed. Any violation, out-of-range token, repeated crease, or trailing output
scores `0`.

## Objective (maximize)
For step `j` committing crease `c` with direction `d`:
`contribution = w_c * j * (1 if d == req_c else 0.5)`.
`RAW = sum of contributions` over all `k` steps. With `Mc, Vc` as above, the
silhouette score is `F = RAW * (1 if Mc - Vc == T else 0.5)`.

## Scoring
The checker also builds its own reference: commit every crease in plain index
order `1, 2, ..., n-1`, always folding **Mountain** (never reading `req_i`,
never reasoning about `T`). Call its score `B` (guaranteed positive). Your
ratio for a test is `min(1000, 100*F/max(1,B)) / 1000`, averaged over 10 tests.

## Constraints
`3 <= n <= 200000`; `|T| <= n-1`; `1 <= w_i <= 50000`. Time limit: 3s. Memory: 512MB.

## Example
`n=5` (creases `1..4`), `T=0`, `req = M V M V`, `w = 10 100 10 10`. Output:
```
4
1 M
4 V
2 V
3 M
```
Step 1 folds crease 1 (boundary) `M` matches `req_1=M` -> `10*1*1=10`.
Step 2 folds crease 4 (boundary) `V` matches `req_4=V` -> `10*2*1=20`.
Step 3 folds crease 2 (exposed: neighbor 1 already committed) `V` matches
`req_2=V` -> `100*3*1=300`.
Step 4 folds crease 3 (exposed: neighbor 2 or 4 already committed) `M` matches
`req_3=M` -> `10*4*1=40`.
`RAW = 370`. Directions used: `M,V,V,M` so `Mc=2, Vc=2, Mc-Vc=0=T` -> full credit,
`F = 370`. The reference `B` folds `1,2,3,4` in that fixed order, always
Mountain (ignoring `req`): crease 1 matches (`req_1=M`) for `10*1*1=10`; creases
2 and 4 want Valley but get Mountain, so they earn half credit
(`100*2*0.5=100`, `10*4*0.5=20`); crease 3 matches for `10*3*1=30`. That is
`RAW_B = 160`, and the reference's own gap is `4` (all Mountain) which misses
`T=0`, halving again: `B = 80`. Ratio `= min(1000, 100*370/80)/1000 = 0.4625`.
Delaying crease 2 (the heaviest weight) to step 3 instead of folding it
immediately is the core idea — folding it at step 1 would only score
`100*1*1=100` toward `RAW` instead of `300`.
