# Blind Delivery Through Road Closures

## Problem

A delivery driver must visit every stop in a town whose road network has
`N` intersections (intersection `1` is the depot) and `M` two-way roads.
You plan the driver's **visiting order** once, in advance. The driver then
executes it on the actual delivery day, but the road authority has **not
told you** which of `K` closure scenarios will occur that day.

Some roads are permanently reliable (they are never closed). The rest are
**fragile**: each closure scenario `s` (1..K) blocks a specific set of
fragile roads for that day. On the actual day, exactly one scenario's
closures are in effect, and the driver follows one **fixed, unintelligent
rule** at every step: *drive the shortest currently-open route to the next
stop on your list.* The driver has no map foresight beyond "shortest open
path right now" — if a stretch of your intended route is closed, the driver
is forced onto whatever detour is shortest given today's closures, however
long that turns out to be.

Since you don't know which scenario will occur, your order is scored across
**all** `K` scenarios, weighted by how much each scenario matters (`w_s`).
Minimize the total weighted travel the driver racks up. A good order isn't
necessarily the geometrically shortest one — it is one whose legs rarely
depend on roads that closure scenarios actually touch.

## Input

```
N M K
```
then `M` lines, one per road (road `i` is the i-th such line, 1-indexed):
```
u v len
```
then `K` closure scenarios, each as two lines:
```
B_s w_s
id_1 id_2 ... id_{B_s}
```
`B_s` road ids (from the numbering above) are blocked under scenario `s`,
which carries importance weight `w_s`.

## Output

Print `N-1` integers: a permutation of `{2, ..., N}`, the order in which the
driver visits the remaining stops after leaving the depot (intersection 1).

## Feasibility

The output must contain every intersection in `{2,...,N}` exactly once
(no repeats, none missing), with no trailing tokens. Any violation scores 0.

## Objective

For scenario `s`, remove its blocked roads from the network and let
`dist_s(x,y)` be the shortest-path distance between `x` and `y` in what
remains (this is exactly the distance the fixed detour rule realizes,
whether or not the driver's original route happened to touch a blocked
road). Walking depot `-> order[1] -> order[2] -> ... -> order[N-1]`, let
`C_s` be the sum of the `N-1` leg distances `dist_s`. Minimize
```
F = sum over s of  w_s * C_s
```

## Scoring

Let `B` be `F` evaluated on the **identity order** `2,3,...,N` (do nothing
clever). Your score for a test case is
```
ratio = min(1000, 100 * B / max(1, F)) / 1000
```
so the identity order scores `0.1`; a genuinely better order scores higher
(capped at `1.0`, ten times better than identity). Per-test ratios are
averaged over all tests.

## Constraints

* `6 <= N <= 131`, `N-1 <= M <= 400`, `2 <= K <= 14`.
* `1 <= len <= 1000000`; `1 <= w_s <= 50`; every scenario blocks `>= 1` road.
* A fixed spanning-tree subset of the roads is never blocked by any scenario
  (the network is always connected, whatever closes).
* Time limit: 3 s. Memory limit: 512 MB.

## Example

`N=5, M=6, K=2`:
```
5 6 2
1 2 5
2 3 5
1 4 5
4 5 5
2 4 2
3 5 6
1 6
5
1 4
6
```
Roads 1-4 (the hub-to-arm chains `1-2-3` and `1-4-5`) are the reliable
backbone. Road 5 (`2-4`, length 2) and road 6 (`3-5`, length 6) are the only
two ways to cross between the two arms without going back through the
depot. Scenario 1 blocks road 5 (weight 6, the *heavier* threat); scenario
2 blocks road 6 (weight 4).

The identity order `2 3 4 5` crosses arms at the `(3,4)` pair, whose
shortest route (`3-2-4`, cost 7) uses road 5. Under scenario 1 (road 5
blocked) this leg detours to cost 11 (`3-5-4` via road 6), so
`C_1 = 5+5+11+5 = 26`; under scenario 2 (road 6 blocked) the leg is
untouched, `C_2 = 5+5+7+5 = 22`. That gives
`B = 6*26 + 4*22 = 244` (this is exactly the checker's baseline; a
submission printing `2 3 4 5` scores `0.1` on this test) — and because road
5 is the cheaper road, the *heavier* scenario (weight 6) is the one that
gets to hurt it.

The order `2 3 5 4` instead crosses arms at the `(3,5)` pair, whose
shortest route is the *direct* road 6 (cost 6). Under scenario 1 (road 5
blocked, irrelevant here) this leg is untouched, `C_1 = 5+5+6+5 = 21`;
under scenario 2 (road 6 blocked) it detours to cost 12 (`3-2-4-5`), so
`C_2 = 5+5+12+5 = 27`. Now `F = 6*21 + 4*27 = 234 < B`, so
`ratio = min(1000, 100*244/234)/1000 = 0.1043` — a real, if modest,
improvement from matching the crossing point to the road that only the
*lighter* scenario can block, instead of the road that is merely nominally
cheapest. On larger inputs with many possible crossing points this effect
compounds substantially.
