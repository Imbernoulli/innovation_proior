# Grid Hardening Attack: Maximizing Source-to-Sink Effective Resistance

## Problem

You are a red-team analyst probing a power distribution grid. The grid is an
undirected electrical network: each transmission line (edge) is a resistor with an
integer resistance, and two special buses are singled out — a **source** `s` and a
**sink** `t`. The grid's electrical robustness between `s` and `t` is measured by the
**effective resistance** `R(s,t)` — the resistance an ohmmeter would read across the
two buses when the whole network is treated as one equivalent resistor.

Your attack is a **line-interdiction with a sabotage budget**. Each line `e` has a
**removal cost** `c_e` (the effort to physically cut it). You may cut any subset `S` of
lines whose total cost does not exceed a budget `B`, subject to the hard constraint that
**`s` and `t` must remain electrically connected** afterwards (you must never fully
islanding the sink from the source). Cutting lines can only *raise* the effective
resistance (Rayleigh's monotonicity law), degrading the grid. Your goal is to cut lines
so as to **maximize** the resulting effective resistance `R(s,t)`.

This is deceptively hard. Cutting the single shortest / lowest-resistance path does
**not** maximize `R(s,t)`: as long as parallel paths remain, resistance barely moves.
The winning attack must funnel current onto ever-longer detours while never severing the
last surviving `s`-`t` route, all under the cost budget — a coupling of a knapsack-style
budget, an `s`-`t` connectivity side-constraint, and a spectral/electrical objective.

## Definitions

The network is a weighted graph. Give each line `e` a **conductance** `g_e = 1 / r_e`.
Parallel lines between the same pair of buses add their conductances. For a graph `H`
in which `s` and `t` lie in the same connected component, the effective resistance
`R_H(s,t)` is defined by the Laplacian minor formula

```
R_H(s,t) = det( L^{(s,t)} ) / det( L^{(s)} )
```

where `L` is the weighted Laplacian of the component containing `s` (diagonal =
sum of incident conductances, off-diagonal `(u,v)` = `-`(sum of conductances of lines
between `u` and `v`)), `L^{(s)}` is `L` with the row and column of `s` deleted, and
`L^{(s,t)}` is `L` with the rows and columns of **both** `s` and `t` deleted. Buses in
other components (islanded by your cuts) do not affect `R(s,t)` and are ignored. This
value equals the classical two-terminal effective resistance in ohms and is a positive
real number.

## Input

```
n m s t B
u_1 v_1 r_1 c_1
u_2 v_2 r_2 c_2
...
u_m v_m r_m c_m
```

- `n` buses (`1`-indexed), `m` lines.
- `s` `t` are the source and sink buses, `s != t`.
- `B` is the total sabotage budget.
- Line `i` connects buses `u_i` and `v_i` (`u_i != v_i`), has integer resistance
  `r_i` and integer removal cost `c_i`. Parallel lines (repeated bus pairs) may occur.
- The input network is connected between `s` and `t`.

## Output

```
K
e_1 e_2 ... e_K
```

- `K` is the number of lines you cut (`0 <= K <= m`).
- The second line lists the `K` distinct line indices (`1`-indexed by input order).
  If `K = 0`, print an empty second line (or omit it).

## Feasibility

An output is feasible iff:
1. `0 <= K <= m` and the `K` line indices are distinct and each in `[1, m]`.
2. The total removal cost `sum_{i in S} c_i <= B`.
3. After deleting the cut lines, `s` and `t` are still in the same connected component.

An infeasible output scores `0`.

## Objective

Let `F = R_H(s,t)` be the effective resistance of the network `H` obtained by deleting
your cut lines. **Maximize `F`.**

## Scoring

Let `Base = R_G(s,t)` be the effective resistance of the *original* network `G` with no
lines cut (this is always feasible — cutting nothing costs `0`). By Rayleigh's
monotonicity, every feasible attack satisfies `F >= Base`. Your ratio is

```
score = min( 1000, 100 * F / Base ) / 1000
```

so cutting nothing scores exactly `0.1`, and driving the effective resistance to `10x`
the original (or more) attains the capped maximum `1.0`. The final score is the mean of
per-test scores.

## Constraints

- `2 <= n <= 600`, `1 <= m <= 4000`.
- `1 <= r_i <= 9`, `1 <= c_i <= 9`.
- `0 <= B < sum_i c_i`.
- Time limit: 5 s. Memory limit: 512 MB.

## Example

Input:
```
4 5 1 4 2
1 2 1 1
2 4 1 1
1 3 1 1
3 4 1 1
1 4 1 1
```

Here `s=1`, `t=4`. Two length-2 paths (`1-2-4` and `1-3-4`, each `2` ohms) and one direct
line `1-4` (`1` ohm) run in parallel, so `Base = R_G(1,4) = 1 / (1/2 + 1/2 + 1) = 0.5`.

A feasible attack: cut lines `3` (`1-3`) and `5` (`1-4`), total cost `2 <= B = 2`. The
survivors are `1-2-4` (a `2`-ohm series path) plus a dead stub `3-4`, which carries no
current. Now `s` and `t` are joined only by the `2`-ohm path, so `F = 2`. The ratio is
`100 * 2 / 0.5 = 400`, giving `score = 400 / 1000 = 0.4`.

Cutting instead the single lowest-resistance line `5` (`1-4`) alone leaves the two
parallel `2`-ohm paths: `F = 1`, ratio `200`, `score = 0.2` — illustrating that hitting
the "obvious" shortest link is far from optimal.
