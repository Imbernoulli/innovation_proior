# Twin-Loop Cooling: Balanced Thermal Zone Split

## Problem

A hyperscale data hall houses `n` server **racks** (numbered `1..n`, with `n` even). Two
independent **cooling loops** — the *blue* loop (`0`) and the *red* loop (`1`) — each draw
waste heat to a separate chiller. Because the two chillers have identical, fixed capacity,
each loop must serve **exactly `n/2` racks**.

Pairs of racks are **thermally coupled**: they share airflow, so heat spilling out of one
warms the other. Each coupling has a positive strength `w`. Operators have learned that
when two strongly-coupled racks sit on the **same** loop their combined heat piles onto one
chiller and forms a hotspot, whereas when they sit on **different** loops the two chillers
split the load and the hotspot dissolves. You therefore want to assign racks to the two
loops so that, subject to the equal-capacity rule, the total strength of couplings whose
two racks land on **different** loops is as **large** as possible.

Formally: you are given an undirected weighted (multi)graph on `n` vertices. Partition the
vertices into two sets of size exactly `n/2` each, maximizing the total weight of edges
whose endpoints fall in different sets.

This is the **maximum balanced bisection** (balanced max-cut) problem: it is NP-hard, the
optimum is unknown, and many heuristics (random balanced split, greedy assignment,
Kernighan–Lin swap local search, randomized restarts) are viable and genuinely diverge.

## Input

The first line contains two integers:

```
n m
```

- `n` — number of racks (an **even** integer), racks are numbered `1..n`.
- `m` — number of thermal couplings (edges).

Each of the next `m` lines describes one coupling:

```
u v w
```

meaning racks `u` and `v` are thermally coupled with strength `w` (`1 <= w`, `u != v`).
Parallel couplings between the same pair may appear and are all counted.

## Output

Print `n` integers `a_1 a_2 ... a_n` (whitespace-separated; newlines are fine). Value
`a_i` is the cooling loop assigned to rack `i`: `0` for the blue loop or `1` for the red
loop.

## Feasibility

An output is feasible iff:

- it contains exactly `n` integers, each equal to `0` or `1`;
- exactly `n/2` of them equal `1` (equivalently exactly `n/2` equal `0`) — the equal-
  capacity rule.

Any infeasible output scores `0`.

## Objective

Let `F` be the total strength of couplings that are **split** across the two loops:

```
F = sum of w over every edge (u, v, w) with a_u != a_v.
```

You are **maximizing** `F`.

## Scoring

Let `B` be the split strength achieved by the trivial reference assignment that places
racks `1..n/2` on the blue loop and racks `n/2+1..n` on the red loop (a feasible, balanced
"do-nothing" split). The score for a test is

```
ratio = min(1.0, 0.1 * F / B)
```

The reference split (`F = B`) scores `0.1`; a ten-fold improvement (`F = 10*B`) caps the
score at `1.0`. The overall score is the mean ratio across all tests.

## Constraints

- `2 <= n <= 160`, `n` even.
- `1 <= m <= 40000`.
- `1 <= w <= 20`.
- Time limit: 5 s. Memory limit: 512 MB.

## Example

Input:

```
4 6
1 2 3
2 3 4
3 4 3
1 4 4
1 3 1
2 4 1
```

The couplings form a 4-cycle `1-2-3-4-1` with heavy edges and two light diagonals.

The reference split (racks `1,2` blue; racks `3,4` red) splits edges `2-3` (4), `1-4` (4),
`1-3` (1) and `2-4` (1) for a total of `B = 10`.

A better balanced split puts racks `1,3` on blue and racks `2,4` on red. Now the heavy
cycle edges `1-2` (3), `2-3` (4), `3-4` (3), `1-4` (4) are all split while the light
diagonals `1-3` and `2-4` are not, giving `F = 14`.

Score for this test: `min(1.0, 0.1 * 14 / 10) = 0.14`.

A valid worked output (rack 1 -> blue, rack 2 -> red, rack 3 -> blue, rack 4 -> red):

```
0 1 0 1
```
