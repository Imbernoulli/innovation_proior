# Hidden Communities Behind Decoy Hubs

## Problem

You are given an undirected weighted graph on `n` vertices built from two hidden
communities glued together by ordinary weak links -- and, on some inputs, by a
handful of decoy "hub" vertices whose edges point mostly at the *other* community,
purely to mislead a solver that trusts raw edge weight. Vertex IDs are **not**
sorted by community: adjacent IDs can belong to either side. Partition the
vertices into two labeled sides so that the weighted **normalized cut** between
them is small, while keeping the two sides balanced.

## Input

```
n m lo hi
u_1 v_1 w_1
...
u_m v_m w_m
```

`n` vertices (1-indexed), `m` undirected edges each with a positive integer weight
`w_i` (no self loops, no duplicate edges). `lo` and `hi` bound the size of side 0.

## Output

One line with `n` integers `label_1 ... label_n`, `label_i` in `{0,1}`: the side
of vertex `i`.

## Feasibility

Exactly `n` tokens, each `0` or `1`. Let `c0` = number of vertices labeled `0`;
you must have `lo <= c0 <= hi`. Any violation (wrong token count, out-of-range
label, unbalanced `c0`, trailing tokens) scores `0`.

## Objective

Let `deg(v)` be the sum of weights of edges touching `v`. For your labeling with
sides `A` (label 0) and `B` (label 1), let `cut` = sum of weights of edges with
endpoints on different sides, `vol(A) = sum_{v in A} deg(v)`, `vol(B)` likewise.
Minimize the **normalized cut**

```
F = cut * (1/vol(A) + 1/vol(B))
```

Low `F` means few, low-weight edges cross a *balanced* boundary; a lopsided or
edge-heavy boundary is penalized twice -- once by `cut`, once by shrunken volume.

## Scoring

The checker also computes `B`, the normalized cut `F` would take under the fixed
reference labeling "vertices `1..floor(n/2)` get side 0, the rest side 1" (a
structure-blind split, since community membership is **not** correlated with ID
order). Your test score is

```
ratio = min(1000, 60 * B / max(F, 1e-9)) / 1000
```

so matching the reference scores `0.06`, and a genuinely better cut scores
higher, up to a cap of `1.0` (achieved only once `F` drops to `1/16.7` of `B` or
below). The final score is the mean ratio over all tests.

## Constraints

`30 <= n <= 5000`; `m` up to about `50000`; `1 <= w_i <= 10`; `1 <= lo <= hi <=
n-1`. Every vertex touches at least one edge (`deg(v) >= 1`). Time limit: 4 s.
Memory limit: 512 MB.

## Example

`n=6`, edges (triangle `1-3-5` weight `3` each, triangle `2-4-6` weight `3` each,
one bridge `5-6` weight `1`), `lo=2, hi=4`:

```
6 7 2 4
1 3 3
3 5 3
1 5 3
2 4 3
4 6 3
2 6 3
5 6 1
```

Output `0 1 0 1 0 1` (vertices `1,3,5` side 0; `2,4,6` side 1) puts only the
bridge edge `5-6` on the boundary: `cut=1`, `vol(A)=vol(B)=19`, `F =
1*(1/19+1/19) = 0.10526`. The reference splits by ID (`{1,2,3}` vs `{4,5,6}`),
cutting 4 of the 7 edges: `cut=12`, `vol({1,2,3})=18`, `vol({4,5,6})=20`, `B =
12*(1/18+1/20) = 1.26667`. Ratio `= min(1000, 60*1.26667/0.10526)/1000 =
0.722` (`B/F = 12.03`, comfortably below the 16.7x needed to hit the cap).

This shows why trusting vertex order -- or trusting a vertex's own loudest
edges -- can be far worse than finding the two sides that make the *whole*
boundary thin: on larger inputs a handful of high-weight decoy edges can
dominate a purely local read of the graph, while a global coordinate that sees
the whole graph at once is not fooled by a few loud vertices, and needs only
local polishing near the boundary afterward.
