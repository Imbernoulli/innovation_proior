# Blinded Wind Tunnel: Sensor-Station Interdiction

## Problem

The *Aeolus* transonic wind tunnel is instrumented by a lattice of **sensor stations**
that relay a pressure signal from the tunnel's **inlet probe** (node `s`) to its
**data-acquisition sink** (node `t`). Neighbouring stations are joined by relay channels;
each channel has a positive relay latency. During a calibration audit an adversary wants
the pressure signal to arrive as *late* as possible. It may **power down at most `k`
sensor stations** — but it may never power down the inlet probe `s` or the sink `t`, and
if powering the stations down ever disconnects the sink from the inlet the whole audit is
invalidated. Powering down a station removes that station **and every relay channel
touching it**. Your job is to choose which stations to power down so that the *fastest
remaining relay route* from `s` to `t` is as **long** as possible.

Formally: you are given an undirected weighted graph on `n` nodes. Choose a set `S` of at
most `k` nodes, with `s, t` not in `S`, such that `s` and `t` remain connected in the graph
induced by the surviving nodes, maximizing the shortest `s`-`t` path length in that induced
subgraph.

This is the **node-deletion (most-vital-nodes) shortest-path interdiction** problem: it is
NP-hard, the optimum is unknown, and many heuristics (degree/centrality greedy,
best-improvement local search, randomized restarts) are viable and diverge.

## Input

The first line contains five integers:

```
n m s t k
```

- `n` — number of sensor stations (nodes), numbered `1..n`.
- `m` — number of relay channels (edges).
- `s` — the inlet probe (source node).
- `t` — the data-acquisition sink (sink node), `s != t`.
- `k` — the maximum number of stations you may power down.

Each of the next `m` lines describes one channel:

```
u v w
```

meaning a two-way relay channel between stations `u` and `v` with latency `w` (`1 <= w`).
The graph may contain parallel channels and is guaranteed to have `s` connected to `t`.

## Output

On the first line print `r` — the number of stations you power down (`0 <= r <= k`).
On each of the next `r` lines print one station index (an integer in `1..n`). All indices
must be distinct, and none may equal `s` or `t`.

Printing `0` on the first line (and nothing else) is always valid.

## Feasibility

An output is feasible iff:

- `0 <= r <= k`;
- every printed index is in `1..n`, all are distinct, and none equals `s` or `t`;
- after removing exactly the chosen stations (and their incident channels), `s` and `t`
  are still connected.

Any infeasible output scores `0`.

## Objective

Let `F` be the shortest-path relay latency from `s` to `t` in the induced subgraph after
your chosen stations are powered down. You are **maximizing** `F`.

## Scoring

Let `B` be the shortest-path relay latency from `s` to `t` in the original network (no
stations powered down). The score for a test is

```
ratio = min(1.0, 0.1 * F / B)
```

A do-nothing submission (`F = B`) scores `0.1`; reaching a ten-fold delay (`F = 10*B`)
caps the score at `1.0`. The overall score is the mean ratio across all tests.

## Constraints

- `2 <= n <= 500`, `1 <= m <= 3000`.
- `1 <= w <= 80`.
- `0 <= k <= 20`.
- Time limit: 5 s. Memory limit: 512 MB.

## Example

Input:

```
5 6 1 5 1
1 2 1
2 5 1
1 3 6
3 5 6
1 4 3
4 5 3
```

Original fastest route: `1 -> 2 -> 5` with latency `2`, so `B = 2`.

With a budget of `k = 1`, powering down station `2` removes both channels through it; the
fastest surviving route becomes `1 -> 4 -> 5` with latency `6`, so `F = 6`. (Powering down
the inlet `1` or the sink `5` is forbidden.)

Score for this test: `min(1.0, 0.1 * 6 / 2) = min(1.0, 0.3) = 0.3`.

A valid worked output:

```
1
2
```
