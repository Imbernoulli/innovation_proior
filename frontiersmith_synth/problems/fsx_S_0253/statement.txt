# Severing the Wildlife Corridor: Budget-Constrained Habitat Interdiction

## Problem

A migratory herd travels every season from its winter **den** (node `s`) to a distant
**watering ground** (node `t`) through a protected mosaic of habitat patches linked by
safe wildlife **corridors**. Each corridor has a positive traversal length (the distance
an animal must walk along it) and a positive **clearing cost** (the money a development
consortium must spend to fell, fence, or build over that corridor and take it out of use).

The consortium has a fixed budget `C`. It may clear any set of corridors whose total
clearing cost is at most `C`, **but** if the den is ever completely cut off from the
watering ground the environmental regulator halts all development and the plan is voided.
Subject to those two rules, the consortium wants the herd's *shortest remaining route*
from den to watering ground to be as **long** as possible (forcing a costly detour).

Your job: choose which corridors to clear so as to maximize the length of the shortest
den-to-watering-ground route in the surviving network, while (a) keeping the total
clearing cost within budget `C`, and (b) keeping `s` and `t` connected.

Formally: you are given an undirected weighted (multi)graph where edge `i` has traversal
length `w_i` and clearing cost `c_i`. Choose a set `S` of edges with
`sum_{i in S} c_i <= C` such that `s` and `t` remain connected, maximizing the length of
the shortest `s`-`t` path after the edges of `S` are removed.

This is a budget-constrained shortest-path interdiction problem: it is NP-hard (it embeds
a knapsack-flavoured choice on top of the interdiction), the optimum is unknown, and many
heuristics (cost-benefit greedy, best-improvement local search, randomized restarts,
Lagrangian-style relaxations) are viable and tend to diverge.

## Input

The first line contains five integers:

```
n m s t C
```

- `n` — number of habitat patches (nodes), numbered `1..n`.
- `m` — number of corridors (edges).
- `s` — the den (source node).
- `t` — the watering ground (sink node), `s != t`.
- `C` — the total clearing budget.

Each of the next `m` lines describes one corridor (edge index `1..m` in input order):

```
u v w c
```

meaning a two-way corridor between patches `u` and `v` with traversal length `w`
(`1 <= w`) and clearing cost `c` (`1 <= c`). The graph may contain parallel corridors
and is guaranteed to have `s` connected to `t`.

## Output

On the first line print `r` — the number of corridors you clear (`0 <= r <= m`).
On each of the next `r` lines print one corridor index (an integer in `1..m`). All indices
must be distinct.

Printing `0` on the first line (and nothing else) is always valid.

## Feasibility

An output is feasible iff:

- `0 <= r <= m`;
- every printed index is in `1..m` and all are distinct;
- the total clearing cost of the chosen corridors is at most `C`;
- after removing exactly the chosen corridors, `s` and `t` are still connected.

Any infeasible output scores `0`.

## Objective

Let `F` be the shortest-path traversal length from `s` to `t` after your chosen corridors
are removed. You are **maximizing** `F`.

## Scoring

Let `B` be the shortest-path traversal length from `s` to `t` in the original network (no
corridors cleared). The score for a test is

```
ratio = min(1.0, 0.1 * F / B)
```

A do-nothing submission (`F = B`) scores `0.1`; forcing a ten-fold detour (`F = 10*B`)
caps the score at `1.0`. The overall score is the mean ratio across all tests.

## Constraints

- `2 <= n <= 450`, `1 <= m <= 2600`.
- `1 <= w <= 60`.
- `1 <= c <= 50`.
- `1 <= C <= 100000`.
- Time limit: 5 s. Memory limit: 512 MB.

## Example

Input:

```
4 5 1 4 6
1 2 1 3
2 4 1 3
1 3 8 10
3 4 8 10
2 3 2 2
```

Original shortest route: `1 -> 2 -> 4` with total length `2`, so `B = 2`.

With a budget of `C = 6`, clearing corridors `1` (the `1-2` link, cost `3`) and `2` (the
`2-4` link, cost `3`) spends exactly `6`. The cheap route is gone; the fastest remaining
route becomes `1 -> 3 -> 4` with length `16`, so `F = 16`. The den and watering ground are
still connected, so this is feasible.

Score for this test: `min(1.0, 0.1 * 16 / 2) = min(1.0, 0.8) = 0.8`.

A valid worked output:

```
2
1
2
```
