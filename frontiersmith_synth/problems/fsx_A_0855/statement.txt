# Punching Holes Without Dropping the Sheet

## Problem

A CNC turret press punches holes in a sheet-metal blank shaped as an `R x C`
grid. Some cells are the **frame** (gripped by two edge clamps, never punched);
some are outside the blank (**void**, never material); the rest are **punch
jobs**, each needing one of `T` turret **tool** positions on a dial `1..T`.
Output the **order** to punch all `N` jobs.

After every punch, the remaining material (frame + not-yet-punched jobs) must
stay attached to the clamps. If a punch severs some *other* unpunched job from
the frame (no 4-directional chain of present cells reaches it), that job
becomes scrap the instant it happens — a **web violation** — even though it
stays technically "present" until punched too. Between consecutive punches
the turret rotates to the next job's tool, costing the shorter arc around the
dial.

## Input

```
R C N T
R lines of C characters (one grid row each):
  '#' = frame (always present)   '.' = a punch job   'x' = void (not part of the blank)
N lines "r c tool" (0-indexed row, col; tool in [1,T]): the punch jobs, listed in
  row-major scan order of the grid's '.' cells. This listing order is exactly what
  your OUTPUT indices 1..N refer to.
```
The frame is always the grid's complete outer border (row `0`, row `R-1`,
column `0`, column `C-1`): a single connected ring, so `(0,0)` is always part
of it. Every `.` cell is guaranteed 4-directionally reachable from the frame
through other `.`/`#` cells in the unpunched (`t=0`) sheet.

## Output

`N` integers `p_1 ... p_N`: a permutation of `1..N`, where `p_k` is the index of
the job punched at step `k`.

## Feasibility

The output must be a permutation of exactly `1..N` (every job punched once, no
repeats, no out-of-range index, no trailing tokens); any violation scores `0`.
Web violations are **not** a feasibility failure — they are a heavy cost
penalty, described next.

## Objective (minimize)

The turret starts parked at tool `1`. `F` = (sum, over consecutive punches, of
the circular tool distance `min(|a-b|, T-|a-b|)`, charging the first punch from
the parked position) `+ T *` (the number of distinct cells that ever become
newly stranded — charged once, the first time each loses its connection).
Minimize `F`.

## Scoring

Let `B` be the objective of the checker's own baseline: punch the jobs in their
listed (row-major) order — no reasoning about tools or geometry at all (floored
at `1` if that construction happens to score `0`). Your score on a test is
```
ratio = min(1000, 100 * B / max(1, F)) / 1000
```
so the raw listing order scores `0.1`, and a genuinely better order scores
higher, capped at `1.0` (ten times the baseline). Per-test ratios are averaged.

## Constraints

`6 <= R <= 12`, `8 <= C <= 16`, `2 <= T <= 4`, `1 <= N <= 145`. Time limit: 3 s.
Memory limit: 256 MB.

## Example

```
6 9 19 3
#########
#...xxxx#
#......x#
#...xxxx#
#.......#
#########
1 1 2
1 2 2
1 3 2
2 1 2
2 2 2
2 3 2
2 4 1
2 5 3
2 6 3
3 1 2
3 2 2
3 3 2
4 1 2
4 2 2
4 3 2
4 4 2
4 5 2
4 6 2
4 7 2
```

Jobs `1-6` and `10-19` are open field, tool `2`. On the original sheet, job
`7` at `(2,4)` is a single-cell **corridor**: the *only* cell adjacent to the
2-cell **room**, jobs `8-9` at `(2,5)`/`(2,6)`, tool `3` — removing it while
the room stands always disconnects the room. (The raw listing order strands
jobs `7-9` together one step earlier still, at job `6`: jobs `1-5` are
already gone by then, so job `6` is the cluster's last link at that moment.
Either way `B = 13`.)

Punching by ascending tool (`7` first — tool `1` is lowest — then all
tool-`2` jobs, then `8`,`9`) severs the room the instant `7` is punched:
rotation `2`, plus one stranded-event penalty `2*3 = 6`, `F = 8`, ratio
`0.1625`. Punching the room first (`9`,`8`), *then* its corridor (`7`), then
everything else never disconnects anything: `F = 3` (rotation only), ratio
`0.4333`. The lesson isn't "batch by tool" — it's "clear what's behind a
doorway before you punch the doorway."
