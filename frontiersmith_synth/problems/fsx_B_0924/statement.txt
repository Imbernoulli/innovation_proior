# Grow a Sharp Crystal, Not a Blob

## Problem

A crystal nucleates from a single seed cell at the origin and grows one cell at a
time by an **Eden process** on the integer lattice: repeatedly, some empty cell
adjacent (4-neighbor) to the current cluster attaches. You do not choose which
cell attaches. Instead, you declare, once, an **anisotropy field**: 72
nonnegative numbers `w[0..71]`, one per 5-degree direction bin (bin `i` covers
angles `[5*i, 5*(i+1))`, measured from the seed). The simulator converts `w`
into an exact per-bin cell **budget** by apportioning `M-1` cells across the 72
bins in proportion to `w` (largest-remainder rounding, so the budgets are
integers summing to `M-1`). Growth then proceeds: at each step a candidate
frontier cell (empty, touching the cluster) is drawn uniformly at random; if
its direction bin's budget is already used up, that cell is set aside;
otherwise it attaches and its bin's spent budget increases by one. Each
direction's final reach is thus a **hard, self-limiting function of its
declared budget** -- no direction can "run away" no matter how large its
weight, since it stops the instant its own budget is spent. If concentrating
almost all budget on a few bins ever makes every reachable candidate blocked
before mass `M` is reached (no path to the budgeted bins exists yet), the
simulator falls back to plain unconstrained growth for the remaining cells --
so mass always reaches exactly `M`, but starving reachability this way never
helps your silhouette match. All randomness comes from a seed in the input
(never from your output), so the same `w` always regrows the exact same
cluster on a given test.

Separately, the input describes a **target shape**: `K` vertices, vertex `i`
placed at angle `2*pi*i/K` and distance `r_i` from the seed, connected in order
(and the last back to the first) into a star-shaped polygon around the seed. A
lattice cell is "inside" the target if its distance from the seed does not
exceed the polygon boundary's distance in that exact direction (the ray from
the seed through the cell). `M` is defined as the number of lattice cells
inside this target region, so your grown cluster and the target region always
have equal size `M`; only their overlap can differ.

## Input

```
SEED K M
r_0 r_1 ... r_{K-1}
```
`SEED` seeds the growth simulator. `4 <= K <= 16`. `r_i` are positive integers.

## Output

One line: 72 nonnegative real numbers `w[0] ... w[71]` (the anisotropy field).

## Feasibility

Exactly 72 tokens, each a finite real in `[0, 1e12]`, no trailing tokens, and not
all zero. Any violation scores `0`.

## Objective

Maximize the overlap between your grown cluster and the target region.

## Scoring

Let the simulator grow a cluster `C` from your `w` (mass `M`, as above), and let
`F = |C intersect Target|`. Let `B` be the intersection size obtained the exact
same way using the **isotropic** field `w[i] = 1` for all `i` (an equal budget
in every direction -- the KPZ-universal round blob of the same mass `M`). Your
ratio for a test is
```
ratio = min(1000, 100 * F / max(1, B)) / 1000
```
so the isotropic split always scores `0.1`, and any anisotropy that raises the
overlap scores higher (capped at `1.0`). Final score is the mean ratio over 10
tests.

## Constraints

* `4 <= K <= 16`, `1 <= r_i <= 200`.
* `M` is at most about `5000` (derived from `K, r_i`; never chosen by you).
* Time limit: 3 s. Memory limit: 512 MB.

## Example

With `K=4` and `r = [6,6,6,6]` the target is a small, direction-independent
diamond: the isotropic field already covers it well, so this scale mainly
sanity-checks the format. On harder tests most vertices sit at a short "facet"
radius (say `6..9`) while a few spike out to a much longer "tip" radius (say
`80..120`): the target becomes a thin, spiky star. An isotropic split gives
every direction the SAME final reach, fixed by `M`'s total area alone -- it
can never stretch to the tips no matter how the single global budget is tuned.
Reading off the target's own boundary radius at a single sample angle per bin
still routes too little budget toward a bin that straddles a spike, since the
true radius there rockets from small to huge within just 5 degrees and a point
sample misses that concentrated area. Properly accounting for how much of the
target's AREA actually falls in each bin (radius squared, integrated across
the bin's arc, not sampled at one angle) routes the right budget to the tips
and starves the facets correctly, matching the star far better than `0.1`.
