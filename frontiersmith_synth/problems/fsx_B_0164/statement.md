# Hump-Yard Dwell Scaling Law: Fit a Law that Extrapolates to a Busier Yard

## Title
Hump-Yard Dwell Scaling Law

## Problem
The Ridgewater classification (hump) yard sorts inbound freight cars. Its performance is
summarised by a single number: **D**, the mean dwell time (in hours) that a car spends in
the yard between arrival and departure on its outbound block.

Operations research says `D` follows a smooth **scaling law** in two design/traffic knobs:

- `T` — the number of active **classification (bowl) tracks** the yard runs with, and
- `V` — the daily **inbound volume** of cars, measured in hundreds of cars/day.

Adding tracks lowers dwell (more parallelism); raising volume increases dwell (congestion).
The exact functional form is unknown to you and is *not* a straight line — it saturates
toward an irreducible floor as tracks grow, and it steepens super-linearly as volume grows.

You are given a table of measured operating points `(T, V, D)` sampled from the yard's
**current, lightly loaded** operating envelope (few tracks, low volume). Your job is to
**discover a closed-form law** `D = f(T, V)` that not only fits these points but, crucially,
**extrapolates** to a **future busier yard** (more tracks *and* much higher volume) that lies
entirely outside the sampled region.

## Input (stdin)
```
Ntrain
T_1 V_1 D_1
T_2 V_2 D_2
...
T_Ntrain V_Ntrain D_Ntrain
```
- Line 1: integer `Ntrain`, the number of measured operating points.
- Each following line: three real numbers `T V D` (tracks, volume in hundreds/day, dwell hours).
- All `T > 0`, `V > 0`, `D > 0`. The measurements contain small multiplicative measurement noise.

## Output (stdout)
A single line: a **closed-form expression** for `D` as a function of the variables `T` and `V`,
written as a Python expression string.

Allowed tokens:
- variables `T` and `V`;
- numeric literals (e.g. `0.85`, `3`, `1e-2`);
- binary operators `+  -  *  /  **`;
- unary `+` / `-`;
- parentheses;
- the constants `pi`, `e`;
- the functions `exp`, `log`, `log10`, `sqrt`, `abs`, `pow`, `sin`, `cos`.

Nothing else (no names, attributes, indexing, comprehensions, or calls to other functions).

**Example of the OUTPUT FORMAT ONLY** (this is an *illustrative shape*, deliberately a
different, unrelated form — it is **NOT** the hidden law; do not submit it):
```
1.0 + log(1 + T) * exp(-V/10)
```

## Feasibility
The submitted expression must:
- parse using only the allowed tokens above;
- evaluate to a **finite real number** at every held-out evaluation point (positive `T`, `V`);
- contain no `nan`/`inf` and raise no math error on the held-out inputs.

Any violation scores `0`.

## Objective
Minimise the **root-mean-square extrapolation error** of your law on a hidden **held-out
region** of larger `T` and larger `V` (a busier future yard), regenerated deterministically
by the grader. Simpler laws are preferred: a mild complexity penalty applies to very large
expressions, so a compact law that generalises beats an over-parameterised one that
memorises the training noise.

## Scoring
Deterministic. Let `F` be the (complexity-adjusted) held-out RMSE of your expression and let
`B` be the held-out RMSE of the grader's internal baseline (a single product power-law fitted
by log-linear regression on the training table). The score is

```
sc    = min(1000, 100 * B / max(1e-9, F))
Ratio = sc / 1000
```

Reproducing the baseline scores ≈ `0.10`; a law that extrapolates ~10x better caps at `1.0`.
Irreducible measurement noise on the held-out region keeps the maximum below `1.0`.

## Constraints
- `Ntrain` = 48 (an 8x6 grid of tracks x volume).
- Held-out region: 16 points at strictly larger `T` and `V` than any training point.
- Expression length ≤ 5000 characters.
- Grader runs in well under the time/memory limits (pure CPU, no randomness in the score).

## Example (worked score)
Suppose the training table were generated by a law whose true dwell at the busier yard has
RMS magnitude ~8 hours, the internal log-linear baseline mispredicts the held-out region with
RMSE `B = 6.0`, and your submitted law achieves held-out RMSE `F = 1.5` with no complexity
penalty. Then `sc = min(1000, 100 * 6.0 / 1.5) = 400` and `Ratio = 0.400`. A constant or a
law of the wrong functional shape would land near `F ≈ B`, i.e. `Ratio ≈ 0.10`.
