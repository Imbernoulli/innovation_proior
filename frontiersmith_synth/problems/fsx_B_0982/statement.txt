# The Oasis Cooperative's Fair Ditch Web

## Problem

An oasis cooperative shares one spring. The spring, the routing junctions, and the
member fields are all nodes of a graph; each candidate ditch segment is an edge with
a **length** (its share of your digging budget) and a **retention rate**: the
fraction of water that survives crossing it, since porous desert soil absorbs some
water into the ground on every segment it flows through. Loss is **multiplicative**
— water crossing a chain of segments survives the *product* of their retention
rates, so a route's real cost compounds with every extra hop, not just with its
physical length. Every field connects to the rest of the network through exactly
one inlet segment. You may build any subset of the candidate segments, and — since
a segment, once dug, can carry water toward several different fields — the routes
you commit for different fields may overlap in part, run fully separately, or
together trace out a network with cycles in it (built segments are shared
infrastructure; each individual field's own route must still be a simple walk
that never revisits a node).

You plan a *water-delivery plan*: out of the spring's fixed total discharge `D`,
decide how much to commit to each of one or more paths ending at a field, and which
segments to build to carry them. Every candidate segment has the same carrying
capacity `CAP`; every commitment that routes through a segment adds to what that
segment must carry. The cooperative's fairness rule: only the **worst-served**
field's moisture counts, so an efficient plan that starves one distant field is
worthless next to a plan that keeps everyone irrigated.

## Input

```
N M F D CAP L
f_1 f_2 ... f_F
u_1 v_1 len_1 ret_1
...
u_M v_M len_M ret_M
```

`N` nodes, `0`-indexed, node `0` is the spring. `M` candidate undirected ditch
segments; `len_i>=1` is its digging cost, `ret_i` in `[500,995]` is its retention in
**parts per thousand** (fraction retained = `ret_i/1000`). No two segments join the
same pair of endpoints, and no segment joins a node to itself. The `F` field node
ids follow on their own line (each has exactly one incident candidate segment).
`D`, `CAP`, `L` are positive integers: total spring discharge, per-segment
capacity, and total ditch-length budget.

## Output

```
K
f_1 X_1 Lp_1 p_1[0] ... p_1[Lp_1-1]
...
f_K X_K Lp_K p_K[0] ... p_K[Lp_K-1]
```

`K` commitments (`0<=K<=600`). Each line: a field `f_i`, a nonnegative real `X_i`
(water pushed into this path from the spring, before any losses), a path length
`Lp_i<=260` and its node ids, `p[0]=0` through `p[Lp-1]=f_i`, consecutive nodes
joined by a real candidate segment, no repeated node. You may commit several
separate paths to the same field.

## Feasibility

Every path must be a real, simple, spring-to-field walk over candidate segments.
Walking a committed path, the amount **entering** its first segment is `X_i`; the
amount entering each later segment is what survived every earlier segment on that
same path. For every segment that appears in *some* committed path, the sum — over
every commitment using it — of the amount entering it there must not exceed `CAP`.
The sum of all `X_i` must not exceed `D`. The total length of the *distinct*
segments referenced by any commitment must not exceed `L`. Any violation scores `0`.

## Objective

For field `f`, its moisture is the sum, over its commitments, of what finally
survives to reach it (the path's full retention product times that commitment's
`X_i`). Maximize `F = min` moisture over all `F` fields.

## Scoring

Let `B` be the delivery of a reference plan that gives every field its fewest-hops
route from the spring and splits `min(D,CAP)` **flatly** across all `F` fields (no
compensation for the worse-off). `ratio = min(1000, 100*F/max(1e-9,B))/1000`, so
the flat-split reference scores `0.1`, and a plan that lifts the worst field well
above that reference scores higher, capped at `1.0`. Per-test ratios are averaged.

## Constraints

`8<=N<=180`, `M<=350`, `2<=F<=10`, `1<=len_i<=10`. Time limit 4s, memory 512MB.

## Example

Spring `0`. Hub `1` (edge `0-1`, len `2`, ret `950`). Near field `2` off the hub
(edge `1-2`, len `1`, ret `900`). Far field `4`, reached only via `1-3` (len `3`,
ret `900`) then `3-4` (len `2`, ret `850`). `D=1000, CAP=1000, L=20`.

The flat-split reference sends `500` to each: field `2` gets
`500*0.95*0.9=427.5`; field `4` gets `500*0.95*0.9*0.85=363.375`; so `B=363.375`.
A plan that instead gives field `2` only `300` and field `4` `700` gets
`300*0.95*0.9=256.5` and `700*0.95*0.9*0.85=508.7`: the minimum *dropped* to
`256.5` — moving flow toward the already-favored near field never helps the
worst-served field. The winning move goes the other way: equalize deliveries by
solving `X_2*0.855 = X_4*0.72675` subject to `X_2+X_4<=1000`, giving roughly
`X_2=459.5, X_4=540.5`, both landing near `392.8` — beating `B` on the minimum,
even though it sends the far field *more* raw water than the near one.
