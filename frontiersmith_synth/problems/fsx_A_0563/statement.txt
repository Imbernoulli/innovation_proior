# Robotic Telescope Night Plan

## Problem
A robotic telescope observes `N` sky targets during one night. Target `i` sits at a
1-D sky coordinate `p_i` and is worth `v_i`. The telescope starts the night at
coordinate `0` with pointing **drift** `0`.

The night proceeds as a sequence of **actions**:

- **Observe target `i`.** The mount first *slews* from its current coordinate `c` to
  `p_i`, travelling a distance `d = |c - p_i|`. Slewing costs `d` units of time and
  **increases the drift by `k*d`** (motion-proportional drift). The observation then
  collects a yield of `v_i * acc[min(drift, Dmax)]`, where the drift is the value
  *after* this slew, and `acc[]` is a fixed, non-increasing accuracy profile given in
  the input (`acc[0] = SCALE`, decaying steeply toward a small floor). The mount stays
  at `p_i`.
- **Recalibrate.** Resets the drift to `0` instantly, but costs `R` units of time. At
  most `K` recalibrations may be performed during the night.

The total time spent (all slews plus all recalibrations) may not exceed the night
budget `Tb`. Each target may be observed at most once; targets you never observe simply
contribute nothing.

Accuracy is therefore a resource the tour itself creates and spends: every slew erodes
it, every recalibration (paid for out of a scarce time budget, and limited to `K`
uses) restores it. Because there are more valuable clusters of targets than there are
recalibrations, you must decide **which** high-value regions deserve a fresh
calibration and route the plan so those regions are observed while the drift is small.

## Input
```
N P k K R Tb SCALE Dmax
p_1 v_1
...
p_N v_N
acc[0] acc[1] ... acc[Dmax]
```
Coordinates `p_i` are distinct integers in `[1, P]`. The final block lists `Dmax+1`
integers, the accuracy profile.

## Output
First line: an integer `A`, the number of actions. Then `A` lines, each one of:

- `1 i` — observe target `i` (`1 <= i <= N`), or
- `0`  — recalibrate.

## Feasibility
The number of `0` actions is at most `K`; no target index appears in two `1 i`
actions; and the total time (sum of slew distances plus `R` per recalibration) is at
most `Tb`. Any violation, or a malformed/trailing token, scores `0`.

## Objective
Maximize `F`, the total yield summed over all observed targets, where each target
contributes `v_i * acc[min(D_i, Dmax)]` and `D_i` is the drift at the instant it is
observed.

## Scoring
Let `B` be the yield of the reference *monotone sweep*: visit every target in
increasing coordinate order starting from `0`, performing no recalibration. Your score
is
```
ratio = min(1.0, 0.1 * F / B)
```
So a plan matching the baseline scores `0.1`; reaching `10*B` (or more) caps at `1.0`.
Feasible-but-infeasible outputs score `0`.

## Constraints
`N` up to ~2000, `P` up to ~7300, `k = 1`, `2 <= K < C` recalibrations. Time limit
3 s, memory limit 512 MB. Each input file is a few hundred KB.

## Example
Input (tiny, `SCALE = 1000`):
```
3 100 1 1 40 200 1000 100
10 5
90 900
12 4
0 0 0 ...   (acc[d] decays; e.g. acc[0]=1000, acc[80]~=6, acc[100]~=1)
```
Sorted by coordinate the targets are `#1(10,5), #3(12,4), #2(90,900)`. The monotone
sweep observes `#2` at drift `90`, where `acc[90]` is tiny, so `B` is small. A stronger
plan slews across the two cheap near targets, **recalibrates**, then slews the short
remaining hop to the jackpot `#2` — observing its value `900` at near-zero drift, for a
far larger `F` and a much higher ratio. Actions:
```
5
1 1
1 3
0
1 2
```
(observe `#1`, `#3`, recalibrate, observe `#2`).
