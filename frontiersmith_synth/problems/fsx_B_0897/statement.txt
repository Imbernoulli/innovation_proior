# Stencil Counters: Bridging the Floating Middles

## Problem

A laser-cut stencil has a rigid outer **frame** and some floating interior pieces
called **counters** (like the middle bar of "H" or the dot of "i") that are not
attached to anything. You must add rectangular blocks of **bridge** material,
cut from the surrounding background, to anchor every counter to the frame — and
you want the bridges to be as visually unobtrusive as possible.

The stencil is a grid of `R` rows x `C` columns (1-indexed). Every cell is one of:
frame (`#`), one of `K` counters (digit `1..K`, a solid axis-aligned rectangle
per counter), or background (`.`, buildable). A second grid gives a **visual
cost** `0-9` per cell (higher near strokes — frame or counter cells — where a
bridge is most distracting).

A **bridge** is an axis-aligned rectangle of background cells you convert to
material, PLUS a chosen orientation `o` (you state it — required widths can
legitimately exceed the span length, so shape alone would be ambiguous):
`o=0` **vertical**, long axis along rows, length `L = h` (row-span), width
`W = w` (column-span); `o=1` **horizontal**, `L = w` (column-span),
`W = h` (row-span). A vertical bridge must have empty rows `r1-1` (its
**front**) and `r2+1` (its **back**) — the single row strip just above and
just below it — each entirely one structure (all `#`, or all one counter's
digit), and the two structures must differ. A horizontal bridge works the same
way with columns `c1-1`/`c2+1`. Bridges may not overlap each other or existing
frame/counter cells.

**The stiffness rule (why shape beats size).** A bridge resists being stretched
along its own long axis cheaply: it contributes `A * W / L` to the stiffness
along its own axis. Perpendicular to its axis it resists bending far less
efficiently (a classic cantilever): it contributes `W^3 / (BD * L^3)` to the
stiffness across its axis (`A`, `BD` given in the input). A **vertical** bridge
feeds its axial term into a touched counter's **row-stiffness** and its bending
term into that counter's **column-stiffness**; a **horizontal** bridge does the
reverse. A counter's row/column-stiffness is the sum of contributions from every
bridge touching it (frame-side or counter-side). Each counter `i` needs BOTH
totals `>= Sreq_i` (given in the input).

## Input

```
R C K A BD
```
`R` lines of `C` characters (structure grid), then `R` lines of `C` characters
(cost grid, digits `0-9`), then `K` lines `id Sreq_id`.

## Output

`M`, then `M` lines `o r1 c1 r2 c2` — one axis-aligned rectangle each, with its
declared orientation.

## Feasibility

Every bridge must obey the geometry rules above (bounds, front/back single
structure, no overlap); every counter must reach the frame through the bridge
graph (frame and counters as nodes, bridges as edges); every counter's row- and
column-stiffness must meet its threshold. Any violation scores `0`.

## Objective

Minimize `F`, the sum of visual costs of all bridge cells.

## Scoring

The checker computes an internal baseline `B`: for each counter, one oversized
(1.4x safety-margin) rod in its nearest single direction. Score per test is
`ratio = min(1000, 100*B/max(1,F)) / 1000`, averaged over tests.

## Constraints

`R, C` up to about `600`; `1 <= K <= 3`; `A, BD` positive integers; time limit
4 s, memory 512 MB.

## Example

`A=22, BD=4`. A 62x62 grid: frame border, one counter at rows/cols `12..51`
(a 40x40 block), gap `L=10` to the frame on every side, `Sreq_1 = 3`. Assume
every open cell costs `1`. One rod straight up needs width `max(ceil(3*10/22),
ceil(10*cbrt(3*4))) = max(2, 23) = 23` (bending, not axial, dictates it) — cost
`23*10 = 230`. The 1.4x-margin baseline uses width `33`, cost `B = 330`
(`ratio = 0.14`). But a **vertical** rod up (width `ceil(3*10/22)=2`, cost `20`)
plus a **horizontal** rod left (same, cost `20`) together give row-stiffness
`22*2/10 = 4.4 >= 3` and column-stiffness `4.4 >= 3` — both satisfied for total
cost `40` (`ratio = 0.825`), less than a sixth of the single fat rod's cost.
