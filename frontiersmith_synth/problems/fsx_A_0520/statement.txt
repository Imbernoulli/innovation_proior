# Courier Depots That Survive Every Season

## Problem
A courier company serves `N` city addresses on an integer grid. Demand is **seasonal**:
there are `K` seasons, and address `i` generates a known integer demand `w[i][k]` in
season `k`. You must place `P` courier depots at integer grid points. The depots are
**built once and stay fixed for every season** — you cannot move them when the season
changes.

In a given season every address is served from its nearest depot (Manhattan / L1
distance). Because the depot layout is frozen, a layout that is cheap on average can be
ruinous in the one season whose hot addresses it neglected. You are graded on your
**worst season**, so you must hedge: site depots that stay tolerable in *every* season's
demand pattern, not just the pooled average.

## Input
```
N P K
x_1 y_1 w_1_1 w_1_2 ... w_1_K
x_2 y_2 w_2_1 w_2_2 ... w_2_K
...
x_N y_N w_N_1 w_N_2 ... w_N_K
```
- `x_i, y_i` are the integer coordinates of address `i`, with `0 <= x_i, y_i <= 100000`.
- `w_i_k` is the (non-negative) integer demand of address `i` in season `k`.
- Addresses may share coordinates.

## Output
Print `P` lines, each `dx dy`: the integer coordinates of a depot, with
`0 <= dx, dy <= 100000`. Depots may coincide (a coincident depot simply wastes capacity).

## Feasibility
Exactly `P` lines, each two integers in `[0, 100000]`. Anything else (wrong count, an
out-of-range or non-integer coordinate, trailing tokens) is infeasible and scores 0.

## Objective (minimize)
For address `i`, let `d_i = min over depots p of ( |x_i - dx_p| + |y_i - dy_p| )` be the
Manhattan distance to its nearest depot. Note `d_i` depends only on the depot layout, not
on the season. The cost of season `k` is
```
cost_k = sum over i of  w[i][k] * d_i
```
and your score is driven by the **worst season**:
```
F = max over k of cost_k
```
You minimize `F`. Lowering the average of the `cost_k` is not enough — a single neglected
season sets `F`.

## Scoring
Let `B` be the worst-season cost `F` of the reference **single-hub** layout that places all
`P` depots at the unweighted integer centroid of the addresses
(`floor(mean x)`, `floor(mean y)`). Your ratio is
```
ratio = min( 1.0 , 0.1 * B / max(1, F) )
```
So the single-hub layout scores about `0.1`; halving the worst-season cost roughly doubles
your ratio; the ratio is capped at `1.0` (a 10x improvement over the single hub). Higher is
better. The 10 test cases are averaged.

## Constraints
- `1 <= K <= 8`, `1 <= P <= 40`, `1 <= N <= 3000`, `P` may be smaller or larger than `K`.
- Time limit: 3 s. Memory limit: 512 MB.
- All demands and coordinates are deterministic functions of the test id.

## Example
```
4 2 2
0 0 10 0
0 10 10 0
100 0 0 10
100 10 0 10
```
Two seasons. Season 1's demand sits on the left two addresses; season 2's on the right two
(nearly disjoint hot zones). With `P=2`, placing one depot on the left cluster
`(0,5)` and one on the right `(100,5)` gives every hot address distance 5, so
`cost_1 = 10*5 + 10*5 = 100`, `cost_2 = 100`, hence `F = 100`. A layout that instead
minimized the pooled average by putting both depots near the middle would leave one season
with distance ~100 addresses and blow up `F`. The single-hub centroid `(50,5)` yields
`d_i = 50` for every hot address, so `B = 10*50 + 10*50 = 1000` and the good layout scores
`0.1 * 1000 / 100 = 1.0`.
```
2
0 5
100 5
```
