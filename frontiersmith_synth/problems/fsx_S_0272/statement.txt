# Aurora Station: Two-Habitat Crew Split

## Problem

The **Aurora** polar research base is being reorganised for the long winter. The `n`
overwintering crew must be split between two pressurised habitats — **Habitat A** and
**Habitat B**. Life-support and rota planning require the two habitats to hold *roughly
the same number of people*: the difference in headcount may be at most `L`.

Long confinement breeds friction. For some pairs of crew members there is a measured
**friction weight** `w > 0` — the higher the weight, the worse the two get on when they
share a habitat. Station morale is best when as much friction as possible is *split across
the two habitats* (people who clash are kept apart). Your job is to choose the split that
**maximises the total friction weight of the pairs whose two members end up in different
habitats**.

Formally: you are given an undirected weighted graph on `n` crew (parallel friction
records allowed). Partition the vertices into two sets `A` and `B` with
`| |A| - |B| | <= L`, maximising the total weight of edges with one endpoint in `A` and
the other in `B` (the *cut weight*).

This is a balance-constrained maximum-cut problem: it is NP-hard, the optimum is unknown,
and many heuristics (contiguous split, single-move local search, swap-based local search,
randomised multi-restart) are viable and diverge.

## Input

The first line contains three integers:

```
n m L
```

- `n` — number of crew members, numbered `1..n`.
- `m` — number of friction records (edges).
- `L` — the maximum allowed difference between the two headcounts.

Each of the next `m` lines describes one friction record:

```
u v w
```

meaning crew members `u` and `v` (`u != v`) clash with friction weight `w` (`1 <= w`).
The same pair may appear more than once (records accumulate).

## Output

On the first line print `a` — the number of crew you assign to **Habitat A**
(`0 <= a <= n`). On each of the next `a` lines print one crew id assigned to Habitat A
(an integer in `1..n`). All printed ids must be distinct. Every crew member **not** listed
is assigned to Habitat B.

## Feasibility

An output is feasible iff:

- `0 <= a <= n`;
- every printed id is in `1..n` and all are distinct;
- the headcount balance holds: `| a - (n - a) | <= L`.

Any infeasible output scores `0`.

## Objective

Let `F` be the total friction weight of all records `(u, v, w)` whose members `u` and `v`
end up in **different** habitats. You are **maximising** `F`.

## Scoring

The checker builds an internal baseline `B`: the cut weight of the *contiguous split* that
places crew `1 .. floor(n/2)` in Habitat A and the rest in Habitat B (always balance-feasible).
The score for a test is

```
ratio = min(1.0, 0.1 * F / B)
```

The contiguous split itself (`F = B`) scores `0.1`; reaching a ten-fold improvement over the
baseline (`F = 10*B`) caps the score at `1.0`. The overall score is the mean ratio across all
tests.

## Constraints

- `2 <= n <= 400`.
- `1 <= m <= 4000`.
- `1 <= w <= 40`.
- `1 <= L <= n`.
- The graph always contains the chain records `(i, i+1)` for `1 <= i < n`, so the baseline
  cut weight `B` is strictly positive.
- Time limit: 5 s. Memory limit: 512 MB.

## Example

Input:

```
6 8 1
1 2 10
2 3 10
3 4 10
4 5 10
5 6 10
1 4 3
2 5 3
3 6 3
```

The baseline contiguous split puts `{1,2,3}` in Habitat A and `{4,5,6}` in Habitat B. The
crossing records are `3-4` (10), `1-4` (3), `2-5` (3) and `3-6` (3), so `B = 19`.

With `L = 1` the two habitats must each hold exactly `3` crew. The alternating split
`A = {1,3,5}`, `B = {2,4,6}` cuts every chain record (`5 * 10 = 50`) plus all three extra
records (`3 * 3 = 9`), giving `F = 59`.

Score for this test: `min(1.0, 0.1 * 59 / 19) = min(1.0, 0.3105) = 0.3105`.

A valid worked output:

```
3
1
3
5
```
