# Cave-In: Collapsing Chambers to Strand the Rival Expedition

## Problem

Two speleology teams are racing to survey the *Hollow Reach* cave system. Your team has
already mapped it: it is a network of **chambers** connected by two-way **passages**, each
passage taking a known amount of crawling time. The rival team always follows the fastest
route from the cave **mouth** (chamber `s`) to the fabled **crystal vault** (chamber `t`).

Your saboteurs can set charges to **collapse at most `k` chambers**, permanently sealing
every passage that touched a collapsed chamber. But collapsing is regulated: you may **not**
collapse the mouth `s` or the vault `t`, and if the vault is ever completely cut off from
the mouth the expedition guild voids your whole operation. Choose which chambers to collapse
so that the rival's *fastest surviving route* from mouth to vault is as **long** as possible.

Formally: you are given an undirected weighted (multi)graph. Choose a set `S` of at most `k`
vertices, with `s, t \notin S`, such that after deleting the vertices of `S` (and all their
incident edges) `s` and `t` remain connected, maximizing the length of the shortest `s`-`t`
path in the surviving graph.

This is a **vertex shortest-path interdiction** problem: it is NP-hard, the optimum is
unknown, and many heuristics (greedy along the current route, best-improvement local search,
randomized multi-restart) are viable and diverge.

## Input

The first line contains five integers:

```
n m s t k
```

- `n` — number of chambers (vertices), numbered `1..n`.
- `m` — number of passages (edges).
- `s` — the cave mouth (source chamber).
- `t` — the crystal vault (sink chamber), `s != t`.
- `k` — the maximum number of chambers you may collapse.

Each of the next `m` lines describes one passage:

```
u v w
```

meaning a two-way passage between chambers `u` and `v` with crawling time `w` (`1 <= w`).
The graph may contain parallel passages and self-loops are absent. It is guaranteed that
`s` is connected to `t`.

## Output

On the first line print `r` — the number of chambers you collapse (`0 <= r <= k`).
On each of the next `r` lines print one chamber index (an integer in `1..n`). All indices
must be distinct, and none of them may equal `s` or `t`.

Printing `0` on the first line (and nothing else) is always valid.

## Feasibility

An output is feasible iff:

- `0 <= r <= k`;
- every printed index is in `1..n`, all are distinct, and none equals `s` or `t`;
- after removing exactly the chosen chambers (and their incident passages), `s` and `t`
  are still connected.

Any infeasible output scores `0`.

## Objective

Let `F` be the shortest-path crawling time from `s` to `t` after your chosen chambers are
collapsed. You are **maximizing** `F`.

## Scoring

Let `B` be the shortest-path crawling time from `s` to `t` in the original cave (no chambers
collapsed). The score for a test is

```
ratio = min(1.0, 0.1 * F / B)
```

A do-nothing submission (`F = B`) scores `0.1`; forcing a ten-fold detour (`F = 10*B`) caps
the score at `1.0`. The overall score is the mean ratio across all tests.

## Constraints

- `2 <= n <= 500`, `1 <= m <= 2500`.
- `1 <= w <= 60`.
- `0 <= k <= 20`.
- Time limit: 5 s. Memory limit: 512 MB.

## Example

Input:

```
5 6 1 5 1
1 2 1
2 5 1
1 3 4
3 5 4
2 4 3
4 5 3
```

Original fastest route: `1 -> 2 -> 5` with length `2`, so `B = 2`.

With a budget of `k = 1`, collapsing chamber `2` seals the two cheap passages `1-2` and
`2-5` (and `2-4`). The fastest surviving route becomes `1 -> 3 -> 5` with length `8`, so
`F = 8`. The mouth and vault stay connected, so the output is feasible.

Score for this test: `min(1.0, 0.1 * 8 / 2) = min(1.0, 0.4) = 0.4`.

A valid worked output:

```
1
2
```
