# Cohort Shuffle: Minimizing Transmission Overlap in a Contact Network

## Problem

A public-health agency is fighting an airborne outbreak inside a large facility. It has
mapped the **contact network**: which people meet, how intensely, and how much physical
distancing each pairing would require to stay safe. To break transmission chains the agency
will split everyone into a fixed number of **staggered cohorts** (time-shifted work/living
blocks). Cohorts are numbered `1..K`; the difference between two cohort numbers measures how
far apart in the daily rotation two people are kept.

Two people who are in contact interfere with each other unless their cohorts are far enough
apart. Concretely, contact `(u, v)` carries an **intensity** `w` and a **separation demand**
`d`: the pairing is fully safe only if the two assigned cohort numbers differ by at least
`d`. If they differ by less than `d`, the pairing contributes residual transmission risk
proportional to how short the separation falls.

You must assign every person a cohort so that the **total residual transmission risk is as
small as possible**. There are only `K` cohorts, so a dense, tightly-demanding contact
network cannot be made perfectly safe — you are minimizing an unavoidable-conflict budget.

This is a weighted distance-constrained graph-labeling (frequency-assignment / T-coloring)
problem: it is NP-hard, the optimum is unknown, and greedy, best-response local search and
randomized-restart heuristics all give different, improvable results.

## Input

The first line contains three integers:

```
n m K
```

- `n` — number of people (nodes), numbered `1..n`.
- `m` — number of contact pairings (edges).
- `K` — number of available cohorts.

Each of the next `m` lines describes one contact pairing:

```
u v w d
```

- `u`, `v` — the two people in contact (`1 <= u, v <= n`, `u != v`).
- `w` — contact intensity (`1 <= w`).
- `d` — separation demand (`1 <= d`).

The network may contain parallel contact pairings between the same two people (they are
counted separately).

## Output

Print `n` integers separated by whitespace: the cohort assigned to person `1`, then person
`2`, and so on up to person `n`. Every cohort number must be an integer in `1..K`.

## Feasibility

An output is feasible iff it contains exactly `n` integers and each is in the range `1..K`.
Any other output (wrong count, out-of-range cohort, extra tokens, non-integer) scores `0`.

## Objective

For a contact pairing `(u, v, w, d)`, let `diff = |cohort(u) - cohort(v)|`. Its residual
risk is

```
w * max(0, d - diff)
```

(zero once the cohorts are at least `d` apart). Let `F` be the sum of residual risk over all
contact pairings. You are **minimizing** `F`.

## Scoring

Let `B` be the residual risk of the **do-nothing** assignment that puts every person in
cohort `1` (so `diff = 0` on every pairing), i.e. `B = sum of w * d`. The score for a test is

```
ratio = min(1.0, 0.1 * B / F)
```

Putting everyone in the same cohort (`F = B`) scores `0.1`; driving the residual risk down
to a tenth of the do-nothing budget (`F <= B/10`) caps the score at `1.0`. The overall score
is the mean ratio across all tests.

## Constraints

- `2 <= n <= 200`.
- `1 <= m <= 3000`.
- `3 <= K <= 8`.
- `1 <= w <= 9`, `1 <= d <= 3`.
- Time limit: 5 s. Memory limit: 512 MB.

## Example

Input:

```
4 4 2
1 2 5 2
2 3 3 1
3 4 4 2
1 3 2 1
```

The do-nothing assignment (everyone in cohort `1`) has residual risk
`B = 5*2 + 3*1 + 4*2 + 2*1 = 23`.

Consider the assignment `cohort = [1, 2, 1, 2]` (person 1 -> cohort 1, person 2 -> 2,
person 3 -> 1, person 4 -> 2):

- pairing `(1,2)`: `diff = 1`, risk `5 * max(0, 2 - 1) = 5`
- pairing `(2,3)`: `diff = 1`, risk `3 * max(0, 1 - 1) = 0`
- pairing `(3,4)`: `diff = 1`, risk `4 * max(0, 2 - 1) = 4`
- pairing `(1,3)`: `diff = 0`, risk `2 * max(0, 1 - 0) = 2`

so `F = 5 + 0 + 4 + 2 = 11`.

Score for this test: `min(1.0, 0.1 * 23 / 11) = min(1.0, 0.209...) = 0.209`.

A valid worked output:

```
1 2 1 2
```
