The thing that keeps nagging me about how we train vision-language models is the sheer waste. Every new VLP system trains a large image network and a large text network jointly, end to end, on a few hundred million image-text pairs, and as the backbones get bigger the bill gets bigger with them. Meanwhile the vision community keeps releasing excellent pre-trained image encoders, and the NLP community keeps releasing large language models that generate fluently and transfer to new tasks zero-shot. Those models cost accelerator-years to make. And my end-to-end pipeline can't really absorb them — it wants to learn everything from a shared starting point. I'm paying, over and over, to relearn things that already exist in a box on a shelf.

So let me take the reuse seriously: keep a pre-trained vision encoder and a pre-trained large language model, and *don't train them*. Freeze both. Two things fall out immediately, both good. Freezing is cheap — the only gradients I compute are for whatever small thing I add. And freezing protects the knowledge: if I were to fine-tune the language model on a narrow vision-language objective it would drift, forgetting the broad competence that made it worth using; locking it in place rules that out by construction.

But the moment I freeze the language model I'm staring at the hard part. This model has never seen an image. Its inputs are text-token embeddings; its whole interior is organized around language. It will not bend toward vision, because I've forbidden it to change. So all of the work of getting visual information into a form it can use has to happen *outside* it, in the bridge I insert between the frozen encoder and the frozen LM. Every bit of cross-modal alignment lives in that bridge. That's the whole problem, restated: align frozen vision to a frozen, vision-blind language model, using only a small trainable module.

Let me look at how people have tried to feed images into a frozen language model. The pattern is consistent: take the image, turn it into something the LM can read, and train the whole thing with an image-to-text generation loss — show the picture, ask for the caption, backprop the language-modeling loss. Frozen does this by learning a vision encoder whose outputs are soft prompts prepended to the LM. Flamingo does it by splicing new cross-attention layers into the frozen LM and training them on billions of pairs. Both lean entirely on the generation loss, because it's the obvious one: it's exactly what the LM already does. So my first instinct is to just do the same — wire the bridge in, prepend or cross-attend, and minimize the caption loss. One objective, simple.

Before I commit to that, let me think about whether the generation loss actually applies the pressure I need. What I need is for the bridge to produce a visual representation that *faithfully encodes the image content*. What the generation loss rewards is making the next text token likely given the conditioning. Are those the same thing? Not obviously, because the LM is frozen and fluent — it carries a strong language prior, so it can produce plausible captions from weak conditioning. Let me try to make that worry concrete instead of hand-waving it.

Take a frozen LM with a unigram-ish prior over caption tokens, and ask: how much of the caption's log-likelihood comes "for free" from the prior versus from the image conditioning? Say a 10-token caption, and the LM's prior alone already puts the per-token probability of the right word at, optimistically, 1/50 (captions are formulaic — "a", "of", "on", "the", common nouns). That's a per-token NLL of ln 50 ≈ 3.9, total ≈ 39 nats with *zero* visual information. If perfect visual conditioning would drive the right tokens to probability ~0.5 (NLL ≈ 0.69/token, ≈ 6.9 nats total), then the generation loss has only ≈ 39 − 7 = 32 nats of headroom that the image can possibly explain — and that headroom is *shared* between "the bridge encoded the image well" and "the LM's prior happened to be confident here." A gradient-descent path that nudges the LM's already-confident tokens, or that produces a vague visual hint the fluent LM smooths over, captures a lot of that headroom without ever forcing the visual vectors to be *specifically* aligned to the content. The loss goes down; the representation stays mushy. The numbers are illustrative, not measured, but the structure is the point: a large fraction of the caption loss is satisfiable by the language prior, so the marginal pressure on the visual representation is diluted. That makes me distrust generation-as-the-only-signal. I'd want, before generation, a stage whose loss *cannot* be paid off by the language prior — one where the only way to reduce it is to make the visual representation itself align with the text. Then generation can ride on top of an already-aligned representation.

Now, what should the bridge even output? The frozen encoder hands me a big, variable-size grid — a couple hundred patch tokens for a high-res image, each a thousand-dimensional vector, and the count changes with resolution. Two problems with shoving that straight at the LM. It's expensive: cross-attention or prefixing over hundreds of visual tokens, inside a huge frozen LM, for every example. And it's mostly irrelevant: any given caption is about a few things in the image, not all of it. So I want to *compress* — turn that variable grid into a small, fixed set of vectors that carry the text-relevant content and drop the rest. A fixed, small output also means the cost into the LM is fixed regardless of image resolution.

How do I turn a variable-size set into a fixed-size one? There's a clean device for this: learn a fixed number of latent query vectors and let them cross-attend to the encoder's features — the queries are the parameters, the features are keys and values, and the number of outputs equals the number of queries, completely independent of how many input features there are. That's the Perceiver move, and the same trick as DETR's object queries. So: a set of, say, 32 learnable query embeddings. They cross-attend into the frozen image features and come out as 32 vectors. For ViT-L the frozen grid is 257×1024; my output is 32×768. Let me put numbers on how hard a squeeze that is: the frozen grid is 257·1024 = 263,168 floats per image, my output is 32·768 = 24,576 floats — about a 10.7× reduction. Most of that grid is patch detail no caption will ever mention, so a 10× cut isn't lossy in the way that matters *if* the queries are pushed to keep the right tenth. So the compression itself is benign; the work is in the supervision. The narrow channel is leverage: 32 vectors can't carry everything, so whatever objective I attach has to decide what survives — and if I get the objective right, what survives is the text-relevant content.

Let me make this a small transformer, not just a single attention layer, so the queries can also reason among themselves and, crucially, interact with text. Here's the shape I want. The queries are the input sequence to an "image transformer." They self-attend to each other; they cross-attend to the frozen image features; and I'll insert the cross-attention only every other block, not every block, because the cross-attention layers are the new randomly-initialized capacity and I don't want to drown the pretrained weights — let self-attention do work in between. I'll initialize the whole thing from BERT-base so the self-attention and feed-forward start from competent language-capable weights; only the cross-attention layers are fresh and random.

But I also need text to flow through this module, because the only way to *supervise* the queries to be language-aligned is to let them be scored against, and interact with, text. So I'll let the same module also process text tokens, sharing the self-attention layers between queries and text. One transformer, two kinds of tokens — image queries and text — sharing self-attention. Now I can control, with attention masks, exactly how much the queries and the text are allowed to see each other. That mask is going to be my knob for getting several different training signals out of one architecture.

So stage one: frozen image encoder, queries + text through this querying module, and I want to drive the queries to extract text-relevant visual information. I learned from the fusion-encoder line that the way to get fine-grained alignment isn't one objective, it's a small family of them, each pressuring a different facet. Let me build three, all sharing the same input format and weights, differing only by the attention mask.

First, contrastive alignment — pull matched image and text together, push mismatched apart. This is exactly the kind of loss the language prior can't pay off: the similarity is between an image-derived vector and a text-derived vector, so the only way to score a matched pair high is to make the image representation actually encode the text's content. Good — that's the property I wanted.

But here's a wrinkle the dual-encoder methods don't have: my image side is Z, *32* vectors, not one. So how do I collapse it to a single image-text similarity? The obvious default is to pool the 32 queries — mean them into one vector, then take cosine with t. Let me not just assume that's fine; let me sanity-check it against the alternative, max over the per-query similarities, on a toy I can compute. Suppose 3 queries (stand-in for 32) and unit-norm vectors in a tiny space. The captioned object lives along one axis; let query q0 specialize on it (q0 ≈ the correct-text direction), while q1 and q2 cover other regions of the image (a different object, background) and point elsewhere. Now score this image against the correct text and against a wrong text.

Mean-pool first averages the three query vectors and then takes similarity. The averaged image vector is dragged toward the off-content queries, so even for the *correct* text I get cosine ≈ 0.37, and for the wrong text ≈ 0.19 — a margin of only about 0.18. Max-pool instead reports each query's similarity and keeps the best: q0 nails the correct text at ≈ 1.00, while the best query for the wrong text is ≈ 0.30 — a margin of ≈ 0.70. So max-pooling gives roughly 4× the separation here. The reason is now clear from the computation, not from a slogan: I don't need *every* query to match the text, I need *some* query to have captured it, and averaging punishes specialization by mixing the on-content query with the off-content ones. So: similarity of each query output to t, take the maximum. Scale by a temperature, softmax over the batch, the usual two-way in-batch contrastive.

Now the mask. My worry is that if I let the queries attend to the text tokens while computing the image representation, the contrastive loss becomes trivial — the queries could just copy t and every matched pair would score 1 regardless of the image. Let me confirm that's not a phantom worry by quantifying the shortcut. Take a batch of 8 random unit text vectors. If the image representation is allowed to be a copy of its paired text (the leak), the in-batch retrieval is solved perfectly — accuracy 1.00, contrastive loss 0.000 — and the image never had to be looked at. If instead the image representation has to come from the image (model it as the matched text plus substantial noise, since a real image only approximates its caption), accuracy drops to ~0.88 and the loss is a real ≈ 0.39 that gradient descent has to work to reduce by *aligning the image*. So leakage genuinely collapses the objective into a copy operation; I have to forbid it. A unimodal mask — queries can't see text tokens and text can't see queries. Side note on negatives: because the image encoder is frozen, the forward pass is cheap and I can fit a big batch on each device, so I don't need the momentum queue the end-to-end methods used to manufacture negatives — plain in-batch negatives suffice.

Second, generation — but now used as a *representation-shaping* signal, not as the only signal. I argued above that generation alone is too weak because the language prior absorbs it; but generation *inside this module*, with the right wiring, can be turned into pressure on the queries specifically. The lever is architectural: in this module only the queries cross-attend to the frozen image features; the text tokens have no cross-attention to the image. So if I forbid the text from attending to the image directly and ask it to predict the caption, the visual information has to reach the text by some other route.

Let me make sure that route actually goes through the queries and nowhere else, because the whole argument depends on it. Lay out the connectivity as a directed graph where an edge a←b means "a can read b": queries read image features (Q←I), queries read each other (Q←Q), and under a causal-on-text mask each text token reads all queries and its own past (T←Q, T←T), with crucially *no* T←I edge. Now ask reachability: can image information reach a text token? Trace it — I→Q (cross-attention) →T (text reads queries): yes, reachable, but every path passes through Q. Then delete the query nodes entirely and re-check: with Q gone there is no T←I edge and no surviving path, so image information cannot reach the text at all. That's the structural guarantee I wanted, and it isn't a slogan now — it's that the only I→T paths in the graph are the ones through Q, so to lower the generation loss the queries are the only thing that can carry the needed visual content. The mask that implements this is causal-on-the-text, UniLM-style: queries attend among themselves but not to text; each text token attends to all the queries and to its own earlier text tokens. And I swap the first text token from [CLS] to a [DEC] token to signal "decode" mode.

Third, matching — the finest-grained signal. A binary classifier: is this image-text pair matched or not? Here I want full fusion, so a bidirectional mask: every query and every text token can attend to everything. Now each output query embedding has soaked up multimodal information. Feed each query's output through a two-class linear head to get a matched/unmatched logit, and average those logits over the 32 queries for the final score. And matching is only as good as its negatives — a random mismatched pair is trivially rejected. So I mine hard negatives: use the contrastive similarities to pick the *most confusable* wrong text for each image (and wrong image for each text) and make the classifier separate those. That's what teaches fine-grained discrimination.

Three objectives, one module, three masks: unimodal for contrastive (no leakage — I checked that leakage collapses it to copying text), causal-on-text for generation (the graph shows info can only reach text through the queries), bidirectional for matching (full fusion). Two of these — contrastive and matching — are losses the frozen LM's language prior can't pay off, because they score an image-derived representation directly, which is precisely the gap I diagnosed in the generation-only setup. So I expect that after this stage the 32 query vectors carry a compact, text-aligned summary of the image rather than a vague hint; I can't measure the alignment from here, but the objectives are now constructed so that reducing the loss *requires* that alignment rather than merely permitting it.

Stage two: now bring in the frozen LLM. I have 32 query vectors that already encode language-relevant visual content. I just need to put them into the LLM's input space. The query outputs live in the module's 768-dim space; the LLM has its own text-embedding dimension. So a single fully-connected layer projects the 32 query vectors into the LLM's embedding dimension, and I *prepend* them to the embeddings of the input text. They act as soft visual prompts — the LLM reads them as if they were leading tokens, and generates conditioned on them.

This is where the bottleneck pays off twice. Because stage one already made the queries carry the useful, language-shaped visual information and dropped the rest, the frozen LLM receives a clean, small, pre-aligned signal rather than a noisy grid it would have to make sense of itself. The module is an information bottleneck feeding the LLM only what's useful. That reduces the alignment burden on the frozen LLM — and since the LLM is frozen, "reducing its burden" is the only lever I have. It's also why catastrophic forgetting isn't a worry: I never touch the LLM's weights; I only learn to speak its input language.

Two flavors of LLM. For a decoder-only LLM, plain language modeling: prepend the projected queries, the LLM generates the caption, cross-entropy on the text. For an encoder-decoder LLM, prefix language modeling: split the text in two; concatenate the visual prompt with the prefix as the encoder's input, and make the suffix the decoder's target. Either way the gradient flows only into the querying module and its projection — both frozen models stay fixed.

Let me pin the concrete choices. 32 queries, each 768-dim — same as the module's hidden size, which I get from initializing from BERT-base. 32 is the budget: small enough to be a real bottleneck and cheap into the LLM, large enough to carry the few things any caption talks about. Cross-attention every *other* block, not every block, to limit the fresh random capacity relative to the pretrained self-attention/FFN. For the frozen image encoder I'll use a strong pre-trained ViT — a CLIP ViT-L/14 or an EVA-CLIP ViT-g/14 — and take the second-to-last layer's features (the very last layer is over-specialized to the encoder's own pretraining head; one layer back transfers a touch better). For the frozen LLM, an OPT model (decoder) or an instruction-tuned FlanT5 (encoder-decoder); cast their weights to FP16 (BF16 for FlanT5) since they're frozen and I just need forward passes — no accuracy cost observed. Optimizer AdamW, β = (0.9, 0.98), weight decay 0.05, cosine schedule, peak learning rate 1e-4 with a 2k-step warmup. Data: the same web-scale image-text mix used before — and I'll use the CapFilt trick, generating synthetic captions for the noisy web images with a captioner and keeping the best by image-text similarity, so the text the queries align to is actually about the image.

Turning stage one into code is direct once the three masks are fixed, because it is the same forward call through one shared, BERT-initialized module, three times, with a different attention pattern and head on top each time. Cross-attention reads the frozen image features as `encoder_hidden_states` every other block; the 32 query parameters are the input on the image side, the tokenized caption on the text side. For ITC, run the module on the queries alone (unimodal mask) and on the text alone, project both into a shared space, take the all-pairs similarity of every query against every caption in the batch, max over the 32 queries, and cross-entropy the resulting batch-by-batch matrix against the diagonal in both directions. For ITG, run it again on the queries plus the same tokenized caption with its first token swapped to `[DEC]`, under the causal-on-text mask, and take the language-modeling loss on the text positions. For ITM, run it on the queries plus text once more — this time the positive pairs together with the hard negatives mined from the ITC similarities — under the fully bidirectional mask, and average a two-class head over the 32 query positions. Stage two adds one linear layer: project the query output into the LLM's embedding width and prepend it to the text embeddings (or, for an encoder-decoder LLM, to the prefix), then hand the concatenation to the frozen LLM's own forward pass with the prepended positions masked out of the loss. One placement detail matters here: the frozen image encoder call sits under `torch.no_grad()`, but the frozen LLM call does not — its weights never move, but the gradient still has to flow back through it into the concatenated input embeddings, all the way to the projection and the queries themselves; wrapping that call in `no_grad()` too would silently zero out the only training signal stage two has.
