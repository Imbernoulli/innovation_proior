The question I set out to settle is sharp and stubborn: take the isotropic Heisenberg Hamiltonian $H = -\sum_{ij} J_{ij}\,\mathbf{S}_i\cdot\mathbf{S}_j$ with short-range exchange, lay it on a chain or a plane instead of a bulk crystal, and ask whether it develops a nonzero spontaneous magnetization at a finite temperature. In three dimensions the answer is plainly yes — real ferromagnets and antiferromagnets order. What I want to know is whether the *same* microscopic spins, now living in one or two dimensions, can still order. The starting clue comes from Bloch's spin-wave count. Quantize deviations from the fully aligned state into magnons; because the exchange is rotationally symmetric, tilting all the spins rigidly costs nothing, so the dispersion is gapless and quadratic, $E(\mathbf{k}) \approx D k^2$ at small $k$. The thermal reduction of the magnetization is $\Delta M(T) \sim \int_0^\infty N(E)/(e^{E/k_BT}-1)\,dE$ with density of states $N(E)\sim E^{(d-2)/2}$ following from $E\sim k^2$ and the volume element $k^{d-1}dk$. In $d=3$ this gives the celebrated $M(0)-\text{const}\cdot T^{3/2}$ and converges. In $d=2$ the density of states is constant and near the band bottom $e^{E/k_BT}-1\approx E/k_BT$, so $\Delta M \sim k_BT\int_0 dE/E$ diverges logarithmically; in $d=1$ it is worse. Read literally, the magnetization reduction is infinite at any $T>0$ — the gapless long-wavelength magnons are infinitely easy to excite in low dimensions and they eat all the order.

That looks like a proof, but it is not one, and the very reason it fails is the reason I cannot trust it: the entire calculation is built on the premise that the spins are aligned. I had to assume the ordered state just to define the magnons. When the correction $\Delta M$ diverges, all I have actually shown is that my expansion is inconsistent — perturbation theory eating its own assumption. The same goes for mean-field theory, which discards fluctuations and so predicts order in every dimension, even $d=1$ where it is known to be wrong; and for the Peierls/Landau displacement-fluctuation arguments for two-dimensional crystals, which are suggestive but perturbative and tied to the harmonic picture. A diverging correction to an assumed ordered state is a red flag, not a theorem. What I need is something that never assumes order in the first place and still pins the magnetization down.

So let me first be honest about what "spontaneous magnetization" means. Because $H$ commutes with every component of the total spin — it has full continuous $SO(3)$ symmetry — the thermal average of $S^z$ in any finite system is exactly zero: for every configuration there is a rotated one with opposite $S^z$ and the same energy, so $\langle S^z\rangle = 0$ identically, ordered or not. The order parameter therefore cannot be a naive average; it has to be a *quasi-average*. I add a small symmetry-breaking field $b$, take the system to infinite size so it can choose a direction, and only then send $b\to 0$:
$$ m = \lim_{b\to 0}\,\lim_{N\to\infty}\, m(T,b). $$
The order of limits is the entire content of "spontaneous." This reframes the whole problem: I do not need to *compute* $m$, I need an *upper bound* on $m(T,b)$ — a function $f(b,T)$ with $m\le f$ and $f\to 0$ as $b\to 0$. If I can produce such an $f$, the spontaneous magnetization is trapped at zero, and I never had to assume anything about the state.

I propose to prove this with the Bogoliubov inequality, and the result is what I will call the Mermin–Wagner theorem: at any nonzero temperature the isotropic, finite-range Heisenberg model has neither spontaneous ferromagnetic nor antiferromagnetic order in one or two dimensions. The engine is an exact inequality, valid in thermal equilibrium for any operators $A$, $C$ and any Hamiltonian $H$ at $T = 1/(k_B\beta)$,
$$ \tfrac12\,\beta\,\langle\{A,A^\dagger\}\rangle\,\langle[[C,H],C^\dagger]\rangle \;\ge\; |\langle[A,C]\rangle|^2 . $$
Its proof assumes nothing about order. On operators define the form $B(X,Y) = \sum_{E_n\ne E_m} w_n\,\langle n|X^\dagger|m\rangle\langle m|Y|n\rangle/(E_n-E_m)$ with weights $w_n = e^{-\beta E_n}/Z$. This is positive-semidefinite because, after symmetrizing the $(n,m)\leftrightarrow(m,n)$ terms, the coefficient of $|\langle m|X|n\rangle|^2$ is $(w_n-w_m)/(E_n-E_m)\ge 0$ — numerator and denominator flip sign together since $w$ decreases with energy. So Schwarz holds, $|B(A,Y)|^2 \le B(A,A)\,B(Y,Y)$. The clever move is to set $Y = [C^\dagger,H]$: the identity $\langle m|[C^\dagger,H]|n\rangle = (E_n-E_m)\langle m|C^\dagger|n\rangle$ cancels the energy denominator exactly, which both lets me drop the $E_n\ne E_m$ restriction and collapses the left slot of Schwarz to a commutator, $B(A,[C^\dagger,H]) = \langle[C^\dagger,A^\dagger]\rangle$, so $|B| = |\langle[A,C]\rangle|$. The same cancellation on $B([C^\dagger,H],[C^\dagger,H])$ telescopes it to the double commutator $\langle[[C,H],C^\dagger]\rangle\ge 0$. For the remaining norm $B(A,A)$ I must cap the dangerous factor $(w_n-w_m)/(E_n-E_m)$; writing it as $[(w_n+w_m)/(E_n-E_m)]\cdot\tanh(\beta(E_m-E_n)/2)$ and using $|\tanh x|<|x|$ gives $(w_n-w_m)/(E_n-E_m) < (\beta/2)(w_n+w_m)$, which washes out the energies and leaves $B(A,A) < (\beta/2)\langle\{A,A^\dagger\}\rangle$. Assembling Schwarz produces the inequality, with no premise about symmetry breaking anywhere.

Everything then rides on choosing $A$ and $C$ so the three pieces become, respectively, the order parameter, a spin-length sum rule, and an energy cost that carries the field and the wavevector. I work in momentum space, where the small-$k$ divergence I am chasing lives. Define $S^\pm(\mathbf{k}) = \sum_i e^{i\mathbf{k}\cdot\mathbf{R}_i} S_i^\pm$, allow an ordering wavevector $\mathbf{K}$ ($\mathbf{K}=0$ for ferromagnetic order, $\mathbf{K}$ at a zone boundary for staggered/antiferromagnetic order), and add the conjugate field $H\to H - b\sum_i e^{-i\mathbf{K}\cdot\mathbf{R}_i} S_i^z$. The order parameter is the (staggered) magnetization $m = (1/N)|\langle S^z(-\mathbf{K})\rangle|$. The choice that makes the commutator $[A,C]$ reproduce $S^z(-\mathbf{K})$ is
$$ C = S^+(\mathbf{k}), \qquad A = S^-(-\mathbf{k}-\mathbf{K}), $$
since the algebra $[S^+(\mathbf{k}_1),S^-(\mathbf{k}_2)] = 2\hbar\,S^z(\mathbf{k}_1+\mathbf{k}_2)$ gives $[A,C] = -2\hbar\,S^z(-\mathbf{K})$, hence $|\langle[A,C]\rangle|^2 = 4\hbar^2 N^2 m^2$. The order parameter now sits squared on the right-hand side, exactly where I want it.

The anticommutator is where the spin algebra pays off. With $A=S^-(\mathbf{q})$, $\mathbf{q}=-\mathbf{k}-\mathbf{K}$, summing over all $\mathbf{k}$ is the same as summing over all $\mathbf{q}$, and $\sum_\mathbf{q} e^{i\mathbf{q}\cdot(\mathbf{R}_i-\mathbf{R}_j)} = N\delta_{ij}$ kills the cross terms, leaving only the on-site piece $\sum_\mathbf{q}\langle\{S^-(\mathbf{q}),S^+(\mathbf{q})\}\rangle = N\sum_i\langle 2(S_i^x)^2 + 2(S_i^y)^2\rangle$. Since $(S^x)^2+(S^y)^2 = \mathbf{S}^2 - (S^z)^2 \le \hbar^2 S(S+1)$, each site contributes at most $2\hbar^2 S(S+1)$, so
$$ \sum_\mathbf{k}\langle\{A,A^\dagger\}\rangle \le 2\hbar^2 S(S+1)\,N^2 . $$
This is the spin-length sum rule. It is an upper bound, in exactly the direction I need, and it is $k$-independent — capped by the finite length of a quantum spin, something a particle-number system would not hand me for free.

The last and hardest piece is the double commutator $\langle[[C,H],C^\dagger]\rangle$ with $C=S^+(\mathbf{k})$ and the full $H$. Split $H$ into the exchange part and the field part. For the exchange, the inner commutator $[S_m^+,H_\text{ex}]$ via $[S_m^+,S_j^-]=2\hbar\delta_{mj}S_m^z$ and $[S_m^+,S_j^z]=-\hbar\delta_{mj}S_m^+$, then the second commutator with $S_p^-$, organizes after averaging into a real positive structure weighted by $(1-\cos(\mathbf{k}\cdot(\mathbf{R}_m-\mathbf{R}_p)))$ — only the symmetric real part of the two phase factors survives because the $J_{mp}$-weighted operator sum is real and symmetric under $m\leftrightarrow p$. I do not need its exact value, only an upper bound, and two moves give it. First, each spin-operator expectation is bounded in magnitude by the spin length, $\sim\hbar^2 S(S+1)$. Second — and this injects the physics — bound the cosine by $1-\cos(\mathbf{k}\cdot(\mathbf{R}_m-\mathbf{R}_p)) \le \tfrac12 k^2(R_m-R_p)^2$. This is where the $k^2$ is *born*: the same quadratic that made the magnon gapless, but now as a rigorous upper bound with no spin-wave assumption. Pulling it together, the exchange contribution is bounded by $\tfrac12 k^2\sum_{mp}|J_{mp}|(R_m-R_p)^2\cdot\text{const}\cdot\hbar^2 S(S+1)$, and right here the short-range condition surfaces on its own: the second moment $\bar J \propto \sum_j|J_{ij}|(R_i-R_j)^2$ must be finite for this bound to be finite. If $J$ fell off slowly — a dipolar $1/r^3$ tail in two dimensions — $\bar J$ would diverge, the bound would be vacuous, and order could survive (a $1/r^3$ tail gives $E\sim k$, hence a convergent magnon integral). The field part, $[[S^+(\mathbf{k}),H_b],S^-(-\mathbf{k})]$ with $H_b = -b\sum_m e^{-i\mathbf{K}\cdot\mathbf{R}_m}S_m^z$, reduces via $[S_m^+,S_m^z]=-\hbar S_m^+$ to a piece proportional to the magnetization itself, $2\hbar^2 N\,b\,m$, and is $k$-independent. Combining,
$$ \langle[[C,H],C^\dagger]\rangle \;\le\; 2N\hbar^2\big(\,|b\,m| + S(S+1)\,\bar J\,k^2\,\big), $$
with the $|bm|$ term from the field and the $k^2$ term from the exchange. Notice this bound never referenced $\mathbf{K}$ except through the field phase absorbed into $m$, so it treats ferromagnetic and staggered order identically — exactly as it should, since "can it order" must not care which kind of order.

Now drop the three blocks into Bogoliubov. The denominator-to-be is small in two ways — small $b$ and small $k$ — and both will fight an integral that piles up modes near $k=0$. Dividing the per-$\mathbf{k}$ inequality by the double commutator, summing over $\mathbf{k}$ to deploy the $k$-independent sum rule on the right, and pulling the $k$-independent $|\langle[A,C]\rangle|^2 = 4\hbar^2 N^2 m^2$ out on the left gives
$$ (2Nm^2)\sum_\mathbf{k}\frac{1}{|bm| + S(S+1)\bar J k^2} \;\le\; \beta\,S(S+1)\,N^2 . $$
Converting $\sum_\mathbf{k}\to [N V_d/(2\pi)^d]\int d^dk$ (which cancels one power of $N$ on each side, dropping to a per-spin statement), restricting to the inscribed sphere of radius $\bar k$ — which only drops positive terms from the left and so only strengthens the inequality — and writing $J = S(S+1)\bar J$, $T = 1/(k_B\beta)$,
$$ S(S+1) \;>\; \frac{2 V_d\,\Omega_d\,m^2\,T}{(2\pi)^d}\int_0^{\bar k} dk\;\frac{k^{d-1}}{|bm| + S(S+1)\bar J\,k^2} , $$
with $\Omega_d$ the surface of the unit $d$-sphere. The dimension lives in exactly one place, the volume element $k^{d-1}dk$; everything else is dimension-blind. The small-$k$ integrand is $k^{d-1}/k^2 = k^{d-3}$, and $\int k^{d-3}dk$ diverges at the origin precisely when $d-3\le -1$, i.e. $d\le 2$. That single power-counting line is the theorem.

Carrying out the integral confirms it. In $d=1$, $\int_0^{\bar k} dk/(bm+Jk^2) = (1/\sqrt{bmJ})\arctan(\bar k\sqrt{J/bm}) \to (\pi/2)/\sqrt{bmJ}$, diverging like $1/\sqrt{bm}$; feeding back yields $m < c_1\,b^{1/3}/T^{2/3}$ with $c_1 = S(S+1)\bar J^{1/3}$, which $\to 0$ as $b\to 0$. In $d=2$, $\int_0^{\bar k} k\,dk/(bm+Jk^2) = (1/2J)\ln(1+J\bar k^2/(bm))$ diverges logarithmically — the very logarithm Bloch's heuristic stumbled into, but now on the rigorous side of an honest inequality — giving $m < c_2/(\sqrt{T}\sqrt{|\ln b|})$ with $c_2 = \sqrt{2\pi}\,S(S+1)\bar J^{1/2}$, which $\to 0$ as $b\to 0$, though only as $1/\sqrt{\ln(1/b)}$, infinitely slowly: the order is destroyed, but barely, by a logarithm — the signature of two dimensions sitting right at the edge. In $d=3$, $\int_0^{\bar k} k^2\,dk/(bm+Jk^2)$ stays finite as $b\to 0$ (the numerator $k^2$ beats the denominator $k^2$, the integrand tends to $1/J$), so the inequality places no vanishing ceiling on $m$ and three-dimensional order is left untouched, exactly as it must be.

What makes this work, and what I want to flag explicitly, is that the two hypotheses are genuinely load-bearing rather than decorative. *Isotropic* is what makes the field the only thing breaking the symmetry and the mode gapless: an easy-axis anisotropy would add a constant gap $\Delta$ to the denominator, $bm+\Delta+Jk^2$, which stays finite as $b\to 0$ and rescues the order. *Short-range* is what makes $\bar J$ finite so the denominator really is $\propto k^2$ at small $k$: a long-range tail changes the small-$k$ power and can restore order. And I assumed nothing classical or large about the spins — $S$ enters only through $S(S+1)$, so the result holds for every spin magnitude including $S=\tfrac12$. The resolution of the opening puzzle is now clean: Bloch's divergent $\Delta M$ and my vanishing $m$ are the *same* $d=2$ logarithm, but with opposite epistemic status. Bloch assumed alignment and got a divergence signaling inconsistency; I assumed nothing, kept the field as a regulator, and got a finite inequality whose $b\to 0$ limit is an honest zero. The logarithm was never a sign that something blew up — it was the infrared catastrophe $\sum_\mathbf{k} 1/k^2$ of a gapless mode in low dimensions, to be used as the denominator of an upper bound, not as a correction to an assumed state.

Stated cleanly, the theorem is this. For the isotropic spin-$S$ Heisenberg model with finite-range exchange on a one- or two-dimensional lattice, at any nonzero temperature $T$, the (staggered) magnetization in an ordering field $b$ obeys $m < c_1\,b^{1/3}/T^{2/3}$ in $d=1$ with $c_1 = S(S+1)\bar J^{1/3}$, and $m < c_2/(\sqrt{T}\sqrt{|\ln b|})$ in $d=2$ with $c_2 = \sqrt{2\pi}\,S(S+1)\bar J^{1/2}$. Both right-hand sides vanish as $b\to 0$ for every finite $T$, and the bound is independent of the ordering wavevector $\mathbf{K}$, so it forbids both ferromagnetic ($\mathbf{K}=0$) and antiferromagnetic/staggered ($\mathbf{K}\ne 0$) spontaneous order. In three dimensions the controlling integral converges and the argument gives no vanishing ceiling, leaving ordinary long-range order intact.

To make the heart of it visible, the divergence is just a number I can watch: integrate $k^{d-1}/(bm + k^2)$ over a small sphere and shrink the field.

```python
import numpy as np
_trapz = getattr(np, "trapezoid", None) or np.trapz  # numpy renamed trapz -> trapezoid

def infrared_integral(d, bm, kbar=1.0, J=1.0, n=200000):
    """I_d(bm) = ∫_0^kbar k^{d-1}/(bm + J k^2) dk. The bound reads
    S(S+1) > const * m^2 * T * I_d(bm); a diverging I_d as bm->0 forces m->0."""
    k = np.linspace(0.0, kbar, n)
    return _trapz(k**(d - 1) / (bm + J * k**2), k)

for d in (1, 2, 3):
    vals = [infrared_integral(d, bm) for bm in (1e-1, 1e-3, 1e-5, 1e-7)]
    print(f"d={d}:  I(bm) for bm=1e-1..1e-7 ->", [f"{v:.3f}" for v in vals])

# d=1:  ~ 1/sqrt(bm)      diverges (x10 per 100x drop)  -> m < c1 b^{1/3}/T^{2/3} -> 0
# d=2:  ~ (1/2) ln(1/bm)  diverges logarithmically       -> m < c2 /(sqrt(T) sqrt(|ln b|)) -> 0
# d=3:  -> finite (~1.0)  converges                       -> no vanishing ceiling; order survives
```
