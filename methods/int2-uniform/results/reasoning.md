Let me start from where the cost actually is. A language model at inference is almost nothing but matrix multiplications inside its linear projections — the QKV and output of attention, the two MLP projections, the LM head — and each of those is a dense full-precision weight matrix that I have to (a) hold in memory, (b) stream from DRAM onto the chip, and (c) multiply, in floating point, against the activations. For a model this size the weights are the memory, the weight traffic is the latency, and the floating-point multiplies are a big chunk of the energy. Two levers fall out of that immediately: fewer bits per weight shrinks (a) and (b), and replacing the floating-point multiply with an integer add shrinks (c). The striking thing is that both levers are the same lever — if the forward weights are tiny integers, a weight times an activation stops being a float multiply and becomes, at most, a sign flip and an accumulate. So the target sharpens: I want the weights that actually get multiplied in the forward pass to live in a small discrete set, one or two bits each, and I want this to be true during training, not bolted on afterward.

Why insist on "during training"? Because the obvious cheap route — train in float, round to low bits afterward — is known to fall apart exactly where I want to be. Post-training quantization degrades gently down to about four bits and then drops off a cliff at two bits and below. The reason isn't mysterious: a model trained for a smooth full-precision weight landscape has no particular reason to have a good two-bit neighbor. Nothing during training ever pushed its weights toward a configuration that survives coarse rounding, so when I finally round, I land somewhere the loss never had to be small. If I want two bits to work, the training itself has to feel the quantization, so the optimizer can drive the latent weights into a place whose rounded image is good. The forward error has to be inside the loss being minimized. That's quantization-aware training, and the question is no longer "what's the best grid" — it's "how do I optimize through a grid at all."

Here is the wall, and it's a hard one. A quantizer is a step function of its input. It's flat between thresholds and jumps at them, so its derivative is zero almost everywhere and undefined on a measure-zero set. If I just write `w_q = quantize(w)` and call `.backward()`, the chain rule hands the latent weight a gradient of exactly zero, and the optimizer does nothing forever. Discrete forward, no gradient — that's the whole difficulty of low-bit training in one sentence, and representation isn't the problem, optimization is.

So I need a surrogate gradient. The cleanest idea I know for pushing gradients through a hard, a.e.-flat decision is the straight-through estimator from Bengio, Léonard and Courbariaux: in the backward pass, pretend the hard nonlinearity was the identity and copy the gradient of its output straight to its input. It's biased — I'm lying about the derivative — but it's a useful lie, because the *direction* it gives is the direction that would help if the quantizer weren't there, and over many small steps that's enough to move the latent weight toward a better quantized configuration. It was framed for stochastic binary neurons, i.e. for activations, but the move transplants directly to a weight quantizer: forward through the step function, backward through the identity.

That immediately raises a second question STE alone doesn't answer: gradient with respect to *what*? The quantized weight is a coarse handful of values; if I tried to accumulate SGD updates directly into it, almost every step would be smaller than the gap between adjacent levels and would simply vanish. Courbariaux, Bengio and David nailed this with BinaryConnect: keep a high-precision latent weight `w` as the *accumulator*, binarize it on the fly each forward pass, and apply the update to `w` while ignoring the binarization in the update. Their argument is the right one — SGD needs resolution in the accumulator because it averages a long stream of small noisy gradients, but it tolerates noise in the propagated weight, and discretization is just such a noise, arguably even a regularizer. So the picture is: a full-precision `w` that the optimizer actually moves, and a discrete `quantize(w)` that the forward pass actually multiplies, joined by STE.

But if I let a latent accumulator drift arbitrarily far past the useful range, I've created another failure mode. Suppose the forward map clamps every value beyond some boundary to the same top level. Pushing the latent value farther changes nothing in the forward pass, yet an unconstrained surrogate can keep moving it farther away from the boundary it would need to cross to matter again. Hubara, Courbariaux and colleagues hit this in BNN and fixed it with the strict saturating STE: pass the gradient through only where the pre-quantization value is inside the representable range, and cancel it outside. In their `±1` case that's `g_w = g_q · 1_{|w|≤1}`, with `htanh(w) = clip(w,-1,1)` as the implied forward surrogate. That gives me the guardrail: the forward value must be clamped to the representable range, and if I want strict BNN-style saturation I add an explicit backward mask. The BitLinear-style detached-difference implementation I want to mirror keeps the forward clamp but uses the identity surrogate in the backward pass, so I have to keep those two ideas separate instead of pretending the clamp alone cancels the gradient.

Now the representation. BinaryConnect and BNN force the weight to exactly `{-1,+1}`. That's too rigid — a layer whose real weights are all small, or all large, can't express its own magnitude with a bare sign. I need a per-matrix scale `s` so the forward weight is `s · grid`. What should `s` be? Don't guess; solve for it. Take the binary case first to get the principle, following Rastegari and colleagues in XNOR-Net. I want to approximate the real weight matrix `W` (flatten it, `n` entries) by `s · B` with `B ∈ {-1,+1}ⁿ` and `s > 0`, minimizing the reconstruction error

  J(s, B) = ‖W − s·B‖².

Expand it: `J = s² BᵀB − 2s WᵀB + WᵀW`. Now `BᵀB = n` because every entry of `B` is `±1`, and `WᵀW` is a constant, call it `c`, so `J = n s² − 2s WᵀB + c`. For fixed `s > 0`, minimizing `J` means maximizing `WᵀB`, and since each `B_i ∈ {-1,+1}` independently, `WᵀB = Σ W_i B_i` is maximized term by term by `B_i = sign(W_i)`. So `B* = sign(W)`, no surprise. Plug that back and minimize over `s`: `∂J/∂s = 2ns − 2 WᵀB* = 0`, giving

  s* = WᵀB* / n = Wᵀ sign(W) / n = (Σ|W_i|) / n = (1/n)‖W‖₁ = mean(|W|).

The L2-optimal per-tensor scale for a sign grid is the mean absolute weight. That's a clean, cheap, outlier-robust scalar — one reduction, no learned parameter. And it makes intuitive sense as a placement: it sets the unit so that the typical weight magnitude lands at the grid's `±1`, putting the bulk of the weight distribution inside the representable range instead of letting one extreme entry set the scale. Contrast it with using the max absolute weight as the scale: one large outlier would blow up `s`, so dividing by it would crush all the ordinary weights toward zero, and most of them would round to the same level — the grid would be wasted on representing the outlier. So absmean, not absmax, for weights. I'll also detach `s` from the autograd graph when I form it (it's a statistic of `w`, not a second path I want the rounding's STE to distort), and floor it with a tiny epsilon so a dead all-zero tensor doesn't divide by zero.

So far I have the recipe for one bit: latent `w`, scale `s = mean(|w|)`, forward weight `s · sign(w)`, saturating STE. Now the actual question that distinguishes this from plain binarization — is one bit the right number? Look at what a third level buys. Ternary `{-1, 0, +1}` adds an explicit zero, which lets a weight be *off*, a kind of learned feature-filtering that a strict `±1` grid can't express; that's `log2 3 ≈ 1.58` bits. The reconstruction logic generalizes cleanly: with the same absmean scale, round each normalized weight to the nearest of `{-1, 0, +1}`. Concretely, normalize `w_n = w / s`, then `quantize = RoundClip(w_n, -1, 1) = clip(round(w_n), -1, 1)`, and the forward weight is `s · quantize`. Round-to-nearest is the right deterministic assignment because for a fixed grid it minimizes the per-element squared error. Good — but I want to push past 1.58 bits and see whether a *little* more resolution buys back accuracy at essentially no extra cost, since the matmul is still integer adds either way.

So go to four levels — exactly two bits, `log2 4 = 2`. The cleanest four-level set on `[-1, 1]` is the symmetric uniform grid

  {-1, -1/3, +1/3, +1},

uniformly spaced by `2/3`, symmetric about zero. Why symmetric, and why uniform? Symmetric means no zero-point offset to store, communicate, or add into the accumulation — the integer matmul stays a pure signed accumulate, which is the whole point of going low-bit, and it matches the symmetric absmax convention I'll use on the activation side. Uniform spacing is the simplest possible grid: round-to-nearest is trivial, the spacing is constant so the worst-case rounding error is bounded by half a step everywhere, and there are no learned grid points to destabilize early training (same reasoning that made me prefer a fixed absmean scale over a learned one). I could imagine a non-uniform grid matched to the weight distribution — finer near zero where weights are dense — but that's a learned, distribution-dependent object, more parameters and more ways to go wrong, and it's a refinement I don't need to justify the step from 1.58 to 2 bits. Uniform symmetric it is.

Notice this grid does *not* contain an exact zero — `{-1, -1/3, +1/3, +1}` straddles zero rather than hitting it. So unlike ternary, two-bit-uniform trades away explicit feature-filtering (the "off" level) for finer magnitude resolution: it can say a weight is small-positive (`+1/3`) or large-positive (`+1`), which ternary cannot, but it cannot say a weight is exactly off. Whether that trade is worth it is precisely the empirical question this grid exists to probe; representationally it's two genuine bits of weight precision against ternary's 1.58.

Now I need the quantizer that lands a normalized weight `w_n ∈ [-1, 1]` on that grid. Let me derive the round-to-nearest map rather than eyeball it, because the boundaries matter. There are four levels; assign them integer codes `0,1,2,3` mapping to `-1, -1/3, +1/3, +1`. The level for code `j` is `level(j) = -1 + j·(2/3)`, since the step is `2/3` and the bottom is `-1`. Invert: given `w_n`, the nearest code is `round((w_n - (-1)) / (2/3)) = round((w_n + 1)·(3/2)) = round((w_n + 1)·1.5)`, clamped to `[0, 3]`. Then the dequantized grid value is `level(code) = -1 + code·(2/3)`. Let me sanity-check the boundaries: `w_n = -1 → round(0) = 0 → -1`; `w_n = 0 → round(1.5) = 2 → -1 + 2·2/3 = 1/3` (zero sits at the boundary between `-1/3` and `+1/3` and rounds up to `+1/3`, which is fine — there's no exact zero in the grid, and the tie goes to one neighbor); `w_n = 1 → round(3) = 3 → +1`; `w_n = 0.4 → round(2.1) = 2 → +1/3`; `w_n = 0.5 → round(2.25)=2 → +1/3`, while `w_n = 0.7 → round(2.55)=3 → +1`, so the `+1/3`-vs-`+1` threshold sits at `w_n = 2/3`, exactly the midpoint of those two levels. The other midpoint is `-2/3`, and the center boundary is `0`. The clamp to `[0,3]` pins any normalized weight beyond `±1` to the extreme code. That is forward saturation; if I wanted strict BNN-style backward saturation, this is where I would apply the explicit gradient mask.

Let me make sure I get the STE wiring right, because there are two non-differentiable operations here — the `round` and the `clamp` — and I want the latent weight to see a clean gradient through both. The idiom that does this in one stroke, the one the binary-weight implementations use, is

  w_forward = w_n + (q − w_n).detach(),

where `q` is the dequantized grid value. In the forward pass the detached difference contributes its numeric value, so `w_forward = w_n + q - w_n = q`, and the computation uses the grid value exactly. In the backward pass the detached term contributes nothing, so `∂w_forward/∂w_n = 1` — the gradient flows straight through, identity, which is precisely STE. The clamp inside `q` is still important because it defines the forward saturation, but this detached-difference form does not by itself zero the gradient outside `[-1,1]`. If I choose the strict BNN cancellation, I multiply the passed gradient by `1_{|w_n|≤1}`; if I mirror the BitLinear-style implementation, I keep the identity surrogate through the hard round-and-clip. Then the actual forward weight is `s · w_forward`: scale times grid, with the detached `s` acting as the per-tensor unit for that pass and the gradient reaching the latent `w` through the normalized straight-through path.

Now the activation side, because the matmul has two operands and I haven't made the second one cheap yet. Here the diagnostic from the PTQ literature is decisive: weights are far easier to quantize than activations. Weight-only schemes survive down to two bits while four-bit *activation* schemes already wreck many models, because activations carry per-token outliers with large dynamic range. The contribution I care about is low-bit *weights*; activations should be only as low as is safe, so I'll quantize them to 8-bit integers, not two. For the scheme, use symmetric absmax, following the LLM.int8 activation convention: with `Q_b = 2^{8-1} = 127`, set the scale by the largest magnitude so nothing clips,

  s_x = max(|x|) / Q_b,   x_q = round( clip( x / s_x, −Q_b, Q_b ) ),

and `x_q · s_x ≈ x`. Absmax rather than absmean here, because for activations the failure mode is the opposite of weights: if I used absmean and an outlier activation exceeded the range, it would be clipped, and a clipped activation is a destroyed activation — whereas a clipped weight is just a slightly misplaced grid assignment. Absmax guarantees the extreme activation is representable, which is exactly what activation quantization must not get wrong. I use the same detached-difference STE on the round-and-clip: forward uses the int8 code, backward passes identity through the normalized activation. I floor the scale with an epsilon for the same dead-tensor reason.

Now assemble the forward and check where the scales belong. The output is `y = (s · w_grid)ᵀ (s_x · x_q) = s · s_x · (w_gridᵀ x_q)`. The inner product `w_gridᵀ x_q` is between four fixed weight levels and int8 activation codes. The reference PyTorch form can write those levels as dequantized grid values; an optimized kernel can store them as two-bit codes and treat the fixed grid constants as part of the low-bit arithmetic. The important accounting point is that the learned per-tensor scales `s` and `s_x` come out and multiply the output, not every original weight. Add the bias in full precision after the rescale and I'm done with the layer.

One more thing nags at me before I trust this in a deep stack: variance. If I quantize both operands and then matmul, does the output keep a sane scale, or does it drift layer over layer and blow up or vanish? Let me estimate it the way BitNet's analysis does. Assume the entries of the grid-weight and of the quantized activation are mutually independent and identically distributed, and independent of each other. For one output element summing over `n` inputs,

  Var(y) = n · Var(w̃ x̃) = n · E[w̃²] E[x̃²].

Write `w̃ = s · g` where `g` is a grid value. For the original sign grid, `g²=1`, so the estimate becomes `Var(y)=n·s²·E[x̃²]`, and with the absmean scale under standard initialization the `n·s²` factor is order one. For the four-level grid, the same calculation keeps the explicit second moment: `Var(y)=n·s²·E[g²]·E[x̃²]`. The extra `E[g²]` is a grid-dependent constant rather than a learned scale, so the activation second moment is still the quantity I can control directly. If I normalize the activation so that `E[x̃²] = 1` before the activation quantizer — a LayerNorm in front of the quantization — then the output remains order one, matching the scale that a deep network expects. That's the role of a normalization layer placed before the activation quant (in the BitLinear analysis this is SubLN). In the GPT-2 harness here, the block already carries a LayerNorm in front of each projection, so it plays that variance-control role; the point is that the absmean weight unit plus a normalized activation keeps the quantized forward from drifting in scale across depth.

There's also an optimization wrinkle worth naming because it changes a hyperparameter, not the math. Because the latent weight only matters through its quantized image, a small SGD step usually doesn't move any weight across a grid boundary — most updates produce zero change in the forward weight, so the quantized weights move in rare, abrupt flips rather than smoothly. The practical consequence is that low-bit training should search learning rates above the conservative full-precision setting: the discrete forward makes small updates inert, so the step has to be large enough to flip levels early. I won't bake a number in here — that lives in the training config — because the design reason is the boundary-crossing problem, not a benchmark outcome.

Let me write the module, filling the two quantizer slots and the forward of the `nn.Linear` drop-in. I keep the latent full-precision `weight` as the trainable parameter, quantize it to the two-bit symmetric uniform grid with the absmean scale, quantize the activation with absmax int8, do the matmul on the quantized operands, and rescale the output by the product of the two scales.

```python
import torch
import torch.nn as nn
import torch.nn.functional as F


def weight_quant(weight):
    # absmean scale (XNOR-Net L2-optimal for a sign grid; reused as the per-tensor unit)
    scale = weight.detach().abs().mean().clamp(min=1e-5)
    w_n = weight / scale                                  # typical |weight| ~ 1 after this
    # round-to-nearest onto the 2-bit symmetric uniform grid {-1, -1/3, +1/3, +1}:
    # codes 0..3 -> levels -1 + code*(2/3); code = round((w_n + 1) * 1.5)
    code = ((w_n + 1.0) * 1.5).round().clamp(0, 3)
    q = -1.0 + code * (2.0 / 3.0)                         # dequantized grid value in [-1, 1]
    # straight-through estimator: forward = q, backward passes identity to w_n
    w_q = w_n + (q - w_n).detach()
    return w_q, scale                                     # w_q * scale ~= weight


def activation_quant(x):
    # symmetric absmax 8-bit (no zero-point): nothing clips, full signed int8 range
    Qb = 127.0
    max_abs = x.detach().abs().max().clamp(min=1e-5)
    scale = max_abs / Qb
    x_n = x / scale
    x_int = x_n.round().clamp(-Qb, Qb)                    # int8-range values
    x_q = x_n + (x_int - x_n).detach()                    # STE: forward int, backward through
    return x_q, scale                                     # x_q * scale ~= x


class BitLinear(nn.Module):
    """Drop-in for nn.Linear. Latent FP weight, but the operands multiplied in
    the forward pass are a 2-bit uniform-symmetric weight grid and int8 activations.
    Same path in train and eval."""

    def __init__(self, in_features, out_features, bias=True):
        super().__init__()
        self.in_features = in_features
        self.out_features = out_features
        self.weight = nn.Parameter(torch.empty(out_features, in_features))   # latent FP weight
        self.bias = nn.Parameter(torch.zeros(out_features)) if bias else None
        nn.init.normal_(self.weight, mean=0.0, std=0.02)

    def forward(self, x):
        w_q, w_scale = weight_quant(self.weight)          # grid weights + absmean scale
        x_q, x_scale = activation_quant(x)                # int8 activations + absmax scale
        out = F.linear(x_q, w_q, None)                    # low-bit inner product in the target kernel
        out = out * (w_scale * x_scale)                   # dequantize by the two operand scales
        if self.bias is not None:
            out = out + self.bias                         # bias added in full precision
        return out
```

So the causal chain, start to finish. The cost of an LLM is the float multiplies and the weight memory in its linear layers, and both shrink at once if the forward weights are tiny integers — so I want low-bit weights in the forward pass. Post-training quantization can't reach two bits because a float-trained model has no good low-bit neighbor, so the quantization has to be felt during training. But a quantizer is piecewise-constant, so its true gradient is zero and learning stalls; the straight-through estimator gets gradient through by treating the quantizer as identity in the backward pass, and a high-precision latent weight (BinaryConnect) is the accumulator the optimizer actually moves. BNN gives the warning about runaway latent values and the strict mask one can add; the BitLinear-style detached-difference code keeps the forward clamp while using identity backward through the normalized value. Forcing weights to a bare sign is too rigid, so I attach a per-tensor scale, and minimizing `‖W − s·B‖²` for a sign grid (XNOR-Net) gives the L2-optimal scale as the mean absolute weight — cheap, outlier-robust, and the right placement. Ternary's extra zero is `1.58` bits; pushing to a four-level symmetric uniform grid `{-1, -1/3, +1/3, +1}` is exactly two bits, finer magnitude resolution at the same low-bit cost, with round-to-nearest derived from the grid geometry and the clamp saturating the forward code. Activations are far harder to quantize than weights, so they stay at int8 via symmetric absmax with `s_x=max(|x|)/127`, while the contribution stays in the low-bit weights. The layer computes the fixed-grid by int8 inner product and then dequantizes by `s · s_x`; a variance estimate gives `Var(y)=n·E[w̃²]E[x̃²]`, so a normalization before the activation quant keeps the quantized forward at order-one variance across depth; and because small updates rarely flip a discrete weight, training should search a larger learning-rate regime than the usual full-precision one. All of it lives behind a single `nn.Linear` drop-in, so the model, optimizer, data pipeline, and loss are reused unchanged.
