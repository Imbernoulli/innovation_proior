I start with the cleanest form of the problem. I have a fixed classifier `f_theta`, I have white-box access so I can backprop and read off `∇_x L(theta, x, y)` for any loss `L`, and I'm handed one image `x` the model already gets right, with a budget `eps`. I want to nudge `x` so the model gets it wrong, but I'm only allowed to move each pixel by at most `eps`, and the result has to stay a valid image. Writing it down: I want `delta` solving

  max_{delta} L(theta, x + delta, y)   subject to   ||delta||_inf <= eps   and   x + delta in [0,1]^d.

So this is a constrained maximization of the loss over a little box around `x`. That's the whole problem. There's no closed form — `L` is a deep network, wildly non-concave in `x` — so I'm not going to *solve* it exactly; I'm going to climb it as well as a gradient method can. The reason I even care to climb it well rather than just "get one misclassification" is that the strength of this maximizer is the thing being measured: at a tight budget, a sloppy attack reports the model as more robust than it is, because the examples it fails to flip are not actually safe, they're just out of its reach. So I want the strongest in-budget maximizer I can build out of gradients.

What's the cheapest thing I could possibly do? Linearize the loss around `x`. To first order, `L(theta, x + delta, y) ≈ L(theta, x, y) + ∇_x L^T delta`. Maximize that linear approximation over the box `||delta||_inf <= eps`. This subproblem I *can* solve exactly, and it's worth doing it carefully rather than guessing, because the answer dictates the entire shape of every attack that follows. I want `argmax_{||delta||_inf <= eps} g^T delta` where `g = ∇_x L`. The constraint `||delta||_inf <= eps` says every coordinate independently satisfies `|delta_i| <= eps` — the coordinates don't couple at all. So I can maximize coordinate by coordinate: `g_i delta_i` is largest, given `|delta_i| <= eps`, by pushing `delta_i` to the boundary in the direction of `g_i`'s sign, i.e. `delta_i = eps * sign(g_i)`. The gain is `sum_i g_i * eps * sign(g_i) = eps * sum_i |g_i| = eps * ||g||_1`. So the optimal single step under an L_inf budget is

  delta = eps * sign(g),   g = ∇_x L(theta, x, y),

and it buys me `eps * ||g||_1`. That's the fast gradient sign step. Notice it lands me at a *corner* of the box — every coordinate maxed out to `+-eps` — because the linear objective is extremized at a vertex of the feasible polytope. And notice *why* it's `sign(g)` and not `g` itself: it's the steepest-ascent direction with respect to the L_inf geometry, not the Euclidean one. (If the budget were L_2, `||delta||_2 <= eps`, the same maximization gives `delta = eps * g/||g||_2` — the raw gradient, normalized. The norm in the constraint decides the shape of the step.) Good, that's a clean, principled, one-gradient attack.

But I should be honest about what I just did: I maximized the *linearization*, not the loss. The approximation `L(x + delta) ≈ L(x) + g^T delta` is only good for `delta` small, and I just took `delta` as large as the budget allows — a full `eps` jump in every coordinate at once. The true loss surface is curved; over a jump that big the linear model is essentially fiction. So `x + eps*sign(g)` lands me at some box corner, but there's no reason that corner is near the actual maximizer of the true loss inside the box. I can feel the loss I'm leaving on the table. Concretely, the gradient I trusted was the gradient *at `x`*; the moment I move, the gradient changes, and the direction I committed to is stale. One linearized step is a guess that's only as good as the surface is flat, and these surfaces are not flat.

I cannot trust one linearization across the whole budget, so I take a *small* step in the sign direction, re-evaluate the gradient where I land, take another small step, and keep doing that. Replace one big leap by many little gradient-sign steps, each one re-reading the local slope. With step size `alpha`:

  x_{t+1} = x_t + alpha * sign( ∇_x L(theta, x_t, y) ),   x_0 = x.

Now I'm following the curved surface instead of betting everything on the tangent plane at the start. But the instant I iterate, I break the constraint. After a few steps of size `alpha`, the accumulated move `x_t - x` can wander outside the eps-box, and `x_t` can leave `[0,1]`. Each step is locally a good ascent direction but globally I've left the feasible set. I need to put the iterate *back* into the box after every step. Wall — but a familiar one: this is exactly what projected gradient methods are for. After each unconstrained ascent step, project onto the feasible set.

So I need the projection. The feasible set is the intersection of two constraints: `||x' - x||_inf <= eps` (stay within the budget) and `x' in [0,1]^d` (stay a valid image). Let me work out the projection onto each, because I want to *know* it's right, not just clamp things and hope. Take the L_inf ball first: I want `Pi(z) = argmin_{||x' - x||_inf <= eps} ||x' - z||_2`. The objective `||x' - z||_2^2 = sum_i (x'_i - z_i)^2` and the constraint `|x'_i - x_i| <= eps` both decouple across coordinates, so I minimize each `(x'_i - z_i)^2` independently subject to `x_i - eps <= x'_i <= x_i + eps`. A 1-D squared distance to a point `z_i`, constrained to an interval `[x_i - eps, x_i + eps]`: the minimizer is `z_i` itself if it's already inside the interval, otherwise the nearer endpoint. That is precisely clamping `x'_i` to `[x_i - eps, x_i + eps]`. So *element-wise clipping is the Euclidean projection onto the L_inf box* — it's not a heuristic, it's exactly `Pi`. Equivalently, write the perturbation `delta = z - x`, clip `delta` to `[-eps, eps]` coordinate-wise, and set `x' = x + delta`. The pixel-box `[0,1]` is the same kind of object — another set of per-coordinate intervals — so its projection is clamping to `[0,1]`. The intersection is also a coordinate-wise box, with coordinate `i` constrained to `[max(x_i - eps, 0), min(x_i + eps, 1)]`, and the Euclidean projection onto that intersection is clamping `z_i` to exactly this interval. Since these intervals share the same axes and `x_i in [0,1]`, the implementation can get the same point by first clipping the perturbation to `[-eps, eps]`, adding it to `x`, and then clipping the image to `[0,1]`; the two clips collapse to the single interval clamp above. Concretely: after the ascent step, clip `(x_{t+1} - x)` to `[-eps, eps]`, add it back to `x`, then clip the whole thing to `[0,1]`. That's projected gradient ascent under L_inf:

  x_{t+1} = clip_{[0,1]} ( x + clip_{[-eps,eps]} ( x_t + alpha*sign(∇_x L(theta, x_t, y)) - x ) ).

This is the multi-step generalization of the sign attack, with the projection making every iterate feasible. Each step is L_inf-steepest ascent of the true loss read locally; the projection keeps me honest about the budget.

Now, how big should `alpha` be, and how many steps? These aren't free knobs; let me reason about what they have to satisfy. If `alpha` is huge, I overshoot the box every single step and the projection just slams me onto the boundary — I'm back to a one-step corner attack with extra cost, the iteration buys me nothing. If `alpha` is tiny, each step is a faithful local ascent but I need a lot of them to get anywhere, and with a fixed budget of steps I might never traverse the ball. There's a concrete requirement hiding here: from *any* starting point inside the eps-ball I should be able to reach the opposite side and still have steps left to move *along* the boundary once I'm pinned there. The diameter of the box in any coordinate is `2*eps` (from `-eps` to `+eps`), so `steps * alpha` had better comfortably exceed `2*eps`. A clean way to set it is `alpha = c * eps / steps` with `c` a small constant above 2 — say `alpha ≈ 2.5 * eps / steps`, so total reach is `2.5*eps`, enough to cross the ball from any interior start and keep moving on the boundary. With a generous step count like 40, even a larger per-step `alpha = eps/4` is fine — `40 * eps/4 = 10*eps` of total reach, far more than the `2*eps` diameter, so the iterate easily explores the whole box and spends most of its steps refining its position on the high-loss boundary rather than crawling toward it. More steps usually make the attack stronger, up to diminishing returns, because extra gradient updates keep correcting stale-gradient error instead of trusting the first tangent plane. So I'll take many steps (tens), with `alpha` set so the total reach overshoots the diameter several times over.

There's still a weakness I haven't addressed, and it's about *where I start*. I've been starting the iteration deterministically at `x_0 = x`. But the very first gradient I read is `∇_x L` evaluated exactly at the clean point `x` — and that gradient is suspect. The loss surface right next to a data point isn't smooth; there are sharp curvature artifacts localized at `x` that can point the gradient in a misleading direction, so the steepest-ascent direction *at `x`* can be a poor guide to where the loss actually climbs a little further out. If I always launch from `x`, I commit my whole trajectory to following that one possibly-corrupted initial direction, and I climb into whatever single local maximum sits in the basin containing `x`. I never see the other basins. Since the inner problem is non-concave, there isn't one maximum — there are many, scattered across the box.

The escape is to not start at `x`. Sample a perturbation with each coordinate uniform in `[-eps, eps]`, set `x_0 = x + u`, clip `x_0` into `[0,1]`, and then run the projected iteration from there. Near a pixel boundary the clipped point is not literally uniform over the feasible intersection, but the implementation is exactly this uniform perturbation followed by the valid-range clip. The random kick jumps me off the non-smooth point `x` before I ever read a gradient, so the first gradient I trust is evaluated somewhere the surface is better-behaved. If I can afford several launches, I can keep the candidate with the highest final loss and sample more than one basin instead of betting on a single trajectory. This is the difference between "follow one trajectory from the one suspicious point" and "probe the box from a less brittle starting point." The random start costs no extra gradient evaluation beyond initialization, and it gives the search a chance to dodge the gradient-masking right at the data point.

Let me sit with whether this iteration-with-restarts is actually finding the worst case or just *a* high-loss point, because the whole claim that this is a *strong* attack rests on it. The inner maximization is non-concave; in principle there could be some isolated, much-higher maximum that gradient ascent never finds, and then I'd be badly underestimating the model's vulnerability. So I need a diagnostic for the landscape: run the projected iteration from many random starts on a fixed model and look at the loss of the final iterate each time. The useful pattern is specific. The loss should climb in a fairly consistent fashion from each random start and then plateau quickly, rather than crawling forever. Across many restarts the plateau values should *concentrate*, clustering around a common value with no extreme outliers. The maxima themselves can be genuinely distinct points — pairwise L_2 distances and angles can look like far-apart, nearly orthogonal random points in the box — while still reaching almost the same loss. If that is what the landscape gives me, then the formal non-concavity is not the practical blocker it first appears to be: many different local maxima are about equally good. The practical upshot is narrower and better than a theorem. Random restarts do not prove there is no hidden higher maximum, but if many starts keep landing on the same loss ceiling, then a first-order adversary using only input-gradients has little evidence of a significantly better point to chase. This projected iteration is the right candidate for the strongest first-order attack because it already uses those gradients repeatedly, from many starts, inside the exact threat set.

I want to name the components against the prior art so I'm sure I haven't just rederived something weaker. The single sign step `x + eps*sign(g)` is the fast gradient sign method — one linearized step, the `t=0` special case of my iteration if I took one step of size `eps` from `x_0 = x`. The deterministic multi-step version is the basic iterative method — the same sign step and coordinate clipping, but launched from `x` with no random start. And the random-pre-step idea is the single-step "prepend a random kick to escape the non-smooth vicinity" trick — which I'm now folding into the start of the *full* iteration rather than using as a one-shot. So what I've built is: take the steepest-ascent-under-L_inf direction (FGSM's insight), apply it iteratively to follow the curved surface (the iterative method's insight), keep every iterate feasible by Euclidean projection onto the box (which I showed is just clipping), and launch from a uniform random perturbation before the first gradient read, optionally repeating the launch when I want a stronger search. It is projected gradient *ascent* on the loss — equivalently projected gradient *descent* on the negative loss, which is why I think of it as PGD — and it strictly contains FGSM and the basic iterative method as degenerate deterministic-start or one-step cases.

One more design lever worth examining: the loss `L` I'm climbing. The obvious choice is the cross-entropy the model was trained with, and it works; it is also the simplest scalar objective to backpropagate through the attack loop. But a classifier's actual failure condition is a logit comparison, not a probability value. If I want the adversarial point to cross the boundary with a prescribed margin, the clean object is the gap between the best wrong logit and the true logit. So an alternative is a margin loss in logit space: maximize `(max_{j != y} z_j) - z_y`, optionally with a confidence offset so the example is not merely barely misclassified. That directly targets the decision boundary and the requested confidence margin. The same projected iteration runs unchanged with this loss swapped in — only the scalar I differentiate changes — so the framework is loss-agnostic; cross-entropy is the simplest default and the margin loss is the drop-in when I want the inner maximization to push on logits rather than probabilities. I'll build with cross-entropy and note the loss is a pluggable choice.

Now I should make sure this attack is *consistent* with how a defender would use it, because that's a sanity check on whether "solve the inner max well" is even the right target. Suppose a defender wants to train against this adversary: their objective is the saddle point `min_theta E[ max_{||delta||_inf<=eps} L(theta, x+delta, y) ]`, and they'll want the gradient of the outer min, `∇_theta` of the inner-max value. Is the gradient of the loss *at the inner maximizer* a valid descent direction for that max-function? This is Danskin's theorem. Let `phi(theta) = max_{delta in S} g(theta, delta)` with `g(theta,delta) = L(theta, x+delta, y)` and `S` compact. Danskin says that if `g(.,delta)` is differentiable for each `delta` and `∇_theta g` is continuous, then `phi` is locally Lipschitz, directionally differentiable, and its directional derivative in direction `d` is `phi'(theta; d) = sup_{delta in S*(theta)} d^T ∇_theta g(theta, delta)`, where `S*` is the set of inner maximizers; and if the maximizer is unique, `phi` is differentiable with `∇phi(theta) = ∇_theta g(theta, delta*)`. The sign matters here. In the unique-max case, set `h = ∇_theta g(theta, delta*)`. The direction `+h` has derivative `phi'(theta; h) = h^T h = ||h||_2^2 > 0`, so it is an ascent direction for the max-function, not a descent direction. The training direction has to be `d = -h`, and Danskin gives `phi'(theta; -h) = -h^T h = -||h||_2^2 < 0` whenever `h` is nonzero. If several exact maximizers are active, the derivative is a supremum over their gradients, so the negative of one active gradient is not automatically a descent direction unless the active gradients align. The safe nonsmooth version is to look at the active-gradient set `A` and choose the minimum-norm point `p` in `conv(A)` when that point is nonzero. Projection geometry gives `p^T a >= ||p||_2^2` for every active gradient `a`, so `phi'(theta; -p) = sup_{a in A} (-p)^T a <= -||p||_2^2 < 0`. In the usual tie-free case this collapses back to the single-gradient rule: find the worst-case `delta`, then backprop the loss at `x + delta` and step opposite that parameter-gradient. The hypotheses don't hold exactly — ReLU and max-pooling make `L` non-differentiable on a measure-zero set, and my PGD only finds *approximate*, *local* maximizers — but the non-differentiability is on a null set I essentially never hit, and for a local maximizer I can restrict to a neighborhood `S'` of the box on which it's the global max and apply the theorem there. So the attack and the defense are two halves of one min-max problem, which is exactly why building a strong inner-max solver is the principled goal: it makes the saddle-point training direction meaningful.

I fill the one empty slot in the harness with the concrete attack loop. The pieces are all settled: sample a uniform perturbation in the eps-box, clip to the valid range; then a fixed number of steps, each computing the input-gradient of cross-entropy at the current iterate, stepping `alpha` along its sign, projecting the perturbation back to `[-eps, eps]` and the image to `[0,1]`; return the final iterate.

```python
import torch
import torch.nn as nn


def run_attack(
    model: nn.Module,
    images: torch.Tensor,   # (N, C, H, W) in [0, 1]
    labels: torch.Tensor,   # (N,)
    eps: float,             # L_inf budget
    device: torch.device,
    n_classes: int,
) -> torch.Tensor:
    import torch.nn.functional as F

    _ = n_classes                      # cross-entropy needs no class count here
    model.eval()
    steps = 40                          # many small steps > one big step: follow the curved loss
    alpha = eps / 4.0                   # per-step size; steps*alpha = 10*eps >> 2*eps box diameter

    x = images.clone().detach().to(device)       # clean reference x; the L_inf ball is centered here
    labels = labels.clone().detach().to(device)

    # random start: sample a uniform perturbation in the eps-box,
    # then project into the valid image range.
    x_adv = x + torch.empty_like(x).uniform_(-eps, eps)
    x_adv = torch.clamp(x_adv, 0.0, 1.0).detach()

    for _ in range(steps):
        x_adv.requires_grad_(True)
        logits = model(x_adv)
        loss = F.cross_entropy(logits, labels)            # loss to ascend (pluggable; CE default)
        grad = torch.autograd.grad(loss, x_adv)[0]        # white-box input-gradient at x_adv

        with torch.no_grad():
            # L_inf-steepest ascent step: move alpha along sign(grad)
            x_adv = x_adv + alpha * grad.sign()
            # project perturbation onto the L_inf eps-ball (= clip delta), exactly Pi onto the box
            delta = torch.clamp(x_adv - x, min=-eps, max=eps)
            # project image back into [0,1]; the two box-clips equal projection onto the intersection
            x_adv = torch.clamp(x + delta, 0.0, 1.0)

        x_adv = x_adv.detach()

    return x_adv
```

I am solving the constrained maximization `max_{||delta||_inf<=eps} L(theta, x+delta, y)` over a box around `x` — strong here means climbing it well, because a weak maximizer overstates robustness. Linearizing the loss and maximizing over the L_inf box has the exact solution `eps*sign(grad)` — the steepest-ascent direction under L_inf is the sign of the gradient, landing at a box corner — which is the single-step sign attack, but it trusts one stale tangent across the whole budget. Replacing one big leap by many small `alpha*sign(grad)` steps that re-read the gradient follows the curved surface instead, but breaks feasibility, so I project after each step; the Euclidean projection onto the L_inf box *is* coordinate-wise clipping, and the image-plus-budget intersection is the coordinate interval `[max(x_i-eps,0), min(x_i+eps,1)]`, implemented by clipping the perturbation and then the image. The gradient at the exact data point is corrupted by sharp local curvature, so I launch from a uniform random perturbation in the eps-box and clip to the image range before the first gradient read, with optional restarts when I want to sample more basins. Probing the landscape shows many distinct maxima with nearly the same loss, so this random-start projected iteration is the strongest practical first-order target I have, while still only supporting a conjecture rather than a theorem about hidden maxima. Cross-entropy is the default loss but the iteration is loss-agnostic. And Danskin's theorem closes the loop in the tie-free exact-max case: the negative loss-gradient at the inner maximizer is a descent direction for the saddle-point objective, so this same maximizer is exactly what a robust-training defense would call in its inner loop. The whole thing is a few tens of gradient-sign-and-clip steps from a random start — projected gradient descent on the negative loss.
