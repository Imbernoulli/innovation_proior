A manuscript has just crossed my desk with a very specific, very checkable physical claim: Nick Herbert's "FLASH" scheme, submitted to Foundations of Physics in 1981, proposes a working design for faster-than-light communication. I don't want to wave it away by citing relativity in the abstract — I want to find the exact place the physics breaks, because the argument is concrete enough that it should be findable.

The setup: a source fires two entangled photons in opposite directions, one toward Alice, one toward Bob. Alice can choose, at the last possible instant, which observable to measure on her photon — linear polarization (H versus V) or circular polarization (R versus L). Entanglement forces Bob's untouched photon into the corresponding definite state relative to whichever basis Alice happened to pick, the moment she measures, independent of the distance between them. Bob doesn't know which basis Alice chose. Herbert's move is to have Bob feed his single photon into a laser gain tube and let stimulated emission blow it up into a macroscopic burst of photons that are, he assumes, faithful copies of whatever polarization state came in. If a burst of identical copies is really available, Bob can measure many of them along one fixed axis, say H/V, and read the statistics: a clean, unanimous outcome if Alice happened to use H/V too, a different statistical signature if she used R/L instead, since an R- or L-polarized photon is an equal superposition of H and V relative to Bob's axis. Either way Bob would learn Alice's basis choice without waiting for any classical signal from her — an instantaneous bit, in flat contradiction with relativity.

Everything in the scheme rides on one physical premise: that a gain medium driven by a single incoming photon in an arbitrary, unknown polarization state produces, via stimulated emission, a burst of photons that are faithful copies of that state — for any input polarization, not only the ones aligned with whatever axis the crystal happens to be built around. This isn't a crazy premise on its face; it is close to the textbook description of how a laser works, since stimulated emission does reproduce the mode of the photon that triggers it. What's not obvious is whether that reproduction survives when the input polarization is a genuine superposition relative to the device's own preferred axis, which is exactly the case Herbert's scheme depends on.

So I turn the physical question into a mathematical one I can actually settle: is there any fixed, physically legitimate evolution — any unitary U acting on the photon plus a blank ancilla standing in for whatever the amplifier leaves behind — that sends |psi>|b> to |psi>|psi> for every possible polarization state |psi>, not merely for two states chosen in advance?

Suppose such a U exists and, in particular, correctly clones the two basis states aligned with Bob's fixed measurement axis: |0>|b> -> |0>|0>, |1>|b> -> |1>|1>. This is exactly the regime a real gain medium is good at: stimulated emission repeated along one preferred axis behaves almost like a classical copier, because H and V here play the same role a 0 or 1 bit plays for a classical repeater — they are already fully distinguishable, so nothing stops a device from copying them individually. FLASH needs more than that, though: it needs the same device to also handle Alice's R and L outcomes, i.e. inputs that are superpositions a|0>+b|1> of the very states it was built to copy. But quantum evolution is linear, and linearity already fixes U's action on any superposition once its action on |0> and |1> is fixed: U(a|0>+b|1>)|b> = a|0>|0> + b|1>|1>. Two genuine copies of an R- or L-polarized photon, on the other hand, should look like (a|0>+b|1>) tensor (a|0>+b|1>) = a^2|00> + ab|01> + ab|10> + b^2|11>. Compare the two term by term: the linear output is missing the cross terms ab|01> and ab|10> outright, and even the surviving terms carry the wrong coefficients, a and b instead of a^2 and b^2. The two agree only when a or b is zero — that is, only when the "superposition" was secretly one of the two basis states all along. For every genuine superposition, a device that faithfully copies |0> and |1> is mathematically barred from also faithfully copying their combinations.

That is the exact spot where Herbert's gain tube must fail, and it fails for a structural reason, not an engineering one. Any device good enough to make many faithful copies of an H- or V-polarized input is, by the linearity of quantum mechanics, automatically the wrong device for an R- or L-polarized input — precisely the case Bob needs to be able to distinguish from a matching-basis input in order to read off Alice's bit. No redesign of the gain medium, no cleverer pump geometry, escapes this, because the argument never used any detail of gain tubes or stimulated emission; it only used that closed quantum evolution is linear.

There's a second route to the same conclusion, one that doesn't privilege the H/V axis at all, which is reassuring, since it shows the obstruction isn't an artifact of the basis I happened to pick. Suppose U clones two states |psi> and |phi> that need not be orthogonal: U|psi>|b> = |psi>|psi>, U|phi>|b> = |phi>|phi>. U is unitary, hence inner-product-preserving, so the inner product of the inputs must equal the inner product of the outputs. The inputs give <psi|phi><b|b> = <psi|phi>. The outputs give <psi|phi>^2. A shared copier can only exist if <psi|phi> = <psi|phi>^2, which forces <psi|phi> to equal exactly 0 or exactly 1 — orthogonal states, or the same state. H/V states and R/L states overlap at 1/sqrt(2), neither 0 nor 1, so they already rule out one common copier. This is the same fact the linearity check surfaced, seen from the geometry of state space rather than from an explicit basis expansion: cloning would have to square every overlap, and squaring an inner product is not something any unitary can realize in general.

This also clarifies why the mistake was subtle enough to be worth publishing in the first place. Ordinary laboratory amplifiers really do behave like copiers for light prepared along one fixed, known axis — that is the entire physical basis of a laser, and it's the same reason a classical bit, or any known orthogonal signal, can be copied without contradiction: distinguishable states never run into the inner-product-squaring problem, since <0|1> is already 0 and 0 squared is still 0. Herbert's scheme is the classical-copying intuition pushed one step past where it's licensed to go — onto an unknown, possibly non-orthogonal state relative to the gain medium's own axis. Asher Peres, refereeing the paper, felt certain it had to be wrong on relativistic grounds but could not immediately point to the specific defect, and recommended publishing it anyway on the reasoning that finding the exact error would itself be worth the trouble. That is exactly what happened: Wootters and Zurek, and independently Dieks, replied within the year with the general argument above.

One more patch is worth ruling out before I call this settled: maybe the gain medium doesn't need to output a clean |psi>|psi>; maybe it's enough if it leaves some correlated residue |A_psi> behind in the crystal, since Bob only needs distinguishing statistics, not a pure two-copy state. Suppose the full output is |psi>|psi>|A_psi> for input psi and |phi>|phi>|A_phi> for input phi. The same unitarity argument now gives <psi|phi> = <psi|phi>^2 <A_psi|A_phi>. Since |<A_psi|A_phi>| <= 1, the right-hand side has magnitude at most |<psi|phi>|^2, strictly smaller than |<psi|phi>| itself whenever 0 < |<psi|phi>| < 1. The contradiction survives, only shrunk by whatever correlation the device manages to hide in its own leftover state. There is no way to buy back cloning of a non-orthogonal pair by stashing information in the apparatus or its environment.

So the conclusion is not a statement about this one gain tube; it rules out every conceivable amplifier at once, because the proof never touched any physical detail of amplification — it used only unitarity and linearity, properties any closed quantum evolution must have. FLASH needed a fixed process able to faithfully reproduce an arbitrary, unknown polarization state, and no such process is compatible with quantum mechanics, full stop. That is strong enough to close off not just the 1981 proposal but any future redesign of it — which is presumably also why a version of this same fact, proved by Park back in 1970 in the different language of measurement theory, sat unnoticed for twelve years: without a concrete, superluminal scheme forcing the question, there was no reason to state it as a theorem about copying at all. The lesson is not that quantum states are fragile; it's that "read out and rewrite freely," the assumption underneath both classical information processing and Herbert's amplifier, is a privilege that unknown quantum information does not carry. Only distinguishable, orthogonal states get to behave like classical labels. Everything else is protected by the same linearity that made the FLASH channel look plausible in the first place — which is why, a few years later, the identical fact that closes off superluminal signaling through EPR pairs turns into the resource that lets quantum key distribution catch an eavesdropper: nobody, relativity-violating physicist or spy, gets to clone an unknown quantum state on the sly.
