When I pick a classification loss I reach for cross-entropy, $-\log(P_t)$ where $P_t$ is the softmax probability my model assigns to the true class, and for imbalanced data I reach for focal loss, $-(1-P_t)^\gamma \log(P_t)$. But these are just two fixed curves. A loss is in principle *any* differentiable map from predictions and labels to a scalar — an enormous space — and I have been picking two points in it out of habit, with no argument that either is the right curve for the architecture and dataset in front of me. What matters for training is not the printed loss value but the gradient as a function of how confident the model already is, and once you write down $-\log(P_t)$ or $-(1-P_t)^\gamma \log(P_t)$ that whole gradient shape is hard-wired. I want to open this space into a small, interpretable family that contains cross-entropy and focal loss as particular members and that I can tune with a tiny task-specific search. The obstacle is that the space is far too large to search directly, so I need a parameterization that is low-dimensional enough to grid over yet rich enough to hold the losses I already trust.

The honest options on the table all fall short in the same way. Cross-entropy is a strong general default but exposes no knob; its $-\log$ shape is simply assumed optimal. Focal loss has exactly one knob, $\gamma \ge 0$, and it can only down-weight easy examples — useful on imbalanced detection, not shown to help balanced classification, and able to slide in only one direction. A prior idea is to truncate the loss to its first few terms to buy mean-absolute-error-like robustness to label noise, but on clean high-class-count data this needs hundreds of terms before it stops losing accuracy, so it yields no compact loss. And black-box meta-learning over a multivariate Taylor parameterization searches a space that grows with the polynomial order, so its wins are confined to low-class-count problems with a handful of parameters; for thousand-class problems where many terms matter the search becomes prohibitive. None of these gives me a compact, interpretable family with a clear gradient meaning.

The way in is to let cross-entropy suggest its own family. The logarithm has the Mercator series $-\log(x) = (1-x) + \tfrac12(1-x)^2 + \tfrac13(1-x)^3 + \dots$, valid for $x \in (0,1]$, exactly the range a probability lives in. Substituting $x = P_t$ gives an exact rewriting,

$$L_{\mathrm{CE}} = -\log(P_t) = \sum_{j\ge 1} \frac{1}{j}(1-P_t)^j = (1-P_t) + \tfrac12(1-P_t)^2 + \tfrac13(1-P_t)^3 + \dots,$$

which says cross-entropy is a weighted sum of polynomial bases $(1-P_t)^j$ with coefficients $\alpha_j = 1/j$. The general family writes itself, $L = \sum_j \alpha_j (1-P_t)^j$; with nonnegative coefficients it is monotone decreasing in $P_t$, and each $\alpha_j$ is a dial on one power of the prediction error, since $(1-P_t)$ is the distance from a perfect prediction and $(1-P_t)^j$ is that distance to the $j$th power. I call this family PolyLoss, and the particular member I will end up proposing is Poly-1. Focal loss lives here too: the modulating factor pulls straight through the series, $-(1-P_t)^\gamma\log(P_t) = \sum_j \frac1j (1-P_t)^{j+\gamma}$, so focal loss is the same $1/j$ profile shifted *horizontally* by $\gamma$ — the coefficient that sat on power $j$ now sits on power $j+\gamma$. That reframing tells me focal loss has exactly one degree of freedom and can only slide one way; if the right move is not a horizontal slide it cannot make it.

What actually trains the network is the gradient, so I look at $-dL/dP_t$. Differentiating term by term, $\frac{d}{dP_t}\frac1j(1-P_t)^j = -(1-P_t)^{j-1}$: the $1/j$ coefficient exactly cancels the power that comes down, leaving the clean geometric series

$$-\frac{dL_{\mathrm{CE}}}{dP_t} = \sum_{j\ge 1}(1-P_t)^{j-1} = 1 + (1-P_t) + (1-P_t)^2 + \dots.$$

Two facts drive the design. The leading term is the constant $1$, independent of $P_t$ — every example, however confidently right or wrong, contributes the same $1$, so summed over a batch this push is just the count of examples and presses hardest on the most numerous class. Focal loss removes exactly this constant: its leading push is $(1+\gamma)(1-P_t)^\gamma$, which fades as $P_t \to 1$, suppressing confident examples. The second fact, measured over ResNet-50/ImageNet-1K training, is that this single leading term $(1-P_t)$ carries more than half of the cross-entropy gradient for roughly the last two thirds of training. So the first coefficient is by far the highest-leverage one to touch.

The tempting move is to drop the tail — set $\alpha_{j>N}=0$ — but that fails on many-class data, and it is worth seeing why precisely. Early in training the model is near chance, so for the true class $P_t \approx 0.001$; the order-$500$ gradient coefficient is then $(1-P_t)^{499} = 0.999^{499} \approx 0.6$, nowhere near negligible. Cleanly, if I cut after $N$ terms the missing tail of the loss is $R_N(p) = \sum_{j>N}\frac1j(1-p)^j$, and dropping the $1/j$ factor bounds it on $p\in[\delta,1]$ by $|R_N(p)| \le (1-p)^{N+1}/p \le (1-\delta)^N/\delta$, with the derivative residual $|R'_N(p)| = (1-p)^N/p \le (1-\delta)^N/\delta$ just as large. Forcing both below a tolerance $\zeta$ needs $N > \log_{1-\delta}(\zeta\delta)$, which explodes as $\delta \to 0$ — exactly the many-class early-training regime. So the tail is load-bearing and must be kept, which also kills retuning all the coefficients: if hundreds matter, no grid or black-box search over hundreds of dials is feasible.

The resolution is to keep the entire cross-entropy profile intact — every $1/j$, tail included — and perturb only the leading coefficients on top of it. Writing the first $N$ as $1/j + \varepsilon_j$ and leaving the rest at $1/j$, the infinite tail collapses back into $-\log(P_t)$ and one is left with cross-entropy plus a short polynomial correction, $L_{\text{Poly-}N} = -\log(P_t) + \sum_{j=1}^N \varepsilon_j (1-P_t)^j$. Because the very first term dominates the gradient, the smallest useful choice is $N=1$, perturbing the single leading coefficient:

$$L_{\text{Poly-1}} = (1+\varepsilon_1)(1-P_t) + \tfrac12(1-P_t)^2 + \tfrac13(1-P_t)^3 + \dots = -\log(P_t) + \varepsilon_1 (1-P_t).$$

This is one extra hyperparameter and one extra term added to cross-entropy, with $\varepsilon_1 = 0$ recovering cross-entropy exactly and $\varepsilon_1 \ge -1$ keeping the leading coefficient nonnegative. The sign has a direct gradient meaning. Adding $\varepsilon_1(1-P_t)$ turns the training push into $(1+\varepsilon_1) + (1-P_t) + (1-P_t)^2 + \dots$, so a positive $\varepsilon_1$ strengthens the constant push that survives all the way until $P_t$ reaches $1$ — it keeps pressing the model toward higher target-class confidence even when it is already fairly confident. On balanced data, where there is no majority class to over-serve, plain cross-entropy can leave this confidence pressure too weak on moderately-correct examples, and a positive $\varepsilon_1$ restores it, nudging predictions to be more decisive. This is precisely the opposite of focal loss: focal loss removes the leading constant because its target failure mode is over-confident easy examples, while $\varepsilon_1 > 0$ adds to that constant because the balanced-classification failure mode is under-confidence. Same coefficient axis, opposite directions, each tied to the task's confidence failure mode rather than a universal sign — and when the failure mode is an over-confident, over-served majority, $\varepsilon_1$ can go negative to reduce the leading push. There is no closed form for the magnitude; it is a one-dimensional grid search whose answer depends on class count, balance, and confidence behavior, and I take $\varepsilon_1 = 2.0$ as the image-classification default, raising the leading push from $1$ to $3$ while leaving the entire higher-order cross-entropy profile in place. Nothing here is exotic: with $y=1$ the base $(1-P_t)$ is $(y-P_t)$, so the whole family is a weighted ensemble of prediction-to-target distances raised to integer powers; I have only made the weighting of that gap tunable through a single dial with a clear gradient meaning. The implementation is almost nothing — I already compute cross-entropy, I gather $P_t$ out of the softmax, and I add $\varepsilon_1(1-P_t)$, averaged over the batch.

```python
import torch
import torch.nn.functional as F


def poly1_cross_entropy(logits, targets, epsilon=2.0):
    """PolyLoss Poly-1: cross-entropy with the leading polynomial coefficient
    perturbed by epsilon.

        L = -log(P_t) + epsilon * (1 - P_t)

    Recovers cross-entropy at epsilon = 0. Keeping epsilon >= -1 leaves the
    leading coefficient nonnegative. P_t is the softmax probability the model
    assigns to the ground-truth class.
    """
    ce = F.cross_entropy(logits, targets, reduction="none")          # -log(P_t), [B]
    p_t = torch.softmax(logits, dim=-1).gather(
        1, targets.unsqueeze(1)).squeeze(1)                          # P_t, [B]
    poly1 = ce + epsilon * (1.0 - p_t)                               # leading-coeff perturbation
    return poly1.mean()
```
