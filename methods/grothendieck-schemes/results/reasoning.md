I start where every classical geometer starts: a system of polynomial equations in k[x_1,...,x_n], cutting out a set of solutions in k^n, and a coordinate ring k[x_1,...,x_n]/I of functions on that set. Over an algebraically closed field this is already a full subject — the Nullstellensatz tells me the maximal ideals of the coordinate ring are exactly the ordinary points of the variety, so ring and point set determine each other. But the picture won't stretch in three places I keep running into. Two rings with the same maximal-ideal points can still differ: k[epsilon]/(epsilon^2) and k both have one classical point, yet the first carries a nonzero epsilon with epsilon^2=0 that no function agreeing on the point can see; the point set has already thrown that information away. Reduction mod p and passage to characteristic zero are handled as two different subjects, because there's no room in "variety over algebraically closed k" for a ring like Z, whose primes aren't all maximal and which isn't a field at all. And a family of varieties gets described fiber by fiber, with no single object holding the generic member and the degenerations together.

The habit of naming subvarieties by a "generic point" is old — the Italian school used it loosely, meaning a point with no special properties, so that whatever holds at it holds at almost every point of the subvariety. Noether and van der Waerden made this precise before the problem ever reached me: a generic point of a subvariety V' of V corresponds to the prime ideal of V' in the coordinate ring, and a classical point of V is just the special case where that prime happens to be maximal. Weil built an entire foundation on this correspondence in 1946: fix a "universal domain" Omega, an algebraically closed field of huge transcendence degree over the base field, and represent the generic point of V' by an n-tuple of elements of Omega that satisfies exactly the equations cutting out V' and no more. It works — hard theorems get proved with it — but it's an awkward machine. The same subvariety has many different generic points, since many different tuples in Omega will do the job, so "the" generic point is really only a role that gets filled non-canonically. And Omega has to be chosen in advance, algebraically closed and large enough for every variety I will ever need, which by itself rules out holding characteristic zero and characteristic p in one object: Omega lives in a single characteristic.

But if a generic point of V' just is the prime ideal of V' — which is exactly what the Noether correspondence says — I don't need Omega's tuples at all. Let the prime ideals themselves be the points. That move isn't mine to claim: Krull tried exactly this before the war, proposing in a Paris lecture that the points of a geometric space attached to any commutative ring A should be all of A's prime ideals, with a Zariski topology on them, not restricted to polynomial rings at all — the generality is obvious the moment I start from Noether's side of the correspondence instead of Weil's, since "prime ideal" never mentions a base field. And it went nowhere. The story is that the audience laughed at him and he dropped it.

I can see why it had to die in that form. A bare topological space of primes is not enough to do geometry with. Spec A and Spec(A/nilradical) have exactly the same prime ideals — killing the nilpotents in A adds or removes not one prime — so a point set with a topology and nothing else cannot tell k[epsilon]/(epsilon^2) apart from k. Krull's space is a shell with nothing living inside it: no functions attached to it, so no way to recover A from the space, so nothing to glue and no natural notion of a map between two such spaces beyond continuity. And there was no problem forcing the generality to pay for itself; ordinary varieties over an algebraically closed field were already fully served by Weil's Omega, so "all primes of any ring" read, to that audience, as generality for its own sake.

What was missing is what Serre supplies a few years later, from a completely different direction: not generic points at all, but a sheaf of local rings living on the space of points, so that every open set carries actual functions and every point carries an actual local ring recording what vanishes there. That is the ringed-space treatment in FAC. It sidesteps Weil's Omega entirely — no universal domain, no choice of tuple — but it stays inside the classical world: the ring is still a finitely generated algebra over a field, the points are still the maximal ideals, the sheaf is still built to match an ordinary reduced variety. It still cannot hold a nonzero nilpotent, for the same underlying reason Weil's apparatus couldn't: both are built to land exactly on classical varieties and no further.

The hinge that turns Krull's dead idea into a working one is a single remark, not a new theorem. Martineau points out to Serre that the arguments of FAC remain valid for an arbitrary commutative ring, not only a finitely generated algebra over a field, as long as "maximal ideal" is replaced by "prime ideal" throughout. That substitution is the whole trick: it grafts Krull's forgotten generality directly onto Serre's sheaf. Cartier writes up a version of this almost at once. I do too, independently, but I don't stop at reproducing varieties, because the problem on my desk — the Weil conjectures, wanting one cohomology theory that behaves uniformly across characteristic zero and characteristic p — needs exactly the generality Krull had and nobody yet had a use for.

So: for a commutative ring A, the points of Spec A are all of its prime ideals, closed sets are V(I) for an ideal I, and now there is a structure sheaf besides — the basic open D(f), the primes not containing f, gets the localization A_f, and the stalk at a prime p is the local ring A_p, whose maximal ideal records the functions vanishing at p. This one addition repairs exactly what killed Krull's version: Spec A and Spec(A/nilradical) are still the same topological space, but they are no longer the same scheme, because their structure sheaves differ. k[epsilon]/(epsilon^2) has a single prime ideal, the same underlying point as Spec k, but its local ring still contains the nonzero epsilon — the scheme is an honest infinitesimal thickening of a point, something neither Weil's Omega nor Serre's field-based sheaf could represent, because both were built to land on ordinary reduced varieties and nothing more.

And unlike Omega, which starts from an algebraically closed field and only afterward is asked to carry arithmetic — backwards from what Weil actually wanted, which was, following Kronecker, a geometry built on the integers themselves — Spec A doesn't care what A is. Spec Z is a spectrum like any other: its points are (0) and the (p), and (0) is a genuine, non-maximal prime, a generic point specializing to every (p). A scheme over Spec Z is one object whose generic fiber is characteristic zero and whose closed fibers are the reductions modulo every prime, related to each other as specializations inside a single space rather than glued together after the fact — one fan of fibers where there used to be a separate variety for every prime and another one over Q.

Families are the same idea one level up, and now for free: a morphism X -> S already is the family, and the fiber over any point s of S — closed point or generic point — is base change to the residue field k(s). There is no separate vocabulary needed for "generic behavior" against "special fiber" against "degeneration," because S's own points already come in both flavors, and specialization among them is already the topology built at the very first step.

The remaining question is why this version takes over the subject while Krull's, and even Cartier's own early write-up — by his own account deliberately confined to a Chevalley-style setting "to avoid an excessively long exposition of the preliminaries" — do not. Sheaves supply the local data the bare spectrum was missing. But there is a second ingredient nobody before had leaned on this hard: the functorial language. Varying the base ring — reducing mod p, extending scalars to a bigger field, taking Z itself as the base — has to become a formal operation on morphisms of ringed spaces rather than a case-by-case argument repeated for every ring in sight, and that is exactly what working inside a category of schemes buys; its absence, I suspect, is no small part of why the earlier attempts stayed timid even once they had most of the right pieces on the table. Gluing finishes the construction: a scheme is a locally ringed space covered by opens each isomorphic to some Spec A_i, glued along isomorphisms of locally ringed spaces, so that a ring map A -> B gives a morphism Spec B -> Spec A in the reverse direction and geometry is recovered from algebra contravariantly — this time with no exception carved out for nilpotents, for Z, or for families.
