## Research question

A physical system evolves in time — a vehicle moving, a particle drifting, a signal generated by some hidden mechanism — and we cannot see it directly. What we get is a stream of measurements, taken at successive instants, each a noisy and possibly partial function of the system's true condition. From `y(t0), y(t1), …, y(t)` we want the best possible estimate of the underlying quantity of interest at some time `t1`: the present (filtering), the past (smoothing), or the future (prediction). "Best" means minimizing the expected loss — for a quadratic penalty, the mean-squared error — given everything observed so far.

There is a known optimal theory for this when the signal and noise are *stationary* random processes with known spectra: form the minimum-mean-square linear filter. But the systems that matter — a spacecraft on a midcourse trajectory, a target being tracked, any time-varying plant — are *not* stationary, the measurements arrive at irregular discrete instants, only some coordinates of the state are observed, and the answer has to be computed on a digital machine with limited memory and limited time. The precise problem is to produce, at each instant, the optimal estimate of the full (possibly unobserved) state, in a form that (i) handles nonstationary and time-varying dynamics with one method, (ii) requires only the data actually available, and (iii) is cheap enough to run online — updating the estimate as each new measurement comes in rather than reprocessing the whole past.

The stakes are practical and immediate. The available optimal theory is frequency-domain, stationary, and delivers the answer as a filter impulse response that is hard to synthesize and "poorly suited to machine computation," with the difficulty growing rapidly as the problem gets bigger. A method that worked in the time domain, on the state directly, recursively, and on a computer would not be a refinement of that theory — it would make optimal estimation usable for the kind of dynamic, nonstationary, partially observed systems the available theory cannot touch.

## Background

**The estimation problem is statistical.** As Wiener emphasized, signal `x1(t)` and noise `x2(t)` are random processes; only their sum `y(t) = x1(t) + x2(t)` is observed. Everything the measurements tell us about the quantity of interest is contained in the conditional distribution `Pr[x1(t1) ≤ ξ | y(t0), …, y(t)]`. A statistical estimate is some function of the observations; to choose one rationally we assign a loss `L(ε)` to the error `ε = x1(t1) − X1(t1)` — positive, nondecreasing in `|ε|`, symmetric — and minimize the expected loss (risk). The squared error `L(ε)=ε²` is the canonical choice.

**Wiener's optimal filter (Wiener 1949; Kolmogorov 1941).** For *stationary* signal and noise with known power spectra, Wiener showed the optimal-prediction/separation problem reduces to the Wiener–Hopf integral equation, and gave a solution by **spectral factorization** in the practically important case of rational spectra. The optimal estimator is a linear time-invariant filter, specified by its impulse response, operating on the entire past of the observation. Zadeh and Ragazzini (1950) solved the finite-memory case; Booton treated the nonstationary Wiener–Hopf equation; eigenfunction approaches (Davis and others) extended it further. This body of work is the prevailing wisdom: optimal estimation = find the impulse response of the optimal Wiener filter.

**The pain points of that wisdom.** The optimal filter is specified by its impulse response, and synthesizing an actual filter from an impulse response is not a simple task; the numerical determination of that impulse response is involved and poorly suited to machine computation, getting rapidly worse as the problem grows; important generalizations — growing-memory filters, nonstationary prediction — each require a fresh derivation of considerable difficulty; and the derivations are not transparent, so the fundamental assumptions and their consequences are obscured. Underlying all four is the frequency-domain, stationary framing: a stationary process has one spectrum for all time, so the machinery is built for time-invariance and balks at systems whose statistics change.

**The whitening / shaping-filter idea (Bode–Shannon 1950).** Bode and Shannon gave a simplified derivation of the Wiener theory built on a single device: represent an arbitrary random signal (up to second-order statistics) as the output of a linear dynamic system driven by **white noise** — uncorrelated, flat-spectrum excitation. Whiten the observation and the optimal predictor becomes almost trivial. This supplies a *model* of the random process as "linear dynamics excited by white noise," and it is by then a standard tool in engineering applications of the Wiener theory. It is still presented in stationary, frequency-domain terms, but it is the seed of describing a random process by a generating dynamic system rather than by a spectrum.

**State and state transition (sampled-data and optimal-control theory).** In parallel, control theory had been recasting linear systems in **state-space** form: a dynamic system is summarized by its *state* `x` — the least information about the past needed to predict the future — evolving by first-order equations `dx/dt = F(t)x + D(t)u`, observed through `y = M(t)x`. In discrete time, `x(t+1) = Φ(t+1;t) x(t) + Δ(t)u(t)`, with `Φ` the transition matrix. Ragazzini's sampled-data work (1952) showed discrete linear systems yield to transform methods just like continuous ones; Laning and Battin (1956) used state-space models for time-varying systems. The noise-free optimal regulator (linear-quadratic) problem had been solved in this language, its solution governed by a matrix Riccati equation. State-space is intrinsically a *time-domain*, possibly *time-varying*, finite-dimensional description — exactly what the spectral framing is not.

**Recursive least squares (Gauss, 1795/1809).** The deterministic ancestor of online estimation: given a linear measurement model `Hx ≈ z` and the least-squares estimate, when a new observation arrives one need not redo all the arithmetic — the old estimate can be *corrected* by a gain times the new residual, with an information/covariance matrix updated recursively. Gauss already framed estimation this way.

**Conditional expectation and projection (Doob, Loève).** In probability theory it is standard that, for a quadratic loss, the optimal estimate of a random variable given others is the **conditional expectation**, and that for jointly Gaussian variables this conditional expectation is a *linear* function of the conditioning variables — geometrically, the **orthogonal projection** onto the subspace they span, with `Euv = 0` as the inner product. These facts (Doob pp. 75–78, 148–155; Loève pp. 455–464) were well known in probability but had not been used much in engineering estimation.

## Baselines

**Wiener filter (Wiener 1949).** Core idea: for stationary signal+noise with known spectra `S_x`, `S_n`, the minimum-mean-square linear estimate is an LTI filter whose transfer function is obtained by spectral factorization solving the Wiener–Hopf equation; output is a convolution of the observation with the optimal impulse response. Algorithm: factor the spectrum, take the causal part, realize the filter. Gap: stationary only; needs the whole past; one spectrum for all time so it cannot follow time-varying statistics; the answer is an impulse response that is hard to realize and hard to compute, and each new variant (finite memory, nonstationary) demands a new derivation. It optimizes over a frequency-domain object, not over the system's state.

**Kolmogorov prediction (1941).** Core idea: the discrete-time stationary version of the same least-squares prediction theory. Algorithm: project the future value onto the closed span of the (infinite) stationary past. Gap: same stationary, whole-past limitation; no finite carry-state, no machine-friendly recursion.

**Bode–Shannon whitening predictor (1950).** Core idea: model the signal as white noise shaped by a linear system; whiten the input, then optimal prediction is the trivial projection of white noise. Algorithm: design the whitening filter from the spectrum, predict, un-whiten. Gap: still stationary and spectral; gives the generating-model idea but not a recursive, nonstationary, finite-dimensional estimator.

**Zadeh–Ragazzini finite-memory filter (1950).** Core idea: the Wiener problem restricted to a finite observation window, with a simplified (Bode–Shannon-style) solution; also the sampled-data setting. Gap: finite memory but still a fresh per-case derivation in the Wiener framework; no single recursion subsuming growing/infinite memory and nonstationary statistics.

**Recursive least squares (Gauss).** Core idea: update a least-squares estimate as each observation arrives — new estimate = old estimate + gain × (new measurement − predicted measurement), with the normal-equations (information) matrix updated incrementally. Algorithm: maintain `x̂` and the information/covariance matrix; on each measurement, compute the gain, correct `x̂`, update the matrix. Gap: it is for a *static* unknown parameter `x` with deterministic measurement model and no dynamics — no notion of a state that *evolves* between measurements, no process noise, no stochastic propagation of uncertainty in time. It is the recursion machinery without the dynamic, stochastic model.

**State-space optimal regulator / LQR (state-transition method).** Core idea: for a linear plant `x(t+1)=Φ̂x(t)+M̂u(t)` and quadratic cost, the optimal control is a time-varying linear feedback `u*(t) = −∆̂*(t)x(t)`, with the feedback gain and cost matrix generated backward by a matrix Riccati recursion. Algorithm: solve the Riccati recursion for the gain sequence; apply the feedback. Gap: a *control* problem, deterministic and noise-free, assuming the full state is measured exactly — not an estimation problem at all. But it lives in exactly the state-space/time-domain/Riccati world that the spectral estimation theory does not, and its Riccati machinery is suggestively similar to what a recursive estimator would need.

## Evaluation settings

The natural problems against which a state estimator of this kind would be exercised are nonstationary prediction and tracking tasks stated directly in state-space form, where closed-form or numerical answers can be compared. Canonical setups: a target whose position and velocity evolve by simple kinematics (`x1(t+1)=x1(t)+x2(t)`, `x2(t+1)=x2(t)`) observed in additive, possibly correlated, noise — recover position and velocity at the time of the last measurement; a signal generated by a known shaping filter driven by white noise, observed in additive noise, with the optimal nonstationary predictor required as a function of time; and stationary cases where the established Wiener solution is available as a cross-check. The instruments of comparison are the expected quadratic loss (the trace of the error-covariance matrix), the closed-form Wiener solution where it exists, and the amount of matrix work and storage required per new observation.
