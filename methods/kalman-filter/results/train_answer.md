A physical system evolves in time — a vehicle moving, a particle drifting, a signal generated by some hidden mechanism — and we never see it directly. What arrives is a stream of measurements taken at successive instants, each a noisy and possibly partial function of the system's true condition, and from that stream we want the best estimate of the underlying state: at the present (filtering), in the past (smoothing), or in the future (prediction). "Best," for a quadratic penalty, means minimizing the mean-squared error given everything observed so far. There is a known optimal theory for exactly this, Wiener's, but it solves the wrong version of the problem. Wiener treats signal and noise as *stationary* random processes with known spectra, reduces the minimum-mean-square problem to the Wiener–Hopf integral equation, and solves it by spectral factorization. The answer comes out as a filter impulse response, and that is the source of four distinct pains: synthesizing a realizable filter from an impulse response is not a simple task; computing the impulse response numerically is involved and gets rapidly worse as the problem grows, which makes it poorly suited to a machine; every change of setting — finite memory, growing memory, nonstationary statistics — demands a fresh and difficult derivation; and the derivations are opaque, so the assumptions and their consequences are buried in the spectral manipulations. The four complaints have one root. The whole apparatus is built in the frequency domain, for stationary processes, and a stationary process has a single spectrum valid for all time. The moment the system is time-varying — and every real system worth tracking is time-varying — the machinery has nothing to grab onto. Kolmogorov's discrete prediction theory and the Bode–Shannon whitening predictor share the same stationary, spectral, whole-past limitation; Zadeh and Ragazzini's finite-memory filter relieves the memory but still needs a fresh per-case derivation; Gauss's recursive least squares has the right incremental shape but is built for a *static* unknown with a fixed measurement model, with no notion of a state that evolves, no process noise, no propagation of uncertainty in time. None of them is one method that handles nonstationarity, time-variation, and partial observation automatically while running online on a computer.

So the question is not how to solve Wiener–Hopf better; it is what to change so that those special cases stop being special. The method I propose is the Kalman filter, and what makes it work is a change of representation followed by a piece of geometry. Describe the random signal not by a spectrum but by its *state* — the least information about the past needed to predict the future — evolving by first-order linear dynamics driven by white noise. This is the Bode–Shannon shaping idea taken to its conclusion: model the world as $x(t+1) = \Phi(t+1;t)\,x(t) + u(t)$ with $u$ white, zero mean, covariance $Q(t)$, observed through $y(t) = M(t)\,x(t)$ where $M$ may be singular so that only some coordinates, or combinations including noise coordinates, are seen. Direct measurement noise can either be folded into the state or kept as a separate covariance $R$ in the recursion. Everything is written in discrete time on purpose: the measurements arrive at discrete instants anyway, and discreteness keeps the mathematics elementary instead of fighting Hilbert-space subtleties. In this representation time-variation is free — $\Phi$, $M$, $Q$ are simply allowed to depend on $t$ — which is precisely what the spectrum cannot tolerate.

Now ask the bare probabilistic question. Having observed $y(t_0),\dots,y(t)$, everything the data say about $x(t_1)$ lives in the conditional distribution, and for quadratic loss the estimate minimizing the conditional risk is the conditional mean $E[x(t_1)\mid Y(t)]$ — no Gaussian assumption needed for that statement. Conditional means need the full distribution, which I refuse to assume I know. The escape comes from probability theory: the observed variables and all their linear combinations form a vector space $Y(t)$, and equipping it with the inner product $\langle u,v\rangle = E[uv]$ turns it into a geometry in which the conditional mean, for the linear/Gaussian case, is nothing but the **orthogonal projection** of $x(t_1)$ onto $Y(t)$. To see this, split $x(t_1) = \hat{x} + \tilde{x}$ with $\hat{x}\in Y(t)$ and $\tilde{x}$ orthogonal to every element of $Y(t)$; then for any other $w\in Y(t)$, $E[(x(t_1)-w)^2] = E[\tilde{x}^2] + E[(\hat{x}-w)^2] \ge E[\tilde{x}^2]$, the cross term vanishing because $\tilde{x}\perp(\hat{x}-w)$. So among linear estimates the projection always minimizes squared error, and if the processes are Gaussian the orthogonal part is independent of $Y(t)$, making the projection the full conditional mean — no nonlinear estimate does better. The estimator therefore runs entirely on first and second moments, and Wiener's opacity complaint evaporates: the assumptions are out in the open, linearity-or-Gaussianness plus quadratic loss.

The danger is that projecting onto an ever-growing $Y(t)$ as each measurement arrives is just the growing-memory problem in new clothes. What rescues it is that the new measurement $y(t)$ is not entirely new: the part already predictable from the past is $M(t)\,x^*(t\mid t-1)$, and the only genuinely new information is the **innovation** — the residual $\tilde{y}(t\mid t-1) = y(t) - M(t)\,x^*(t\mid t-1)$ orthogonal to $Y(t-1)$. So $Y(t) = Y(t-1)\oplus Z(t)$ is an *orthogonal* sum, and projection onto an orthogonal direct sum splits additively: $\hat{E}[x\mid Y(t)] = \hat{E}[x\mid Y(t-1)] + \hat{E}[x\mid Z(t)]$. The optimal estimate updates by *adding* a correction that lives entirely in the one new direction, so the history collapses into the previous estimate and the present enters only through the residual. For the predictor case $t_1 = t+1$, the first term is just the model carrying the old estimate forward, $\Phi(t+1;t)\,x^*(t\mid t-1)$ — the process noise $u(t)$, being white, projects to zero on the past — and the second term is a linear map of the residual, giving

$$x^*(t+1\mid t) = \Phi(t+1;t)\,x^*(t\mid t-1) + \Delta^*(t)\,[\,y(t) - M(t)\,x^*(t\mid t-1)\,].$$

This is exactly Gauss's shape, new estimate equals propagated old estimate plus a gain times residual, but now with the $\Phi x^*$ propagation that Gauss never had: the unknown moves between measurements and its uncertainty is carried through the dynamics. The gain is fixed by optimality. The new error must be orthogonal to the residual just used, which gives the normal equation $E[x(t+1)\,\tilde{y}'] = \Delta^*(t)\,E[\tilde{y}\,\tilde{y}']$. Writing $P^*(t) = E[\tilde{x}(t\mid t-1)\,\tilde{x}'(t\mid t-1)]$ for the prediction-error covariance and using $\tilde{y} = M(t)\tilde{x}(t\mid t-1)$, the residual covariance is $M(t)P^*(t)M'(t)$ and the cross-covariance is $\Phi(t+1;t)P^*(t)M'(t)$, so

$$\Delta^*(t) = \Phi(t+1;t)\,P^*(t)\,M'(t)\,[\,M(t)\,P^*(t)\,M'(t)\,]^{-1}.$$

Physically this gain is an automatic, time-varying balance: when the prediction is shaky relative to the measurement the gain is large and the estimate moves hard toward the data, and when the prediction is sharp and the measurement noisy the gain is small and the data are mostly ignored. I did not posit it; demanding orthogonality of the error to the residual chose it. The remaining worry — needing $P^*(t)$ — dissolves because the error itself obeys a linear dynamic system. Subtracting the estimate recursion from the truth gives $\tilde{x}(t+1\mid t) = \Phi^*(t+1;t)\,\tilde{x}(t\mid t-1) + u(t)$ with $\Phi^*(t+1;t) = \Phi(t+1;t) - \Delta^*(t)M(t)$, and the optimal-gain normal equation forces $M(t)P^*(t)\Phi^{*\prime} = 0$, so the covariance collapses to a single nonlinear difference equation in $P^*$ alone:

$$P^*(t+1) = \Phi(t+1;t)\,\big\{\,P^*(t) - P^*(t)M'(t)[\,M(t)P^*(t)M'(t)\,]^{-1}M(t)P^*(t)\,\big\}\,\Phi'(t+1;t) + Q(t),$$

started from $P^*(t_0) = E[x(t_0)x'(t_0)]$ and cranked forward from the model alone. This is a matrix **Riccati** recursion — the time-domain replacement for Wiener–Hopf, an ordinary difference equation in a finite matrix that integrates forward step by step on a machine — and its trace, $\operatorname{trace} P^*(t)$, is the filter's own expected quadratic loss, so the estimator reports its accuracy as it runs. The same Riccati equation, under transpose-and-time-reversal, is precisely the one solved for the noise-free optimal regulator: estimation and control are dual, observation playing the role of control ($M \leftrightarrow \hat{M}$), the error covariance the role of the cost matrix, so the regulator's convergence and stability arguments carry across under matching observability and controllability hypotheses.

For a machine I reorganize the single predictor step into a self-contained **time update** and **measurement update**, carrying one state estimate $x$ and one covariance $P$. The time update advances the estimate between measurements, $x \leftarrow Fx$ and $P \leftarrow FPF' + Q$ with $F=\Phi$. The measurement update folds in $z$: form the innovation $y = z - Hx$ with $H=M$, its covariance $S = HPH' + R$ keeping direct measurement noise as the separate term $R$, the gain $K = PH'S^{-1} = PH'(HPH'+R)^{-1}$, correct $x \leftarrow x + Ky$, and shrink $P$. For the covariance shrink I use the symmetric Joseph form $P \leftarrow (I-KH)P(I-KH)' + KRK'$ rather than the compact $(I-KH)P$, because although the two agree for the exact optimal gain in exact arithmetic, the Joseph form preserves symmetry and positive semidefiniteness when arithmetic is finite and $K$ is not exactly optimal. The $P$ in the update is the post-prediction, pre-correction covariance, and the one-step predictor gain is just $\Delta^* = \Phi K$ with the propagation pulled out into the time-update step. The result is a short, exact, recursive program that runs in fixed memory with fixed matrix work per step, subsuming filtering, prediction, and smoothing, stationary and nonstationary statistics, growing and infinite memory, and partial observation — the cases Wiener handled only by separate, opaque derivations.

```python
import numpy as np
from copy import deepcopy
from math import log
import sys
from numpy import dot, zeros, eye, isscalar

def reshape_z(z, dim_z, ndim):
    z = np.asarray(z)
    if z.ndim == 0:
        z = z.reshape(1, 1)
    return z.reshape(dim_z, 1) if ndim == 2 else z.reshape(dim_z)

class KalmanFilter(object):
    """Recursive state estimator for a linear dynamic system."""

    def __init__(self, dim_x, dim_z, dim_u=0):
        if dim_x < 1:
            raise ValueError("dim_x must be 1 or greater")
        if dim_z < 1:
            raise ValueError("dim_z must be 1 or greater")
        if dim_u < 0:
            raise ValueError("dim_u must be 0 or greater")

        self.dim_x = dim_x
        self.dim_z = dim_z
        self.dim_u = dim_u

        self.x = zeros((dim_x, 1))           # state
        self.P = eye(dim_x)                  # uncertainty covariance
        self.Q = eye(dim_x)                  # process uncertainty
        self.B = None                        # control transition matrix
        self.F = eye(dim_x)                  # state transition matrix
        self.H = zeros((dim_z, dim_x))       # measurement function
        self.R = eye(dim_z)                  # measurement uncertainty
        self._alpha_sq = 1.0                 # fading memory control
        self.M = np.zeros((dim_x, dim_z))    # process-measurement cross correlation
        self.z = np.array([[None] * self.dim_z]).T

        self.K = np.zeros((dim_x, dim_z))    # kalman gain
        self.y = zeros((dim_z, 1))
        self.S = np.zeros((dim_z, dim_z))    # system uncertainty
        self.SI = np.zeros((dim_z, dim_z))   # inverse system uncertainty
        self._I = eye(dim_x)

        self.x_prior = self.x.copy()
        self.P_prior = self.P.copy()
        self.x_post = self.x.copy()
        self.P_post = self.P.copy()
        self._log_likelihood = log(sys.float_info.min)
        self._likelihood = sys.float_info.min
        self._mahalanobis = None
        self.inv = np.linalg.inv

    def predict(self, u=None, B=None, F=None, Q=None):
        if B is None:
            B = self.B
        if F is None:
            F = self.F
        if Q is None:
            Q = self.Q
        elif isscalar(Q):
            Q = eye(self.dim_x) * Q

        if B is not None and u is not None:
            self.x = dot(F, self.x) + dot(B, u)
        else:
            self.x = dot(F, self.x)

        self.P = self._alpha_sq * dot(dot(F, self.P), F.T) + Q
        self.x_prior = self.x.copy()
        self.P_prior = self.P.copy()

    def update(self, z, R=None, H=None):
        self._log_likelihood = None
        self._likelihood = None
        self._mahalanobis = None

        if z is None:
            self.z = np.array([[None] * self.dim_z]).T
            self.x_post = self.x.copy()
            self.P_post = self.P.copy()
            self.y = zeros((self.dim_z, 1))
            return

        if R is None:
            R = self.R
        elif isscalar(R):
            R = eye(self.dim_z) * R

        if H is None:
            z = reshape_z(z, self.dim_z, self.x.ndim)
            H = self.H

        self.y = z - dot(H, self.x)
        PHT = dot(self.P, H.T)
        self.S = dot(H, PHT) + R
        self.SI = self.inv(self.S)
        self.K = dot(PHT, self.SI)
        self.x = self.x + dot(self.K, self.y)

        I_KH = self._I - dot(self.K, H)
        self.P = dot(dot(I_KH, self.P), I_KH.T) + dot(dot(self.K, R), self.K.T)
        self.z = deepcopy(z)
        self.x_post = self.x.copy()
        self.P_post = self.P.copy()
```
