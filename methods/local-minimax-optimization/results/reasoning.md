Let me start from what is actually bothering me, which is not an algorithm but a definition. Everyone is training GANs and adversarial classifiers by running gradient descent ascent on $\min_{\mathbf x}\max_{\mathbf y} f(\mathbf x,\mathbf y)$ with $f$ nonconvex–nonconcave, and the algorithm sometimes settles down to a point. And I cannot say what kind of point it is. In ordinary minimization this question has a crisp answer: gradient descent finds a local minimum, a local minimum is $\nabla f=0$ with $\nabla^2 f\succeq 0$, and a local minimum always exists because a global minimum is one. I want the analogue of "local minimum" for the minimax problem, and when I reach for the obvious candidate it keeps failing me. So let me figure out, from scratch, what the right object is.

The obvious candidate is the Nash equilibrium and its local version. A Nash equilibrium of the zero-sum game is a point $(\mathbf x^\star,\mathbf y^\star)$ where neither player wants to move: $f(\mathbf x^\star,\mathbf y)\le f(\mathbf x^\star,\mathbf y^\star)\le f(\mathbf x,\mathbf y^\star)$ for all $\mathbf x,\mathbf y$ — so $\mathbf x^\star$ globally minimizes $f(\cdot,\mathbf y^\star)$ and $\mathbf y^\star$ globally maximizes $f(\mathbf x^\star,\cdot)$. Localize it the naive way — demand the two inequalities only in a $\delta$-ball — and I get the local Nash equilibrium that Daskalakis–Panageas, Mazumdar–Ratliff, and Adolphs et al. all study. Its conditions are the symmetric ones you'd guess: stationarity, and $\nabla^2_{\mathbf y\mathbf y}f\preceq 0$ together with $\nabla^2_{\mathbf x\mathbf x}f\succeq 0$; with strict definiteness on both blocks it's a strict local Nash. Clean. So why am I unhappy.

First, it doesn't always exist, and the counterexample is embarrassingly simple. Take $f(x,y)=\sin(x+y)$. The gradient is $(\cos(x+y),\cos(x+y))$, so stationary points need $x+y=(k+\tfrac12)\pi$. At those points $\nabla^2_{xx}f=\nabla^2_{yy}f=-\sin(x+y)$, which is $+1$ for odd $k$ and $-1$ for even $k$ — but always the *same* sign in both blocks. For a local Nash I need $\nabla^2_{yy}f\le 0$ and $\nabla^2_{xx}f\ge 0$ simultaneously, and that never happens here. No local Nash anywhere, on a perfectly nice bounded smooth function. That's a bad sign for a notion that's supposed to be the default target of a local algorithm.

But the deeper problem isn't existence; it's that local Nash is answering a different question than the one $\min\max$ asks. A Nash equilibrium is a *simultaneous*-game concept — both players pick at the same time, neither sees the other's choice, and the definition is symmetric in $\mathbf x$ and $\mathbf y$. Look at the second-order conditions again: $\nabla^2_{\mathbf x\mathbf x}f\succeq 0$ and $\nabla^2_{\mathbf y\mathbf y}f\preceq 0$, two separate block conditions, and the cross term $\nabla^2_{\mathbf x\mathbf y}f$ doesn't appear at all. The two players are interchangeable. But $\min_{\mathbf x}\max_{\mathbf y}$ is not symmetric. The notation says $\mathbf x$ goes first and $\mathbf y$ goes second. This is a *sequential* game: a leader commits to $\mathbf x$, then a follower, having *seen* $\mathbf x$, picks the best $\mathbf y$. That's exactly the structure of the applications — a GAN's discriminator is trained to react to the current generator; adversarial training wants a classifier robust to a perturbation chosen *after* the classifier is fixed. The order is the whole point.

In the convex–concave world I could get away with ignoring the order, because of von Neumann's minimax theorem: for convex–concave $f$ on compact convex domains, $\min_{\mathbf x}\max_{\mathbf y}f=\max_{\mathbf y}\min_{\mathbf x}f$, and Sion later relaxed convex–concave to quasiconvex–quasiconcave. When that equality holds, the leader and follower are worth the same whichever moves first, a saddle exists, and Nash = saddle = global optimum. But I am nonconvex–nonconcave, and there $\min\max\neq\max\min$ in general. So which player commits first genuinely changes the answer, and a symmetric, order-blind notion is structurally incapable of capturing it. Local Nash is the localization of the *wrong* global object for my problem.

So let me find the right global object first, and only then localize. The right global object for a sequential game is the Stackelberg solution. The follower, seeing $\mathbf x$, plays the maximizer and realizes value $\phi(\mathbf x):=\max_{\mathbf y\in\mathcal Y} f(\mathbf x,\mathbf y)$. The leader, anticipating this, picks $\mathbf x^\star$ to minimize $\phi$. So the global sequential solution — call it a global minimax point — is: $\mathbf y^\star$ a global max of $f(\mathbf x^\star,\cdot)$, and $\mathbf x^\star$ a global min of $\phi$. Equivalently, $f(\mathbf x^\star,\mathbf y)\le f(\mathbf x^\star,\mathbf y^\star)\le \max_{\mathbf y'}f(\mathbf x,\mathbf y')$ for all $\mathbf x,\mathbf y$. And this object always exists — $\phi$ is continuous when $f$ is and $\mathcal X,\mathcal Y$ compact, so the extreme-value theorem hands me a minimizer. Good: existence is no longer fragile at the global level. The trouble is that finding the global min of $\phi$ is NP-hard in general (it contains nonconvex minimization as a special case). So just as in plain optimization I trade the global minimum for a local minimum, here I want a *local surrogate for the global minimax point*. That object does not exist in the literature yet. I have to define it.

The naive move is to localize both the $\max$ and the $\min$ to the same $\delta$-ball — but that just gives me local Nash again, because $\max_{\|\mathbf y'-\mathbf y^\star\|\le\delta}f(\mathbf x,\mathbf y')$ over the same radius as the $\mathbf x$ neighborhood collapses the asymmetry. I want to preserve the fact that the follower reacts *within its own neighborhood* after the leader makes a *small* move, and crucially the follower's neighborhood can be of a *different* size than the leader's step. Let the leader's perturbation have radius $\delta$ and let the follower be allowed to range over a radius $h(\delta)$, with $h$ some function I get to choose, subject only to $h(\delta)\to 0$ as $\delta\to 0$ so the whole thing stays genuinely local. Then I ask: is $\mathbf x^\star$ a local min of the *local* worst-case value, for every small $\delta$?

So the definition I'll write down: $(\mathbf x^\star,\mathbf y^\star)$ is a local minimax point if there exist $\delta_0>0$ and a function $h$ with $h(\delta)\to0$ as $\delta\to0$, such that for all $\delta\in(0,\delta_0]$ and all $(\mathbf x,\mathbf y)$ with $\|\mathbf x-\mathbf x^\star\|\le\delta$, $\|\mathbf y-\mathbf y^\star\|\le\delta$,
$$f(\mathbf x^\star,\mathbf y)\ \le\ f(\mathbf x^\star,\mathbf y^\star)\ \le\ \max_{\mathbf y':\,\|\mathbf y'-\mathbf y^\star\|\le h(\delta)} f(\mathbf x,\mathbf y').$$
The left inequality says $\mathbf y^\star$ is a local max of $f(\mathbf x^\star,\cdot)$ — the follower is locally optimal at the committed $\mathbf x^\star$. The right inequality says: if the leader deviates to any $\mathbf x$ within $\delta$, the follower's *local* best response (within radius $h(\delta)$) is at least as good for the follower as the equilibrium value — i.e. the leader cannot improve by deviating. The free $h$ is doing the real work. Because $h$ is arbitrary subject to $h(\delta)\to0$, the *ratio* $h(\delta)/\delta$ can be anything, even $\to\infty$. That is exactly the leader-commits / follower-reacts asymmetry: the follower's reaction neighborhood is allowed to dwarf the leader's step. Letting $h$ go to zero keeps it a truly local, infinitesimal-neighborhood property of $f$; letting the ratio be free keeps the order in.

Let me stress the definition on a tiny example before I trust it, picking one where the leader's own curvature is wrong so the follower has to carry the optimality. $f(x,y)=-x^2+5xy-y^2$ at the origin. As a local Nash candidate it fails immediately: $\nabla^2_{xx}f=-2<0$, so the origin is a local *max* in $x$, not a local min. What does my definition say? First the easy inequality: $\nabla^2_{yy}f=-2<0$, so $y=0$ is a local max of $f(0,\cdot)=-y^2$ — good. The interesting one is the leader inequality. Take $h(\delta)=\delta$ and ask whether $\max_{|y|\le\delta} f(x,y)\ge f(0,0)=0$ for every $|x|\le\delta$. The inner max of $-x^2+5xy-y^2$ over $y$ is at $y=\tfrac52 x$ if that's feasible, i.e. $|x|\le 2\delta/5$, giving $-x^2+5x\cdot\tfrac52 x-(\tfrac52 x)^2=\tfrac{21}{4}x^2\ge0$; otherwise the maximizer is the boundary $y=\operatorname{sign}(x)\delta$ with value $\delta^2[-(|x|/\delta)^2+5(|x|/\delta)-1]$, which equals $\tfrac{21}{25}\delta^2>0$ at $|x|/\delta=2/5$ and increases on $[2/5,1]$. So algebraically the inner max never drops below $0$.

I don't fully trust hand algebra on a constrained max, so let me just grid it. Set $\delta=0.1$, $h=\delta$, scan $x\in[-\delta,\delta]$ and for each $x$ take $\max_{|y|\le\delta}f(x,y)$ numerically. The smallest value of that local max over all $x$ in the band comes out to $0.0$, never negative — the leader inequality holds with room to spare, while $f(0,y)$ does peak at $y=0$. So the origin passes local minimax and fails local Nash on the same function. The thing that makes this work is the cross term: the follower's reaction $y\approx\tfrac52 x$ converts the leader's *deviation* into a value gain that swamps the $-x^2$ the leader would otherwise enjoy. Local Nash, which only ever looks at the diagonal blocks $\nabla^2_{xx}$ and $\nabla^2_{yy}$, has no way to see that the follower is on the leader's side here. So local minimax accepts points local Nash rejects — which makes me suspect the inclusion runs one way, every local Nash being local minimax but not conversely. Let me check that suspicion rather than assume it.

If every local Nash is local minimax then the new notion loses nothing and only gains acceptances, so I want that direction airtight. Take $h\equiv 0$. If $(\mathbf x^\star,\mathbf y^\star)$ is local Nash, then within $\delta$ I have $f(\mathbf x^\star,\mathbf y)\le f(\mathbf x^\star,\mathbf y^\star)\le f(\mathbf x,\mathbf y^\star)$, and the inner max over a radius-$0$ ball is just $\max_{\|\mathbf y'-\mathbf y^\star\|\le 0}f(\mathbf x,\mathbf y')=f(\mathbf x,\mathbf y^\star)$. So the local Nash inequalities *are* the local minimax inequalities, with $h\equiv0$ as the witness function (which trivially satisfies $h(\delta)\to0$). So local Nash $\Rightarrow$ local minimax, and the $-x^2+5xy-y^2$ example I just gridded is a witness that the converse fails. The inclusion is therefore strict, exactly as the asymmetry suggested, and local minimax should inherit existence in more cases.

I want a second, equivalent way to see the definition, the one phrased like the Stackelberg story: the follower achieves a local-max value and the leader minimizes that. Define $g_\epsilon(\mathbf x):=\max_{\|\mathbf y-\mathbf y^\star\|\le\epsilon}f(\mathbf x,\mathbf y)$. I claim $(\mathbf x^\star,\mathbf y^\star)$ is local minimax iff $\mathbf y^\star$ is a local max of $f(\mathbf x^\star,\cdot)$ and there's $\epsilon_0>0$ such that $\mathbf x^\star$ is a local min of $g_\epsilon$ for *every* $\epsilon\in(0,\epsilon_0]$. Let me convince myself this is the same thing. Write $\mathcal P(\delta,\epsilon)$ for "$f(\mathbf x^\star,\mathbf y^\star)\le g_\epsilon(\mathbf x)$ for all $\|\mathbf x-\mathbf x^\star\|\le\delta$." Monotonicity is obvious: if $\mathcal P(\delta,\epsilon)$ holds then $\mathcal P(\delta',\epsilon')$ holds for $\delta'\le\delta$, $\epsilon'\ge\epsilon$ (smaller $\mathbf x$-ball, larger inner radius only make the inequality easier). The definition says $\mathcal P(\delta,h(\delta))$ for all small $\delta$. For the "only if": given the $h$-form, for any small $\epsilon$ pick $\delta$ with $h(\delta)\le\epsilon$ (possible since $h\to0$); then $\mathcal P(\delta,h(\delta))$ plus monotonicity gives $\mathcal P(\delta,\epsilon)$, i.e. $\mathbf x^\star$ is a local min of $g_\epsilon$. For the "if": I'm handed, for each small $\epsilon$, a radius $q(\epsilon)>0$ with $\mathcal P(q(\epsilon),\epsilon)$; I need to invert this into a single $h$. Set $h(\delta)=\inf\{\epsilon:\ \epsilon\le\tilde\epsilon_0,\ q(\epsilon)\ge\delta\}$. This is well-defined and monotone, and $h(\delta)\to0$ as $\delta\to0$ because for any target $\epsilon'$ there's $\delta'=q(\epsilon')$ with $h(\delta)\le\epsilon'$ once $\delta\le\delta'$; and by continuity of $f$, taking $\epsilon_i\downarrow h(\delta)$ with $q(\epsilon_i)\ge\delta$ gives $\mathcal P(\delta,\epsilon_i)$ for all $i$, hence $\mathcal P(\delta,h(\delta))$ in the limit. So the two forms coincide. The "for every small $\epsilon$" in this equivalent version is the same freedom as the arbitrary $h$ — it's what keeps the relative rate of the two neighborhoods unconstrained, and it's the static fingerprint of "the follower fully reacts before the leader moves."

(I should also note a wrong notion to steer clear of. Evtushenko in 1974 proposed calling $(\mathbf x^\star,\mathbf y^\star)$ "local" if there's *some* neighborhood $\mathcal W$ in which it's the global minimax of $f$ restricted to $\mathcal W$. That sounds local but isn't: whether the point qualifies can depend on values of $f$ far away — enlarge $\mathcal W$ and the verdict can flip, because the inner $\max$ over $\mathcal W$ now sees a higher peak elsewhere. So it won't satisfy any infinitesimal first/second-order test. My definition with $h(\delta)\to0$ is built precisely to be readable from an arbitrarily small neighborhood.)

Now the part I actually need for it to be usable: first- and second-order conditions, the analogue of $\nabla f=0$, $\nabla^2 f\succeq 0$. First order. $\mathbf y^\star$ is a local max of $f(\mathbf x^\star,\cdot)$, so $\nabla_{\mathbf y}f=0$ immediately. For $\nabla_{\mathbf x}f$, let $\boldsymbol\delta_y^\star(\boldsymbol\delta_x):=\arg\max_{\|\boldsymbol\delta_y\|\le h(\delta)}f(\mathbf x^\star+\boldsymbol\delta_x,\mathbf y^\star+\boldsymbol\delta_y)$, which has $\|\boldsymbol\delta_y^\star\|\le h(\delta)\to0$. Local minimaxity gives, for small $\boldsymbol\delta_x$,
$$0\le f(\mathbf x^\star+\boldsymbol\delta_x,\mathbf y^\star+\boldsymbol\delta_y^\star)-f(\mathbf x^\star,\mathbf y^\star).$$
Split the difference through the intermediate point $f(\mathbf x^\star,\mathbf y^\star+\boldsymbol\delta_y^\star)$: the piece $f(\mathbf x^\star,\mathbf y^\star+\boldsymbol\delta_y^\star)-f(\mathbf x^\star,\mathbf y^\star)\le0$ because $\mathbf y^\star$ is a local max of $f(\mathbf x^\star,\cdot)$, so it only helps the bound, leaving
$$0\le f(\mathbf x^\star+\boldsymbol\delta_x,\mathbf y^\star+\boldsymbol\delta_y^\star)-f(\mathbf x^\star,\mathbf y^\star+\boldsymbol\delta_y^\star)=\nabla_{\mathbf x}f(\mathbf x^\star,\mathbf y^\star+\boldsymbol\delta_y^\star)^\top\boldsymbol\delta_x+o(\|\boldsymbol\delta_x\|).$$
Since $\boldsymbol\delta_y^\star\to0$ as $\delta\to0$, $\nabla_{\mathbf x}f(\mathbf x^\star,\mathbf y^\star+\boldsymbol\delta_y^\star)\to\nabla_{\mathbf x}f(\mathbf x^\star,\mathbf y^\star)$, so the inequality $0\le\nabla_{\mathbf x}f^\top\boldsymbol\delta_x+o(\|\boldsymbol\delta_x\|)$ holds for every small $\boldsymbol\delta_x$, both signs — forcing $\nabla_{\mathbf x}f(\mathbf x^\star,\mathbf y^\star)=0$. So a local minimax point is stationary, $\nabla_{\mathbf x}f=\nabla_{\mathbf y}f=0$, same as local Nash.

The second-order condition is where the order finally shows up in the algebra, so let me grind it out carefully. Abbreviate $\mathbf A:=\nabla^2_{\mathbf x\mathbf x}f$, $\mathbf B:=\nabla^2_{\mathbf y\mathbf y}f$, $\mathbf C:=\nabla^2_{\mathbf x\mathbf y}f$ at $(\mathbf x^\star,\mathbf y^\star)$ (so $\mathbf C^\top=\nabla^2_{\mathbf y\mathbf x}f$). Stationarity kills the linear terms, so
$$f(\mathbf x^\star+\boldsymbol\delta_x,\mathbf y^\star+\boldsymbol\delta_y)-f(\mathbf x^\star,\mathbf y^\star)=\tfrac12\boldsymbol\delta_x^\top\mathbf A\boldsymbol\delta_x+\boldsymbol\delta_x^\top\mathbf C\boldsymbol\delta_y+\tfrac12\boldsymbol\delta_y^\top\mathbf B\boldsymbol\delta_y+o(\|\boldsymbol\delta\|^2).$$
$\mathbf y^\star$ being a local max of $f(\mathbf x^\star,\cdot)$ (set $\boldsymbol\delta_x=0$) gives $\mathbf B\preceq0$ right away — that's the first half of the necessary condition and it's not surprising. The interesting half is what the leader's local-min requirement does. Suppose $\mathbf B\prec0$ (strictly, so $\mathbf B$ is invertible). For a fixed small $\boldsymbol\delta_x$, the inner objective in $\boldsymbol\delta_y$ is $\boldsymbol\delta_x^\top\mathbf C\boldsymbol\delta_y+\tfrac12\boldsymbol\delta_y^\top\mathbf B\boldsymbol\delta_y$, a strictly concave quadratic in $\boldsymbol\delta_y$ (since $\mathbf B\prec0$), so its unconstrained maximizer is interior for a large enough inner radius and is found by setting the $\boldsymbol\delta_y$-gradient to zero:
$$\mathbf C^\top\boldsymbol\delta_x+\mathbf B\boldsymbol\delta_y=0\ \Longrightarrow\ \boldsymbol\delta_y^\star=-\mathbf B^{-1}\mathbf C^\top\boldsymbol\delta_x.$$
Its norm is $\le\|\mathbf B^{-1}\mathbf C^\top\|\,\|\boldsymbol\delta_x\|$, so it sits inside an inner radius $h'(\delta)=2\|\mathbf B^{-1}\mathbf C^\top\|\delta$ which also vanishes as $\delta\to0$. The definition gives the lower bound for the max over radius $h(\delta)$; enlarging the radius to $\max\{h(\delta),h'(\delta)\}$ can only increase that max, and the enlarged local problem has this interior maximizer. Plug it back. Let me do the substitution honestly:
$$\boldsymbol\delta_x^\top\mathbf C\boldsymbol\delta_y^\star+\tfrac12\boldsymbol\delta_y^{\star\top}\mathbf B\boldsymbol\delta_y^\star=\boldsymbol\delta_x^\top\mathbf C(-\mathbf B^{-1}\mathbf C^\top\boldsymbol\delta_x)+\tfrac12(-\mathbf B^{-1}\mathbf C^\top\boldsymbol\delta_x)^\top\mathbf B(-\mathbf B^{-1}\mathbf C^\top\boldsymbol\delta_x).$$
The first piece is $-\boldsymbol\delta_x^\top\mathbf C\mathbf B^{-1}\mathbf C^\top\boldsymbol\delta_x$. The second is $\tfrac12\boldsymbol\delta_x^\top\mathbf C\mathbf B^{-1}\mathbf B\mathbf B^{-1}\mathbf C^\top\boldsymbol\delta_x=\tfrac12\boldsymbol\delta_x^\top\mathbf C\mathbf B^{-1}\mathbf C^\top\boldsymbol\delta_x$ (using $\mathbf B^{-1}=\mathbf B^{-\top}$, $\mathbf B$ symmetric). Sum: $-\tfrac12\boldsymbol\delta_x^\top\mathbf C\mathbf B^{-1}\mathbf C^\top\boldsymbol\delta_x$. Add the $\tfrac12\boldsymbol\delta_x^\top\mathbf A\boldsymbol\delta_x$ term:
$$\max_{\boldsymbol\delta_y}\big[\cdots\big]=\tfrac12\boldsymbol\delta_x^\top\big(\mathbf A-\mathbf C\mathbf B^{-1}\mathbf C^\top\big)\boldsymbol\delta_x+o(\|\boldsymbol\delta_x\|^2).$$
And the local-minimax inequality says this is $\ge0$ for all small $\boldsymbol\delta_x$. Therefore the *Schur complement* of $\mathbf B$,
$$\mathbf A-\mathbf C\mathbf B^{-1}\mathbf C^\top\ =\ \nabla^2_{\mathbf x\mathbf x}f-\nabla^2_{\mathbf x\mathbf y}f\,(\nabla^2_{\mathbf y\mathbf y}f)^{-1}\nabla^2_{\mathbf y\mathbf x}f\ \succeq\ 0,$$
is the necessary second-order condition (given $\mathbf B\prec0$). And for sufficiency, run it forward: at a stationary point with $\mathbf B\prec0$, the same maximizer $-\mathbf B^{-1}\mathbf C^\top\boldsymbol\delta_x$ has norm $\le\|\mathbf B^{-1}\mathbf C^\top\|\|\boldsymbol\delta_x\|$, so it lies within $h(\delta)=\|\mathbf B^{-1}\mathbf C^\top\|\delta$ whenever $\|\boldsymbol\delta_x\|\le\delta$; evaluating the inner max at that maximizer gives $\max_{\|\boldsymbol\delta_y\|\le h(\delta)}f(\mathbf x^\star+\boldsymbol\delta_x,\cdot)-f(\mathbf x^\star,\mathbf y^\star)=\tfrac12\boldsymbol\delta_x^\top(\mathbf A-\mathbf C\mathbf B^{-1}\mathbf C^\top)\boldsymbol\delta_x+o(\|\boldsymbol\delta_x\|^2)$, and if $\mathbf A-\mathbf C\mathbf B^{-1}\mathbf C^\top\succ0$ this is $>0$ for small nonzero $\boldsymbol\delta_x$. So: a stationary point with $\mathbf B\prec0$ and $\mathbf A-\mathbf C\mathbf B^{-1}\mathbf C^\top\succ0$ is a (strict) local minimax point. When $\mathbf B$ is nondegenerate the necessary and sufficient conditions match up to the boundary $\succeq$ vs $\succ$, so the characterization is essentially tight.

Set this next to local Nash. Local Nash's sufficient condition was $\mathbf A\succ0$, $\mathbf B\prec0$. Local minimax keeps $\mathbf B\prec0$ but replaces "$\mathbf A\succ0$" by "$\mathbf A-\mathbf C\mathbf B^{-1}\mathbf C^\top\succ0$." Two things change. The cross term $\mathbf C=\nabla^2_{\mathbf x\mathbf y}f$ now enters — the interaction between the players is part of the optimality test, as it should be in a coupled game. And the condition is no longer symmetric in $\mathbf x$ and $\mathbf y$: the Schur complement is taken *of the follower block $\mathbf B$* and folded into the leader's condition; swapping the roles (a local *maximin*) would give $\mathbf B-\mathbf C^\top\mathbf A^{-1}\mathbf C\preceq0$ instead. The order of play now lives in the linear algebra. And since $-\mathbf C\mathbf B^{-1}\mathbf C^\top\succeq0$ whenever $\mathbf B\prec0$, the leader is *allowed negative curvature* in $\mathbf A$ as long as the follower's reaction term $-\mathbf C\mathbf B^{-1}\mathbf C^\top$ supplies enough convexity to compensate.

This is the place to come back to $-x^2+5xy-y^2$ and see if the Hessian test agrees with the brute-force grid. There $\mathbf A=-2$, $\mathbf B=-2$, $\mathbf C=5$, so the Schur complement is $-2-25/(-2)=-2+12.5=10.5$. So $\mathbf B=-2\prec0$ and Schur $=10.5\succ0$ — the sufficient condition fires, and it declares the origin a strict local minimax. That matches the grid, which found the leader inequality holding strictly. The two routes — definition-level grid and second-order algebra — land on the same verdict on the same point, which is the consistency check I wanted before believing the Schur condition. And the number makes the mechanism quantitative: the follower contributes $+12.5$ of effective convexity, more than enough to bury the leader's own $-2$.

Before I move on I should record that this notion, unlike local minima, can be *absent*, and even worse, the global object it surrogates can fail to be an instance of it. The absence: $f(x,y)=y^2-2xy$ on $[-1,1]^2$. A local minimax needs $y^\star$ a local max of $f(x^\star,\cdot)=y^2-2x^\star y$, but that's convex in $y$, so its only local maxima on the box are at the corners $y=\pm1$. Take the corner $y^\star=1$ and any $x^\star$, say $x^\star=0.3$, and test the leader condition there. With $\epsilon=0.05$, $g_\epsilon(x)=\max_{|y-1|\le\epsilon}f(x,y)$, and since $f(x,\cdot)$ is convex the inner max is at an endpoint $y\in\{1-\epsilon,1\}$. Computing: $g(0.3)=f(0.3,1)=0.4$, but moving the leader to $x=0.35$ gives $g=0.3$, strictly *below* $g(0.3)$ — so $x^\star=0.3$ is not a local min of $g_\epsilon$, and the leader inequality fails. The same one-sided slope kills every corner choice (the leader can always slide the cross term $-2x^\star y$ downward), so local minimax doesn't exist. So I should not claim universal existence; I should instead find regularity under which it's guaranteed. The mismatch with the global object is sharper and worth dwelling on. In plain minimization a global minimum is always a local minimum. Here a *global* minimax point can be neither local minimax nor even stationary. Back to $f(x,y)=0.2xy-\cos y$ on $[-1,1]\times[-2\pi,2\pi]$. Gradient $(0.2y,\,0.2x+\sin y)$. For each $x$ the follower's two maxima solve $0.2x+\sin y^\star=0$ with $y_1^\star(x)\in(-\tfrac{3\pi}2,-\tfrac\pi2)$ and $y_2^\star(x)\in(\tfrac\pi2,\tfrac{3\pi}2)$; the value along the lower branch decreases in $x$, along the upper branch increases, and by symmetry they're equal at $x=0$ where $y_1^\star=-\pi$, $y_2^\star=\pi$. So $\phi(x)=\max_y f$ is minimized at $x=0$ and the global minimax points are $(0,-\pi)$ and $(0,\pi)$. But at $(0,\pm\pi)$ the gradient is $(0.2\cdot(\pm\pi),\,0)=(\pm0.2\pi,0)\neq0$ — *not stationary*, hence by the first-order condition *not local minimax*. The reason is structural: the global object minimizes a *global* inner max while local minimax minimizes a *local* inner max, and those decouple. This is the cleanest statement of why minimax is harder than minimization and why it needs its own theory. (I can recover the nice "global $\Rightarrow$ local" behavior only under extra regularity: if for every $\mathbf x$ the function $f(\mathbf x,\cdot)$ is strongly concave near its maxima and all its local maxima are global — a property that's been established for several matrix-factorization landscapes — then the implicit best response $\boldsymbol\delta_y^\star(\boldsymbol\delta_x)=-\mathbf B^{-1}\mathbf C^\top\boldsymbol\delta_x+o(\|\boldsymbol\delta_x\|)$ is well-defined and the global min of $\phi$ is also a local minimax. Then local minimax points exist.)

Now the payoff I actually care about: does this notion describe what GDA does? This is where local Nash failed loudly — Daskalakis–Panageas proved the strict containment $\textsf{local-Nash}\subsetneq\textsf{GDA-stable}$, meaning GDA stably parks at points that are not local Nash, and nobody knew what those points were. Let me see whether local minimax fills that gap. GDA with a step-size ratio: descend $\mathbf x$ with step $\eta/\gamma$, ascend $\mathbf y$ with step $\eta$, so the leader moves $\gamma$ times slower than the follower. As $\eta\to0$ this is the flow $\dot{\mathbf x}=-\tfrac1\gamma\nabla_{\mathbf x}f$, $\dot{\mathbf y}=\nabla_{\mathbf y}f$. Linearize at a stationary point: $\tfrac{d}{dt}\boldsymbol\delta_x=-\tfrac1\gamma(\mathbf A\boldsymbol\delta_x+\mathbf C\boldsymbol\delta_y)$, $\tfrac{d}{dt}\boldsymbol\delta_y=\mathbf C^\top\boldsymbol\delta_x+\mathbf B\boldsymbol\delta_y$, so the Jacobian is
$$\mathbf J_\gamma=\begin{pmatrix}-\tfrac1\gamma\mathbf A & -\tfrac1\gamma\mathbf C\\[2pt]\mathbf C^\top & \mathbf B\end{pmatrix}.$$
A fixed point is (strictly) linearly stable iff every eigenvalue has negative real part. Note this Jacobian is not symmetric — the off-diagonal blocks $-\tfrac1\gamma\mathbf C$ and $\mathbf C^\top$ have *opposite* signs — so its eigenvalues are generally complex and the linearized flow rotates. That rotation is why GDA spirals and needs tiny steps, and it makes me doubt that a *fixed* $\gamma$ can match my static notion. Before building any general theory, let me test that doubt on quadratics where I can read off $\mathbf A,\mathbf B,\mathbf C$ and compute the eigenvalues by hand.

Take $f=-x^2+2\sqrt\epsilon\,xy-(\epsilon/2)y^2$ with $\epsilon=1/\gamma$, so $\mathbf A=-2$, $\mathbf B=-\epsilon$, $\mathbf C=2\sqrt\epsilon$. My static test: $\mathbf B=-\epsilon\prec0$ and Schur $=-2-(2\sqrt\epsilon)^2/(-\epsilon)=-2+4=2\succ0$, so this *is* a strict local minimax. Now its GDA Jacobian at $\gamma=1/\epsilon$ is $\mathbf J=\begin{pmatrix}-\epsilon\mathbf A & -\epsilon\mathbf C\\ \mathbf C & \mathbf B\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}2\epsilon & -2\epsilon^{3/2}\\ 2\sqrt\epsilon & -\epsilon\end{pmatrix}$. Trace $=2\epsilon-\epsilon=\epsilon$, determinant $=2\epsilon\cdot(-\epsilon)-(-2\epsilon^{3/2})(2\sqrt\epsilon)=-2\epsilon^2+4\epsilon^2=2\epsilon^2$. The eigenvalues solve $\lambda^2-\epsilon\lambda+2\epsilon^2=0$, i.e. $\lambda=\tfrac{\epsilon\pm\sqrt{\epsilon^2-8\epsilon^2}}2=\tfrac\epsilon2(1\pm i\sqrt7)$. Their real part is $\epsilon/2>0$ — *positive*. So this point is a strict local minimax that GDA at $\gamma=1/\epsilon$ pushes *away* from. I plugged in $\epsilon=0.3$ to be sure I hadn't slipped a sign: the $2\times2$ Jacobian has eigenvalues $0.15\pm0.397i$, real part $+0.15$, unstable. One direction of the fixed-$\gamma$ correspondence is dead.

Now the other failure. Take $f=x_1^2+2\sqrt\epsilon\,x_1y_1+(\epsilon/2)y_1^2-x_2^2/2+2\sqrt\epsilon\,x_2y_2-\epsilon y_2^2$, a block-diagonal pair. Here $\mathbf B=\mathrm{diag}(\epsilon,-2\epsilon)$ — *indefinite*, so the point is neither local minimax (needs $\mathbf B\prec0$) nor local maximin (needs $\mathbf B\succ0$); it's not optimal under either order. But does GDA know that? Each $2\times2$ block has the same structure as before up to signs; computing the spectrum of the $4\times4$ $\mathbf J_\gamma$ at $\epsilon=0.3$ gives the doubled eigenvalue $-0.15\pm0.397i=\tfrac\epsilon2(-1\pm i\sqrt7)$, real part $-0.15<0$ — strictly *stable*. So GDA happily converges to a point that is optimal under neither leader/follower order. Both failure modes are real, computed, not imagined, so a fixed $\gamma$ genuinely cannot equal my notion in either direction. The mismatch has to be resolved by *which* $\gamma$.

So push $\gamma\to\infty$, i.e. $\epsilon=1/\gamma\to0$ — the leader frozen relative to the follower. Now I want the eigenvalues of $\mathbf J_\gamma$, equivalently of
$$\mathbf J_\epsilon=\begin{pmatrix}-\epsilon\mathbf A & -\epsilon\mathbf C\\ \mathbf C^\top & \mathbf B\end{pmatrix},$$
as $\epsilon\to0$. Two scales should appear: the follower block $\mathbf B$ is $O(1)$, the leader block is $O(\epsilon)$. Let me extract them from the characteristic polynomial $p_\epsilon(\lambda)=\det\begin{pmatrix}\lambda\mathbf I+\epsilon\mathbf A & \epsilon\mathbf C\\ -\mathbf C^\top & \lambda\mathbf I-\mathbf B\end{pmatrix}$. At $\epsilon=0$ this is $p_0(\lambda)=\lambda^{d_1}\det(\lambda\mathbf I-\mathbf B)$, whose roots are $0$ (multiplicity $d_1$) and the eigenvalues $\nu_i$ of $\mathbf B$. By continuity of polynomial roots in the coefficients (Zedek's lemma), for small $\epsilon$, $d_2$ roots stay near the $\nu_i$ (these are the $O(1)$, follower-scale eigenvalues) and $d_1$ roots stay near $0$ (the leader-scale ones). The $\nu_i$-cluster controls one half: stability there needs $\Re(\nu_i)<0$ for all $i$, i.e. $\mathbf B\prec0$. The cluster near $0$ needs a finer look, because "near 0" doesn't tell me the sign. Rescale $\lambda=\epsilon\theta$ and divide by $\epsilon^{d_1}$:
$$q_\epsilon(\theta):= p_\epsilon(\epsilon\theta)/\epsilon^{d_1}=\det\begin{pmatrix}\theta\mathbf I+\mathbf A & \mathbf C\\ -\mathbf C^\top & \epsilon\theta\mathbf I-\mathbf B\end{pmatrix}.$$
Send $\epsilon\to0$: $q_0(\theta)=\det\begin{pmatrix}\theta\mathbf I+\mathbf A & \mathbf C\\ -\mathbf C^\top & -\mathbf B\end{pmatrix}$. Block-triangularize using $\mathbf B$ invertible — factor out the bottom-right block via the Schur complement:
$$\begin{pmatrix}\theta\mathbf I+\mathbf A & \mathbf C\\ -\mathbf C^\top & -\mathbf B\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}\theta\mathbf I+\mathbf A-\mathbf C\mathbf B^{-1}\mathbf C^\top & \mathbf C\\ \mathbf 0 & -\mathbf B\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}\mathbf I & \mathbf 0\\ \mathbf B^{-1}\mathbf C^\top & \mathbf I\end{pmatrix},$$
which I can verify block-by-block: bottom-left of the product is $-\mathbf B\cdot\mathbf B^{-1}\mathbf C^\top=-\mathbf C^\top$ ✓, top-left is $(\theta\mathbf I+\mathbf A-\mathbf C\mathbf B^{-1}\mathbf C^\top)+\mathbf C\mathbf B^{-1}\mathbf C^\top=\theta\mathbf I+\mathbf A$ ✓. Taking determinants, $q_0(\theta)=(-1)^{d_2}\det(\mathbf B)\det\big(\theta\mathbf I+\mathbf A-\mathbf C\mathbf B^{-1}\mathbf C^\top\big)$. So the $d_1$ rescaled roots are exactly the eigenvalues $\mu_i$ of $-(\mathbf A-\mathbf C\mathbf B^{-1}\mathbf C^\top)$, meaning the small eigenvalues of $\mathbf J_\epsilon$ are $\lambda_i\approx-\epsilon\,\mathrm{eig}_i(\mathbf A-\mathbf C\mathbf B^{-1}\mathbf C^\top)$. Their real parts are negative iff the Schur complement $\mathbf A-\mathbf C\mathbf B^{-1}\mathbf C^\top$ is positive definite.

For large $\gamma$, then, $(\mathbf x^\star,\mathbf y^\star)$ is a strict linearly stable point of $\gamma$-GDA iff $\mathbf B\prec0$ (from the $\nu_i$ cluster) and $\mathbf A-\mathbf C\mathbf B^{-1}\mathbf C^\top\succ0$ (from the rescaled $\mu_i$ cluster). Those are the same two inequalities I derived as the strict local minimax conditions from the definition — the asymptotics has reproduced them.

This is a derivation through two limits and a root-perturbation argument, so I want to watch the split actually happen on a number before I rely on it. Pick a random nondegenerate case, $\mathbf A=\bigl(\begin{smallmatrix}1.5&0.3\\0.3&2\end{smallmatrix}\bigr)$, $\mathbf B=\bigl(\begin{smallmatrix}-1&0.2\\0.2&-3\end{smallmatrix}\bigr)\prec0$, $\mathbf C=\bigl(\begin{smallmatrix}0.7&-0.4\\0.1&0.5\end{smallmatrix}\bigr)$. Here $\mathrm{eig}(\mathbf B)=\{-0.98,-3.02\}$ and $\mathrm{eig}(\mathbf A-\mathbf C\mathbf B^{-1}\mathbf C^\top)=\{1.73,2.38\}$ (so both conditions hold and the point *should* become stable). Forming $\mathbf J_\gamma$ and diagonalizing: at $\gamma=100$ the four eigenvalues are $\{-3.018,-0.975,-0.0239,-0.0173\}$, and at $\gamma=10^4$ they are $\{-3.0198,-0.9802,-2.38\!\times\!10^{-4},-1.73\!\times\!10^{-4}\}$. The two $O(1)$ roots are converging onto $\mathrm{eig}(\mathbf B)$ and the two small roots onto $-\tfrac1\gamma\,\mathrm{eig}(\mathrm{Schur})$ — at $\gamma=10^4$, $-10^{-4}\cdot\{2.38,1.73\}$ to the digit. The predicted two-scale split is exactly what the spectrum does, and all four real parts are negative, so the point is stable as the conditions promised. So in the $\gamma\to\infty$ limit, the stable fixed points of GDA *are* the strict local minimax points, except for points on the follower-Hessian degeneracy boundary where this Schur-complement split cannot be applied. Stating the set inclusions carefully with the two set-theoretic limits $\underline{\infty\text{-GDA}}=\liminf_\gamma$ and $\overline{\infty\text{-GDA}}=\limsup_\gamma$ of the stable sets:
$$\textsf{local-minimax}\ \subset\ \underline{\infty\text{-GDA}}\ \subset\ \overline{\infty\text{-GDA}}\ \subset\ \textsf{local-minimax}\ \cup\ \{\text{stationary points where }\nabla^2_{\mathbf y\mathbf y}f\text{ is degenerate}\}.$$
The left inclusion is the sufficiency direction: a strict local minimax has $\mathbf B\prec0$, Schur $\succ0$, so by the eigenvalue split it's stable for all large enough $\gamma$. The right inclusion is the necessity direction. If a point is stable for arbitrarily large $\gamma$ and $\mathbf B$ is invertible, the $O(1)$ eigenvalue cluster forces $\mathbf B\prec0$, and the slow cluster first gives Schur $\succeq0$. If the Schur complement had a zero eigenvalue with unit vector $\mathbf w$, then a direct check would give $\mathbf J_\epsilon\,(\mathbf w,\,-\mathbf B^{-1}\mathbf C^\top\mathbf w)^\top=\mathbf 0$ — bottom block $\mathbf C^\top\mathbf w+\mathbf B(-\mathbf B^{-1}\mathbf C^\top\mathbf w)=0$, top block $-\epsilon(\mathbf A\mathbf w-\mathbf C\mathbf B^{-1}\mathbf C^\top\mathbf w)=-\epsilon(\mathbf A-\mathbf C\mathbf B^{-1}\mathbf C^\top)\mathbf w=0$ — so $\mathbf J_\epsilon$ would have a zero eigenvalue and the point would not be strictly stable. Thus invertible $\mathbf B$ upgrades Schur $\succeq0$ to Schur $\succ0$, and the point is strict local minimax. The leftover points are exactly the ones where $\mathbf B$ (the follower Hessian) is singular and the Schur analysis cannot resolve the sign. Up to that degenerate follower-Hessian set, $\infty$-GDA $=$ local minimax, so every nondegenerate stable limit point of GDA-with-large-timescale-separation is locally optimal in the sequential, leader/follower sense. Recall the gap I started from: local Nash was *strictly contained* in GDA-stable, leaving a band of stable points with no characterization. The band has a name now — its nondegenerate members are precisely the local minimax points that are not local Nash, the $-x^2+5xy-y^2$-type points where the cross term carries the optimality. The earlier counterexamples bracket this from both sides: at finite $\gamma$ a local minimax point could be unstable and a non-optimal point stable, but as $\gamma\to\infty$ the two sets close onto each other up to the degenerate boundary.

This also tells me *why* the timescale separation $\gamma\to\infty$ is the right knob and not an arbitrary trick. The static definition demanded the follower's local max be solved *for every small inner radius before the leader's perturbation matters* — the "for all small $\epsilon$" / arbitrary-$h$ clause. Dynamically that is precisely the leader moving infinitely slower than the follower: with $\gamma$ huge, in the time the leader drifts by $\boldsymbol\delta_x$ the follower has already climbed to the local max of $f(\mathbf x+\boldsymbol\delta_x,\cdot)$. The follower's fast subsystem solves the inner problem; the leader's slow subsystem then descends the implicit worst-case $\phi$. The two-scale eigenvalue split I just computed is the linear-algebra shadow of that two-timescale picture. So the definition and the algorithm were always the same idea seen from two sides, and pushing $\gamma\to\infty$ is what makes them coincide.

Let me also nail down the one regime where the inner problem is genuinely easy, because it sharpens the picture. Suppose I have a max-oracle: for any $\mathbf x$ I can get $\hat{\mathbf y}$ with $f(\mathbf x,\hat{\mathbf y})\ge\max_{\mathbf y}f(\mathbf x,\mathbf y)-\epsilon$. Then $\min_{\mathbf x}\max_{\mathbf y}f$ collapses to minimizing $\phi(\mathbf x)=\max_{\mathbf y}f(\mathbf x,\mathbf y)$, and I can alternate: maximize $\mathbf y$ approximately, then a gradient step on $\mathbf x$ using $\nabla_{\mathbf x}f(\mathbf x,\hat{\mathbf y})$. The wrinkle is that $\phi$ need not be differentiable even when $f$ is smooth — the max of smooth functions has kinks — so "approximately stationary" needs care; the subgradient norm is a discontinuous, unreliable measure. But $\phi$ has structure: if $f$ is $\ell$-gradient-Lipschitz then $\phi(\mathbf x)+\tfrac\ell2\|\mathbf x\|^2$ is convex, i.e. $\phi$ is $\ell$-*weakly convex*. For weakly convex functions the right stationarity gauge is the gradient of the Moreau envelope $\phi_\lambda(\mathbf x)=\min_{\mathbf x'}\phi(\mathbf x')+\tfrac1{2\lambda}\|\mathbf x-\mathbf x'\|^2$ with $\lambda<1/\ell$: small $\|\nabla\phi_\lambda(\mathbf x)\|$ certifies that $\mathbf x$ is near a point $\hat{\mathbf x}$ that is genuinely near-stationary for $\phi$. Running the subgradient-style analysis with the oracle's $\epsilon$ error carried through gives $\mathbb E\|\nabla\phi_{1/(2\ell)}(\bar{\mathbf x})\|^2\le 2\frac{(\phi_{1/(2\ell)}(\mathbf x_0)-\min\phi)+\ell L^2\gamma^2}{\gamma\sqrt{T+1}}+4\ell\epsilon$ — a $1/\sqrt T$ rate to a $4\ell\epsilon$ neighborhood. So when the inner max is tractable, the whole minimax problem is just nonsmooth weakly-convex minimization of $\phi$, and convergence is clean; it's the inseparable, GDA-coupled case that needed the local-minimax theory.

One last loose end, to be honest about the boundaries of the notion: all of this is pure-strategy. If I instead let the players randomize — strategies are distributions $\mu,\nu$ and the payoff is $\mathbb E_{\mathbf x\sim\mu,\mathbf y\sim\nu}f$ — then even for nonconvex–nonconcave $f$ the minimax theorem is *restored* (Glicksberg's continuous-game extension of the Kakutani fixed-point argument), so $\min_\mu\max_\nu=\max_\nu\min_\mu$, the order stops mattering, and mixed Nash equilibria always exist. Mixed strategy lives in an infinite-dimensional space of measures, but one side of an approximate mixed Nash can be reduced to a finite-dimensional *pure* global minimax: replace $\mu$ by an empirical distribution over $N$ samples and solve $\min_{\mathbf x_1,\dots,\mathbf x_N}\max_{\mathbf y}\frac1N\sum_i f(\mathbf x_i,\mathbf y)$; for $L$-Lipschitz $f$ on a diameter-$D$ domain, $N\gtrsim d_2(LD/\epsilon)^2\log(LD/\epsilon)$ suffices, by Hoeffding over the $N$ samples plus a union bound over an $\epsilon/(2L)$-cover of $\mathcal Y$. Solving the symmetric maximin construction gives the empirical distribution for the other player, and together they form an approximate mixed Nash. So randomization is the escape hatch when it's allowed; but in GANs and adversarial training we commit to *single* networks, which is the pure-strategy, order-sensitive world where local minimax is the right object.

To recap the causal chain: GDA solves $\min\max$ but I couldn't say what point it finds; local Nash is the wrong target because it's a simultaneous-game, order-blind notion that can fail to exist and that GDA provably overshoots; the applications are sequential (Stackelberg), so the correct global object is the leader minimizing the follower's worst-case value $\phi=\max_{\mathbf y}f$; localizing *that* — with the follower's neighborhood allowed to shrink at its own rate $h(\delta)\to0$ — gives local minimax; first-order it's just stationarity, but second-order the leader's condition becomes the Schur complement $\nabla^2_{\mathbf x\mathbf x}f-\nabla^2_{\mathbf x\mathbf y}f(\nabla^2_{\mathbf y\mathbf y}f)^{-1}\nabla^2_{\mathbf y\mathbf x}f\succeq0$ (with $\nabla^2_{\mathbf y\mathbf y}f\prec0$), which finally puts the cross term and the player order into the algebra; and the same Schur complement reappears as the slow-mode eigenvalues of the timescale-separated GDA Jacobian, so as $\gamma\to\infty$ the stable fixed points of GDA are exactly the strict local minimax points up to degenerate $\nabla^2_{\mathbf y\mathbf y}f$ — giving every nondegenerate stable limit point of GDA, at last, a meaning.
