Let me start from what actually goes wrong when I train these graph networks. I have a message-passing GNN: each node carries a feature vector, and at each layer it updates that vector by combining its own vector with some aggregate of its neighbors' vectors — h_v^(k) = COMBINE(h_v^(k-1), AGGREGATE({h_u^(k-1): u in N(v)})) — and after K rounds I collapse all the node vectors into one graph vector with a readout, h_G = READOUT({h_v^(K)}), and classify that. People have written down dozens of these: GCN averages the neighbors, GraphSAGE takes a max, others use attention or an LSTM, and the readouts range from a plain sum to sorted pooling to hierarchical clustering. They all roughly work, and they were all arrived at by intuition and trying things. What bothers me is that I have no idea *which* of these choices actually decides what the network can represent. When my GNN can't fit the training set on a graph dataset where the structure is the whole signal — and that happens, models genuinely underfit — is that an optimization problem or is the network simply incapable of telling the two graphs apart no matter how I train it? I can't answer that, and until I can, every architecture decision is a guess.

So let me make the question precise. Forget generalization for a moment; ask the rawest thing: which non-isomorphic graphs can an aggregation-based GNN map to *different* embeddings, and which pairs is it provably forced to collapse together? If I had that, "underfitting" would split cleanly into "can't represent" versus "can't optimize," and I'd know exactly what to fix. The ability to give every pair of non-isomorphic graphs distinct embeddings is, stated baldly, the graph isomorphism problem, which has no known polynomial algorithm — so I shouldn't expect to *solve* it. I want a sharp characterization, not a miracle. Is there a known, computable proxy for "as discriminative as you could reasonably hope to be" that I can compare my GNN against?

There is, and it's been staring at me: the Weisfeiler-Lehman test of graph isomorphism. Its one-dimensional form, naive vertex refinement, works like this — start with each node's label, then repeatedly relabel every node by hashing the pair (its own current label, the *collection* of its neighbors' current labels) into a brand-new label, and declare two graphs non-isomorphic the first iteration their overall label collections differ. The structural echo with my GNN is impossible to miss: WL aggregates neighbor labels and updates; my GNN aggregates neighbor vectors and updates. Same loop. And WL is *powerful* — it separates a very broad class of graphs, failing only on some highly regular families. So WL is exactly the proxy I want: a computable yardstick for graph-discriminating power, structurally the same shape as my network. If I can relate my GNN's power to WL's, I'll have my characterization.

Now, the one thing that makes WL as strong as it is. Look at the relabel step: it applies a *hash that never collides on distinct inputs* — two nodes whose (own label, neighbor-label collection) differ always receive different new labels. That injectivity is the whole engine. If the hash ever merged two genuinely different neighborhoods into one label, that distinction would be gone forever and could never be recovered downstream; WL would lose discriminating power exactly there. So whatever my AGGREGATE and COMBINE turn out to be, they will have to mimic that property.

But first, the ceiling. Can an aggregation-based GNN ever *beat* WL — separate two graphs WL can't? Let me try to prove it can't and see where the argument forces my hand. Suppose, for contradiction, my GNN A gives two graphs G1 and G2 different embeddings, but WL never distinguishes them — at every iteration from 0 to k, G1 and G2 have the identical collection of WL node labels, and so also the identical collection of node-with-neighborhood pairs (l_v, {l_u: u in N(v)}); if those pairs ever differed, the injective WL hash would have produced different labels at the next iteration, contradicting that WL never separates them. Now I'll show that *within a single graph*, whenever two nodes share a WL label they also share a GNN vector — meaning the GNN is never finer than WL on this graph. Induct on the iteration. At i=0 it's trivial: WL and the GNN both start from the same node features, so equal labels mean equal vectors. Suppose it holds at iteration j. If two nodes u, v get the same WL label at j+1, then by WL's injective hash their pairs must have matched at j: (l_v^(j), {l_w^(j): w in N(v)}) = (l_u^(j), {l_w^(j): w in N(u)}). By the inductive hypothesis I can swap every WL label for the corresponding GNN vector, so (h_v^(j), {h_w^(j): w in N(v)}) = (h_u^(j), {h_w^(j): w in N(u)}). The GNN applies the *same* AGGREGATE and COMBINE to every node, so identical inputs produce identical outputs — h_v^(j+1) = h_u^(j+1). The induction closes: at every iteration, "same WL label" implies "same GNN vector," i.e. there's a well-defined map phi with h_v^(i) = phi(l_v^(i)). Apply it across the two graphs: since G1 and G2 share their collection of (WL-label, neighborhood) data at iteration k, they share their collection of (GNN-vector, neighborhood) data, hence the same multiset of node vectors {h_v^(k)}. The readout is permutation-invariant over that multiset, so A(G1) = A(G2). That contradicts my assumption that A separated them. So no aggregation-based GNN can ever out-discriminate WL: WL is a hard upper bound on this entire family. Good — now I know the best I can hope for, and I know the proof leaned on exactly two things: the GNN applying the same functions everywhere, and the readout being permutation-invariant.

So the live question flips: can I *reach* the WL ceiling? When is my GNN as powerful as WL, not just no more powerful? Let me reread my own proof, because it almost certainly contains the recipe. The only place the GNN could have "lost" relative to WL is where WL used injectivity and my GNN might not. WL's injective hash guarantees distinct (own label, neighbor multiset) get distinct new labels. My GNN's per-step map is the composition of AGGREGATE over the neighbor multiset and COMBINE with the node's own vector. If *that composition is injective* — distinct (own vector, neighbor multiset) always producing distinct outputs — then I can run the contradiction argument the other way. Suppose WL declares G1, G2 non-isomorphic at iteration K, so the WL label multisets differ. I claim, by induction, there's an *injective* map varphi with h_v^(k) = varphi(l_v^(k)) at every k. At k=0, varphi is the identity (same starting features). Assume it at k-1. Substitute h_v^(k-1) = varphi(l_v^(k-1)) into the GNN update; since varphi is injective and AGGREGATE/COMBINE are injective, and a composition of injective maps is injective, the GNN output is some injective function psi of (l_v^(k-1), {l_u^(k-1): u in N(v)}). But WL's own update is l_v^(k) = g(l_v^(k-1), {l_u^(k-1)}) for an injective, hence invertible-on-its-range, hash g. So h_v^(k) = psi(g^{-1}(l_v^(k))) = (psi ∘ g^{-1})(l_v^(k)), and psi ∘ g^{-1} is injective as a composition of injective maps — that's my varphi at level k. Induction closes. Then at iteration K the WL label multisets differ across G1, G2, and applying the injective varphi node-wise keeps them differing, so the GNN's node-vector multisets differ. And now the readout has to finish the job: if the graph-level readout is itself injective on the multiset of node vectors, it maps those two differing multisets to two different graph vectors, so A(G1) ≠ A(G2). The GNN matches WL.

So the conditions to hit the ceiling crystallize, and they're clean: the per-step neighbor aggregation must be injective on multisets, the per-step update must be injective, and the **readout must be injective on the multiset of node vectors**. Three injective bottlenecks in series. And notice the readout is not a tack-on at the end — it's the last of the three, and if it collapses two distinct node-vector multisets, all the injectivity I bought in the layers is wasted at the finish line. That reframes the whole readout-design problem for me: the readout's job is to be an injective function of a multiset of vectors. Whatever clever pooling I might dream up, if it isn't injective on that multiset it is throwing away representational power the layers already secured.

There's a subtlety I should kill before it bites me: "injective" only makes sense if the space of things being mapped is well-behaved. The WL argument talks about labels from a discrete alphabet, but my GNN vectors live in R^n. Are there even countably many distinct node vectors to be injective about? Start from countable input features — node labels really are from a discrete, countable set, and for finite graphs that's fine. Does countability survive the layers? I need: the range of a layer, given inputs from a countable set and over multisets of bounded size, is again countable, so it can feed the next layer. The cleanest way to see it: finite Cartesian products of countable sets are countable. It suffices to show N×N is countable, and induction handles N^k; an explicit bijection is (m,n) -> 2^(m-1)(2n-1), which hits every positive integer exactly once because every positive integer factors uniquely as a power of two times an odd number. Now take a multiset of bounded size over a countable set X: enumerate X by Z: X -> N, sort the multiset's elements by Z, and pad with a dummy out to the common bound. That gives an injection from bounded multisets into (X ∪ {dummy})^N, a finite product of countable sets, hence the multiset domain is countable. The image of a countable domain under any layer function is countable too: enumerate the domain, apply the function, and delete repeats in the output list. Countability propagates across all layers. So I'm entitled to talk about injective functions at every depth. Good — bookkeeping done, back to the real work.

Now I have to actually *build* an injective aggregator over multisets. What's the canonical way to make a permutation-invariant function of a set? The sum-decomposition result: for inputs from a countable universe, any permutation-invariant set function factors as rho(sum_{x in X} phi(x)) — an element-wise map phi, a sum, then an outer map rho. That's promising, but I have a *multiset*, not a set, and that distinction is exactly where the popular aggregators die. So let me ask the sharp version: does there exist an element map f such that h(X) = sum_{x in X} f(x) is injective *over multisets* of bounded size? Let me try to construct one and either succeed or learn why I can't. Enumerate the countable universe by Z: X -> N. Multisets are bounded, say strictly below N in size. Try f(x) = N^{-Z(x)}. Then sum_{x in X} f(x) is a number whose base-N expansion has, in the Z(x)-th digit place, the *count* of how many copies of x are in X — and since every count is below N, there are no carries, so different multisets give different digit patterns give different sums. It's an injective N-ary place-value code for the multiset. So yes: sum aggregation, with the right element map, is injective on multisets. And because it's injective, for *any* multiset function g I want, I can write g(X) = phi(sum f(x)) by simply defining phi on the (distinct) values of the injective sum to output g(X). Sum doesn't just preserve information — it's a universal multiset representation.

This immediately tells me why the popular aggregators are doomed, and each failure falls straight out of the same multiset arithmetic. Take mean. Compare a multiset X1 = (S, m) — underlying set S with multiplicities m — against X2 = (S, k·m), the same elements each repeated k times. Then |X2| = k|X1| and sum over X2 = k·(sum over X1), so the mean over X2 = (1/(k|X1|))·k·sum_{X1} f = (1/|X1|)·sum_{X1} f = mean over X1. The two multisets land on the *same* vector. Mean captures only the *distribution* of element types, never the multiplicities. The most brutal special case: a graph where every node has the identical feature a. Then f(a) is one fixed vector, the mean of any number of copies of f(a) is just f(a), and by induction every node ends up with the same vector at every layer — mean cannot see degree, cannot see structure at all on an unlabeled graph. Now max. If X1 and X2 share the same underlying set S, then max_{x in X1} f(x) = max_{x in S} f(x) = max_{x in X2} f(x): max collapses a multiset all the way down to its *set*, losing multiplicity entirely. A neighborhood {green, red} is indistinguishable from {green, red, red}. So the ranking falls out as a theorem, not a hunch: sum keeps the full multiset, mean keeps only the proportions, max keeps only the underlying set. (And to confirm max captures *exactly* the set: take f(x) to be the indicator one-hot of Z(x); then the coordinate-wise max is 1 in exactly the coordinates of elements present, which is the one-hot of the underlying set — injective on sets, nothing more.) Sum strictly dominates, and for a maximally powerful GNN I have no choice: it must be sum.

One more aggregator failure I should derive, because it's about the *update* network, not the pooling. Lots of GNNs use a one-layer perceptron, sigma(Wx), instead of a real MLP. Is that enough to realize my injective f? Let me find a counterexample. Take the multisets X1 = {1,1,1,1,1} and X2 = {2,3} — different multisets, same element-sum (5). For any linear W, since all elements are positive, at each output coordinate Wx has a fixed sign across all the elements, so ReLU either kills that coordinate for all of them (then both sums are zero there) or passes it linearly for all of them. Where it passes linearly, sum_{x in X} ReLU(Wx) = ReLU(W·sum_{x in X} x) by homogeneity and linearity of ReLU on a fixed sign. Since sum_{X1} x = sum_{X2} x = 5, the two multisets get the *same* aggregate. The one-layer perceptron behaves like a linear map and folds distinct multisets with equal sums together; it is not a universal approximator of multiset functions. So I need an MLP to model f (and the outer map), and the universal approximation theorem says an MLP can — two layers with a nonlinearity suffice to approximate the continuous maps I need. One-layer won't do.

So far my injective per-step map is "apply f to each neighbor, sum, then apply some outer map." But Theorem's condition was about (own vector, neighbor multiset) jointly — I have to keep the node's own contribution distinguishable from its neighbors', not just aggregate the neighbors. If I naively threw the center node into the same multiset as its neighbors and summed, I'd conflate "I am an a with neighbors {b,b}" against "I am a b with neighbors {a,b}" in cases where the merged multiset {a,b,b} matches. The center must be weighted *separately*. The cleanest injective combiner over the pair (c, X) — center c, neighbor multiset X — that I can think of is h(c, X) = (1+epsilon)·f(c) + sum_{x in X} f(x): give the center an adjustable weight relative to the unit-weight neighbors. Is this injective over (c, X)? Suppose two pairs collide, (c, X) and (c', X') with the same value. Split into cases. Case one, c' = c: then the collision forces sum_{x in X} f(x) = sum_{x in X'} f(x), which by the place-value f I built means X = X' — so the pairs were equal after all, no genuine collision. Case two, c' ≠ c: rearrange (1+epsilon)f(c) + sum_X f = (1+epsilon)f(c') + sum_{X'} f into epsilon·(f(c) - f(c')) = (f(c') + sum_{X'} f) - (f(c) + sum_X f). With f(x) = N^{-Z(x)}, every f value is rational, f(c) - f(c') is a *nonzero* rational, so the left side is epsilon times a nonzero rational, while the right side is a finite sum of rationals, hence rational. If epsilon is irrational, the left side is irrational and the equation can't hold — contradiction. So every irrational epsilon works, and the possible collision-causing values are only a thin exceptional set. The 1+epsilon weighting on the center isn't a knob I need in order to decorate the model; it is the device that keeps the root distinct from its neighborhood while staying injective. I can expose epsilon as learnable, or I can fix epsilon to zero for a simpler center-plus-neighbor-sum variant, but the exact injectivity proof I just wrote belongs to values that avoid those collisions.

Now collapse the abstraction into a layer I can actually run. The per-step map is "transform each neighbor by f, sum, add the (1+epsilon)-weighted transformed center, then apply an outer map." I model the maps by MLPs, and since an MLP can represent a composition of functions, I fold the outer map of layer k together with the element map of layer k+1 into a single MLP per layer. That gives the update

  h_v^(k) = MLP^(k)( (1 + epsilon^(k)) · h_v^(k-1) + sum_{u in N(v)} h_u^(k-1) ).

In the very first layer, if inputs are one-hot, I don't even need an MLP before the sum, because summing one-hot vectors is already injective (it just counts). I'll keep the implementation switchable: learn epsilon when I want the cleaner expressivity route, or fix epsilon to 0 when I want the simpler variant with fewer parameters and accept the small theoretical gap. This update — sum the neighbors, reweight the center by 1+epsilon when that parameter is active, push through a two-layer MLP — is the aggregation-and-update I was chasing: with the injective choice of epsilon it is injective on (center, neighbor multiset), so by my theorem it matches WL layer for layer.

Two layers in, the node vectors capture rooted subtrees of growing height. But here's a tension I have to face for the readout. A sufficient number of iterations is *necessary* for discriminative power — shallow networks can't see far enough to tell distant structures apart. Yet I also know the receptive field doesn't grow gently: after k rounds, a node's vector draws on roughly the spread of a k-step random walk, which in densely-connected regions blows up to almost the whole graph within a logarithmic number of steps, so deep node vectors in such regions get *washed out* — dominated by global averages, carrying little about the node itself — while in tree-like regions they stay informative and local. So depth is a double-edged thing: I need it for power, but the deepest layer's vectors aren't uniformly the best summary of every node; earlier-layer vectors, being more local, sometimes generalize better. If I read out only from the final layer, I'm betting the whole graph vector on whichever scale the last layer happens to encode, and I've thrown away the complementary scales the earlier layers built.

So don't pick a layer — keep them all. Read out from *every* iteration and combine. Concretely, pool each layer's node vectors into a per-layer graph vector, then combine across layers:

  h_G = COMBINE_over_layers( READOUT({h_v^(k): v in G}) | k = 0, 1, ..., K ).

Two design choices left. First, the node-level READOUT inside each layer. My whole theory says the last injective bottleneck must be injective on the multiset of node vectors — so this readout has to be *sum*, for the exact reason the aggregator had to be sum: mean would fold a graph against a k-times-replicated copy of itself, max would fold it down to its set, and either way I'd lose, at the very last step, distinctions the layers fought to preserve. Sum at readout is what makes the whole network provably generalize the WL test and the WL subtree kernel — the kernel's graph feature is literally counts of subtree labels, and summing the subtree-encoding node vectors *is* that count, lifted into a learned, similarity-aware embedding space instead of one-hot identifiers. Second, how to combine the per-layer graph vectors. The candidates I know are concatenation, a coordinate-wise max across layers, and an LSTM-with-attention over layers. Max and the LSTM both *select* — they decide, per coordinate or per node, which layer to trust — which is powerful and adaptive but throws away whatever layers it doesn't pick and, in the LSTM case, adds a lot of parameters that will overfit on small graphs (and these graph-classification datasets are tiny — MUTAG is 188 graphs). Concatenation, by contrast, keeps *every* layer's pooled vector intact, losslessly, side by side, and lets the downstream classifier learn the weighting — no selection, no information discarded, minimal parameters, and weight-sharing across the combine that helps on exactly these small, fairly regular graphs. For a maximally-information-preserving readout on small graphs, concatenation of the per-layer sums is the right call. So the graph vector is the concatenation, over all layers, of the sum-pooled node vectors of that layer — width hidden_dim times the number of layers, no bottleneck projection, because shrinking it would reintroduce exactly the kind of lossy collapse I just spent the whole derivation avoiding.

Let me write the causal chain back to myself before I code it. I started unable to tell whether my GNNs underfit because they can't optimize or because they can't represent. I made that precise by asking which non-isomorphic graphs a GNN can separate, and recognized the WL test as the structurally-identical, computable yardstick — powerful exactly because its relabel hash is injective. I proved any aggregation-based GNN is at most as powerful as WL (same functions everywhere, permutation-invariant readout, induction + contradiction), and that it *reaches* WL precisely when its neighbor aggregation, its update, and its graph readout are all injective on multisets — three injective bottlenecks in series, the readout being the last. I checked countability propagates so injectivity is well-defined at depth. I constructed an injective multiset sum via a place-value element map, which made sum a *universal* multiset representation, and derived that mean keeps only distributions and max only the underlying set, so sum is forced. I added a (1+epsilon)-weighted center to keep the root distinct from its neighbors, injective for irrational epsilon by a rational-vs-irrational contradiction, realized the maps with MLPs (one-layer perceptrons provably fold equal-sum multisets), and folded them into the GIN layer. For the readout I kept every layer (depth is needed for power but washes out locality, and early layers carry complementary local scale), used sum at the node level for injectivity, and concatenated across layers to preserve everything losslessly on small graphs. Now the code — the neighbor aggregation is PyG's add aggregation, the graph readout is per-layer `global_add_pool`, and the across-layer combine is Jumping-Knowledge concatenation:

```python
import torch.nn as nn
import torch.nn.functional as F
from torch_geometric.nn import GINConv, JumpingKnowledge, global_add_pool


class GINBackbone(nn.Module):
    """Sum neighbors, add the weighted center node, and update with an MLP."""

    def __init__(self, input_dim, hidden_dim, num_layers, train_eps=False):
        super().__init__()
        self.convs = nn.ModuleList()
        self.norms = nn.ModuleList()

        for layer in range(num_layers):
            in_dim = input_dim if layer == 0 else hidden_dim
            update = nn.Sequential(
                nn.Linear(in_dim, hidden_dim),
                nn.BatchNorm1d(hidden_dim),
                nn.ReLU(),
                nn.Linear(hidden_dim, hidden_dim),
            )
            self.convs.append(GINConv(update, eps=0.0, train_eps=train_eps, aggr="add"))
            self.norms.append(nn.BatchNorm1d(hidden_dim))

    def forward(self, x, edge_index, batch=None):
        layer_outputs = []
        for conv, norm in zip(self.convs, self.norms):
            x = conv(x, edge_index)
            x = F.relu(norm(x))
            layer_outputs.append(x)
        return x, layer_outputs


class SumJKReadout(nn.Module):
    """Sum-pool each layer's node embeddings, then concatenate the graph vectors."""

    def __init__(self, hidden_dim, num_layers):
        super().__init__()
        self.output_dim = hidden_dim * num_layers
        self.jk = JumpingKnowledge(mode="cat")

    def forward(self, x, edge_index, batch, layer_outputs):
        pooled = [global_add_pool(h, batch) for h in layer_outputs]
        return self.jk(pooled)


class GraphClassifier(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, input_dim, hidden_dim, num_classes, num_layers, dropout=0.5,
                 train_eps=False):
        super().__init__()
        self.backbone = GINBackbone(input_dim, hidden_dim, num_layers, train_eps)
        self.readout = SumJKReadout(hidden_dim, num_layers)
        self.classifier = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(self.readout.output_dim, hidden_dim),
            nn.ReLU(),
            nn.Dropout(dropout),
            nn.Linear(hidden_dim, num_classes),
        )

    def forward(self, data):
        x, edge_index, batch = data.x, data.edge_index, data.batch
        node_emb, layer_outputs = self.backbone(x, edge_index, batch)
        graph_emb = self.readout(node_emb, edge_index, batch, layer_outputs)
        return self.classifier(graph_emb)
```

The summing readout is the injective bottleneck the theory demanded, and the per-layer concatenation is the keep-every-scale combine, feeding a graph vector of width hidden_dim·num_layers into the classifier. The aggregator that built these node vectors — add the neighbors, keep the center as a separately weighted term, push through an MLP — is the matching bottleneck one stage upstream. When epsilon takes one of the collision-avoiding values from the proof, the end-to-end network is as discriminative as the WL test, which is the most any aggregation-based GNN can be; the fixed-zero switch is the simpler implementation variant with the small theoretical gap kept visible.
