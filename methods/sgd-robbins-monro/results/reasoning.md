I start with the root problem, not with gradients. I have a level `x`, an unknown mean response `M(x)`, and a target `alpha`. The only observation is a random `y` with mean `M(x)`. If `M` is increasing and `theta` solves `M(theta)=alpha`, then the sign information is still present in expectation: above `theta`, the mean response is too large; below `theta`, the mean response is too small. That is the only directional information I can trust.

My first instinct is to fall back to deterministic numerical equation solving. That whole tradition works because an equation can be evaluated, bracketed, and refined by iteration, Newton-Raphson, or false position. If I knew `M(x_n)`, I would move against `M(x_n)-alpha`. But I do not know it. If I simply replace `M(x_n)` by one noisy response and keep a fixed gain, the mean motion contracts but the random part never fades. Each new response kicks the iterate by a fixed-size random amount. That cannot give convergence to a point; at best it gives a cloud around the point. So the gain cannot stay fixed.

The other instinct is to estimate first and move later. At each `x_n`, I could repeat the experiment many times, average the responses, and then use the average as if it were `M(x_n)`. This is statistically familiar: repeated experiments reduce the uncertainty of a mean, and small-sample theory exists precisely because a short series may not settle that mean accurately. But it feels wrong for a search problem. I may be far from the target, and I would be spending many samples to learn an irrelevant value accurately. The search only needs to know which way to move and roughly how hard; it does not need a precise estimate of the whole response curve. So I want each observation to do two jobs at once: point the iteration in the correct average direction and contribute one unit of averaging over time.

The sign fixes the update before the proof starts. If `x_n` is too high, then `M(x_n)-alpha` is positive, so the expected increment should be negative. If `x_n` is too low, the expected increment should be positive. With a positive gain `a_n`, the natural rule is `x_{n+1} = x_n + a_n (alpha - y_n)`.

Now the whole question is what the gains must do. They should go to zero, otherwise the noise keeps rattling the process. But they cannot go to zero too fast, otherwise the process may freeze before reaching `theta`. I need a way to see both requirements in the same calculation.

I look at squared error because it separates drift and noise cleanly. Let `V_n=(x_n-theta)^2`. Conditioning on the past gives `E[V_{n+1} | F_n] = V_n - 2 a_n (x_n - theta)(M(x_n) - alpha) + a_n^2 E[(y_n - alpha)^2 | F_n]`.

This is the decisive split. The middle term is the useful drift. It is nonnegative before the minus sign because `M` is monotone: `x_n-theta` and `M(x_n)-alpha` have the same sign. The last term is pure cost from using a noisy response. If the responses have bounded second moment, this cost is on the order of `a_n^2`. Therefore `sum a_n^2 < infinity` is not a decorative assumption; it is exactly what makes the total injected variance finite.

But this only tells me the squared error has a chance to settle. A settled process can still settle at the wrong place if the steps have run out. I need the deterministic pull to remain inexhaustible. That is the role of `sum a_n = infinity`. The proof has to account for the fact that the slope of `M` may only be controlled on the region the iterate can reach, so the lower bound on the useful drift is written as a sequence: `d_n >= k_n b_n`, where `b_n=E[(x_n-theta)^2]`. Under the monotonicity and local slope assumptions, the accumulated weighted lower bound diverges for type-`1/n` gains. Since the drift series is already forced to be finite by the squared-error recursion, the only possible limiting squared error is zero.

So the two gain conditions have separate jobs. Square summability damps the noise. Nonsummability keeps enough total motion to reach the root. A gain like `c/n` is the simplest compromise: the random kicks become small fast enough for their variances to add to a finite number, while the total deterministic travel is still unbounded.

This also clarifies why the method is larger than stochastic gradient descent. If the unknown residual happens to be an expected gradient, then the same recursion is SGD. But the root-finding problem does not require a loss function, an analytic gradient, or differentiability. It only needs a mean response whose sign around the target is informative. The response/nonresponse quantile problem is the clean example: a Bernoulli observation at a dose has mean `F(x)`, and the target is the dose where `F(x)=alpha`.

The optimization version follows the same pattern once I stop thinking of maximization as a different problem. The maximizer is a root of the derivative, but the derivative is not observed. I can manufacture a noisy derivative estimate by probing two nearby points, `z_n-c_n` and `z_n+c_n`, and taking a finite difference. Then the update is again a noisy root-finding step, now aimed at `M'(theta)=0`. This introduces a second knob. The width `c_n` must go to zero so the finite difference becomes a derivative, but dividing by `c_n` amplifies observation noise. That is why the maximum-finding scheme needs more than `sum a_n^2 < infinity`: it needs the accumulated bias, roughly `sum a_n c_n`, to be finite, and the amplified variance, roughly `sum a_n^2/c_n^2`, to be finite. With `a_n=1/n` and `c_n=n^{-1/3}`, both tails are summable while the gains themselves still add up forever.

In the modern martingale language, I can see the same idea without committing to one-dimensional notation. The squared error is an almost-supermartingale: its conditional expectation is at most the current value, plus a summable noise allowance, minus a nonnegative progress term. The almost-supermartingale theorem says the error has a finite limit and the total progress term is finite. The nonsummable gains and the sign/slope condition then rule out any positive limiting error. The method is therefore an averaging process disguised as search: it never stops sampling, never separately estimates the whole curve, and never lets old noise keep full influence.
