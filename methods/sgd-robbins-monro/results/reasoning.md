I start with the root problem, not with gradients. I have a level `x`, an unknown mean response `M(x)`, and a target `alpha`. The only observation is a random `y` with mean `M(x)`. If `M` is increasing and `theta` solves `M(theta)=alpha`, then the sign information is still present in expectation: above `theta`, the mean response is too large; below `theta`, the mean response is too small. That is the only directional information I can trust.

My first instinct is to fall back to deterministic numerical equation solving. That whole tradition works because an equation can be evaluated, bracketed, and refined by iteration, Newton-Raphson, or false position. If I knew `M(x_n)`, I would move against `M(x_n)-alpha`. But I do not know it. What happens if I just substitute one noisy response for `M(x_n)` and keep a fixed gain? Let me not hand-wave this; let me actually run it and look at the spread. I take the logistic case `M(x)=1/(1+e^{-x})` with `alpha=0.5`, so the true root is `theta=0`, gain `a=0.1`, start at `x_0=-2`, and look at where 40 independent runs land. At 5000 steps the run-to-run standard deviation is about `0.21`. If the fixed gain were actually settling the process, ten times as many steps should shrink that spread. At 50000 steps the standard deviation is about `0.26` â€” it does not shrink at all. So the mean motion does contract toward the root, but the random part never fades: each new response kicks the iterate by a fixed-size amount forever, and the iterate just rattles in a cloud around `theta`. A fixed gain cannot converge to a point. The gain has to shrink.

The other instinct is to estimate first and move later. At each `x_n`, I could repeat the experiment many times, average the responses, and then use the average as if it were `M(x_n)`. This is statistically familiar: repeated experiments reduce the uncertainty of a mean, and small-sample theory exists precisely because a short series may not settle that mean accurately. But it feels wrong for a search problem. I may be far from the target, and I would be spending many samples to learn an irrelevant value accurately. The search only needs to know which way to move and roughly how hard; it does not need a precise estimate of the whole response curve. So I want each observation to do two jobs at once: point the iteration in the correct average direction, and contribute one unit of averaging over time.

The sign fixes the shape of the update before I prove anything. If `x_n` is too high, then `M(x_n)-alpha` is positive, so the expected increment should be negative. If `x_n` is too low, the expected increment should be positive. With a positive gain `a_n`, the simplest rule with that property is `x_{n+1} = x_n + a_n (alpha - y_n)`. In expectation the increment is `a_n(alpha - M(x_n))`, which has exactly the sign I want.

Now the whole question is what the gains must do. The fixed-gain experiment already told me they should go to zero, otherwise the noise keeps rattling the process. But they cannot go to zero too fast, otherwise the process may freeze before reaching `theta`. I need a single calculation that exposes both requirements at once.

I look at squared error because it should separate drift and noise cleanly. Let `V_n=(x_n-theta)^2`. Squaring the update, `V_{n+1} = (x_n - theta)^2 + 2 a_n (x_n-theta)(alpha - y_n) + a_n^2 (alpha-y_n)^2`. Taking the conditional expectation given the past, and using `E[y_n|F_n]=M(x_n)`,

```text
E[V_{n+1} | F_n] = V_n - 2 a_n (x_n - theta)(M(x_n) - alpha) + a_n^2 E[(y_n - alpha)^2 | F_n].
```

Before I lean on this, I should make sure I expanded it correctly, because the whole argument rests on these three terms. I check it numerically on one concrete state. Take the logistic `M` again, `alpha=0.5`, `theta=0`, and put the iterate at `x_n=1.0` with `a_n=0.3`. Here `M(1)=1/(1+e^{-1})=0.7311`, and `y_n\in\{0,1\}` with `P(y_n=1)=0.7311`. The two possible next iterates are `x+0.3(0.5-1)=0.85` and `x+0.3(0.5-0)=1.15`, so the direct expectation is `0.7311\cdot0.85^2 + 0.2689\cdot1.15^2 = 0.883865`. Plugging the same numbers into the formula: `V_n=1`, drift `=-2(0.3)(1)(0.7311-0.5)=-0.138635`, noise `=0.09\cdot[0.7311\cdot0.25+0.2689\cdot0.25]=0.0225`, giving `1-0.138635+0.0225=0.883865`. The two match to ten digits, so the recursion is right.

This split is decisive. The middle term is the useful drift, and the numeric check just showed it is negative here (`-0.138635`): it pulls `V_n` down. That sign is not an accident of this example â€” `M` is monotone, so `x_n-theta` and `M(x_n)-alpha` always share a sign, making `(x_n-theta)(M(x_n)-alpha) >= 0` everywhere. The last term is pure cost from using a noisy response; in the check it was `0.0225`, on the order of `a_n^2`. If the responses have bounded second moment, this cost is on the order of `a_n^2` at every step. So the total noise injected over the whole run is on the order of `sum a_n^2`. For the squared error to even have a chance of settling, that sum has to be finite. `sum a_n^2 < infinity` is therefore not a decorative assumption â€” it is exactly the condition that the total injected variance is finite.

But a settled squared error can still settle at the wrong place if the deterministic pull runs out of fuel first. I need the drift to remain inexhaustible. Summing the drift terms, the total progress is on the order of `sum a_n (x_n-theta)(M(x_n)-alpha)`. The recursion already forces this sum to be finite, because `V_n >= 0` and the noise allowance is finite: rearranging, `sum 2 a_n (x_n-theta)(M(x_n)-alpha) <= V_1 + sum a_n^2 E[(y_n-alpha)^2|F_n] < infinity`. Under the monotonicity and a local slope bound on `M`, the per-step drift is at least `k_n a_n b_n` with `b_n=E[(x_n-theta)^2]` and `k_n` a positive slope factor on the reachable region. If `b_n` stayed bounded away from zero, this lower bound would accumulate like `sum a_n`, which has to be infinite for the gains to keep moving. A finite drift sum and a divergent `sum a_n` cannot coexist unless `b_n \to 0`. So the only way out is that the squared error goes to zero. That is the job of `sum a_n = infinity`.

So the two gain conditions have genuinely separate jobs, and I want to be sure they are not in conflict â€” that some schedule satisfies both. The obvious candidate is `a_n = c/n`. Let me actually add the two series rather than assert it. Taking `c=1`, the partial sum of `1/n` to a million terms is about `14.39`, tracking `ln N = 13.82` and climbing without bound, so `sum a_n = infinity`. The partial sum of `1/n^2` to a million terms is `1.644933`, sitting right at `pi^2/6 = 1.644934`, so `sum a_n^2 < infinity`. Both hold for the same schedule. The random kicks become small fast enough for their variances to add to a finite number, while the total deterministic travel is still unbounded. So `c/n` is the simplest schedule that damps the noise and still reaches the root.

I should also confirm the assembled method actually finds roots, not just that each piece is individually sound. I run the recursion on the logistic example: `observe` returns a Bernoulli with mean `1/(1+e^{-x})`, `alpha=0.5`, `x_0=-2`, `c=2`, 5000 steps. Across 20 seeds the estimates have mean `-0.03` and standard deviation `0.04`, clustered tightly around the true root `theta=0` â€” and unlike the fixed-gain cloud, this spread is small and would keep shrinking with more steps. To check it is tracking the actual quantile and not just landing on zero by symmetry, I retarget `alpha=0.7`. The true root is `theta = logit(0.7) = ln(0.7/0.3) = 0.8473`, and the mean estimate over 20 seeds is `0.805`, close and biased only by the finite horizon. The recursion is solving `F(theta)=alpha`, which is exactly the quantile problem.

This also clarifies how the method relates to stochastic gradient descent. If the unknown residual happens to be an expected gradient, then the same recursion is SGD. But nothing in the derivation used a loss function, an analytic gradient, or differentiability of `M`. It only needed a mean response whose sign around the target is informative. The response/nonresponse quantile problem is the clean example: a Bernoulli observation at a dose has mean `F(x)`, and the target is the dose where `F(x)=alpha`. SGD is the special case; the root-finding view is the broader thing.

The optimization version follows the same pattern once I stop treating maximization as a separate problem. The maximizer is a root of the derivative, but the derivative is not observed. I can manufacture a noisy derivative estimate by probing two nearby points, `z_n-c_n` and `z_n+c_n`, and taking a finite difference `(y_+ - y_-)/(2c_n)`. Then the update is again a noisy root-finding step, now aimed at `M'(theta)=0`. This introduces a second knob, `c_n`, and it is genuinely in tension with itself. The finite difference is only an unbiased derivative as `c_n \to 0`, so bias pushes me to shrink `c_n` fast. But dividing by `c_n` amplifies the observation noise by `1/c_n`, so the per-step variance is on the order of `a_n^2/c_n^2`, and shrinking `c_n` fast inflates it.

My first reflex is to make `c_n` as small as possible â€” say `c_n = 1/n`, matching the gains â€” to kill the bias quickly. Let me check the amplified-variance series before committing. With `a_n=1/n` and `c_n=1/n`, the variance term is `a_n^2/c_n^2 = (1/n^2)/(1/n^2) = 1`, and summing a constant diverges (a thousand terms already sum to a thousand). That schedule would inject unbounded noise â€” the cure is worse than the disease. So `c_n` must shrink, but strictly slower than `a_n`. I try the gentler `c_n = n^{-1/3}`. Now the bias term `sum a_n c_n = sum n^{-1} n^{-1/3} = sum n^{-4/3}`, and the amplified variance `sum a_n^2/c_n^2 = sum n^{-2}/n^{-2/3} = sum n^{-(2-2/3)} = sum n^{-4/3}`. Both reduce to the same series `sum n^{-4/3}`, exponent `4/3 > 1`, which converges; numerically the partial sums sit around `3.57`. Meanwhile `sum a_n = sum 1/n` still diverges. So `a_n=1/n`, `c_n=n^{-1/3}` controls bias and amplified noise simultaneously while keeping the motion inexhaustible. The two conditions collapsing to the identical exponent is a small coincidence of this schedule, not a general rule, but it makes the choice easy to justify.

In the modern martingale language, the one-dimensional bookkeeping above is a special case of a cleaner statement. The squared error is an almost-supermartingale: its conditional expectation is at most the current value, plus a summable noise allowance `a_n^2 E[(y_n-alpha)^2|F_n]`, minus a nonnegative progress term `2a_n(x_n-theta)(M(x_n)-alpha)`. The almost-supermartingale convergence theorem then says the error has a finite limit and the total progress term is finite â€” which is the same two facts I extracted by hand. The nonsummable gains and the sign/slope condition rule out any positive limiting error. So the method is an averaging process disguised as search: it never stops sampling, never separately estimates the whole curve, and never lets old noise keep full influence.
