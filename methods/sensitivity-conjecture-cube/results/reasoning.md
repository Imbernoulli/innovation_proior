Let me get the pain straight before I reach for any machinery. I have a Boolean function $f:\{0,1\}^n\to\{0,1\}$, and a whole zoo of complexity measures that are all polynomially tied to one another — block sensitivity, certificate complexity, decision-tree depth, the degree of the representing polynomial, query complexity. They form one cluster. And then there is sensitivity $s(f)$, the most childishly simple of them: stand at an input $x$, count how many single-bit flips change the answer, maximize over $x$. It is the one measure that nobody can fit into the cluster. The only thing I get for free is $s(f)\le bs(f)$, because a single flipped coordinate is a block of size one. The reverse — bounding $bs$ by a fixed power of $s$ — is open, and the best anyone has is exponential: $bs(f)=O(e^{s(f)}\sqrt{s(f)})$. I want a constant $C$ with $bs(f)\le s(f)^C$.

Why is this hard in a way the other relations aren't? Because $bs$ is allowed to be greedy across the whole input: it can collect $k$ disjoint blocks, each one flipping the output, gathering evidence from $k$ far-apart regions of the cube. Sensitivity sees only one point and its $n$ immediate neighbors at a time. So I'm trying to bound something global by something local, and the local thing can be tiny while the global thing, a priori, runs off. That asymmetry is the enemy.

Let me not bang on $s$ versus $bs$ directly — that's where everyone's been stuck for thirty years. Let me see what's already been *converted* into something more tractable. There's a beautiful reduction sitting here. Gotsman and Linial turned "$s$ versus $\deg$" into a pure question about the cube graph $Q^n$. Let me reconstruct it, because if I'm going to lean on it I need to actually believe it.

Identify $f$ with a $\pm1$ coloring of the vertices of $Q^n$. The trouble with reading sensitivity off a coloring directly is that $s(f,x)$ counts neighbors of $x$ with the *opposite* color — that's a "boundary" notion, awkward. So twist: multiply $f$ by the parity function $\chi_{[n]}(x)=(-1)^{\sum_i x_i}$, getting $g=f\cdot\chi_{[n]}$. Parity flips sign across every single edge of $Q^n$ (the cube is bipartite, even versus odd weight). So an edge $x\sim x^{\{i\}}$ where $f$ *agreed* becomes an edge where $g$ *disagrees*, and vice versa. Counting: $g(x)\ne g(x^{\{i\}})$ exactly when $f(x)=f(x^{\{i\}})$, so
$$s(g,x)=n-s(f,x).$$
And in Fourier terms $\widehat g(S)=\widehat f([n]\setminus S)$ — multiplying by the top character $\chi_{[n]}$ swaps a set with its complement — so the average $\mathbb E[g]=\widehat g(\varnothing)=\widehat f([n])$, the top Fourier coefficient of $f$. That coefficient is nonzero precisely when $f$ has full degree $n$.

Now look at what low sensitivity means for $g$. If $s(f)$ is small, then $s(g,x)=n-s(f,x)$ is *large* at every $x$: every vertex has almost all $n$ neighbors of its own color under $g$. Split $Q^n$ by the color of $g$: let $H$ be the induced subgraph on $\{g=+1\}$ and its complement on $\{g=-1\}$. "$g(x)$ agrees with most of its neighbors" means "in its own monochromatic induced subgraph, $x$ has high degree." So $s(f)$ small forces some induced subgraph of $Q^n$ to have a high-degree vertex. Set $\Gamma(H)=\max\{\Delta(H),\Delta(Q^n-H)\}$. The clean statement (any monotone $h$): for every induced $H$ with $|V(H)|\ne 2^{n-1}$, $\Gamma(H)\ge h(n)$, *if and only if* for every $f$, $s(f)\ge h(\deg(f))$. The condition $|V(H)|\ne 2^{n-1}$ is exactly $\mathbb E[g]\ne 0$, i.e. $\deg(f)=n$; restricting to a top-degree monomial reduces the general case to this. Good — I believe the bridge. So my job is now entirely on the cube: lower-bound the maximum degree of an induced subgraph of $Q^n$ that has more than half the vertices.

What's the target number? Either $H$ or its complement $Q^n-H$ has at least $2^{n-1}+1$ vertices, and $\Delta$ is monotone, so it suffices to handle the case $|V(H)|=2^{n-1}+1$. What can I hope for? Chung, Füredi, Graham, and Seymour already built a $(2^{n-1}+1)$-vertex induced subgraph whose maximum degree is exactly $\lceil\sqrt n\rceil$ — so I cannot possibly prove more than $\sqrt n$. And their *lower* bound, by counting, only reaches $(\tfrac12-o(1))\log_2 n$. There's the gap I want to close: a construction at $\sqrt n$, a proof at $\log n$. If I can prove $\Delta(H)\ge\sqrt n$, that's tight, and through the bridge it gives $s(f)\ge\sqrt{\deg(f)}$, i.e. $\deg(f)\le s(f)^2$.

Let me first feel why their counting tops out at $\log n$, so I don't repeat the mistake. The CFGS engine is: a subgraph of $Q^n$ with average degree $\bar d$ has at least $2^{\bar d}$ vertices (an isoperimetric-flavored induction splitting the cube into two subcubes), then applied per direction and summed. Everything is edges-and-walks accounting, and the logarithm is baked into "$\ge 2^{\bar d}$ vertices" — you exponentiate the degree to get a vertex count, so inverting it you only ever extract a *logarithm* of the size as a degree. To get $\sqrt n$ I'd need accounting that's polynomially stronger, and I don't see how to squeeze that out of counting walks. I need a different kind of object — something that knows about the whole subgraph at once, not edge by edge.

Eigenvalues are the natural "whole graph at once" handle. There's a clean and elementary inequality: for any graph $H$, the maximum degree is at least the largest adjacency eigenvalue, $\Delta(H)\ge\lambda_1(H)$. Let me re-derive it so I trust it and see exactly what it needs. Let $\vec v$ be a top eigenvector, $A\vec v=\lambda_1\vec v$. Pick the coordinate $i$ where $|v_i|$ is largest. Then
$$|\lambda_1||v_i|=|(A\vec v)_i|=\Big|\sum_{j} A_{ij}v_j\Big|=\Big|\sum_{j\sim i}A_{ij}v_j\Big|\le\sum_{j\sim i}|A_{ij}|\,|v_i|\le \Delta(H)\,|v_i|,$$
using that $A_{ij}$ vanishes for non-neighbors, that $|v_j|\le|v_i|$, and that the entries have absolute value $\le 1$. Divide by $|v_i|>0$: $|\lambda_1|\le\Delta(H)$. So the eigenvalue inequality only needs the matrix to be symmetric, supported on the edges, with entries bounded by $1$ in absolute value. That's a *lot* of freedom — the entries don't have to be $0/1$.

So the plan crystallizes: if I can show that *every* $(2^{n-1}+1)$-vertex induced subgraph $H$ has a large $\lambda_1$, I'm done. And the mechanism to force a large eigenvalue on every large *sub*graph from a property of the whole cube is eigenvalue interlacing. Cauchy's theorem: if $B$ is a principal submatrix of a symmetric matrix $A$ (delete the same rows and columns), with eigenvalues $\mu_1\ge\dots\ge\mu_m$ and $\lambda_1\ge\dots\ge\lambda_N$, then $\lambda_i\ge\mu_i\ge\lambda_{i+N-m}$. The induced subgraph $H$ on $m=2^{n-1}+1$ vertices is exactly a principal submatrix of the cube's matrix on $N=2^n$ vertices. So $\lambda_1(H)\ge\mu_1\ge\lambda_{1+N-m}(A)=\lambda_{2^{n-1}}(A)$. The whole thing comes down to: *what is the $2^{n-1}$-th largest eigenvalue of the cube's matrix?*

Let me try this with the honest, plain adjacency matrix of $Q^n$ first. Its eigenvalues are the integers $n-2k$ for $k=0,\dots,n$, with $n-2k$ having multiplicity $\binom{n}{k}$. I need the $2^{n-1}$-th one from the top, out of $2^n$ total. The spectrum is symmetric about $0$, and the multiplicities $\binom{n}{k}$ pile up near the middle $k\approx n/2$ where the eigenvalue $n-2k\approx 0$. So the $2^{n-1}$-th eigenvalue — right at the halfway mark — sits in that crowded middle, at a value that is $0$ (for $n$ even) or barely positive, and in any case $o(n)$: the central binomial coefficient $\binom{n}{n/2}$ is an enormous fraction of $2^n$, so a band of eigenvalues of value close to $0$ straddles the halfway index. Concretely for $Q^3$ the eigenvalues are $3,1,1,1,-1,-1,-1,-3$, so $\lambda_{2^{3-1}}=\lambda_4=1$ — already small relative to $n=3$ — and as $n$ grows the central concentration drives $\lambda_{2^{n-1}}\to o(n)$, in fact to $0$ for even $n$. The interlacing bound becomes $\lambda_1(H)\ge\lambda_{2^{n-1}}(A)$, which is $o(n)$ and gives nothing like $\sqrt n$. The plain spectrum is too crushed together near the middle; interlacing barely bites.

So the obstruction is precise and instructive: I need a symmetric matrix supported on the edges of $Q^n$, entries bounded by $1$, whose $2^{n-1}$-th largest eigenvalue is *as big as possible*. The plain $0/1$ adjacency has the wrong spectrum — most of its eigenvalues huddle near $0$. The eigenvalue inequality $\Delta\ge\lambda_1$ didn't demand $0/1$ entries; it allowed signs. That's the lever. I get to *choose signs* on the edges to reshape the spectrum.

What spectrum would be ideal? I want the $2^{n-1}$-th eigenvalue to be large. The most extreme thing I could ask for: push *all* of the top half of the spectrum up to a single large value, and the bottom half down to its negative. If the matrix had only two distinct eigenvalues, $+\theta$ with multiplicity $2^{n-1}$ and $-\theta$ with multiplicity $2^{n-1}$, then $\lambda_{2^{n-1}}=+\theta$ — the largest value it could possibly be — and interlacing would give $\lambda_1(H)\ge\theta$ for every large induced subgraph. So I want a signed matrix $A$ with exactly two eigenvalues $\pm\theta$, each of multiplicity $2^{n-1}$.

A matrix with eigenvalues only $\pm\theta$ satisfies $A^2=\theta^2 I$. That's the whole demand, packaged: **find a $\pm1,0$ symmetric matrix supported on the edges of $Q^n$ with $A^2=\theta^2 I$.** And then trace forces the multiplicities: $\operatorname{Tr}(A)=0$ (it's an adjacency-type matrix, zero diagonal), and the trace is the sum of eigenvalues $=(\#{+\theta})\theta+(\#{-\theta})(-\theta)$, so the two multiplicities are equal, hence each is $2^{n-1}$. Beautiful — the trace does the bookkeeping for free.

What's $\theta$ going to be? Look at the diagonal of $A^2$. $(A^2)_{ii}=\sum_j A_{ij}A_{ji}=\sum_j A_{ij}^2$. Each vertex of $Q^n$ has exactly $n$ neighbors, each contributing an entry $\pm1$, and $(\pm1)^2=1$, so $(A^2)_{ii}=n$ regardless of the signs. So if $A^2$ is a multiple of $I$ at all, the multiple is forced: $A^2=nI$, and $\theta=\sqrt n$. That's exactly the number I'm hunting — the CFGS construction sits at $\sqrt n$. The whole approach wants $A^2=nI$.

So the only real question left is the off-diagonal: can I sign the edges of $Q^n$ so that $(A^2)_{ij}=0$ for all $i\ne j$? Let me see what $(A^2)_{ij}$ is. It's $\sum_k A_{ik}A_{kj}$, a sum over common neighbors $k$ of $i$ and $j$ — i.e. over length-two paths from $i$ to $j$. In $Q^n$ two vertices have a common neighbor only if they are at Hamming distance $2$, and then they have *exactly two* common neighbors: if $i$ and $j$ differ in coordinates $a$ and $b$, the two paths go through "flip $a$ then $b$" and "flip $b$ then $a$." Those two paths, together with $i$ and $j$, are precisely a $4$-cycle (a square) of the cube. Each length-two path contributes the product of its two edge-signs. So
$$(A^2)_{ij}=\text{(sign of path 1)}+\text{(sign of path 2)},$$
two terms each $\pm1$. They cancel to $0$ iff the two paths have *opposite* signs — iff the product of the four edge-signs around that square is $-1$. So the condition I need is purely local and geometric: **sign the edges so that every $4$-cycle of $Q^n$ carries an odd number of $-1$ edges.** Then every off-diagonal of $A^2$ cancels, the diagonal is $n$, and $A^2=nI$ exactly. (If instead $i\ne j$ are not at distance $2$, they share no common neighbor and $(A^2)_{ij}=0$ automatically.)

Now I just need an explicit such signing, and I want it to be checkable by induction, because $A^2=nI$ is what everything hinges on. Build the cube recursively: $Q^n$ is two copies of $Q^{n-1}$ (the subcubes $x_n=0$ and $x_n=1$) joined by a perfect matching in direction $n$. In block form a signed matrix for $Q^n$ looks like
$$A_n=\begin{bmatrix} A_{n-1} & D\\ D & B \end{bmatrix},$$
where the diagonal blocks $A_{n-1},B$ sign the two subcubes and the off-diagonal $D$ signs the matching. The matching is one edge per vertex straight across, so $D$ should be a signed identity; take $D=I$. For the diagonal blocks, the simplest choice is to reuse the same signing $A_{n-1}$ on the first subcube — but I have to pick the second block to make the square work. Compute the square in blocks:
$$A_n^2=\begin{bmatrix} A_{n-1}^2+ I & A_{n-1}+B\\ B + A_{n-1} & I+B^2\end{bmatrix}.$$
(Here I used $D=I$, so the top-left is $A_{n-1}A_{n-1}+I\cdot I$, top-right is $A_{n-1}\cdot I+I\cdot B$, etc.) For this to be a scalar matrix the off-diagonal blocks must vanish: $A_{n-1}+B=0$, so $B=-A_{n-1}$. Try it. Then
$$A_n=\begin{bmatrix} A_{n-1} & I\\ I & -A_{n-1}\end{bmatrix},\qquad A_n^2=\begin{bmatrix} A_{n-1}^2+I & 0\\ 0 & A_{n-1}^2+I\end{bmatrix}.$$
The off-diagonal blocks died because $A_{n-1}\cdot I+I\cdot(-A_{n-1})=0$. And the bottom-right is $I\cdot I+(-A_{n-1})(-A_{n-1})=A_{n-1}^2+I$, same as the top-left. So if inductively $A_{n-1}^2=(n-1)I$, then $A_n^2=\bigl((n-1)+1\bigr)I=nI$. The base case: $Q^1$ is a single edge, $A_1=\bigl[\begin{smallmatrix}0&1\\1&0\end{smallmatrix}\bigr]$, and $A_1^2=I=1\cdot I$. Induction closes. The recursion
$$A_1=\begin{bmatrix}0&1\\1&0\end{bmatrix},\qquad A_n=\begin{bmatrix}A_{n-1}&I\\ I&-A_{n-1}\end{bmatrix}$$
gives $A_n^2=nI$ for every $n$.

Let me sanity-check that this matrix really is a signing of the cube and not some unrelated object. The nonzero entries of $A_n$: the two $A_{n-1}$ blocks sit on the two subcubes, the two $I$ blocks sit on the cross-matching. If I change every $-1$ entry to $+1$, the diagonal blocks become the (all-positive) adjacency of $Q^{n-1}$ and the off-diagonal blocks stay $I$ — which is exactly the adjacency matrix of $Q^n$. So the *support* of $A_n$ is precisely the edge set of $Q^n$, and $A_n$ is a $\{-1,0,1\}$ matrix supported on those edges. That's all the eigenvalue lemma needs. And the $-A_{n-1}$ block is exactly what flips an odd number of signs on each square straddling the two subcubes — the geometric condition I derived is realized automatically by the recursion. (I can feel why this had to work: the blocks $A_{n-1}$ and the new "direction-$n$" matching act like operators that *anticommute* — the cross term $A_{n-1}I+I(-A_{n-1})$ is a commutator-like cancellation — so squaring the sum gives the sum of squares with no cross terms, $A_n^2=\sum_{\text{directions}}(\pm1)^2 I=nI$. It's the same algebra that makes a sum of anticommuting square-roots-of-identity square to a scalar.)

So now I have the spectrum of $A_n$. From $A_n^2=nI$, every eigenvalue $\lambda$ satisfies $\lambda^2=n$, so $\lambda\in\{+\sqrt n,-\sqrt n\}$. And $\operatorname{Tr}(A_n)=0$ (zero diagonal — the cube has no loops), so the eigenvalues sum to zero, forcing exactly half to be $+\sqrt n$ and half $-\sqrt n$: each value has multiplicity $2^{n-1}$. There it is — the two-eigenvalue spectrum I wanted, with $\theta=\sqrt n$, the $2^{n-1}$-th largest eigenvalue equal to $+\sqrt n$.

Now I need Cauchy interlacing to actually be true, not just invoked, because it carries the whole transfer. Let me prove it for removing one row/column, then iterate. The clean route is through real-rootedness of characteristic polynomials. Two real-rooted polynomials $f$ (roots $r_1\le\dots\le r_N$) and $g$ (roots $s_1\le\dots\le s_{N-1}$) *interlace* if $r_1\le s_1\le r_2\le s_2\le\dots\le s_{N-1}\le r_N$, and a standard characterization is: $f$ and $g$ interlace iff every real combination $f+\alpha g$ is real-rooted. Take $A$ symmetric, and write it (after permuting rows/columns, which doesn't change eigenvalues) as
$$A=\begin{bmatrix} B & c\\ c^{\!\top} & d\end{bmatrix},$$
so $B$ is the principal submatrix obtained by deleting the last row and column. Expand the characteristic determinant by linearity in the last column:
$$\det(A-xI)=\det\begin{bmatrix} B-xI & c\\ c^{\!\top} & d-x\end{bmatrix}.$$
Add a parameter $\alpha$ to the corner and split:
$$\det\begin{bmatrix} B-xI & c\\ c^{\!\top} & d-x+\alpha\end{bmatrix}=\det\begin{bmatrix} B-xI & c\\ c^{\!\top} & d-x\end{bmatrix}+\det\begin{bmatrix} B-xI & c\\ 0 & \alpha\end{bmatrix}=\det(A-xI)+\alpha\det(B-xI).$$
The left side is the characteristic polynomial (up to sign) of the symmetric matrix $A$ with $\alpha$ added to its bottom-right entry — still symmetric, still real-rooted — for *every* real $\alpha$. So $\det(A-xI)+\alpha\det(B-xI)$ is real-rooted for all $\alpha$, which is exactly the statement that the characteristic polynomials of $A$ and $B$ interlace. Hence the eigenvalues of $B$ interlace those of $A$. Iterating, delete rows/columns one at a time from $A$ ($N\times N$) down to $A_H$ ($m\times m$); at each step the eigenvalues interlace the previous, and stacking the inequalities gives the general form $\lambda_i(A)\ge\mu_i(A_H)\ge\lambda_{i+N-m}(A)$ for all $1\le i\le m$.

Now assemble. Let $H$ be any $(2^{n-1}+1)$-vertex induced subgraph of $Q^n$, and let $A_H$ be the principal submatrix of $A_n$ on the vertices of $H$. Since $A_n$ is symmetric, $\{-1,0,1\}$-valued, and supported exactly on the edges of $Q^n$, its restriction $A_H$ is symmetric, $\{-1,0,1\}$-valued, and supported exactly on the edges of $H$ — non-adjacent vertices of $H$ give a zero entry. So the eigenvalue lemma applies to $A_H$ and $H$:
$$\Delta(H)\ge\lambda_1(A_H).$$
And interlacing with $N=2^n$, $m=2^{n-1}+1$, $i=1$ gives
$$\lambda_1(A_H)\ge\lambda_{1+N-m}(A_n)=\lambda_{2^{n}-(2^{n-1}+1)+1}(A_n)=\lambda_{2^{n-1}}(A_n)=\sqrt n,$$
because the $2^{n-1}$-th largest eigenvalue of $A_n$ is exactly the smallest of its $+\sqrt n$ eigenvalues. Chain them:
$$\Delta(H)\ge\lambda_1(A_H)\ge\sqrt n.$$
That's the theorem: every $(2^{n-1}+1)$-vertex induced subgraph of $Q^n$ has maximum degree at least $\sqrt n$.

Let me double check the count in the interlacing index, because an off-by-one there would be fatal. $A_n$ has $N=2^n$ eigenvalues; the top $2^{n-1}$ are all $+\sqrt n$, so $\lambda_1=\dots=\lambda_{2^{n-1}}=\sqrt n$ and $\lambda_{2^{n-1}+1}=\dots=\lambda_{2^n}=-\sqrt n$. With $m=2^{n-1}+1$, the lower interlacing index is $i+N-m=1+2^n-(2^{n-1}+1)=2^n-2^{n-1}=2^{n-1}$. So $\mu_1\ge\lambda_{2^{n-1}}=\sqrt n$. Exactly the boundary — the $+\sqrt n$ eigenvalues are $2^{n-1}$ of them, and I'm asking for the $2^{n-1}$-th, which is the last $+\sqrt n$. One fewer vertex in $H$ (taking $m=2^{n-1}$) would land the index at $2^{n-1}+1$, the first $-\sqrt n$, and the bound would collapse to $\ge-\sqrt n$ — that's precisely why the threshold is "more than half," and why "half" itself (the even vertices, an independent set, $\Delta=0$) is not forced. The single extra vertex is doing real work; the bound jumps from $0$ to $\sqrt n$ the instant you cross the halfway line.

Is it tight? CFGS built a $(2^{n-1}+1)$-vertex induced subgraph of maximum degree $\lceil\sqrt n\rceil$, which equals $\sqrt n$ exactly when $n$ is a perfect square. So $\sqrt n$ cannot be improved, and the bound is best possible. Good.

I can squeeze a little more out of the same proof and it sharpens the statement. The chain actually shows $\lambda_1(A_H)\ge\sqrt n$, an eigenvalue lower bound, and the eigenvalue lemma's intermediate quantity is the spectral radius. In fact $\lambda_1(H)\ge\lambda_1(A_H)$, where $\lambda_1(H)$ is the largest eigenvalue of $H$'s ordinary $0/1$ adjacency matrix: flipping all the signs of $A_H$ back to $+1$ only enlarges the top eigenvalue (the all-nonnegative adjacency matrix dominates entrywise, and its Perron eigenvalue is at least the spectral radius of any matrix it dominates in absolute value). So $\lambda_1(H)\ge\lambda_1(A_H)\ge\sqrt n$, and since $\Delta(H)\ge\lambda_1(H)$ always, this is genuinely stronger than the degree statement. And the spectral version is best possible for *every* $n$, not just perfect squares: take all $2^{n-1}$ even vertices plus one odd vertex. That odd vertex is adjacent to all $n$ of its (even) neighbors, every other even vertex is isolated in the induced subgraph, so $H$ is a star $K_{1,n}$ together with isolated vertices, and $\lambda_1(K_{1,n})=\sqrt n$ exactly. So $\lambda_1(H)\ge\sqrt n$ is sharp for all $n$.

Now back out through the bridge. I've shown $\Gamma(H)\ge\sqrt n$ for every induced subgraph with $|V(H)|\ne 2^{n-1}$ (one side has $>2^{n-1}$ vertices, $\Delta$ monotone). By Gotsman–Linial with $h(n)=\sqrt n$, this is equivalent to $s(f)\ge\sqrt{\deg(f)}$ for every Boolean $f$, i.e.
$$\deg(f)\le s(f)^2.$$
And this is itself tight: the AND-of-ORs function on $m^2$ variables, $\bigwedge_{i=1}^m\bigvee_{j=1}^m x_{ij}$, has degree $m^2$ and sensitivity $m$, so $\deg=s^2$ exactly.

The last step is to convert "degree" back to "block sensitivity," and that's already in hand from before: degree and block sensitivity are quadratically tied — $bs(f)\le\deg(f)^2$ (the symmetrization-plus-Markov argument, in its sharpened constant). Compose:
$$bs(f)\le\deg(f)^2\le\bigl(s(f)^2\bigr)^2=s(f)^4.$$
There is the absolute constant: $C=4$. Sensitivity sits inside the polynomial cluster after all.

Let me retrace the whole causal chain in one breath. Sensitivity is local and the obstruction was bounding the global $bs$ by it; Gotsman–Linial converts $s$-versus-$\deg$ into a max-degree question on large induced subgraphs of the cube; CFGS's construction says the answer should be $\sqrt n$ but their counting only reached $\log n$ because counting walks exponentiates degrees; eigenvalues are the global handle, and $\Delta\ge\lambda_1$ holds for any signed edge-matrix, which frees me to choose signs; interlacing transfers a property of $Q^n$ to every large subgraph, but the plain adjacency's middle eigenvalue is $0$ so I must reshape the spectrum; demanding only two eigenvalues $\pm\theta$ means $A^2=\theta^2 I$, the diagonal forces $\theta=\sqrt n$, and the off-diagonal forces each $4$-cycle to carry an odd number of $-1$ edges, realized by the recursion $A_n=\bigl[\begin{smallmatrix}A_{n-1}&I\\ I&-A_{n-1}\end{smallmatrix}\bigr]$; trace zero splits the spectrum evenly so $\lambda_{2^{n-1}}=\sqrt n$; interlacing then gives every $(2^{n-1}+1)$-vertex induced subgraph $\lambda_1\ge\sqrt n$, hence $\Delta\ge\sqrt n$, tight against CFGS; the bridge turns this into $\deg(f)\le s(f)^2$, and the known $bs(f)\le\deg(f)^2$ closes it to $bs(f)\le s(f)^4$.

For the record, here is the theorem with its full proof, exactly as the chain produced it.

**Theorem.** For every integer $n\ge 1$, every $(2^{n-1}+1)$-vertex induced subgraph $H$ of $Q^n$ satisfies $\Delta(H)\ge\sqrt n$. This is tight when $n$ is a perfect square. Consequently $s(f)\ge\sqrt{\deg(f)}$ and $bs(f)\le s(f)^4$ for every Boolean function $f$.

*Eigenvalue lemma.* Let $H$ be an $m$-vertex graph and $A$ a symmetric matrix with entries in $\{-1,0,1\}$, indexed by $V(H)$, with $A_{uv}=0$ whenever $u\not\sim v$. Then $\Delta(H)\ge\lambda_1(A)$. *Proof.* If $A\vec v=\lambda_1\vec v$ and $|v_i|$ is the largest coordinate, $|\lambda_1||v_i|=\bigl|\sum_{j\sim i}A_{ij}v_j\bigr|\le\sum_{j\sim i}|v_i|\le\Delta(H)|v_i|$, so $|\lambda_1|\le\Delta(H)$. $\square$

*Spectral lemma.* Define $A_1=\bigl[\begin{smallmatrix}0&1\\1&0\end{smallmatrix}\bigr]$ and $A_n=\bigl[\begin{smallmatrix}A_{n-1}&I\\ I&-A_{n-1}\end{smallmatrix}\bigr]$. Then $A_n^2=nI$, so $A_n$ has eigenvalues $\pm\sqrt n$, each with multiplicity $2^{n-1}$. *Proof.* $A_1^2=I$. Inductively, $A_n^2=\bigl[\begin{smallmatrix}A_{n-1}^2+I&0\\0&A_{n-1}^2+I\end{smallmatrix}\bigr]=nI$, so eigenvalues are $\pm\sqrt n$; $\operatorname{Tr}A_n=0$ splits them evenly. $\square$

*Interlacing.* For symmetric $A$ ($N\times N$) and principal submatrix $B$ ($m\times m$), $\lambda_i(A)\ge\lambda_i(B)\ge\lambda_{i+N-m}(A)$, since linearity of the determinant in the last column gives $\det(A-xI)+\alpha\det(B-xI)=\det\bigl[\begin{smallmatrix}B-xI&c\\ c^\top&d-x+\alpha\end{smallmatrix}\bigr]$, real-rooted for all real $\alpha$, so the characteristic polynomials interlace; iterate. $\square$

*Main proof.* $A_n$ is $\{-1,0,1\}$-valued and supported on the edges of $Q^n$ (change each $-1$ to $1$ to recover the adjacency matrix). For $|V(H)|=m=2^{n-1}+1$, the principal submatrix $A_H$ satisfies the eigenvalue lemma, so $\Delta(H)\ge\lambda_1(A_H)$; interlacing gives $\lambda_1(A_H)\ge\lambda_{2^{n-1}}(A_n)=\sqrt n$. Hence $\Delta(H)\ge\sqrt n$. Through the Gotsman–Linial equivalence ($h(n)=\sqrt n$) this is $\deg(f)\le s(f)^2$, and with $bs(f)\le\deg(f)^2$ it gives $bs(f)\le s(f)^4$. $\square$
