Let me start from where the money is actually being spent. I'm training Transformer language models and the bill is dominated by training compute, which scales hard with model size. Inside a decoder block the two big consumers are attention and the position-wise feed-forward network, and the FFN is the fatter one by parameter count — it projects each token up to four times the model width, applies a pointwise nonlinearity, and projects back down. Two dense maps with an activation wedged between them, run independently at every position. Now, the two matmuls are load-bearing — I can't make them cheaper without changing what the layer computes — but that activation in the middle is special: it has no parameters of its own, and swapping it costs essentially nothing in FLOPs. So if I want a free lever on quality, that's the one place to push. The question I actually care about isn't raw step time, it's compute-to-quality: does a different activation make the model learn more per token, by enough that I reach a target loss in fewer total steps? Even a small per-step gain compounds across every layer and every model in a scaling sweep. So: hold the FFN structure fixed, treat the middle activation as the design slot, and ask what shape of nonlinearity squeezes more sample efficiency out of the same two matmuls.

What do I have on the shelf? The original block uses ReLU, max(x,0): hard gate by sign, kill the negatives, pass the positives straight through. The de-facto upgrade in the big LMs is GELU, x·Φ(x) where Φ is the standard-normal CDF — it smooths ReLU's corner and lets a sliver of negative signal leak through, and it trains a touch better, which is why BERT and GPT reach for it. There's also Swish, x·σ(βx), another smooth self-gated unit. Let me actually look at the *shape* of these three rather than just naming them, because shape is the only thing I'm allowed to change. ReLU is exactly the identity for x>0. GELU's Φ(x)→1 as x grows, so GELU→x. Swish's σ(βx)→1, so Swish→x too. All three are asymptotically linear: a strongly-firing unit and a barely-firing unit get pushed through the activation at nearly the same slope ≈1. The smooth ones differ from ReLU only near the origin — the leak, the soft knee — not out in the tail. So whatever distinguishes GELU from ReLU is a small-input effect, and it shows up as roughly-equal LM perplexity between them in practice. If I want a real change, fiddling with the knee isn't going to do it. I'd be reshuffling deck chairs around x=0 while the bulk of the function stays linear.

So maybe the lever isn't "smooth the knee" but "change the asymptotics." That's a bigger move and I should be careful, but let me follow it. What else is out there that isn't asymptotically linear?

There's a whole separate tradition that replaces the additive activation with a *multiplicative* one. Gated Linear Units, from Dauphin, Fan, Auli and Grangier — instead of squashing one projection, take two linear projections of the input and multiply them elementwise, one of them passed through a sigmoid: GLU(X) = (XW+b) ⊗ σ(XV+c). Their argument for why this helps is worth reconstructing, because it's about gradients, not about the forward shape. Let p = XW+b and q = XV+c. Differentiate p⊗σ(q): by the product rule the local variation is dp⊗σ(q) + p⊗σ'(q)⊗dq. Look at the first term, dp⊗σ(q): the gradient has a route through the *linear* branch multiplied only by the gate value σ(q), with no derivative-of-nonlinearity factor crushing that route. Compare to a plain saturating unit, where the gradient always gets multiplied by the activation's derivative, which is small wherever the unit saturates. The gate gives a multiplicative path along which gradient can travel through depth without that attenuation — a multiplicative skip connection, effectively. That's a genuinely different mechanism from "smooth the knee."

And there's a family that ran with this for the Transformer FFN — Shazeer's GLU variants. Same idea, but swap the squashing function and keep the gate: FFN_ReGLU(x) = (max(xW,0) ⊗ xV) W2, FFN_GEGLU = (GELU(xW) ⊗ xV) W2, FFN_SwiGLU = (Swish₁(xW) ⊗ xV) W2, and the ungated bilinear (xW ⊗ xV) W2. On the T5 span-filling benchmark GEGLU and SwiGLU came out ahead of the plain ReLU/GELU/Swish FFNs on held-out perplexity. So the multiplicative route demonstrably helps language modeling. Good — that's the direction. But there's a cost baked into all of these, and it bugs me. Each variant has *three* weight matrices — W, V, and W2 — where the original FFN has two. To keep the parameter count and the FLOPs matched to the baseline they shrink the inner width d_ff by a factor of 2/3, so W and V each map to (2/3)·d_ff units. So the gain isn't free; it comes bundled with an extra matrix and a 2/3 bookkeeping rule, and the best-performing members (GEGLU, SwiGLU) also drag along a smooth gate that needs an erf or a sigmoid per element. It's not a drop-in. I wanted to change *only* the activation, no new weights, no shrinking any dimension, no retuning. The GLU variants violate that on the first line. Wall.

So let me hold two facts side by side. Fact one: the multiplicative interaction is what's buying the perplexity — a product of two linear projections beats a squash of one. Fact two: the price is a whole extra projection matrix. Can I get the multiplicative effect *without* the extra matrix? That's the real question. A product needs two factors. In the GLU variants the two factors are two different linear maps of x, xW and xV. What if the two factors were the *same* linear map of x? Then I don't need a second matrix at all — I just multiply a projection by itself.

Let me make that precise and see if it's even sensible. Take ReGLU and force its two weight matrices to be equal, V = W = let's call it U, and look at one hidden unit. ReGLU's unit is max(uᵀx, 0)·(vᵀx); with u = v it becomes max(uᵀx, 0)·(uᵀx). Let z = uᵀx be the pre-activation that the up-projection already produces. Two cases. If z > 0, then max(z,0) = z, and the unit is z·z = z². If z ≤ 0, then max(z,0) = 0, and the whole product is 0. So tying the weights turns ReGLU's unit into "z² when z>0, else 0" — which is exactly (max(z,0))² = relu(z)². The two factors collapsed into one: the activation is just relu of the pre-activation, squared. And crucially, I don't need a V matrix anymore, because both factors are the *same* projection xW that the FFN's first matrix already computes. The squared ReLU is ReGLU-with-tied-weights, applied to the linear pre-activation the up-projection hands me for free. So I can capture the GLU-family multiplicative benefit with one matrix instead of two, no width shrink, a literal drop-in into the existing FFN. That's the move — the second factor was never something I needed a new matrix for, it was sitting right there in the up-projection's own output.

Now I'm nervous, because I just changed the asymptotics, and that's where things blow up if you're careless. relu(z)² grows like z² for large positive z, super-linearly, unlike ReLU/GELU/Swish which all top out at slope ≈1. Let me check whether super-linear is a feature or a footgun. The derivative of relu(z)² for z>0 is d/dz[z²] = 2z = 2·relu(z); for z<0 it's 0. So the backward gate isn't a 0/1 mask like ReLU's — it's 0 for dead units and 2·relu(z) for live ones, which grows *linearly* with the pre-activation and is unbounded above. Contrast that with GELU and Swish, whose derivatives saturate near 1 as z grows: a strongly-firing GELU unit and a mildly-firing one send back nearly the same gradient. With squared ReLU, a unit that fires hard gets a proportionally *larger* gradient. So the activation isn't just shaping the forward pass; it's telling the optimizer "the confident, strongly-active units matter more, push them harder," and it does this for free because the magnitude information is already in z. That's a real inductive bias, not a cosmetic change. The flip side is the danger: a quadratic with an unbounded-above gradient could, in principle, run away — if pre-activations grow, the output grows quadratically and the gradient linearly, and in low precision that's an overflow risk. So I need to make sure the *power* I pick is the gentlest one that still buys the effect.

Why power 2, then, and not 3 or 4? Rectified polynomials, F(x) = xⁿ for x≥0 and 0 otherwise, aren't new as a functional form — they show up in Krotov and Hopfield's associative-memory work, where increasing n makes each pattern's contribution *sharper* and raises capacity (it scales like Nⁿ⁻¹). So there's precedent that going super-linear with a rectified polynomial buys representational sharpness, and that higher n is "sharper" still. But sharper cuts both ways for an activation I have to train in bf16: the general rectified power has derivative n·relu(z)ⁿ⁻¹, which for n=3 is 3z² — quadratic gradient growth, much easier to blow up — and the forward zⁿ overflows sooner. n=2 is the *minimal* super-linear rectified polynomial: the smallest step away from linear that still gives me the multiplicative second-order term and the magnitude-aware gradient, with the gradient growing only linearly (2z) rather than faster. It's the safe rung on that ladder. So square, specifically, is the gentlest choice that changes the asymptotics at all.

And I should double-check that I want to *rectify* it, rather than just use a plain x². It's tempting — x² is even simpler, no max. But stare at x² for a second: it's symmetric, x² = (−x)², so it fires identically for +z and −z. That throws away the sign of the pre-activation entirely — every neuron becomes blind to which side of zero it's on, and the function is non-monotonic, dropping then rising as z passes through 0. That collapses half the representational axis; two inputs that the up-projection deliberately separated by sign would be mapped to the same output. relu(z)², by contrast, is monotone non-decreasing — flat-zero on the negative half, rising on the positive half — so it keeps ReLU's "off" behavior for negatives and only adds the super-linear shaping where the unit is actually on. The rectification isn't decoration; it's what preserves the gate while letting the active half curve upward. So relu(z)², not z².

Let me also place this in the broader picture so I'm sure it's the same mechanism the GLU variants were exploiting and not a coincidence. A product of two linear forms, (uᵀx)(vᵀx), is a second-order multiplicative interaction; with u = v it is the simplest self-product. Jayakumar and colleagues make the case that exactly these multiplicative interactions strictly enrich what a single layer can represent compared to a purely additive one — gating, attention, GLU are all instances of it. And it's hiding in plain sight elsewhere: the popular tanh-approximation of GELU, 0.5x(1 + tanh(√(2/π)(x + 0.044715x³))), carries an x³ term — even the "smooth standard" activation secretly has a higher-order polynomial component. So the thread connecting ReGLU, GEGLU, the bilinear unit, and approximate GELU is that they all inject higher-order/multiplicative structure that the asymptotically-linear ReLU lacks. relu(z)² is the leanest possible way to get onto that thread: a single self-multiplication, the minimal degree-2 rectified interaction, no extra parameters. That's why I believe it captures the GLU benefit while being simpler — it's the same second-order interaction, stripped to the bone.

So the forward is settled: in the FFN, replace the activation so that h = xW_fc gives the pre-activation, a = relu(h)², and the output is aW_proj. Now I want this to actually be *fast*, not just parameter-free, because an activation that swaps in for GELU should avoid GELU's expensive elementwise path. relu and a square are cheaper per element than GELU's erf/tanh, so the forward is already lighter. But the backward is where I can be sloppy or sharp. If I just let autograd build the graph for relu(h)², it'll record intermediate tensors and recompute through the elementwise chain; I'd rather hand-write the backward and save exactly what I need. Let me derive it carefully, because a wrong sign or a dropped factor here is a silent training bug.

Forward, with the matmuls: h = x W_fcᵀ (so h is the up-projected pre-activation), let r = relu(h), a = r·r = r² (elementwise), and y = a W_projᵀ. I'm given grad_output = ∂L/∂y. Walk it back.

Through the second matmul y = a W_projᵀ: the gradient to a is ∂L/∂a = grad_output · W_proj (since y = a W_projᵀ means ∂y/∂a contracts with W_proj; check shapes — y is (N, n_embd), a is (N, 4·n_embd), W_proj is (n_embd, 4·n_embd), so grad_output (N, n_embd) times W_proj (n_embd, 4·n_embd) gives (N, 4·n_embd), matching a ✓). Call this d_a.

Through the square-of-relu, a = relu(h)²: by the chain rule ∂L/∂h = d_a · d(relu(h)²)/dh. The derivative of relu(h)² with respect to h is 2·relu(h)·1[h>0]. But note 1[h>0] is redundant against relu(h): where h≤0, relu(h)=0 already, so 2·relu(h)·1[h>0] = 2·relu(h) exactly — the indicator is automatically enforced by the relu factor being zero on the dead side. So ∂L/∂h = 2·relu(h)·d_a = 2·r·d_a. That's the one place I need r = relu(h) in the backward; everything else is matmuls. Call this d_h.

Weight gradients. For W_proj: y = a W_projᵀ, so ∂L/∂W_proj = grad_outputᵀ · a (shapes: grad_outputᵀ (n_embd, N) times a (N, 4·n_embd) → (n_embd, 4·n_embd) = W_proj's shape ✓). For W_fc: h = x W_fcᵀ, so ∂L/∂W_fc = d_hᵀ · x (shapes: d_hᵀ (4·n_embd, N) times x (N, n_embd) → (4·n_embd, n_embd) = W_fc's shape ✓). And the input gradient: ∂L/∂x = d_h · W_fc (shapes: d_h (N, 4·n_embd) times W_fc (4·n_embd, n_embd) → (N, n_embd) = x's shape ✓).

So the backward is two cheap elementwise ops (the 2·r·d_a step) plus three matmuls, and the only nonlinear-specific value I need in order to apply the chain rule is r = relu(h). I could stash h and recompute relu(h) in the backward, or stash relu_h directly and skip the recompute. The torch baseline keeps both the pre-activation and relu(h), so I will mirror that exactly: h is the saved pre-activation, relu_h supplies the 2·relu(h) factor and the activation value for the second weight gradient. Either way the activation backward is dominated by the matmuls I'd pay anyway, and the elementwise part is a multiply-by-two-and-multiply, far cheaper than differentiating an erf. That's the throughput case, made concrete. I'll wrap this in a custom autograd Function so forward stashes (x, W_fc, W_proj, h, relu_h) and backward executes exactly the derivation above — no autograd graph through the elementwise chain, just the hand-written gradients.

One precision note before I write it. The pre-activations and outputs are computed in low precision (bf16) under autocast, but I want the backward matmuls and the 2·relu(h) multiply to be done in the gradient's dtype consistently, casting the saved tensors and weights to grad_output's dtype so I don't accidentally mix a bf16 weight with an fp32 grad. It's a small thing but it's the kind of mismatch that silently slows things down or NaNs. And I keep the unbounded-gradient worry in the back of my mind — relu(h)² can produce large activations if h drifts up — but adding a clamp inside the activation would change the function I just derived. Power 2 is the controlled step, with gradient growth only linear in relu(h), so I keep the activation clean and let the surrounding optimizer handle scale.

Let me write it as the FFN core, filling the one empty slot — the activation between the two matmuls and its hand-written backward:

```python
import torch
from torch.nn import functional as F


def fused_mlp_forward(x, w_fc, w_proj):
    """FFN core with squared-ReLU activation and a hand-written backward.

    x:      (B*T, n_embd)        input rows
    w_fc:   (4*n_embd, n_embd)   up-projection weight
    w_proj: (n_embd, 4*n_embd)   down-projection weight
    returns (B*T, n_embd)
    """

    class ReLUSquaredMLP(torch.autograd.Function):
        @staticmethod
        def forward(ctx, x, w_fc, w_proj):
            h = x @ w_fc.t()              # up-project: pre-activation z
            relu_h = F.relu(h)            # r = relu(z)
            act = relu_h * relu_h         # a = relu(z)^2  (the second-order, parameter-free term)
            out = act @ w_proj.t()        # down-project
            # mirror the torch baseline: h is the pre-activation, relu_h supplies 2*relu(z)
            ctx.save_for_backward(x, w_fc, w_proj, h, relu_h)
            return out

        @staticmethod
        def backward(ctx, grad_output):
            x, w_fc, w_proj, h, relu_h = ctx.saved_tensors
            dtype = grad_output.dtype
            # through the down-projection:  dL/da = grad_output @ w_proj
            d_act = grad_output @ w_proj.to(dtype)
            # through relu^2:  d/dz[relu(z)^2] = 2*relu(z)  (the 1[z>0] is subsumed by relu)
            d_h = 2 * relu_h.to(dtype) * d_act
            # weight gradients
            act_sq = (relu_h * relu_h).to(dtype)         # a = relu(z)^2
            grad_w_proj = grad_output.t() @ act_sq       # dL/dW_proj = grad_output^T @ a
            grad_w_fc = d_h.t() @ x.to(dtype)            # dL/dW_fc   = d_h^T @ x
            grad_x = d_h @ w_fc.to(dtype)                # dL/dx      = d_h @ W_fc
            return grad_x, grad_w_fc, grad_w_proj

    return ReLUSquaredMLP.apply(x, w_fc, w_proj)
```

Let me trace the causal chain one more time to be sure it holds together. The bill is training compute; the FFN dominates it; the activation between its two matmuls is the only parameter-free knob, so that's where I push. ReLU, GELU and Swish are all asymptotically linear — they differ only at the knee, which is why GELU barely beats ReLU on LM perplexity — so reshaping the knee can't be the lever; the lever has to be the asymptotics. The multiplicative GLU variants do change the game and do improve perplexity, but at the cost of a third weight matrix and a 2/3 width shrink, so they aren't drop-ins. Asking whether I can get the multiplicative effect without the extra matrix, I forced ReGLU's two weight matrices equal and watched its unit collapse to relu(z)² — meaning the second factor was the up-projection's own output all along, so squared ReLU is the GLU benefit with one matrix instead of two.
