Let me start from what actually goes wrong, not from the loss I'd write down by reflex. I'm pretraining a GPT-2-medium next-token model, everything frozen except the function that turns final logits into a scalar loss, and the default there is plain cross-entropy. So the whole game lives inside one expression. For a single position with logits z over the vocabulary and target index y, the loss is −log softmax(z)_y = −z_y + log Σ_j exp(z_j). I keep writing that down and moving on, but let me actually sit with it. To make this small I push z_y up and the log-sum-exp down, and the log-sum-exp is dominated by whichever logit is largest. So the loss decreases monotonically as I widen the gap z_y − max_{j≠y} z_j, and it only reaches its infimum as that gap goes to +∞. There is no finite z that minimizes this. The objective is, quite literally, an instruction to make the logits diverge.

That reframes the problem for me. The usual story is "cross-entropy is the right maximum-likelihood loss," and it is, but the thing it's telling gradient descent to do — on data that's even close to separable, which token-level LM data effectively is at the margin — is "keep growing the logits forever." On real training that's exactly what I see described: logit magnitudes drift upward across the run, the model gets more and more confident, its predicted probabilities outrun its actual accuracy. So the pain isn't an exotic failure; it's baked into the loss I'm using. A better objective at this boundary would damp that runaway without changing which token the model actually prefers. That last clause matters — softmax only cares about logit *differences*, and the argmax over the vocabulary is what determines the prediction, so whatever I do had better not scramble the ordering of the logits. I want to tame their *scale*, not their *order*.

Why do I even care about scale if softmax is shift-invariant and only differences matter? Because of the arithmetic underneath. I'm training in mixed precision — float32 master weights, but the matmuls and activations, and crucially the logits, flow through bfloat16. And bfloat16 keeps seven mantissa bits where float32 keeps twenty-three. Within any binade that's sixteen fewer bits, so about 2^16 ≈ 65,536 times the roundoff error; and because a fixed mantissa width spans each interval [2^k, 2^{k+1}), the absolute roundoff *grows with the magnitude of the number*. Now look at where my logits go: straight into an exponential. The softmax exponentiates them. An exponential has the nasty property that a small additive perturbation of the input is a multiplicative perturbation of the output — δ in the logit becomes a factor e^δ on the unnormalized probability. So a roundoff error that's negligible on a small logit becomes a real distortion of the probability once the logit is large. I can make this concrete the way I've seen it laid out: imagine ten logits sitting at 128 and one at 128.5, in bfloat16, after softmax subtracts the max for stability. That 0.5 gap is below the bfloat16 resolution up at 128, so it rounds away — the 128.5 collapses to 128 — and the target probability lurches from exp(0)/(exp(0)+10·exp(−0.5)) ≈ 0.142 to exp(0)/(exp(0)+10·exp(0)) ≈ 0.091. A 36% swing in a probability, manufactured entirely by roundoff, because the logits were large. So the unbounded growth the loss encourages isn't just a calibration nuisance; in low precision it's a direct route to noisy, unstable gradients through the softmax, and the bigger the vocabulary the more exp() terms there are to corrupt. Large logits are dangerous, and my loss is busy manufacturing them.

The first instinct is to attack the *target* rather than the logits, and that's a real, principled move, so let me think it through honestly before reaching for anything fancier. If the trouble is that the one-hot target demands probability 1 on y, which is a demand for an infinite logit gap, then soften the target: put mass 1−ε on y, spread the remaining ε over the V−1 other tokens, and minimize cross-entropy against that. Now what gap does the loss actually want? I should derive this rather than recite it. The softened cross-entropy is −Σ_k t_k log softmax(z)_k with t_y = 1−ε and t_{j≠y} = ε/(V−1); its gradient with respect to z_k is softmax(z)_k − t_k, so the stationary point is exactly softmax(z) = t, i.e. softmax(z)_y = 1−ε and softmax(z)_{j≠y} = ε/(V−1). If at that optimum I set every non-target logit to 0 and the target logit to the gap Δ, then softmax_y = e^Δ/(e^Δ + (V−1)) and setting that to 1−ε gives e^Δ = (1−ε)(V−1)/ε, so Δ = log((1−ε)(V−1)/ε). Let me put numbers on it to make sure it's actually finite and sane: with ε=0.1 and V=50257 (the GPT-2 vocabulary), Δ = log(0.9·50256/0.1) ≈ log(452304) ≈ 13.0 nats; plugging Δ=13.0 back in, e^13/(e^13 + 50256) = 442413/(442413+50256) = 0.897 ≈ 1−ε. Good — the formula is self-consistent and the target gap is a comfortable 13, not infinity. The divergent incentive is gone. This is label smoothing, and it genuinely helps calibration. But stare at what it does and doesn't fix. It removes the incentive for *one specific* gap to run away, but it puts no ceiling on the absolute magnitude of the logits — a model can sit at logits of (1000, 990, 990, …) and have exactly the smoothed-optimal gaps while being numerically enormous. And the roundoff problem I just derived is about magnitude, not about gaps after the max-subtraction is undone by the exp scaling, so smoothing the target doesn't touch it. There's a second wrinkle that matters here: the validation metric is the honest modeling cross-entropy, and a method may not lower it by distorting the distribution. Label smoothing optimizes against a *different* target distribution than the one I'm evaluated on; if I leave it on at eval it changes the reported number, and if I switch it off at eval I'm training and testing different objectives. It's a legitimate baseline but it's attacking the wrong end of −z_y + logsumexp(z) for the failure I actually care about. I want to bound the logits, not soften the labels.

So, bound the logits. The crudest version: clamp them. z ← clamp(z, −s, s), forcing every logit into [−s, s] before the softmax. That certainly caps the magnitude and kills the roundoff blow-up. But now think about the gradient, because I'm going to be backpropagating through this for thirteen thousand steps. clamp has derivative exactly 1 inside (−s, s) and exactly 0 outside. Let me make the failure mode concrete with s=15 and a coordinate that has run away to z=40. The clamp maps 40 → 15, fine; but its local derivative there is 0, so dloss/dz at that coordinate is (p − 1[target])·0 = 0 regardless of what the cross-entropy wants. That coordinate gets no learning signal at all through this loss-layer map — even though it is precisely the over-large logit I most want to pull back down, it is stuck behind a dead derivative. And it's not just the extreme outlier: any logit above 15 is in the dead zone. At z=20 the clamp derivative is also 0, at z=16 it's already 0; the cutoff is a cliff. Worse, there's a kink at ±s: the function isn't differentiable there, and Adam's per-coordinate statistics will see a discontinuous slope. This isn't hypothetical — I know from the stabilization literature that hard constraints can backfire. People have tried tightening *update* clipping hard enough to stabilize a large model and watched the quality fall off a cliff, far worse than the unstable baseline, while a smooth z-loss penalty on router logits stabilized the same model without that quality loss. The lesson generalizes: if I'm going to constrain the logits, the constraint must be smooth and must keep a nonzero gradient path everywhere, so that an over-large logit is still connected to the task gradient rather than abandoned behind a hard zero derivative. Hard clamp is the wrong shape.

There's also the soft-penalty route, which I should weigh because it's the one that most directly targets the precision story. Instead of constraining the logits structurally, add a term to the loss that *prefers* small logits: a penalty on the log-partition, L_z = (1/B) Σ (logsumexp(z))², added as L_CE + c_z·L_z with a small coefficient. Since logsumexp(z) ≈ max_j z_j when the logits are peaked, squaring and penalizing it pushes the whole vector toward small magnitude — straight at the "large numbers into exp" failure. This works; it's known to stabilize big models with essentially no quality cost. But it has properties I don't love for this slot. It introduces another hyperparameter, the coefficient, to balance against the main loss. Its gradient is mixed into every step everywhere, an extra force on every logit rather than a structural guarantee. It only makes logits small *on average* — it enforces no actual bound on any single logit, so a particularly excited coordinate can still spike. And it changes the loss *value*: the number I'd report is L_CE + c_z·L_z, not the modeling loss, which is awkward when the metric I'm graded on is the clean cross-entropy. A penalty nudges; I'd rather have a structural bound that's part of the forward map itself and leaves the loss a pure cross-entropy of a transformed logit.

So I want: a transformation g applied to the logits before the softmax/cross-entropy, that (1) bounds their range so they can't run away or blow up the low-precision exp, (2) is smooth with a nonzero gradient everywhere so no coordinate goes dead, and (3) is strictly monotone so it never reorders the logits and the model's preferred token is preserved. Let me take those three as hard constraints and see what's left. A function that maps the whole real line into a bounded interval, smoothly, and is strictly increasing throughout — bounded above and below, monotone, no flat spots — has to level off at both ends without ever turning around. That's the shape of an S-curve, a squashing function. The first one I'd reach for is the hyperbolic tangent. Let me check it against the three requirements one at a time rather than wave at it. Bounded: tanh: R → (−1, 1), so yes, its range is a finite interval. Smooth with live gradient: its derivative is 1 − tanh²(u), which is 1 at the origin and decreases toward 0 as |u|→∞ but is strictly positive for every finite u — so unlike clamp it never flattens to a hard zero slope on a finite region, every finite logit keeps a nonzero gradient path. Monotone: that same derivative 1 − tanh² is positive everywhere, so tanh is strictly increasing, and a strictly increasing map preserves order exactly. So tanh satisfies all three. It's not the unique such function, but it's a clean one I can compute and differentiate cheaply, so I'll build on it.

But tanh alone lands in (−1, 1), which is far too tight — I don't want to crush every logit into a unit interval, that would destroy all the useful spread. I want to cap at some scale s, say 30, not at 1. So scale the output: s·tanh(·). And I have to think about what goes *inside*. The naive thing is s·tanh(z), and I almost just write that down — but let me check its slope at the origin before I commit, because the slope at 0 is what the map does to normal-sized logits. d/dz [s·tanh(z)] = s·(1−tanh²(z)), which at z=0 is s. With s=15 that's a slope of 15 (I differentiated s·tanh(z) at 0 symbolically to be sure: it returns 15). So a small logit doesn't pass through — it gets *amplified* fifteenfold before the tanh starts to bend: a logit of 0.1 comes out near 0.1·15 = 1.5, a healthy gap of half a nat gets blown up to ~7. That's backwards; it's distorting exactly the normal operating range I wanted to leave alone. And this isn't a strawman I invented — the earliest use of tanh logit clipping I know, in a pointer-network RL setting, used softmax(C·tanh(u)) where C "controls the range of the logits and hence the entropy" of the attention distribution; they clipped to [−10,10] and it helped exploration. But notice their *purpose*: they wanted a temperature/entropy knob, a dial that sharpens or flattens the whole distribution, and the slope-C-at-the-origin amplification is a feature for that — it's an entropy controller. My purpose is the opposite. I want the transformation to be the *identity* on healthy, normal-sized logits and to bend only the large outliers. So s·tanh(z) is the right family but the wrong parameterization for me; I need slope 1 at the origin, not slope s.

The fix is to divide by s *inside* the tanh as well: g(z) = s·tanh(z/s). Now d/dz [s·tanh(z/s)] = s·(1/s)·(1−tanh²(z/s)) = 1−tanh²(z/s), which is exactly 1 at z=0 and decays to 0 for large |z|. For small z, tanh(z/s) ≈ z/s, so g(z) ≈ s·(z/s) = z — the map is the identity for logits small compared to s. And for large z, tanh(z/s) → ±1, so g(z) → ±s — a smooth, asymptotic cap at ±s. Let me actually run this on a concrete logit vector with s=15 to see it do what I claim, rather than trust the asymptotics. Take z = (1.2, −0.5, 8.0, 40.0, 0.3): the 40.0 is a runaway, and index 3 is the argmax. After g: (1.197, −0.500, 7.319, 14.856, 0.300). The small logit 1.2 came out 1.197, essentially untouched (identity, as wanted); the runaway 40.0 was pulled down to 14.856, just under the cap of 15; and the argmax is still index 3 — the order is preserved, the model's preferred token didn't change. That's the behavior the three requirements asked for, demonstrated on a real vector. The range is guaranteed too: g(z) ∈ (−s, +s) for every real z since tanh ∈ (−1,1), so the logits feeding the exp can never exceed s in magnitude, which kills the low-precision roundoff catastrophe. The /s inside is the whole difference between "an entropy knob that reshapes everything" and "an identity-plus-soft-ceiling that only touches outliers." This is a soft cap.

Let me pin the gradient down precisely and compare it against clamp's at the same points, because the dead gradient is the exact reason I rejected clamp and I should confirm the soft cap actually fixes it rather than assume it. With z̃ = g(z) = s·tanh(z/s), the chain rule gives dz̃/dz = 1 − tanh²(z/s) = 1 − (z̃/s)². Now line the two derivatives up at the logit values I used for the clamp failure, s=15: at z=0, softcap slope 1.000 vs clamp 1.0; at z=5, softcap 0.897 vs clamp 1.0; at z=16, softcap 0.379 vs clamp 0.0; at z=20, softcap 0.243 vs clamp 0.0; at z=40, softcap 0.019 vs clamp 0.0. There's the whole story in five numbers. Where clamp has already gone to a hard 0 (z=16, 20, 40) the soft cap still carries a strictly positive slope — 0.379, 0.243, 0.019 — so the over-large coordinate that clamp abandoned still has a live gradient path under the soft cap. It's small at z=40, as it should be (the logit is deep in saturation), but it's not zero: if the cross-entropy gradient wants that coordinate lower it can still move it. And the soft-cap column has no jump anywhere — it slides 1.000 → 0.897 → 0.379 → 0.243 → 0.019 continuously, where clamp jumps 1.0 → 0.0 at the boundary. So no dead zone and no kink. The profile is a smooth bell, 1 in the middle and tapering at the tails — exactly the "fully train the normal ones, softly saturate the outliers" behavior the three requirements demanded, and the over-large coordinate that clamp left stranded is not stranded here.

What about the cap value s? For the final output logits over the vocabulary, I want s large enough that the healthy operating range of logit gaps is essentially untouched (so I'm not throwing away modeling capacity) but small enough to actually bound the exp inputs. A value on the order of a few tens does that — a final-logit cap of 30 leaves normal gaps of a handful of nats completely in the identity regime while clamping the runaway tails. If I were also capping attention pre-softmax scores, those live on a different, typically larger scale, so they use a looser cap, 50. In this slot I only have the final logits feeding the cross-entropy, so the final-layer cap is the one I care about. The exact number isn't sacred; what's sacred is that s sits comfortably above the normal logit range and comfortably below where bfloat16 exp starts to hurt.

Now I have a clean method: z̃ = s·tanh(z/s), then cross-entropy on z̃. Let me write it and then look hard at whether I can do better numerically, because I'm going to run this transformation on a (B·T, V) tensor with V in the tens of thousands every single step, and the cross-entropy right after it, so the cost and the intermediate tensors matter. The straightforward form materializes the full capped logit tensor z̃, then F.cross_entropy materializes the log-softmax over it. tanh itself is exp-based — tanh(u) = (e^u − e^{−u})/(e^u + e^{−u}) = 1 − 2/(e^{2u}+1) — so each tanh is essentially a sigmoid in disguise. That nudges me: is there an equivalent form built directly on the sigmoid that's cheaper or folds more cleanly into a fused cap-plus-cross-entropy kernel?

There is, and it's exact, not an approximation. The identity is tanh(u) = 2σ(2u) − 1, where σ is the logistic sigmoid — let me verify it so I trust it. σ(2u) = 1/(1+e^{−2u}); 2σ(2u) − 1 = 2/(1+e^{−2u}) − 1 = (2 − 1 − e^{−2u})/(1+e^{−2u}) = (1 − e^{−2u})/(1+e^{−2u}), and tanh(u) = (e^u − e^{−u})/(e^u + e^{−u}) = (1 − e^{−2u})/(1 + e^{−2u}) after multiplying through by e^{−u}. They're equal. Good. So tanh is an affine reparameterization of the sigmoid, and a symmetric soft cap can be rewritten entirely in sigmoid terms. If I take the 15-cap variant, g(z) = 15·tanh(z/15) = 15·(2σ(2z/15) − 1) = 30·σ(z/7.5) − 15. So 15·tanh(z/15) and 30·σ(z/7.5) − 15 are the *same function*, exactly. The sigmoid version's range is (−15, +15) just like the tanh version — σ(z/7.5) runs over (0,1), times 30 is (0,30), minus 15 is (−15,15) — same bounded soft cap, written with one sigmoid and an affine wrap.

Why bother, if they're equal? Two reasons. First, the −15 constant: an additive constant on *all* logits is invisible to the softmax, because softmax(z + c·1)_i = e^{z_i+c}/Σ_j e^{z_j+c} = e^c e^{z_i}/(e^c Σ_j e^{z_j}) = softmax(z)_i — the e^c factors out of numerator and denominator and cancels. Let me confirm it numerically so I'm not fooling myself: softmax of (2.0, −1.0, 3.5, 0.0) is (0.176466, 0.008786, 0.790866, 0.023882), and softmax of the same vector minus 15, (−13, −16, −11.5, −15), is (0.176466, 0.008786, 0.790866, 0.023882) — identical to six places. So if every capped logit carries the same −15, the cross-entropy genuinely cannot see it, and I can drop it. Pointwise, 30·σ(z/7.5) is the symmetric 15-cap shifted upward by 15; as a softmax input it is cross-entropy-equivalent to 15·tanh(z/15). The whole thing collapses to a single sigmoid times a scale. Second, the implementation: when I fuse the cap with the cross-entropy in one kernel, the sigmoid form lets me compute the capped value, the log-sum-exp, and the per-target term in one pass over the vocabulary without ever materializing a separate capped-logit tensor — exactly the kind of memory-traffic saving that matters at V in the tens of thousands. The cap and the loss become one fused operation instead of cap-then-loss with an intermediate the size of the logits.

Let me also nail the gradient in the sigmoid parameterization, both to confirm it's still the right shape and because I'll need it for the fused backward. With z̃ = A·σ(u), u = (z + B)/C — I'm now allowing a general affine inside, A the output scale, C the input scale, B a shift — the derivative is dz̃/dz = A·σ'(u)·(1/C) = (A/C)·σ(u)(1 − σ(u)) = (1/C)·z̃·(1 − σ(u)), equivalently (1/C)·z̃·(1 − z̃/A). It's a bell again: zero in both tails (σ(1−σ) → 0 as u → ±∞), peaked at the midpoint u=0 where σ=1/2 with height A/(4C). Same everywhere-positive-but-vanishing-at-the-extremes profile as the tanh slope, which is the property I care about — outliers stay softly connected to the task gradient, the central region keeps a smooth usable slope, nothing is behind a dead derivative, and there is no kink. The parameters A, B, C are just the scale, shift, and slope of the sigmoid; A sets the cap height (the range becomes (0, A)), C the steepness (how quickly it saturates), and B moves the inflection point to z=−B.

So why would I shift B off zero and re-tune A away from the clean (A, B, C) = (30, 0, 7.5) that came straight from the s=15 tanh? Because once I've accepted that the cap is a bounded, smooth, strictly-monotone squashing of the logits — and that a global additive output constant washes out of the softmax while the *shape* of the saturation matters — the exact constants are knobs for this particular regime: a short nanoGPT run, this model size, this vocabulary, bfloat16. The constraints that are load-bearing are the qualitative ones: bounded range (so the exp is safe), strict monotonicity (so the token ranking is preserved), smooth positive gradient for every finite input (so training stays healthy). A re-tune in this family is A = 23, B = 5, C = 7.5. Before I trust it I should check it still satisfies those three, because shifting B and shrinking A could in principle break something. Range: A·σ(u) runs over (0, A) = (0, 23) as u sweeps the real line, so the exp inputs are bounded by 23 — confirmed by evaluating the limits, 23·σ(z/7.5+...) → 0 as z→−∞ and → 23 as z→+∞. The maximum slope is A/(4C) = 23/30 = 0.7667, and it occurs at u=0, i.e. z = −B = −5; evaluating the derivative (1/C)·z̃·(1 − z̃/A) on a grid, it peaks at 0.7667 right at z=−5 and is positive across the whole operating range, only vanishing asymptotically in the deep tails (same as the tanh slope — never identically zero for finite z). Positive slope everywhere means strictly increasing, so the argmax is preserved. So all three hold for the tuned curve. Note this is *not* pointwise identical to 15·tanh(z/15), nor is it just that curve plus a softmax-invisible constant — the shifted midpoint (z=−5 instead of 0) and smaller height (23 instead of 30) make it a different, tuned member of the same bounded-monotone family, which is fine: what I needed to preserve was the three qualitative properties, and those I just checked.

Let me sanity-check that this transformation really is a faithful modeling loss and not a sneaky distortion. A temperature scaling would be z ↦ z/τ, a *linear* squeeze that uniformly flattens or sharpens every gap and can mechanically shift the loss without the model getting better. My soft cap is not that. It's a *nonlinear* monotone map whose local slope depends on where a logit sits and whose tails saturate; it doesn't uniformly rescale gaps, it specifically refuses to let any single logit run to infinity. The cross-entropy is then computed honestly on the capped logits — it is the true negative log-likelihood of the model whose output head is "linear layer followed by the soft cap." The cap is part of the model's forward map, so the loss is the genuine modeling loss of that better-behaved model, not a cosmetic rescaling of an unchanged model's distribution. That's the line I have to stay on, and the soft cap stays on it: it changes the model, not just the number.

The code now has one job: apply the strictly increasing bounded squash to the logits and then call plain cross-entropy. I cast the logits to float32 before the sigmoid so the saturating exp runs in high precision — the whole point was numerical safety, so I do the exp-bearing part in the higher precision; in the fused path the kernel can cast each loaded value to float32 before evaluating the sigmoid and log-sum-exp the same way. I keep ignore_index = −1 for padded positions, matching the existing harness. This fills the one open slot, the body of compute_loss:

```python
import torch
import torch.nn.functional as F


def compute_loss(logits, targets):
    """Next-token cross-entropy with a smooth soft cap on the final logits.

    Each logit is passed through a strictly-increasing, bounded squashing map
    before the softmax/cross-entropy, so logits cannot run away (bounding the
    low-precision exp) and the token ranking is preserved (argmax unchanged),
    while the gradient stays smooth and nonzero everywhere.
    """
    # Tuned sigmoid soft cap. The exact tanh identity for the symmetric 15-cap is
    # 15*tanh(z/15) = 30*sigmoid(z/7.5) - 15; the -15 term is invisible to
    # softmax. A, B, C below are a retune, not a pointwise-identical tanh curve.
    A, B, C = 23.0, 5.0, 7.5
    capped_logits = A * torch.sigmoid((logits.float() + B) / C)
    return F.cross_entropy(
        capped_logits.view(-1, capped_logits.size(-1)),
        targets.view(-1),
        ignore_index=-1,
    )
```

And I'll keep in my back pocket the symmetric tanh form, the version I'd reach for when I want a centered cap around zero or when capping attention scores too:

```python
def softcap(logits, cap):
    # logits <- cap * tanh(logits / cap): identity slope at 0, smooth ceiling at +/-cap
    return cap * torch.tanh(logits / cap)

def compute_loss_tanh(logits, targets, final_cap=30.0):
    z = softcap(logits.float(), final_cap)
    return F.cross_entropy(z.view(-1, z.size(-1)), targets.view(-1), ignore_index=-1)
```

When I want it fused — cap and cross-entropy in one pass over the vocabulary without materializing the capped tensor — the sigmoid form is what makes that clean: for each row compute z̃_j = A·σ((z_j + B)/C), accumulate the log-sum-exp over j online, read off the target term lse − z̃_y, and in the backward use the gradient I derived, dz̃/dz = (1/C)·z̃·(1 − σ(u)), times the usual softmax-minus-onehot, p_j − 1[j=y]. That's exactly the cap-aware cross-entropy gradient, no autograd graph for the intermediate tensor needed.

So the chain closes. The plain cross-entropy I started with has no finite minimizer and actively drives the logits to grow without bound, which is over-confidence by design and, through the exponentiated softmax in bfloat16, a direct cause of roundoff-driven instability — larger logits, larger absolute roundoff, multiplicatively distorted probabilities. Softening the target (label smoothing) removes one divergent incentive but leaves the magnitudes and the precision problem untouched and changes the evaluated objective; a soft global penalty on the log-partition (z-loss) nudges magnitudes down on average but adds a coefficient, mixes a force into every step, and perturbs the loss value without guaranteeing any per-logit bound. A hard clamp would bound the logits but blocks the gradient of every out-of-range coordinate and adds a kink; related hard update clipping can stabilize while damaging quality. What survives all three requirements — bounded range, smooth positive gradient for every finite input, strict monotonicity — is to pass the logits through a scaled tanh, s·tanh(z/s), whose /s-inside makes it the identity on healthy logits and a smooth asymptotic cap at ±s on the runaways, with a gradient 1−(z̃/s)² that's full in the middle and tapers without a dead interval. Rewriting the s=15 version through tanh(u)=2σ(2u)−1 gives the exact identity 15·tanh(z/15)=30·σ(z/7.5)−15, and the −15 is invisible to softmax. The implemented sigmoid cap keeps that bounded monotone shape but retunes the constants to 23·σ((z+5)/7.5), which is cheaper, folds into a fused cap-plus-cross-entropy kernel, and is not pointwise identical to the symmetric tanh curve. The result is an honest cross-entropy of a better-behaved output head rather than a cosmetic rescaling of the same distribution. The objective at the loss boundary stops asking the logits to diverge and starts capping them smoothly, in place, as part of the model's forward map.
