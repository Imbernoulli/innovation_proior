I want the largest power of $2$ dividing $3^{2024}-1$, which means I want the exact exponent $e$ with $2^e \mid 3^{2024}-1$ and $2^{e+1}\nmid 3^{2024}-1$, and then the answer is $2^e$. Writing out $3^{2024}-1$ is hopeless — it has roughly a thousand digits — and in any case I do not care about the number, only about how many times $2$ divides it. So the right move is to name that quantity and manipulate it directly: for a prime $p$ and a nonzero integer $x$, let $v_p(x)$ be the $p$-adic valuation, the largest $k$ with $p^k\mid x$. I want $v_2(3^{2024}-1)$. The obvious handles all fall short. The factorization $a^n-1=(a-1)(a^{n-1}+\cdots+1)$ peels off one factor of $2$ from $a-1=2$, but leaves a sum of $2024$ odd terms — even, hence carrying more $2$s — that does not visibly simplify. Direct computation modulo growing powers of $2$ is just the giant number in disguise. What is missing is a law that makes the exponent $2024$ appear *inside* the valuation rather than inside the number, so that counting reduces to arithmetic on $a$, $b$, and $n$.

I propose the Lifting the Exponent lemma (LTE), which does exactly that: it expresses $v_p(a^n-b^n)$ (and $v_p(a^n+b^n)$) in terms of $v_p$ of the base difference (or sum) and $v_p$ of the exponent alone. The clean form holds for odd primes, and $p=2$ needs one structural correction, which is precisely the case the problem lands in. The derivation has two load-bearing ideas. The first is the base case: from $a^n-b^n=(a-b)\sum_{i=0}^{n-1}a^{n-1-i}b^i$, when $p\mid a-b$ we have $a\equiv b\pmod p$, so the cofactor is $\equiv n\,b^{n-1}\pmod p$, a unit times $n$; hence when $p\nmid n$ all the $p$ sits in $a-b$ and $v_p(a^n-b^n)=v_p(a-b)$. The second is the lifting step: replacing the exponent $m$ by $pm$ multiplies $a^m-b^m$ by the cyclotomic quotient
$$Q=\frac{a^{pm}-b^{pm}}{a^m-b^m}=\sum_{j=0}^{p-1}a^{m(p-1-j)}b^{mj}.$$
Setting $D=a^m-b^m$ with $p\mid D$ and expanding each term to first order in $D$ via $a^m=b^m+D$,
$$Q\equiv p\,b^{m(p-1)}+\frac{p(p-1)}{2}b^{m(p-2)}D\pmod{D^2}.$$
For an *odd* prime $\frac{p-1}{2}$ is an integer, so the second term is divisible by $p\cdot p$ (one $p$ from the factor, one from $p\mid D$), and the error is divisible by $D^2$ hence by $p^2$; meanwhile $b^{m(p-1)}$ is a unit mod $p$. Thus $Q$ is divisible by $p$ exactly once. So each factor of $p$ in the exponent lifts $v_p$ by exactly one, and writing $n=p^\ell m$ with $p\nmid m$ and inducting gives
$$v_p(a^n-b^n)=v_p(a-b)+v_p(n)\qquad(p\text{ odd},\ p\mid a-b,\ p\nmid a,b).$$
The companion sum law drops out for free: for odd $n$, $a^n+b^n=a^n-(-b)^n$ with $p\mid a-(-b)=a+b$, so $v_p(a^n+b^n)=v_p(a+b)+v_p(n)$ when $p\mid a+b$. Oddness of $n$ is essential there, since $(-b)^n=-b^n$ only for odd $n$.

The design choice that makes LTE work at all is to track $Q$ to *second order* — to know $Q$ is divisible by $p$ but not $p^2$ — rather than merely noting $p\mid Q$. The obvious alternative, observing that each of the $p$ terms of $Q$ is $\equiv1\pmod p$ so $Q\equiv p\equiv0$, only shows $Q$ donates *at least* one $p$; it never bounds the donation from above, and without the upper bound the induction cannot pin the valuation to an exact value. The first-order expansion in $D$ supplies exactly that upper bound. This is also precisely where $p=2$ breaks: the "$\times2$ in the exponent" quotient is just $\frac{a^{2m}-b^{2m}}{a^m-b^m}=a^m+b^m$, a sum of two odd numbers, which is always even and carries its own $2$-adic weight depending on $a^m\bmod 4,\,8,\dots$ — and the discarded $\frac{p(p-1)}{2}\cdots$ term no longer vanishes because $\frac{p-1}{2}=\frac12$ is not an integer. So the per-step "adds exactly one" count is simply wrong at $2$, and I cannot patch the odd-prime argument; I have to compute the $2$-content of the doubling factors head-on.

The way to expose where the $2$s live is to peel $a^n-b^n$ by repeated difference of squares rather than by a single cyclotomic quotient. Writing $n=2^s t$ with $t$ odd and $s=v_2(n)\ge1$, iterating $X^2-Y^2=(X-Y)(X+Y)$ gives
$$a^n-b^n=(a^t-b^t)(a^t+b^t)\prod_{j=1}^{s-1}\bigl(a^{2^j t}+b^{2^j t}\bigr).$$
Now I evaluate each factor's valuation exactly. Since $t$ is odd, the quotients $(a^t-b^t)/(a-b)$ and $(a^t+b^t)/(a+b)$ are each sums of $t$ odd terms, hence odd, so $v_2(a^t-b^t)=v_2(a-b)$ and $v_2(a^t+b^t)=v_2(a+b)$. For each $j\ge1$ the exponent $2^j t$ is even, so $a^{2^j t}$ and $b^{2^j t}$ are odd perfect squares, each $\equiv1\pmod 8$ and so $\equiv1\pmod4$; their sum is $\equiv2\pmod4$, divisible by $2$ but not $4$, contributing exactly $v_2=1$. There are $s-1$ such factors ($j=1,\dots,s-1$). Summing,
$$v_2(a^n-b^n)=v_2(a-b)+v_2(a+b)+(s-1)=v_2(a-b)+v_2(a+b)+v_2(n)-1\qquad(a,b\text{ odd},\ n\text{ even}).$$
The extra $v_2(a+b)$ channel — absent for odd primes, where no analogous "$a+b$" doubling factor exists — and the $-1$ are exactly what the naive lift was missing. They do real work only when $2\,\|\,(a-b)$; when $4\mid a-b$ one has $a+b=(a-b)+2b\equiv2\pmod4$, so $v_2(a+b)=1$ and the law collapses to $v_2(a-b)+v_2(n)$, the ordinary odd-prime shape, which is why that case is usually stated separately. For odd $n$ the difference reduces to $v_2(a^n-b^n)=v_2(a-b)$, and the $2$-adic sum law splits as $v_2(a^n+b^n)=v_2(a+b)$ for odd $n$ (apply the odd-difference case to $a,-b$) and $v_2(a^n+b^n)=1$ for even $n$ (both $a^n,b^n\equiv1\pmod8$, so the sum is $\equiv2\pmod8$).

The anchor problem is now one substitution. Take $a=3$, $b=1$ (both odd), $n=2024$ (even). Here $a-b=2$, so $2\,\|\,(a-b)$ and not $4\mid a-b$ — the genuinely special case where the correction matters. With $v_2(a-b)=v_2(2)=1$, $v_2(a+b)=v_2(4)=2$, and $v_2(2024)=3$ since $2024=2^3\cdot253$ with $253=11\cdot23$ odd,
$$v_2(3^{2024}-1)=v_2(3-1)+v_2(3+1)+v_2(2024)-1=1+2+3-1=5.$$
So $2^5\mid 3^{2024}-1$ and $2^6\nmid 3^{2024}-1$: the largest power of $2$ dividing $3^{2024}-1$ is $2^5=32$.

The finished lemma, in all cases, for nonzero integers $a,b$ and $n\ge1$ with the displayed expression nonzero. For an odd prime $p$ with $p\nmid a$ and $p\nmid b$: if $p\mid a-b$ then
$$v_p(a^n-b^n)=v_p(a-b)+v_p(n)\quad\text{for all }n\ge1,$$
and if $p\mid a+b$ then
$$v_p(a^n+b^n)=v_p(a+b)+v_p(n)\quad\text{for odd }n.$$
For $p=2$ with $a,b$ odd: the difference with $n$ odd is $v_2(a^n-b^n)=v_2(a-b)$; the difference with $n$ even is
$$v_2(a^n-b^n)=v_2(a-b)+v_2(a+b)+v_2(n)-1,$$
which reduces to $v_2(a-b)+v_2(n)$ when $4\mid a-b$; the sum with $n$ odd is $v_2(a^n+b^n)=v_2(a+b)$; and the sum with $n$ even is $v_2(a^n+b^n)=1$. Applied to $a=3,\,b=1,\,n=2024$, this gives $v_2(3^{2024}-1)=5$ and the answer $\boxed{2^5=32}$.
