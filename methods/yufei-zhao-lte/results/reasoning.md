I want the largest power of $2$ dividing $3^{2024}-1$. So I want the exact exponent $e$ with $2^e \mid 3^{2024}-1$ and $2^{e+1}\nmid 3^{2024}-1$. Computing $3^{2024}-1$ outright is absurd — that number has roughly a thousand digits. I only care about how many times $2$ divides it, nothing else about it. Let me give that quantity a name so I can manipulate it: for a prime $p$ and a nonzero integer $x$, let $v_p(x)$ be the number of times $p$ divides $x$, i.e. the largest $k$ with $p^k\mid x$. So I want $v_2(3^{2024}-1)$, and the answer to the problem is $2$ to that power.

The first reflex with $a^n-1$ is that it factors. $a^n-1=(a-1)(a^{n-1}+a^{n-2}+\cdots+a+1)$. With $a=3$, $a-1=2$, which already contributes a factor of $2$. But the cofactor $3^{2023}+\cdots+3+1$ is a sum of $2024$ odd terms, hence even, so it contributes more $2$s, and now I have to understand $v_2$ of that messy sum. That doesn't obviously simplify. Let me not commit to a formula yet and instead just look at what $v_2(3^n-1)$ does for small $n$, to feel out the pattern before trying to prove anything.

$n=1$: $3-1=2$, so $v_2=1$. $n=2$: $9-1=8$, $v_2=3$. $n=3$: $27-1=26=2\cdot13$, $v_2=1$. $n=4$: $81-1=80=16\cdot5$, $v_2=4$. $n=5$: $243-1=242=2\cdot121$, $v_2=1$. $n=6$: $729-1=728=8\cdot 91$, $v_2=3$. $n=8$: $6561-1=6560=32\cdot205$, $v_2=5$. Let me line these up. For odd $n$ ($1,3,5$) the valuation is stuck at $1$. For even $n$: $n=2\to3$, $n=4\to4$, $n=6\to3$, $n=8\to5$. So among even $n$ the valuation moves, and it seems to track how divisible $n$ itself is by $2$. Check: $v_2(2)=1$ and got $3$; $v_2(4)=2$ and got $4$; $v_2(6)=1$ and got $3$; $v_2(8)=3$ and got $5$. In every even case the answer is exactly $v_2(n)+2$. And the constant $2$ is suspicious: $v_2(3-1)=1$ and $v_2(3+1)=v_2(4)=2$, and $1+2=3$, hmm, or maybe the $2$ is $v_2(3+1)$ alone. I can't read off the right decomposition from $3$ alone because $3-1$ and a possible "$+1$" term are tangled. I'll come back to pin down what that constant really is; first I should understand the odd-prime version, where the picture is cleaner, and then see what's special about $2$.

So step back to a general prime $p$ and ask: when $p\mid a-1$, how does $v_p(a^n-1)$ grow with $n$? Take the factorization again, $a^n-1=(a-1)\cdot S$ where $S=a^{n-1}+\cdots+a+1$. If $p\mid a-1$ then $a\equiv1\pmod p$, so each term $a^j\equiv1$, and $S\equiv \underbrace{1+\cdots+1}_{n}=n\pmod p$. That's a clean fact: modulo $p$, the cofactor is just $n$. So if $p\nmid n$, then $p\nmid S$, and all the $p$ in $a^n-1$ comes from $a-1$: $v_p(a^n-1)=v_p(a-1)$. Good, that explains the odd-$n$ flatness above for $p=2$… except wait, for $p=2$ I need to be careful, because $S\equiv n\pmod 2$ only tells me $S$ is odd when $n$ is odd, which does give $v_2(a^n-1)=v_2(a-1)$ for odd $n$. That matches: $v_2(3^n-1)=1=v_2(3-1)$ for $n=1,3,5$. Fine. The interesting case is when $p\mid n$, because then $S\equiv0\pmod p$ and the cofactor donates extra factors of $p$, and I need to count exactly how many.

Let me isolate the mechanism that adds one factor of $p$ at a time. Suppose I know $v_p(a^m-1)$ and I replace $m$ by $pm$. Then
$$\frac{a^{pm}-1}{a^m-1}=a^{m(p-1)}+a^{m(p-2)}+\cdots+a^{m}+1.$$
This is a sum of $p$ terms. If $p\mid a-1$ then $a\equiv1$, so $a^m\equiv1$, so each of the $p$ terms is $\equiv1$, and the whole sum is $\equiv p\pmod p$ — i.e. divisible by $p$. So multiplying the exponent by $p$ multiplies $a^m-1$ by something divisible by $p$: at least one new factor of $p$ appears. The question is whether it's exactly one. I'd want this quotient to be divisible by $p$ but not $p^2$. Let me look harder. Write $a^m=1+pc$ where $c$ may carry more powers of $p$, but at least $a\equiv1\pmod p$ guarantees $a^m\equiv1\pmod p$ so $a^m-1$ is divisible by $p$; say $a^m-1$ is divisible by $p$ to some order, and consider the quotient modulo a higher power. Each term $a^{mj}=(1+(a^m-1))^j\equiv 1+j(a^m-1)\pmod{(a^m-1)^2}$. Summing $j=0,\dots,p-1$,
$$\frac{a^{pm}-1}{a^m-1}\equiv \sum_{j=0}^{p-1}\bigl(1+j(a^m-1)\bigr)=p+\frac{p(p-1)}{2}(a^m-1)\pmod{(a^m-1)^2}.$$
Now $a^m-1$ is divisible by $p$, so $(a^m-1)^2$ is divisible by $p^2$, and the congruence is in particular a congruence mod $p^2$. The first term is $p$. The second term is $\frac{p(p-1)}{2}(a^m-1)$. Here is where $p=2$ wants to misbehave: for an odd prime, $\frac{p-1}{2}$ is an integer, so this term is $p\cdot(p-1)/2\cdot(a^m-1)$, which is divisible by $p\cdot p=p^2$ because $p\mid a^m-1$. So mod $p^2$ the quotient is just $p$. That means the quotient is divisible by $p$ exactly once. So for an odd prime, each time I multiply the exponent by $p$, the valuation $v_p$ goes up by exactly $1$.

That gives me the odd-prime law by induction. Start from $n$ with $p\nmid n$: $v_p(a^n-1)=v_p(a-1)$, as shown. Now write a general $n=p^{\ell}m$ with $p\nmid m$. Going from exponent $m$ to $pm$ to $p^2m$ ... to $p^{\ell}m$, I apply the "$\times p$ in the exponent adds one to $v_p$" step exactly $\ell$ times, and $\ell=v_p(n)$. So
$$v_p(a^n-1)=v_p(a^m-1)+\ell=v_p(a-1)+v_p(n),$$
valid for any odd prime $p$ with $p\mid a-1$ and $p\nmid a$ (the latter is automatic since $a\equiv1$). And nothing about this used $b=1$ in any essential way; if I want $a^n-b^n$ with $p\mid a-b$ and $p\nmid a,b$, the base case comes from
$$a^n-b^n=(a-b)(a^{n-1}+a^{n-2}b+\cdots+b^{n-1}),$$
whose cofactor is $\equiv n b^{n-1}\pmod p$, a unit times $n$. For the lifting step I have to be a little more precise than saying every term is $1$. Let $D=a^m-b^m$, so $p\mid D$, and look at
$$Q=\frac{a^{pm}-b^{pm}}{a^m-b^m}=\sum_{j=0}^{p-1}a^{m(p-1-j)}b^{mj}.$$
Since $a^m=b^m+D$, expanding each term to first order in $D$ gives
$$Q\equiv p\,b^{m(p-1)}+\frac{p(p-1)}{2}b^{m(p-2)}D\pmod{D^2}.$$
The second term and the error are divisible by $p^2$, while $b^{m(p-1)}$ is a unit modulo $p$, so $Q$ is exactly divisible by one power of $p$. Same conclusion:
$$v_p(a^n-b^n)=v_p(a-b)+v_p(n)\qquad(p\text{ odd},\ p\mid a-b,\ p\nmid a,b).$$

While I'm here, the "$+$" companion drops out for free. If $p\mid a+b$ instead, then $b\equiv-a$, and for odd $n$, $a^n+b^n=a^n-(-b)^n$ with $p\mid a-(-b)=a+b$, so applying the minus-law to $a$ and $-b$,
$$v_p(a^n+b^n)=v_p(a+b)+v_p(n)\qquad(p\text{ odd},\ p\mid a+b,\ p\nmid a,b,\ n\text{ odd}).$$
The oddness of $n$ is essential: $(-b)^n=-b^n$ only for odd $n$, and indeed for even $n$ the quantity $a^n+b^n$ need not even be divisible by $p$.

Now back to the wall at $p=2$. The whole odd-prime argument leaned on $\frac{p(p-1)}{2}(a^m-1)$ being divisible by $p^2$. For $p=2$ the relevant "$\times2$ in the exponent" quotient is
$$\frac{a^{2m}-1}{a^m-1}=a^m+1.$$
That's the entire cyclotomic quotient — just $a^m+1$, two terms. And here's the trouble: $a$ is odd (I need $a$ odd for $2\mid a-1$ to do anything, since if $a$ is even $a^n-1$ is odd and there's nothing to count), so $a^m$ is odd, so $a^m+1$ is even. The quotient is always even, so it always donates at least one factor of $2$ — that part is fine, it's the source of the growth. But how many? Unlike the odd-prime case, the extra factor here is not pinned at exactly one by a clean $\frac{p-1}{2}$ integrality; the factor $a^m+1$ carries its own $2$-adic content that depends on $a^m\bmod 4$, on $8$, and so on, and the term I previously discarded does not vanish. So I can't just say "each doubling of the exponent adds one to $v_2$." The naive lift overcounts or undercounts depending on how $a$ sits modulo higher powers of $2$. I need to actually compute the $2$-content of these doubling-factors instead of waving at them.

So let me do it head-on. Take $n$ even and split off all its factors of $2$: write $n=2^{s}t$ with $t$ odd and $s=v_2(n)\ge1$. I'll peel the difference of powers all the way down using the difference-of-squares repeatedly:
$$a^{n}-1=a^{2^{s}t}-1=\bigl(a^{2^{s-1}t}-1\bigr)\bigl(a^{2^{s-1}t}+1\bigr)=\cdots,$$
and iterating,
$$a^{n}-1=(a^{t}-1)\,(a^{t}+1)\,(a^{2t}+1)\,(a^{4t}+1)\cdots(a^{2^{s-1}t}+1).$$
Let me sanity-check the bookkeeping: starting from $a^{2^s t}-1$, one difference-of-squares gives $(a^{2^{s-1}t}-1)(a^{2^{s-1}t}+1)$; expand the $-1$ factor again, $s$ times total, until I reach $a^{t}-1$. Along the way I generate the "$+1$" factors $a^{2^{s-1}t}+1,\ a^{2^{s-2}t}+1,\ \dots,\ a^{2t}+1,\ a^{t}+1$, which is one $+1$ factor for each of the $s$ peels: exponents $2^{j}t$ for $j=0,1,\dots,s-1$. So
$$v_2(a^n-1)=v_2(a^t-1)+v_2(a^t+1)+\sum_{j=1}^{s-1}v_2\!\left(a^{2^{j}t}+1\right).$$
Now each piece. First, $t$ is odd, so by the odd-$n$ flat case proved above, $v_2(a^t-1)=v_2(a-1)$. Symmetrically $a^t+1$: since $t$ is odd, $a^t+1=a^t-(-1)^t=a^t-(-1)$ — concretely $a^t+1$ and $a+1$ differ by $a+1$ times the integer $a^{t-1}-a^{t-2}+\cdots+1$, a sum of $t$ odd terms (odd count, odd terms) which is odd, so $v_2(a^t+1)=v_2(a+1)$. Good. Now the heart of it: for $j\ge1$, the exponent $2^{j}t$ is even, so $a^{2^{j}t}=(a^{2^{j-1}t})^2$ is an odd perfect square. An odd square is $\equiv1\pmod8$, so in particular $\equiv1\pmod4$. Therefore $a^{2^{j}t}+1\equiv2\pmod4$: divisible by $2$ but not by $4$. So each of those factors contributes exactly $v_2=1$. There are $s-1$ of them ($j=1$ to $s-1$). Putting it together,
$$v_2(a^n-1)=v_2(a-1)+v_2(a+1)+(s-1)=v_2(a-1)+v_2(a+1)+v_2(n)-1,$$
using $s=v_2(n)$, and this is for $n$ even, $a$ odd.

Let me make sure I believe the "$-1$" by testing it against the small data I already computed for $a=3$. $v_2(3-1)=v_2(2)=1$, $v_2(3+1)=v_2(4)=2$, so the formula predicts $v_2(3^n-1)=1+2+v_2(n)-1=v_2(n)+2$ for even $n$. Against the table, $n=2$ gives $3$, $n=4$ gives $4$, $n=6$ gives $3$, and $n=8$ gives $5$, exactly as before. So the mysterious constant I couldn't decompose earlier is exactly $v_2(a-1)+v_2(a+1)=1+2=3$, with the $-1$ folded in to give $2$. The split into a $(a-1)$ part and a separate $(a+1)$ part is what I was missing when staring at $3$ alone, and the extra $a+1$ term is precisely what the odd-prime law lacks — for odd $p$ there is no analogous "$a+1$" channel because the doubling factor $a^m+1$ only exists at $p=2$.

Two refinements before I trust this as the general law. The two-variable version: for odd $a,b$ and even $n$, the same difference-of-squares peeling on $a^n-b^n$ gives $(a^t-b^t)(a^t+b^t)\prod_{j\ge1}(a^{2^jt}+b^{2^jt})$. The first two valuations really are $v_2(a-b)$ and $v_2(a+b)$ because the quotients $(a^t-b^t)/(a-b)$ and $(a^t+b^t)/(a+b)$ are sums of $t$ odd terms, hence odd. Each later $a^{2^jt}+b^{2^jt}$ is a sum of two odd squares $\equiv1+1=2\pmod4$, contributing $1$. So
$$v_2(a^n-b^n)=v_2(a-b)+v_2(a+b)+v_2(n)-1\qquad(a,b\text{ odd},\ n\text{ even}).$$
And the degenerate edge: what if $4\mid a-b$? Then $a\equiv b\pmod4$, and since $a+b=(a-b)+2b\equiv2b\equiv2\pmod4$ (as $b$ is odd), we have $v_2(a+b)=1$, so the formula collapses to $v_2(a-b)+1+v_2(n)-1=v_2(a-b)+v_2(n)$ — exactly the clean odd-prime-shaped law, now holding at $p=2$ too. That's the reason people state the $4\mid a-b$ case separately: there the "$2$ is special" correction disappears and $2$ behaves like an ordinary prime. The genuinely special situation is $2\,\|\,(a-b)$, i.e. $a\equiv b\pmod2$ but not mod $4$, where the $v_2(a+b)$ term and the $-1$ are doing real work.

Collecting everything, here is the complete law. For an odd prime $p$ with $p\nmid a$, $p\nmid b$: if $p\mid a-b$ then $v_p(a^n-b^n)=v_p(a-b)+v_p(n)$ for all $n\ge1$; and if $p\mid a+b$ then $v_p(a^n+b^n)=v_p(a+b)+v_p(n)$ for odd $n$. For $p=2$ with $a,b$ odd: if $n$ is odd then $v_2(a^n-b^n)=v_2(a-b)$; if $n$ is even then $v_2(a^n-b^n)=v_2(a-b)+v_2(a+b)+v_2(n)-1$ (which reduces to $v_2(a-b)+v_2(n)$ when $4\mid a-b$). The sum at $p=2$ has its own quick split: for odd $n$, applying the odd-difference case to $a$ and $-b$ gives $v_2(a^n+b^n)=v_2(a+b)$; for even $n$, both $a^n$ and $b^n$ are $1\pmod8$, so $a^n+b^n\equiv2\pmod8$ and $v_2(a^n+b^n)=1$. This is the lifting-the-exponent law: the power of $p$ dividing a power difference is the power dividing the base difference, lifted by the power of $p$ in the exponent, with $2$ requiring the extra $a+b$ correction in the even-exponent difference case.

Now the original problem is a single substitution. I want $v_2(3^{2024}-1)$, so $a=3$, $b=1$, both odd, and $n=2024$. Is $4\mid a-b$? No: $a-b=2$, so $2\,\|\,(a-b)$ — the genuinely special case, good thing I derived the correction. Is $n$ even? Yes. So I use $v_2(a^n-b^n)=v_2(a-b)+v_2(a+b)+v_2(n)-1$. Compute the pieces: $v_2(a-b)=v_2(2)=1$; $v_2(a+b)=v_2(4)=2$; and $v_2(2024)$: $2024=2\cdot1012=4\cdot506=8\cdot253$, and $253=11\cdot23$ is odd, so $2024=2^3\cdot253$ and $v_2(2024)=3$. Therefore
$$v_2(3^{2024}-1)=1+2+3-1=5.$$
So $2^5\mid 3^{2024}-1$ and $2^6\nmid 3^{2024}-1$: the largest power of $2$ dividing $3^{2024}-1$ is $2^5=32$.

The whole route, in one breath: I only ever wanted the $2$-adic size of $3^{2024}-1$, so I named it $v_2$ and tried to make the exponent $2024$ visible inside the valuation rather than inside the giant number. The factorization $a^n-1=(a-1)\cdot(\text{cofactor}\equiv n)$ showed that for an odd prime the cofactor donates exactly one factor of $p$ per factor of $p$ in $n$, giving $v_p(a^n-b^n)=v_p(a-b)+v_p(n)$. At $p=2$ that clean per-step counting broke, because the doubling factor $a^m+1$ is itself even and carries its own weight; peeling $a^n-1$ by repeated difference of squares exposed exactly where the extra $2$s live — one in $a-1$, one in $a+1$, and one apiece in the $v_2(n)-1$ intermediate $+1$ factors — which is the $v_2(a-b)+v_2(a+b)+v_2(n)-1$ correction. Feeding $a=3,b=1,n=2024$ into it gives $5$, so the answer is $32$.
