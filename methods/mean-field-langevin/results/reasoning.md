I start with the mismatch between the statistical size of the problem and the behavior of ordinary training. The target is `g(Ux)` with only a fixed number of hidden directions, so on isotropic data I should not need more than order `d` samples just to locate the relevant subspace. But at a random initialization a neuron's overlap with a true direction is only order `d^{-1/2}`. If the first useful correlation term of the link has degree `s`, the population pull is of order `overlap^{s-1}`. For `s >= 3`, the gradient signal is so small that ordinary SGD spends a long search phase near the uninformative equator. In the multi-index setting the same obstruction appears as leap complexity: a direction may not become visible until earlier coordinates have been learned. So the first thing I need is not a better representation of `g`; the network can represent it. I need a training dynamics whose statistical guarantee is not controlled by that weak local gradient.

The permutation symmetry of a wide two-layer network is the opening. The network output is an average over neurons, so I can represent the first layer by the empirical measure `mu_W = (1/m) sum_j delta_{w_j}`. Then `yhat(x; mu) = integral Psi(x; w) dmu(w)` is linear in `mu`. With a convex loss in the prediction, the risk is convex as a functional of `mu`, even though it is non-convex in the finite matrix `W`. This does not yet give me a usable algorithmic theorem, but it changes what I am trying to optimize: I want a particle system that tracks descent in a convex space of measures, not a single trajectory trying to find a hidden direction by a tiny local signal.

A bare Wasserstein gradient flow of the risk is still not enough. I need a unique regularized minimizer and a convergence handle. Adding entropy gives both. The free energy is the empirical risk plus `(lambda/2) integral ||w||^2 dmu` plus `(1/beta) H(mu)`. In the Euclidean case, the quadratic penalty and entropy combine into a KL term against a Gaussian base measure `gamma` with covariance `(lambda beta)^{-1} I`: up to an additive constant, the regularizer is `(1/beta) H(mu | gamma)`. This is why the ordinary `L2` penalty is not a separate heuristic in the measure view. It is the Gaussian reference measure for the entropic problem.

The gradient flow of that free energy has exactly the form of a nonlinear Langevin diffusion. The density equation is a drift term from the first variation plus a `beta^{-1}` Laplacian, and the particle SDE is `dw_t = -grad J'[mu_t](w_t) dt + sqrt(2/beta) dB_t`. The sign is important: the drift is negative gradient of the first variation, and the diffusion coefficient is `sqrt(2 beta^{-1})`. When I discretize a time step of length `eta`, Brownian motion contributes a standard Gaussian multiplied by `sqrt(2 eta / beta)`. That is the Euler-Maruyama noise constant.

Now I need to match the finite network scaling. The empirical predictor averages over `m` neurons, and the regularizer is also averaged over `m`, so the gradient with respect to one neuron's weight is order `1/m`. The measure-space drift is order one per particle. Therefore the finite-particle update multiplies the ordinary gradient by `m`: `w_j^{l+1} = w_j^l - m eta grad_{w_j} Jhat_lambda(W) + sqrt(2 eta / beta) xi_j^l`. Expanding the regularizer confirms the sign and constant: since `Jhat_lambda` contains `(lambda/2m) sum_j ||w_j||^2`, the scaled regularizer gradient contributes `- eta lambda w_j`. So the update is data-gradient descent, weight decay, and Gaussian parameter noise.

The convergence proof is controlled by a proximal Gibbs law. If I freeze the current measure in the first variation, the relevant density is proportional to `exp(-beta J'[mu])` with respect to the base measure. A log-Sobolev inequality for these Gibbs laws gives the free-energy contraction `exp(-2t/(beta C_LSI))`. The signs line up: the Gibbs density has `-beta` times the first variation, the flow dissipates KL to that Gibbs law, and the factor `2` in the exponent comes from the standard LSI normalization `H <= (C_LSI/2) I`.

In Euclidean space the LSI constant is the dangerous part. The first variation includes the quadratic `(lambda/2)||w||^2`, so the Gaussian reference measure has LSI constant `1/(beta lambda)`. The data-fit part is a bounded perturbation because the activation is bounded by `iota` and the loss derivative is bounded by `C_rho`; the first variation oscillation is at most `4 C_rho iota`, producing `C_LSI <= (beta lambda)^{-1} exp(4 C_rho iota beta)` by Holley-Stroock. That exponential in `beta` is the compute price.

The statistical part uses the effective dimension. I use the scaled convention where `U` has rows `u_i^T / sqrt(k)`, so `r_x = ||Sigma^{1/2} U^T||_F` and `d_eff = tr(Sigma) / r_x^2`. On isotropic inputs, `r_x^2 = 1`, so `d_eff = d`, not `d/k` under this convention. The theorem sets `lambda = lambda_tilde r_x^2` and `beta` on the order of `d_eff / lambda_tilde` up to logs and accuracy factors. With that choice, the empirical regularized minimizer generalizes with `n = Otilde(d_eff)`. The link's information or leap exponent has moved out of the sample complexity; the remaining bad dependence is in compute through the LSI constant.

I also need to keep the Riemannian part in its proper lane. Constraining the weight space to a positively curved compact manifold, such as the sphere, removes the Euclidean quadratic confinement and allows a Bakry-Emery LSI bound of the form `C_LSI <= (rho d - beta C_rho K)^{-1}` when `beta < rho d/(C_rho K)`. This is polynomial-looking, but it is conditional on a compact-manifold entropy assumption: there must be a good target measure with empirical risk at most `bar_epsilon` and entropy at most `bar_Delta` against the uniform measure. For fixed-`k` multi-index models, matching `bar_Delta` to the Euclidean `d_eff` in this Riemannian setting remains open. So I should not present spherical projection as the proven finite-time algorithm for the general fixed-`k` theorem. It is a route under additional spread assumptions, not the main Euclidean guarantee.

The architecture has another subtlety. In the main Euclidean theory, the second layer is fixed at `+1`; signed functions are represented by making one neuron parameter contain two halves, `w = (omega_1, omega_2)`, and defining `Psi(x; w) = phi(<x_tilde, omega_1>) - phi(<x_tilde, omega_2>)`. This is equivalent in a scalar-neuron implementation to using signed second-layer weights, half positive and half negative, but the theoretical object is a measure over paired neurons with all outer weights fixed. The bias coordinate is also scaled as `x_tilde = (x, r_tilde_x)` in the proof, while the notebook appends `1` for experiments.

Finally I check the reference code. The official notebook computes the gradient as two factors scaled by `1/sqrt(n)`, so their product is the usual mean squared-loss gradient. It initializes rows on the sphere, uses signed second-layer weights `+1/m` and `-1/m`, appends a bias coordinate in the effective-dimension experiment, and updates `W = W - lr*m*grad - lr*weight_decay*W + 2*sqrt(lr*inv_temp)*randn_like(W)`. If `inv_temp = beta^{-1}`, that notebook noise is `sqrt(4 lr / beta)`, a factor `sqrt(2)` larger than the Euler-Maruyama coefficient `sqrt(2 lr / beta)`. The deliverable has to preserve this distinction: the derivation uses Eq. 10, while a code-faithful reproduction of the notebook uses its `2*sqrt(lr*inv_temp)` coefficient.

Putting it together, the method is noisy, width-scaled, weight-decayed first-layer training justified by convex optimization over neuron distributions. It reaches sample complexity `Otilde(d_eff)` for general locally Lipschitz multi-index links under the Euclidean theorem, while its worst-case width and iteration bounds are exponential in `d_eff`. The compact Riemannian analysis explains how positive curvature can remove the exponential LSI dependence under an additional entropy-spread assumption, but it does not erase the open question for fixed-`k` multi-index targets.
