I start with the mismatch between the statistical size of the problem and the behavior of ordinary training. The target is `g(Ux)` with only a fixed number of hidden directions, so on isotropic data I should not need more than order `d` samples just to locate the relevant subspace. But at a random initialization a neuron's overlap with a true direction is only order `d^{-1/2}`. If the first useful correlation term of the link has degree `s`, the population pull is of order `overlap^{s-1}`. For `s >= 3`, the gradient signal is so small that ordinary SGD spends a long search phase near the uninformative equator. In the multi-index setting the same obstruction appears as leap complexity: a direction may not become visible until earlier coordinates have been learned. So the first thing I need is not a better representation of `g`; the network can already represent it. What I need is a training dynamics whose sample complexity is not throttled by that weak local gradient.

The permutation symmetry of a wide two-layer network is the opening I want to exploit. The network output is an average over neurons, so I can represent the first layer by the empirical measure `mu_W = (1/m) sum_j delta_{w_j}`. Then `yhat(x; mu) = integral Psi(x; w) dmu(w)` is linear in `mu`. With a convex pointwise loss, the risk is convex as a functional of `mu`, even though it is non-convex in the finite matrix `W`. By itself this is just a re-description and gives no algorithm. But it changes the object I optimize: instead of one trajectory in `R^{m d}` hunting a hidden direction through a tiny local signal, I can think about descent of a convex functional on the space of measures, where there is no spurious local structure to get trapped in.

A bare Wasserstein gradient flow of the risk is still not enough to pin down a target and a rate. I want a unique regularized minimizer and a contraction handle. Adding entropy gives both. The free energy I will descend is the empirical risk plus `(lambda/2) integral ||w||^2 dmu` plus `(1/beta) H(mu)`. The reason this particular combination is natural, and not two separate heuristics, is that the quadratic penalty and the entropy fuse into a single KL term against a Gaussian base measure. Let me actually check this rather than assert it. With `gamma = N(0, (lambda beta)^{-1} I)` in `R^D`, write out `beta^{-1} H(mu | gamma) = beta^{-1} integral mu log(mu/gamma)`. The Gaussian log-density is `log gamma(w) = (D/2) log(lambda beta / 2pi) - (lambda beta / 2) ||w||^2`, so

```text
beta^{-1} H(mu|gamma)
  = beta^{-1} integral mu log mu
    - beta^{-1} integral mu [ (D/2) log(lambda beta/2pi) - (lambda beta/2)||w||^2 ]
  = beta^{-1} H(mu|Leb) + (lambda/2) integral ||w||^2 dmu  -  (D/(2 beta)) log(lambda beta/2pi).
```

The last term is independent of `mu`. Rearranged, `(lambda/2) integral ||w||^2 dmu + beta^{-1} H(mu|Leb) = beta^{-1} H(mu|gamma) + const`. I sanity-checked this numerically on a one-dimensional Gaussian `mu = N(0.4, 0.9^2)` with `lambda = 0.7`, `beta = 1.3`: the left side evaluated to `-0.6711` and `beta^{-1} H(mu|gamma) + const` to `-0.6710`, agreeing to Monte-Carlo error. So weight decay is not an add-on in the measure view â€” it is exactly the Gaussian reference measure for the entropic problem, and the quadratic confinement is what makes that reference a proper probability measure.

The gradient flow of this free energy has the form of a nonlinear Langevin diffusion: the density evolves by a drift from the first variation plus a `beta^{-1}` Laplacian, and the particle SDE is `dw_t = -grad J'[mu_t](w_t) dt + sqrt(2/beta) dB_t`. Two things in the constants need care. The drift is the negative gradient of the first variation, and the diffusion coefficient is `sqrt(2/beta)`, not `sqrt(1/beta)`; the factor `2` is what makes the stationary density of the linear part match `exp(-beta V)` rather than `exp(-2 beta V)`. When I discretize a step of length `eta` by Euler-Maruyama, Brownian motion over `[0, eta]` contributes `sqrt(eta)` times a standard Gaussian, so the noise increment is `sqrt(2 eta / beta) xi`, `xi ~ N(0, I)`.

Now I have to translate the measure-space drift into a finite-network update, and the scaling is where mistakes creep in. The empirical predictor and the regularizer are both averages over `m` neurons, so the objective `Jhat_lambda(W)` carries a `1/m` in front. Its gradient with respect to a single neuron weight `w_j` is therefore `O(1/m)`, while the measure-space drift on one particle is order one. So the finite-particle update has to multiply the ordinary gradient by `m`:

```text
w_j^{l+1} = w_j^l - m eta grad_{w_j} Jhat_lambda(W^l) + sqrt(2 eta / beta) xi_j^l.
```

I want to be sure this `m` lands the regularizer in the right place. The regularizer inside `Jhat_lambda` is `(lambda / 2m) sum_j ||w_j||^2`, whose gradient at neuron `j` is `(lambda/m) w_j`. Multiplying by `-m eta` gives `-eta lambda w_j` â€” exactly. I checked the arithmetic on a concrete `w_j = (1, 2, -0.5)` with `lambda = 0.7`, `lr = 0.05`, `m = 8`: `-lr m (lambda/m) w_j = (-0.035, -0.07, 0.0175)`, which is identical to `-lr lambda w_j`. So the `m` cancels the `1/m` in the penalty, and the regularizer contribution is plain weight decay with coefficient `lambda` independent of width, while the data-gradient term keeps its `m` factor (it must, because each `a_j ~ 1/m` already shrinks the data gradient per particle). The update is thus: width-scaled data-gradient descent, ordinary weight decay, and Gaussian parameter noise.

Before trusting the data-gradient term I should make sure I know exactly what quantity the reference `first_layer_gradient` computes, because the convergence story assumes it is the loss gradient. The function forms two factors each scaled by `1/sqrt(n)` and contracts them: `v1 = phiprime(W X^T) * a / sqrt(n)` and `v2 = X * (yhat - y) / sqrt(n)`, returning `v1 @ v2`. The two `1/sqrt(n)` produce a `1/n`, and the product is `(1/n) sum_i a phiprime(<w,x_i>) x_i (yhat_i - y_i)`. I traced this on a tiny instance (`n = 2`, `d = 2`, `m = 2`, signed `a = (+1/2, -1/2)`) against PyTorch autograd. Autograd of the mean squared loss `mean((yhat - y)^2)` returned `[[-0.25, 0.875], [0, -0.875]]`, but the function returned `[[-0.125, 0.4375], [0, -0.4375]]` â€” exactly half. The factor of two is the derivative of the square that the function omits: it is the gradient of the *half* squared loss `(1/2n) sum (yhat_i - y_i)^2`, and autograd of `0.5 * mean((yhat - y)^2)` matched it exactly. That is a benign convention (a factor of two absorbable into `lr`), but it is worth recording precisely, because "the usual MSE gradient" would be off by two and the deliverable should say half-loss, not whole-loss.

Next, the convergence rate. The handle is a proximal Gibbs law: freeze the current measure inside the first variation, and the density `nu_mu(dw) proportional exp(-beta J'[mu](w)) tau(dw)` is the instantaneous target of the flow. If these Gibbs laws satisfy a log-Sobolev inequality with constant `C_LSI`, the free energy contracts as `F_beta(mu_t) - F_beta(mu_beta*) <= exp(-2t/(beta C_LSI)) (F_beta(mu_0) - F_beta(mu_beta*))`. The `-beta` in the Gibbs density, the dissipation of KL toward it, and the `2` in the exponent (from the LSI normalization `H <= (C_LSI/2) I`) all line up. So the entire rate question collapses to: how large is `C_LSI` for these Gibbs laws?

In Euclidean space this is the expensive part, and I should see exactly how expensive. The first variation contains the quadratic `(lambda/2)||w||^2`, so the base Gaussian `gamma` already satisfies an LSI with constant `1/(beta lambda)`. The data-fit piece is a bounded perturbation of the potential: the activation is bounded by `iota` and the loss derivative by `C_rho`, so the oscillation of `beta J'_data` across its range is at most `4 C_rho iota beta`. Holley-Stroock then multiplies the base LSI constant by `exp(osc)`, giving `C_LSI <= (beta lambda)^{-1} exp(4 C_rho iota beta)`. I want to feel the size of this, so I plugged in `C_rho = 1.5`, `iota = 2`, `lambda = 0.7` and swept `beta`: at `beta = 1` the bound is about `2.3e5`, at `beta = 2` about `1.9e10`, at `beta = 4` about `2.5e20`, at `beta = 8` about `8.8e40`. The contraction rate `2/(beta C_LSI)` collapses correspondingly â€” at `beta = 3` it is already `~3e-16`. So the exponential in `beta` is not cosmetic; it is the real compute price of the method, and any sample-complexity win has to be read against it.

That win comes from `beta` and `lambda` being tuned to the statistical scale rather than to the link. The relevant scale is the effective dimension, and I should fix the convention before quoting it, because it is easy to write `d/k` here by reflex. With `U` having rows `u_i^T / sqrt(k)`, `r_x = ||Sigma^{1/2} U^T||_F` and `d_eff = tr(Sigma)/r_x^2`. For isotropic inputs `r_x^2 = ||U^T||_F^2 = sum_i ||u_i||^2 / k = k/k = 1`, so `d_eff = tr(Sigma) = d`. I confirmed this on a random orthonormal frame in `d = 30`, `k = 3`: `r_x^2` came out to `1.000000` and `d_eff = 30.0`, exactly `d` and *not* `d/k`. The `1/sqrt(k)` normalization in `U` is precisely what cancels the `k` that one might expect. (When high-variance directions of `Sigma` are aligned with the target subspace, `d_eff` drops below `d`; in the same `d = 30` experiment, concentrating variance into the subspace pushed `d_eff` to `5.7`. When the high-variance directions are unrelated to the target, it does not improve.) Setting `lambda = lambda_tilde r_x^2` and `beta = Otilde(d_eff / lambda_tilde)`, the empirical regularized minimizer generalizes with `n = Otilde(d_eff)`. The information or leap exponent of `g` has moved out of the sample complexity entirely; it survives only in the compute budget, through that exponential LSI constant.

I should keep the Riemannian variant in its proper lane and not oversell it. Constraining the weights to a positively curved compact manifold (the sphere) removes the Euclidean quadratic confinement, and Bakry-Emery then bounds the LSI constant by curvature minus perturbation: `C_LSI <= (rho d - beta C_rho K)^{-1}`, valid only while `beta < rho d/(C_rho K)`. To check this behaves as I claim, I took `rho = 0.5`, `d = 20`, `C_rho = 1.5`, `K = 1`, giving a critical `beta = rho d/(C_rho K) = 6.67`. At `beta = 1` the bound is `0.118`, at `beta = 3` it is `0.182`, at `beta = 6` it is `1.0`, and at `beta = 6.6` (just below critical) it is `10.0` and climbing â€” the denominator goes to zero at the boundary and the bound is meaningless past it. So at fixed `beta` this is polynomial in `d`, a genuine improvement over the Euclidean `exp(4 C_rho iota beta)`. But the improvement is conditional: it requires a good target measure with empirical risk at most `bar_epsilon` and entropy at most `bar_Delta` against the uniform measure, and matching `bar_Delta` to the Euclidean `d_eff` for fixed-`k` multi-index targets is something I have no derivation of here. So I should present spherical projection as a route under additional spread assumptions, not as the proven finite-time algorithm for the general fixed-`k` theorem.

The architecture has a subtlety I do not want to paper over. In the main Euclidean theory the second layer is fixed at `+1`; signed functions are represented by making one neuron parameter carry two halves, `w = (omega_1, omega_2)`, with `Psi(x; w) = phi(<x_tilde, omega_1>) - phi(<x_tilde, omega_2>)`. In a scalar-neuron implementation this is the same as using signed outer weights, half `+1/m` and half `-1/m`, but the theoretical object is a measure over paired neurons with all outer weights fixed. The bias is scaled as `x_tilde = (x, r_tilde_x)` in the proof, while a notebook simply appends `1`.

Finally the reference code, where the constants I derived have to be matched against what is actually run. The notebook initializes rows on the sphere, uses signed second-layer weights `+1/m` and `-1/m`, appends a bias coordinate in the effective-dimension experiment, and updates `W = W - lr*m*grad - lr*weight_decay*W + 2*sqrt(lr*inv_temp)*randn_like(W)`. With `inv_temp = beta^{-1}`, that noise coefficient is `2 sqrt(lr/beta) = sqrt(4 lr/beta)`, whereas my Euler-Maruyama derivation gave `sqrt(2 lr/beta)`. The ratio is `sqrt(2)`, which I verified numerically at `beta = 50`, `lr = 0.1`: the notebook constant is `0.0894` and the derived constant `0.0632`, ratio `1.41421...`. This is a real discrepancy, not a rounding artifact, so the deliverable has to keep both: the derivation uses the Eq. 10 coefficient `sqrt(2 lr/beta)`, while a code-faithful reproduction of the notebook uses its `2 sqrt(lr inv_temp)`.

Putting it together, what falls out is noisy, width-scaled, weight-decayed first-layer training, justified as descent of a convex free energy over neuron distributions. It reaches sample complexity `Otilde(d_eff)` for general locally Lipschitz multi-index links under the Euclidean theorem, with the link's exponent gone from the sample count; the cost is paid back in width and iteration bounds that are exponential in `d_eff` in the worst case, exactly the size of the Holley-Stroock LSI constant I computed. The compact Riemannian analysis shows how positive curvature can convert that exponential into a polynomial under an additional entropy-spread assumption, but it does not close the open question for fixed-`k` multi-index targets.
