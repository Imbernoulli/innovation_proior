# Context: first-order minimization of nonsmooth convex functions on structured domains

## Research question

We want to solve
> minimize f(x) subject to x ∈ X ⊂ R^n,

where f is convex and Lipschitz continuous (with constant L_f) but possibly nonsmooth, X is a closed
convex set, and the dimension n is enormous — think millions of variables, as in computerized
tomography. The optimal set is nonempty and a subgradient g ∈ ∂f(x) is computable at any x. We are
content with an ε-accurate point: an x ∈ X with f(x) − min_X f ≤ ε.

The pain point is a scaling one. Second-order and interior-point methods solve a linear system at every
iteration; for a few thousand variables they are already strained, and for genuinely large, nonsmooth,
constrained problems they are defeated. First-order methods that touch the data only through a
subgradient and an easy projection are the natural candidates — provided two things hold: (i) the
per-iteration work is cheap (one subgradient, one projection onto a simple set), and (ii) the number of
iterations needed to reach accuracy ε is *almost independent of the dimension n*. A method whose
iteration count blows up with n is useless at the target scale, no matter how cheap each step is.

The sharper question underneath: the natural first-order method (projected subgradient) does deliver a
dimension-mild rate, but its constants are measured in the Euclidean norm. When the feasible set X is a
non-Euclidean object — the probability simplex being the canonical example — the Euclidean yardstick is
the wrong one and silently injects a √n factor into the rate. What would it take to measure progress in
a geometry adapted to X instead, and recover a rate that is mild — logarithmic, not polynomial — in n?

## Background

**Convex first-order optimization.** For convex f, a subgradient g ∈ ∂f(x) supports f from below:
f(y) ≥ f(x) + ⟨g, y − x⟩ for all y. This single inequality is the engine of every first-order rate.
For an x ∈ X and the optimum x*, convexity gives f(x) − f(x*) ≤ ⟨g, x − x*⟩, turning the suboptimality
of a *convex* function into a *linear* quantity that the analysis can control.

**Norms, dual norms, and where gradients live.** Fix a norm ‖·‖ on R^n. Its dual norm is
‖z‖_* = max{ ⟨z, x⟩ : ‖x‖ ≤ 1 }. A subgradient is a linear form on the space of x's — it is naturally a
*dual* object, and its size is measured by ‖·‖_*. The generalized Cauchy–Schwarz inequality
⟨z, x⟩ ≤ ‖z‖_* ‖x‖ is the only pairing available. In a Euclidean (Hilbert) space the Riesz
representation theorem identifies the space with its dual isometrically, so one can freely write
x − η g; in a general normed (Banach) space ℓ_p, p ≠ 2, there is no such identification, and the
expression x − η g — subtracting a dual vector from a primal point — does not even type-check.

**Strong convexity.** A function ψ is σ-strongly convex w.r.t. ‖·‖ if
ψ(x) ≥ ψ(y) + ⟨∇ψ(y), x − y⟩ + (σ/2)‖x − y‖². The gap in that inequality — the amount by which ψ at x
exceeds its tangent approximation built at y — is nonnegative and vanishes only at x = y. Bregman (1967),
in his relaxation method for convex feasibility, and the proximal-point literature after him (Chen and
Teboulle, 1993) studied such tangent-gap quantities generated by a convex potential.

**Convex duality.** For a proper convex ψ, the conjugate ψ*(z) = max_x { ⟨z, x⟩ − ψ(x) } satisfies
∇ψ* = (∂ψ)^{-1} (Rockafellar, 1970): the gradient of the conjugate is the inverse of the subdifferential.
σ-strong convexity of ψ is equivalent to ∇ψ* being everywhere single-valued and (1/σ)-Lipschitz.

**The simplex and its geometry — the diagnostic example.** The probability simplex
Δ_n = { x ∈ R^n : x ≥ 0, Σ_j x_j = 1 } is the home of expert-mixture and distribution problems. Two
pre-existing facts about it set up the whole story, and both are knowable before any algorithm is
designed:
- Its Euclidean diameter is a constant (≈ √2), but the ℓ_1 and ℓ_2 norms differ by up to √n on it:
  ‖x‖_2 ≥ ‖x‖_1/√n, and for a gradient the natural bound is in ℓ_∞ (e.g. expert losses in [0, 1] give
  ‖g‖_∞ ≤ 1), with ‖g‖_2 then as large as √n ‖g‖_∞.
- The relative-entropy / Kullback–Leibler divergence KL(x‖y) = Σ_j x_j ln(x_j / y_j) is the canonical
  notion of distance on Δ_n, and Pinsker's inequality KL(x‖y) ≥ ½‖x − y‖_1² ties it to the ℓ_1 norm.
Thus on the simplex a gradient that is small in the natural (ℓ_∞) norm can be √n times larger in ℓ_2,
and the squared-Euclidean yardstick is not the geometry the set actually carries. This mismatch is the
observed phenomenon the method must repair.

**Prevailing wisdom of the time.** Subgradient methods were known to converge slowly (a 1/√k rate, no
better in the worst case for nonsmooth problems) but with a constant *almost independent of n* —
already attractive for huge problems where only low accuracy is required. A geometric complexity
viewpoint had made clear that structured domains need not be measured with the plain Euclidean ruler,
and large-scale tomography work had demonstrated this concretely. The proximal-point literature
(Bregman, 1967; Rockafellar; Chen–Teboulle, 1993) had developed convex-potential and convex-duality
machinery, studied there for the full proximal step.

## Baselines

**Projected subgradient method.** The standard method for min_X f:
> x_{k+1} = Π_X( x_k − t_k g_k ),  g_k ∈ ∂f(x_k), t_k > 0,
where Π_X(x) = argmin{ ‖x − y‖_2 : y ∈ X } is the Euclidean projection. Its strength is simplicity: when
X is a simple set (halfspace, ball, box, simplex) the projection is cheap. With a horizon-tuned stepsize
of order Diam(X)/√k it achieves
> min_{1 ≤ s ≤ k} f(x^s) − min_X f ≤ O(1)·L_f·Diam(X)·k^{-1/2},
where Diam(X) is the Euclidean diameter and L_f the Lipschitz constant w.r.t. ‖·‖_2. The rate is slow
(low accuracy only) but mildly dependent on dimension. The gap it leaves: both L_f and Diam(X) are tied
to the Euclidean norm, so on a domain like Δ_n the bound carries a hidden √n through ‖g‖_2.

**Proximal / linearized-proximal viewpoint.** The subgradient step can be read as one step of a
linearized proximal algorithm:
> x_{k+1} = argmin_{x ∈ X} { ⟨g_k, x⟩ + (1/2t_k)‖x − x_k‖_2² }.
A quick stationarity check returns x_k − t_k g_k followed by projection, i.e. the same iteration. The
squared-Euclidean term is the "don't move too far" penalty. The gap: this reading, like the iteration
itself, still measures the penalty with the Euclidean norm, and on a domain like Δ_n the same hidden √n
persists.

**Bregman-function proximal methods.** In the proximal-point literature the quadratic penalty
½‖x − x_k‖² had already been replaced by Bregman-like distances D(x, x_k) built from a convex potential,
to reflect the geometry of X. These methods were analyzed for the *full* proximal step (minimizing f
itself plus the Bregman penalty), not for the cheap *linearized* subgradient step. The gap: the
analyses stop at the expensive full-minimization step and yield no worst-case efficiency estimate that
isolates the dependence on n.

**Multiplicative-weights / exponentiated-gradient updates.** In learning theory, the weighted-majority
and Hedge algorithms (Littlestone–Warmuth 1994; Freund–Schapire 1997) and the exponentiated-gradient
predictor (Kivinen–Warmuth 1997) maintain a distribution over experts and update it multiplicatively,
w_j ← w_j · exp(−η·loss_j), then renormalize. Their analyses use an exponential potential or, equivalently,
a relative-entropy / KL argument, and they achieve regret that scales like √(T log n) — only
*logarithmic* in the number of experts n. The exponentiated-gradient work explicitly contrasts this
multiplicative rule against additive gradient descent and observes that the relative entropy plays the
role for EG that the squared Euclidean distance plays for GD. The gap: each rule arrives with its own
bespoke potential-function proof, and the parallel between the entropy case and the Euclidean case is
noted only as an analogy, with no common derivation behind it.

## Evaluation settings

The natural yardstick is nonsmooth convex minimization over a simple convex set, judged by the
worst-case efficiency estimate — the number of subgradient evaluations to drive
min_{s ≤ k} f(x^s) − min_X f below ε, and crucially how that count depends on the dimension n. The
canonical structured domains are the unit simplex Δ_n (expert/distribution problems, LP relaxations,
tomography reconstructions) and Euclidean balls / boxes. The relevant measurements are: the Lipschitz
constant of f in the norm adapted to the domain (ℓ_∞ for the simplex, ℓ_2 for a ball), the diameter of
the domain in the matching geometry, and the resulting k^{-1/2} constant. The comparison of interest is
the *constant* in the 1/√k rate as a function of n: polynomial (√n) versus logarithmic (√log n).
