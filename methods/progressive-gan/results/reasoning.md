OK, let me reason from the failure, not from architecture fashion. I want a generator that can reach 1024x1024, but the adversarial game gives G only whatever gradient D hands back. At low resolution the game is hard for D and manageable for G: a 4x4 face-like image has only coarse layout, so real and generated distributions have a chance to overlap. At high resolution the asymmetry flips. Real photographs have a dense set of high-frequency cues, and a random or half-trained generator gets many of them wrong. D gets millions of coordinates to find a tell, so it can become nearly perfect while G is still trying to learn global structure. That is the regime where the JS-style discriminator diagnosis says the gradient becomes useless, and even WGAN-GP only fixes the distance side, not the fact that I asked for the whole latent-to-megapixel map at once.

So maybe I should not present the final problem at the start. I can train the same adversarial pair first on the smallest image where the distribution has only coarse structure, then introduce the next octave of detail only after the old scale has learned something. The old layers should stay trainable, because they may need to adjust when later details arrive, but the new work at each step is local: turn an already meaningful R x R representation into a 2R x 2R representation. That converts one brutal optimization into a sequence of smaller ones, and most of the early iterations are cheap because the networks are shallow and the images are tiny. Whether this actually buys stability rather than just deferring the same hard problem to the last block is the thing I have to keep checking as I work out the mechanics.

The obvious danger is what happens at the moment of growth. If I append a random high-resolution block and immediately route the output through it, the old trained generator is suddenly connected to random filters, D sees garbage, and the gradient can destroy the lower-resolution solution. So the network has to behave exactly like the old network at the instant of growth, then hand control to the new block gradually. The old generator output at 2R is just the R image projected to RGB and upsampled. The new output is the new block followed by its own toRGB. During the transition I should output some old_weight times the old upsampled RGB plus new_weight times the new RGB, with old_weight starting near 1 and ending at 0.

I need a clean way to drive those two weights with a single schedule variable. Let me track a single scalar `lod` (level-of-detail) that is high at the beginning and decreases as resolution increases. For a final 1024 model, stable 4x4 is `lod=8`, stable 8x8 is `lod=7`, and a transition from 4x4 to 8x8 has `lod` between 8 and 7. I want the active new stage selected by `floor(lod)`, and the fractional part of `lod` to drive the blend. The natural definition is

```text
old_weight = lod - floor(lod)
new_weight = 1 - old_weight.
```

That sign is exactly the kind of thing I get backwards, so let me actually evaluate it at the boundaries instead of trusting it. With `max_stage=8`, `top = max_stage - floor(lod)`. At `lod=8.0`: `floor=8`, `top=0`, `old_weight=0` — pure 4x4, no fade yet, good. At `lod=7.999`: `floor=7`, `top=1`, `old_weight=0.999`, `new_weight=0.001` — the just-added block contributes almost nothing, the old upsampled path carries the image, which is what I wanted at the start of a fade. At `lod=7.5`: `old_weight=0.5`, an even mix. At `lod=7.001`: `old_weight=0.001` — the new block now owns essentially all of the output. At `lod=7.0`: `floor=7`, `top=1`, `old_weight=0.0`, `new_weight=1.0` — the new block fully owns the output and the old toRGB is gone. So the convention runs continuously from "new block invisible" to "new block in full control" across one unit of `lod`, with no discontinuity at the moment of insertion. That is the property the growth needs, and the weights come out the right way round.

The generator blend is therefore

```text
image = new_weight * toRGB_new(block(features_R))
      + old_weight * upscale(toRGB_old(features_R)).
```

D has to use the same convention in reverse. It first maps the sharp 2R input through fromRGB_new and the new down block, producing features at R. The old path downsamples the image to R and applies fromRGB_old. Those two R-resolution feature tensors are blended with the same weights:

```text
features = new_weight * downblock(fromRGB_new(x_2R))
         + old_weight * fromRGB_old(downsample(x_2R)).
```

The real images need the same treatment before D sees them, and this is where I almost left a hole. If real images are fully sharp during the transition while fake images are a mixture of sharp and blocky paths, D can exploit high-frequency sharpness as a shortcut and the fade buys nothing. So I take the current real image x, make a one-octave blurred version by average-pooling and tiling it back, and use

```text
x_faded = (1 - old_weight) * x + old_weight * upscale(downscale(x)).
```

Let me check that this actually removes the high-frequency shortcut at the start of a fade. I measure the residual between the faded real and its own blurred version — that residual is exactly the high-frequency content D could latch onto. On a random 8x8 image: at `old_weight=0` (end of fade), the residual is 0.65, i.e. the image is fully sharp; at `old_weight=0.5` it is 0.32; at `old_weight=1.0` (start of fade) it is exactly 0.0, meaning the faded real has become identical to its blurred version and carries no high-frequency content at all. So at the instant a new block appears — when it is still random and producing only blocky output — the real images D sees are equally blocky. The shortcut closes exactly when it would otherwise open. That is the case I was worried about, and the fade handles it.

To keep tensor shapes uniform across stages I let G and the real-image pipeline both emit at a fixed full size: I upsample by `2^floor(lod)` after the blend, and D downsamples the full tensor by the same factor before its active fromRGB path. I should confirm this really is size-uniform rather than just plausible. For `max_stage=8`, the current resolution at stage `top` is `4 * 2^top`, and the emitted size is `current * 2^floor(lod)`. At `lod=8` the current res is 4 and `floor(lod)=8`, so emitted = 4*256 = 1024. At `lod=7`, current 8, factor 128, emitted 1024. At `lod=4`, current 64, factor 16, emitted 1024. At `lod=0`, current 1024, factor 1, emitted 1024. Every stage emits 1024x1024, so a single dataloader and a single optimizer config can serve all stages without reshaping. Good.

Now variation. A discriminator that scores each image independently can be fooled by a generator that repeats a few convincing samples, because any one sample can look real. The old minibatch discrimination idea gives D cross-sample information, but it does so with a learned tensor and extra design choices. The simplest statistic that says "this batch lacks variety" is standard deviation across the minibatch. For activations `x` with shape `[N,C,H,W]`, I split into groups, subtract each group's mean, compute the standard deviation over the group axis for each channel and location, average that to one scalar per group, tile it as a constant `[N,1,H,W]` map, and concatenate it near D's final 4x4 layers. There are no learnable parameters.

I should make sure this scalar actually carries the signal I claim, because a feature that is always the same value would be useless to D. I run two cases through it. A collapsed batch — eight identical images — produces an appended-feature value of 0.0001, essentially zero (the residual `eps`). A varied batch of eight independent samples produces 0.82. So the feature is near zero exactly when G has collapsed and clearly positive when it has not; D gets a clean, single-number handle on batch diversity and can drive it up by punishing low values. That is the behavior I needed, and it falls out of plain standard deviation with no extra parameters.

The magnitude problem is separate. Collapse often starts when D overshoots, gradients get exaggerated, and both networks participate in a magnitude race. BatchNorm is not obviously the thing I want if the issue is not covariate shift but adversarial scale escalation, especially with small high-resolution minibatches. I want a parameter-free brake in G. For each pixel, I normalize the feature vector across channels to unit RMS:

```text
b_{x,y} = a_{x,y} / sqrt(mean_j (a^j_{x,y})^2 + 1e-8).
```

The point of this is to kill the scale degree of freedom while leaving the direction of each pixel's feature vector untouched, so I should verify it does both. On a random feature tensor scaled and shifted to an arbitrary range, the per-pixel RMS across channels after normalization comes out to 1.0 at every pixel (min and max both 1.0), and the cosine similarity between a pixel's pre- and post-normalization feature vector is 1.0 — direction perfectly preserved. So it removes only the magnitude, which is the one degree of freedom feeding the race. It belongs in G after each 3x3 convolution, and also on the latent input in the implementation; it is not used in D, where I do want the critic free to express magnitude.

There is another scale issue that lives in the optimizer. He initialization sets each layer's effective weight scale to `gain/sqrt(fan_in)`, but Adam and RMSProp normalize updates by a running gradient scale. If the trainable weights themselves live at different dynamic ranges, then the same normalized update is a different fraction of each layer's natural range, so layers learn at effectively different rates. The fix I want is to store all weights at unit normal scale and multiply by the He constant at runtime:

```text
w_eff = w * gain / sqrt(fan_in).
```

For this to be admissible it has to leave the forward-pass statistics identical to ordinary He init; otherwise I am changing the network, not just the parameterization. Let me check the variance directly. Take a 3x3 conv with 256 input channels, so `fan_in = 9*256 = 2304` and `c = sqrt(2)/sqrt(2304) = 0.0295`. Under He init the weights are drawn at std `c`, and a single output unit summing `fan_in` independent products of unit-variance inputs and weights has variance `fan_in * c^2 * 1 = gain^2 = 2`. Under the equalized scheme the stored weights are unit-normal and scaled by `c` at the forward pass, so each effective weight again has variance `c^2` and the predicted output variance is the same `2`. Drawing 30000 random output units empirically gives variance 1.999. So the forward pass has exactly He's variance behavior while the learned parameters all live in the same unit dynamic range, which is precisely what makes Adam's per-coordinate normalization treat every layer equally. Concretely I store each weight at unit normal scale and, on every forward call, multiply by `std = gain/sqrt(fan_in)` before using it; the bias is unscaled.

For the loss, I keep the WGAN-GP signs straight. D minimizes `D(fake) - D(real)` plus the penalty on interpolates, with `lambda=10` and target gradient norm 1, and G minimizes `-D(fake)`. I also add a tiny drift term, `0.001 * E[D(real)^2]`. I want to be honest about what this does: its gradient with respect to `D(real)` is `0.002 * D(real)`, a force toward zero whose strength scales with the score. At a normal critic score of about 1 that force is 0.002 against a WGAN gradient of order 1 — a 0.2% perturbation, negligible for the ordering of real vs fake. But if the scores start to run away to, say, 20, the drift force grows to 0.04 and keeps growing, so it acts as a weak anchor that does almost nothing in the normal regime and actively brakes a magnitude blow-up. So it is not literally orthogonal to the objective, but it is small enough at sane scores that it does not distort the ranking, which is the property I need. The default unconditional case has no label penalty; I could add AC-GAN-style label losses if labels were available, but that is not part of the ordinary unsupervised face setup.

None of this is worth trusting unless I have a way to tell whether each piece is actually earning its place, and the existing yardsticks are not built for that job. Inception Score wants a classifier confident about natural image categories, but a face dataset or a single LSUN scene category is not a labeled-category problem, so a class-confidence score does not mean anything here. MS-SSIM sidesteps that by comparing generated images only to each other, so it does catch a generator that has collapsed onto a handful of near-duplicate outputs — but it never looks at the training images at all. If I compare my worst training configuration against my best, the images are obviously far more convincing in the good one, yet MS-SSIM barely moves between them, because two bland, low-variation outputs can look just as different from each other as two good ones do; it is measuring self-similarity, not fidelity to the data. I want a number that is anchored to the training distribution and that reacts to the kind of variation loss that lives in color and texture, not only in gross layout.

The natural comparison is not whole images — there is no correspondence between an arbitrary training photo and an arbitrary generated one, so nothing like a pixelwise error is meaningful — but the distribution of small patches drawn from each set. If I pull enough 7x7 neighborhoods out of real images and enough out of generated ones, I have two empirical distributions over the same 147-dimensional space that I can compare directly with a distributional distance, and this needs no alignment: it only asks whether local structure is statistically the same. But comparing patches at full resolution only conflates every spatial scale into one number, and a generator that nails broad layout while smearing fine texture (or the reverse) should not average out to "looks fine." So I decompose each image into a Laplacian pyramid first — a blurry base at 16x16, then a sequence of residual levels each doubling in resolution up to the final size — and compare patch distributions level by level. Each level is one spatial-frequency band, so the distance at the coarsest level is a statement about large-scale structure and the distance at the finest level is about pixel-level sharpness and noise, and I get one diagnostic per band instead of one number that hides which scale is failing.

The remaining obstacle is that an honest multi-dimensional Wasserstein distance between two clouds of millions of 147-dimensional patches is an optimal-transport assignment problem, not something to solve directly at that scale. But in one dimension the Wasserstein-1 distance between two equal-size empirical samples has a closed form: sort both samples and average the absolute gaps between matched order statistics, no assignment problem at all. So I project the patches onto a random direction, take that free 1-D distance, and average it over many random directions to approximate the full-dimensional distance — the sliced-Wasserstein construction. Concretely: sample 16384 images and pull 128 patches per Laplacian level from each set. Let me check that this gives the patch count I expect: 16384 = 2^14 and 128 = 2^7, so the product is 2^21, a little over two million patches per level — that is the number of points each 1-D projection has to sort. I normalize each patch's three channels to zero mean and unit variance first, so the metric is not dominated by a global brightness or color-balance offset that has nothing to do with structure, then average the projected distance over 512 random directions. A small value at some level means the training-set and generated-set patch distributions coincide there in both typical appearance and spread; a generator that has quietly narrowed its texture variety shows up as a smaller spread at the fine levels even while individual images still look sharp, which is exactly the failure MS-SSIM cannot see because it never touches the training patches at all. This is the check that actually tells me, level by level, whether progressive growing, the minibatch-stddev feature, and the two normalizations are each doing what I designed them to do, instead of trusting one global score that is blind to the data distribution.

Stepping back, the pieces now fit together rather than each fixing a separate symptom: growing the resolution turns one impossible adversarial game into a sequence of feasible ones; the `frac(lod)` fade — which I checked runs continuously from invisible to fully-in-control — keeps each growth from destroying the previous solution; fading the reals by the same amount, which I checked actually zeros the high-frequency shortcut at the start of a fade, keeps D honest during the transition; the minibatch-stddev feature, which I checked goes to zero exactly under collapse, gives D a diversity handle; pixelwise norm, which I checked removes only magnitude, and equalized learning rate, which I checked preserves He's forward variance, together defuse the magnitude race; and WGAN-GP plus a small drift keeps the gradients informative and bounded. So: start at 4x4 from a 512-dimensional latent normalized to the hypersphere, use mirror generator and discriminator blocks, keep old layers trainable, fade each new resolution with `frac(lod)` as the old-path weight, fade real images by the same amount, add a minibatch-stddev feature near D's end, use pixelwise normalization only in G, use runtime He scaling for every learned weight, and train with WGAN-GP plus drift using Adam `lr=0.001`, `beta1=0`, `beta2=0.99`, `epsilon=1e-8`. For the full CelebA-HQ runs I budget 800k real images for the initial 4x4 phase, then 800k to fade and 800k to stabilize each added block; these are exposed as schedule parameters so they can be retuned (a lighter preset with 600k transition/training phases works too). The method is the same; the constants have to be named as schedule choices rather than hidden in the equations.
