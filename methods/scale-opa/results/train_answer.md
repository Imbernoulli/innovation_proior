When I fit a scene made of $N$ anisotropic 3D Gaussians to a handful of posed photographs, the only thing I optimize is a photometric loss, $L_{\text{photo}} = 0.8\,\mathrm{L1}(\text{render}, \text{gt}) + 0.2\,(1-\mathrm{SSIM}(\text{render}, \text{gt}))$. That is a pure data term: it rewards *any* configuration of Gaussians that reproduces the training views and is otherwise blind to how that configuration is built. The fit is therefore wildly under-determined — enormously many Gaussian arrangements render the training images to within a hair of each other, and gradient descent simply settles into whichever one it stumbles into. The ones it stumbles into are ugly. I get faint, near-transparent floaters hovering in regions no training camera looks at closely; hugely anisotropic needles, one axis stretched far past the others, that shave a little error on one view but read as streaks from any new angle; and oversized splats smeared over fine geometry because that was cheaper for the data term than resolving the detail. None of these hurt the training views much — that is exactly why the optimizer keeps them — but they wreck the held-out novel views I actually care about, and every junk Gaussian is one more Gaussian I pay for, because the cost of this representation is essentially the count of Gaussians.

So I need to add something to the loss that expresses a preference the data term lacks: *use Gaussians efficiently*, and when a Gaussian is not earning its keep, drive it out of the representation entirely rather than merely shrinking it. The framework around me makes "gone" productive — every hundred iterations it finds "dead" Gaussians whose opacity has dropped below roughly $0.005$ and recycles them, teleporting each onto a live, useful Gaussian (sampling targets with probability proportional to opacity) and letting the live count creep toward a budget. So steady downward pressure that pushes a useless Gaussian toward that dead threshold is not destructive but generative: it feeds the recycle loop. The existing options do not give me this. The original adaptive density control (Kerbl et al. 2023) is a tower of hand-tuned thresholds — a positional-gradient threshold, a split factor $\phi = 1.6$, a prune threshold, a 3000-iteration opacity-reset cadence — that must be re-tuned per scene, makes the final Gaussian count unpredictable, and reacts to gradients rather than encoding any stated preference for parsimony. Anisotropy penalties on $\max(s)/\min(s)$ kill needles only, and let an isotropic-but-huge or merely redundant Gaussian off for free. Neighbour-consistency priors smooth the field without removing anything, and invite infeasible all-pairs $N\times N$ computation. And the obvious move — push opacity down so faint Gaussians fall below the prune threshold — fails on half the problem, as I will show.

I propose the **scale and opacity L1 regularizer**: an L1 penalty on each Gaussian's *activated* scale and *activated* opacity, each with a small weight, added directly to the photometric loss every step. The starting instinct, penalizing opacity alone, $\lambda \sum_i o_i$, is the thing to test against, because it rests on the assumption that "faint" means "harmless." It does not. Read how a Gaussian actually shows up in an image: its contribution at a pixel is a *product*,
$$a_i(x) = o_i \cdot \exp\!\Big(\!-\tfrac{1}{2}\,(x-\mathrm{proj}(\mu_i))^{\top}\,\mathrm{proj}_\theta(\Sigma_i)^{-1}\,(x-\mathrm{proj}(\mu_i))\Big),$$
the opacity $o_i$ out front times a spatial bump whose size is induced by the covariance. The total mass a Gaussian dumps into the image is opacity times the size of that bump, and the bump grows with the Gaussian's volume, proportional to $\sqrt{\det \Sigma_i} = \prod_j s_j$. A Gaussian is loud iff it is opaque *and* large. That exposes exactly how opacity-only pressure fails: a big, space-filling splat that the optimizer holds at opacity $\approx 0.01$, just above the dead threshold, pays almost nothing to an opacity penalty — the loss value is already tiny, and through the sigmoid the logit gradient is scaled by $o(1-o)$, so near threshold the stored parameter barely feels a push — yet it remains a region-spanning artifact the data term is happy to keep, because removing it would change many pixels by a little. The over-reconstruction and big-floater cases go untouched. And the mirror image: a tiny but fully opaque speck ($o = 0.99$, microscopic scale) contributes essentially nothing to any image, yet an opacity term actively penalizes it for being opaque. Existence is not opacity; existence is opacity-times-extent.

The fix falls straight out of the product structure. If footprint is set jointly by opacity and extent, then to reliably make a Gaussian vanish I must be able to drive *either* factor toward zero, so I press on *both*: penalize opacity, so faint useless Gaussians fade and cross the recycler's dead threshold; penalize extent, so big useless Gaussians shrink even where opacity alone would leave them alive. Opacity is the explicit death channel; scale collapse removes the footprint that made a Gaussian harmful. This covers the big floater (extent collapses it) and the needle (its huge axis gets hammered). To make "extent" concrete, recall the covariance is stored factored, $\Sigma = R S S^{\top} R^{\top}$ with $S = \mathrm{diag}(s_1, s_2, s_3)$ and $R$ a rotation. Because $R$ is orthogonal, $\Sigma = R\,\mathrm{diag}(s_1^2, s_2^2, s_3^2)\,R^{\top}$ is already the eigendecomposition of $\Sigma$: its eigenvalues are the variances $s_j^2$, so $\sqrt{\mathrm{eig}_j(\Sigma)} = s_j$ is the physical half-width along axis $j$. Penalizing extent is therefore just penalizing $\sum_j s_j$ — no eigen-solvers or determinants at runtime, the scales sit right there as a parameter. I deliberately sum the three scales *additively* rather than penalizing the volume $\prod_j s_j$: an additive per-axis term makes the gradient on each axis $\partial\,\exp(\ell_j)/\partial \ell_j = s_j$, independent of the other axes, so a needle's giant axis takes a giant gradient and gets squashed, whereas a volume penalty would be satisfied by collapsing one axis while keeping the others huge — exactly the needle shape I am trying to kill.

Now the kind of penalty. I want useless Gaussians to go to *zero*, not hover at small-but-nonzero values forever, which is the textbook L1-versus-L2 split. With L2, $\sum w^2$, the gradient $2w$ fades as $w \to 0$: pressure dies right where I need it most, leaving a haze of small-but-present Gaussians that never cross the dead threshold. With L1, $\sum |w|$, the subgradient is the constant $\mathrm{sign}(w)$, so the push toward zero does not weaken as the value shrinks. That is the sparsity behavior I want, so L1 on both factors. There is a subtlety about *which* number I take the L1 of. Opacity is stored as a logit through a sigmoid, scale as a log-scale through an exp, and I must not penalize the raw stored parameters: driving a logit toward $-\infty$ to "minimize" it never reaches a physical zero, and a log-scale toward $-\infty$ is the same trap. The quantity that determines existence is the *activated* value — the real opacity $\sigma(z_i) \in (0,1)$ and the real scale $\exp(\ell_{ij}) > 0$ — and those are what enter the blending equation, so those are what I penalize, with the stored logits and log-scales receiving the smooth chain-rule gradient from the activation. A numerical gift drops out for free: both activated quantities are already nonnegative, so the absolute value in the L1 is a no-op, $|\sigma(z)| = \sigma(z)$ and $|\exp(\ell)| = \exp(\ell)$. The L1 collapses to a plain mean of the activated values — no differentiating $|\cdot|$ at zero, no $\log(0)$, no place for a NaN gradient to crawl in, and the only overflow risk (a scale exploding inside the exp) is precisely what this term suppresses, so it is self-stabilizing.

Putting it together, the term is
$$L_{\text{reg}} = \lambda_o\,\operatorname*{mean}_i \sigma(z_i) \;+\; \lambda_s \operatorname*{mean}_{ij} \exp(\ell_{ij}),$$
opacity pressure plus extent pressure, both L1, both on the activated quantity, both normalized as means so the coefficient does not scale with the Gaussian count — an elementwise activation and a reduction, no neighbour search, no all-pairs $N\times N$, perfectly affordable at every one of 30k steps. The weight matters: this is a *prior*, not a competing objective, and it must never overwhelm the data term, or the optimizer would fade and shrink everything into a uniform gray nothing that minimizes the regularizer while ignoring the photos. I want it to tip the balance only where the data term is flat — the floater regions and the redundant Gaussians the photos do not constrain — so I set $\lambda_o = \lambda_s = 0.01$, equal small weights on the two footprint factors. End to end the dynamics check out: a faint floater is pressed by the opacity L1, the data term does not object in an unconstrained region, it crosses $0.005$ and is recycled; a big over-reconstructing splat held at low-but-alive opacity is barely moved by the opacity term but collapsed by the scale L1; a needle takes the largest scale gradient on its long axis and is squashed toward isotropy; and a tiny opaque speck doing real work is barely touched by the scale term and held up against the gentle opacity pressure by the data term it is reducing, so it correctly survives. The product structure of the footprint is mirrored by the sum structure of the penalty: opacity-or-extent makes a Gaussian loud, so penalizing opacity-and-extent makes it quiet, and the two terms cover complementary failure modes of a product, which is why I want both and not either alone.

```python
import torch

SCALE_REG = 1e-2      # weight on the extent (scale) penalty
OPACITY_REG = 1e-2    # weight on the opacity penalty


def compute_regularizer(splats, step, scene_scale):
    """L1 parsimony prior on per-Gaussian scale and opacity.

    Footprint a(x) is a PRODUCT of opacity and a projected Gaussian bump, so press
    on opacity and extent. L1 gives steady pressure in the activated variables,
    unlike L2 near zero. Penalize the ACTIVATED quantities; both are nonnegative
    so |.| is just a mean (smooth, NaN-free). O(N).
    """
    # exp(log_scales) = actual per-axis extents s_j = sqrt(eig_j(Sigma)); shrink every axis.
    scale_loss = torch.exp(splats["scales"]).mean()
    # sigmoid(opacity_logits) = actual opacity in (0,1); fade faint useless Gaussians.
    opa_loss = torch.sigmoid(splats["opacities"]).mean()
    return SCALE_REG * scale_loss + OPACITY_REG * opa_loss
```

The same scalar can be written as the two reusable reductions called by the training loop with both weights set to $0.01$:

```python
import torch
from torch import Tensor


def opacity_reg_loss(opacities: Tensor) -> Tensor:
    """Mean activated opacity. Penalizes high opacity to encourage transparency."""
    return torch.sigmoid(opacities).mean()


def scale_reg_loss(log_scales: Tensor) -> Tensor:
    """Mean activated scale. Penalizes large Gaussian extent."""
    return torch.exp(log_scales).mean()
```
