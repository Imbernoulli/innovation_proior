Let me start where the pain actually is. I have a matroid $M=([n],\mathcal I)$ of rank $r$, handed to me only through an independence oracle, and I want to count its bases up to $(1\pm\varepsilon)$. Exact counting is $\#P$-hard, and there's a brutal oracle lower bound saying no deterministic algorithm with polynomially many queries can do better than a $2^{\Omega(r/\log n)}$ factor. So I am forced into randomization, and the standard randomized route is: count by sampling. The problem is self-reducible — deleting an element and contracting an element ($M/i$) both give smaller matroids, and the bases of $M$ split into "bases through $i$" (contract) and "bases avoiding $i$" (delete). By the Jerrum–Valiant–Vazirani equivalence, for a self-reducible problem an FPRAS is the same thing as a fully polynomial almost-uniform sampler. So my whole task collapses to one question: can I sample an almost-uniform random basis of an arbitrary matroid in polynomial time?

There's an obvious chain to try. Sit at a basis $B$. Drop a uniformly random element $i\in B$; now I have an independent set of size $r-1$, and among all $j$ that complete it back to a basis I pick one. This bases-exchange walk is reversible with uniform stationary distribution, and it's connected because of the exchange axiom. The only question is whether it mixes fast. And this is exactly the thirty-year-old Mihail–Vazirani question in disguise: they conjectured the bases-exchange graph $G_M$ has edge expansion at least $1$, and conductance is what controls mixing. So if I can show this chain mixes rapidly, I get both the sampler and, via Cheeger, the expansion.

Now, who has gotten anywhere on this? Feder and Mihail. Their move was: assume the matroid is *balanced* — meaning $M$ and every minor have the property that the uniform basis measure is pairwise negatively correlated, $\Pr[i,j\in B]\le \Pr[i]\Pr[j]$. With that, by an induction on the ground set exploiting negative association, the bases-exchange walk has good conductance and mixes. Beautiful, but it lives or dies on the negative-correlation inequality, and that inequality is just *false* for many matroids. The canonical wall is the matroid of acyclic edge-subsets of size at most $k$ of a graph for $k<|V|-1$: not balanced. So this route, taken literally, can never reach all matroids — the very hypothesis it leans on doesn't hold in general. I can't patch it by being cleverer about canonical paths; the pairwise correlation structure simply isn't there.

So let me drop the entrywise inequality and ask what's *actually* true about every matroid. I do know something deep: the bases generating polynomial $g_M(x)=\sum_{B}x^B$ is log-concave on the positive orthant — that's the Hodge-theory result for matroids, and a subset of these ideas already gave a deterministic $2^{O(r)}$-approximation. Let me write down what log-concavity even means as a linear-algebra statement, because "log $g_M$ is concave" feels too analytic to connect to a Markov chain. For a homogeneous $p$ with nonnegative coefficients,
$$\nabla^2\log p=\frac{p\,\nabla^2 p-(\nabla p)(\nabla p)^\intercal}{p^2}.$$
The numerator subtracts a rank-one PSD matrix $(\nabla p)(\nabla p)^\intercal$ from $p\,\nabla^2 p$. By Cauchy interlacing, subtracting a rank-one PSD matrix can drag at most one eigenvalue down across zero. So $\nabla^2\log p\preccurlyeq 0$ — log-concavity — is equivalent to saying $\nabla^2 p$ has *at most one positive eigenvalue* at $x$. That's a much cleaner object. Log-concavity of $g_M$ is "the Hessian of $g_M$ has one positive eigenvalue." And the $2^{O(r)}$ algorithm only used this fact about $g_M$ itself, at the top level. The error being exponential in $r$ tells me top-level log-concavity alone is too coarse — I'm controlling one global quantity and paying for it. I need something local.

What's the local structure of a Markov chain on a downward-closed family of sets? Independent sets of a matroid form a simplicial complex — the independence complex — pure of dimension $r$, with the bases as maximal faces. And there's a whole machinery for walks on simplicial complexes that I keep circling around. Let me recall it precisely, because I think the chain I want *is* one of those high-order walks. Given a pure $d$-complex with a balanced weight function $w$ (meaning $w(\tau)=\sum_{\sigma\supset\tau,\ \sigma\in X(k+1)}w(\sigma)$), build the bipartite incidence graph $G_k$ between $X(k)$ and $X(k+1)$, edge $\{\tau,\sigma\}$ of weight $w(\sigma)$ when $\tau\subset\sigma$. Walk two steps on it: from a $k$-face go up to a $(k{+}1)$-face proportional to weight, then drop a uniform element to land on a $k$-face — that's the up-walk $P_k^{\wedge}$ on $X(k)$. The opposite parity gives the down-walk $P_{k+1}^{\vee}$ on $X(k+1)$. Let me write the up-walk entries out, because I want to make sure my bases-exchange chain is literally one of these. From $\tau\in X(k)$: choose $\sigma\supset\tau$, $\sigma\in X(k+1)$, with probability $w(\sigma)/w(\tau)$ (using balancedness so these sum to $1$), then delete a uniform one of the $k+1$ elements. The chance to land at $\tau'$ is the chance of having chosen $\sigma=\tau\cup\tau'$ times $\tfrac{1}{k+1}$, so
$$P_k^{\wedge}(\tau,\tau')=\begin{cases}\frac{1}{k+1}&\tau=\tau'\\[2pt]\frac{w(\tau\cup\tau')}{(k+1)\,w(\tau)}&\tau\cup\tau'\in X(k+1)\\[2pt]0&\text{else.}\end{cases}$$
And the down-walk on $X(k+1)$ first drops a uniform element to a $k$-face, then re-completes proportional to weight. Take $X$ to be the independence complex with $w\equiv 1$ on bases (so $w$ on a face counts, up to a factorial, the bases above it), $d=r$. Then the down-walk $P_r^{\vee}$ on the top faces $X(r)=\mathcal B$ is *exactly* my bases-exchange chain: drop a uniform element of the basis, re-add proportional to weight (uniform among completions, in the unweighted case). Good — so $\mathcal M_\mu = P_r^{\vee}$, and I'm not inventing a chain, I'm recognizing it.

Now I need a clean handle on this two-step walk. Let $P_k$ be the simple walk on the bipartite $G_k$,
$$P_k=\begin{bmatrix}0&P_k^{\downarrow}\\ P_k^{\uparrow}&0\end{bmatrix},$$
with $P_k^{\downarrow},P_k^{\uparrow}$ stochastic. It's self-adjoint with respect to the weight-induced inner product on $X(k)\sqcup X(k+1)$ (with the $(k+1)$ factor on the $X(k+1)$ part). Squaring,
$$P_k^2=\begin{bmatrix}P_k^{\downarrow}P_k^{\uparrow}&0\\ 0&P_k^{\uparrow}P_k^{\downarrow}\end{bmatrix},$$
so $P_k^{\wedge}=P_k^{\uparrow}P_k^{\downarrow}$ and $P_{k+1}^{\vee}=P_k^{\downarrow}P_k^{\uparrow}$ are the two diagonal blocks. Both are stochastic, self-adjoint w.r.t. $w$, PSD (they're squares of a self-adjoint operator restricted to a block), and since $AB$ and $BA$ share nonzero spectrum, $P_k^{\wedge}$ and $P_{k+1}^{\vee}$ have the same nonzero eigenvalues. That's the lever: I can study the up-walk on $X(k)$ and read off facts about the down-walk on $X(k+1)$, and in particular $\lambda^*(P_r^{\vee})=\lambda^*(P_{r-1}^{\wedge})$.

So how do I bound $\lambda^*(P_{r-1}^{\wedge})$? This is where the high-dimensional-expander machine comes in, and I have to be careful about which version. Kaufman–Mass started it; Dinur–Kaufman got a clean gap $\frac{1}{k+2}-O((k+1)\lambda)$ but under a *two-sided* hypothesis: every link's $1$-skeleton walk must have *all* nontrivial eigenvalues within $\pm\lambda$ of zero, with $\lambda\ll 1/k^2$. Let me sanity-check that against matroids. A link of the independence complex at a face is the independence complex of a contraction $M/S$ — another matroid. Its $1$-skeleton walk is essentially a normalized Hessian, and there's no reason its negative eigenvalues are tiny; matroid links are full of large negative eigenvalues. So the two-sided requirement is hopeless here. That's the wall again, but now I can see exactly what's wrong with the requirement: it's *symmetric*. It's policing the negative eigenvalues, and the negative eigenvalues are precisely what matroids refuse to control.

But Kaufman and Oppenheim noticed something I should lean on hard: for optimal mixing you only need a *one-sided* condition. Their decomposition theorem says that if only the *second-largest* eigenvalue of each link walk is $\le\lambda$ — with the negative eigenvalues left totally free — you still get the top up-walk a gap $\frac{1}{k+2}-(k+1)\lambda$. In their words, negative eigenvalues of the links don't matter. Stare at that for a second in light of the log-concavity fact. Log-concavity of a polynomial said: the Hessian has *at most one positive eigenvalue*. A link walk has at most one positive eigenvalue means: its top eigenvalue is the trivial $1$ (it's stochastic), and *everything else is $\le 0$*. That is $\lambda=0$ in the one-sided condition — the strongest, best possible case. So the one-sided HDX condition at $\lambda=0$ and "the link's Hessian is log-concave" look like they might be the very same statement. If that's true, then strong log-concavity — log-concavity not just of $g_M$ but of *all* its derivatives, since the link at a face $\tau$ is governed by $\partial_\tau g_M$ — is exactly the hypothesis that makes the high-order walk mix optimally. The exponential-in-$r$ loss of the top-level argument came from only knowing log-concavity at the top; demanding it of every derivative is exactly the locality I was missing.

Let me make the dictionary precise instead of waving at it. I want to associate, to any multiaffine $d$-homogeneous polynomial $p=\sum_S c_S x^S$ with $c_S\ge 0$, a weighted complex $X^p$: maximal faces are the support, $w(S)=c_S$, lower faces by balanced extension. The link of $\tau$ should correspond to differentiation. Let $p_\tau=\big(\prod_{i\in\tau}\partial_i\big)p$, which is $(d-k)$-homogeneous for $\tau\in X^p(k)$. First I need the weights to match the derivatives. Claim: $w(\tau)=(d-k)!\,p_\tau(\mathbf 1)$. Induct downward on $\dim\tau$. At the top, $\dim\tau=d$, $p_\tau=c_\tau$ and $w(\tau)=c_\tau$, done. Stepping down, balancedness gives $w(\tau)=\sum_{\sigma\in X^p(k+1),\sigma\supset\tau}w(\sigma)=\sum_{i\in X^p_\tau(1)}w(\tau\cup\{i\})$. By induction each $w(\tau\cup\{i\})=(d-k-1)!\,p_{\tau\cup\{i\}}(\mathbf 1)$, and since $\partial_i p_\tau=0$ for $i\notin X^p_\tau(1)$ I can extend the sum over all $i$,
$$w(\tau)=(d-k-1)!\sum_{i=1}^n \partial_i p_\tau(\mathbf 1)=(d-k)!\,p_\tau(\mathbf 1),$$
the last step by Euler's identity $\sum_i x_i\partial_i p_\tau=(d-k)p_\tau$ evaluated at $\mathbf 1$. So the dictionary is: dimension $d$ $\leftrightarrow$ degree $d$; weight of $\emptyset$ $\leftrightarrow$ evaluation at $\mathbf 1$; link $\leftrightarrow$ differentiation.

The last row of the dictionary is the one I really need: link's local walk $\leftrightarrow$ (normalized) Hessian. Take the non-lazy version of the link's $1$-skeleton walk, $\tilde P^{\wedge}_{\tau,1}=2(P^{\wedge}_{\tau,1}-I/2)$, whose entries for $i,j\in X^p_\tau$ are $\tilde P^{\wedge}_{\tau,1}(i,j)=\frac{w(\tau\cup\{i,j\})}{w(\tau\cup\{i\})}$. Now look at a normalized derivative-Hessian,
$$\tilde\nabla^2 p_\tau=\frac{1}{d-k-1}\,\mathrm{diag}(\nabla p_\tau(\mathbf 1))^{-1}\,\nabla^2 p_\tau(\mathbf 1),$$
whose $(i,j)$ entry is $\frac{(\partial_i\partial_j p_\tau)(\mathbf 1)}{(d-k-1)(\partial_i p_\tau)(\mathbf 1)}$. Plug the weight identity into both: $w(\tau\cup\{i,j\})=(d-k-2)!\,(\partial_i\partial_j p_\tau)(\mathbf 1)$ and $w(\tau\cup\{i\})=(d-k-1)!\,(\partial_i p_\tau)(\mathbf 1)$, so the ratio is exactly $\frac{(\partial_i\partial_j p_\tau)(\mathbf 1)}{(d-k-1)(\partial_i p_\tau)(\mathbf 1)}$. They match. The link's non-lazy local walk *is* the normalized Hessian of the corresponding derivative:
$$\tilde\nabla^2 p_\tau=\tilde P^{\wedge}_{\tau,1}.$$

Why normalize by $\mathrm{diag}(\nabla p_\tau(\mathbf 1))^{-1}$ at all, instead of staring at $\nabla^2 p_\tau$ raw? Because I want a *stochastic* matrix — a genuine random-walk operator — so I can talk about its spectral gap and mixing. The Hessian is symmetric but its rows don't sum to anything nice; the diagonal scaling fixes the row sums to $1$ (that's exactly Euler's identity again: $(\nabla^2 p_\tau)\mathbf 1=(d-k-1)\nabla p_\tau$). And here's why I get away with it: scaling on the left by a PSD diagonal can't manufacture new positive eigenvalues. Precisely, if $A$ is symmetric with at most one positive eigenvalue and $B\succcurlyeq 0$, then $BA$ has at most one positive eigenvalue. Proof: write $B=C^\intercal C$; $BA=C^\intercal CA$ has the same nonzero spectrum as $CAC^\intercal$; and congruence $A\mapsto CAC^\intercal$ preserves "at most one positive eigenvalue." That last claim is itself just Cauchy interlacing: $A=B'+vv^\intercal$ with $B'\preccurlyeq 0$ (peel off the one positive eigendirection), so $CAC^\intercal=CB'C^\intercal+(Cv)(Cv)^\intercal$, a NSD matrix plus a rank-one bump, which interlacing says has at most one positive eigenvalue. So with $B=\frac{1}{d-k-1}\mathrm{diag}(\nabla p_\tau(\mathbf 1))^{-1}\succcurlyeq 0$ (nonnegative coefficients make $\nabla p_\tau(\mathbf 1)\ge 0$), if $\nabla^2 p_\tau(\mathbf 1)$ has at most one positive eigenvalue then so does $\tilde\nabla^2 p_\tau=\tilde P^{\wedge}_{\tau,1}$.

That closes the loop. $p$ strongly log-concave means $\nabla^2 p_\tau(\mathbf 1)$ has at most one positive eigenvalue for *every* derivative $p_\tau$. Translating: every link's non-lazy local walk $\tilde P^{\wedge}_{\tau,1}$ has at most one positive eigenvalue, i.e. $\lambda_2(\tilde P^{\wedge}_{\tau,1})\le 0$. That's precisely the definition of a $0$-local spectral expander. So:

strongly log-concave polynomial $\Longleftrightarrow$ its complex is a $0$-local spectral expander.

Now I need the local-to-global step: $0$-local spectral expander $\Rightarrow$ the top walk has a real spectral gap. I'll reprove the $\lambda=0$ case from scratch, because I want a clean self-contained statement and, ideally, control of *all* the eigenvalues, not just the second one. The engine is a single Loewner inequality comparing the up-walk and down-walk on the same level:
$$P_k^{\wedge}\preccurlyeq \frac{k}{k+1}P_k^{\vee}+\frac{1}{k+1}I.$$
Let me prove it. Set $M=P_k^{\wedge}-\big(\frac{k}{k+1}P_k^{\vee}+\frac{1}{k+1}I\big)$ and split it by the codimension-one face shared by two $k$-faces: for $\eta\in X(k-1)$ define $M_\eta$ supported on pairs $\tau,\sigma\supset\eta$ with $\tau\cap\sigma=\eta$ off-diagonal, and the right diagonal correction. Then $M=\sum_{\eta\in X(k-1)}M_\eta$, so it suffices that each $M_\eta\preccurlyeq 0$. Computing the off-diagonal entry for $\tau\ne\sigma$, $\tau\cap\sigma=\eta$,
$$M(\tau,\sigma)=\frac{1}{k+1}\Big(\frac{w(\tau\cup\sigma)}{w(\tau)}-\frac{w(\sigma)}{w(\tau\cap\sigma)}\Big)=\frac{1}{k+1}\cdot\frac{w(\tau\cup\sigma)w(\eta)-w(\tau)w(\sigma)}{w(\tau)w(\eta)},$$
and the diagonal entry $M_\eta(\tau,\tau)=-\frac{1}{k+1}\frac{w(\tau)}{w(\eta)}$. A bit of bookkeeping puts this in the form
$$M_\eta=\frac{1}{(k+1)w(\eta)}\,\mathrm{diag}(w_\eta)^{-1}\big(w(\eta)A_\eta-w_\eta w_\eta^\intercal\big),$$
where $w_\eta$ is the vector of $w(\tau)$ over $\tau\supset\eta$ and $A_\eta$ is the weighted adjacency of the $1$-skeleton of the link $X_\eta$ (entries $w(\tau\cup\sigma)$ for $\tau\cup\sigma\in X(k+1)$, $\tau\cap\sigma=\eta$). Now $M_\eta$ is NSD with respect to the $w$-inner product iff $\mathrm{diag}(w_\eta)M_\eta$ is NSD in the usual sense (the diagonal absorbs into the inner product), so the whole thing reduces to
$$A_\eta\preccurlyeq \frac{w_\eta w_\eta^\intercal}{w(\eta)}.$$
And this is where $0$-local spectral expansion pays off. $A_\eta$, as the adjacency of the link's $1$-skeleton, is $(k+1)\,\mathrm{diag}(w_\eta)\,\tilde P^{\wedge}_{\eta,1}$; since the complex is a $0$-local spectral expander, $\tilde P^{\wedge}_{\eta,1}$ has at most one positive eigenvalue, hence (by the same $B\succcurlyeq 0$ times "one positive eigenvalue" lemma) so does $A_\eta$. For a symmetric, entrywise-nonnegative $A$ with at most one positive eigenvalue and row sums $w(i)$, I claim $A\preccurlyeq \frac{ww^\intercal}{\sum_i w(i)}$. Conjugate by $W^{-1/2}$, $W=\mathrm{diag}(w)$: $\mathcal A=W^{-1/2}AW^{-1/2}$ still has at most one positive eigenvalue, and because $A$ has row sums $w$, $\mathcal A\sqrt w=\sqrt w$, so $\sqrt w$ is the unique positive eigendirection with eigenvalue $1$, giving $\mathcal A\preccurlyeq \frac{\sqrt w\sqrt w^\intercal}{\|\sqrt w\|^2}$; conjugate back. Balancedness gives $w(\eta)=\sum_{\tau\supset\eta}w(\tau)=\sum_i w_\eta(i)$, so this is exactly $A_\eta\preccurlyeq w_\eta w_\eta^\intercal/w(\eta)$. So $M_\eta\preccurlyeq 0$ for every $\eta$, and the Loewner inequality holds.

With that inequality I run an induction on $k$ that tracks the *number* of large eigenvalues. Claim: for a $0$-local spectral expander and $0\le k<d$, for each $-1\le i\le k$, $P_k^{\wedge}$ has at most $|X(i)|$ eigenvalues exceeding $1-\frac{i+1}{k+1}$ (with $X(-1)=\emptyset$). Base case $k=1$: $P_1^{\wedge}=\frac{1}{2}(\tilde P_1^{\wedge}+I)$, and $\tilde P_1^{\wedge}$ has exactly one positive eigenvalue (value $1$), so $P_1^{\wedge}$ has eigenvalue $1$ once and everything else $\le 1/2$ — matching the count. Inductive step: $P_{k+1}^{\vee}$ has the same nonzero spectrum as $P_k^{\wedge}$, so the eigenvalue counts for $P_{k+1}^{\vee}$ are inherited; then the Loewner inequality at level $k+1$,
$$P_{k+1}^{\wedge}\preccurlyeq \frac{k+1}{k+2}P_{k+1}^{\vee}+\frac{1}{k+2}I,$$
pushes each count through the increasing affine map $t\mapsto \frac{k+1}{k+2}t+\frac{1}{k+2}$. A threshold $1-\frac{i+1}{k+1}$ for $P_{k+1}^{\vee}$ maps to $\frac{k+1}{k+2}\big(1-\frac{i+1}{k+1}\big)+\frac{1}{k+2}=1-\frac{i+1}{k+2}$, so $P_{k+1}^{\wedge}$ has at most $|X(i)|$ eigenvalues above $1-\frac{i+1}{k+2}$, for $-1\le i\le k$; and the $i=k+1$ bound is the trivial "$P_{k+1}^{\wedge}$ is $|X(k+1)|\times|X(k+1)|$." Induction complete. Taking $i=0$ gives: exactly one eigenvalue above $\frac{k}{k+1}$ (the trivial $1$), so $\lambda_2(P_k^{\wedge})\le \frac{k}{k+1}$.

Now assemble the mixing theorem. Let $\mu$ be a $d$-homogeneous strongly log-concave distribution, $\mathcal M_\mu=P_d^{\vee}$. Since $\mu$ is strongly log-concave, $X^{g_\mu}$ is a $0$-local spectral expander, so by the count theorem $\lambda^*(P_d^{\vee})=\lambda^*(P_{d-1}^{\wedge})\le \frac{d-1}{d}=1-\frac{1}{d}$ (the walks are PSD, so $\lambda^*$ is just $\lambda_2$). Spectral gap $\ge 1/d$. Diaconis–Stroock then bounds the mixing time started at $\tau$,
$$t_\tau(\varepsilon)\le \frac{1}{1-\lambda^*(P_\mu)}\log\frac{1}{\varepsilon\,\mu(\tau)}\le d\,\log\frac{1}{\varepsilon\,\mu(\tau)}.$$
For a rank-$r$ matroid with $\mu$ uniform on bases, $d=r$ and $\mu(\tau)\ge 1/|\mathcal B|\ge 1/n^r$, so starting from any basis $B$,
$$t_B(\varepsilon)\le r\,\log(n^r/\varepsilon)\le r^2\log(n/\varepsilon).$$
Polynomial. There's the sampler. And only an independence oracle is needed to run the chain — checking $B-i+j\in\mathcal I$ is one query. Feed the almost-uniform sampler into the self-reducible sampling-to-counting reduction (restrict / contract) and I get an FPRAS for $|\mathcal B|$ in time polynomial in $n,r,1/\varepsilon,\log(1/\delta)$. Counting independent sets of a fixed size $k$ comes for free by truncating $M$ to rank $k$ (still a matroid), and from those counts the reliability polynomial $C_M(p)=\sum_{S:\mathrm{rank}(S)=r}(1-p)^{|S|}p^{n-|S|}$ for any matroid, via complementation through the dual.

And the Mihail–Vazirani conjecture drops out almost for free. Let $H_M$ be the weighted graph of $P_r^{\vee}=\mathcal M_\mu$ (uniform weights, so each vertex has weighted degree $1$ and $P_r^{\vee}$ is its adjacency matrix). I have $\lambda_2(P_r^{\vee})\le 1-1/r$, so Cheeger gives conductance $\phi(H_M)\ge\frac{1-\lambda_2}{2}\ge\frac{1}{2r}$. Now connect conductance to the *combinatorial* expansion of the unweighted bases-exchange graph $G_M$. A single off-diagonal weight is small: if $P_r^{\vee}(\tau,\tau')>0$ then $|\tau\cap\tau'|=r-1$, and balancedness forces $w(\tau\cap\tau')\ge w(\tau)+w(\tau')=2$, so
$$P_r^{\vee}(\tau,\tau')=\frac{w(\tau')}{r\,w(\tau\cap\tau')}\le \frac{1}{2r}.$$
Therefore for any $S$ with $|S|\le|\mathcal B|/2$, since $\mathrm{vol}_H(S)=|S|$,
$$\phi(H_M)\le \phi(S)=\frac{\sum_{\tau\in S,\ \tau'\notin S}P_r^{\vee}(\tau,\tau')}{|S|}\le \frac{\tfrac{1}{2r}\,|E(S,\bar S)|}{|S|}=\frac{h(S)}{2r}.$$
Combine with $\phi(H_M)\ge\frac{1}{2r}$: $\frac{1}{2r}\le\frac{h(S)}{2r}$, i.e. $h(S)\ge 1$ for all such $S$. So $h(G_M)\ge 1$ — the bases-exchange graph of *every* matroid is a $1$-expander. The conjecture.

There's one debt left. I've been *assuming* the uniform basis distribution is strongly log-concave. Top-level log-concavity of $g_M$ I can take from Hodge theory, but strong log-concavity demands it of every derivative, and I'd rather not lean the whole mixing argument on the heavy Chow-ring machinery. Can I get a self-contained, elementary certificate of strong log-concavity for $g_M$? Here Oppenheim's "descent of spectral gaps" is the converse direction of my dictionary — connectivity of all the link $1$-skeletons plus the spectral condition only at the *bottom* (codimension-two) links forces $0$-local spectral expansion of the whole complex. Translated into polynomial language: to certify strong log-concavity of a $d$-homogeneous $p$ it should be enough that (1) every derivative $\partial_{i_1}\cdots\partial_{i_k}p$ ($k\le d-2$) is *indecomposable* (the polynomial analogue of "the link's $1$-skeleton is connected"), and (2) every $(d-2)$-th derivative — a quadratic — is log-concave at $\mathbf 1$ (the spectral condition at the bottom). Let me prove this directly with calculus and linear algebra rather than via cohomology.

First, why does indecomposability correspond to connectivity, and why is it even necessary? Call $p$ decomposable if $p=g+h$ with $g,h$ in disjoint sets of variables. If a homogeneous nonnegative-coefficient $p$ of degree $\ge 2$ were decomposable and log-concave, then $g,h$ are restrictions of $p$ (set the other variables to $0$), hence each log-concave, hence each has exactly one positive eigenvalue in its Hessian at $\mathbf 1$; but then $\nabla^2 p=\mathrm{diag}(\nabla^2 g,\nabla^2 h)$ has *two* positive eigenvalues — not log-concave. So indecomposability is forced. Good, condition (1) is genuinely necessary.

Now the main induction, on $\deg p$. Degree $\le 2$ is immediate. For $d\ge 3$, write $p_i=\partial_i p$; by induction each $p_i$ is strongly log-concave, so each $\nabla^2 p_i(\mathbf 1)$ has exactly one positive eigenvalue (eigenvector $\mathbf 1$ after normalization). I work with the normalized Hessian $\tilde\nabla^2 p=\frac{1}{d-1}\mathrm{diag}(\nabla p(\mathbf 1))^{-1}\nabla^2 p(\mathbf 1)$, which is stochastic (top eigenvector $\mathbf 1$). To handle a non-symmetric stochastic operator cleanly I use the right inner product,
$$\langle\phi,\psi\rangle_p=(d-1)\sum_j \phi(j)\psi(j)\,\partial_j p(\mathbf 1),$$
with respect to which $\tilde\nabla^2 p$ is self-adjoint and $\langle\phi,(\tilde\nabla^2 p)\psi\rangle_p=\langle\phi,\nabla^2 p(\mathbf 1)\psi\rangle$. By Euler's identity $(d-2)\nabla^2 p=\sum_k x_k\nabla^2 p_k$ evaluated at $\mathbf 1$, I can decompose,
$$\langle\phi,(\tilde\nabla^2 p)\psi\rangle_p=\frac{1}{d-2}\sum_{k=1}^n\langle\phi,(\tilde\nabla^2 p_k)\psi\rangle_{p_k}.$$
Let $\mu$ be an eigenvalue of $\tilde\nabla^2 p$ with eigenvector $\phi$. I'll show $\mu\le\mu^2$, which forces $\mu\le 0$ or $\mu\ge 1$; since the operator is stochastic $\mu\le 1$, so $\mu\le 0$ or $\mu=1$; and indecomposability of $p$ makes the underlying weighted graph of $\nabla^2 p$ connected, so by Cheeger the eigenvalue $1$ is simple — only the constant direction. That gives exactly one positive eigenvalue, i.e. log-concavity of $p$; combined with the inductive strong log-concavity of the $p_i$, $p$ is strongly log-concave.

So the whole thing rests on $\mu\le\mu^2$. Start from
$$\mu\|\phi\|_p^2=\langle\phi,(\tilde\nabla^2 p)\phi\rangle_p=\frac{1}{d-2}\sum_k\langle\phi,(\tilde\nabla^2 p_k)\phi\rangle_{p_k}.$$
For each $k$, split $\phi$ orthogonally along $\mathbf 1$ in the $\langle\cdot,\cdot\rangle_{p_k}$ inner product, $\phi=\phi_k^{\perp\mathbf 1}+\phi_k^{\mathbf 1}$ with $\phi_k^{\mathbf 1}=\frac{\langle\phi,\mathbf 1\rangle_{p_k}}{\|\mathbf 1\|_{p_k}^2}\mathbf 1$. Because $\tilde\nabla^2 p_k$ has its single positive eigenvalue exactly in the $\mathbf 1$ direction, the component orthogonal to $\mathbf 1$ contributes nonpositively: $\langle\phi_k^{\perp\mathbf 1},(\tilde\nabla^2 p_k)\phi_k^{\perp\mathbf 1}\rangle_{p_k}\le 0$. So
$$\mu\|\phi\|_p^2\le \frac{1}{d-2}\sum_k\langle\phi_k^{\mathbf 1},(\tilde\nabla^2 p_k)\phi_k^{\mathbf 1}\rangle_{p_k}=\frac{1}{d-2}\sum_k\langle\phi_k^{\mathbf 1},\phi_k^{\mathbf 1}\rangle_{p_k}=\frac{1}{d-2}\sum_k\frac{\langle\phi,\mathbf 1\rangle_{p_k}^2}{\langle\mathbf 1,\mathbf 1\rangle_{p_k}},$$
where the middle equality used $\tilde\nabla^2 p_k\mathbf 1=\mathbf 1$. Now evaluate the two pieces. By Euler, $\langle\mathbf 1,\mathbf 1\rangle_{p_k}=(d-2)\sum_i\partial_i p_k(\mathbf 1)=(d-2)(d-1)p_k(\mathbf 1)$. And $\langle\phi,\mathbf 1\rangle_{p_k}=(d-2)\sum_i\phi(i)\partial_i p_k(\mathbf 1)=(d-2)\,(\nabla^2 p(\mathbf 1)\phi)(k)$, since $\partial_i p_k(\mathbf 1)=(\nabla^2 p(\mathbf 1))_{ik}$. So the ratio is
$$\frac{\langle\phi,\mathbf 1\rangle_{p_k}}{\langle\mathbf 1,\mathbf 1\rangle_{p_k}}=\frac{(\nabla^2 p(\mathbf 1)\phi)(k)}{(d-1)p_k(\mathbf 1)}=(\tilde\nabla^2 p(\mathbf 1)\phi)(k)=\mu\,\phi(k),$$
the last step because $\phi$ is the $\mu$-eigenvector. Substitute back:
$$\mu\|\phi\|_p^2\le \frac{1}{d-2}\sum_k\frac{\langle\phi,\mathbf 1\rangle_{p_k}^2}{\langle\mathbf 1,\mathbf 1\rangle_{p_k}}=\frac{\mu}{d-2}\sum_k\phi(k)\langle\phi,\mathbf 1\rangle_{p_k}=\mu\sum_k\phi(k)(\nabla^2 p(\mathbf 1)\phi)(k)=\mu\,\langle\phi,(\tilde\nabla^2 p)\phi\rangle_p=\mu^2\|\phi\|_p^2.$$
So $\mu\le\mu^2$, as needed. The characterization is proved.

Now cash it in for the matroid. The derivatives of $g_M$ are themselves basis generating polynomials of contractions: $\partial_S g_M=g_{M/S}$. (If $S$ has a repeat, multiaffineness kills it; if $S\notin\mathcal I$, no basis contains $S$ and it's zero; otherwise $g_{M/S}=\sum_{B\supset S}\boldsymbol\lambda^{B\setminus S}x^{B\setminus S}$.) For condition (1): for $|S|<r$, $M/S$ is a matroid of rank $\ge 2$, and the exchange axiom immediately makes $g_{M/S}$ indecomposable — if it split into disjoint variable blocks, two bases in different blocks couldn't be connected by an exchange, contradicting the axiom. For condition (2): take $|S|=r-2$, so $M/S$ has rank $2$ and $g_{M/S}=\sum_{\{i,j\}\text{ indep in }M/S}\lambda_i\lambda_j\,x_ix_j$ is quadratic, with Hessian
$$(\nabla^2 g_{M/S})_{ij}=\begin{cases}\lambda_i\lambda_j&\{i,j\}\text{ independent in }M/S\\0&\text{otherwise.}\end{cases}$$
I need this to have at most one positive eigenvalue. Here the matroid partition property is exactly the right structural fact: the non-loops of $M/S$ partition into parallel classes $B_1,\dots,B_m$ (parallel = the pair is dependent), and $\{i,j\}$ is independent iff $i,j$ lie in *different* classes. So the "missing" entries (the zeros) are exactly the within-class pairs, and
$$\nabla^2 g_{M/S}=\boldsymbol\lambda_B\boldsymbol\lambda_B^\intercal-\sum_{t=1}^m\boldsymbol\lambda_{B_t}\boldsymbol\lambda_{B_t}^\intercal\preccurlyeq \boldsymbol\lambda_B\boldsymbol\lambda_B^\intercal,$$
where $\boldsymbol\lambda_T$ is $\boldsymbol\lambda$ restricted to $T$. The right side is rank-one PSD, so $\nabla^2 g_{M/S}$ has at most one positive eigenvalue — log-concave. Both conditions hold, so $g_M$ is strongly log-concave for every matroid and every positive external field $\boldsymbol\lambda$, with no appeal to Hodge theory in this route. The mixing theorem now applies unconditionally to all matroids.

The same quadratic-Hessian template handles the other distributions, which is the payoff of having an elementary criterion. Take the random-cluster polynomial $f_{M,k,q}(x)=\sum_{|S|=k}q^{-\mathrm{rank}(S)}\boldsymbol\lambda^S x^S$ for $0<q\le 1$. Its derivatives have a monomial for every small set, so indecomposability is immediate. For the quadratic at $|S|=k-2$, $(\nabla^2\partial_S f)_{ij}=q^{-\mathrm{rank}(S)}q^{-\mathrm{rank}_{M/S}(\{i,j\})}\lambda_i\lambda_j$; setting $A=q^{\mathrm{rank}(S)}\nabla^2\partial_S f$ and choosing $v$ with $v_i=\lambda_i$ on loops of $M/S$ and $v_i=q^{-1}\lambda_i$ on non-loops, a check gives $(vv^\intercal-A)_{ij}=(q^{-2}-q^{-1})\lambda_i\lambda_j$ on parallel non-loop pairs and $0$ elsewhere, so $vv^\intercal-A=(q^{-2}-q^{-1})\sum_t\boldsymbol\lambda_{B_t}\boldsymbol\lambda_{B_t}^\intercal$. Since $0<q\le1$ makes $q^{-2}-q^{-1}\ge0$, this is PSD, hence $A\preccurlyeq vv^\intercal$, at most one positive eigenvalue. So $f_{M,k,q}$ is strongly log-concave, giving an FPRAS for $Z_M(p,q)=q^{r+1}\sum_k p^k f_{M,k,q}(\mathbf 1)$ for all $0<q\le1$, $p\ge0$ — and through the standard change of variables, an FPRAS for the Tutte polynomial $T_M(x,y)$ on the region $y\ge1$, $0\le(x-1)(y-1)\le1$. (As $q\to0$, $q^r f_{M,r,q}$ tends coefficientwise to $g_M$, so this even subsumes the bases case.)

One more closure property, again purely from the quadratic test. Claim: raising every coefficient of a multiaffine homogeneous strongly log-concave $f=\sum_S c_S x^S$ to a power $0\le\alpha\le1$ keeps it strongly log-concave. $\alpha=1$ is trivial; $\alpha=0$ by coefficientwise limit; so take $0<\alpha<1$ and (by limits) all $c_S>0$. For $T$ with $|T|=k-2$, $\nabla^2\partial_T f=[c_{T\cup\{i,j\}}]_{ij}$ has at most one positive eigenvalue and nonnegative entries, so write it as $vv^\intercal-A$ with $A\succcurlyeq0$; Perron–Frobenius (the matrix has strictly positive entries) makes $v$ strictly positive. Then $c_{T\cup\{i,j\}}^\alpha=(v_iv_j-A_{ij})^\alpha=v_i^\alpha v_j^\alpha\big(1-\tfrac{A_{ij}}{v_iv_j}\big)^\alpha$, and I Taylor-expand $(1-x)^\alpha$. Its series about $0$ is
$$\sum_{m\ge0}\Big(\prod_{j=0}^{m-1}\frac{\alpha-j}{1+j}\Big)(-1)^m x^m=1-\sum_{m\ge1}\Big(\prod_{j=0}^{m-1}\Big|\frac{\alpha-j}{1+j}\Big|\Big)x^m,$$
where the sign flip uses $0<\alpha<1$ so that every factor $\frac{\alpha-j}{1+j}$ with $j\ge1$ is negative — every $x^m$ coefficient ($m\ge1$) is negative. The radius of convergence is $1$ by the ratio test ($\big|\frac{a_{m+1}}{a_m}\big|=\frac{|\alpha-m|}{1+m}\to1$), and the expansion is valid at $x=\frac{A_{ij}}{v_iv_j}$ because $|A_{ij}|<v_iv_j$: on the diagonal $A_{ii}\ge0$ and $v_i^2-A_{ii}=(\nabla^2\partial_T f)_{ii}>0$ give $0\le A_{ii}<v_i^2$; off-diagonal, PSD of $A$ forces the $2\times2$ minor $A_{ii}A_{jj}-A_{ij}^2\ge0$, so $|A_{ij}|\le\sqrt{A_{ii}A_{jj}}<v_iv_j$. Assembling entrywise,
$$\nabla^2\partial_T f_\alpha=\underbrace{[v_i^\alpha v_j^\alpha]_{ij}}_{(1)}-\underbrace{\sum_{m\ge1}\Big(\prod_{j=0}^{m-1}\big|\tfrac{\alpha-j}{1+j}\big|\Big)\Big([v_i^\alpha v_j^\alpha]_{ij}\circ\big[\tfrac{A_{ij}}{v_iv_j}\big]^{\circ m}\Big)}_{(2)}.$$
Piece (1) $=[v_i^\alpha]_i[v_i^\alpha]_i^\intercal$ is rank-one PSD. Piece (2): $\big[\tfrac{A_{ij}}{v_iv_j}\big]=A\circ\big[\tfrac{1}{v_iv_j}\big]$ is PSD by the Schur product theorem (Hadamard product of two PSD matrices), and iterating Schur, every $[v_i^\alpha v_j^\alpha]\circ\big[\tfrac{A_{ij}}{v_iv_j}\big]^{\circ m}\succcurlyeq0$, so (2) is a nonnegative combination of PSD matrices, hence PSD. So $\nabla^2\partial_T f_\alpha=$ (rank-one PSD) $-$ (PSD), at most one positive eigenvalue. Done — and this unifies several previously separate facts about $k$-DPP weights $\det(L_S)^\alpha$ for $\alpha\in\{0,\tfrac12,1\}$ into one statement valid for all $\alpha\in[0,1]$, so I can sample the smoothed $k$-DPP $\Pr[S]\propto\det(L_S)^\alpha$ in polynomial time.

Let me trace the causal chain once. The chain I want to run is the down-walk on the top faces of the matroid independence complex; its spectral gap is what I cannot see directly. Encoding the distribution as the generating polynomial turns differentiation into "take a link" and the normalized derivative-Hessian into the link's local random-walk matrix — so "the Hessian has at most one positive eigenvalue" (log-concavity of that derivative) becomes "the link walk has spectral gap, with negative eigenvalues free" ($0$-local spectral expansion). Strong log-concavity says this holds at *every* link, which is exactly the one-sided high-dimensional-expander hypothesis at its best value $\lambda=0$; a single Loewner inequality plus Cauchy interlacing propagates it up the dimensions and pins the top walk's second eigenvalue at $\le 1-1/d$. For matroids $d=r$, and an elementary indecomposable-plus-quadratic criterion — leaning on the exchange axiom and the matroid partition property, not on Hodge theory — certifies the strong log-concavity. Spectral gap $1/r$ gives mixing time $r\log(n^r/\varepsilon)$, hence an almost-uniform basis sampler, hence (self-reducibly) an FPRAS for the number of bases; and Cheeger turns the same gap into edge expansion $\ge1$ of the bases-exchange graph, settling Mihail–Vazirani.

The clean final statements I've landed on:

**Theorem (rapid mixing from strong log-concavity).** Let $\mu:2^{[n]}\to\mathbb R_{\ge0}$ be a $d$-homogeneous strongly log-concave distribution, $\mathcal M_\mu$ the down-up walk on $\mathrm{supp}(\mu)$ (drop a uniform element, re-add proportional to $\mu$). Then $\mathcal M_\mu$ has spectral gap $\ge 1/d$, and for any $\tau\in\mathrm{supp}(\mu)$ and $0<\varepsilon<1$,
$$t_\tau(\varepsilon)\le d\,\log\frac{1}{\varepsilon\,\mu(\tau)}.$$

**Corollary (matroid bases).** For a rank-$r$ matroid on $[n]$ given by an independence oracle, the bases-exchange walk mixes in $t_B(\varepsilon)\le r\log(n^r/\varepsilon)\le r^2\log(n/\varepsilon)$ steps; hence there is an FPRAS for the number of bases (and of independent sets of any fixed size) running in time polynomial in $n,r,1/\varepsilon,\log(1/\delta)$, using only the oracle.

**Theorem (Mihail–Vazirani).** For every matroid $M$, the bases-exchange graph has edge expansion $h(G_M)\ge1$.
