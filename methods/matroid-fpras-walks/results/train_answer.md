I have a matroid $M=([n],\mathcal I)$ of rank $r$ handed to me only through an independence oracle, and I want to count its bases $|\mathcal B|$ up to a $(1\pm\varepsilon)$ multiplicative factor in time polynomial in $n,r,1/\varepsilon,\log(1/\delta)$. Exact counting is $\#P$-hard, and there is a brutal oracle lower bound: no deterministic algorithm making polynomially many independence queries can beat a $2^{\Omega(r/\log n)}$ approximation factor. So I am pushed into randomization, and the standard randomized route is to count by sampling. The problem is self-reducible — deleting an element and contracting an element $M/i$ both give smaller matroids, and the bases of $M$ split into those through $i$ (a contraction) and those avoiding $i$ (a deletion) — so by the Jerrum–Valiant–Vazirani equivalence an FPRAS is the same thing as a fully polynomial almost-uniform sampler. The whole task collapses to one question: can I sample an almost-uniform random basis of an arbitrary matroid in polynomial time? The obvious chain is the bases-exchange (down–up) walk: sit at a basis $B$, drop a uniformly random element $i\in B$, and among all $j$ that complete $B-i$ back to a basis pick one. It is reversible with uniform stationary distribution and connected by the exchange axiom; the only question is whether it mixes fast — which is exactly the thirty-year-old Mihail–Vazirani conjecture that the bases-exchange graph $G_M$ has edge expansion at least $1$.

The existing attacks all stop short. Feder and Mihail handle *balanced* matroids — those where $M$ and every minor have pairwise negatively correlated uniform basis measure, $\Pr[i,j\in B]\le\Pr[i]\Pr[j]$ — by an induction exploiting negative association, but that hypothesis is simply false for many matroids (the canonical wall is the matroid of acyclic edge-subsets of size at most $k$ of a graph for $k<|V|-1$), so the route can never reach all matroids. A deterministic algorithm built on log-concavity of the bases generating polynomial gives only a $2^{O(r)}$-approximation — exponential in the rank, not a $(1\pm\varepsilon)$ scheme — because it controls the polynomial only at the top level. And the high-dimensional-expander machinery of Kaufman–Mass and Dinur–Kaufman gives a clean spectral gap $\frac{1}{k+2}-O((k+1)\lambda)$, but under a *two-sided* hypothesis requiring every link's $1$-skeleton walk to have all nontrivial eigenvalues within $\pm\lambda$; matroid links are full of large negative eigenvalues, so the two-sided requirement is hopeless and the bound is vacuous. The failure is always the same: the hypothesis being imposed is symmetric, policing exactly the negative eigenvalues that matroids refuse to control.

I propose to certify rapid mixing of the bases-exchange walk through *strong log-concavity* of the bases generating polynomial, by way of a precise dictionary between multiaffine polynomials and weighted simplicial complexes that turns log-concavity into the one-sided high-dimensional-expander condition at its best value. Encode a $d$-homogeneous distribution $\mu:2^{[n]}\to\mathbb R_{\ge0}$ by its generating polynomial $g_\mu(x)=\sum_S\mu(S)\,x^S$ (multiaffine, degree $\le1$ in each variable), and build a weighted complex $X^{g_\mu}$ whose maximal faces are $\mathrm{supp}(\mu)$ with weight $w(S)=\mu(S)$ and whose lower faces are filled in by balanced extension $w(\tau)=\sum_{\sigma\supset\tau,\ \sigma\in X(k+1)}w(\sigma)$. The dictionary reads: dimension $d$ $\leftrightarrow$ degree $d$; weight of $\emptyset$ $\leftrightarrow$ evaluation at $\mathbf 1$; the link of a face $\tau$ $\leftrightarrow$ the derivative $p_\tau=\big(\prod_{i\in\tau}\partial_i\big)g_\mu$; and — the load-bearing row — the local random walk on the link's $1$-skeleton $\leftrightarrow$ the normalized Hessian of that derivative.

The first identity grounding the dictionary is the weight–derivative correspondence $w(\tau)=(d-k)!\,p_\tau(\mathbf 1)$ for $\tau\in X^{g_\mu}(k)$, proved by downward induction on $\dim\tau$: at the top $p_\tau=c_\tau=w(\tau)$, and the step uses balancedness together with Euler's identity $\sum_i x_i\partial_i p_\tau=(d-k)p_\tau$ at $\mathbf 1$, extending the sum over all $i$ because $\partial_i p_\tau=0$ off the link. From this the last row is exact. The non-lazy version of the link's $1$-skeleton walk has entries $\tilde P^{\wedge}_{\tau,1}(i,j)=w(\tau\cup\{i,j\})/w(\tau\cup\{i\})$, and plugging in the weight identity ($w(\tau\cup\{i,j\})=(d-k-2)!\,(\partial_i\partial_j p_\tau)(\mathbf 1)$, $w(\tau\cup\{i\})=(d-k-1)!\,(\partial_i p_\tau)(\mathbf 1)$) gives exactly the $(i,j)$ entry of the normalized derivative-Hessian

$$\tilde\nabla^2 p_\tau=\frac{1}{d-k-1}\,\mathrm{diag}(\nabla p_\tau(\mathbf 1))^{-1}\,\nabla^2 p_\tau(\mathbf 1),$$

so $\tilde\nabla^2 p_\tau=\tilde P^{\wedge}_{\tau,1}$. The diagonal normalization is not cosmetic: I want a genuine stochastic operator so I can speak of its spectral gap, and $\mathrm{diag}(\nabla p_\tau(\mathbf 1))^{-1}$ forces row sums to $1$ (Euler again, $(\nabla^2 p_\tau)\mathbf 1=(d-k-1)\nabla p_\tau$) while never manufacturing a new positive eigenvalue — left-multiplication by a PSD diagonal $B$ preserves "at most one positive eigenvalue," because $B=C^\intercal C$ makes $BA$ share its nonzero spectrum with the congruence $CAC^\intercal$, and congruence preserves that count by Cauchy interlacing (peel off the one positive eigendirection, $A=B'+vv^\intercal$ with $B'\preccurlyeq0$).

This is the conceptual hinge. A polynomial is log-concave iff its Hessian has at most one positive eigenvalue (because $\nabla^2\log p=\frac{p\,\nabla^2 p-(\nabla p)(\nabla p)^\intercal}{p^2}$ subtracts a rank-one PSD matrix, which by interlacing drags at most one eigenvalue below zero). So *strong* log-concavity — log-concavity not only of $g_\mu$ but of every derivative $p_\tau$ — says exactly that every link walk $\tilde P^{\wedge}_{\tau,1}$ has $\lambda_2\le0$, i.e. the complex is a $0$-local spectral expander, the strongest case of the *one-sided* high-dimensional-expander condition where the negative link eigenvalues are entirely free. That is precisely the structure matroids do possess and the symmetric two-sided hypothesis wrongly threw away.

What remains is local-to-global: a $0$-local spectral expander forces a real spectral gap at the top. The walks themselves come from the bipartite incidence walk $P_k=\begin{bmatrix}0&P_k^{\downarrow}\\P_k^{\uparrow}&0\end{bmatrix}$ between $X(k)$ and $X(k+1)$; squaring gives the up-walk $P_k^{\wedge}=P_k^{\uparrow}P_k^{\downarrow}$ on $X(k)$ and the down-walk $P_{k+1}^{\vee}=P_k^{\downarrow}P_k^{\uparrow}$ on $X(k+1)$ as the two diagonal blocks — both stochastic, $w$-self-adjoint, PSD, and sharing their nonzero spectrum because $AB$ and $BA$ do. The bases-exchange chain is literally $\mathcal M_\mu=P_d^{\vee}$, so $\lambda^*(\mathcal M_\mu)=\lambda^*(P_{d-1}^{\wedge})$. The engine is a single Loewner inequality on each level,

$$P_k^{\wedge}\preccurlyeq\frac{k}{k+1}P_k^{\vee}+\frac{1}{k+1}I,$$

proved by decomposing $M=P_k^{\wedge}-(\frac{k}{k+1}P_k^{\vee}+\frac{1}{k+1}I)=\sum_{\eta\in X(k-1)}M_\eta$ over codimension-one faces and showing each $M_\eta\preccurlyeq0$. Each $M_\eta$ reduces (the diagonal absorbs into the $w$-inner product) to the claim $A_\eta\preccurlyeq w_\eta w_\eta^\intercal/w(\eta)$, where $A_\eta$ is the weighted adjacency of the link $X_\eta$'s $1$-skeleton. Since $A_\eta=(k+1)\,\mathrm{diag}(w_\eta)\,\tilde P^{\wedge}_{\eta,1}$ and $0$-local expansion makes $\tilde P^{\wedge}_{\eta,1}$ have at most one positive eigenvalue, the PSD-times-one-positive-eigenvalue lemma gives the same for $A_\eta$; and a symmetric entrywise-nonnegative matrix with at most one positive eigenvalue and row sums $w$ satisfies $A\preccurlyeq ww^\intercal/\sum_i w(i)$ (conjugate by $W^{-1/2}$: $\sqrt w$ becomes the unique positive eigendirection with eigenvalue $1$), and balancedness gives $w(\eta)=\sum_i w_\eta(i)$, closing it. With the Loewner inequality, an induction on $k$ tracks the *number* of large eigenvalues: $P_k^{\wedge}$ has at most $|X(i)|$ eigenvalues exceeding $1-\frac{i+1}{k+1}$ for each $-1\le i\le k$. The base case $k=1$ is $P_1^{\wedge}=\frac12(\tilde P_1^{\wedge}+I)$ with one positive eigenvalue; the step uses that $P_{k+1}^{\vee}$ inherits the spectrum of $P_k^{\wedge}$ and pushes each count through the increasing affine map $t\mapsto\frac{k+1}{k+2}t+\frac{1}{k+2}$, under which the threshold $1-\frac{i+1}{k+1}$ maps to $1-\frac{i+1}{k+2}$. Taking $i=0$: exactly one eigenvalue above $\frac{k}{k+1}$ (the trivial $1$), so $\lambda_2(P_k^{\wedge})\le\frac{k}{k+1}$ and $\lambda^*(\mathcal M_\mu)\le1-1/d$. The spectral gap is at least $1/d$, and Diaconis–Stroock turns it into the mixing-time bound.

I owe one debt: that the uniform basis distribution actually *is* strongly log-concave. Top-level log-concavity of $g_M$ follows from Hodge theory, but I do not want to lean the whole argument on the Chow-ring machinery to get it for every derivative, so I give a self-contained criterion. A $d$-homogeneous nonnegative-coefficient $p$ is strongly log-concave at $\mathbf 1$ provided (1) every derivative $\partial_{i_1}\cdots\partial_{i_k}p$ with $k\le d-2$ is *indecomposable* (does not split into two disjoint variable blocks — the polynomial analogue of "the link's $1$-skeleton is connected," and genuinely necessary, since a decomposable log-concave $p$ would give a block-diagonal Hessian with two positive eigenvalues), and (2) every $(d-2)$-th derivative — a quadratic — is log-concave at $\mathbf 1$. The proof inducts on $\deg p$ with each $p_i=\partial_i p$ already strongly log-concave. For an eigenpair $(\mu,\phi)$ of $\tilde\nabla^2 p$, the Euler decomposition $(d-2)\nabla^2 p=\sum_k x_k\nabla^2 p_k$ at $\mathbf 1$ lets me write $\langle\phi,(\tilde\nabla^2 p)\phi\rangle_p=\frac{1}{d-2}\sum_k\langle\phi,(\tilde\nabla^2 p_k)\phi\rangle_{p_k}$ in the inner product $\langle\phi,\psi\rangle_{p_k}=(d-1)\sum_j\phi(j)\psi(j)\partial_j p_k(\mathbf 1)$. Splitting $\phi$ along $\mathbf 1$ in each $\langle\cdot,\cdot\rangle_{p_k}$, the component orthogonal to $\mathbf 1$ contributes nonpositively (each $\tilde\nabla^2 p_k$ has its lone positive eigenvalue exactly in the $\mathbf 1$ direction), and using $\frac{\langle\phi,\mathbf 1\rangle_{p_k}}{\langle\mathbf 1,\mathbf 1\rangle_{p_k}}=(\tilde\nabla^2 p(\mathbf 1)\phi)(k)=\mu\,\phi(k)$ one gets $\mu\|\phi\|_p^2\le\mu^2\|\phi\|_p^2$. Hence $\mu\le0$ or $\mu=1$; indecomposability makes the graph of $\nabla^2 p$ connected, so by Cheeger the eigenvalue $1$ is simple — exactly one positive eigenvalue, i.e. log-concavity of $p$.

Cashing this in for the matroid: the derivatives are themselves basis generating polynomials of contractions, $\partial_S g_M=g_{M/S}$. For $|S|<r$, $M/S$ has rank $\ge2$ and the exchange axiom forces $g_{M/S}$ to be indecomposable (two bases in disjoint variable blocks could not be joined by an exchange). For $|S|=r-2$, $g_{M/S}$ is the quadratic with $(\nabla^2 g_{M/S})_{ij}=\lambda_i\lambda_j$ when $\{i,j\}$ is independent in $M/S$ and $0$ otherwise; the matroid partition property says the non-loops partition into parallel classes $B_1,\dots,B_m$ with $\{i,j\}$ independent iff they lie in different classes, so the zeros are exactly the within-class pairs and

$$\nabla^2 g_{M/S}=\boldsymbol\lambda_B\boldsymbol\lambda_B^\intercal-\sum_{t=1}^m\boldsymbol\lambda_{B_t}\boldsymbol\lambda_{B_t}^\intercal\preccurlyeq\boldsymbol\lambda_B\boldsymbol\lambda_B^\intercal,$$

a rank-one PSD upper bound, so at most one positive eigenvalue. Both conditions hold, so $g_M$ is strongly log-concave for every matroid and every positive external field $\boldsymbol\lambda$, with no appeal to Hodge theory. The mixing theorem now applies unconditionally. The same quadratic-Hessian template extends the result: for the random-cluster polynomial $f_{M,k,q}$ with $0<q\le1$ the quadratic $A=q^{\mathrm{rank}(S)}\nabla^2\partial_S f$ satisfies $A\preccurlyeq vv^\intercal$ with $v_i=\lambda_i$ on loops and $q^{-1}\lambda_i$ on non-loops (because $vv^\intercal-A=(q^{-2}-q^{-1})\sum_t\boldsymbol\lambda_{B_t}\boldsymbol\lambda_{B_t}^\intercal\succcurlyeq0$), giving an FPRAS for $Z_M(p,q)$ and the Tutte polynomial on $\{y\ge1,\ 0\le(x-1)(y-1)\le1\}$; and raising every coefficient to a power $\alpha\in[0,1]$ preserves strong log-concavity (Taylor-expand $(1-x)^\alpha$ with all higher coefficients negative, then the Schur product theorem), unifying the $k$-DPP weights $\det(L_S)^\alpha$ into one statement.

The clean final statements I have landed on are these. *Theorem (rapid mixing from strong log-concavity).* Let $\mu:2^{[n]}\to\mathbb R_{\ge0}$ be a $d$-homogeneous strongly log-concave distribution, $\mathcal M_\mu$ the down–up walk on $\mathrm{supp}(\mu)$ (drop a uniform element, re-add proportional to $\mu$). Then $\mathcal M_\mu$ has spectral gap $\ge1/d$, and for any $\tau\in\mathrm{supp}(\mu)$ and $0<\varepsilon<1$,

$$t_\tau(\varepsilon)\le d\,\log\frac{1}{\varepsilon\,\mu(\tau)}.$$

*Corollary (matroid bases).* For a rank-$r$ matroid on $[n]$ given by an independence oracle, the bases-exchange walk mixes in $t_B(\varepsilon)\le r\log(n^r/\varepsilon)\le r^2\log(n/\varepsilon)$ steps; hence there is an FPRAS for the number of bases (and of independent sets of any fixed size, by truncating to rank $k$) running in time polynomial in $n,r,1/\varepsilon,\log(1/\delta)$, using only the oracle. *Theorem (Mihail–Vazirani).* For every matroid $M$, the bases-exchange graph has edge expansion $h(G_M)\ge1$ — proved because the weighted graph $H_M$ of $P_r^{\vee}$ has $\lambda_2\le1-1/r$, so Cheeger gives $\phi(H_M)\ge\frac{1}{2r}$, while each off-diagonal weight $P_r^{\vee}(\tau,\tau')=\frac{w(\tau')}{r\,w(\tau\cap\tau')}\le\frac{1}{2r}$ (balancedness gives $w(\tau\cap\tau')\ge2$), so for $|S|\le|\mathcal B|/2$ one has $\frac{1}{2r}\le\phi(S)\le\frac{h(S)}{2r}$, i.e. $h(S)\ge1$.

The chain itself is the obvious one; the certificate that it mixes in polynomial time is the whole contribution, and only the independence oracle is ever queried — checking $B-i+j\in\mathcal I$ is one query.

class Matroid:
    def __init__(self, n, independent_fn):
        self.n = n
        self.independent = independent_fn          # frozenset -> bool

def sample_basis(M, B0, eps, rng):
    """Almost-uniform basis via the bases-exchange (down-up) walk.
       Strong log-concavity of g_M => spectral gap >= 1/r => this many steps suffice."""
    r = len(B0)
    T = int(np.ceil(r * (r * np.log(max(M.n, 2)) + np.log(1.0 / eps))))  # r*log(n^r/eps)
    B = set(B0)

        i = rng.choice(list(B)); B.remove(i)        # drop a uniform element

        B.add(rng.choice(add))                       # re-add a uniform completion
