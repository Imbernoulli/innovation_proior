Let me start from what actually hurts. I have a language model of fixed size, a fixed optimizer, a fixed data stream, and a fixed compute budget, and the one lever still meaningfully moving my final validation loss is the learning-rate schedule — the function that says how big a step to take at each iteration. Everyone, including me, reaches for the same curve: warm up linearly from near zero to a peak `η` over the first slice of steps, then anneal `η` down a cosine all the way to a small floor, roughly a tenth of the peak, over the rest of the run. Loshchilov and Hutter gave me that cosine shape, `η_t = η_min + ½(η_max − η_min)(1 + cos(π · T_cur/T_i))`, as a smooth replacement for the old step-decay-by-a-constant-factor-at-preset-epochs; the single-cycle version, warmup then one cosine descent to the floor, is what large-model practice settled on. It works. So why am I unhappy with it?

The unhappiness is concrete and it is about one hyper-parameter. The cosine has a length, `T` — the step at which it first hits its floor — and in practice I set `T` to the total number of steps `S` of the run. And it has to be `T = S`, not just out of taste. Hoffmann and colleagues showed that `T > S` — a cosine cycle longer than the run, so I stop while the rate is still partway down — measurably hurts the final model, and that a cosine whose cycle far overshoots the run overestimates the loss it would actually reach at intermediate points; meanwhile `T = S` is both the best and the most compute-efficient. And the other direction is just as pinned: Kaplan and colleagues showed the final loss improves as the learning rate *summed over the whole run* goes up, as long as I keep a warmup and still anneal to near zero at the end. A cosine with `T < S` starts descending too early, so it bleeds summed learning rate, and it loses. The small-model sweep says the same thing: on a 0.036B net at `S = 20N, 40N, 60N, 80N`, the lowest loss at each budget is always the cosine whose `T` exactly equals that `S`. Both shorter and longer cycles lose. So the standard schedule is genuinely good *only* on the diagonal `T = S`.

Now sit with what that means operationally, because that is where the real cost is. The rate at *every* step in the middle of my run depends on where the end is. If I want a converged model at a 40N budget and another at an 80N budget, I cannot get them from one run — the 40N cosine was already curving down toward step 40N, so by the time I reach 80N I am long past its floor and the schedule is meaningless there. I have to launch two separate full runs, each redoing the entire expensive high-rate phase from scratch, just because the *ending* differs. I cannot leave a run open-ended and decide later how long to train. And if I want to study how loss scales across, say, six model sizes and six data sizes — fit `L(N, D)` to populate a grid — the standard way needs a separate full run per cell, so the cost is quadratic in the number of points along the two axes. All of this traces back to the same fact: the cosine welds the rate I use *today* to the horizon I picked *yesterday*.

So let me ask the sharper question. *Why* does `T = S` cosine win? If I can name the mechanism rather than just the curve, maybe I can get the mechanism without the welding. Looking at the evidence, two things are happening and the cosine bundles them. First, compared to a shorter cycle, `T = S` keeps the rate high for longer — and a long high-rate phase seems to help the optimizer find a better region of the landscape, which lines up with Kaplan's "more summed learning rate is better." Second, compared to a constant rate or an overlong cycle, `T = S` actually carries out a *thorough* final descent of the rate, and that final descent seems to do something special — a fast, late drop in loss that a high constant rate just never produces. The first effect wants the high-rate phase to be as long as the budget allows. The second wants a genuine, complete decay at the end. And here is the thing that bothers me: the cosine ties the *length* of the high phase to the *timing and shape* of the decay through the one number `T`. They are not actually the same decision. "How long do I explore at high rate" and "how do I bring the rate down to converge" are two questions, and the cosine forces me to answer them with a single knob.

What if I just refuse to entangle them? Make the schedule out of explicit, separately specified pieces. Warm up to `η`. Then hold the rate flat at `η` — the full peak — for as long as I like, an open-ended stable stretch whose length I do *not* have to know in advance. Then, whenever I decide to produce a model, run a decay from `η` down to a small value over a short final window. Three phases: warmup, stable, decay. Let me write that as a function of the step `s`, with `W` the end of warmup and `T` the end of the stable phase:

  rate(s) = (s/W)·η          for s < W
          = η                for W < s < T
          = f(s − T)·η       for T < s

with `f` some decreasing function, `f(0) ≈ 1` and `f` shrinking toward a small floor. The whole point of writing it this way is that the stable rate is `η` regardless of when I stop — the high phase doesn't know or care about the horizon. The horizon only enters through *when I choose to start the decay*. That is exactly the decoupling I wanted: the rate I use today no longer depends on a horizon I must fix today.

But before I get attached, let me check this against the two facts I am bound by, because a schedule that violates either is dead. Summed learning rate: the stable phase sits at the *full peak* `η`, not a descending value, so per step it accumulates the maximum possible learning rate — this strictly beats a cosine, which is already curving down through the whole middle of the run. So on Kaplan's criterion I am ahead, not behind. Good. The thorough final descent: I have a decay phase, an explicit one, so I keep effect two. So structurally I have both effects the cosine bundled, but now controlled independently. Where could it fail? The obvious worry is that a flat high rate forever, then a sudden decay, might not reach the same loss as the smooth cosine — maybe the smoothness was load-bearing. I cannot settle that by armchair reasoning; it is the one thing I will actually have to validate. But let me at least convince myself the *mechanism* is plausible, because if I understand why the decay drops the loss, I will trust the structure.

So: why would bringing the rate down at the end produce a fast loss drop, and why would a high constant rate not? Let me think about what the optimizer is doing during the flat stable phase at rate `η`. The rate is high, so each AdamW step is large. If the loss surface near where I am has any sharpness or curvature — and for these models it does — then a large step overshoots: I take a big move, the loss does not drop much net, I bounce to the other side of the valley, the next big step bounces back. Consecutive update directions partly cancel. If I imagine recording the gradient at each step and taking the cosine of the angle between consecutive gradients, in this regime it should hover near zero or negative — the directions are not consistent, they are oscillating across the valley. The weights are moving a lot (the per-element update size tracks the rate, so it is large), but the loss is barely improving, because the motion is mostly side-to-side thrash, not descent. That matches the picture: big weight changes, little loss progress.

Now I drop the rate. Steps get small. A small step in a curved valley no longer overshoots; consecutive steps stop cancelling and start *aligning* — the gradient-to-gradient cosine should swing positive, meaning the iterate now moves consistently in one direction, downhill, step after step. And because it is finally descending coherently instead of thrashing, the loss falls fast even though each individual weight update is tiny. I would expect, as the rate decays, the gradient norm to shrink, the first-order directional derivative along the path to fall roughly in step with the rate, and the local curvature the iterate sees to *rise* — that last one is the signature of settling into the bottom of a basin. A high constant rate never gets this: it stays in the overshoot regime forever, perpetually bouncing, so it never collects the coherent-descent loss drop. That is why effect two needs an *actual* decay and a constant rate cannot fake it. Good — the mechanism makes the three-phase structure feel right, not arbitrary, and it tells me the decay is doing real work, not cosmetic smoothing.

Now I have to pin down the pieces I waved at. First, how long should the decay window be? Too short and I have not let the iterate settle — I cut off the coherent descent before it finishes. Too long and I am wasting budget at low rates where progress is slow, *and* I am defeating the whole purpose: the appeal of a short decay is that I can cheaply produce a converged model from a stable checkpoint, and a long decay makes that expensive. So I want the shortest decay window that still leaves enough small-rate steps for settling. The mechanism suggests this should be a fraction of the run, not a constant: the coherent descent needs enough small-rate steps to traverse the basin, which scales with how far high-rate training pushed the iterate, i.e. with the run length. A practical default is to decay over roughly the last ten percent of the budget, while treating much shorter windows as risky until checked. The required fraction should *shrink* for very long runs, which is consistent with "enough small-rate steps to settle," not "a fixed fraction is magic."

Second, the shape of `f` inside the decay. I need `f` decreasing from about 1 down to a small floor; beyond that, what shape? Let me reason about the family rather than guess one. The crudest decreasing schedule is a couple of discrete drops — multiply the rate by a constant factor at, say, 80% of the run and again at 90%, landing near a tenth of the peak (two factors of about 0.316, since 0.316² ≈ 0.1). That is the staircase idea, and it already buys the main prize: the first, highest plateau is flat, so training done there is reusable when I later extend the run — the very reuse a cosine cannot give. But two abrupt drops are a coarse approximation of "bring the rate down smoothly to let the iterate settle"; the iterate gets jolted at each drop rather than easing in. So a graded `f` should do at least as well and probably better.

The simplest graded `f` is linear: over a decay window of length `D` starting at `T`, let
  f(s − T) = 1 − (s − T)/D,
so the rate slides straight from `η` down to (essentially) zero — or, if I want a nonzero floor `m`, I interpolate, rate = m + (η − m)(1 − (s−T)/D). Clean, one line, monotone. Is linear best, though? Think about where the coherent-descent payoff comes from: it comes from the iterate spending steps at *small* rates, settling. A linear ramp spends equal step-count at every rate between `η` and the floor; it lingers at high-ish rates as long as at low ones. A shape that gets the rate *down* faster and then spends more of the window crawling at small rates should give the basin more time to be found. Concavity, in other words — drop fast early, flatten late. The square-root cooldown does exactly that:
  f(s − T) = 1 − sqrt((s − T)/D).
At the start of the window its slope is steep (the derivative of `−sqrt` blows up near zero), so the rate plunges off the peak quickly; toward the end it flattens, so the iterate spends a long tail at low rates settling in. Compare the two at the window midpoint, `(s−T)/D = ½`: linear gives `f = 0.5`, while `1 − sqrt` gives `1 − 0.707 ≈ 0.293` — the square-root schedule is already well below half the peak halfway through, exactly the "get small fast, then dwell small" profile the mechanism asks for. So I expect `1 − sqrt` to match or beat linear, with the gap widening for longer runs where there is more basin to settle into, and both should reach the cosine. Linear is the safe default; `1 − sqrt` is the refinement.

The trapezoidal constant-rate-plus-cooldown line is a useful sanity check here: it asks the same shape question directly instead of hiding it inside a full-run cosine. It uses exactly this normalized cooldown progress `u = (s − T)/D` and compares simple monotone choices such as linear, half-cosine cooldown, and `1 − sqrt(u)`. The half-cosine cooldown is
  f(u) = 0.5(1 + cos(πu)),
which starts at 1, ends at 0, and has zero slope at both ends. That makes it smoother at the boundaries than linear, while still being a bounded cooldown to a chosen floor. There is also an exponential form in the broader family,
  f(s − T) = 0.5^{(s − T)/D'},
which halves the rate every `D'` steps. Exponential is the continuous version of repeated halving and it never hits zero unless I clamp it, so it is natural for an open-ended mathematical `f`; the reusable scheduler API, though, wants an explicit final ratio, and the canonical bounded default is the half-cosine cooldown with linear and `1 − sqrt` as exposed alternatives. So really `f` is a family — discrete steps, linear, `1 − sqrt`, exponential, cosine cooldown — all monotone from ~1 to small; the phase structure is the invariant idea, and the exact cooldown shape is a secondary knob.

Third, the warmup. I keep the linear ramp for the same reason everyone does — the adaptive optimizer's second-moment estimates are cold at the start, so a full-size step early is unstable. If the implementation has an explicit floor `min_lr`, I can express the ramp as a multiplicative factor that starts at `min_lr / η` and rises to 1; when the floor is zero, this is the usual near-zero-to-peak warmup. The good news is the warmup length is forgiving: as long as it is "enough," it barely moves the final loss, so I do not need to tune it carefully and can drop it from the rest of the analysis. The floor at the end is the same ratio in reverse: the decay formula gives a raw factor between 1 and 0, then I rescale it to land at `min_lr / η` rather than below the configured floor.

Let me make sure I have not lost anything the cosine had. Smooth descent throughout? I gave that up on purpose — the middle of the cosine's descent was costing me summed learning rate and welding the rate to the horizon, and the mechanism says the *late* descent is what matters, not a descent through the middle. The cosine's floor at a tenth of the peak? I keep a small floor too, or shrink past it. The cosine's `T = S` optimality? The structure is aiming at the same loss by a *different* route: hold at the peak (more summed learning rate than the cosine) and then decay (the same thorough final anneal), and the validation that this lands at the cosine-matched loss is the one empirical thing I genuinely have to check rather than derive. Everything else — the structure, the decay length, the shape family — I can motivate from the two facts and the mechanism.

Now the payoff that makes this more than a tie. Because the stable phase is horizon-free, a stable run should be usable at any token count `D` by paying only for a short decay from the stable checkpoint at step `D`. So to trace the loss-versus-compute envelope of a fixed-size model, I do not relaunch; I keep one stable run going and branch off cheap decays at a series of `D` values, each decay being about ten percent of its branch point. Each branch's end-of-decay loss is intended to be a point on the optimal envelope. That collapses the scaling-law experiment from quadratic to linear: to fill an `(N, D)` grid for `m` model sizes I run `m` stable runs and decay each at a handful of `D`s, instead of `m` separate full runs per data point. Let me carry the scaling-law algebra through so I can actually use these points. Fit
  L(N, D) = C_N N^{−α} + C_D D^{−β} + L_0,
and fix compute `C = 6ND`, so `D = C/(6N)`. Substitute and minimize over `N`:
  L(N) = C_N N^{−α} + C_D (C/(6N))^{−β} + L_0 = C_N N^{−α} + C_D (6N/C)^{β} + L_0.
Differentiate and set to zero:
  dL/dN = −α C_N N^{−α−1} + β C_D (6/C)^{β} N^{β−1} = 0,
so
  α C_N N^{−α−1} = β C_D (6/C)^{β} N^{β−1},
  N^{β−1+α+1} = N^{α+β} = (α C_N)/(β C_D) · (C/6)^{β},
  N_opt = [ (α C_N)/(β C_D) ]^{1/(α+β)} · (C/6)^{β/(α+β)}.
By the symmetric argument minimizing over `D` with `N = C/(6D)`,
  D_opt = [ (β C_D)/(α C_N) ]^{1/(α+β)} · (C/6)^{α/(α+β)}.
Their ratio:
  N_opt/D_opt = [ (α C_N)/(β C_D) ]^{2/(α+β)} · (C/6)^{(β−α)/(α+β)} = K² (C/6)^{eta_scale},
with `K = ((α C_N)/(β C_D))^{1/(α+β)}` and `eta_scale = (β − α)/(α + β)`, the scaling exponent that I keep separate from the peak learning rate `η`. The structure is readable: if `α = β` then `eta_scale = 0`, the ratio is constant in compute, and I recover the Chinchilla "scale model and data together" claim; if `α < β`, the optimal `N/D` ratio grows with compute and I should emphasize parameter scaling; if `α > β`, the optimal `N/D` ratio shrinks with compute and I should emphasize data scaling. The cheap branches do not change the algebra; they make the envelope points cheap enough that I can estimate the exponents without rerunning every `(N, D)` cell from scratch.

Let me write the schedule as the function the training loop calls, filling the one empty slot — `get_lr(it, warmup_iters, lr_decay_iters, learning_rate, min_lr)`. The loop hands me the iteration and the four numbers; I return the rate. To keep this faithful to the canonical scheduler, I treat `lr_decay_iters` as the total training horizon, reserve the last tenth as the decay window, infer the stable length as the remaining steps after warmup and decay, use linear warmup, use the half-cosine cooldown (`decay_type="cosine"`, `num_cycles=0.5`), and apply the floor as `min_lr_ratio = min_lr / learning_rate`:

```python
import math


def get_lr(it, warmup_iters, lr_decay_iters, learning_rate, min_lr):
    """Warmup-stable-decay schedule adapted to the training loop's get_lr slot."""
    if learning_rate == 0:
        return 0.0

    num_training_steps = max(1, lr_decay_iters)
    num_warmup_steps = max(0, warmup_iters)
    num_decay_steps = max(1, int(0.1 * num_training_steps))
    if num_warmup_steps + num_decay_steps > num_training_steps:
        num_decay_steps = max(1, num_training_steps - num_warmup_steps)
    num_stable_steps = max(0, num_training_steps - num_warmup_steps - num_decay_steps)
    min_lr_ratio = max(0.0, min(1.0, min_lr / learning_rate))

    if it < num_warmup_steps:
        progress = float(it) / float(max(1, num_warmup_steps))
        factor = progress * (1.0 - min_lr_ratio) + min_lr_ratio
        return learning_rate * max(0.0, factor)

    if it < num_warmup_steps + num_stable_steps:
        return learning_rate

    if it < num_warmup_steps + num_stable_steps + num_decay_steps:
        progress = float(it - num_warmup_steps - num_stable_steps) / float(max(1, num_decay_steps))
        num_cycles = 0.5
        factor = 0.5 * (1.0 + math.cos(math.pi * num_cycles * 2.0 * progress))
        factor = factor * (1.0 - min_lr_ratio) + min_lr_ratio
        return learning_rate * max(0.0, factor)

    return min_lr
```

So the causal chain. I was stuck on the cosine schedule, which is only good when its cycle length `T` equals the run length `S`: `T < S` bleeds summed learning rate (Kaplan), `T > S` hurts the final model (Hoffmann), so the rate I use at every middle step is welded to a horizon I must fix in advance — making runs unextendable, intermediate states unconverged, and scaling-law sweeps quadratic. Asking *why* `T = S` wins separated two bundled effects: a long high-rate phase that aids exploration (and maximizes summed learning rate), and a thorough final decay that triggers a fast late loss drop. The cosine ties their controls together through `T`; refusing to entangle them gives an explicit warmup-stable-decay structure — hold at the full peak for an open-ended stable phase, then decay over a short final window — which keeps both effects, beats the cosine on summed learning rate (peak, not descending), and crucially makes the stable rate independent of the horizon. The decay's loss drop I justified mechanistically: a high constant rate overshoots a curved valley and thrashes (consecutive gradients near-orthogonal, big weight moves, little loss progress), while a small rate lets consecutive steps align and descend coherently into the basin (gradient cosine positive, curvature rising, loss falling fast) — so the decay must be real, not cosmetic, and a constant rate cannot fake it. The decay length follows from needing enough small-rate steps to settle, about ten percent of the budget. The decay shape is a family — discrete halving, linear, `1 − sqrt`, exponential, cosine cooldown — all monotone from ~1 to small; the canonical scheduler uses the half-cosine cooldown by default and exposes linear and `1 − sqrt` as alternatives. And because the stable phase is horizon-free, one stable run plus cheap decay branches traces the whole loss envelope at linear cost, letting me fit `L(N,D) = C_N N^{−α} + C_D D^{−β} + L_0` and derive `N_opt/D_opt = K²(C/6)^{eta_scale}` with `K = ((αC_N)/(βC_D))^{1/(α+β)}` and `eta_scale = (β−α)/(α+β)` — the same Chinchilla machinery, now cheaper to measure.
