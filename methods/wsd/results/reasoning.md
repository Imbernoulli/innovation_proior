I have a language model of fixed size, a fixed optimizer, a fixed data stream, and a fixed compute budget, and the one lever still meaningfully moving my final validation loss is the learning-rate schedule — the function that says how big a step to take at each iteration. Everyone, including me, reaches for the same curve: warm up linearly from near zero to a peak `η` over the first slice of steps, then anneal `η` down a cosine all the way to a small floor, roughly a tenth of the peak, over the rest of the run. Loshchilov and Hutter gave me that cosine shape, `η_t = η_min + ½(η_max − η_min)(1 + cos(π · T_cur/T_i))`, as a smooth replacement for the old step-decay-by-a-constant-factor-at-preset-epochs; the single-cycle version, warmup then one cosine descent to the floor, is what large-model practice settled on. It works. So why am I unhappy with it?

The unhappiness is concrete and it is about one hyper-parameter. The cosine has a length, `T` — the step at which it first hits its floor — and in practice I set `T` to the total number of steps `S` of the run. And it has to be `T = S`, not just out of taste. Hoffmann and colleagues showed that `T > S` — a cosine cycle longer than the run, so I stop while the rate is still partway down — measurably hurts the final model, and that a cosine whose cycle far overshoots the run overestimates the loss it would actually reach at intermediate points; meanwhile `T = S` is both the best and the most compute-efficient. And the other direction is just as pinned: Kaplan and colleagues showed the final loss improves as the learning rate *summed over the whole run* goes up, as long as I keep a warmup and still anneal to near zero at the end. A cosine with `T < S` starts descending too early, so it bleeds summed learning rate, and it loses. The small-model sweep says the same thing: on a 0.036B net at `S = 20N, 40N, 60N, 80N`, the lowest loss at each budget is always the cosine whose `T` exactly equals that `S`. Both shorter and longer cycles lose. So the standard schedule is genuinely good *only* on the diagonal `T = S`.

Now sit with what that means operationally, because that is where the real cost is. The rate at *every* step in the middle of my run depends on where the end is. If I want a converged model at a 40N budget and another at an 80N budget, I cannot get them from one run — the 40N cosine was already curving down toward step 40N, so by the time I reach 80N I am long past its floor and the schedule is meaningless there. I have to launch two separate full runs, each redoing the entire expensive high-rate phase from scratch, just because the *ending* differs. I cannot leave a run open-ended and decide later how long to train. And if I want to study how loss scales across, say, six model sizes and six data sizes — fit `L(N, D)` to populate a grid — the standard way needs a separate full run per cell, so the cost is quadratic in the number of points along the two axes. All of this traces back to the same fact: the cosine welds the rate I use *today* to the horizon I picked *yesterday*.

So let me ask the sharper question. *Why* does `T = S` cosine win? If I can name the mechanism rather than just the curve, maybe I can get the mechanism without the welding. Looking at the evidence, two things are happening and the cosine bundles them. First, compared to a shorter cycle, `T = S` keeps the rate high for longer — and a long high-rate phase seems to help the optimizer find a better region of the landscape, which lines up with Kaplan's "more summed learning rate is better." Second, compared to a constant rate or an overlong cycle, `T = S` actually carries out a *thorough* final descent of the rate, and that final descent seems to do something special — a fast, late drop in loss that a high constant rate just never produces. The first effect wants the high-rate phase to be as long as the budget allows. The second wants a genuine, complete decay at the end. And here is the thing that bothers me: the cosine ties the *length* of the high phase to the *timing and shape* of the decay through the one number `T`. They are not actually the same decision. "How long do I explore at high rate" and "how do I bring the rate down to converge" are two questions, and the cosine forces me to answer them with a single knob.

What if I just refuse to entangle them? Make the schedule out of explicit, separately specified pieces, one per question, so I can set each on its own. Warm up to `η` — that part everyone keeps. The "how long do I explore at high rate" question I want to answer by *holding the rate flat at `η`*, the full peak, for as long as I like — an open-ended stretch whose length I do *not* have to commit to in advance, since a flat segment doesn't change when I extend it. And the "how do I bring the rate down" question I answer separately, whenever I decide to produce a model, by running a decay from `η` to a small value over a short final window. That gives three segments rather than one curve. Let me write it as a function of the step `s`, with `W` the end of warmup and `T` the end of the flat stretch:

  rate(s) = (s/W)·η          for s < W
          = η                for W < s < T
          = f(s − T)·η       for T < s

with `f` some decreasing function, `f(0) ≈ 1` and `f` shrinking toward a small floor. The thing I care about is that the stable rate is `η` regardless of when I stop — the high phase doesn't know or care about the horizon. The horizon only enters through *when I choose to start the decay*. If this holds up, it is the decoupling I wanted: the rate I use today would no longer depend on a horizon I must fix today.

Before I get attached, I should check this against the two facts I am bound by. Take Kaplan's criterion first — summed learning rate — and make it concrete rather than waving at it. Compare a `T = S` cosine and a WSD schedule over the same `S` steps, both warming up the same way, and integrate the rate over the descent region. The cosine spends its whole post-warmup budget at `0.1η + 0.45η(1 + cos(πu))`, `u` going 0→1; the average of `(1 + cos(πu))` over `[0,1]` is 1 (the cosine integrates to zero over a half period), so the cosine's mean rate across that stretch is `0.1η + 0.45η = 0.55η`. WSD sits at the full `η` for the entire stable stretch and only gives up rate during the final tenth. So over the bulk of training WSD runs at `η` against the cosine's `≈0.55η` — close to double the per-step learning rate through the middle. On Kaplan's "more summed learning rate is better, given warmup and a real final anneal," WSD is strictly ahead, not behind. The thorough final descent — Hoffmann's effect two — I keep, because I have an explicit decay phase. So structurally I have both effects the cosine bundled, now controlled independently.

That `0.55η` number is reassuring but it is also where I should be honest about what I have *not* shown. More summed learning rate is necessary, not sufficient — a constant rate at `η` has even more summed learning rate than WSD and is known to be worse, because it has no final anneal at all. So the summed-rate check tells me WSD is not disqualified and is on the right side of Kaplan; it does not tell me WSD reaches the cosine's loss. The obvious worry is that a flat high rate forever, then a sudden decay, might land somewhere worse than the smooth cosine — maybe the smoothness through the middle was load-bearing. I cannot settle that by armchair reasoning; it is the one thing I will actually have to validate empirically. But let me at least convince myself the *mechanism* is plausible, because if I understand why the decay drops the loss, I will trust the structure.

So: why would bringing the rate down at the end produce a fast loss drop, and why would a high constant rate not? What is the optimizer doing during the flat stable phase at rate `η`? The rate is high, so each AdamW step is large. If the loss surface near where I am has any sharpness or curvature — and for these models it does — then a large step should overshoot: a big move, a bounce to the other side of the valley, the next big step bouncing back, the loss not netting down much. That is a guess dressed as a mechanism, so let me pin it down on the cleanest toy that has the same structure. Take a 1-D quadratic `L(x) = ½κx²`, gradient `κx`, plain gradient step `x ← x − η_eff·κx = (1 − η_eff κ)x`. The whole behavior is governed by the single number `g = η_eff κ` and the multiplier `(1 − g)`. Iterating from `x₀ = 1` at three values of `g`:

At `g = 0.5` (a small rate), the multiplier is `+0.5`, and the iterates run `1 → 0.5 → 0.25 → 0.125 → 0.0625 → 0.0313`: monotone, same sign throughout, marching into the minimum. At `g = 1.5` (a rate past the threshold), the multiplier is `−0.5`, and the iterates run `1 → −0.5 → 0.25 → −0.125 → 0.0625 → −0.0313`: the sign *flips every step* — `x` lands on the far side of the minimum each time — while the magnitude still shrinks. At `g = 2.5` (rate too high), the multiplier is `−1.5`, and the iterates run `1 → −1.5 → 2.25 → −3.375 → 5.06 → −7.59`: sign-flipping *and* blowing up. So the recurrence draws three regimes for me without my having assumed them: `g < 1` monotone descent, `1 < g < 2` overshoot-but-converge with the iterate alternating sides, `g > 2` divergence. In the alternating regime the gradient `κx` flips sign every step too, so the cosine of the angle between consecutive gradients is `−1`, straight off the sequence — the "directions cancel" picture. The high-dimensional landscape won't give a clean `−1`, but the sign of the effect is pinned: at high rate, in the sharp directions (large `κ`, hence large `g`), consecutive gradients anti-align, the weights move a lot, and the loss barely nets down because the motion is side-to-side thrash. Big weight changes, little loss progress — that is what I would expect to see logged during the stable phase.

Now I drop the rate, and the same iteration tells me what happens: once `g < 1` (the `g = 0.5` sequence above), the multiplier is positive and less than 1, so `x` marches monotonically toward 0 — no sign flips, consecutive gradients now point the *same* way, and the iterate descends coherently instead of bouncing. Carrying that back: as the decay lowers `η_eff`, every sharp direction whose `g` was above 1 crosses below 1, the gradient-to-gradient cosine swings from `−1` toward positive, and the iterate stops thrashing and moves consistently downhill. And because it is finally descending coherently rather than bouncing, the loss falls fast even though each individual weight update is tiny — the fast late drop, explained. I would expect, as the rate decays, the gradient norm to shrink, the directional derivative along the path to fall roughly in step with the rate, and the local curvature the iterate sees to *rise* — the signature of settling into the bottom of a basin; those are predictions I could check against training logs, not things the toy proves. What the toy *does* settle is the contrast with a constant rate: a flat high rate keeps every sharp direction stuck at `g > 1` forever, perpetually in the alternating regime, so it never collects the coherent-descent drop. So the decay is not cosmetic smoothing — it is what moves the iterate out of the bouncing regime into the descending one, and a constant rate structurally cannot do that. That makes the flat-then-decay shape feel earned rather than arbitrary.

Now I have to pin down the pieces I waved at. First, how long should the decay window be? Too short and I have not let the iterate settle — I cut off the coherent descent before it finishes. Too long and I am wasting budget at low rates where progress is slow, *and* I am defeating the whole purpose: the appeal of a short decay is that I can cheaply produce a converged model from a stable checkpoint, and a long decay makes that expensive. So I want the shortest decay window that still leaves enough small-rate steps for settling. The toy is suggestive here too: the number of small-rate steps needed to traverse the basin scales with how far the iterate sits from the minimum when the decay starts, and high-rate training pushes the iterate out in proportion to how long it ran. So the decay budget should be a fraction of the run, not a constant, and the mechanism alone does not pin down which fraction — that is a number I have to find empirically rather than derive. A reasonable starting point to test is something like the last ten percent of the budget, generous enough that a settle-time argument this crude should not be starving it; if the fraction turns out to need adjusting with run length, that too is consistent with "enough small-rate steps to settle" rather than "a fixed fraction is magic" — the mechanism only commits me to *some* fraction, not to ten percent specifically.

Second, the shape of `f` inside the decay. I need `f` decreasing from about 1 down to a small floor; beyond that, what shape? The crudest decreasing schedule is a couple of discrete drops — multiply the rate by a constant factor at, say, 80% of the run and again at 90%, landing near a tenth of the peak. For that landing to work the two factors have to compose to `0.1`: if each is `r`, then `r² = 0.1`, so `r = √0.1 ≈ 0.3162`, and indeed `0.3162² = 0.09999…`, which is why the staircase uses `×0.316` twice. That already buys the main prize: the first, highest plateau is flat, so training done there is reusable when I later extend the run — the very reuse a cosine cannot give. But two abrupt drops are a coarse approximation of "bring the rate down smoothly to let the iterate settle"; the iterate gets jolted at each drop rather than easing in. So a graded `f` should do at least as well and probably better.

The simplest graded `f` is linear: over a decay window of length `D` starting at `T`, let
  f(s − T) = 1 − (s − T)/D,
so the rate slides straight from `η` down to (essentially) zero — or, if I want a nonzero floor `m`, I interpolate, rate = m + (η − m)(1 − (s−T)/D). Clean, one line, monotone. Is linear best, though? The coherent-descent payoff comes from the iterate spending steps at *small* rates, settling. A linear ramp spends equal step-count at every rate between `η` and the floor; it lingers at high-ish rates as long as at low ones. A shape that gets the rate *down* faster and then spends more of the window crawling at small rates should give the basin more time to be found. Concavity, in other words — drop fast early, flatten late. The square-root cooldown does exactly that:
  f(s − T) = 1 − sqrt((s − T)/D).
At the start of the window its slope is steep (the derivative of `−sqrt` blows up near zero), so the rate plunges off the peak quickly; toward the end it flattens, so the iterate spends a long tail at low rates settling in. Let me put numbers on the difference at the window midpoint, `u = (s−T)/D = ½`. Linear gives `f = 1 − 0.5 = 0.5`. The square-root gives `f = 1 − √0.5 = 1 − 0.7071 = 0.2929`. So halfway through the window the square-root schedule is already at ~29% of the peak while the linear one is still at 50% — exactly the "get small fast, then dwell small" profile the mechanism asks for. By the same mechanism, `1 − sqrt` should be at least as good as linear and plausibly better on longer runs, where there is more basin to settle into — though whether either actually lands on the cosine's loss is exactly the empirical question I already flagged, not something the shape argument alone can promise. Linear is the safe default; `1 − sqrt` is the refinement.

The trapezoidal constant-rate-plus-cooldown line is a useful sanity check here: it asks the same shape question directly instead of hiding it inside a full-run cosine. It uses exactly this normalized cooldown progress `u = (s − T)/D` and compares simple monotone choices such as linear, half-cosine cooldown, and `1 − sqrt(u)`. The half-cosine cooldown is
  f(u) = 0.5(1 + cos(πu)),
which starts at 1 (`cos 0 = 1`), ends at 0 (`cos π = −1`), and has zero slope at both ends. Let me check where it sits at the midpoint against the other two: `f(½) = 0.5(1 + cos(π/2)) = 0.5(1 + 0) = 0.5` — identical to linear at the midpoint, and well above the square-root's 0.293. So the half-cosine is *not* the aggressive-early-drop shape; it is the boundary-smooth one, easing off the peak and into the floor with no kink, while crossing the midpoint at the same height as linear. That tells me the three candidates are genuinely different: linear (constant slope), `1 − sqrt` (concave, fast-early), half-cosine (smooth at both ends, linear-like in the middle). There is also an exponential form in the broader family,
  f(s − T) = 0.5^{(s − T)/D'},
which halves the rate every `D'` steps. Exponential is the continuous version of repeated halving and it never hits zero unless I clamp it, so it is natural for an open-ended mathematical `f`; the reusable scheduler API, though, wants an explicit final ratio, and the canonical bounded default is the half-cosine cooldown with linear and `1 − sqrt` as exposed alternatives. So really `f` is a family — discrete steps, linear, `1 − sqrt`, exponential, cosine cooldown — all monotone from ~1 to small; the phase structure is the invariant idea, and the exact cooldown shape is a secondary knob.

Third, the warmup. I keep the linear ramp for the same reason everyone does — the adaptive optimizer's second-moment estimates are cold at the start, so a full-size step early is unstable. If the implementation has an explicit floor `min_lr`, I can express the ramp as a multiplicative factor that starts at `min_lr / η` and rises to 1; when the floor is zero, this is the usual near-zero-to-peak warmup. The good news is the warmup length is forgiving: as long as it is "enough," it barely moves the final loss, so I do not need to tune it carefully and can drop it from the rest of the analysis. The floor at the end is the same ratio in reverse: the decay formula gives a raw factor between 1 and 0, then I rescale it to land at `min_lr / η` rather than below the configured floor.

Have I lost anything the cosine had? Smooth descent throughout — I gave that up on purpose: the middle of the cosine's descent was costing me summed learning rate (the `0.55η` versus `η` I worked out above) and welding the rate to the horizon, and the mechanism says the *late* descent is what matters, not a descent through the middle. The cosine's floor at a tenth of the peak — I keep a small floor too, or shrink past it. The cosine's `T = S` optimality — the structure aims at the same loss by a *different* route: hold at the peak (more summed learning rate than the cosine) and then decay (the same thorough final anneal); whether that route actually lands at the cosine-matched loss is the empirical check I flagged above. Everything else — the structure, the decay length, the shape family — I can motivate from the two facts and the mechanism.

Now the payoff that makes this more than a tie. Because the stable phase is horizon-free, a stable run should be usable at any token count `D` by paying only for a short decay from the stable checkpoint at step `D`. So to trace the loss-versus-compute envelope of a fixed-size model, I do not relaunch; I keep one stable run going and branch off cheap decays at a series of `D` values, each decay being about ten percent of its branch point. Each branch's end-of-decay loss is intended to be a point on the optimal envelope. That collapses the scaling-law experiment from quadratic to linear: to fill an `(N, D)` grid for `m` model sizes I run `m` stable runs and decay each at a handful of `D`s, instead of `m` separate full runs per data point. Let me carry the scaling-law algebra through so I can actually use these points. Fit
  L(N, D) = C_N N^{−α} + C_D D^{−β} + L_0,
and fix compute `C = 6ND`, so `D = C/(6N)`. Substitute and minimize over `N`:
  L(N) = C_N N^{−α} + C_D (C/(6N))^{−β} + L_0 = C_N N^{−α} + C_D (6N/C)^{β} + L_0.
Differentiate and set to zero:
  dL/dN = −α C_N N^{−α−1} + β C_D (6/C)^{β} N^{β−1} = 0,
so
  α C_N N^{−α−1} = β C_D (6/C)^{β} N^{β−1},
  N^{β−1+α+1} = N^{α+β} = (α C_N)/(β C_D) · (C/6)^{β},
  N_opt = [ (α C_N)/(β C_D) ]^{1/(α+β)} · (C/6)^{β/(α+β)}.
That exponent owes a consistency check: if I scale compute up by a factor `t`, `C → tC`, then `N_opt` scales by `t^{β/(α+β)}` and (by the symmetric formula below) `D_opt` by `t^{α/(α+β)}`; the two exponents `β/(α+β)` and `α/(α+β)` sum to `1`, so `N_opt·D_opt` scales by `t`, i.e. linearly in `C` — which is exactly what `C = 6ND` demands. The exponents partition unity, so the derivation is internally consistent. By the symmetric argument minimizing over `D` with `N = C/(6D)`,
  D_opt = [ (β C_D)/(α C_N) ]^{1/(α+β)} · (C/6)^{α/(α+β)}.
Their ratio:
  N_opt/D_opt = [ (α C_N)/(β C_D) ]^{2/(α+β)} · (C/6)^{(β−α)/(α+β)} = K² (C/6)^{eta_scale},
with `K = ((α C_N)/(β C_D))^{1/(α+β)}` and `eta_scale = (β − α)/(α + β)`, the scaling exponent that I keep separate from the peak learning rate `η`. Read the limiting cases to make sure the structure is sane. If `α = β` then `eta_scale = (β − α)/(α + β) = 0`, so `(C/6)^0 = 1` and the ratio is constant in compute — I recover the Chinchilla "scale model and data together" claim, the cleanest possible check that the formula is not nonsense. If `α < β`, then `eta_scale > 0`, the optimal `N/D` ratio grows with compute and I should emphasize parameter scaling; if `α > β`, then `eta_scale < 0`, the optimal `N/D` ratio shrinks with compute and I should emphasize data scaling. The cheap branches do not change the algebra; they make the envelope points cheap enough that I can estimate the exponents without rerunning every `(N, D)` cell from scratch.

Let me write the schedule as the function the training loop calls, filling the one empty slot — `get_lr(it, warmup_iters, lr_decay_iters, learning_rate, min_lr)`. The loop hands me the iteration and the four numbers; I return the rate. To keep this faithful to the canonical scheduler, I treat `lr_decay_iters` as the total training horizon, reserve the last tenth as the decay window, infer the stable length as the remaining steps after warmup and decay, use linear warmup, use the half-cosine cooldown (`decay_type="cosine"`, `num_cycles=0.5`), and apply the floor as `min_lr_ratio = min_lr / learning_rate` on both ends — so the warmup ramp starts at `min_lr` rather than at zero, and the decay curve approaches `min_lr` through the whole window without ever dropping below it, with the loop's fallback branch clamping to exactly `min_lr` once the horizon is reached. That function is what appears in the answer.

The one thing I have not derived and genuinely have to validate empirically is that this route — hold at the peak, then decay — actually reaches the cosine-matched loss; everything else here follows from the two binding facts and the overshoot-versus-alignment mechanism.
