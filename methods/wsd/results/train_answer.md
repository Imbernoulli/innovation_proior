With model size, optimizer, data stream, and total compute all fixed, the one lever that still moves my final validation loss by a meaningful margin is the learning-rate schedule — the function setting the step size at every iteration. The schedule has to do two things that pull against each other: keep the rate high through the early and middle of training so the optimizer explores broadly and makes fast progress through the bulk of the loss, then bring it down to a small value at the end so the iterate settles into a good minimum instead of rattling at the noise floor. Everyone, myself included, reaches for the same curve to do this: a linear warmup from near zero up to a peak $\eta$ over the first slice of steps (to keep AdamW's second-moment estimates from destabilizing a full-size step while they are still cold), then a single cosine descent $\eta_t = \eta_{\min} + \tfrac{1}{2}(\eta_{\max} - \eta_{\min})\big(1 + \cos(\pi\, T_{\mathrm{cur}}/T)\big)$ down to a floor at roughly a tenth of the peak. It works well, and that is exactly the problem — it works well only on a knife's edge.

The unhappiness is concrete and it lives in one hyper-parameter. The cosine has a length $T$, the step at which it first reaches its floor, and in practice $T$ must be set to the total run length $S$. It has to be $T = S$, not as a matter of taste: setting $T > S$, so the run stops while the rate is still partway down, measurably hurts the final model and overestimates the loss the curve would actually reach at intermediate points (Hoffmann et al. 2022), while $T = S$ is simultaneously the best and the most compute-efficient choice. The other direction is just as pinned, because the final loss improves as the learning rate summed over the whole run increases, provided a warmup is kept and the rate still anneals to near zero at the end (Kaplan et al. 2020) — and a cosine with $T < S$ starts descending too early, bleeding summed learning rate, so it loses too. Small-model sweeps confirm the diagonal exactly: on a 0.036B network at $S = 20N, 40N, 60N, 80N$, the lowest loss at each budget is always the cosine whose $T$ equals that very $S$. The operational cost of this is severe. The rate I use at every middle step is welded to where the run ends: I cannot leave a run open-ended, I cannot cleanly extend it (the cosine was already curving toward the old endpoint), my intermediate checkpoints are never converged because the curve is mid-descent there, and to populate an $(N, D)$ scaling-law grid I must launch a separate full run per cell, each redoing the entire expensive high-rate phase, so the cost is quadratic. The constant-rate and periodic-cosine-loop baselines escape the horizon dependence but never perform the thorough final descent, so their loss stays above the matched cosine; staircase multi-step decay (Bi et al. 2024) reuses its flat first plateau but anneals in two coarse abrupt drops.

I propose WSD — Warmup-Stable-Decay — a schedule built by refusing to entangle the two effects the cosine bundles together. Asking *why* the $T = S$ cosine wins separates them cleanly: a long high-rate phase aids exploration and maximizes summed learning rate, and a thorough final decay triggers a fast, late loss drop. The cosine ties both of these to the single knob $T$, but "how long do I explore at high rate" and "how do I bring the rate down to converge" are genuinely two questions. So I make the schedule out of three explicitly separated phases — warm up linearly to $\eta$, hold flat at the full peak $\eta$ for an open-ended stable stretch whose length I never have to fix in advance, then decay from $\eta$ down to a small floor over a short final window — which I write as a function of the step $s$, with $W$ the end of warmup and $T$ the end of the stable phase:
$$\mathrm{rate}(s) = \begin{cases} (s/W)\,\eta & s < W \\ \eta & W < s < T \\ f(s - T)\,\eta & T < s \end{cases}$$
with $f$ decreasing, $f(0) \approx 1$, shrinking toward a small floor. The point of writing it this way is that the stable rate is $\eta$ regardless of when I stop — the high phase does not know or care about the horizon, which only enters through *when I choose to start the decay*. That is the decoupling: the rate I use today no longer depends on a horizon I must fix today. Checked against the two binding facts, the structure is ahead, not behind. On summed learning rate, the stable phase sits at the *full peak* $\eta$ rather than a descending value, so per step it accumulates the maximum possible — strictly beating a cosine that is already curving down through the entire middle. And it keeps an explicit decay phase, so it retains the thorough final anneal. Both effects survive, now controlled independently, and the flat plateau means stable-phase training is reusable when the run is later extended, which a descending cosine can never offer.

What makes the decay do real work, rather than cosmetic smoothing, is a mechanistic story about the optimizer. During the flat stable phase at high rate $\eta$, each AdamW step is large, so in any curved or sharp region of the loss surface a step overshoots: the iterate takes a big move, bounces to the other side of the valley, the next big step bounces back, and consecutive update directions partly cancel. If I recorded the cosine of the angle between consecutive gradients it would hover near zero or negative — the directions are oscillating across the valley, the weights move a lot, but the loss barely improves because the motion is side-to-side thrash, not descent. Drop the rate and the steps get small; a small step in a curved valley no longer overshoots, consecutive steps stop cancelling and start *aligning*, the gradient-to-gradient cosine swings positive, and the iterate finally moves coherently downhill step after step, so the loss falls fast even though each individual update is tiny. As the rate decays I expect the gradient norm to shrink, the directional derivative along the path to fall roughly in step with the rate, and the local curvature the iterate sees to rise — the signature of settling into the bottom of a basin. A high constant rate stays in the overshoot regime forever and never collects this drop, which is precisely why the second effect needs an *actual* decay that a constant rate cannot fake.

The remaining pieces follow from that mechanism. The decay window should be the shortest that still leaves enough small-rate steps for the iterate to settle: too short cuts off the coherent descent before it finishes, too long both wastes budget at low rates and defeats the whole appeal of producing a converged model cheaply from a stable checkpoint. Because the basin to traverse grows with how far high-rate training pushed the iterate, the required length scales with the run, so a practical default is the last ten percent of the budget, with the fraction allowed to shrink for very long runs. The decay shape $f$ is a secondary knob — a family of monotone descents from $\approx 1$ to small. The crudest is two discrete drops, multiplying by $\approx 0.316$ at 80% and again at 90% (so $0.316^2 \approx 0.1$), which already buys reuse but jolts the iterate. Linear, $f(u) = 1 - u$ over normalized progress $u = (s-T)/D$, is the clean safe default. Concavity should do better, because the payoff comes from spending steps at *small* rates: a linear ramp lingers at high-ish rates as long as at low ones, whereas $f(u) = 1 - \sqrt{u}$ plunges off the peak fast and then dwells at small rates — at the midpoint $u = \tfrac12$, linear gives $0.5$ but $1 - \sqrt{u}$ gives $\approx 0.293$, already well below half. The canonical scheduler defaults to a half-cosine cooldown $f(u) = 0.5\,(1 + \cos(\pi u))$, which starts at 1, ends at 0, and has zero slope at both ends (smoother at the boundaries than linear), and exposes linear and $1 - \sqrt{u}$ as alternatives; an exponential $f(s-T) = 0.5^{(s-T)/D'}$ rounds out the family. The phase structure, not the exact cooldown shape, is the contribution. I keep the linear warmup because the adaptive optimizer's second moments are cold at the start; its length is forgiving and barely moves the final loss, so it needs no careful tuning. With an explicit floor I express both warmup and decay through the factor $\mathrm{min\_lr\_ratio} = \mathrm{min\_lr}/\eta$, rescaling so the ramp starts at that ratio and the decay lands on it.

The payoff that makes this more than a tie is that the horizon-free stable phase lets one run be used at any token count $D$ by paying only for a short decay from the stable checkpoint at step $D$. To trace the loss-versus-compute envelope of a fixed-size model I keep one stable run going and branch off cheap decays — about ten percent of each branch point — at a series of $D$ values, collapsing the scaling-law experiment from quadratic to linear: filling an $(N, D)$ grid for $m$ model sizes costs $m$ stable runs each decayed at a handful of $D$s, not $m$ full runs per cell. Carrying the algebra through, fit $L(N, D) = C_N N^{-\alpha} + C_D N^{-\beta} \!\!\big|_{D} + L_0$, i.e. $L(N, D) = C_N N^{-\alpha} + C_D D^{-\beta} + L_0$, fix compute $C = 6ND$ so $D = C/(6N)$, substitute, and minimize over $N$: from $\mathrm{d}L/\mathrm{d}N = -\alpha C_N N^{-\alpha-1} + \beta C_D (6/C)^{\beta} N^{\beta-1} = 0$ one gets $N^{\alpha+\beta} = (\alpha C_N)/(\beta C_D)\cdot(C/6)^{\beta}$, hence
$$N_{\mathrm{opt}} = \Big[\tfrac{\alpha C_N}{\beta C_D}\Big]^{1/(\alpha+\beta)} (C/6)^{\beta/(\alpha+\beta)}, \qquad D_{\mathrm{opt}} = \Big[\tfrac{\beta C_D}{\alpha C_N}\Big]^{1/(\alpha+\beta)} (C/6)^{\alpha/(\alpha+\beta)},$$
$$\frac{N_{\mathrm{opt}}}{D_{\mathrm{opt}}} = K^2\,(C/6)^{\mathrm{eta\_scale}}, \quad K = \Big(\tfrac{\alpha C_N}{\beta C_D}\Big)^{1/(\alpha+\beta)}, \quad \mathrm{eta\_scale} = \frac{\beta - \alpha}{\alpha + \beta}.$$
Here $\mathrm{eta\_scale}$ is the scaling-law exponent, kept distinct from the peak rate $\eta$: $\alpha = \beta$ gives a constant ratio (the Chinchilla "scale model and data together" claim), $\alpha < \beta$ means emphasize parameter scaling as compute grows, and $\alpha > \beta$ means emphasize data scaling. The cheap branches do not change the algebra; they make the envelope points cheap enough to estimate the exponents without rerunning every cell.

Implemented as the one empty slot the fixed pretraining loop hands me — the iteration and four numbers — I treat `lr_decay_iters` as the total horizon, reserve the last tenth as the decay window, infer the stable length as what remains after warmup and decay, warm up linearly, decay with the half-cosine cooldown (`num_cycles = 0.5`), and apply the floor as `min_lr_ratio = min_lr / learning_rate`:

```python
import math


def get_lr(it, warmup_iters, lr_decay_iters, learning_rate, min_lr):
    """Warmup-stable-decay schedule adapted to a fixed training loop."""
    if learning_rate == 0:
        return 0.0

    num_training_steps = max(1, lr_decay_iters)
    num_warmup_steps = max(0, warmup_iters)
    num_decay_steps = max(1, int(0.1 * num_training_steps))
    if num_warmup_steps + num_decay_steps > num_training_steps:
        num_decay_steps = max(1, num_training_steps - num_warmup_steps)
    num_stable_steps = max(0, num_training_steps - num_warmup_steps - num_decay_steps)
    min_lr_ratio = max(0.0, min(1.0, min_lr / learning_rate))

    if it < num_warmup_steps:
        progress = float(it) / float(max(1, num_warmup_steps))
        factor = progress * (1.0 - min_lr_ratio) + min_lr_ratio
        return learning_rate * max(0.0, factor)

    if it < num_warmup_steps + num_stable_steps:
        return learning_rate

    if it < num_warmup_steps + num_stable_steps + num_decay_steps:
        progress = float(it - num_warmup_steps - num_stable_steps) / float(max(1, num_decay_steps))
        num_cycles = 0.5
        factor = 0.5 * (1.0 + math.cos(math.pi * num_cycles * 2.0 * progress))
        factor = factor * (1.0 - min_lr_ratio) + min_lr_ratio
        return learning_rate * max(0.0, factor)

    return min_lr
```
