I start from the reduction problem: a sample is large, the parameter is small, and I need to know what part of the sample is relevant to that parameter. Consistency is too weak. A statistic may converge to the right value and still waste observations, as the mean does for a Cauchy location family. Variance is useful, but if I compare variances only after choosing statistics, I never get the model-level scale that tells me what the best possible statistic could achieve.

The likelihood is the natural place to look, because it asks how well each parameter value would have produced the observed data. But I have to be careful. Likelihood is not probability over the parameter, and its absolute height can be rescaled. So the information cannot be the likelihood value itself. What survives rescaling and reparameterization is local shape: how fast the log-likelihood changes when I nudge the parameter, and how much curvature appears near the truth.

I first try the derivative of the log-likelihood. For one observation this score is `S_theta(X) = partial_theta log p_theta(X)`. It is the local direction in which the probability law changes. But the derivative itself cannot be the information, because in a regular model its expectation at the true parameter is zero. The positive and negative slopes cancel. That cancellation is a useful wall: it tells me the first-order signed change is not the persistent quantity.

The next invariant thing is the squared size of that local derivative. Averaging `S_theta(X)^2` under the model gives a nonnegative number. If the density changes sharply when `theta` moves, the score is often large in magnitude; if nearby parameter values produce nearly the same distribution, the score is small. This also matches the efficient-variance scale: if an efficient estimator from `n` observations has variance `A/n`, then the local accuracy of one observation is `1/A`, and the squared log-density derivative is the way to compute it.

I check that this is not just a convenient definition. Since `integral p_theta(x) dx = 1`, differentiating gives `E_theta[S_theta] = 0`. Differentiating once more, under the regularity conditions that allow the derivative to pass through the integral, gives the identity between the average squared score and the negative expected second derivative of log-likelihood. So the same quantity appears in two ways: as the variance of the tangent direction and as expected curvature.

Now additivity falls into place. For independent observations the log-likelihood is a sum, so the score is a sum of independent score contributions. The mean-zero cross terms vanish, and the information in the sample is the sum of the information in its observations. This is exactly what an information measure should do: two independent observations carry two contributions, not a new arbitrary scale.

The variance bound also stops being mysterious. Suppose an estimator `T` has mean `psi(theta)`. Differentiating its expectation under the integral gives `partial_theta E_theta[T] = E_theta[T S_theta]`. Since the score has mean zero, this is `Cov_theta(T, S_theta) = psi'(theta)`. The estimator must therefore have a fixed inner product with the score direction if it is to track `psi(theta)`. Cauchy-Schwarz says that a fixed inner product with a vector of squared length `I(theta)` forces `Var_theta(T) >= psi'(theta)^2 / I(theta)`. The inverse information is not a separate rule; it is the dual variance scale created by the same local tangent length.

For several parameters the same idea cannot remain a single number. Each parameter coordinate has its own score component, and the relevant object is the Gram matrix `E_theta[S_i S_j]`. Diagonal entries give squared lengths, off-diagonal entries give local angles, and small eigenvalues reveal directions in which the model barely moves. In this form the object transforms like a metric tensor, so the geometry belongs to the family of distributions rather than to one arbitrary coordinate system.

This also explains the geometric language. A tiny displacement `dtheta` changes the model by a squared local amount `dtheta^T I(theta) dtheta`. The parameter space inherits its local length from the probability laws. Estimation limits, sufficiency comparisons, loss of information under reduction, and efficient maximum-likelihood behavior all become different views of the same object: the squared length of the likelihood tangent.
