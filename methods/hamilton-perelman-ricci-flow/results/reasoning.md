I start with the topology problem and refuse to choose the splitting surfaces by hand. A closed 3-manifold is supposed to decompose along spheres and tori into geometric pieces, but the surfaces that do the work are not visible from an arbitrary presentation. I can put a smooth metric on the manifold for free, so I try to let the metric expose the topology.

The natural evolution is Ricci flow, `partial_t g = -2 Ric`. It is intrinsic, it respects diffeomorphisms, and its curvature evolution has the heat-equation shape plus nonlinear curvature terms. In positively curved three-dimensional cases, Hamilton already shows that the flow rounds the metric. That is the first clue: topology might be attacked by making geometry move until the manifold either becomes visibly geometric or breaks in a meaningful place.

But I cannot treat Ricci flow as a smoothing machine. In a general 3-manifold the curvature can blow up in finite time. At first that looks like a failure of the plan. Then I notice that the expected failure is topological information: a round shrinking cylinder `S^2 x R` pinches through 2-sphere cross-sections, and cutting along such a sphere is a standard topological operation. The singularity is not something to hide from; it may be where the manifold tells me where to cut.

The wall is that I do not yet know the singularity is a neck. A blow-up limit around a high-curvature point only helps if the rescaled geometry converges to something complete and nondegenerate. Curvature bounds alone do not give that. A region could collapse locally so that every ball at the relevant scale has tiny volume, and then the limiting model would not be a clean ancient solution. This is where the program stalls if I only have the flow equation and maximum principles.

So I need a quantity that sees degeneration at the correct scale. I make the flow gradient-like. The functional `F = integral (R + |grad f|^2)e^{-f} dV` gives monotonicity for the lowest eigenvalue of `-4 Delta + R`. The scale-sensitive functional `W = integral [tau(R + |grad f|^2) + f - n](4 pi tau)^(-n/2)e^{-f} dV`, with `tau_t = -1`, satisfies `dW/dt = 2 tau integral |Ric + Hess f - (1/(2 tau))g|^2 (4 pi tau)^(-n/2)e^{-f} dV`. This is the first important correction to my naive picture: I should not only watch curvature; I should watch a scale-normalized quantity whose monotonicity becomes rigid exactly at shrinking solitons.

From that monotonicity I get noncollapsing. If a smooth finite-time flow on a closed manifold had bounded curvature at a scale but the volume at that scale were too small, the `mu` entropy would be forced toward `-infinity`, contradicting its earlier lower bound. Thus, under the curvature bound `|Rm| <= r^{-2}`, a ball of radius `r` has volume at least `kappa r^3` for a fixed `kappa` on the finite time interval. Now Hamilton compactness becomes usable near singularities: rescale around high-curvature points, and subsequences can converge to ancient solutions instead of disappearing into collapsed geometry.

The reduced-distance construction gives the same lesson from a more local angle. I measure backward curves by `L(gamma) = integral sqrt(tau)(R + |gamma'|^2) d tau`, define `l = L/(2 sqrt(tau))`, and integrate `tau^{-n/2} exp(-l)` to get reduced volume. This reduced volume is nonincreasing as the backward parameter `tau` increases. Equivalently, when I move forward toward the base time, it cannot lose volume in the wrong direction. This is not a second story; it is the local comparison-geometry version of the same control, and it is especially useful when I need to reason about where high curvature can spread.

In dimension three another piece enters. Hamilton-Ivey pinching says that at high scalar curvature the negative curvature part is small relative to the scalar curvature. So a blow-up model near a sufficiently high-curvature point has nonnegative curvature in the limit. Combining this with noncollapsing, I am led to ancient `kappa`-solutions. Those are the possible local models I can trust.

Now the singularity can be made geometric. Sufficiently high-curvature points have canonical neighborhoods: after scaling, they are strong necks, caps, or closed positive-curvature pieces from the ancient-solution picture. This is the moment the topology becomes actionable. A neck is not just an analogy; it is a region close enough to `S^2 x I` with the round product geometry at the relevant scale. A cap is the controlled end that can replace the horn tip after a cut. A closed positive-curvature component either becomes extinct or is one of the standard spherical-type components that the construction can remove from the active flow.

I still have to be careful. A surgery rule needs more than the word "neck." I separate the small cutoff scale `h`, where the neck is cut, from the larger scale `r`, below which high-curvature geometry is known to be standard. At a singular time I keep the bounded-curvature region. In each remaining high-curvature horn, I find a sufficiently round neck of radius `h`, cut through its middle 2-sphere, remove the horn end, and glue in an almost standard cap. The cap is chosen so the pinching condition survives, and the cutoff is chosen small enough that the canonical-neighborhood and noncollapsing induction can restart.

After surgery I continue the flow. I cannot allow the cuts to accumulate chaotically on a finite time interval, and I cannot let the surgery caps corrupt the estimates needed for the next round. The delta-cutoff framework handles this by making the cutoff necks sufficiently standard and sufficiently small, while the admissible-curve reduced-length estimates recover noncollapsing for the flow with surgery. The surgery is therefore not an external topological trick attached to an analytic argument; it is part of the controlled evolution.

Once the flow is defined through surgeries, the topological consequences separate into two regimes. If the prime decomposition has no aspherical factors, the flow with surgery becomes extinct in finite time. In the simply connected closed case that leaves only the spherical conclusion. If the flow persists, the long-time geometry has a thick-thin form: thick regions, after scaling by `t^{-1}`, approach finite-volume hyperbolic manifolds, while sufficiently collapsed thin regions with local lower curvature bounds are graph manifolds. That is exactly the structure required by geometrization.

So the method is not merely "run Ricci flow and cut when it looks singular." The insight is to make singularity formation quantitatively trustworthy. `W`, `mu`, and reduced volume prevent collapse; Hamilton-Ivey pinching and ancient-solution analysis supply canonical local models; and delta-cutoff surgery continues the flow without losing the estimates. The topology is recovered because the analytic failure modes have been forced into the same shapes that topological decomposition already knows how to use.
