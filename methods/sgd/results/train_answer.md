We want to minimize an expected loss $F(w) = \mathbb{E}_z[Q(z, w)]$ — the loss on a fresh example drawn from the data distribution — but we cannot see $F$, and we cannot even see its gradient. The distribution of $z$ is unknown, so the most we can do is replace it by a finite sample of $n$ examples and minimize the empirical average $R_n(w) = \tfrac{1}{n}\sum_i Q(z_i, w)$. The textbook move is steepest descent on $R_n$, $w_{t+1} = w_t - \gamma\,\tfrac{1}{n}\sum_i \nabla Q(z_i, w_t)$, and its theory is reassuring: with an $L$-Lipschitz gradient the quadratic upper bound $F(w') \le F(w) + \nabla F(w)^\top(w'-w) + \tfrac{L}{2}\|w'-w\|^2$ guarantees a small enough step strictly decreases the objective, and under strong convexity the error contracts geometrically, residual $\sim \rho^t$ with $\rho < 1$ — linear convergence. It is also useless at scale, because every single step costs $n$ per-example gradients while the iteration count to a target accuracy barely depends on $n$; the total work therefore scales with $n$, and when $n$ is in the millions an entire pass over the data buys one tiny step. Throwing curvature at it — scaling the step by an approximate inverse Hessian for fast local convergence — only makes things worse, since now we form and invert a $w$-by-$w$ matrix on top of paying $n$ per step. Batch least squares and other closed-form fits are no escape either: they need the whole sample at once and presume a fixed functional form for the response. So the exact-gradient route is structurally too expensive; we need a step whose cost does not grow with $n$.

The cheapest possible estimate of the gradient is the gradient on a single randomly drawn example, $g_t = \nabla Q(z_t, w_t)$, whose expectation over the draw is exactly the batch gradient — an unbiased, $O(1)$-cost estimate — and, when the example is drawn from the true data stream, an unbiased estimate of $\nabla F$ itself. The temptation is just to use it, $w_{t+1} = w_t - \gamma\,\nabla Q(z_t, w_t)$, one example per step, never even remembering which examples were seen. The catch is that this single-example gradient is a wildly noisy draw of the average: far from the optimum even a noisy direction is mostly downhill, but near the optimum the true gradient goes to zero while the variance of the one-sample gradient does not — different examples disagree about the gradient even at the minimizer — so with a fixed $\gamma$ the iterate rattles around the optimum forever rather than settling. The whole question is whether that noisy one-sample step can be made exact, and what the step must do for it to work.

I propose Stochastic Gradient Descent. The method is the bare recursion
$$w_{t+1} = w_t - \gamma_t\,\nabla Q(z_t, w_t),$$
with $g_t = \nabla Q(z_t, w_t)$ an unbiased, noisy estimate of $\nabla F(w_t)$ — $O(1)$ per step, first-order only, no matrices — and the entire art is in the step-size schedule $\gamma_t$. To see why it works, strip the problem to one-dimensional mean estimation: chase the root of $h(\theta) = \theta - x$ from noisy measurements $\theta - x_n$ via $\theta_n = \theta_{n-1} - \gamma_n(\theta_{n-1} - x_n)$. Writing $\theta_n - x = (1-\gamma_n)(\theta_{n-1} - x) + \gamma_n(x_n - x)$ and unrolling to $\theta_0$ splits the error into a deterministic part — the fading memory of the start, $\prod_{k=1}^n (1-\gamma_k)(\theta_0 - x)$ — and a random part, the accumulated measurement noise. Taking second moments with $\mathbb{E}[(x_i - x)^2] = \sigma^2$ gives
$$\mathbb{E}\|\theta_n - x\|^2 = \prod_{k=1}^{n}(1-\gamma_k)^2\,\|\theta_0 - x\|^2 \;+\; \sum_{i=1}^{n}\gamma_i^2\prod_{k=i+1}^{n}(1-\gamma_k)^2\,\sigma^2,$$
and the two terms demand opposite things. The bias term needs $\prod(1-\gamma_k)^2 \to 0$; since $\log\prod(1-\gamma_k)^2 \approx -2\sum_k \gamma_k$, this forces $\sum_t \gamma_t = \infty$ — the steps must not be summable, or the method can never crawl out of a bad start. The variance term, on the other hand, settles to a constant floor $\sim \sigma^2\gamma/2$ under a fixed step, so killing it needs $\gamma_t \to 0$ with the squared steps controlled, i.e. $\sum_t \gamma_t^2 < \infty$. The borderline schedule $\gamma_t \sim 1/t$ threads both ($\sum 1/t = \infty$, $\sum 1/t^2 = \pi^2/6 < \infty$), and the punchline that earns trust is that plugging $\gamma_n = 1/n$, $\theta_0 = 0$ into the recursion reproduces $\theta_n = \tfrac{1}{n}\sum_{k=1}^n x_k$ exactly — the running sample mean. The noisy one-sample recursion *is* online averaging.

These two conditions survive the lift to general noisy root-finding, which is what makes the construction rigorous rather than a heuristic. For an unknown monotone $M(x) = \mathbb{E}[Y(x)]$ observed through bounded noisy $Y$ (with $\Pr[|Y| \le C] = 1$), the recursion $x_{n+1} - x_n = a_n(\alpha - y_n)$ chases the root of $M(x) = \alpha$. Tracking the mean-square error $b_n = \mathbb{E}(x_n - \theta)^2$ and expanding one step gives
$$b_{n+1} = b_n + a_n^2 e_n - 2 a_n d_n,$$
where the drift $d_n = \mathbb{E}[(x_n - \theta)(M(x_n) - \alpha)] \ge 0$ because monotonicity through the root makes $(x_n - \theta)$ and $(M(x_n) - \alpha)$ share a sign, and the spread $e_n$ is bounded, $0 \le e_n \le (C + |\alpha|)^2$. Summing from $1$ to $n$ and using $b_{n+1} \ge 0$ gives $2\sum_j a_j d_j \le b_1 + \sum_j a_j^2 e_j$; since $\sum a_j^2 < \infty$ makes the right side finite, the positive series $\sum a_n d_n$ converges and $b_n$ has a limit $b \ge 0$. If $b > 0$, then lower-bounding the drift by $d_n \ge \bar k_n b_n$ with $\bar k_n = \inf\{(M(x)-\alpha)/(x-\theta)\}$ over the reachable interval $|x-\theta| \le A_n$ (where $A_n = |x_1 - \theta| + (C+|\alpha|)\sum_{k<n} a_k$ bounds the iterates with probability one) yields $\sum a_n d_n \ge \tfrac{b}{2}\sum a_n \bar k_n$. The second step-size condition $\sum_{n\ge 2} a_n/(a_1 + \cdots + a_{n-1}) = \infty$ — which itself entails $\sum a_n = \infty$ — makes $\sum a_n \bar k_n = \infty$, contradicting the finiteness of $\sum a_n d_n$, so $b = 0$ and $x_n \to \theta$ in quadratic mean and in probability. The argument never uses the form of the noise distribution beyond zero mean and bounded variance, and never assumes a functional form for $M$: it is distribution-free, which is precisely what least squares cannot give.

The bridge to optimization is the observation that minimizing a loss means finding where the gradient vanishes, and $\nabla F(w) = \nabla\,\mathbb{E}_z[Q(z, w)] = \mathbb{E}_z[\nabla Q(z, w)]$ — the gradient of the expected loss is the expectation of the per-example gradient. That is exactly the root-finding shape with the unknown mean function $M(w) = \nabla F(w)$, target $\alpha = 0$, and noisy response the single-example gradient. So $w_{t+1} = w_t - \gamma_t\,\nabla Q(z_t, w_t)$ is a Robbins–Monro recursion whose convergence is already proved, provided $\sum_t \gamma_t = \infty$ and $\sum_t \gamma_t^2 < \infty$; the reckless-looking shortcut is the right method once it inherits the schedule the analysis demands. Because each $z_t$ is drawn fresh, it optimizes the expected risk $F$ directly rather than the empirical $R_n$, and never needs to remember which examples it saw.

To read off how the step trades against the noise in the optimization language, apply the Lipschitz descent bound to the random iterate and take the expectation over $z_k$ given $w_k$, using $\mathbb{E}[g_k \mid w_k] = \nabla F(w_k)$ and the bounded-variance assumption $\mathbb{E}[\|g_k\|^2 \mid w_k] \le M + M_G\|\nabla F(w_k)\|^2$ (here $M \ge 0$ is the irreducible variance that survives even at a stationary point, $M_G \ge 1$):
$$\mathbb{E}[F(w_{k+1}) \mid w_k] - F(w_k) \le -\left(\mu - \tfrac{1}{2}\gamma_k L M_G\right)\gamma_k\,\|\nabla F(w_k)\|^2 + \tfrac{1}{2}\gamma_k^2 L M.$$
The first term is the signal, negative and linear in $\gamma_k$; the second is the noise floor, positive and quadratic in $\gamma_k$, present even when $\nabla F = 0$. With $\mu \le 1$ allowing $g_k$ to be merely a descent direction in expectation, choosing the conservative $\gamma_k \le \mu/(L M_G)$ keeps the signal coefficient at least $\mu/2$. Bringing in strong convexity, $2c(F(w) - F^\ast) \le \|\nabla F(w)\|^2$, a constant step $\gamma_k = \gamma \le \mu/(L M_G)$ turns this into the affine recursion $\mathbb{E}[F(w_{k+1}) - F^\ast] \le (1 - \gamma c\mu)\mathbb{E}[F(w_k) - F^\ast] + \tfrac{1}{2}\gamma^2 L M$, whose fixed point gives
$$\mathbb{E}[F(w_k) - F^\ast] \le \frac{\gamma L M}{2 c\mu} + (1 - \gamma c\mu)^{k-1}\!\left(F(w_1) - F^\ast - \frac{\gamma L M}{2 c\mu}\right) \;\longrightarrow\; \frac{\gamma L M}{2 c\mu}.$$
A fixed step gives fast linear convergence but only to a noise ball of radius $\gamma L M/(2c\mu)$ — proportional to $\gamma$, so a smaller step shrinks the ball but slows the contraction, and with $M = 0$ it recovers exact linear convergence. This is the right reading of a fixed learning rate run to a plateau: a speed/accuracy dial, not an asymptotically exact method. To actually reach $F^\ast$, shrink the step. Taking $\gamma_k = \beta/(\gamma + k)$ and proving $\mathbb{E}[F(w_k) - F^\ast] \le \nu/(\gamma + k)$ by induction, the inductive step reduces (after using $\nu/\hat k - \nu/\hat k^2 \le \nu/(\hat k + 1)$ with $\hat k = \gamma + k$) to requiring $(\beta c\mu - 1)\nu \ge \beta^2 L M/2$; choosing $\beta > 1/(c\mu)$ makes the coefficient positive, and $\nu = \max\{\beta^2 L M/(2(\beta c\mu - 1)),\,(\gamma+1)(F(w_1) - F^\ast)\}$ closes both base case and step, giving $O(1/k)$ convergence with no floor. The condition $\beta > 1/(c\mu)$ is the sharp "don't shrink the steps too fast," the optimization echo of $\sum\gamma = \infty$. Since $\gamma_k = \beta/k$ can mean an enormous first step, in practice start from a sane value and decay into the regime with $\gamma_k = \gamma_0/(1 + \gamma_0\lambda k)$, equal to $\gamma_0$ at $k=0$ and behaving like $1/(\lambda k)$, chosen with slack so $1/\lambda > 1/(c\mu)$.

Per iteration SGD is genuinely worse than batch descent — $1/k$ versus linear $\rho^t$ — but on the scoreboard that matters for learning, expected risk per unit compute, the accounting flips: batch costs $n$ per step and SGD costs $1$, so in the large-scale regime where time is the bottleneck SGD processes far more examples in the allotted budget and lands at a better expected risk, and the slow $1/k$ tail never bites because we stop at a plateau long before it matters. Scaling the step by curvature does not help the leading behavior, because it warps the signal term but leaves the noise term $\gamma_k^2 M$ untouched — the variance is unchanged — so the only way past the noise wall is averaging: more samples per step (the mini-batch $\bar y_n = \tfrac{1}{r}\sum y$ divides $e_n$ by $r$ at unchanged drift), decaying steps, or running a one-line iterate average $\bar w_t = \tfrac{1}{t - t_0}\sum_{i > t_0} w_i$ and reporting $\bar w$ to cancel the zero-mean rattle.

Filling the one empty slot of the diagonal-net optimizer harness, the canonical minimal form is constant-step SGD: subtract a fixed multiple of the gradient from each parameter vector and return the updated tuple.

```python
from typing import Any
import torch


def get_hyperparameters(dim: int, sparsity: int, delta: float) -> dict[str, Any]:
    """Single knob: the step size gamma."""
    return {"lr": 0.1}


def init_state(u: torch.Tensor, v: torch.Tensor,
               hyperparameters: dict[str, Any]) -> dict[str, Any]:
    """Bare SGD keeps no per-parameter state."""
    return {"t": 0}


def step(u: torch.Tensor, v: torch.Tensor,
         grad_u: torch.Tensor, grad_v: torch.Tensor,
         state: dict[str, Any], hyperparameters: dict[str, Any]
         ) -> tuple[torch.Tensor, torch.Tensor, dict[str, Any]]:
    """w_{t+1} = w_t - gamma * g_t (stochastic-approximation recursion, alpha=0)."""
    lr = float(hyperparameters["lr"])
    state["t"] = state.get("t", 0) + 1
    return u - lr * grad_u, v - lr * grad_v, state
```
