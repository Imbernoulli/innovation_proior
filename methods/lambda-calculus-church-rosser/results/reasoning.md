I built this calculus out of three moves only — a variable, an abstraction `λx.M`, an application `(M N)` — and one computational rule, `(λx.M)N -> M[x:=N]`, substituting the argument into the body wherever the bound variable occurs. Curry already showed, for the combinatory calculus that is exactly interchangeable with mine, a real if modest kind of consistency: you can add axioms letting more combinators be proved equal without ever being able to prove `K = S`. Not every function collapses into every other function. That mattered because this whole apparatus was also dressed up as a foundation for logic — implication, quantifiers, a notion of provable proposition riding on the same syntax. Then Kleene and Rosser (1935) found a variant of the Richard paradox living inside exactly that logical dressing: read as a logic, the system proves every proposition, mine included, so it is worthless as a foundation for mathematics. Strip the borrowed logic back off, though, and what remains is not nothing — a bare rewriting system, terms and one substitution rule — and the only honest question left about it is computational, not logical: when a term offers several places I could reduce, does the choice matter?

A term can carry several redexes at once — several subterms of the shape `(λx.M)N` waiting to be contracted — and contracting one can create, duplicate, or delete the others, so two evaluators making different choices watch the same starting term pass through completely different shapes. If those two paths could land somewhere genuinely incompatible, the calculus would not describe one notion of "the value of an expression"; it would describe a family of evaluators that happen to share notation. So the property I need is: whenever `X0` reduces, in any number of steps, to `X1` and, by a different sequence of choices, to `X2`, there is some `X3` reachable by further reduction from both. Confluence, not agreement on one canonical route — the paths are allowed to diverge, they are just not allowed to diverge forever.

The cheapest way to get this would be one step at a time: if `X0` reduces by a single contraction to `X1` and, differently, to `X2`, find a single contraction from each of `X1`, `X2` down to a common `X3`. When the two redexes sit in disjoint parts of `X0` this is immediate — contract in each branch whichever redex is still sitting untouched from the other branch. The trouble starts when one redex's argument is being duplicated by the other. Take `X0 = (λx. (f x)(g x)) V` with `V = (λy.y) a`. Contract the inner redex first: `V` reduces to `a`, giving `X2 = (λx. (f x)(g x)) a`, one further contraction away from `X3 = (f a)(g a)`. Contract the outer redex first instead: substituting `V` for both free occurrences of `x` produces, in one step, `X1 = (f V)(g V) = (f ((λy.y) a))(g ((λy.y) a))` — two fresh copies of the very redex I hadn't touched yet. `X1` now needs two separate contractions, one per copy, before it reaches `X3`; there is no single contraction taking `X1` anywhere `X2` can also reach in one step, since `X2`'s only redex is the outer one and it goes straight to `X3`. Substitution is exactly what breaks the naive one-step diamond: contracting a redex whose bound variable occurs `n` times manufactures `n` copies of whatever was still unreduced in the argument, and undoing that costs `n` steps, not one. This is not a corner case to special-case away — it happens for every redex whose bound variable occurs more than once, and terms exist precisely so that a variable can occur many times.

If a single contraction cannot see all the copies a substitution is about to create, the unit I reason about has to be fattened enough to create them together. So I stop working with one redex at a time and define a coarser one-step relation, parallel reduction `M ⇒ M'`: every term parallel-reduces to itself; if `M ⇒ M'` then `λx.M ⇒ λx.M'`; if `M ⇒ M'` and `N ⇒ N'` then `M N ⇒ M' N'`; and, the clause that does the real work, if `M ⇒ M'` and `N ⇒ N'` then `(λx.M) N ⇒ M'[x:=N']`. That last clause contracts the redex and simultaneously finishes whatever reduction was already in progress inside the body and the argument, so every copy the substitution is about to manufacture is already the settled `N'`, never the stale `N`. Re-running the example: from `X0` I can reach `X3` directly in one parallel step, by letting the body reduce to itself while the argument reduces `V ⇒ a` and then applying the redex clause — duplication and the reduction of the duplicated piece now happen in the same step instead of `n` sequential ones. Ordinary one-step reduction sits inside `⇒`, and `⇒` sits inside the transitive closure of ordinary reduction, so proving the diamond property for `⇒` changes nothing about what is reachable — it only fattens the unit I induct on.

Proving `⇒` has the diamond property, by induction on the term, comes down to exactly the nested case: `X0 = (λx.M)N` parallel-reducing two ways, `X0 ⇒ M1[x:=N1]` via `M⇒M1, N⇒N1` and `X0 ⇒ M2[x:=N2]` via `M⇒M2, N⇒N2`. The induction hypothesis on the smaller terms `M, N` supplies `M3, N3` with `M1⇒M3, M2⇒M3, N1⇒N3, N2⇒N3`. What still has to be checked is that `M1[x:=N1] ⇒ M3[x:=N3]`, and symmetrically from the other side — that parallel reduction commutes with substitution: `P⇒P', Q⇒Q'` implies `P[x:=Q] ⇒ P'[x:=Q']`. That commuting fact is proved by induction on how `P⇒P'` itself was derived, and its one nontrivial case is a redex being contracted inside `P`, where the outer substitution has to slide past a substitution that was just freshly created. The elementary fact that makes it slide is that two substitutions into the same term always reorder correctly, `M[x:=N][y:=L] = M[y:=L][x:=N[y:=L]]` whenever `x ≠ y` and `x` is not free in `L` — nothing about reduction, just about how substitution composes. Granting that, the nested-redex case of the diamond closes, with `M3[x:=N3]` as the common reduct. So `⇒` itself has the diamond property, and the standard tiling argument — lay the reduction sequence from `X0` to `X1` out as one column of diamonds, the sequence to `X2` as another, and complete the grid one cell at a time — upgrades that into full confluence of iterated reduction. That is the theorem.

What I actually wanted from the start is the corollary: a term's normal form, if it has one, does not depend on how I got there. If `X` reduces to two normal forms `A` and `B`, confluence gives some `P` with `A` reducing to `P` and `B` reducing to `P`; but `A, B` have no redex left to touch, so the only reduction available from either is the empty one, forcing `P = A = B` up to renaming of bound variables. That handles the case where I only ever reduce. But provable equality — the symmetric, transitive notion of conversion that allows β-expansions as well as reductions, which is the notion the consistency question is really about — is more general than "reduces to," and a normal form could in principle be reached by a path that expands before it reduces. That case needs its own induction, on the length of the conversion chain: any single zigzag inside it — expand once, then reduce twice, landing on the normal form — collapses, because the two reductions bracketing the expansion share a common downstream term by confluence, and since the far end is already normal, that common term can only be the far end itself up to renaming; so the expansion drops out of the chain without changing where it lands. Chase this collapsing step down the whole chain and I am back in the pure-reduction case already solved. So even under the full symmetric notion of equality, not just "this reduces to that" but "these are provably the same term," normal forms stay unique.

There is a temptation, once the argument is phrased as diamonds and squares, to think the content is purely graph-theoretic — that any relation drawn with the diamond shape would hand me this theorem for free, whatever its nodes are made of. Newman's abstract restatement of the argument a few years later does exactly that: strip away the syntax, keep only an abstract relation with the diamond shape, and confluence follows by the same shape of induction. But running the lambda calculus itself through that abstract theorem, or through Curry's later generalization of it, does not simply reproduce what I did here — the specific hypotheses the abstract theorem demands turned out not to hold for this term-rewriting relation, and a counterexample to the naive transfer surfaced later. The geometric picture is real, and it is where the intuition should live, but it does not substitute for the substitution lemma above; the diamond has to be built out of the actual algebra of `M[x:=N]`, not merely asserted of a graph that happens to look the same shape from a distance.

What I have, once this is all in hand, is a rewriting system where local choice — which redex to contract, in what order — is completely free, and yet the global notion of what a term equals is not hostage to that choice: strategies can disagree on the route, and even on whether they terminate at all, and still cannot disagree on the destination once one is reached. That is the real content of calling this a consistency theorem for a calculus that had just failed, as a logic, to be consistent at all: I can no longer promise every provable proposition is true, but I can still promise the pure computational core underneath it does not contradict itself.

This is not, historically, quite the route taken in 1936: the original proof of the theorem that now carries the Church-Rosser name was long and worked directly with chains of ordinary reductions and heavy case analysis, without any fattened parallel step. Newman's abstract, topological restatement came in 1942 and, as noted, does not by itself reach the lambda calculus's own theorem — a gap that Curry's 1952 generalization inherited too, and that a counterexample recorded a few years later made explicit. The parallel-reduction shortcut above traces to lectures of Tait's that Martin-Löf wrote up in 1972, with Rosser's own later account (1982/1984) giving what he considered the shortest version — inducting on the size of the term rather than on the length of a reduction sequence, which is the measure I used above. That the fix was simply "stop reducing one redex at a time and reduce a whole marked set of them together" took some thirty-six years to surface after the original 1936 theorem, not because the idea is deep once seen, but because nobody had isolated the duplication failure precisely enough to see what shape of unit would repair it.
