The thing that bothers me is concrete: I have a graph convolution that works beautifully at two layers and falls apart at four. Every time I stack more layers expecting a wider receptive field, accuracy goes *down*. On images, depth is the whole game — the deeper I go the better, up to dozens or hundreds of layers. On this citation graph, depth is poison. I want to understand exactly why, because whatever I do about it has to attack the actual mechanism, not just bolt a vision trick onto a graph and hope.

So let me write down the layer and stare at it. $\mathbf H^{(\ell+1)}=\sigma(\tilde{\mathbf P}\mathbf H^{(\ell)}\mathbf W^{(\ell)})$, with $\tilde{\mathbf P}=\tilde{\mathbf D}^{-1/2}\tilde{\mathbf A}\tilde{\mathbf D}^{-1/2}$ the renormalized symmetric adjacency of the self-looped graph. Strip the weights and nonlinearities for a moment to see the pure propagation: $K$ layers, ignoring $\mathbf W$ and $\sigma$, is just $\tilde{\mathbf P}^{K}\mathbf x$ on a signal. That's a fixed operator raised to the $K$-th power. What does $\tilde{\mathbf P}^{K}$ do as $K$ grows? $\tilde{\mathbf P}$ is symmetric and stochastic-like; the renormalization trick was designed to pin its spectrum into $[-1,1]$ with the top eigenvalue at 1. So I build a tiny concrete graph and look at what the spectrum actually is. A triangle on nodes $0,1,2$ plus a pendant node $3$ hanging off node $0$. Adding self-loops gives degrees $d_i+1=(4,3,3,2)$, and the eigenvalues of the resulting $\tilde{\mathbf P}$ come out to $(-0.148,\,0,\,0.564,\,1)$. So yes: the top is exactly 1, everything else is strictly inside $(-1,1)$. Raise it to the $K$ and every eigencomponent except the top one decays geometrically; only the eigenvector for $\lambda=1$ survives. So $\tilde{\mathbf P}^{K}\mathbf x$ converges to a fixed vector that has nothing to do with the high-frequency content of $\mathbf x$ — it's a projection onto a single mode.

I want to know the *form* of that surviving mode, because the form tells me exactly what's being destroyed. Let me work the limit by hand. I'll actually use the lazy version $(\mathbf I+\tilde{\mathbf P})/2$ — it's the natural one once a residual connection is in play and it has the same stationary state with cleaner convergence. The trick is to conjugate it into a random walk. Note $\mathbf I = \tilde{\mathbf D}^{-1/2}\tilde{\mathbf D}^{1/2}$, so
$$\Big(\tfrac{\mathbf I+\tilde{\mathbf P}}{2}\Big)^{K}=\Big(\tilde{\mathbf D}^{-1/2}\,\tfrac{\mathbf I+\tilde{\mathbf A}\tilde{\mathbf D}^{-1}}{2}\,\tilde{\mathbf D}^{1/2}\Big)^{K}=\tilde{\mathbf D}^{-1/2}\Big(\tfrac{\mathbf I+\tilde{\mathbf A}\tilde{\mathbf D}^{-1}}{2}\Big)^{K}\tilde{\mathbf D}^{1/2},$$
because the inner $\tilde{\mathbf D}^{1/2}\tilde{\mathbf D}^{-1/2}$ pairs telescope. The middle factor $(\mathbf I+\tilde{\mathbf A}\tilde{\mathbf D}^{-1})/2$ is precisely the transition matrix of a *lazy random walk* on the self-looped graph — column-stochastic, with probability $1/2$ of staying put. Apply it to a basis vector $\mathbf e_i$ and the $K$-th power is the $K$-step distribution $\mathbf p^{(K)}_i$ of a walk started at $i$. A lazy walk on a connected graph converges to the unique stationary distribution, which for a walk on $\tilde G$ is proportional to the degrees: $\mathbf p^{(K)}_i(j)\to (d_j+1)/(2m+n)$. There's a Cheeger-type bound on how fast — the $j$-th entry sits within $\sqrt{(d_j+1)/(d_i+1)}\,(1-\lambda_{\tilde G}^2/2)^{K}$ of the stationary value, where $\lambda_{\tilde G}$ is the spectral gap of the self-looped graph's normalized Laplacian.

Now expand $\tilde{\mathbf D}^{1/2}\mathbf x=\sum_i x(i)\sqrt{d_i+1}\,\mathbf e_i$, push the walk through, and pull $\tilde{\mathbf D}^{-1/2}$ back out. Entry $j$ of the result is
$$\mathbf h^{(K)}(j)=\sum_i \sqrt{d_i+1}\,x(i)\,\frac{1}{\sqrt{d_j+1}}\Big(\frac{d_j+1}{2m+n}\pm\sqrt{\tfrac{d_j+1}{d_i+1}}\big(1-\tfrac{\lambda_{\tilde G}^2}{2}\big)^{K}\Big),\quad\text{so}\quad\Big|\mathbf h^{(K)}(j)-\sum_i \frac{\sqrt{(d_j+1)(d_i+1)}}{2m+n}x(i)\Big|\ \le\ \Big(\sum_i |x_i|\Big)\big(1-\tfrac{\lambda_{\tilde G}^2}{2}\big)^{K}$$
by the triangle inequality on each term $\sqrt{d_i+1}\,x(i)/\sqrt{d_j+1}$ times its own $\pm$ bound.
So in the limit my prediction is $\mathbf h^{(K)}\to\boldsymbol\pi=\frac{\langle\tilde{\mathbf D}^{1/2}\mathbf 1,\mathbf x\rangle}{2m+n}\tilde{\mathbf D}^{1/2}\mathbf 1$ — every node's value is just $\sqrt{d_j+1}$ times one global scalar, the inner product of the features with the degree-weighted all-ones vector.

That's a strong claim, so let me test it on the four-node graph before I build anything on it. Pick a feature signal with real high-frequency content, $\mathbf x=(1,-1,0.5,2)$. Here $2m+n=\sum_i(d_i+1)=12$, the degree-weighted projection scalar $\langle\tilde{\mathbf D}^{1/2}\mathbf 1,\mathbf x\rangle/12$ comes out to about $0.330$, and $\boldsymbol\pi=0.330\cdot(\sqrt4,\sqrt3,\sqrt3,\sqrt2)=(0.660,0.572,0.572,0.467)$. Now iterate $\tilde{\mathbf P}^{K}\mathbf x$ and watch:

| $K$ | $\tilde{\mathbf P}^{K}\mathbf x$ | $\|\tilde{\mathbf P}^{K}\mathbf x-\boldsymbol\pi\|$ |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | $(0.813,\,0.122,\,0.122,\,1.354)$ | $1.10$ |
| 4 | $(0.689,\,0.491,\,0.491,\,0.626)$ | $1.98\times10^{-1}$ |
| 16 | $(0.660,\,0.572,\,0.572,\,0.467)$ | $2.07\times10^{-4}$ |
| 64 | $(0.660,\,0.572,\,0.572,\,0.467)$ | $8\times10^{-15}$ |

It lands exactly on $\boldsymbol\pi$, and the geometric decay is visible in the error column. And the decisive structural check: at $K=256$ the ratio $\tilde{\mathbf P}^{K}\mathbf x(j)/\sqrt{d_j+1}$ is $0.330$ at *every* node — a single constant. The deep representation is exactly the degree vector scaled by one global number. After enough layers, the only things a node's representation knows are its own degree and a single feature summary of the whole graph. Two nodes with the same degree are *identical*, regardless of what they actually are. That is over-smoothing, and now I've watched it happen rather than asserted it: the deep network throws away exactly the per-node feature information I need to classify with. Notice node $3$ (degree $2$, the lowest) is also the slowest to converge in the table — which fits the bound, since the perturbation term scales like $1/\sqrt{d_j+1}$.

That rate difference is worth pinning down exactly. The bound above is an *absolute* one — the same size for every node $j$ — but $\pi_j=\sqrt{d_j+1}\,C$ (with $C=\sum_i\sqrt{d_i+1}\,x(i)/(2m+n)$ the same global scalar for every node) itself grows with $\sqrt{d_j+1}$. Dividing the absolute bound by $|\pi_j|$ turns it into a genuinely *relative* one:
$$\frac{|\mathbf h^{(K)}(j)-\pi_j|}{|\pi_j|}\ \le\ \frac{\sum_i|x_i|}{|C|\sqrt{d_j+1}}\big(1-\tfrac{\lambda_{\tilde G}^2}{2}\big)^K,$$
and since $C$ is the same for every node, the right side still shrinks like $1/\sqrt{d_j+1}$ — high-degree nodes over-smooth first (in relative terms; the table above already shows it in absolute terms too, since node $3$, the lowest degree, has the largest absolute error at every finite $K$).

So the disease is clear: stacking the fixed operator is a contraction toward a degree-only fixed point. The standard cure for "deep networks degrade" is ResNet — a residual connection, where instead of replacing the signal a layer learns a correction added to it, $\mathbf H^{(\ell+1)}=\sigma((\tilde{\mathbf P}\mathbf H^{(\ell)}+\mathbf H^{(\ell)})\mathbf W^{(\ell)})$. Let me see what that actually buys. The propagation part becomes $(\mathbf I+\tilde{\mathbf P})\mathbf H^{(\ell)}$ — exactly the lazy walk I just analyzed (up to the $\tfrac12$, which the weights absorb). And a lazy walk converges to the *same* stationary distribution as the non-lazy one; laziness slows the mixing, it doesn't move the fixed point. I check it directly, because the whole point of trying the residual is to escape the degree vector. On the same graph I iterate $((\mathbf I+\tilde{\mathbf P})/2)^{K}\mathbf x$ to large $K$: it converges to $(0.660,0.572,0.572,0.467)$ — *identical* to the plain $\tilde{\mathbf P}^{K}$ limit, with the same constant ratio $0.330$ across nodes. So the ResNet residual to the previous layer just makes the contraction slower; it postpones over-smoothing, it doesn't prevent it. That matches what I see empirically: residual GCNs go a bit deeper before collapsing, then collapse anyway, and a 2-layer plain GCN still beats them. The skip-to-previous-layer is aimed at the wrong target, because the previous layer is *itself already partly smoothed* — adding it back only feeds smoothed signal into more smoothing.

That reframes the question precisely. The problem isn't "add a skip"; it's *what do I skip back to*. The previous layer is contaminated. The one representation that is guaranteed un-smoothed is the *input* — layer zero, the freshly transformed features, before any propagation touched them. So skip to *that*. At every layer, mix the smoothed representation with a fixed fraction of the original input:
$$\mathbf H^{(\ell+1)}=\big((1-\alpha)\,\tilde{\mathbf P}\mathbf H^{(\ell)}+\alpha\,\mathbf H^{(0)}\big),$$
where $\mathbf H^{(0)}$ is a learned map of the raw features. What's the limit now? Unroll it: $\mathbf H^{(K)}=\sum_{\ell}(1-\alpha)^{\ell}\alpha\,\tilde{\mathbf P}^{\ell}\mathbf H^{(0)}+(1-\alpha)^K\tilde{\mathbf P}^K\mathbf H^{(0)}$, a geometric mixture of all hop-distances of the input, whose fixed point should be $\alpha(\mathbf I-(1-\alpha)\tilde{\mathbf P})^{-1}\mathbf H^{(0)}$ — the personalized-PageRank diffusion of the input. Let me check that against the degree vector on the toy graph, because the entire claim is that this limit is *different* from $\boldsymbol\pi$. With $\alpha=0.1$ and $\mathbf H^{(0)}=\mathbf x$, iterating the recurrence to convergence gives $(0.723,0.332,0.482,0.782)$, and the closed form $\alpha(\mathbf I-0.9\tilde{\mathbf P})^{-1}\mathbf x$ gives the same vector — good, the unroll is right. And the decisive test: the ratio $\mathbf H(j)/\sqrt{d_j+1}$ is now $(0.362,0.192,0.278,0.553)$ — *not* constant. The fixed point is no longer proportional to the degree vector; it carries the per-node features. So re-injecting an $\alpha$-fraction of $\mathbf H^{(0)}$ at every layer anchors the representation to the input and prevents the collapse to the pure degree mode. This is the teleport idea: a random surfer that with probability $\alpha$ jumps back to where it started keeps locality forever. The initial residual, by itself, fixes the over-smoothing of the *propagation*. I'll want $\alpha$ small — around $0.1$ — so propagation still dominates and the receptive field genuinely widens, but large enough that the input anchor never vanishes.

But this isn't the end, and the reason is the form of the update I just wrote: it's *linear* in $\mathbf H^{(\ell)}$ — no weight matrix, no nonlinearity per layer. If I want the expressive power of a *deep nonlinear* network — the thing that makes depth pay on images — I need a learnable $\mathbf W^{(\ell)}$ and a $\sigma$ at each layer. The obvious move is to put them back: $\mathbf H^{(\ell+1)}=\sigma\big(((1-\alpha)\tilde{\mathbf P}\mathbf H^{(\ell)}+\alpha\mathbf H^{(0)})\mathbf W^{(\ell)}\big)$. And the moment I do, depth starts hurting again — but now for a *different* reason than over-smoothing. It's the weight matrices.

Why would the weights hurt? Two things converge on the same answer. First, there's a result on the rate of subspace collapse: a $K$-layer stack drives features into a low-dimensional subspace at a rate controlled by $s^{K}$, where $s$ is the largest singular value of the per-layer weight matrices. If the effective transforms become contractions, $s^{K}$ decays and the feature space collapses; if they become expansive, the product is unstable. The only stable target for a deep product is to keep the useful singular values near 1. Second, there's the small-data reality: $20$ labels per class is almost nothing, and $K$ free $H\times H$ weight matrices is a lot of parameters to fit from that — they overfit, and in semi-supervised graphs the specific failure mode is *too much interaction between feature dimensions*, the dense remixing that a full $\mathbf W$ performs every layer. So I want each layer's weight matrix to be *close to the identity* — both to keep $s\approx1$ (no collapse, no blowup) and to do only a gentle, regularizable amount of feature mixing.

How do I make a learnable weight matrix that defaults to identity? Take the ResNet lesson literally at the level of the matrix itself: write the layer's effective transform as $(1-\beta)\mathbf I+\beta\mathbf W^{(\ell)}$. When $\beta\to0$ this is the identity — the layer ignores its weights entirely and just propagates-with-teleport, i.e. it falls back to the linear PageRank diffusion that I already checked doesn't over-smooth. When $\beta$ is moderate, the layer does a $\beta$-sized amount of learned feature transformation on top. So
$$\mathbf H^{(\ell+1)}=\sigma\Big(\big((1-\alpha_\ell)\tilde{\mathbf P}\mathbf H^{(\ell)}+\alpha_\ell\mathbf H^{(0)}\big)\big((1-\beta_\ell)\mathbf I+\beta_\ell\mathbf W^{(\ell)}\big)\Big).$$
There's a reason this identity-mapping form is *safe* to regularize hard, beyond hand-waving. For a linear residual network $\mathbf H(\mathbf W+\mathbf I)$ it's known that the optimal weights have small norm and that the only critical point is the global minimum. So pushing $\mathbf W$ toward zero (strong weight decay on $\mathbf W$ specifically) is not fighting the model — the optimum lives there anyway — and it removes the spurious local minima that scarce-label training would otherwise get trapped in. Does small $\mathbf W$ actually keep $s$ near 1, though? I check numerically. With strong $L_2$ keeping $\|\mathbf W\|$ small (say entries $\sim\mathcal N(0,0.3^2)$ on an $8\times8$ block), the mean largest singular value of $(1-\beta)\mathbf I+\beta\mathbf W$ over many draws is: $1.028$ at $\beta=0.41$ (layer 1's $\beta$), $0.999$ at $\beta=0.12$ (layer 4's), $0.999$ at $\beta=0.031$ (layer 16's), $1.000$ at $\beta=0.0078$ (layer 64's) — all sitting right at 1. So $s\approx1$, $s^{K}$ stays $O(1)$, and the subspace collapse is defused. The two cures interlock: initial residual stops the *propagation* from smoothing, identity mapping stops the *weights* from collapsing the rank or overfitting.

Now $\beta_\ell$. I don't want a single constant. The deeper a layer sits, the *more* I want it to behave like a pure identity (just propagate, don't transform), because the cumulative product of transforms is what blows the singular values around, and the late layers are where over-transformation does the most damage. So $\beta_\ell$ should *decay* with depth $\ell$. A natural decaying schedule is $\beta_\ell=\log(\frac{\lambda}{\ell}+1)\approx \lambda/\ell$ for a single hyperparameter $\lambda$. With $\lambda=0.5$ this gives $\beta_1=0.41,\,\beta_4=0.12,\,\beta_{16}=0.031,\,\beta_{64}=0.0078$ — layer 1 gets a real transformation budget, and by layer 64 the effective weight is $0.992\,\mathbf I+0.008\,\mathbf W$, essentially pure identity. (And those are exactly the $\beta$ values whose $s_{\max}\approx1$ I just checked above, so the schedule and the stability check agree.) This way the *transformation* decays adaptively as I stack more layers while the *propagation-with-anchor* keeps going — exactly the asymmetry I want.

Let me make sure this actually restores the thing over-smoothing destroyed: the ability to be a flexible filter. The deep-GCN pathology was that $K$ layers realize a *fixed* polynomial filter $\tilde{\mathbf P}^{K}\mathbf x$ — predetermined coefficients, no freedom. Can this new layer realize an *arbitrary* order-$K$ polynomial in the operator? Take the weak linearized version of my layer to test the coefficient mechanism: fix $\alpha_\ell=\tfrac12$, absorb the harmless factor $\tfrac12$ into a scalar $\gamma_\ell$, keep the activation in a region where ReLU acts as the identity, and let the effective weight be $\gamma_\ell\mathbf I$. The recurrence I need to analyze is
$$\mathbf H^{(\ell+1)}=\gamma_\ell(\tilde{\mathbf P}\mathbf H^{(\ell)}+\mathbf x)=\gamma_\ell((\mathbf I-\tilde{\mathbf L})\mathbf H^{(\ell)}+\mathbf x),$$
with $\mathbf H^{(0)}=\mathbf 0$ and $\mathbf x$ the fixed initial representation being re-injected. Unroll it exactly:
$$\mathbf H^{(K)}=\Big(\sum_{\ell=0}^{K-1}\Big(\textstyle\prod_{r=K-\ell-1}^{K-1}\gamma_r\Big)(\mathbf I-\tilde{\mathbf L})^{\ell}\Big)\mathbf x.$$
On the other side, an arbitrary order-$(K-1)$ filter $\big(\sum_k\theta_k\tilde{\mathbf L}^{k}\big)\mathbf x$ re-expands in powers of $(\mathbf I-\tilde{\mathbf L})$ by the binomial theorem, $\tilde{\mathbf L}=\mathbf I-(\mathbf I-\tilde{\mathbf L})$:
$$\Big(\sum_{k}\theta_k\tilde{\mathbf L}^{k}\Big)\mathbf x=\Big(\sum_{\ell}\Big(\sum_{k\ge\ell}\theta_k(-1)^{\ell}\tbinom{k}{\ell}\Big)(\mathbf I-\tilde{\mathbf L})^{\ell}\Big)\mathbf x.$$
Match coefficients of $(\mathbf I-\tilde{\mathbf L})^{\ell}$ between the two: I need
$$\prod_{r=K-\ell-1}^{K-1}\gamma_r=\sum_{k=\ell}^{K-1}\theta_k(-1)^{\ell}\binom{k}{\ell}\quad\text{for every }\ell=0,\ldots,K-1.$$
The left side is a *partial product* of the $\gamma$'s, so I can solve telescopically. Let $c_\ell=\sum_{k=\ell}^{K-1}\theta_k(-1)^\ell\binom{k}{\ell}$. Then $\gamma_{K-1}=c_0=\sum_k\theta_k$, and for $\ell\ge1$ I set $\gamma_{K-\ell-1}=c_\ell/c_{\ell-1}$.

Telescoping divisions are easy to get backwards, so I check the construction directly. Take $K=3$ on the toy graph, a target filter with deliberately arbitrary coefficients $\theta=(0.7,-0.4,0.9)$. Computing the target $(\sum_k\theta_k\tilde{\mathbf L}^k)\mathbf x$ directly gives $(0.739,-1.086,0.714,1.373)$. Now run the recipe: $c=(1.2,-1.4,0.9)$, which gives $\gamma_2=c_0=1.2$, $\gamma_1=c_1/c_0=-1.167$, $\gamma_0=c_2/c_1=-0.643$. Check the partial-product identity: $\prod$ over the last one, two, three $\gamma$'s gives $1.2,\,-1.4,\,0.9$ — matching $c_0,c_1,c_2$ exactly. And running the scalar recurrence $\mathbf H\leftarrow\gamma_\ell(\tilde{\mathbf P}\mathbf H+\mathbf x)$ from $\mathbf 0$ produces $(0.739,-1.086,0.714,1.373)$ — *identical* to the target filter. So the per-layer scalars really do realize an arbitrary order-$(K-1)$ filter. If a denominator $c_{\ell-1}$ is exactly zero the ratio is singular, but that's a measure-zero set: an arbitrarily small perturbation of the coefficients gives an arbitrarily close achievable filter. The free $\beta_\ell$/$\mathbf W^{(\ell)}$ are exactly what supply the per-hop coefficients that the fixed GCN couldn't. Arbitrary coefficients mean the network can *choose* ones whose limit keeps the input — e.g. the geometric $\theta'_i=\alpha(1-\alpha)^i$ that converges to the PageRank vector, a function of both graph and features — instead of being forced into the degree-only collapse. That's the formal statement of why depth is now a resource: the network can *learn* not to over-smooth, where before the coefficients were nailed down for it.

A cross-check from a completely different direction. Consider recovering a sparse code: $\min_x \tfrac12\|\mathbf B\mathbf x-\mathbf y\|^2+\lambda\|\mathbf x\|_1$, with $\mathbf y$ the observed node feature and $\mathbf x$ the embedding to recover, $\mathbf B$ a learned dictionary. The iterative shrinkage step is $\mathbf x^{t+1}=P_{\mu\lambda}\big(\mathbf x^t-\mu\mathbf B^\top\mathbf B\mathbf x^t+\mu\mathbf B^\top\mathbf y\big)$. Reparameterize $-\mathbf B^\top\mathbf B$ as a learned $\mathbf W$ and it becomes $\mathbf x^{t+1}=P_{\mu\lambda}\big((\mathbf I+\mu\mathbf W)\mathbf x^t+\mu\mathbf B^\top\mathbf y\big)$ — and now I read off the pieces: $(\mathbf I+\mu\mathbf W)$ is exactly my identity-mapped weight, $\mu\mathbf B^\top\mathbf y$ (a fixed function of the observation $\mathbf y$, re-added every step) is exactly my initial residual to $\mathbf H^{(0)}$, and the soft-threshold $P$ plays the role of ReLU. So the layer I derived from spectral over-smoothing arguments is *also* one unrolled step of recovering a sparse representation of the node's own features — the identity mapping and the initial residual aren't two ad-hoc patches, they're the two terms that any such iterative reconstruction must have.

Now the architecture around the layer. I keep a *single fixed hidden width* throughout — the input features get mapped down to $H$ by one fully-connected layer to form $\mathbf H^{(0)}$, then *every* propagation layer is $H\to H$ (so $\mathbf H^{(0)}$ and $\mathbf H^{(\ell)}$ live in the same space and the initial residual is a legal add), and a final fully-connected layer maps $H$ to the class logits. Crucially the per-layer weights are $H\times H$, *not* feature-sized, so the parameter count is $\text{(input FC)}+\text{(output FC)}+L\cdot H^2$ — it grows only linearly in depth with a small constant, which is what makes "go deep" affordable here. Dropout before each propagation layer and before the output FC; ReLU as $\sigma$; and the weight decay split so that the $\mathbf W^{(\ell)}$ inside the convolution layers get *strong* regularization (the small-norm-optimum argument licenses it) while the input/output FC layers get the usual mild decay.

Let me consolidate $\beta_\ell$ into the layer so the code is exact. Inside one layer, with $\theta_\ell\equiv\beta_\ell=\log(\lambda/\ell+1)$: compute the smoothed message $\mathbf h_i=\tilde{\mathbf P}\mathbf H^{(\ell)}$, form the teleported support $\mathbf s=(1-\alpha)\mathbf h_i+\alpha\mathbf H^{(0)}$, then apply the identity-mapped weight, which expands as $\mathbf s\big((1-\theta)\mathbf I+\theta\mathbf W\big)=(1-\theta)\mathbf s+\theta\,\mathbf s\mathbf W$ — a $\theta$-weighted blend of the untransformed support and its $\mathbf W$-transform — and ReLU. That blend form is the cheap, numerically clean way to write the identity mapping. The normalized sparse adjacency $\tilde{\mathbf P}$ is prepared once from the self-looped graph, so the layer itself is exactly a sparse matrix multiply followed by the support blend.

```python
def forward(self, x, adj, h0, lamda, alpha, layer):
    theta = math.log(lamda / layer + 1.0)               # beta_l = log(lambda/l + 1)
    hi = torch.spmm(adj, x)                              # P̃ H^{(l)}
    support = (1 - alpha) * hi + alpha * h0              # initial residual to H^{(0)}
    return theta * torch.mm(support, self.weight) + (1 - theta) * support
```

That's the whole layer: one sparse matmul, a teleport blend toward $\mathbf H^{(0)}$, and a depth-decaying identity-weighted transform. Stacked at the fixed width $H$ — one input FC forming $\mathbf H^{(0)}$, $L$ of these layers, one output FC, dropout before each, strong weight decay only on the $\mathbf W^{(\ell)}$'s — depth is finally a resource instead of a liability.
