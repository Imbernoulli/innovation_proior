The thing that bothers me is concrete: I have a graph convolution that works beautifully at two layers and falls apart at four. Every time I stack more layers expecting a wider receptive field, accuracy goes *down*. On images, depth is the whole game — the deeper I go the better, up to dozens or hundreds of layers. On this citation graph, depth is poison. I want to understand exactly why, because the fix has to attack the actual mechanism, not just bolt a vision trick onto a graph and hope.

So let me write down the layer and stare at it. $\mathbf H^{(\ell+1)}=\sigma(\tilde{\mathbf P}\mathbf H^{(\ell)}\mathbf W^{(\ell)})$, with $\tilde{\mathbf P}=\tilde{\mathbf D}^{-1/2}\tilde{\mathbf A}\tilde{\mathbf D}^{-1/2}$ the renormalized symmetric adjacency of the self-looped graph. Strip the weights and nonlinearities for a moment to see the pure propagation: $K$ layers, ignoring $\mathbf W$ and $\sigma$, is just $\tilde{\mathbf P}^{K}\mathbf x$ on a signal. That's a fixed operator raised to the $K$-th power. What does $\tilde{\mathbf P}^{K}$ do as $K$ grows? $\tilde{\mathbf P}$ is a symmetric stochastic-like operator; its top eigenvalue is exactly 1 (the renormalization trick was designed to pin the spectrum into $[-1,1]$ with the top at 1), and every other eigenvalue is strictly less than 1 in magnitude. Raise it to the $K$ and every eigencomponent except the top one decays geometrically; only the eigenvector for $\lambda=1$ survives. So $\tilde{\mathbf P}^{K}\mathbf x$ converges to a fixed vector that has nothing to do with the high-frequency content of $\mathbf x$ — it's a projection onto a single mode.

Let me actually compute that limit, because the *form* of it tells me what's being destroyed. I want $\big((\mathbf I+\tilde{\mathbf D}^{-1/2}\tilde{\mathbf A}\tilde{\mathbf D}^{-1/2})/2\big)^{K}\mathbf x$ — I'll use the lazy version $(\mathbf I+\tilde{\mathbf P})/2$ because it's the natural one once a residual connection is in play and it has the same stationary state with cleaner convergence. The trick is to conjugate it into a random walk. Note $\mathbf I = \tilde{\mathbf D}^{-1/2}\tilde{\mathbf D}^{1/2}$, so
$$\Big(\tfrac{\mathbf I+\tilde{\mathbf P}}{2}\Big)^{K}=\Big(\tilde{\mathbf D}^{-1/2}\,\tfrac{\mathbf I+\tilde{\mathbf A}\tilde{\mathbf D}^{-1}}{2}\,\tilde{\mathbf D}^{1/2}\Big)^{K}=\tilde{\mathbf D}^{-1/2}\Big(\tfrac{\mathbf I+\tilde{\mathbf A}\tilde{\mathbf D}^{-1}}{2}\Big)^{K}\tilde{\mathbf D}^{1/2},$$
because the inner $\tilde{\mathbf D}^{1/2}\tilde{\mathbf D}^{-1/2}$ pairs telescope. The middle factor $(\mathbf I+\tilde{\mathbf A}\tilde{\mathbf D}^{-1})/2$ is precisely the transition matrix of a *lazy random walk* on the self-looped graph — column-stochastic, with probability $1/2$ of staying put. Apply it to a basis vector $\mathbf e_i$ and the $K$-th power is the $K$-step distribution $\mathbf p^{(K)}_i$ of a walk started at $i$. A lazy walk on a connected graph converges to the unique stationary distribution, which for a walk on $\tilde G$ is proportional to the degrees: $\mathbf p^{(K)}_i(j)\to (d_j+1)/(2m+n)$. There's a Cheeger-type bound on how fast — the $j$-th entry sits within $\sqrt{(d_j+1)/(d_i+1)}\,(1-\lambda_{\tilde G}^2/2)^{K}$ of the stationary value, where $\lambda_{\tilde G}$ is the spectral gap of the self-looped graph's normalized Laplacian.

Now expand $\tilde{\mathbf D}^{1/2}\mathbf x=\sum_i x(i)\sqrt{d_i+1}\,\mathbf e_i$, push the walk through, and pull $\tilde{\mathbf D}^{-1/2}$ back out. Entry $j$ of the result is
$$\mathbf h^{(K)}(j)=\sum_i \sqrt{d_i+1}\,x(i)\,\frac{1}{\sqrt{d_j+1}}\Big(\frac{d_j+1}{2m+n}\pm\sqrt{\tfrac{d_j+1}{d_i+1}}\big(1-\tfrac{\lambda_{\tilde G}^2}{2}\big)^{K}\Big)=\sum_i \frac{\sqrt{(d_j+1)(d_i+1)}}{2m+n}x(i)\ \pm\ \Big(\sum_i x_i\Big)\big(1-\tfrac{\lambda_{\tilde G}^2}{2}\big)^{K}.$$
So in the limit $\mathbf h^{(K)}\to\boldsymbol\pi=\frac{\langle\tilde{\mathbf D}^{1/2}\mathbf 1,\mathbf x\rangle}{2m+n}\tilde{\mathbf D}^{1/2}\mathbf 1$. Look hard at that vector. It is proportional to $\tilde{\mathbf D}^{1/2}\mathbf 1$ — every node's value is just $\sqrt{d_j+1}$ times one global scalar, the inner product of the features with the degree-weighted all-ones vector. After enough layers, the *only* things a node's representation knows are its own degree and a single number summarizing the whole graph's features. Two nodes with the same degree are *identical*, regardless of what they actually are. That is over-smoothing, derived rather than asserted: the deep network throws away exactly the per-node feature information I need to classify with.

And the per-node rate is the part I'll come back to. If I factor the last display as
$$\mathbf h^{(K)}(j)=\sqrt{d_j+1}\left(\sum_i\frac{\sqrt{d_i+1}}{2m+n}x(i)\ \pm\ \frac{\sum_i x_i}{\sqrt{d_j+1}}\big(1-\tfrac{\lambda_{\tilde G}^2}{2}\big)^K\right),$$
then the perturbation inside the parentheses is divided by $\sqrt{d_j+1}$ while the stationary value itself carries the outside $\sqrt{d_j+1}$. So the relative distance to the stationary state shrinks faster for high-degree nodes. High-degree nodes over-smooth first. (Worth a check on the data later: bin nodes by degree and watch which lose accuracy first as I deepen.)

So the disease is clear: stacking the fixed operator is a contraction toward a degree-only fixed point. Now, what's the standard cure for "deep networks degrade"? ResNet. A residual connection: instead of replacing the signal, learn a correction added to it, $\mathbf H^{(\ell+1)}=\sigma((\tilde{\mathbf P}\mathbf H^{(\ell)}+\mathbf H^{(\ell)})\mathbf W^{(\ell)})$. Let me see what that buys. The propagation part becomes $(\mathbf I+\tilde{\mathbf P})\mathbf H^{(\ell)}$ — exactly the lazy walk I just analyzed (up to the $\tfrac12$, which the weights absorb). But a lazy walk *still converges to the same stationary distribution*; laziness only slows the mixing, it doesn't change the fixed point. So a residual to the previous layer just makes the contraction slower — it postpones over-smoothing, it doesn't prevent it. That matches what I see: residual GCNs go a bit deeper before collapsing, then collapse anyway, and a 2-layer plain GCN still beats them. The skip-to-previous-layer is to the wrong target, because the previous layer is *itself already partly smoothed*. Adding it back only feeds smoothed signal into more smoothing.

That reframes the question precisely. The problem isn't "add a skip"; it's *what do I skip back to*. The previous layer is contaminated. The one representation that is guaranteed un-smoothed is the *input* — layer zero, the freshly transformed features, before any propagation touched them. So skip to *that*. At every layer, mix the smoothed representation with a fixed fraction of the original input:
$$\mathbf H^{(\ell+1)}=\big((1-\alpha)\,\tilde{\mathbf P}\mathbf H^{(\ell)}+\alpha\,\mathbf H^{(0)}\big),$$
where $\mathbf H^{(0)}$ is a learned map of the raw features. Now think about the limit. Each layer re-injects an $\alpha$-fraction of $\mathbf H^{(0)}$, so no matter how deep I go, the representation is *anchored* to the input and can never collapse to the pure degree vector. In fact unroll it: $\mathbf H^{(K)}=\sum_{\ell}(1-\alpha)^{\ell}\alpha\,\tilde{\mathbf P}^{\ell}\mathbf H^{(0)}+\dots$, which is a geometric mixture of all hop-distances of the input — and as $K\to\infty$ that converges not to the degree vector but to $\alpha(\mathbf I-(1-\alpha)\tilde{\mathbf P})^{-1}\mathbf H^{(0)}$, the personalized-PageRank diffusion of the input. A fixed point that *carries the features*, not one that erases them. This is the teleport idea: a random surfer that with probability $\alpha$ jumps back to where it started keeps locality forever. So the initial residual, alone, fixes the over-smoothing of the *propagation*. $\alpha$ around $0.1$ — small enough that propagation dominates and the receptive field genuinely widens, large enough that the input anchor never vanishes.

But here's the catch, and it's the reason this isn't the end. The update I just wrote is *linear* in $\mathbf H^{(\ell)}$ — there's no weight matrix and no nonlinearity per layer. If I want the expressive power of a *deep nonlinear* network — the thing that makes depth pay on images — I need a learnable $\mathbf W^{(\ell)}$ and a $\sigma$ at each layer. The obvious move is to put them back: $\mathbf H^{(\ell+1)}=\sigma\big(((1-\alpha)\tilde{\mathbf P}\mathbf H^{(\ell)}+\alpha\mathbf H^{(0)})\mathbf W^{(\ell)}\big)$. And the moment I do, depth starts hurting again — but now for a *different* reason than over-smoothing. It's the weight matrices.

Why would the weights hurt? Two things converge on the same answer. First, there's a result on the rate of subspace collapse: a $K$-layer stack drives features into a low-dimensional subspace at a rate controlled by $s^{K}$, where $s$ is the largest singular value of the per-layer weight matrices. If the effective transforms become contractions, $s^{K}$ decays and the feature space collapses; if they become expansive, the product is unstable. The only stable target for a deep product is to keep the useful singular values near 1. Second, there's the small-data reality: $20$ labels per class is almost nothing, and $K$ free $H\times H$ weight matrices is a lot of parameters to fit from that — they overfit, and in semi-supervised graphs the specific failure mode is *too much interaction between feature dimensions*, the dense remixing that a full $\mathbf W$ performs every layer. So I want each layer's weight matrix to be *close to the identity* — both to keep $s\approx1$ (no collapse, no blowup) and to do only a gentle, regularizable amount of feature mixing.

How do I make a learnable weight matrix that defaults to identity? Take the ResNet lesson literally at the level of the matrix itself: write the layer's effective transform as $(1-\beta)\mathbf I+\beta\mathbf W^{(\ell)}$. When $\beta\to0$ this is the identity — the layer ignores its weights entirely and just propagates-with-teleport, i.e. it falls back to the linear PageRank diffusion that I already proved doesn't over-smooth. When $\beta$ is moderate, the layer does a $\beta$-sized amount of learned feature transformation on top. So
$$\mathbf H^{(\ell+1)}=\sigma\Big(\big((1-\alpha_\ell)\tilde{\mathbf P}\mathbf H^{(\ell)}+\alpha_\ell\mathbf H^{(0)}\big)\big((1-\beta_\ell)\mathbf I+\beta_\ell\mathbf W^{(\ell)}\big)\Big).$$
There's a clean reason this identity-mapping form is *safe* to regularize hard, beyond hand-waving. For a linear residual network $\mathbf H(\mathbf W+\mathbf I)$ it's known that the optimal weights have small norm and that the only critical point is the global minimum. So pushing $\mathbf W$ toward zero (strong weight decay on $\mathbf W$ specifically) is not fighting the model — the optimum lives there anyway — and it removes the spurious local minima that scarce-label training would otherwise get trapped in. Strong $L_2$ on $\mathbf W$ keeps $\|\mathbf W\|$ small, so $(1-\beta)\mathbf I+\beta\mathbf W$ stays near the identity, so $s\approx1$, so $s^{K}$ stays $O(1)$ and the subspace collapse is defused. The two cures interlock: initial residual stops the *propagation* from smoothing, identity mapping stops the *weights* from collapsing the rank or overfitting.

Now $\beta_\ell$. I don't want a single constant. The deeper a layer sits, the *more* I want it to behave like a pure identity (just propagate, don't transform), because the cumulative product of transforms is what blows the singular values around, and the late layers are where over-transformation does the most damage. So $\beta_\ell$ should *decay* with depth $\ell$. The natural decaying schedule is $\beta_\ell=\log(\frac{\lambda}{\ell}+1)\approx \lambda/\ell$ for a single hyperparameter $\lambda$: layer 1 gets the most transformation budget, and by layer 30 the weight is almost fully identity. $\lambda$ around $0.5$ sets the overall scale. This way the *transformation* decays adaptively as I stack more layers while the *propagation-with-anchor* keeps going — exactly the asymmetry I want.

Let me make sure this actually restores the thing over-smoothing destroyed: the ability to be a flexible filter. The deep-GCN pathology was that $K$ layers realize a *fixed* polynomial filter $\tilde{\mathbf P}^{K}\mathbf x$ — predetermined coefficients, no freedom. Can this new layer realize an *arbitrary* order-$K$ polynomial in the operator? Take the weak linearized version of my layer to test the coefficient mechanism: fix $\alpha_\ell=\tfrac12$, absorb the harmless factor $\tfrac12$ into a scalar $\gamma_\ell$, keep the activation in a region where ReLU acts as the identity, and let the effective weight be $\gamma_\ell\mathbf I$. The recurrence I need to analyze is
$$\mathbf H^{(\ell+1)}=\gamma_\ell(\tilde{\mathbf P}\mathbf H^{(\ell)}+\mathbf x)=\gamma_\ell((\mathbf I-\tilde{\mathbf L})\mathbf H^{(\ell)}+\mathbf x),$$
with $\mathbf H^{(0)}=\mathbf 0$ and $\mathbf x$ the fixed initial representation being re-injected. Now the unroll is exact:
$$\mathbf H^{(K)}=\Big(\sum_{\ell=0}^{K-1}\Big(\textstyle\prod_{r=K-\ell-1}^{K-1}\gamma_r\Big)(\mathbf I-\tilde{\mathbf L})^{\ell}\Big)\mathbf x.$$
On the other side, an arbitrary order-$K$ filter $\big(\sum_k\theta_k\tilde{\mathbf L}^{k}\big)\mathbf x$ re-expands in powers of $(\mathbf I-\tilde{\mathbf L})$ by the binomial theorem, $\tilde{\mathbf L}=\mathbf I-(\mathbf I-\tilde{\mathbf L})$:
$$\Big(\sum_{k}\theta_k\tilde{\mathbf L}^{k}\Big)\mathbf x=\Big(\sum_{\ell}\Big(\sum_{k\ge\ell}\theta_k(-1)^{\ell}\tbinom{k}{\ell}\Big)(\mathbf I-\tilde{\mathbf L})^{\ell}\Big)\mathbf x.$$
Match coefficients of $(\mathbf I-\tilde{\mathbf L})^{\ell}$ between the two: I need
$$\prod_{r=K-\ell-1}^{K-1}\gamma_r=\sum_{k=\ell}^{K-1}\theta_k(-1)^{\ell}\binom{k}{\ell}\quad\text{for every }\ell=0,\ldots,K-1.$$
The left side is a *partial product* of the $\gamma$'s, so I can solve telescopically. Let $c_\ell=\sum_{k=\ell}^{K-1}\theta_k(-1)^\ell\binom{k}{\ell}$. Then $\gamma_{K-1}=c_0=\sum_k\theta_k$, and for $\ell\ge1$ I set $\gamma_{K-\ell-1}=c_\ell/c_{\ell-1}$. If a denominator is exactly zero, that coefficient in the target expansion is exactly zero, so the exact ratio is singular; an arbitrarily small perturbation of the coefficients gives an arbitrarily close filter. So there *exists* a setting of the per-layer scalars realizing any nondegenerate order-$K$ filter coefficients, with the degenerate cases reachable by approximation. The free $\beta_\ell$/$\mathbf W^{(\ell)}$ are exactly what supply the per-hop coefficients that the fixed GCN couldn't. Arbitrary coefficients mean I can choose ones whose limit *keeps* the input — e.g. the geometric $\theta'_i=\alpha(1-\alpha)^i$ that converges to the PageRank vector, a function of both the graph and the features — instead of being forced into the degree-only collapse. That's the formal statement of why depth is now a resource: the network can *learn* not to over-smooth, where before the coefficients were nailed down for it.

One more sanity check from a different direction, because I always trust a derivation more when two stories land on the same structure. Consider recovering a sparse code: $\min_x \tfrac12\|\mathbf B\mathbf x-\mathbf y\|^2+\lambda\|\mathbf x\|_1$, with $\mathbf y$ the observed node feature and $\mathbf x$ the embedding to recover, $\mathbf B$ a learned dictionary. The iterative shrinkage step is $\mathbf x^{t+1}=P_{\mu\lambda}\big(\mathbf x^t-\mu\mathbf B^\top\mathbf B\mathbf x^t+\mu\mathbf B^\top\mathbf y\big)$. Reparameterize $-\mathbf B^\top\mathbf B$ as a learned $\mathbf W$ and it becomes $\mathbf x^{t+1}=P_{\mu\lambda}\big((\mathbf I+\mu\mathbf W)\mathbf x^t+\mu\mathbf B^\top\mathbf y\big)$ — and now I read off the pieces: $(\mathbf I+\mu\mathbf W)$ is exactly my identity-mapped weight, $\mu\mathbf B^\top\mathbf y$ (a fixed function of the observation $\mathbf y$, re-added every step) is exactly my initial residual to $\mathbf H^{(0)}$, and the soft-threshold $P$ plays the role of ReLU. So the layer I derived from spectral over-smoothing arguments is *also* one unrolled step of recovering a sparse representation of the node's own features — the identity mapping and the initial residual aren't two ad-hoc patches, they're the two terms that any such iterative reconstruction must have.

Now the architecture around the layer. I keep a *single fixed hidden width* throughout — the input features get mapped down to $H$ by one fully-connected layer to form $\mathbf H^{(0)}$, then *every* propagation layer is $H\to H$ (so $\mathbf H^{(0)}$ and $\mathbf H^{(\ell)}$ live in the same space and the initial residual is a legal add), and a final fully-connected layer maps $H$ to the class logits. Crucially the per-layer weights are $H\times H$, *not* feature-sized, so the parameter count is $\text{(input FC)}+\text{(output FC)}+L\cdot H^2$ — it grows only linearly in depth with a small constant, which is what makes "go deep" affordable here. Dropout before each propagation layer and before the output FC; ReLU as $\sigma$; and the weight decay split so that the $\mathbf W^{(\ell)}$ inside the convolution layers get *strong* regularization (the small-norm-optimum argument licenses it) while the input/output FC layers get the usual mild decay.

Let me consolidate $\beta_\ell$ into the layer so the code is exact. Inside one layer, with $\theta_\ell\equiv\beta_\ell=\log(\lambda/\ell+1)$: compute the smoothed message $\mathbf h_i=\tilde{\mathbf P}\mathbf H^{(\ell)}$, form the teleported support $\mathbf s=(1-\alpha)\mathbf h_i+\alpha\mathbf H^{(0)}$, then apply the identity-mapped weight, which expands as $\mathbf s\big((1-\theta)\mathbf I+\theta\mathbf W\big)=(1-\theta)\mathbf s+\theta\,\mathbf s\mathbf W$ — a $\theta$-weighted blend of the untransformed support and its $\mathbf W$-transform — and ReLU. That blend form is the cheap, numerically clean way to write the identity mapping. The normalized sparse adjacency $\tilde{\mathbf P}$ is prepared once from the self-looped graph, so the layer itself is exactly a sparse matrix multiply followed by the support blend.

```python
import math

import torch
import torch.nn as nn
import torch.nn.functional as F
from torch.nn.parameter import Parameter


class GraphConvolution(nn.Module):
    """One layer fills the derived update; `adj` is the precomputed P̃ sparse matrix."""

    def __init__(self, channels):
        super().__init__()
        self.weight = Parameter(torch.FloatTensor(channels, channels))   # H x H, depth-independent size
        self.reset_parameters()

    def reset_parameters(self):
        stdv = 1.0 / math.sqrt(self.weight.size(1))
        self.weight.data.uniform_(-stdv, stdv)

    def forward(self, x, adj, h0, lamda, alpha, layer):
        theta = math.log(lamda / layer + 1.0)              # beta_l = log(lambda/l + 1)
        hi = torch.spmm(adj, x)                            # P̃ H^{(l)}
        support = (1 - alpha) * hi + alpha * h0            # initial residual to H^{(0)}
        return theta * torch.mm(support, self.weight) + (1 - theta) * support


class GCNII(nn.Module):
    """Input FC -> L identity-mapped, initial-residual graph conv layers -> output FC."""

    def __init__(self, in_channels, hidden_channels, out_channels,
                 num_layers, dropout, alpha=0.1, lamda=0.5):
        super().__init__()
        self.convs = nn.ModuleList(GraphConvolution(hidden_channels) for _ in range(num_layers))
        self.fcs = nn.ModuleList([
            nn.Linear(in_channels, hidden_channels),
            nn.Linear(hidden_channels, out_channels),
        ])
        self.params1 = list(self.convs.parameters())       # use strong weight decay on these
        self.params2 = list(self.fcs.parameters())         # use ordinary dense-layer decay
        self.act_fn = nn.ReLU()
        self.dropout = dropout
        self.alpha = alpha
        self.lamda = lamda

    def forward(self, x, adj):
        x = F.dropout(x, self.dropout, training=self.training)
        layer_inner = self.act_fn(self.fcs[0](x))           # H^{(0)} = transformed features
        h0 = layer_inner
        for i, conv in enumerate(self.convs):
            layer_inner = F.dropout(layer_inner, self.dropout, training=self.training)
            layer_inner = self.act_fn(
                conv(layer_inner, adj, h0, self.lamda, self.alpha, i + 1)
            )
        layer_inner = F.dropout(layer_inner, self.dropout, training=self.training)
        layer_inner = self.fcs[-1](layer_inner)
        return F.log_softmax(layer_inner, dim=1)
```

The causal chain, end to end: deep graph convolution fails because $K$ layers are the fixed operator $\tilde{\mathbf P}^{K}$, which I showed (via conjugation to a lazy random walk and a Cheeger bound) contracts every signal to the degree-only stationary vector $\boldsymbol\pi=\frac{\langle\tilde{\mathbf D}^{1/2}\mathbf 1,\mathbf x\rangle}{2m+n}\tilde{\mathbf D}^{1/2}\mathbf 1$ — over-smoothing, fastest at high-degree nodes; a ResNet residual to the *previous* layer only slows that lazy walk, never changes its fixed point, so I skip back to the *input* instead, $(1-\alpha)\tilde{\mathbf P}\mathbf H^{(\ell)}+\alpha\mathbf H^{(0)}$, whose limit is the input-carrying PageRank diffusion rather than the degree vector; but restoring per-layer nonlinear weights reintroduces a *second* depth pathology — singular-value-driven subspace collapse and small-label overfitting from too much feature mixing — which I defuse by writing each weight as the identity-mapped $(1-\beta_\ell)\mathbf I+\beta_\ell\mathbf W^{(\ell)}$ with $\beta_\ell=\log(\lambda/\ell+1)$ decaying with depth and strong $L_2$ on $\mathbf W$ (safe because the linear-residual optimum has small norm and a unique global minimum); together they let a $K$-layer model express an *arbitrary* order-$K$ polynomial filter (proved by matching the unrolled $\prod\gamma_k$ coefficients to the binomial re-expansion of $\sum\theta_k\tilde{\mathbf L}^k$) instead of the fixed one — and the same two terms fall out independently as the initial-residual and identity-mapping pieces of one iterative-shrinkage step for sparse feature reconstruction; stacked at fixed hidden width with depth-independent $H\times H$ weights, the model goes genuinely deep at a parameter cost linear in depth.
