Let me set up coordinates and see what the problem is really asking. The shoreline is a straight line; I'll lay it along the $x$-axis. The lifeguard starts at $A$, sitting $30\ \mathrm{m}$ up the beach, so $A=(0,30)$ if I put its foot on the shoreline at the origin. The swimmer is at $B$, $40\ \mathrm{m}$ out into the water on the other side and $100\ \mathrm{m}$ down the shore, so $B=(100,-40)$. On the sand the lifeguard runs at $v_1=5\ \mathrm{m/s}$; in the water she swims at $v_2=2\ \mathrm{m/s}$. She runs straight to some entry point $P=(x,0)$ on the shoreline, then swims straight to $B$. I want the $x$ that minimizes the total time.

The brute-force version is obvious. The total time is
$$T(x)=\frac{\sqrt{x^2+30^2}}{5}+\frac{\sqrt{(100-x)^2+40^2}}{2},$$
and I set $T'(x)=0$. Differentiating,
$$T'(x)=\frac{x}{5\sqrt{x^2+30^2}}-\frac{100-x}{2\sqrt{(100-x)^2+40^2}}=0.$$
I can already see the two fractions are nice geometric objects. The first one, $x/\sqrt{x^2+30^2}$, is the sine of the angle the running segment makes with the vertical — with the normal to the shoreline at $P$. The second, $(100-x)/\sqrt{(100-x)^2+40^2}$, is the sine of the angle the swimming segment makes with that same normal. So if I call those angles $\theta_1$ (sand side) and $\theta_2$ (water side), the stationarity condition is just
$$\frac{\sin\theta_1}{v_1}=\frac{\sin\theta_2}{v_2}.$$
That's clean, and I trust it because it's grounded in the calculus. But the calculus is doing something slightly mysterious: I had a transcendental equation in $x$, and out fell a relation purely between two *angles*, with the actual distances $30$, $40$, $100$ gone from the local condition. The positions of $A$ and $B$ still decide which crossing point can realize those angles, but the rule that must hold at the crossing point itself only talks about slopes and speeds. That smells like there is a more fundamental statement here, one that produces the angle law without ever writing down $T(x)$, and that's the thing I actually want to understand. If I can get the law $\sin\theta_1/v_1=\sin\theta_2/v_2$ from a principle instead of from a derivative, then I'll know *why* the distances drop out of the boundary condition, and I'll have a tool that works when $T(x)$ is too ugly to differentiate.

So let me throw away the calculus and start over from what "least time" actually means physically.

Travelling from $A$ at a speed that depends on which region you're in — that is exactly how a *light ray* behaves in a medium whose refractive index changes across a boundary. Light slows from $c$ to $c/n$; here the lifeguard slows from $v_1$ to $v_2$. And light is not an optimizer that solves $T'(x)=0$ by hand; it just goes. The path light takes has stationary travel time, and in this two-medium crossing that stationary path is the minimum I am looking for. If I believe that the lifeguard's least-time problem and a light ray's behaviour are the same mathematical problem, then I can borrow whatever machinery optics uses to *find* the ray, and that machinery had better hand me the angle law directly.

But I should be careful about *why* light's path is least-time, because that's the part I want to actually reconstruct, not just invoke. The honest mechanism is the wave. A point disturbance spreads out as a spherical wave; in a uniform medium a wavefront — the locus of points the disturbance has reached at a given instant — is a sphere expanding at the local speed. How do you propagate a wavefront forward in time when you don't already know the ray? You treat every point currently on the wavefront as a *new* point source. After a short time $dt$ each such source has emitted its own little spherical wavelet of radius (local speed)$\times dt$. The new wavefront is the surface that is tangent to all those wavelets at once — their envelope. And the ray, the path the energy actually travels, is everywhere perpendicular to the wavefronts, because each secondary wavelet leaves its source radially and the envelope is reached at the point of tangency, which lies along the radius.

That last sentence is the whole engine, so let me make sure I believe the "perpendicular" claim before I lean on it. Take one source point on the old wavefront. Its wavelet after $dt$ is a tiny sphere of radius $v\,dt$ centred on it. The envelope touches this sphere at exactly one point, and the radius drawn to a tangent point is perpendicular to the tangent surface — that's just a circle's geometry. So the displacement of the wavefront, from each source to the corresponding point of the new wavefront, is along the local radius, i.e. perpendicular to the wavefront, and has length $v\,dt$. Good: the wavefront advances *normal to itself* at the local speed. The ray is that normal direction. This is the thing that's going to let me avoid calculus, because now I can talk about how fast the wavefront moves without solving any minimization.

Let me also reassure myself that this envelope construction reproduces something I can check, before I aim it at the boundary. Forget two regions for a second; take a single point source $S$ moving in a straight line through one uniform medium, faster than the waves it makes — source speed $v$, wave speed $c<v$. At a sequence of past instants it sat at points along its track and emitted circular wavelets; the one emitted when the source was a distance $d$ behind its current position $O$ has had time $d/v$ to grow, so it has radius $c\cdot d/v=(c/v)d$. Every such circle has radius proportional to the distance of its centre from $O$, so a straight line through $O$ is tangent to all of them simultaneously — the envelope is a straight cone with apex at $O$. The half-angle $\beta$ of that cone, measured at the apex between the track and the cone surface, satisfies $\sin\beta=\dfrac{c\,d/v}{d}=\dfrac{c}{v}$: the radius of a wavelet over the distance of its source from the apex. A sine equal to (wave speed)/(some other speed). That's exactly the shape of the law I'm chasing, and it came out of the envelope-of-circles picture with no calculus at all. So the construction is trustworthy and it *natively speaks in sines of speed ratios*. Now I bring it to the boundary.

Picture a wavefront sweeping down through region 1 toward the shoreline, the lifeguard's "reachable in time $t$" frontier expanding at $v_1$. At some instant a stretch of this wavefront reaches the boundary. The boundary is where the local speed drops from $v_1$ to $v_2$. What happens to the wavefront as it crosses? On the region-2 side, each point of the boundary that the wavefront has already touched becomes a fresh Huygens source radiating *into the water* — but now its wavelets grow at the slower speed $v_2$. So region 2 gets its own wavefront, built as the envelope of these slower wavelets emitted from the boundary. The geometry of how the fast wavefront strikes the boundary sets the timing of when each boundary source switches on, and that timing plus the slow growth rate fixes the tilt of the region-2 envelope. That tilt is the refracted ray direction. Let me make it quantitative.

Here is the quantity that ties the two sides together: the speed at which the *intersection of the wavefront with the boundary* travels along the boundary. Call it $u$, the trace speed. It has to be one and the same number viewed from either region, because it's literally the motion of a single geometric point — the place where the wavefront currently meets the shoreline — and that point doesn't get to be in two places. This is the continuity I was missing in the calculus; here it's forced by the picture.

Now compute $u$ from the region-1 side. The incoming wavefront in region 1 makes some angle with the boundary. Let $\theta_1$ be the angle between the ray (the wavefront normal) and the normal to the boundary; then the wavefront itself makes angle $\theta_1$ with the boundary line. In a time $dt$ the wavefront advances $v_1\,dt$ perpendicular to itself. I want how far its footprint slides along the boundary in that same $dt$. Drop the little right triangle: the wavefront moved forward by $v_1\,dt$ along its normal, and the point where it meets the boundary slid forward by some $u\,dt$ along the boundary; the perpendicular advance is the boundary slide times $\sin\theta_1$, because the wavefront is inclined to the boundary by $\theta_1$. So $v_1\,dt=(u\,dt)\sin\theta_1$, giving
$$u=\frac{v_1}{\sin\theta_1}.$$
Let me sanity-check the limiting cases of this little formula. If the ray hits the boundary head-on, along the normal, then $\theta_1\to0$ and the wavefront is nearly parallel to the boundary; locally the whole boundary is reached at nearly the same instant, so the intersection trace races along it and $u\to\infty$. If instead the ray is nearly grazing the boundary, then $\theta_1\to90^\circ$ and the wavefront is nearly perpendicular to the boundary; its intersection slides along the boundary at the actual wavefront speed, $u\to v_1$. Both limits match $u=v_1/\sin\theta_1$. Good, the trace can exceed $v_1$; it's not a physical signal speed, just the sweep of a geometric intersection, so there's nothing wrong with it being large.

The region-2 wavefront is built from sources sitting on that same boundary, switching on in the same sequence and at the same along-boundary rate $u$ — because they're switched on by the very wavefront whose footprint moves at $u$. By identical geometry on the water side, with the refracted wavefront inclined to the boundary at angle $\theta_2$ and advancing at $v_2$,
$$u=\frac{v_2}{\sin\theta_2}.$$

Setting the two expressions for the single trace speed equal,
$$\frac{v_1}{\sin\theta_1}=\frac{v_2}{\sin\theta_2}\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad \frac{\sin\theta_1}{v_1}=\frac{\sin\theta_2}{v_2}\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad \frac{\sin\theta_1}{\sin\theta_2}=\frac{v_1}{v_2}.$$
There it is — the refraction law, with no $T(x)$ and no derivative, falling straight out of the demand that the wavefront stay continuous across the boundary. And now I see exactly why the distances $30$, $40$, $100$ evaporated from the calculus condition: the law is a statement about the wavefront's slope at the crossing point, a purely *local* matching of trace speeds. The global positions of $A$ and $B$ still choose the point where that local matching can happen. The optimizer was rediscovering, the hard way, a continuity condition the wave satisfies automatically.

This also tells me the qualitative shape of the answer before I touch the numbers. Since $v_2<v_1$, I need $\sin\theta_2<\sin\theta_1$, so $\theta_2<\theta_1$: the path bends *toward* the normal on entering the slower water. That matches the intuition that you want to spend less of your travel in the slow region, so you cut more steeply across the boundary to shorten the swim — the law just makes "less time in the slow medium" quantitative.

Now apply it to my lifeguard. With the foot of $A$ at the origin and the crossing point at $P=(x,0)$, the running segment goes from $(0,30)$ to $(x,0)$, and its angle from the (vertical) normal has
$$\sin\theta_1=\frac{x}{\sqrt{x^2+30^2}}.$$
The swimming segment goes from $(x,0)$ to $(100,-40)$, so
$$\sin\theta_2=\frac{100-x}{\sqrt{(100-x)^2+40^2}}.$$
The law $\sin\theta_1/\sin\theta_2=v_1/v_2=5/2$ becomes
$$\frac{x}{\sqrt{x^2+900}}=\frac{5}{2}\cdot\frac{100-x}{\sqrt{(100-x)^2+1600}}.$$
Before solving, a feasibility check on the angle relation itself says $\sin\theta_1\le1$ forces $\tfrac52\sin\theta_2\le1$, i.e. $\sin\theta_2\le0.4$. So the swimmer must be approached at a fairly steep angle to the normal — under about $23.6^\circ$. Here $B$ is far down the beach ($100\ \mathrm{m}$ across versus $40\ \mathrm{m}$ out), so I expect a fairly large $x$, close to under $B$, with a steep entry — consistent with $\sin\theta_2$ ending up small.

This is one equation in one unknown and it's monotonic in the right way — the left side increases in $x$ and the right side decreases in $x$ — so there's a unique crossing. Squaring and clearing would give a quartic, but there's no need to fight it by hand; I'll locate the root numerically. Let me just feel out where it is. Take $x=80$: left $=80/\sqrt{7300}=0.936$; right $=2.5\cdot20/\sqrt{2000}=2.5\cdot0.447=1.118$ — left is smaller, so I need a larger $x$. Take $x=90$: left $=90/\sqrt{9000}=0.949$; right $=2.5\cdot10/\sqrt{1700}=2.5\cdot0.243=0.606$ — now left overshoots, so the root is between. Take $x=84$: left $=84/\sqrt{7956}=0.9417$; right $=2.5\cdot16/\sqrt{1856}=2.5\cdot0.3714=0.9285$ — left a hair high, root just under $84$. Tightening gives $x\approx83.7\ \mathrm{m}$.

At that crossing point, $\sin\theta_1=83.74/\sqrt{83.74^2+900}=0.9414$ and $\sin\theta_2=16.26/\sqrt{16.26^2+1600}=0.3766$, and indeed $0.9414/0.3766=2.500=v_1/v_2$, so the refraction law holds exactly, as it must. The angles are $\theta_1=\arcsin0.9414\approx70.3^\circ$ on the sand and $\theta_2=\arcsin0.3766\approx22.1^\circ$ in the water — the path bends toward the normal in the slow water, as predicted. The travel time is
$$T=\frac{\sqrt{83.74^2+900}}{5}+\frac{\sqrt{16.26^2+1600}}{2}=\frac{88.95}{5}+\frac{43.18}{2}\approx17.79+21.59\approx39.4\ \mathrm{s}.$$
For contrast, the geometrically straight dash from $A$ to $B$ would cross the boundary at $x=100\cdot\frac{30}{30+40}\approx42.9\ \mathrm{m}$ and take about $45.3\ \mathrm{s}$; the refracted path beats it by nearly six seconds, exactly because it trades a longer run on the fast sand for a shorter swim in the slow water — which is the trade the refraction law balances.

So the whole thing collapses to one idea: don't minimize the travel time as a function of the crossing point — propagate a wavefront. Each point of the wavefront is a Huygens source; the envelope of its wavelets is the next wavefront; the ray is the wavefront's normal; and the single condition that the wavefront's intersection with the boundary moves at one shared trace speed $u=v_1/\sin\theta_1=v_2/\sin\theta_2$ is the refraction law $\sin\theta_1/v_1=\sin\theta_2/v_2$ outright. Reading the two sines off the lifeguard's geometry and solving the resulting one-variable equation puts the entry point at $x\approx83.7\ \mathrm{m}$ and the least time at about $39.4\ \mathrm{s}$.
