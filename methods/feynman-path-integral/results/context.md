# Context: quantizing the action — what was on the table

## Research question

Quantum mechanics, as it stands, is built entirely on the *Hamiltonian* side of classical
mechanics. The dynamical variables are canonical coordinates and momenta `(q, p)`; the
bridge to the quantum theory is the commutator `[q, p] = iℏ`; time evolution is generated by
the Hamiltonian operator `Ĥ` through Schrödinger's equation `iℏ ∂ψ/∂t = Ĥψ` or, equivalently,
through Heisenberg's matrix equations. But classical mechanics has a *second*, in several
respects deeper, formulation — the *Lagrangian* one, organized around the action
`S = ∫ L(q, q̇) dt` and the principle that the realized motion makes `S` stationary, `δS = 0`.
This second formulation has no counterpart in the quantum theory.

The question is: **what plays the role, in quantum mechanics, of the classical Lagrangian and
of the action `S`?** Concretely — is there a way to *state* quantum mechanics directly in terms
of the action functional `S[x(t)]` along trajectories, rather than in terms of operators and the
Hamiltonian? The action principle packages all the equations of motion into one scalar
statement and is manifestly Lorentz-invariant, whereas the Hamiltonian formalism singles out a
time axis. There are also physical systems — an electrodynamics formulated as direct action-at-
a-distance between charges, with no independent field degrees of freedom and hence no
Hamiltonian — that one can only write down as an *action*; for such a system the standard
recipe "write the Hamiltonian, promote to operators" has nothing to act on.

## Background

**The two equivalent formulations of quantum mechanics.** Modern quantum mechanics began
with two dissimilar mathematical languages: Schrödinger's wave-mechanical differential
equation and Heisenberg's matrix algebra. They were proved mathematically equivalent and
were synthesized in Dirac's *transformation theory*, which represents a state by amplitudes in
a chosen basis and connects two bases (say one diagonal in coordinates `q`, one diagonal in
momenta or in the coordinates `Q` at another time) by a *transformation function* `⟨q'|Q'⟩`.
Born and Jordan, and von Neumann, supplied the statistical interpretation: the amplitude's
squared modulus is a probability, and amplitudes for *indistinguishable* alternatives are added
(then squared), whereas amplitudes for *distinguished* alternatives have their probabilities
added. This superposition-of-amplitudes rule — interference — is the operative content of
quantum mechanics.

**The kernel / propagator.** Within transformation theory there is a quantity that carries the
wave function forward in time: a kernel `K(x', t+ε; x, t)` such that
`ψ(x', t+ε) = ∫ K(x', x) ψ(x, t) dx`. It is equivalent to the differential equation but is an
integral object — it composes by `K(t₂, t₀) = ∫ K(t₂, t₁) K(t₁, t₀) dq₁`, integrating over the
intermediate coordinate.

**The classical action and least action.** In Lagrangian mechanics `S[q(t)] = ∫ L dt` and the
classical path extremizes it: `δS = 0` gives the Euler–Lagrange equations. Hamilton's principal
function — the action evaluated along the classical path as a function of its endpoints —
generates the canonical transformation linking initial and final `(q, p)`. The *phase* of a
semiclassical wave is known to track this principal function. So there is already, classically, a
tight link between `S` and the generator of time evolution.

**Dirac's 1933 observation.** Dirac, in *The Lagrangian in
Quantum Mechanics* (Phys. Z. Sowjetunion **3**, 64, 1933), asked exactly the research
question above and got remarkably far. He argued that the classical generating function `S` of
a contact transformation has, as its quantum analogue, the *phase* of the transformation
function: writing `⟨q'|Q'⟩ = e^{iU/ℏ}`, the operator relations `p_r = ∂U/∂q_r`,
`P_r = −∂U/∂Q_r` reproduce the classical `p_r = ∂S/∂q_r`, `P_r = −∂S/∂Q_r`, so `U` is the
quantum image of `S`. Applied to time evolution he concluded that the finite-time
transformation function `⟨q_t|q_T⟩` **"corresponds to"** `exp[ (i/ℏ) ∫_T^t L dt ]`, and
infinitesimally `⟨q_{t+dt}|q_t⟩` **"corresponds to"** `exp[ (i/ℏ) L dt ]`. Using the composition
law `⟨q_t|q_T⟩ = ∫ ⟨q_t|q_m⟩ dq_m ⟨q_m|q_{m−1}⟩ … dq_1 ⟨q_1|q_T⟩`, he noted that the
integrand is `e^{iF/ℏ}` with `F` finite as `ℏ → 0`; the rapid oscillation kills the integral except
where `F` is *stationary* in the intermediate `q`'s, and `F`'s classical analogue is the sum of the
slice-actions `∑∫L dt`. Thus the *classical least-action principle is recovered as the `ℏ → 0`
limit* of the quantum composition. Dirac stated these as *correspondences* — writing
"corresponds to" / "analogous" rather than "equals" — without fixing a constant of
proportionality relating `⟨q_{t+dt}|q_t⟩` to `e^{iLdt/ℏ}`.

## Baselines

These are the prior formulations of quantum mechanics on the table.

- **Schrödinger wave mechanics (1926).** State = complex wave function `ψ(x, t)`; dynamics
  `iℏ ∂ψ/∂t = [−(ℏ²/2m)∇² + V]ψ`. Computes everything via a differential equation in
  configuration space, built on the Hamiltonian `Ĥ`.

- **Heisenberg matrix mechanics (1925) / Dirac–Jordan transformation theory.** State =
  vector; observables = non-commuting matrices/operators with `[q, p] = iℏ`; evolution by
  Heisenberg's equations or by the transformation function `⟨q'|Q'⟩` between bases.

- **Dirac's 1933 Lagrangian-in-QM proposal.** Brings the Lagrangian and `S` into quantum
  mechanics and recovers least action as `ℏ → 0` (see Background), at the level of a
  correspondence `⟨q_{t+dt}|q_t⟩` *corresponds to* `e^{iLdt/ℏ}`.

- **Wentzel–Kramers–Brillouin (WKB) / semiclassical wave.** Writes `ψ ≈ A e^{iS_cl/ℏ}` with
  `S_cl` the classical action, valid when `ℏ` is small compared with the action scale, tied to a
  single classical path and its neighborhood. It places `S/ℏ` in the phase of the wave.

## Evaluation settings

The natural yardsticks are analytical, not benchmark datasets:

- **Recovery of Schrödinger's equation.** For a non-relativistic particle of mass `m` in a
  potential `V(x)` in one dimension (and its `k`-dimensional generalization), the construction
  must reproduce `iℏ ∂ψ/∂t = [−(ℏ²/2m)∂²_x + V]ψ` exactly, in the limit of vanishing time
  slice. The Lagrangian `L = ½ m ẋ² − V(x)` (quadratic in the velocity) is the test case,
  because that is the class for which Schrödinger's equation has been experimentally verified.

- **Recovery of the classical limit.** As `ℏ → 0` the construction must reduce to the
  least-action principle `δS = 0` — the realized trajectory is the stationary-action (classical)
  path.

- **Consistency / normalization.** Probabilities must be conserved: an event that is certain has
  total probability one, which fixes the measure/normalization on the space of paths. The
  exactly solvable references are the free particle and the harmonic oscillator (quadratic
  Lagrangians), where the integrals can be done in closed form.

- **Internal consistency of the operator correspondence.** The momentum and energy read off
  from the new formulation must match the standard `p = −iℏ∂_x`, `E = iℏ∂_t`, and the
  formalism's relation to matrix/operator algebra must be reproducible.
