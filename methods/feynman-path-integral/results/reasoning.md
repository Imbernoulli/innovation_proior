I keep coming back to the same itch, and I want to follow it all the way down this time. Quantum mechanics, the way I have it in my hands, is entirely a Hamiltonian theory. Everything is `(q, p)`, commutators `[q,p] = iℏ`, and a Hamiltonian operator `Ĥ` grinding the wave function forward through `iℏ ∂ψ/∂t = Ĥψ`. That is fine when there *is* a Hamiltonian. But the thing I actually want to quantize is an electrodynamics written as direct action between charges — half-advanced, half-retarded, with a delay built into the interaction. That has an *action*, a perfectly good `S = ∫ L dt`, but no Hamiltonian I can write down, because the interaction reaches across time and there is no honest instantaneous energy function to promote to an operator. So the standard recipe — write `Ĥ`, impose commutators — has nothing to grab. I am stuck unless I can find a way to do quantum mechanics that *starts from the action* and never needs a Hamiltonian at all.

So the real question is sharper than "is there a Lagrangian version of quantum mechanics?" It is: can I make the action functional `S[x(t)]` itself the object that computes amplitudes? Classically the Lagrangian side is in some ways the *more* fundamental one — it collects every equation of motion into the single scalar statement `δS = 0`, and `S` is a relativistic invariant, whereas the Hamiltonian picks out a time axis the moment you write it. If the quantum theory only ever imported the Hamiltonian half, then there is a whole half of mechanics with no quantum image, and the half I'm missing is exactly the half I need.

Let me first get straight what quantum mechanics is really *saying*, operationally, before I reach for any machinery. The one law that is genuinely quantum, the thing that is *not* classical, is how alternatives combine. Suppose I have three successive measurements `A`, `B`, `C` — and to be concrete let them all be position measurements at successive instants. Classically, if `A` gives `a` and `C` gives `c`, the system passed through *some* definite intermediate value `b`, and the probabilities multiply and the intermediate sums: `P_ac = ∑_b P_ab P_bc`. That is the classical law, and it is exactly the law of ordinary statistics — go from `a` to some `b`, then from `b` to `c`. In quantum mechanics that is *false* whenever I don't actually look at `B`. What's true instead is that there are complex *amplitudes* `φ_ab`, `φ_bc`, with `P_ab = |φ_ab|²`, and the *amplitudes* compose: `φ_ac = ∑_b φ_ab φ_bc`, and only *then* do I square, `P_ac = |φ_ac|²`. The probability law `P_ac = ∑_b P_ab P_bc` is what I get only if I *measure* `B` and destroy the interference. If I don't, the cross terms survive — that is the entire wave nature of matter, sitting in one equation.

Stare at the structure of `φ_ac = ∑_b φ_ab φ_bc`. Two operations. Along a *single* history `a → b → c` the amplitude is a *product* `φ_ab φ_bc`. Across the *different possible* intermediate values `b`, the amplitudes *add*. Multiply along a history, add over histories. And there is nothing special about three measurements — string `K` of them together, `a, b, c, d, …, k` at times `t₁, t₂, …` spaced by some interval `ε`, and the amplitude for one whole specified history is the product `φ_ab φ_bc φ_cd ⋯`, while if I leave the intermediate ones unmeasured I sum each over all its values. Now the successive position values `x₁, x₂, x₃, …` at times `t_i = t_{i-1} + ε` — from a classical eye, that sequence is just a *path* `x(t)`, sampled. So the quantum law is telling me something I should take literally: the amplitude to go from start to finish is a *sum over sampled paths*, where each sampled path contributes a *product* of single-step amplitudes. The probability that the path lies in some region `R` of space-time is `|φ(R)|²` with `φ(R)` a sum of one contribution per path through `R`. If I let the spacing `ε → 0`, "sampled path" becomes "path," and the sum becomes an integral over all paths. That much is forced on me by the superposition rule alone; I haven't put in any new physics yet. The new physics will be *what the contribution of a single path is*.

So write the postulate I've arrived at: an ideal measurement of whether the particle's path lies in a region gives a probability equal to `|φ(R)|²`, and `φ(R)` is a sum of complex contributions, one per path, all of *equal weight in magnitude* — `φ(R) = lim_{ε→0} ∫_R Φ[path] ⋯ dx_{i+1} dx_i ⋯`, integrating over the coordinates `x_i` at the intermediate times. Why equal magnitude? Because if the magnitudes varied from path to path I would need a *second* principle to set them, and I have no such principle and no need of one — let every path count the same in size, and let *only the phase* distinguish them. If that fails to reproduce known physics I'll revisit it, but it is the economical guess and it puts all the discriminating power into the phase, where interference lives.

Which leaves the real question: what is the phase of a single path's contribution? `Φ[x(t)] = (magnitude) · e^{iθ[x(t)]}`, and I need `θ`. I'd been turning this over with no handle on it — what *functional* of a whole trajectory could the phase be? — when Jehle, at the beer party, said Dirac had a paper where at least the Lagrangian comes into quantum mechanics. Let me chase that, because a Lagrangian is a functional of a trajectory, which is exactly the shape of object I'm missing.

Here is what Dirac does. He starts from the same complaint — quantum mechanics took over the Hamiltonian apparatus wholesale because canonical `(q,p)` had a clean quantum analogue, but the Lagrangian, which is the more fundamental and the relativistic one, got left out. He can't take the Lagrangian *equations* over directly — they involve `∂L/∂q`, `∂L/∂q̇`, and there is no sensible partial differentiation with respect to quantum dynamical variables (the only differentiation you can do is forming Poisson brackets, which lands you back in the Hamiltonian theory). So he takes over the *ideas*, not the equations. Classically a contact transformation between `(q,p)` and `(Q,P)` is generated by a function `S`: `p_r = ∂S/∂q_r`, `P_r = −∂S/∂Q_r`. Quantum-mechanically there is a transformation function `⟨q'|Q'⟩` between the representation diagonal in `q` and the one diagonal in `Q`. Dirac shows — writing `⟨q'|Q'⟩ = e^{iU/ℏ}` and using `⟨q'|p_r|Q'⟩ = −iℏ ∂/∂q'_r ⟨q'|Q'⟩` — that this forces `p_r = ∂U/∂q_r` and `P_r = −∂U/∂Q_r` as operator relations. Those are the *same shape* as the classical generating-function equations. So `U`, the phase of the transformation function times `ℏ`, is the quantum analogue of the classical generator `S`.

Now apply that to time. The values `(q_t, p_t)` at time `t` are connected to `(q_T, p_T)` at an earlier `T` by a contact transformation whose generator is the time-integral of the Lagrangian over `[T, t]`. So Dirac concludes the transformation function `⟨q_t|q_T⟩` **corresponds to** `exp[(i/ℏ) ∫_T^t L dt]`, and infinitesimally `⟨q_{t+dt}|q_t⟩` **corresponds to** `exp[(i/ℏ) L dt]`.

There it is — `exp[(i/ℏ) ∫ L dt]`. The phase functional I was missing is the *action over `ℏ`*. And it has exactly the right shape: it is `e^{i(\text{functional of the whole trajectory})/ℏ}`. The action goes in the exponent, divided by `ℏ` to make it dimensionless, and the imaginary `i` makes every path a pure phase of equal magnitude — which is precisely the "equal magnitude, phase carries everything" postulate I'd already been pushed toward. Two independent roads meet here.

But I read Dirac's word again and it nags me. *Corresponds to.* *Analogous.* Not *equals.* I asked Jehle, what does "analogous" mean, what is the *use* of it? He told me Europeans don't always demand a use — but I want to know whether these two things are actually the same number or just structurally similar. I said, surely Dirac means they're equal. No, Jehle said, he doesn't mean equal. Fine — let me just *try setting them equal* and see what falls out. If it's nonsense I'll have learned what "corresponds to" had to mean instead.

Take the simplest case, one particle in 1-D, `L = ½ m ẋ² − V(x)`. Posit a short-time kernel that carries `ψ` from `t` to `t+ε`:
`ψ(x', t+ε) = ∫ K(x', x) ψ(x, t) dx`,
and *set* `K(x', x) = (1/A) · exp[(i/ℏ) S(x', x)]`, where `S(x', x)` is the classical action of the slice and `A` is some constant I'll have to fix — because Dirac only ever gave a *correspondence*, he never told me the constant of proportionality, so I have to carry an unknown `A` and let the demand "reproduce quantum mechanics" pin it. For one short slice the action is, to leading order, the Lagrangian times `ε` with `ẋ ≈ (x' − x)/ε`:
`S(x', x) = [ (m/2)( (x'−x)/ε )² − V(x') ] · ε`.

Cleaner to put the new point at `x` and integrate over the old point: let the old point be `x − ξ`, so `ξ = x − x_{old}` and `(x − x_{old})/ε = ξ/ε`. Then
`ψ(x, t+ε) = (1/A) ∫ exp[ (i/ℏ)( (m/2)(ξ/ε)² ε − ε V(x) ) ] ψ(x − ξ, t) dξ`
`           = (1/A) ∫ exp( i m ξ² / 2ℏε ) · exp( − i ε V(x)/ℏ ) · ψ(x − ξ, t) dξ.`

Now *look* at that first exponential, `exp(i m ξ² / 2ℏε)`. As `ε → 0` it oscillates insanely fast in `ξ` — the phase `m ξ²/2ℏε` runs through many cycles unless `ξ` is tiny, of order `(ℏε/m)^{1/2}`. So for small `ε`, only `ξ ≈ 0` survives; everywhere else the rapid oscillation cancels itself. That means I only need `ψ(x − ξ, t)` for small `ξ`, so I Taylor-expand:
`ψ(x − ξ, t) = ψ(x,t) − ξ ∂ψ/∂x + (ξ²/2) ∂²ψ/∂x² − ⋯`.
And `exp(−iεV/ℏ) = 1 − (iε/ℏ)V + ⋯`, which I keep only to first order in `ε`.

Now I need the Gaussian moments of that oscillatory weight. These are Fresnel integrals; let me actually do them. With `a = m/(2ℏε)`,
`∫_{−∞}^{∞} exp(i a ξ²) dξ = √(π/(−i a)) = √(iπ/a) = (2π ℏ ε i / m)^{1/2}`.
The odd moment vanishes by symmetry, `∫ ξ exp(i a ξ²) dξ = 0`. The second moment is
`∫ ξ² exp(i a ξ²) dξ = (1/(−2 i a)) ∫ exp(i a ξ²) dξ = (i ℏ ε/m)·(2π ℏ ε i/m)^{1/2}`,
so the *ratio* `⟨ξ²⟩ = (i ℏ ε/m)` — order `ε`, exactly as expected since `ξ ∼ ε^{1/2}`.

Assemble, keeping everything to first order in `ε`. The `1 − iεV/ℏ` multiplies the bracket; the `−ξ ∂ψ/∂x` term integrates against `∫ξ → 0` and drops; the surviving pieces are the `ψ` term and the `½ξ² ∂²ψ` term:
`ψ(x, t+ε) = (1/A)·(2π ℏ ε i/m)^{1/2} · ( 1 − iεV/ℏ ) · [ ψ + (1/2)(iℏε/m) ∂²ψ/∂x² + ⋯ ].`

Match orders in `ε`. At *zeroth* order, the left side is just `ψ(x,t)`, so I need the prefactor `(1/A)(2πℏεi/m)^{1/2} = 1`. **That fixes the constant** — `A = (2π ℏ ε i / m)^{1/2}`. So `A` is *not* a free dial; the identity limit of the short-time evolution *forces* it, including the `1/√ε` divergence (the measure has to blow up like `ε^{-1/2}` per slice) and the phase `√i = e^{iπ/4}`. Good — that is what "I had to put a constant of proportionality in, suitably adjusted" was really about: the constant is *determined*.

With `A` fixed, the prefactor is `1`, and to first order in `ε`:
`ψ + ε ∂ψ/∂t = ( 1 − iεV/ℏ )( ψ + (iℏε/2m) ∂²ψ/∂x² )`
`            = ψ + (iℏε/2m) ∂²ψ/∂x² − (iε/ℏ) V ψ + O(ε²).`
Cancel `ψ` from both sides, divide by `ε`:
`∂ψ/∂t = (iℏ/2m) ∂²ψ/∂x² − (i/ℏ) V ψ.`
Multiply through by `−ℏ/i = iℏ`:
`iℏ ∂ψ/∂t = − (ℏ²/2m) ∂²ψ/∂x² + V ψ.`

That is Schrödinger's equation. Out it came, on its own, from setting Dirac's "corresponds to" to an honest equality and Taylor-expanding. So Dirac didn't quite mean "equal" and didn't quite mean only "analogous" — he meant *proportional*, with a definite constant `(2πℏεi/m)^{1/2}` that he had not fixed, and once I fix it the infinitesimal kernel *is* Schrödinger's equation. The equivalence to ordinary quantum mechanics isn't an analogy any more; I derived it.

Now the gap that's still open: I've connected the Lagrangian to quantum mechanics only across an *infinitesimal* time, with a wave function. I want the amplitude across a *finite* interval. So iterate. To get `ψ` at `t + ε` I convolved one factor `(1/A) e^{iS(x',x)/ℏ}` against `ψ(t)`. To get `ψ` at `t + 2ε`, convolve another such factor against *that*. And `t + 3ε`, another. Doing this `N` times, the kernel from `x₀` at `t₀` to `x_N` at `t_N = t₀ + Nε` is a stack of integrals over every intermediate position `x₁, x₂, …, x_{N−1}`:
`K(x_N, x_0) = lim_{ε→0} ∫ ⋯ ∫ ∏_{i=0}^{N−1} (1/A) exp[ (i/ℏ) S(x_{i+1}, x_i) ] dx_1 dx_2 ⋯ dx_{N−1}.`
Now look at the integrand. It is a *product* of exponentials, which is the exponential of a *sum*:
`∏ exp[(i/ℏ) S(x_{i+1}, x_i)] = exp[ (i/ℏ) ∑_i S(x_{i+1}, x_i) ].`
And `∑_i S(x_{i+1}, x_i)` is a sum of slice-actions, each `≈ L · ε`. As `ε → 0` that is precisely a Riemann sum for the integral — `∑_i L_i ε → ∫ L dt = S[x(t)]`, the action of the *whole* path. So the finite-time amplitude is
`K(x_N, x_0) = lim_{ε→0} (1/A^N) ∫ ⋯ ∫ exp[ (i/ℏ) S[x(t)] ] dx_1 ⋯ dx_{N−1}.`

I've represented quantum mechanics directly in terms of the action. The amplitude to go from one space-time point to another is a sum over *every path* connecting them, each path contributing `e^{iS/ℏ}` — equal in magnitude, differing only in phase — with the measure `∏ dx_i / A` and `A = (2πℏεi/m)^{1/2}` per slice. Multiply the single-step amplitudes *along* a path (that's why the exponent is a sum, the action of the whole path); add the contributions *across* paths (that's the integral over intermediate positions). The two operations I read off the superposition law at the very start are exactly the "along the path / across paths" structure, and the phase is the classical action. The contribution of a single path `x(t)` is `Φ[x(t)] = const · exp[(i/ℏ) S[x(t)]]`, with `S = ∫ L dt` the classical action, and the total amplitude is the sum over all paths.

Let me make sure the rest of ordinary quantum mechanics is really inside this, not just the equation. Why does the thing I built up, `ψ(x, t)`, deserve to be called *the* wave function? Because the action sum factorizes at any chosen time. The Lagrangian couples only neighbouring instants — `S = ∑_i S(x_{i+1}, x_i)`, each term touching only `x_i` and `x_{i+1}`. So if I pick a time `t = t_k` and split the exponent into the part with indices `≥ k` and the part with indices `< k`, the whole multi-integral factors at `x_k`:
`φ(R', R'') = ∫ χ*(x, t) ψ(x, t) dx,`
where `ψ(x_k, t) = lim_{ε→0} ∫_{R'} exp[(i/ℏ) ∑_{i<k} S(x_{i+1}, x_i)] dx_{k−1}/A ⋯` collects everything from the *past* region `R'` up to `x_k`, and `χ*` collects everything *after*. This `ψ` depends only on the past and on `t` — it carries *all* the information from the history that is needed to predict the future, and nothing about what will be done later. That is exactly the defining property of a state / wave function. Two preparations that yield the same `ψ` are indistinguishable by any future measurement, since `φ = ∫ χ* ψ dx` only sees `ψ`. So `ψ` *is* the state, the same `ψ` as Schrödinger's — and I already showed it obeys his equation. The new formulation contains the old one whole.

Now I have to be honest about subtleties, because there are two factors I waved at that bite back if I'm careless. First: which paths actually dominate? The important `ξ` per slice is of order `(ℏε/m)^{1/2}`, so the typical "velocity" `ξ/ε ∼ (ℏ/mε)^{1/2}`, which *diverges* as `ε → 0`. The paths carrying the amplitude are continuous but *nowhere differentiable* — jagged, Brownian-like, not the smooth trajectories of classical intuition. That's not a bug; it's why the kinetic phase `m ξ²/2ℏε` is order one (since `ξ² ∼ ε`) and survives the limit. But it does mean I must compute the slice action `S(x_{i+1}, x_i)` to high enough accuracy, because `(x_{i+1} − x_i)` is of order `ε^{1/2}`, not `ε`. For a plain potential `V(x)` it doesn't matter whether I evaluate `V` at the start, end, or midpoint of the slice — those differ in the exponent by `ε·(x_{i+1}−x_i) ∼ ε^{3/2}`, negligible at the order I keep. But for a physical magnetic term `(e/c) A(x)·ẋ`, the action contribution is `(e/c)A·(x_{i+1}−x_i)`, and evaluating `A` at the endpoint rather than the midpoint differs by a term proportional to `(x_{i+1}−x_i)^2 ∼ ε` — first order, *not* negligible. The endpoint rule then gives a Hamiltonian differing from the midpoint, gauge-covariant one by `(ℏ e/2imc)∇·A`. So for the magnetic case I must use the *midpoint* rule, `S = ε L((x_{i+1}−x_i)/ε, (x_{i+1}+x_i)/2)`, not the endpoint rule. The jaggedness of the dominant paths is exactly what forces that care.

Second: the classical limit, which had better be sitting inside this for free, and it is — it was Dirac's original observation and now it's exact. In `∫ e^{iS[x]/ℏ} 𝒟x`, take `ℏ → 0`. The phase `S/ℏ` becomes enormous, so as I vary the path the contribution spins through many cycles and neighbouring paths cancel — *except* near a path where `S` is *stationary*, `δS = 0`, because there the phase is locally flat and neighbouring paths add in step. So as `ℏ → 0` the sum over all paths collapses onto the single path of stationary action — the classical path, the least-action trajectory. The classical principle `δS = 0` is the stationary-phase limit of the quantum sum over histories. Quantum mechanically, *all* paths contribute; the classical path is just the one around which they stop cancelling when `ℏ` is small. Paths within `ΔS ∼ ℏ` of the stationary one interfere constructively — that band *is* the fuzz of quantum mechanics around the classical trajectory.

One loose thread I should flag to myself: the clean Schrödinger derivation above leans on `L` being *quadratic in the velocity* and *local in time*, so the slice action is `(m/2)(ξ/ε)²ε − εV` and the Gaussian integrals close. If the action has a genuine *delay* — `L` depending on the trajectory at two different times — there is no single-time wave function to propagate; I cannot say "given the amplitude everywhere at time `t`, compute it at `t+ε`." Then the natural object is the amplitude for a whole source-to-detector experiment, `∫ e^{iS/ℏ} 𝒟x` between fixed endpoints, with no intermediate state. That is exactly why I needed an action-based formulation in the first place. But for the non-relativistic particle in a potential — the case that has to match known physics — everything closes exactly.

The amplitude (kernel/propagator) to go from `(x_a, t_a)` to `(x_b, t_b)` is

```
K(x_b, t_b ; x_a, t_a)
   = ∫  exp[ (i/ℏ) S[x(t)] ]  𝒟x(t)          # sum over ALL paths x(t) with x(t_a)=x_a, x(t_b)=x_b
   = lim_{N→∞}  (1/A^N) ∫···∫ exp[ (i/ℏ) Σ_{i=0}^{N-1} S(x_{i+1}, x_i) ]  dx_1 ··· dx_{N-1},

   with   ε = (t_b - t_a)/N,
          A = (2π ℏ ε i / m)^{1/2}          # i.e. each slice carries (m / 2π i ℏ ε)^{1/2}
         S(x_{i+1}, x_i) = ε [ (m/2)((x_{i+1}-x_i)/ε)² − V(x_{i+1}) ]
              # scalar V may be moved to another endpoint at this order; use the midpoint for velocity-linear terms
```

each path weighted equally in magnitude and by the phase `e^{iS/ℏ}`; as `ℏ→0`, stationary phase picks out `δS=0`, the classical path; and the one-slice version, expanded to first order in `ε`, is

```

          ⇒  iℏ ∂ψ/∂t = −(ℏ²/2m) ∂²ψ/∂x² + V(x) ψ ,
```
