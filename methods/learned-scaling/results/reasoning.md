Let me start from the thing that keeps biting me: depth should be free, and it isn't. The expressive power of a network grows roughly exponentially in its depth ŌĆö a few dozen layers can already carve the input space into astronomically many regions ŌĆö and the obvious way to get more capacity is to stack more layers. A deeper network can always represent everything a shallower one can, just by setting the extra layers to the identity. So adding layers to a model that already works should, at worst, leave it where it was. Yet that is emphatically not what happens. Past a few dozen layers the optimizer stalls. A deeper *plain* network reaches *higher* training error than a shallower one ŌĆö not a generalization gap, an optimization failure: the solution that copies the shallow net and idles the rest is representationally available, and gradient descent cannot find it. So the obstruction isn't representational. It's something about how gradients move through depth.

Let me make the mechanism quantitative. Picture a deep stack of width-preserving maps, `x_{i+1} = F[W_i](x_i)`. Perturb the input by a little, `╬┤x_0`, and ask how big the perturbation is at the output. Each layer is a function, so to first order it multiplies the perturbation by its Jacobian; across `L` layers the perturbation is multiplied by the product of `L` Jacobians. If, on average, one layer rescales a perturbation by a factor `r`, then after `L` layers the rescaling is `r^L`. And the backward pass is governed by the *same* product of Jacobians, transposed ŌĆö that's just the chain rule ŌĆö so the gradient that reaches the early layers is scaled by the same `r^L`. Now `r^L` is a brutal function of `L`. If `r` is even slightly above 1, gradients blow up exponentially in depth; if it's slightly below 1, they vanish exponentially. The only survivable regime is `r Ōēł 1` held across *every* layer, so that nothing compounds. That's the needle I have to thread: keep the per-layer signal-and-gradient gain pinned at one, at every layer, for arbitrary `L`.

So how does the field hold `r` near one today, and where does each approach leak? The cleanest framing I have is the signal-propagation picture from the mean-field analyses of random deep nets. Take two distinct inputs, push both through the random network, and track the cosine of the angle between them at depth `i`: `x_i ┬Ę x'_i / (||x_i|| ||x'_i||)`. This cosine flows to a fixed point as `i` grows. If the fixed point is 1, the two signals become parallel ŌĆö *every* input is squashed toward the same output direction, so the output stops distinguishing inputs and the gradient that depends on that distinction vanishes; that's the ordered, stable phase. If the fixed point is 0, arbitrarily close inputs are flung to orthogonal outputs, tiny differences get amplified without bound, gradients explode; that's the chaotic phase. Trainability is the boundary between them, the edge of chaos ŌĆö exactly the `r Ōēł 1` condition restated in terms of how pairs of signals evolve.

To diagnose which side I'm on, look at the input-output Jacobian directly, `J_io ŌēĪ Ōłéx_L / Ōłéx_0`. Its singular values are the gains of the network along the principal input directions. The mean squared singular value, call it `Žć`, is the average gain; `Žć Ōēł 1` is the edge of chaos, the average perturbation neither grows nor shrinks. That's the condition the old Glorot and He initializations were really targeting when they rescaled the weight variances. But I want to check whether `Žć Ōēł 1` on average is actually sufficient, because the whole acceleration story rides on it. Suppose the singular values average to one but the spectrum is spread ŌĆö concretely, take a `J_io` with singular values `10^4` and `10^{-4}`. The mean squared singular value is `(10^8 + 10^{-8})/2 Ōēł 5├Ś10^7`, so that example doesn't even have `Žć = 1`; to make `Žć = 1` I'd need the values balanced as, say, `ŌłÜ2` and `ŌłÜ2┬Ę10^{-8}` so the mean square is `(2 + 2┬Ę10^{-16})/2 Ōēł 1`. Now a data perturbation aligned with the first singular vector is amplified by `ŌłÜ2`, fine, but one aligned with the second is attenuated by a factor `~10^{-8}` ŌĆö that direction's contribution to the loss gradient is multiplied by `10^{-8}` and is effectively dead, even though `Žć` reads exactly one. So `Žć Ōēł 1` genuinely does not protect every direction; the mean hides the spread. The condition I actually need is stronger: *all* the singular values of `J_io` close to one, not just their mean. That's dynamical isometry ŌĆö every input perturbation propagates through the whole network with gain Ōēł 1, none amplified, none suppressed. If I could arrange that, the effective `r` along *every* direction is one, so `r^L = 1` for all of them, and depth would stop compounding. The open question is whether anything cheap can actually deliver it.

Now, can I get dynamical isometry by initialization? For some activations, yes ŌĆö there are orthogonal-init schemes that engineer it. But there's a wall I can't init my way around: with a ReLU nonlinearity, dynamical isometry is *impossible*. ReLU maps a half-space to zero, so a perturbation that pushes a pre-activation from positive into the killed region produces zero output change ŌĆö a zero singular value, structurally, no matter how I pick the weights. And it gets worse for the architecture I actually care about, the Transformer, where two of the core components are each individually hostile to isometry. Take LayerNorm: it subtracts the mean and divides by the standard deviation of its input, `LayerNorm(x) = (x ŌłÆ E[x])/ŌłÜVar(x) ┬Ę ╬│ + ╬▓`. By construction, if I perturb `x` in a way that only shifts its mean, the subtraction erases it ŌĆö the output doesn't move at all; likewise a perturbation that only rescales the variance gets normalized away. Each of those invariances is a direction with zero gain, a zero singular value of the layer's Jacobian. Applied independently across all `n` tokens of the sequence, that's `2n` guaranteed-zero singular values per Transformer layer. And self-attention has its own collapse mode: `softmax(QK^T/ŌłÜd)┬ĘV`. In the small-score regime the softmax row is nearly uniform, every entry Ōēł `1/n`, so attention just averages all `n` value vectors into essentially one shared vector. That averaging collapses an `n├Śd` set of directions down to about `d` surviving ones; of the `n├Śd` Jacobian singular values, only `d` are nonzero, and the rest of the input signal is simply gone. A residual connection bolted around it claws some back, but unevenly ŌĆö some directions amplified, others attenuated, which is precisely the spread that breaks isometry. So if I stack these, the input-output Jacobian of a deep Transformer at init has a huge fraction of its singular values decayed to machine precision. No wonder deep Transformers are a nightmare to train; the signal is gone before it reaches the loss.

So how does each existing fix survive this, and where does it actually leak? The original move was the residual connection: `x_{i+1} = Žā(x_i + F(x_i))`. The intuition is lovely ŌĆö if the optimal thing for a block to do is close to nothing, it's easier to drive `F` toward zero than to fit the identity out of a nonlinear stack ŌĆö and it made hundreds of conv layers trainable. But stare at initialization. `F` is initialized with full-strength weights, so at step zero the block is `x + F(x)` with `F(x)` not small. The block is *not* the identity at init; the residual branch is shouting from the very first forward pass, and the output variance of a residual block still compounds with depth at init ŌĆö roughly a constant factor per layer, so something like `2^L` over `L` layers if each block adds order-one variance. The shortcut tames the *worst* of `r^L`, but it doesn't set `r = 1` at init; it just makes `r` less catastrophic. The identity-at-init that dynamical isometry actually wants is left on the table.

Highway Networks went further with learned gates: `x_{i+1} = C(x)┬Ęx_i + T(x)┬ĘF(x_i)`, where `T = Žā(W_T^T x + b_T)` and `C = 1 ŌłÆ T`, and you bias `b_T` negative so the network leans toward carrying the signal. That does expose the right kind of knob ŌĆö how much of `F` should enter the stream ŌĆö but the Highway gate is a full function of `x`, with its own weights and compute, and a finite negative bias only leans toward carrying; it does not make the block an exact identity. The gated-ResNet simplification strips the gate down to a single scalar by setting `W_T = 0`, `b_T = ╬▒`, and `C = 1 ŌłÆ T`, so it is cheaper and closer to the object I want. But the sigmoid transform/carry split is still the wrong constraint: to get exact identity I would need `T = 0` and `C = 1` at initialization, and a finite scalar through a sigmoid only approaches that, never reaches it. There's the zero-`╬│` trick: if a residual branch ends in a normalization layer with a learnable scale `╬│`, zero-init that `╬│` and the branch outputs zero at step zero, so the block *does* start as the identity. That's the right initialization idea ŌĆö but it only works when such a norm layer exists, and it zero-inits a whole *vector* of `╬│`'s, one per channel, rather than a single knob; it's welded to normalization. And FixUp comes at it top-down: demand that one SGD step change the network *function* by `╬ś(╬Ę)`, independent of depth; its analysis shows a standard-init residual net's logits blow up at init, and the cure is a coordinated bundle ŌĆö zero-init the last layer of each branch, rescale the branch weights by `L^{ŌłÆ1/(2mŌłÆ2)}` where `m` is the branch depth, add a per-branch scalar multiplier initialized at *one* and scalar biases at zero. It works without normalization, but it's an elaborate, architecture-aware recipe; the rescaling exponent depends on how many layers each branch has, and crucially its scalar multiplier starts at one, not at a value that makes the block an identity.

So I line them all up and ask what they have in common. Every one of these either (a) does *not* make the residual block the identity at initialization ŌĆö ResNet, Highway, Gated ResNet, FixUp's multiplier-at-one ŌĆö so the signal is already mangled at step zero and the optimizer has to climb out of a badly conditioned hole; or (b) achieves identity-at-init but only in an architecture-specific, multi-piece, normalization-tied way ŌĆö zero-`╬│`. And separately, the whole apparatus of learning-rate warm-up exists for the same underlying reason: the original Post-LN Transformer has large parameter gradients near the output at init, so a full learning rate immediately destabilizes it, so you ramp the rate up slowly to survive the early steps. Warm-up is a band-aid over bad init-time signal propagation. That negative pattern is suggestive. The dynamical-isometry condition I derived above wants every singular value of `J_io` near one, and `J_io = I` would give that exactly. So the property worth chasing might be: make the block be *exactly* the identity at initialization ŌĆö not approximately, not after a careful per-layer recipe, but exactly. That would be powerful for a specific reason: if the *block* is the identity, I don't need `F` itself to be isometric at all. The composition `x_{i+1} = x_i` has `J_io = I`, every singular value is exactly 1, dynamical isometry holds trivially, and it holds no matter what `F` would have done ŌĆö even ReLU and self-attention, whose own Jacobians have vanishing singular values, contribute nothing at init because at init `F` doesn't get to act. That's the property I couldn't get by initialization alone earlier: identity-at-the-block sidesteps the ReLU impossibility entirely, because it never asks `F` to be isometric.

How do I make `x + F(x)` collapse to `x` at init without throwing `F` away? The cheapest knob I can imagine is a single scalar multiplying the residual branch, with the branch and the shortcut otherwise untouched:

  x_{i+1} = x_i + ╬▒_i F(x_i),   with ╬▒_i = 0 at initialization.

At init every `╬▒_i = 0`, so `x_{i+1} = x_i` exactly, every block is the identity, `J_io = I`, dynamical isometry is exact ŌĆö and this is indifferent to what `F` is, because `╬▒ = 0` annihilates `F`'s entire output, vanishing singular values and all. One learnable scalar for each residual layer. Compare it against the schemes I just surveyed: it's not data-dependent like Highway's gate; it's not a vector like zero-`╬│`; it's not a depth-dependent rescaling like FixUp. It is strictly less machinery than any of them while still hitting identity-at-init exactly. And `╬▒` is trainable, so the network is not stuck at the identity forever ŌĆö gradient descent can grow the `╬▒_i`'s to whatever the task needs.

But I need to convince myself this actually *trains*, and trains *fast*, not just that it starts in a nice place. The danger with "start at identity" is that the gradients into `F` might be zero forever ŌĆö if `╬▒ = 0` kills `F`'s output, doesn't it also kill `F`'s gradient and freeze the weights? Let me work the smallest model that still shows the `r^L` pathology, so I can see the dynamics exactly. Take `L` single-neuron layers, all sharing one weight `w`, all sharing one residual scalar `╬▒`, no bias. Each layer does `x_{i+1} = x_i + ╬▒ w x_i = (1 + ╬▒w) x_i`, so the whole network is

  x_L = (1 + ╬▒w)^L x_0,

and the input-output Jacobian is just the scalar `J_io = (1 + ╬▒w)^L`. This little model is too simple to have an order/chaos transition, but it captures the vanishing/exploding gradient problem exactly, because `(1+╬▒w)^L` is precisely an `r^L`.

Now compare the two initializations. Fix `╬▒ = 1` ŌĆö that's the vanilla residual, `x_{i+1} = (1+w)x_i`. With `w Ōēł 1`, `J_io = (1+w)^L = 2^L`, the output is exquisitely sensitive: a perturbation of the input is amplified `2^L`-fold. To do gradient descent on `w` without overshooting, my steps in the output have to stay order one, which means the learning rate has to be shrunk by roughly that amplification ŌĆö let me actually get the gradient. With cost `C` evaluated at `x_L`, the update to `w` is, by the chain rule through all `L` factors,

  w ŌåÉ w ŌłÆ ╬╗ L ╬▒ x_0 (1 + ╬▒w)^{LŌłÆ1} Ōłé_x C|_{x = x_L}.

At `╬▒ = 1, w Ōēł 1` that gradient carries a `(1+w)^{LŌłÆ1} = 2^{LŌłÆ1}` factor, so to keep the step bounded I need

  ╬╗ ŌłØ L^{ŌłÆ1} (1 + w)^{ŌłÆ(LŌłÆ1)},

a learning rate *exponentially small in depth*. And it's a knife edge: nudge `w` above 1 and the gradient explodes, nudge `w` toward `ŌłÆ1` and `(1+w)^{LŌłÆ1}` collapses and the gradient vanishes. This is the `r^L` disease in miniature ŌĆö the vanilla residual sits right on top of it.

Now set `╬▒ = 0` instead. First, `J_io = (1 + 0)^L = 1` ŌĆö input signal exactly preserved, as designed. Second, look at the same gradient for `w`: it has an explicit factor of `╬▒` out front, `w ŌåÉ w ŌłÆ ╬╗ L ╬▒ x_0 (1+╬▒w)^{LŌłÆ1} Ōłé_x C`, so at `╬▒ = 0` the gradient on `w` is *zero*. That's the deep stack of weights *not* getting a giant ill-conditioned update on the first step; `F`'s parameters hold still while things are still at the identity, so nothing blows up. But this is exactly the freeze-forever worry from before ŌĆö if `w` gets no gradient, does anything move at all? Compute the gradient on `╬▒` itself. Differentiating `x_L = (1+╬▒w)^L x_0` with respect to `╬▒` gives `Ōłéx_L/Ōłé╬▒ = L w x_0 (1+╬▒w)^{LŌłÆ1}`, so

  ╬▒ ŌåÉ ╬▒ ŌłÆ ╬╗ L w x_0 (1 + ╬▒w)^{LŌłÆ1} Ōłé_x C|_{x = x_L}.

At `╬▒ = 0` this is `╬▒ ŌåÉ ŌłÆ╬╗ L w x_0 Ōłé_x C|_{x=x_0}` ŌĆö finite and nonzero. And notice *where* the cost gradient is evaluated: at `x_L = x_0`, the input itself, a perfectly well-conditioned point, not at some `2^L`-amplified output. So the freeze-forever worry was wrong on a structural point: `╬▒`'s own gradient is alive at `╬▒ = 0` even though `w`'s is dead, because the two gradients differ exactly by which variable carries the explicit prefactor (`╬▒` for the `w`-update, `w` for the `╬▒`-update).

The algebra above only pins down the instant after the first step; the actual claim ŌĆö that the `╬▒ = 0` model trains where the `╬▒ = 1` model can't ŌĆö is about a whole trajectory. So let me run it: `L = 50` shared-`w` neurons, `x_0 = 1`, fit `x_L` to a target `0.5` under plain gradient descent, and watch both inits. With `╬▒ = 1, w = 1` the network starts at `x_L = 2^{50} Ōēł 1.1├Ś10^{15}`, and the first `w`-gradient is `(x_L ŌłÆ 0.5)┬ĘL┬Ęx_0┬Ę2^{49} Ōēł 3├Ś10^{31}`; the step overflows double precision on iteration 1, and it keeps overflowing even when I crank the learning rate all the way down to `10^{-17}` ŌĆö the rate that keeps the step finite is impractically tiny, somewhere past `10^{-30}`, which is the `(1+w)^{ŌłÆ(LŌłÆ1)}` penalty made concrete. With `╬▒ = 0, w = 1` the first `╬▒`-gradient is `(x_0 ŌłÆ 0.5)┬ĘL┬Ęw┬Ęx_0 = 0.5┬Ę50 = 25` ŌĆö five-ish orders of magnitude is an understatement; the two first steps differ by a factor of `~10^{30}`. At `╬╗ = 10^{-3}` the `╬▒ = 0` model converges to `x_L = 0.5` with cost `Ōēł 2├Ś10^{-30}`, with `╬▒` settling around `ŌłÆ0.014` and `w` barely moving from 1, i.e. `1 + ╬▒w` stays right next to one the whole way. So `╬▒` does wake up first on a clean gradient and steer the trajectory through well-conditioned territory, exactly as the algebra predicted, and the vanilla init is not merely slower but numerically impractical at this depth ŌĆö the only rates that avoid overflow are so far below any usable step size that training would never move.

The same experiment also tells me something I'll need later about *how* to train the `╬▒`'s. Pushing the `╬▒ = 0` model to `╬╗ = 10^{-1}` overflows by step 2, and at `╬╗ = 10^{-2}` it overshoots into a bad fixed point (`x_L ŌåÆ 0`, cost `0.125`). So even from the safe init the scalar is a high-leverage knob: it tolerates a small steady rate but punishes an aggressive one. That's a real constraint surfacing from the dynamics, not a stylistic preference. The remaining explicit depth factor in the safe first step is just `L`, so the learning rate can scale polynomially (something like `1/L`) rather than carrying the exponential `(1+w)^{ŌłÆ(LŌłÆ1)}` penalty ŌĆö same model, same expressivity once `╬▒` grows, but the optimization path routed through well-conditioned territory instead of over the cliff.

This also tells me why the initialization has to be *zero* and not just "small," and certainly not one: any nonzero `╬▒` plants the trajectory somewhere on the `(1+╬▒w)^L` surface where the conditioning already depends on `╬▒w`, off the safe identity point ŌĆö `╬▒ = 1` is exactly the vanilla residual I just watched overflow, so that value is no fallback at all. The `J_io = 1` and dead-`w`-gradient facts both hold *only* at `╬▒ = 0` exactly, so zero isn't a heuristic small number; it's the unique value that simultaneously gives exact identity, exact dynamical isometry, and the dead-`w`-gradient-but-live-`╬▒`-gradient structure that let the run above bootstrap gently.

Let me carry this back to the Transformer concretely. A Transformer layer has two sublayers ŌĆö multi-head self-attention and a point-wise feed-forward network ŌĆö and the original wraps each in a residual-plus-LayerNorm, `x_{i+1} = LayerNorm(x_i + sublayer(x_i))`. The object that needs to be controlled is the sublayer contribution before it re-enters the residual stream, so that contribution gets multiplied by a learnable residual weight and added back:

  x_{i+1} = x_i + ╬▒_i ┬Ę sublayer(x_i),   sublayer Ōłł {self-attention, feed-forward},

with `╬▒_i = 0` at init. Do I need a *separate* `╬▒` for attention and for the feed-forward within a layer? I don't think so, and the toy analysis says why: what buys me dynamical isometry at init is that the whole block reduces to the identity, and a single shared `╬▒_i = 0` already zeroes both sublayer contributions at once. One scalar per layer is the minimal object that does the job; splitting it into two would add parameters without changing the init behavior I'm relying on. So I share one `╬▒_i` across the attention and feed-forward sublayers of layer `i`. And what about LayerNorm ŌĆö keep it or drop it? The reason LayerNorm was in there was to control signal scale across depth, which is exactly the job the `╬▒`-gated identity now does at init; and LayerNorm was *contributing* `2n` vanishing singular values per layer, actively fighting the isometry I want. So I remove it. The scalar replaces the normalization rather than supplementing it. And warm-up falls away too ŌĆö warm-up only existed to survive the large init-time gradients of Post-LN, and an identity-initialized block has well-behaved gradients at step zero by construction, so there's nothing to warm up over.

There's a subtlety in *training* the `╬▒`'s that I already saw in the toy run and should pin down before I write code: these scalars sit at a highly leveraged point in the network. Recall that the `╬▒ = 0` model converged fine at `╬╗ = 10^{-3}` but overshot into a bad fixed point at `╬╗ = 10^{-2}` and overflowed at `╬╗ = 10^{-1}`. A single big step on an `╬▒` swings a whole layer's contribution and can destabilize everything downstream, because `╬▒` multiplies the entire residual branch. So the `╬▒`'s want a gentler, smaller, steadier learning rate than the weights; in a setting with an aggressive one-cycle schedule I'd hold the residual-weight learning rate small and constant rather than letting it ride the peak.

What should the `╬▒_i`'s do over training? They start at zero, and the first-step analysis says they grow ŌĆö I'd expect them to rise and bring layers online while staying modest; at depth `L`, if many layers share the work and the cumulative effect stays order one, a natural scale to watch is `O(1/L)`. The other diagnostic is the singular-value histogram of `J_io`: if this design does what the derivation says, those singular values should stay concentrated near one rather than collapsing toward zero or spreading wildly.

So the encoder layer becomes: the sublayers stay ordinary ŌĆö multi-head self-attention, then a point-wise feed-forward network ŌĆö the normalization layers disappear, and a single zero-initialized `resweight` lives on the layer, multiplying both the attention output and the feed-forward output before each is added back to the residual stream.
