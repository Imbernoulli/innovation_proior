\begin{abstract}
Chain-of-thought prompting combined with pre-trained large language models has achieved encouraging results on complex reasoning tasks. In this paper, we propose a new decoding strategy, \emph{self-consistency}, to replace the naive greedy decoding used in chain-of-thought prompting. It first samples a diverse set of reasoning paths instead of only taking the greedy one, and then selects the most consistent answer by marginalizing out the sampled reasoning paths. Self-consistency leverages the intuition that a complex reasoning problem typically admits multiple different ways of thinking leading to its unique correct answer.  Our extensive empirical evaluation shows that self-consistency boosts the performance of chain-of-thought prompting with a striking margin on a range of popular arithmetic and commonsense reasoning benchmarks, including GSM8K (+17.9\%), SVAMP (+11.0\%), AQuA (+12.2\%), StrategyQA (+6.4\%) and ARC-challenge (+3.9\%).

\section{Introduction}

Although language models have demonstrated remarkable success across
a range of NLP tasks, %the ability to do reasoning is often seen as a limitation of language models 
their ability to demonstrate reasoning is often seen as a limitation,
which cannot be overcome solely by increasing model scale \citep[][\textit{inter alia}]{rae2021scaling,bigbench}.
In an effort to address this shortcoming, \citet{wei2022chain} have proposed \textit{chain-of-thought prompting}, where a language model is prompted to generate a series of short sentences that mimic the reasoning process
a person might employ in solving a task.
For example, given the question \textit{``If there are 3 cars in the parking lot and 2 more cars arrive, how many cars are in the parking lot?''}, instead of directly responding with \textit{``5''},
a language model would be prompted to respond with the entire chain-of-thought: \textit{``There are 3 cars in the parking lot already. 2 more arrive. Now there are 3 + 2 = 5 cars. The answer is 5.''}. 
It has been observed that chain-of-thought prompting significantly improves model performance across a variety of multi-step reasoning tasks \citep{wei2022chain}.


In this paper, we introduce a novel decoding strategy called \textit{self-consistency} to replace the greedy decoding strategy used in chain-of-thought prompting \citep{wei2022chain}, that further improves language models' reasoning performance by a significant margin.
Self-consistency leverages the intuition that complex reasoning tasks typically admit multiple reasoning paths that reach a correct answer \citep{stanovich2000individual}. 
The more that deliberate thinking and analysis is required for a problem \citep{evans2010intuition}, the greater the diversity of reasoning paths that can recover the answer.

\cref{fig:overview} illustrates the self-consistency method with an example.
We first prompt the language model with chain-of-thought prompting, 
then instead of greedily decoding the optimal reasoning path, we propose a ``sample-and-marginalize'' decoding procedure: we first \textit{sample} from the language model's decoder to generate a \textit{diverse} set of reasoning paths; each reasoning path might lead to a different final answer, so we determine the optimal answer by \textit{marginalizing out} the sampled reasoning paths to find the most consistent answer in the final answer set.
Such an approach is analogous to the human experience that if multiple different ways of thinking lead to the same answer, one has greater confidence that the final answer is correct.
Compared to other decoding methods, self-consistency avoids the repetitiveness and local-optimality that plague greedy decoding, while mitigating the stochasticity of a single  sampled generation.
% One can think of this process as introducing an additional latent variable $r_j$ representing the ``rationales'' used to reach each final answer $a_i$. 
% We couple the generation of $r_j, a_i$ where $r_j \rightarrow a_i$ and utilize the stochasticity in $r_j$ to improve the accuracy on $a_i$, by sampling from the decoder for all possible $r_j$'s and marginalizing it out in the end.

Self-consistency is far simpler than prior approaches that either train an additional verifier \citep{cobbe2021training} or train a re-ranker given additional human annotations to improve generation quality \citep{thoppilan2022lamda}.
Instead, self-consistency is entirely \textit{unsupervised}, works off-the-shelf with pre-trained language models, requires no additional human annotation, and avoids any additional training, auxiliary models or fine-tuning.
% Compared to popular generate-then re-rank approaches \citep{cobbe2021training,thoppilan2022lamda}, self-consistency does not require a specially-trained re-ranker.
Self-consistency also differs from a typical ensemble approach where multiple models are trained and the outputs from each model are aggregated, it acts more like a ``self-ensemble'' that works on top of a \textit{single} language model.
% We empirically evaluate self-consistency on a range of arithmetic and commonsense reasoning tasks, finding that it improves performance over generating a single chain of thought by a surprising amount.
% Self-consistency improves performance over a single generation by X\% on average over 5 arithmetic reasoning datasets and by Y\% on average over 4 commonsense reasoning datasets.
% Notably, performance on the challenging GSM8K and SVAMP datasets improves from 17\% to 25\%, and from 39\% to 52\%, respectively.

We evaluate self-consistency on a wide range of arithmetic and commonsense reasoning tasks over four language models with varying scales: the public UL2-20B \citep{ul2} and GPT-3-175B \citep{brown2020language}, and two densely-activated decoder-only language models: LaMDA-137B \citep{thoppilan2022lamda} and PaLM-540B \citep{palm}.
On all four language models, self-consistency improves over chain-of-thought prompting by a striking margin across all tasks.
% Specifically, on LaMDA-137B, self-consistency achieves +10.6\% on \citep[GSM8K;][]{cobbe2021training}, +14.4\% on \citep[SVAMP;][]{patel-etal-2021-nlp}, and +23.9\% on MultiArith \citep{roy-roth-2015-solving}; 
% for commonsense reasoning, we also observe significant gains in CommonsenseQA \citep{talmor-etal-2019-commonsenseqa} (+5\%), and +4\% and +4.7\% on the AI2 Reasoning Challenge (ARC) dataset \citep{Clark2018ThinkYH}.
% Similarly, on PaLM-540B, self-consistency achieves substantial gains over greedy decoding across many tasks: SVAMP (+7.6\%), AQuA (+12.5\%), GSM8K (+17.9\%), StrategyQA (+6.3\%), ARC-challenge (+3.5\%), and we are able to achieve the new state-of-the-art performance across almost all tasks.
In particular, when used with PaLM-540B or GPT-3, self-consistency achieves new state-of-the-art levels of performance across arithmetic reasoning tasks, including GSM8K \citep{cobbe2021training} (+17.9\% absolute accuracy gains), SVAMP \citep{patel-etal-2021-nlp} (+11.0\%), AQuA \citep{ling-etal-2017-program} (+12.2\%), and across commonsense reasoning tasks such as StrategyQA \citep{geva-etal-2021-aristotle} (+6.4\%) and  ARC-challenge \citep{Clark2018ThinkYH} (+3.9\%).
In additional experiments, we show self-consistency can robustly boost performance on NLP tasks where adding a chain-of-thought might hurt performance compared to standard prompting \citep{ye2022unreliability}.
We also show self-consistency significantly outperforms sample-and-rank, beam search, ensemble-based approaches, and is robust to sampling strategies and imperfect prompts.

\section{Self-Consistency over Diverse Reasoning Paths}

A salient aspect of humanity is that people think differently.
It is natural to suppose that in tasks requiring deliberate thinking, there are likely 
several ways to attack the problem.
% , all of which lead to the same, correct answer.
% When humans solve a reasoning task that requires deliberate thinking, since different people think differently, for any given problem there likely exist many sets of reasoning processes that lead to the same final solution.
% If some of the reasoning chains lead to the same answer, then a human would be more confident that this answer is indeed correct.
% The goal of chain of thought prompting is to allow models to produce a chain of thought that mimics the reasoning process a person may have when responding to a question. 
We propose that such a process can be simulated in language models via sampling from the language model's decoder. 
For instance, as shown in \cref{fig:overview}, a model can generate several plausible responses to a math question that all arrive at the same correct answer (Outputs 1 and 3).
Since language models are not perfect reasoners, the model might also produce an incorrect reasoning path or make a mistake in one of the reasoning steps (e.g., in Output 2), but such solutions are less likely to arrive at the \textit{same} answer.
That is, we hypothesize that correct reasoning processes, even if they are diverse, tend to have greater agreement in their final answer than incorrect processes.
% This is a key fact that we utilize to identify the correct reasoning chains along with their answers that we denote as ``self-consistency'' below.

We leverage this intuition by proposing the following \textit{self-consistency} method.
First, a language model is prompted with a set of manually written chain-of-thought exemplars \citep{wei2022chain}.
Next, we sample a set of candidate outputs from the language model's decoder,  generating a diverse set of candidate reasoning paths. 
Self-consistency is compatible with most existing sampling algorithms, including temperature sampling \citep{ACKLEY1985147,ficler-goldberg-2017-controlling}, top-$k$ sampling \citep{fan-etal-2018-hierarchical,holtzman-etal-2018-learning,Radford2019LanguageMA}, and nucleus sampling \citep{nucleus_sampling}.
Finally, we aggregate the answers by marginalizing out the sampled reasoning paths and choosing the answer that is the most consistent among the generated answers.
% In our experimental investigation, we find that combining chain of thought prompting with self-consistency leads to substantially better results than simply using chain of thought alone, which only considers a single generated path.

In more detail, assume the generated answers $\mathbf{a}_i$ are from a fixed answer set, $\mathbf{a}_i\in \mathbb{A}$, where $i=1, \ldots, m$ indexes the $m$ candidate outputs sampled from the decoder. 
Given a prompt and a question, self-consistency introduces an additional latent variable $\mathbf{r}_i$, which is a sequence of tokens representing the reasoning path in the $i$-th output, then couples the generation of $(\mathbf{r}_i, \mathbf{a}_i)$ where $\mathbf{r}_i\rightarrow \mathbf{a}_i$, i.e., generating a reasoning path $\mathbf{r}_i$ is optional and only used to reach the final answer $\mathbf{a}_i$.
As an example, consider Output 3 from \cref{fig:overview}: 
the first few sentences ``\textit{She eats 3 for breakfast\ ...\ So she has 9 eggs * \$2 = \$18.}'' constitutes $\mathbf{r}_i$, while the answer \textit{18} from the last sentence, ``\textit{The answer is \$18}'', is parsed as $\mathbf{a}_i$.\footnote{The parser is task dependent. For arithmetic reasoning, we parse the first numerical part as the final answer after the model generates ``The answer is ''. For commonsense reasoning, we parse the full string answer as the final answer after the model generates ``The answer is ''. Most generated outputs have a consistent format of ``\{Reasoning paths\}. The answer is X.'' if we prompt the language model in this format.}
% Then the \textit{self-consistency} procedure aggregates the $m$ reasoning paths by taking the plurality vote over the final answers $a_i$, i.e.,
% $
% a_{\text{best}} = \argmax_{a_i, i\in{1, \ldots, m}} \sum_{j=1}^m \mathbbm{1} (a_j = a_i).
% $
% In other words, we choose the optimal answer $a_{\text{best}}$ to be the answer that is most \textit{consistent} among the generated answers (possibly with different reasoning paths).\footnote{When there are ties, i.e., answers with equal votes, we take the answer in the order first encountered.}
After sampling multiple $(\mathbf{r}_i, \mathbf{a}_i)$ from the model's decoder, self-consistency applies a marginalization over $\mathbf{r}_i$ by taking a majority vote over $\mathbf{a}_i$, i.e., $\argmax_a\sum\nolimits_{i=1}^m \mathbbm{1} (\mathbf{a}_i = a)$, or as we defined as the most ``consistent'' answer among the final answer set.


% Hence, given a question $x$,  then  in $p(a_i, r_i | x)$ via sampling  to determine the best $a_i$.
% This observation allows us apply voting on a tractable set of potential final answers given by the generation of various reasoning paths.
% For a given language model, we sample $m$ candidate outputs $\{r_i, a_i\}, i\in{1, \ldots, m}$, from its decoder, where each generated output consists of a reasoning path $r_i$, along with a final answer $a_i$.


In Table~\ref{tab:aggregation}, we show the test accuracy over a set of reasoning tasks by using different answer aggregation strategies.
In addition to majority vote, one can also weight each $(\mathbf{r}_i, \mathbf{a}_i)$ by $P(\mathbf{r}_i, \mathbf{a}_i \mid \text{prompt}, \text{question})$ when aggregating the answers.
Note to compute $P(\mathbf{r}_i, \mathbf{a}_i \mid \text{prompt}, \text{question})$, we can either take the unnormalized probability of the model generating $(\mathbf{r}_i, \mathbf{a}_i)$ given $(\text{prompt}, \text{question})$, or we can normalize the conditional probability by the output length \citep{brown2020language}, i.e., 
\begin{align}
\label{eq2}
P(\mathbf{r}_i, \mathbf{a}_i \mid \text{prompt}, \text{question}) = \exp^{\frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^K {\log P(t_k \mid \text{prompt}, \text{question}, t_1, \ldots, t_{k-1})}},
\end{align}
where $\log P(t_k \mid \text{prompt}, \text{question}, t_1, \ldots, t_{k-1})$ is the log probability of generating the $k$-th token $t_k$ in $(\mathbf{r}_i, \mathbf{a}_i)$ conditioned on the previous tokens, and $K$ is the total number of tokens in $(\mathbf{r}_i, \mathbf{a}_i)$.
In Table~\ref{tab:aggregation}, we show that taking the ``unweighted sum'', i.e., taking a majority vote directly over $\mathbf{a}_i$ yields a very similar accuracy as aggregating using the ``normalized weighted sum''.
We took a closer look at the model's output probabilities and found this is because for each $(\mathbf{r}_i, \mathbf{a}_i)$, the normalized conditional probabilities $P(\mathbf{r}_i, \mathbf{a}_i \mid \text{prompt}, \text{question})$ are quite close to each other, i.e., the language model regards those generations as ``similarly likely''.\footnote{This also means that the language model is not well calibrated and thus cannot distinguish well between correct solutions and wrong solutions, which also explains why additional re-rankers were trained to better judge the quality of the solutions in previous work \citep{cobbe2021training,thoppilan2022lamda}.}
Additionally, when aggregating the answers, the results in Table~\ref{tab:aggregation} show that the ``normalized'' weighted sum (i.e., \Eqref{eq2}) yields a much higher accuracy compared to its unnormalized counterpart.
% For self-consistency in practice, one can either calculate the exact normalized weighted sum (\Eqref{eq1} and \ref{eq2}) or take a majority vote over $\mathbf{a}_i$ directly as an approximation, where the latter has a strictly lower computational cost. In our experiments, for simplicity we use majority vote to report all results.
For completeness, in Table~\ref{tab:aggregation} we also report the results by taking a ``weighted average'', i.e., each $a$ gets a score of its weighted sum divided by $\sum\nolimits_{i=1}^m \mathbbm{1} (\mathbf{a}_i = a)$, which results in a much worse performance.

Self-consistency explores an interesting space between open-ended text generation and optimal text generation with a fixed answer.
Reasoning tasks typically have fixed answers, which is why researchers have generally considered greedy decoding approaches  \citep{Radford2019LanguageMA,wei2022chain,palm}. 
However, we have found that even when the desired answer is fixed, introducing diversity in the reasoning processes can be highly beneficial; therefore we leverage sampling, as commonly used for open-ended text generation \citep{Radford2019LanguageMA,brown2020language,thoppilan2022lamda}, to achieve this goal.
% In a more-general sense, self-consistency can even be viewed as a general decoding method which considers a natural language chain of thought before providing the final answer.
One should note that self-consistency can be applied only to problems where the final answer is from a fixed answer set, but in principle this approach can be extended to open-text generation problems if a good metric of consistency can be defined between multiple generations, e.g., whether two answers agree or contradict each other.


\section{Experiments}
We conducted a series of experiments to compare the proposed self-consistency method with existing approaches on a range of reasoning benchmarks.
We find that self-consistency robustly improves reasoning accuracy for every language model considered, spanning a wide range of model scales.  %across multiple language models and model scales.

    and the AI2 Reasoning Challenge (ARC) \citep{Clark2018ThinkYH}.
    % In addition, we also included two other datasets that require reasoning from  \cite{bigbench}: Date Understanding and Sports Understanding.
    \item \textbf{Symbolic Reasoning}. We evaluate two symbolic reasoning tasks: last letter concatenation (e.g., the input is ``Elon Musk'' and the output should be ``nk''), and Coinflip (e.g., a coin is heads-up, after a few flips is the coin still heads-up?) from \cite{wei2022chain}. 
\end{itemize}

\vspace{-0.1in}
\paragraph{Language models and prompts.}
We evaluate self-consistency over four transformer-based language models with varying scales: 
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=0.6cm]
\vspace{-0.05in}
    \item UL2 \citep{ul2} is an encoder-decoder model trained on a mixture of denoisers with 20-billion parameters. UL2 is completely open-sourced\footnote{Model checkpoints at \scriptsize{\url{https://github.com/google-research/google-research/tree/master/ul2}}.} and has similar or better performance than GPT-3 on zero-shot SuperGLUE, with only 20B parameters and thus is more compute-friendly;
    \item GPT-3 \citep{brown2020language} with 175-billion parameters. We use two public engines \textit{code-davinci-001} and \textit{code-davinci-002} from the Codex series \citep{chen2021evaluating} to aid reproducibility;\footnote{Public API available at \scriptsize{\url{https://openai.com/api/}.}}
    \item LaMDA-137B \citep{thoppilan2022lamda} is a dense left-to-right, decoder-only language model with 137-billion parameters, pre-trained on a mixture of web documents, dialog data and
Wikipedia; 
\item PaLM-540B \citep{palm} is a dense left-to-right, decoder-only language model with 540-billion parameters, pre-trained on a high quality corpus of 780 billion tokens with filtered webpages, books, Wikipedia, news articles, source
code, and social media conversations.
\end{itemize}

\vspace{-0.1in}
We perform all experiments in the few-shot setting, without training or fine-tuning the language models. 
For a fair comparison we use the same prompts as in \citet{wei2022chain}: for all arithmetic reasoning tasks we use the same set of 8 manually written exemplars; for each commonsense reasoning task, 4-7 exemplars are randomly chosen from the training set with manually composed chain-of-thought prompts.\footnote{Self-consistency is robust to different sets of prompts and we provide a study in Appendix~\ref{sec:robustness_diff_prompts}.}
Full details on the prompts used are given in Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix-prompt}.

\vspace{-0.1in}
\paragraph{Sampling scheme.}
To sample diverse reasoning paths, we followed similar settings to those suggested in \citet{Radford2019LanguageMA,nucleus_sampling} for open-text generation.
In particular, for UL2-20B and LaMDA-137B
we applied temperature sampling with $T=0.5$ and truncated at the top-$k$ ($k=40$) tokens with the highest probability, for PaLM-540B  we applied $T=0.7, k=40$, and for GPT-3 we use $T=0.7$ without top-$k$ truncation.  
We provide an ablation study in Section~\ref{sec:ablation_sampling} to show that self-consistency is generally robust to sampling strategies and parameters.

\subsection{Main Results}
We report the results of self-consistency averaged over 10 runs, where we sampled 40 outputs independently from the decoder in each run. The baseline we compare to is chain-of-thought prompting with greedy decoding \citep{wei2022chain}, referred to as \textbf{CoT-prompting}, which has been previously used for decoding in large language models \citep{palm}.

We further compare self-consistency to ensemble-based methods for few-shot learning. 
In particular, we consider ensembling by:
(1) prompt order permutation: we randomly permute the exemplars in the prompt 40 times to mitigate model's sensitivity to prompt order \citep{pmlr-v139-zhao21c,Lu2021FantasticallyOP};
and (2) multiple sets of prompts \citep{gao-etal-2021-making}: we manually write $3$ different sets of prompts.
We took majority vote of the answers from greedy decoding in both approaches as an ensemble.
% Note that a key difference here is that self-consistency ensembles results in the ``output'' space (during decoding) without any changes to the inputs or the prompts, while existing work ensembles the results by varying the ``input'' space, e.g., the prompts.
Table~\ref{tab:ensemble} shows that compared to self-consistency, existing ensemble-based approaches achieve a much smaller gain.\footnote{Self-consistency is compatible with both ensemble approaches and we show the results in Appendix~\ref{sec:combine}.}
In addition, note that self-consistency is different from a typical model-ensemble approach, where \textit{multiple} models are trained and their outputs are aggregated. Self-consistency acts more like a ``self-ensemble'' on top of a \textit{single} language model. We additionally show the results of ensembling multiple models in Appendix~\ref{sec:compare_ensemble} where the model-ensembles perform much worse compared to self-consistency.

\subsection{Additional Studies}
\label{sec:ablation_sampling}
We conducted a number of additional experiments to analyze different aspects of the self-consistency method, including its robustness to sampling strategies and parameters, and how it works with imperfect prompts and  non-natural-language reasoning paths.

\vspace{-0.1in}
\paragraph{Self-Consistency is Robust to Sampling Strategies and Scaling}
We show self-consistency is robust to sampling strategies and parameters, by varying $T$ in temperature sampling \citep{ACKLEY1985147,ficler-goldberg-2017-controlling}, 
$k$ in top-$k$ sampling \citep{fan-etal-2018-hierarchical,holtzman-etal-2018-learning,Radford2019LanguageMA}, and $p$ in nucleus sampling \citep{nucleus_sampling}, over PaLM-540B in Figure~\ref{fig:gsm_temp} (left).
Figure~\ref{fig:gsm_temp} (right) shows that self-consistency robustly improves performance across all scales for the LaMDA-137B model series. 
The gain is relatively lower for smaller models due to certain abilities (e.g., arithmetic) only emerge when the model reaches a sufficient scale \citep{brown2020language}.

\paragraph{Self-Consistency Improves Robustness to Imperfect Prompts}
For few-shot learning with manually constructed prompts, human annotators sometimes make minor mistakes when creating the prompts.
We further study if self-consistency can help improve a language model's robustness to imperfect prompts.\footnote{We use the same prompts as before, but swap all the numbers in the reasoning paths with random numbers except the final answer, e.g., from ``\textit{There are 3 cars in the parking lot already. 2 more arrive. Now there are 3 + 2 = 5 cars.}'' to ``\textit{There are \textcolor{red}{7} cars in the parking lot already. \textcolor{red}{6} more arrive. Now there are \textcolor{red}{7 + 6} = 5 cars.}''.}
We show the results in Table~\ref{tab:robustness}: while imperfect prompts decrease accuracy with greedy decoding (17.1 $\rightarrow$ 14.9), self-consistency can fill in the gaps and robustly improve the results.

Additionally, we found that the consistency (in terms of \% of decodes agreeing with the final aggregated answer) is highly correlated with accuracy (Figure~\ref{fig:error_rate}, over GSM8K). 
This suggests that one can use self-consistency to provide an \textit{uncertainty estimate} of the model in its generated solutions.  In other words, one can use low consistency as an indicator that the model has low confidence; i.e., self-consistency confers some ability for the model to ``know when it doesn't know''.

\paragraph{Self-Consistency Works for Non-Natural-Language Reasoning Paths and Zero-shot CoT}
We also tested the generality of the self-consistency concept to alternative forms of intermediate reasoning like equations (e.g., from ``\textit{There are 3 cars in the parking lot already. 2 more arrive. Now there are 3 + 2 = 5 cars.}'' to ``\textit{3 + 2 = 5}''). The results are shown in Table~\ref{tab:robustness} (``Prompt with equations''): self-consistency still improves accuracy by generating intermediate equations; however, compared to generating natural language reasoning paths, the gain is smaller since the equations are much shorter and less opportunity remains for generating diversity in the decoding process.
% Nevertheless, this experiment indicates that self-consistency might be useful for any reasoning task where  intermediate reasoning steps are natural, such as when performing arithmetic or executing programs \citep{brown2020language,chen2021evaluating,scratchpad}, since diversifying and marginalizing over the intermediate steps appears to be robustly beneficial for improving the accuracy of final answers.
In addition, we tested self-consistency with zero-shot chain-of-thought \citep{zero_shot_cot} and show that self-consistency works for zero-shot CoT as well and improves the results  significantly (+26.2\%) in Table~\ref{tab:robustness}.


\section{Related work}
\paragraph{Reasoning in language models.}
Language models are known to struggle in Type 2 tasks, such as arithmetic, logical and commonsense reasoning \citep{evans2010intuition}.
Previous work has primarily focused on \textit{specialized} approaches for improving reasoning \citep{andor-etal-2019-giving,ran-etal-2019-numnet,geva-etal-2020-injecting,piekos-etal-2021-measuring}.
% or using multi-task learning to improve commonsense reasoning \citep{Lourie2021UNICORNOR}.
Compared to prior work, self-consistency is applicable to a wide range of reasoning tasks without any additional supervision or fine-tuning,  while still substantially improving the performance of the chain-of-thought prompting approach proposed in \citet{wei2022chain}.

\vspace{-0.12in}
\paragraph{Sampling and re-ranking in language models.}
%Depend on the applications, 
Multiple decoding strategies for language models have been proposed in the literature, e.g., temperature sampling \citep{ACKLEY1985147,ficler-goldberg-2017-controlling}, top-$k$ sampling \citep{fan-etal-2018-hierarchical,holtzman-etal-2018-learning,Radford2019LanguageMA}, nucleus sampling \citep{nucleus_sampling}, minimum Bayes risk decoding \citep{eikema-aziz-2020-map,shi-etal-2022-natural}, and typical decoding \citep{meister2022typical}.
% , where the sampling parameters can be tuned based on how much diversity is needed in the generation process.
Other work has sought to explicitly promote diversity in the decoding process \citep{10.1007/978-3-642-33715-4_1,DBLP:journals/corr/LiMJ16,diverse_beam}.

Re-ranking is another common approach to improve generation quality in language models \citep{adiwardana2020humanlike,shen-etal-2021-generate-rank}.
\citet{thoppilan2022lamda} collect additional human annotations to train a re-ranker for response filtering.
\citet{cobbe2021training} train a ``verifier'' to re-rank generated solutions, which substantially improves the solve rate on math tasks compared to just fine-tuning the language model.
\citet{elazar-etal-2021-measuring} improve the consistency of factual knowledge extraction by extending pre-training with an additional consistency loss.
All these methods require either training an additional re-ranker or collecting additional human annotation, while self-consistency requires no additional training, fine-tuning, nor  extra data collection.

\vspace{-0.12in}
\paragraph{Extract reasoning paths.}
Some previous work has considered task-specific approaches for identifying reasoning paths, such as constructing semantic graphs \citep{xu-etal-2021-exploiting-reasoning}, learning an RNN to retrieve reasoning paths over the Wikipedia graph \citep{Asai2020Learning}, fine-tuning with human annotated reasoning paths on math problems \citep{cobbe2021training}, or training an extractor with heuristic-based pseudo reasoning paths %via connecting entities 
\citep{DBLP:journals/corr/abs-1910-02610}.
More recently, the importance of diversity in the reasoning processes has been noticed, but only leveraged via task-specific training, either through an additional QA model over extracted reasoning paths \citep{DBLP:journals/corr/abs-1910-02610}, or by the introduction of latent variables in a commonsense knowledge graph \citep{yu2022diversifying}.
Compared to these approaches, self-consistency is far simpler and requires no additional training. The approach we propose simply couples the generation of reasoning paths and a final answer  by sampling from the decoder, using aggregation to recover  the most consistent answer without additional modules.

\vspace{-0.12in}
\paragraph{Consistency in language models.}
Some prior work has shown that language models can suffer from inconsistency in conversation \citep{adiwardana2020humanlike}, 
explanation generation \citep{camburu-etal-2020-make},
and factual knowledge extraction \citep{elazar-etal-2021-measuring}.
\cite{welleck-etal-2020-consistency} use ``consistency'' to refer to generating an infinite-length sequence in recurrent language models.
\cite{nye2021improving} improve the logical consistency of samples from a System 1 model by adding a System 2-inspired logical reasoning module.
In this paper we focus on a slightly different notion of ``consistency'', i.e., utilizing answer consistency among diverse reasoning paths to improve accuracy.


\section{Conclusion and Discussion}
We introduced a simple yet effective method called self-consistency, and observed that it significantly improves accuracy in a range of arithmetic and commonsense reasoning tasks, across four large language models with varying scales.
Beyond accuracy gains, self-consistency is also useful for collecting rationales when performing reasoning tasks with language models, and for providing uncertainty estimates and improved calibration of language model outputs.

One limitation of self-consistency is that it incurs more computation cost. 
In practice people can try a small number of paths (e.g., 5 or 10) as a starting point to realize most of the gains while not incurring too much cost, as in most cases the performance saturates quickly (Figure \ref{fig:exp_results}). As part of future work, one could use self-consistency to generate better supervised data to fine-tune the model, such that the model can give more accurate predictions in a single inference run after fine-tuning.
In addition, we observed that language models can sometimes generate incorrect or nonsensical reasoning paths (e.g., the StrategyQA example in Table~\ref{tab:example_path}, the two population numbers are not exactly correct), and further work is needed to better ground models' rationale generations.

\section*{Ethics Statement}
As we stated in the discussion, language models can sometimes generate nonsensical or non-factual reasoning paths, so one should use language models' outputs with extra caution. 
We deal with reasoning tasks mostly and the generated rationales are only used for inspecting how a model reaches its answer. One could potentially use the generated rationales to further check why the model makes certain mistakes or whether the model contains any biases when performing a certain task.
For language model in real-world use, further work is needed to better ground models' predictions and improve model's factuality and safety, to ensure the models do not cause harms to users.
