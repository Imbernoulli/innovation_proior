The thing that will not leave me alone is the table. Column after column of primes, and beside it the running count of how many primes lie below each magnitude. The primes themselves are hopeless — there is no rule that hands me the next one, Euler said as much, no order and no rule, and staring at $2,3,5,7,11,13$ I believe him. But the *count* is not hopeless. Gauss saw it from the tables and Legendre saw it too: the number of primes below $x$, this $\pi(x)$, hugs a smooth curve. Gauss's version is the cleanest — the density of primes near $x$ is about $1/\log x$, so the count should be near $\int_0^x dt/\log t$, the logarithmic integral $\mathrm{Li}(x)$. And it *is* near it, uncomfortably near, all the way up to three million where the counts have been carried. The count runs a little under $\mathrm{Li}(x)$, the gap creeps up slowly, and around that creeping gap there is a jitter — the density wobbles above and below its trend, and nobody has said why. So I have a smooth law that is almost true and a residue of fluctuation that is a complete mystery. That residue is the whole problem. If I could write down what it is, I would have the primes.

Real analysis gives me nothing to grip here, because $\pi(x)$ is a staircase — it sits flat and then jumps by one at each prime — and the primes are exactly the irregular set I cannot describe. I need to get off the staircase and onto something I can differentiate, integrate, deform. I need a single object that already contains every prime, so that I am not chasing them one at a time but interrogating one function.

There is exactly one such object I know, and it is Euler's. Unique factorization says every integer is a product of prime powers in one way, and the geometric series says $\frac{1}{1-p^{-s}} = 1 + p^{-s} + p^{-2s}+\cdots$. Multiply these geometric series over all primes and, term by term, I am choosing one prime-power factor from each and multiplying — which is to say I am running over every integer exactly once:
$$\prod_{p} \frac{1}{1-p^{-s}} \;=\; \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^s}.$$
This is the bridge. On the left, all the primes, explicitly, one factor each. On the right, a perfectly smooth-looking sum over the integers. The same function two ways. Let me call it $\zeta(s)$ and treat $s$ as a complex variable, not just a real one — because if I am going to use the tools of function theory, I want a function on the plane, not on a line.

But here is the wall, immediately. Both the product and the sum converge only for $\mathrm{Re}(s) > 1$. At $s=1$ the sum is the harmonic series and blows up; below, it is nonsense as written. So all of my prime information is trapped in the half-plane $\mathrm{Re}(s)>1$, and in that half-plane *nothing interesting can happen* — the product is a product of nonzero factors, so $\zeta$ has no zeros there, no poles, nothing for a contour to catch. The very tools I want — residues, deformation of contours, the location of zeros — all need the function to live where the series does not. If $\zeta$ only exists for $\mathrm{Re}(s)>1$, complex analysis buys me nothing. So before anything else I must give $\zeta$ a meaning on the whole plane. I must continue it.

How do you continue a Dirichlet series? You convert the sum into an integral whose integrand you understand everywhere. Euler's factorial integral is built for this. With $\Pi(s-1) = \int_0^\infty e^{-x} x^{s-1}\,dx$ — the Gamma function, $\Pi(s-1)=\Gamma(s)$ — substitute $x \to nx$ and you get
$$\int_0^\infty e^{-nx} x^{s-1}\,dx \;=\; \frac{\Pi(s-1)}{n^s}.$$
One term of my series has become an integral. Now sum over all $n\ge 1$ and pull the sum inside:
$$\Pi(s-1)\,\zeta(s) \;=\; \sum_{n=1}^\infty \int_0^\infty e^{-nx}x^{s-1}\,dx \;=\; \int_0^\infty x^{s-1}\sum_{n=1}^\infty e^{-nx}\,dx \;=\; \int_0^\infty \frac{x^{s-1}}{e^x-1}\,dx,$$
because $\sum_{n\ge1} e^{-nx} = 1/(e^x-1)$. The whole series is now a single integral, and crucially the integrand $1/(e^x-1)$ is a function I know everywhere — it is analytic except at the points where $e^x = 1$, that is, at $x = 2\pi i n$. It has *poles*, at known places, marching up the imaginary axis. That is what I was missing: an explicit object with explicit poles. A contour can be pushed past poles, and each pole pays a residue.

So instead of the integral along the positive real axis, take a contour that comes in from $+\infty$, loops around the origin, and goes back out to $+\infty$, and put $(-x)^{s-1}$ in the numerator so the thing is single-valued away from a cut — I fix the logarithm in $(-x)^{s-1}=e^{(s-1)\log(-x)}$ so that $\log(-x)$ is real when $x$ is negative. Consider
$$\int \frac{(-x)^{s-1}}{e^x-1}\,dx$$
around this keyhole. The two straight runs, just above and just below the positive axis, carry the same $1/(e^x-1)$ but their $(-x)^{s-1}$ differ by the phase you pick up going around: one has $e^{-\pi i(s-1)}$, the other $e^{+\pi i(s-1)}$. Their difference is $(e^{-\pi s i}-e^{\pi s i})\int_0^\infty \frac{x^{s-1}}{e^x-1}dx$. Up to the sign and the $i$, that factor is $-2i\sin\pi s$. Tidying,
$$2\sin\pi s\,\Pi(s-1)\,\zeta(s) \;=\; i\int_{\infty}^{\infty} \frac{(-x)^{s-1}}{e^x-1}\,dx,$$
the integral taken around the keyhole. And now look at what I have. The right-hand side is a contour integral whose integrand is analytic *everywhere on the contour* — the contour dodges the origin and stays away from the other poles. So the right-hand side is an entire function of $s$; it makes sense for *every* complex $s$. Therefore this equation *defines* $\zeta(s)$ for every $s$ — I just divide by $2\sin\pi s\,\Pi(s-1)$. The series only ever held for $\mathrm{Re}(s)>1$; this contour expression always holds. I have my continuation. $\zeta$ is now a single-valued function, finite for all finite $s$ except $s=1$ (where dividing leaves a pole — that is the old harmonic divergence, still there, isolated now as a single simple pole). And there is a bonus I read straight off: at the negative even integers, $\sin\pi s\,\Pi(s-1)$ blows up while the finite contour integral cannot keep pace, so $\zeta$ must *vanish* there. Zeros, handed to me, at $s=-2,-4,-6,\dots$. My first zeros.

That same keyhole contour can be opened the other way. For $\mathrm{Re}(s)<0$ the integrand decays well enough that, instead of hugging the origin, I can blow the contour outward to swallow *all the other poles* — the ones at $x=2\pi i n$, $n\ne 0$ — taking it now in the negative sense around the big region containing them. The integral becomes the sum of residues at those poles. At $x = n\cdot 2\pi i$ the residue of the integrand contributes $(-n\,2\pi i)^{s-1}(-2\pi i)$, and pairing the pole at $+n$ with the one at $-n$ to keep things real, summing over $n$,
$$2\sin\pi s\,\Pi(s-1)\,\zeta(s) \;=\; (2\pi)^s \sum_n n^{s-1}\bigl((-i)^{s-1}+i^{s-1}\bigr).$$
Stare at the right-hand side. The sum $\sum_n n^{s-1}$ is $\zeta(1-s)$. So this is a relation between $\zeta(s)$ and $\zeta(1-s)$ — a law connecting the value of $\zeta$ at $s$ to its value at the reflected point $1-s$. The plane has a symmetry axis, $\mathrm{Re}(s)=\tfrac12$, the midline between $s$ and $1-s$. Working the $\Pi$ and the trigonometric factors with the known reflection and duplication identities for the factorial, the relation rearranges into something that loses its lopsidedness completely:
$$\Pi\!\Bigl(\frac{s}{2}-1\Bigr)\pi^{-s/2}\zeta(s) \;\text{ is unchanged when } s \text{ is replaced by } 1-s.$$
That is, $\Pi(\frac{s}{2}-1)\pi^{-s/2}\zeta(s) = \Pi(\frac{1-s}{2}-1)\pi^{-(1-s)/2}\zeta(1-s)$.

I want to see *why* this symmetry is there, not just that it falls out of the residues, because the symmetric combination with $\pi^{-s/2}$ and $\Pi(s/2-1)$ — the half-arguments — is too pretty to be an accident. Where would $s/2$ and $\pi^{-s/2}$ come from naturally? From $n^2$. If, instead of $e^{-nx}$, I feed the Gamma integral $e^{-n^2\pi x}$, I get
$$\int_0^\infty e^{-n^2\pi x}\,x^{s/2-1}\,dx \;=\; \Pi\!\Bigl(\frac{s}{2}-1\Bigr)\pi^{-s/2}\,\frac{1}{n^s},$$
exactly the symmetric package, with the $s/2$ and the $\pi^{-s/2}$ appearing for free. Sum over $n$ and define $\psi(x)=\sum_{n\ge1}e^{-n^2\pi x}$:
$$\Pi\!\Bigl(\frac{s}{2}-1\Bigr)\pi^{-s/2}\zeta(s) \;=\; \int_0^\infty \psi(x)\,x^{s/2-1}\,dx, \qquad \mathrm{Re}(s)>1.$$
And now $\psi$ is a *theta* series, and theta series have a self-duality I know from the elliptic-function world — Jacobi's, $2\psi(x)+1 = x^{-1/2}\bigl(2\psi(1/x)+1\bigr)$, the Gaussian's self-reciprocity under summation. That self-duality is a symmetry under $x \mapsto 1/x$. And the integral against $x^{s/2-1}$ over $(0,\infty)$ is *exactly* the kind of integral that turns $x\mapsto 1/x$ into $s \mapsto 1-s$. So the symmetry of the functional equation is nothing but the self-duality of theta, viewed through the Mellin integral. That is the real reason. Let me carry it out.

Split the integral at $1$:
$$\int_0^\infty \psi(x)x^{s/2-1}dx = \int_1^\infty \psi(x)x^{s/2-1}dx + \int_0^1 \psi(x)x^{s/2-1}dx.$$
In the second piece substitute $x\to 1/x$ and use Jacobi to rewrite $\psi(1/x)= -\tfrac12 + \tfrac12 x^{1/2} + x^{1/2}\psi(x)$. The $\psi(x)$ part folds onto the first integral with $x^{s/2-1}$ becoming $x^{(1-s)/2-1}$; the two bare powers give $\tfrac12\int_1^\infty\bigl(x^{-(s+1)/2}-x^{-s/2-1}\bigr)dx$. Doing that elementary piece carefully, this is $\tfrac12\bigl(\tfrac{2}{s-1}-\tfrac{2}{s}\bigr)=\tfrac1{s-1}-\tfrac1s=\tfrac1{s(s-1)}$, the same as Riemann's $-\tfrac1{s(1-s)}$. So
$$\Pi\!\Bigl(\frac{s}{2}-1\Bigr)\pi^{-s/2}\zeta(s) \;=\; \frac{1}{s(s-1)} \;+\; \int_1^\infty \psi(x)\bigl(x^{s/2-1}+x^{-(1+s)/2}\bigr)\,dx.$$
This is the convenient expression I was hoping for, and it does everything at once. The integral on the right converges for *all* $s$ (the $\psi(x)$ decays like $e^{-\pi x}$, killing any power) — so this is another, cleaner continuation of $\zeta$ to the whole plane. And it is manifestly symmetric under $s\mapsto 1-s$: the two exponents $x^{s/2-1}$ and $x^{-(1+s)/2}=x^{(1-s)/2-1}$ swap into each other, and $\tfrac1{s(s-1)}$ is itself symmetric in $s\leftrightarrow 1-s$. The functional equation is no longer a computation I have to trust; it is sitting on the page as the symmetry of an integral. The whole right-hand side is invariant when I reflect $s$ across $\tfrac12$.

The expression also shows me its two blemishes: the term $\tfrac1{s(s-1)}$ has simple poles at $s=0$ and $s=1$. The pole at $s=1$ is $\zeta$'s genuine pole (the old harmonic divergence). The pole at $s=0$ is coming from the factor $\Pi(s/2-1)=\Gamma(s/2)$, which has its own pole at $s=0$. I would like a version of this object that is clean — entire, no poles at all — because an entire function is the kind of thing I can hope to write as a product over its zeros, the way Euler wrote $\sin\pi x$ as $\pi x\prod(1-x^2/k^2)$, a polynomial of infinite degree built from where it vanishes. To kill both poles I multiply by $\tfrac12 s(s-1)$, which vanishes at exactly $s=0$ and $s=1$ and — being $\tfrac12 s(s-1)$, symmetric under $s\mapsto 1-s$ — does not spoil the reflection symmetry. Define
$$\xi(s) \;=\; \tfrac12\,s(s-1)\,\pi^{-s/2}\,\Pi\!\Bigl(\frac{s}{2}-1\Bigr)\,\zeta(s).$$
Now $\xi$ is entire, and $\xi(s)=\xi(1-s)$ exactly. If I set $s=\tfrac12+ti$ to put the symmetry axis at $t=0$, the symmetry becomes evenness in $t$, and the convenient expression gives
$$\Xi(t)=\xi\!\bigl(\tfrac12+ti\bigr) \;=\; \tfrac12 - \bigl(t^2+\tfrac14\bigr)\int_1^\infty \psi(x)\,x^{-3/4}\cos\!\bigl(\tfrac12 t\log x\bigr)\,dx,$$
which is real for real $t$, even in $t$, and (since $\psi$ decays so fast) develops in powers of $t^2$ as a very rapidly converging series — an entire function of $t^2$, finite for every finite $t$.

The next thing to ask is where this entire function can vanish. Inside $\xi$ sit the zeros of $\zeta$. The factor $\Pi(s/2-1)$ has poles, not zeros, so it contributes no zeros to $\xi$; the powers of $\pi$ and the $\tfrac12 s(s-1)$ contribute none in the interesting region. So the zeros of $\xi$ are precisely the zeros of $\zeta$ — except for the trivial ones at $s=-2,-4,\dots$, which were exactly cancelled by the poles of $\Pi(s/2-1)$ when I built $\xi$. Where can the *remaining* zeros be? For $\mathrm{Re}(s)>1$, $\log\zeta(s) = -\sum_p\log(1-p^{-s})$ is finite — the Euler product has no zero factors — so $\zeta\ne 0$ there. By the reflection symmetry, $\zeta\ne0$ for $\mathrm{Re}(s)<0$ apart from the trivial zeros. So every nontrivial zero $\rho$ is squeezed into the strip $0<\mathrm{Re}(s)<1$. And because $\xi(s)=\xi(1-s)$ and $\xi$ is real on the real axis (so $\xi(\bar s)=\overline{\xi(s)}$), the zeros come in the quadruple $\rho,\,1-\rho,\,\bar\rho,\,1-\bar\rho$ — symmetric about the line $\mathrm{Re}(s)=\tfrac12$ and about the real axis. With $s=\tfrac12+ti$, a zero of $\Xi(t)$ has $t$ with imaginary part between $-\tfrac12 i$ and $\tfrac12 i$, i.e. $\rho$ in the strip.

How many are there, up to height $T$? I can count them with the argument principle: $\int d\log\xi$ around the box with $0<\mathrm{Re}(s)<1$, $0<\mathrm{Im}(s)<T$, equals $2\pi i$ times the number of zeros enclosed. Tracking the growth of $\log\xi$ around that boundary — the $\Pi$ factor grows like $t\log t$ — the integral comes out to about $\bigl(T\log\tfrac{T}{2\pi}-T\bigr)i$, so the number of zeros with imaginary part between $0$ and $T$ is approximately
$$\frac{T}{2\pi}\log\frac{T}{2\pi} - \frac{T}{2\pi}.$$
The zeros thin out only logarithmically — their density near height $t$ grows like $\log\frac{t}{2\pi}$. And then, when I look along the real $t$-axis, I find approximately this many real roots. It is very probable that all the roots of this even function of $t$ are real, which in the original $s=\tfrac12+it$ variable says that all nontrivial zeros sit on $\mathrm{Re}(s)=\tfrac12$. I would dearly like a proof. I make a few attempts and they do not close; I set it aside, because — as I will see — it is not needed for the count of primes I am actually after, and the count is what I came for. But let me record the guess plainly, because it is the crux: the nontrivial zeros all have real part $\tfrac12$.

Since $\xi$ is entire and I have its zeros as the remaining data, I write it the way Euler wrote $\sin$ — as a product over those zeros. With $\rho$ running over the nontrivial zeros in the $s$-plane,
$$\xi(s) \;=\; \xi(0)\prod_{\rho}\Bigl(1-\frac{s}{\rho}\Bigr),$$
the product taken in the symmetric order that pairs the reflected zeros so it converges; because the density of the roots grows only like $\log$, this product has the right growth and $\log\xi$ behaves at infinity like $t\log t$, matching what I found from the count. In the $t$-variable this is the same statement as $\log\Xi(t)=\log\Xi(0)+\sum_\alpha\log(1-t^2/\alpha^2)$, with $\alpha$ not assumed real unless the conjecture is true. Taking logarithms,
$$\log\zeta(s) \;=\; \sum_\rho \log\Bigl(1-\frac{s}{\rho}\Bigr) + \log\xi(0) - \log(s-1) - \log\Pi\!\Bigl(\frac{s}{2}\Bigr) + \frac{s}{2}\log\pi,$$
where the $-\log(s-1)$ and $-\log\Pi(s/2)$ and $\tfrac{s}{2}\log\pi$ are just $\xi$'s definition undone. The first sum is the one that matters: the prime side of the world is about to be expressed through the zeros $\rho$.

Now I go back and fetch the primes, and I have to be a little clever about *which* counting function to chase. Take the logarithm of the Euler product:
$$\log\zeta(s) = -\sum_p \log(1-p^{-s}) = \sum_p\sum_{m\ge1}\frac{1}{m}\,p^{-ms}.$$
The natural variable here is not $\pi(x)$ but the function that weights each prime power $p^m$ by $1/m$. Let $F(x)$ jump by $1/m$ at each prime power $p^m$ (and, at a jump, take the midpoint value). Away from jumps, $F(x)=\sum_{p^m<x}\tfrac1m$; at a jump the equality is read with half of the jump included. I claim $\log\zeta(s)$ is its transform. Replace each $p^{-ms}$ by an integral: since $p^{-ms} = s\int_{p^m}^\infty x^{-s-1}dx$,
$$\frac{\log\zeta(s)}{s} \;=\; \int_1^\infty F(x)\,x^{-s-1}\,dx, \qquad \mathrm{Re}(s)>1.$$
This is the marvel — the first place $\log\zeta$ and a prime-counting function meet as an exact pair. It is a Mellin/Fourier transform of $F$. And a transform can be inverted: this is Fourier's theorem. Writing $g(s)=\int h(x)x^{-s}\,d\log x$ and splitting $s=a+bi$, Fourier's inversion against $\cos(b\log x)$ and $\sin(b\log x)$, integrated over $b$ from $-\infty$ to $\infty$, returns $h$. Applied here it gives, for any $a>1$,
$$F(x) \;=\; \frac{1}{2\pi i}\int_{a-\infty i}^{a+\infty i} \frac{\log\zeta(s)}{s}\,x^{s}\,ds.$$
I have $F$ as a contour integral of $\log\zeta(s)/s$, and I have $\log\zeta(s)$ written out in terms of the zeros. Substituting that expansion in and integrating term by term — after an integration by parts, because the individual terms' integrals do not separately converge to infinity — each piece becomes a recognizable integral. The $-\log(s-1)$ term yields $\mathrm{Li}(x)$. The zero-sum $\sum_\rho\log(1-s/\rho)$ term yields a sum over zeros of $\mathrm{Li}(x^\rho)$, with the reflected partner $1-\rho$ kept beside $\rho$; if the conjecture is true, that reflected partner is just the conjugate. The $-\log\Pi(s/2)$ term — the term that accounts for the trivial zeros — yields a single convergent integral. And the constant gives $\log\xi(0)=-\log 2$. Assembling,
$$F(x) \;=\; \mathrm{Li}(x) \;-\; \sum_{\mathrm{Im}\,\rho>0}\Bigl(\mathrm{Li}(x^\rho)+\mathrm{Li}\bigl(x^{1-\rho}\bigr)\Bigr) \;+\; \int_x^\infty \frac{dt}{t(t^2-1)\log t} \;+\; \log\xi(0),$$
with the zero sum taken in the symmetric limiting order, and with $F$ taking midpoint values at jumps. In the $t$-notation this same pair is $\mathrm{Li}(x^{1/2+i\alpha})+\mathrm{Li}(x^{1/2-i\alpha})$, with $\alpha$ a root of $\Xi$ and not assumed real. There it is — an exact equality, not an approximation. The smooth main term $\mathrm{Li}(x)$ that Gauss saw; the constant $-\log2$; a slow correction from the trivial zeros; and the one term that was the whole mystery — the sum over the nontrivial zeros. Every fluctuation in the prime count, the jitter nobody could explain, lives in that sum.

And I can read off precisely *how* the zeros govern the primes. For the size of one oscillating contribution, pair $\rho=\beta+i\gamma$ with its conjugate and use the leading behavior of the logarithmic integral: $\mathrm{Li}(x^\rho)+\mathrm{Li}(x^{\bar\rho})$ is governed by a term proportional to $x^\beta\cos(\gamma\log x+\phi)/\log x$. The real part $\beta$ fixes the power of the amplitude, and the imaginary part $\gamma$ fixes the frequency in $\log x$. The primes' fluctuation is a superposition of these waves, one per zero. The further right a zero sits — the larger $\beta$ — the louder its wave and the larger the error in the prime count. This is exactly why the location of the zeros is the whole story: because the symmetry also reflects $\beta$ to $1-\beta$, the smallest possible worst exponent occurs when every nontrivial zero sits on $\beta=\tfrac12$. That is the meaning of putting the zeros on the line $\mathrm{Re}(s)=\tfrac12$: it is the statement that the primes are as regular as this explicit formula permits, with only square-root-size oscillations up to the logarithmic factors and the prime-power bookkeeping. The conjecture and the regularity of the primes are the same statement.

The last step is bookkeeping. I wanted $\pi(x)$, and I have $F(x)$, but the two are tied by the weighting $\tfrac1m$ on prime powers: away from jumps, $F(x) = \sum_{n}\tfrac1n\,\pi(x^{1/n})$, because $\pi(x^{1/n})$ counts the primes $p$ with $p^n<x$, weighted $\tfrac1n$; at jumps I keep the same midpoint convention. This relation inverts by Möbius: if $F=\sum_n\tfrac1n\pi(x^{1/n})$ then
$$\pi(x) \;=\; \sum_{n}\frac{\mu(n)}{n}\,F\bigl(x^{1/n}\bigr) \;=\; F(x) - \tfrac12 F\bigl(x^{1/2}\bigr) - \tfrac13 F\bigl(x^{1/3}\bigr) - \tfrac15 F\bigl(x^{1/5}\bigr) + \tfrac16 F\bigl(x^{1/6}\bigr) + \cdots,$$
with $\mu(n)$ the Möbius coefficient, $0$ if $n$ has a squared prime factor and $(-1)^{\#\text{primes}}$ otherwise. Substituting the exact expression for each $F(x^{1/n})$ gives $\pi(x)$ exactly, as $\mathrm{Li}(x)$ corrected by the lower-order $\mathrm{Li}(x^{1/n})$ pieces and, through every one of them, the same oscillating sum over zeros. The known approximation $\pi(x)\approx\mathrm{Li}(x)$ now stands explained: $\mathrm{Li}(x)$ is the leading term, the next correction $-\tfrac12\mathrm{Li}(x^{1/2})$ accounts for the systematic shortfall the tables show (the count running *below* $\mathrm{Li}$ at the square-root scale), and the wobble is the chorus of the zeros.

Let me say the whole chain back to myself once. The primes look lawless, but their count hugs $\mathrm{Li}(x)$ with an unexplained fluctuation. To get a tool on them I take Euler's product $\prod_p(1-p^{-s})^{-1}=\sum_n n^{-s}=\zeta(s)$, the one object holding all primes — but it only lives where $\mathrm{Re}(s)>1$, useless to function theory. So I rewrite $\zeta$ through the Gamma integral as $\int x^{s-1}/(e^x-1)\,dx$, whose integrand has explicit poles; a keyhole contour then continues $\zeta$ to the entire plane and hands me the trivial zeros at the negative even integers. Pushing that contour the other way, around the poles, relates $\zeta(s)$ to $\zeta(1-s)$; and feeding $e^{-n^2\pi x}$ instead reveals the relation as the theta self-duality, giving the symmetric, manifestly $s\leftrightarrow1-s$-invariant form $\Pi(\tfrac{s}{2}-1)\pi^{-s/2}\zeta(s) = \tfrac1{s(s-1)}+\int_1^\infty\psi(x)(x^{s/2-1}+x^{(1-s)/2-1})dx$. Killing the two poles with $\tfrac12 s(s-1)$ makes $\xi(s)=\tfrac12 s(s-1)\pi^{-s/2}\Pi(\tfrac s2-1)\zeta(s)$ entire with $\xi(s)=\xi(1-s)$, whose only zeros are the nontrivial zeros of $\zeta$, all in $0<\mathrm{Re}(s)<1$, numbering $\frac{T}{2\pi}\log\frac{T}{2\pi}-\frac{T}{2\pi}$ up to height $T$. Writing $\xi$ as a symmetric product over those zeros and inverting the Mellin pair $\log\zeta(s)/s=\int F(x)x^{-s-1}dx$ by Fourier's theorem gives the explicit formula $F(x)=\mathrm{Li}(x)-\sum_{\mathrm{Im}\rho>0}(\mathrm{Li}(x^\rho)+\mathrm{Li}(x^{1-\rho}))+\int_x^\infty\frac{dt}{t(t^2-1)\log t}+\log\xi(0)$, and Möbius inversion turns $F$ into $\pi(x)$. Each zero $\rho=\beta+i\gamma$ contributes an oscillation whose power is set by $\beta$ and whose frequency is set by $\gamma$, so the distribution of primes is governed, fluctuation and all, by where the zeros lie — and the conjecture that they all lie on $\mathrm{Re}(s)=\tfrac12$ is exactly the statement that the primes are as regular as this formula allows.
