I start with the wrong object in front of me: the frequencies themselves. If I know every `m_i`, then `F_2 = sum_i m_i^2` is immediate, but keeping the whole vector is exactly the memory cost I am trying to escape. The stream is asking me to remember a quadratic fact about a vector without remembering the vector. So I stop asking how to store many counters and ask a different question: what random quantity can I update as each item arrives, and whose distribution at the end still knows the moment?

The first path is to sample an update position rather than a value name. If I sample a universe value uniformly, I almost surely miss the heavy values that dominate high moments. But a uniformly sampled stream position chooses value `i` with probability `m_i / m`, which is the bias I want. Suppose the sampled position holds value `i`, and from that position onward I count `r`, the number of remaining occurrences of `i` including the sampled one. If this sampled occurrence is the `c`th one from the end among the `m_i` occurrences, then `c` is uniform in `{1, ..., m_i}`. I want a per-occurrence contribution that telescopes into `m_i^k`, and the differences of consecutive `k`th powers are the obvious candidate: `c^k - (c-1)^k`. Summing over `c` should collapse, but I do not want to trust "should" on a power identity, so I sum it out for a few `k` and `m_i` by hand before building anything on top of it.

For `k=2`, `m_i=3`: `(1-0) + (4-1) + (9-4) = 1 + 3 + 5 = 9 = 3^2`. For `k=3`, `m_i=3`: `(1-0)+(8-1)+(27-8) = 1+7+19 = 27 = 3^3`. The telescoping is real: `sum_{c=1}^{m_i}(c^k-(c-1)^k) = m_i^k` because all interior terms cancel. So if I output `m(r^k - (r-1)^k)` and `r` is the suffix count, conditioning on value `i` averages `(1/m_i) sum_c (c^k-(c-1)^k) = m_i^{k}/m_i = m_i^{k-1}`, and multiplying by `m` and weighting by `m_i/m` gives `sum_i m_i \cdot m_i^{k-1} = F_k`. I do not fully trust the bookkeeping of the two `m` factors and the `1/m_i`, so I run it on a concrete stream `2,1,2,3,2,3,1` (frequencies `1->2, 2->3, 3->2`), enumerating all seven positions as the uniform choice. For `k=2` the seven outputs of `X = m(r^2-(r-1)^2)` average to `17`, and `F_2 = 2^2+3^2+2^2 = 17`; for `k=1` they average to `7 = F_1`, for `k=3` to `43 = F_3`. The estimator is unbiased, and I only ever kept the sampled value and its suffix count. Reservoir sampling even removes the need to know `m` in advance.

That is a real opening, but when I check the variance the price is too high for the squared moment. The second-moment bound is of the form `E[X^2] <= k F_1 F_{2k-1} <= k n^{1-1/k} F_k^2`, so for general `k` the relative variance carries an `n^{1-1/k}` factor; for `k=2` that is `n^{1/2}`. Position sampling proves moments can be estimated without a histogram, but the `F_2` case still costs polynomial space here. It is not yet the special explanation for why `F_2` might collapse to logarithmic space.

For `F_2`, I try to exploit its geometry. It is the squared Euclidean length of the frequency vector. A one-pass stream update can maintain linear functions of that vector very naturally: choose coefficients `c_i`, keep `Z = sum_i c_i m_i`, and when item `i` arrives add `c_i` to `Z`. A deterministic choice such as `c_i = 1` only gives `F_1`; a single linear functional cannot equal a quadratic norm. But I do not need `Z` itself to be the answer. I can square it at the end, and a squared linear form is at least the right algebraic degree.

So I write out what squaring produces. Let the coefficients be random signs, `epsilon_i in {-1, +1}`, and maintain `Z = sum_i epsilon_i m_i`. Then

`Z^2 = sum_i epsilon_i^2 m_i^2 + sum_{i != j} epsilon_i epsilon_j m_i m_j = sum_i m_i^2 + sum_{i != j} epsilon_i epsilon_j m_i m_j`,

since every sign squares to one. The diagonal is exactly the squared moment I want; the off-diagonal terms are the only obstruction. If the signs are unbiased and pairwise independent, then `E[epsilon_i epsilon_j] = E[epsilon_i]E[epsilon_j] = 0` for `i != j`, so each cross term vanishes in expectation and `E[Z^2] = F_2`. I am no longer counting collisions explicitly; I am letting random cancellation erase the interactions between different values while leaving self-interactions intact. To make sure I have not fooled myself with the index algebra, I take `m = (1,2,3)`, enumerate all `2^3` sign patterns with equal weight, and average `Z^2`: it comes out to `14`, and `F_2 = 1+4+9 = 14`. Unbiased, exactly.

Unbiasedness is not enough; a clean expectation can sit on top of a hopeless variance. I need `Var(Z^2) = E[Z^4] - F_2^2`, so I expand `Z^4 = sum_{i,j,k,l} epsilon_i epsilon_j epsilon_k epsilon_l m_i m_j m_k m_l`. A monomial survives in expectation only if every sign in it appears an even number of times, because any sign appearing an odd number of times factors out an `E[epsilon] = 0` (this is where I must be able to factor that one sign away from the other three at once). The four indices can match in only a few patterns. If all four are equal I get `epsilon_i^4 = 1` and the term `m_i^4`, summing to `F_4`. If they split into two equal pairs, say two indices take value `i` and two take value `j != i`, I get `epsilon_i^2 epsilon_j^2 = 1` and the term `m_i^2 m_j^2`. Every other pattern has some index appearing once or three times and dies.

I need the multiplicity of the two-pairs pattern, and I would rather count than guess. Among the four ordered positions `(i,j,k,l)`, choosing which two of the four hold value `i` is `C(4,2) = 6`, and the other two automatically hold `j`; so each unordered pair `{i,j}` contributes `6 m_i^2 m_j^2`. (I confirm this by brute force: enumerating all ordered 4-tuples over a small index set, every unordered pair appears in exactly 6 tuples of type `(a,a,b,b)`.) Writing `S = sum_{i<j} m_i^2 m_j^2`,

`E[Z^4] = F_4 + 6S`.

Now the variance. `F_2^2 = (sum_i m_i^2)^2 = sum_i m_i^4 + 2 sum_{i<j} m_i^2 m_j^2 = F_4 + 2S`, so

`Var(Z^2) = E[Z^4] - F_2^2 = (F_4 + 6S) - (F_4 + 2S) = 4S = 2(F_2^2 - F_4) <= 2 F_2^2`.

I check the whole chain numerically on `m=(1,2,3)` rather than trust the pattern-counting: enumerating all sign patterns gives `E[Z^4] = 392`; here `F_4 = 1+16+81 = 98` and `S = 1\cdot4 + 1\cdot9 + 4\cdot9 = 49`, so `F_4 + 6S = 98 + 294 = 392`, matching. Then `Var(Z^2) = 392 - 14^2 = 196`, and indeed `4S = 196 = 2(196-98)`, and `2F_2^2 = 392` bounds it. The estimator has constant relative variance. It is noisy, but no longer wild.

But the variance derivation used a fact about *four* signs at once â€” the survival rule that an odd-occurrence index can be factored out â€” and pairwise independence only controls signs two at a time. So I should ask whether pairwise independence, which was all unbiasedness needed, is also enough for the variance, before paying for anything stronger. I build a genuinely pairwise-independent sign family that is not four-wise independent: take a uniform seed `y in GF(2)^t` and set `epsilon_i = (-1)^{<y, b_i>}` for distinct nonzero columns `b_i`. Pairwise independence holds because for `i != j` the pair `(<y,b_i>, <y,b_j>)` is uniform over `GF(2)^2`. On `m = (1,2,3,1,2)` with `t=3`, averaging over all `2^3` seeds gives `E[Z^2] = 19`, and `F_2 = 1+4+9+1+4 = 19`: unbiasedness survives, exactly as the pairwise argument promised. But `E[Z^4]` comes out to `1141`, whereas the survival-rule formula predicts `F_4 + 6S = 115 + 6 \cdot 123 = 853` (here `F_4 = 115`, `S = 123`). They disagree by `288`. So pairwise independence is genuinely not enough: with only two-wise control, some odd-occurrence cross terms in `Z^4` fail to cancel and the variance is not what the clean formula claims. The factoring step I waved at really did need four signs to be jointly controllable. That is the precise role of four-wise independence â€” not as ornament and not as full randomness, but as exactly the amount the fourth-moment calculation consumes.

This also explains why I should resist full independence. Independent signs over all `n` items would store too much random information, almost as bad as the histogram. Since the analysis never asks about five signs at once, a four-wise-independent family suffices, and such families are cheap: BCH-code orthogonal arrays, or equivalently finite-field polynomial constructions, describe the sign function with `O(log n)`-bit seeds and evaluate the sign of the current item during the pass.

A single copy still has relative standard deviation on the order of one (the bound `Var(Z^2) <= 2F_2^2` is exactly that statement), so I cannot stop there. If I average `s_1` independent copies of `Z^2`, the expectation stays `F_2` and the variance falls by `s_1`. Chebyshev then says `s_1 = Theta(lambda^{-2})` gives constant success probability for relative error `lambda`. But pushing the failure probability to `delta` by averaging alone would cost `1/delta`, the wrong dependence. I instead make several independent averages and take their median. Once each average is good with probability above one half, a Chernoff bound says the median fails only when more than half of the independent groups fail, so `Theta(log(1/delta))` groups suffice.

So Chernoff is the reliability wrapper, not the source of the space saving. It only amplifies after the estimator exists. The work that buys the logarithmic space is constructing a tiny stream-maintainable random variable whose diagonal terms are the frequency moment and whose off-diagonal terms cancel, and then the fourth-moment calculation â€” including the concrete check that pairwise independence is too weak and four-wise is the right amount â€” that controls its variance.

I also resist generalizing too soon. A norm-estimation language suggests random linear observations with some heavier distribution, but the squared moment does not need that machine: the finite sign construction already puts the diagonal terms in for free, cancels the cross terms in expectation, and has a fourth-moment variance bound I have now verified numerically.

So the path is coherent. Sampling update positions gives a general telescoping estimator for `F_k`, which I confirmed is unbiased on a concrete stream but pays `n^{1-1/k}` in variance; signed linear summaries give the exceptional logarithmic-space estimator for `F_2`. The probabilistic move is to stop representing frequencies one coordinate at a time and instead represent the frequency vector through a random observation designed so that the desired moment is the squared length of that observation. Once that viewpoint is in place, small space is no longer paradoxical: the stream summary is tiny because it never tries to remember which values collided; it arranges for different values to cancel and equal values to reinforce.
