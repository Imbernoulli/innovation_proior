I want to know when an equation can be solved by radicals, and the honest situation is that nobody knows *why* the quadratic, cubic, and quartic give way and the quintic does not. Cardano has the cubic, Ferrari the quartic, and then for two hundred and fifty years the fifth degree sits there. Abel's proof rules out a general formula for the quintic, but it follows radical expressions layer by layer, and at the end I still cannot look at a *particular* equation and say "this one yields, that one does not." I do not want one more clever substitution. I want the thing inside an equation that decides the matter, an object I can hold and inspect, that makes Cardano and Ferrari fall out as easy cases and makes the quintic's resistance visible as a property of that object.

So let me start where the only real progress was. Lagrange looked at all the classical solutions and found they are the same trick wearing different clothes. You form a function of the roots and watch what happens as you permute the roots. For the cubic, take $x_1+\omega x_2+\omega^2 x_3$ with $\omega^3=1$; cube it. Under the six permutations of three roots, that cube takes only two values — the three even permutations leave it alone, the three odd ones send it to its companion. Two values means it satisfies a quadratic. So I solve a quadratic, take a cube root, and I am done. For the quartic a suitable function takes three values, so a cubic resolvent appears. Each time, the auxiliary equation has *lower* degree than the original, and the whole engine is: a function of the roots whose orbit under permutation is small.

Now the quintic. I form Lagrange's resolvent for five roots and count its values, and they come to six. The auxiliary equation has degree *six*, higher than five. The method does not descend; it climbs. So Lagrange's machine, which was the only general thing anyone had, stalls exactly at the fifth degree, and it stalls because of *how the permutations of the roots act on a function* — because of the sizes of the orbits, which is to say because of the structure of the permutations themselves.

That last phrase is the one I keep returning to. The roots of an equation I do not know are inaccessible analytically; I cannot see $x_1$. But the *symmetry among the roots* — the ways I can permute them — is a finite, combinatorial thing, and Cauchy has shown how to compute with it: composition of substitutions, cycles, the fact that the number of distinct values a function takes divides $n!$. If solvability is really decided by the permutations, then the object I am hunting for is some set of permutations attached to the equation. Lagrange waved at this. Let me try to make it exact.

But first I have to be careful about what "known" even means, because the whole drama of solving is the slow enlargement of the known. A coefficient is known. A rational function of the coefficients is known. The instant I extract a root and adjoin it, more things become known, and an equation that was irreducible can suddenly factor — I have watched this with Gauss's cyclotomic auxiliaries: adjoin one root of $\frac{x^n-1}{x-1}=0$ and it splits. So "rational" is not absolute; it is relative to a field of currently-adjoined quantities. Let me fix that vocabulary: a quantity is *rational* if it is a rational function of the coefficients together with some quantities I have agreed to treat as known. To solve is to keep adjoining radicals until the roots become rational. Good. Now the permutations have to be defined *relative to this same field of known quantities*, because as the field grows the relevant symmetry should shrink, and when the roots are finally known the symmetry should be trivial. That coupling — field grows, symmetry shrinks — is going to be the spine of everything.

Here is the leap I want to test. Suppose I could attach to the equation a definite collection of permutations of its roots with this property: a function of the roots is rationally known **if and only if** it is left unchanged by every permutation in the collection. If that biconditional held, it would be everything. "What can I express rationally" would be the same question as "what is fixed by these permutations" — the analytic question becomes a combinatorial one, exactly, with no slippage. Let me see whether such a collection exists and what it is.

To get my hands on it I need a single quantity that sees all the roots at once. Lagrange's resolvent is the hint, but I want something cleaner: a function $V$ of the roots whose value *changes under every permutation*, so that the permutations are faithfully indexed by the values of $V$. Take
$$V = Aa + Bb + Cc + \cdots,$$
a linear combination of the roots $a,b,c,\dots$ with integer coefficients $A,B,C,\dots$ chosen so that all the permuted values of $V$ are distinct. I can always do this — there are finitely many permutations, each forces a polynomial condition on the $A,B,C,\dots$ for two values to collide, and I just avoid those, picking integers off the bad hypersurfaces. So now I have a $V$ with $n!$ distinct conjugates under the full symmetric group, no coincidences.

Why is this the right primitive element? Because every root is now a rational function of $V$. Let me prove it, because if it is true the entire splitting field collapses to $F(V)$ and I can work with one number instead of $n$ inaccessible ones. Write $V=\varphi(a,b,c,\dots)$. Form the product
$$\big(V-\varphi(a,b,c,\dots)\big)\big(V-\varphi(a,c,b,\dots)\big)\big(V-\varphi(a,b,d,c,\dots)\big)\cdots$$
over all permutations that fix the first root $a$ and shuffle the rest. This product is symmetric in $b,c,d,\dots$, so its coefficients are rational in $a$ and the elementary symmetric functions of the others — that is, rational in $a$ and the coefficients of the equation. Call it $F(V,a)=0$: a relation tying $V$ to $a$ with known coefficients. Now $a$ is a common root of $F(V,x)=0$ and of the original equation, and it is the *only* common root: if some other root $b$ also satisfied $F(V,b)=0$, then one of the factors $\varphi(b,\dots)$ would equal one of the factors $\varphi(a,\dots)$, i.e. two of my conjugates of $V$ would coincide — contradicting the choice that made them all distinct. One common root, found by taking the greatest common divisor of $F(V,x)$ and the original polynomial, and the GCD is computed rationally in $V$. Therefore $a$ is a rational function of $V$. The same for every root. So $F(V)$ generates them all; the splitting field is $F(V)$, exactly as I wanted.

Now I can build the collection of permutations honestly. Take the irreducible factor over $F$ of the equation satisfied by $V$; let its roots be $V, V', V'', \dots, V^{(r-1)}$ — these are the conjugates of $V$ that are *indistinguishable over the known field*. Each root of the original equation is some rational function $\varphi_i(V)$. Now write down the array: across the top row put $\varphi V,\ \varphi_1 V,\ \dots,\ \varphi_{m-1}V$ — that is the roots, listed via $V$. Below it, the same functions evaluated at $V'$; below that, at $V''$; and so on down to $V^{(r-1)}$:
$$
\begin{array}{cccc}
(V) & \varphi V & \varphi_1 V & \cdots \\
(V') & \varphi V' & \varphi_1 V' & \cdots \\
(V'') & \varphi V'' & \varphi_1 V'' & \cdots \\
\vdots & & & \\
(V^{(r-1)}) & \varphi V^{(r-1)} & \varphi_1 V^{(r-1)} & \cdots
\end{array}
$$
Each row is a permutation of the roots — because (by the lemma that says if $a=f(V)$ is a root then $f(V')$ is also a root, which follows from the irreducible factor dividing the symmetric product) substituting $V'$ for $V$ carries roots to roots. This array of $r$ permutations is my candidate. I will call it **the group of the equation**.

Does it have the double property I demanded? Take a function $F$ of the roots invariant under all the substitutions of this group. Write it via $V$, say $F=\Psi(V)$. Invariance under the group means $\Psi(V)=\Psi(V')=\Psi(V'')=\cdots=\Psi(V^{(r-1)})$ — $F$ takes the same value at every conjugate of $V$. Then $F$ is a *symmetric* function of the roots of the irreducible equation for $V$, so it is rational in that equation's coefficients, which are rational in the known field. So $F$ is rationally known. That is one direction. Conversely, suppose $F$ is rationally known and set $F=\Psi(V)$. Since the equation for $V$ is irreducible over the known field and $V$ satisfies the equation $\Psi(V)=F$ with $F$ a known (rational) constant, *every* conjugate $V'$ must satisfy the same equation $\Psi(V')=F$ — an irreducible polynomial cannot have one of its roots satisfy an extra rational relation without all of them doing so. Hence $\Psi(V)=\Psi(V')=\cdots$, which says $F$ is invariant under the group. Both directions hold.

I want to sit with this for a second, because it is the whole thing. *Rationally known* and *invariant under the group* are the same property. I have converted the moving, analytic notion of "what I can currently express" into a fixed combinatorial object — a set of permutations. In field language, the same set is the automorphisms of the field generated by the roots that fix the current field of known quantities pointwise. A larger field of known quantities means fewer such automorphisms; a subgroup fixes exactly the quantities that form an intermediate field; and an intermediate field is recovered from the substitutions that fix it. The correspondence reverses inclusion: grow the field, shrink the group. For the completely general equation, where the coefficients are independent and impose only the symmetric relations, the starting group is *all* $1\cdot2\cdot3\cdots m$ permutations. When every root is known, no nontrivial permutation can fix all the roots, so the group is the identity alone. Solving the general equation is therefore driving this group down from the full symmetric group to the identity. The size of the group at any moment measures exactly how much remains unknown.

One more thing the proof quietly told me, and I should name it because it characterizes the group intrinsically: the substitutions are what matter, not the starting arrangement of the letters. If I begin from a different first permutation and apply the same substitutions of letters, I get a group with identical properties. So the group is a set of *substitutions* closed under composition — if $S$ and $T$ are in it, so is $ST$ — independent of any base point. Cauchy's permutation calculus, but now pinned to the equation and the field.

Now the real question: what does *adjoining a radical* do to this group? Because solving is a sequence of radical adjunctions, and I have just made the group the meter of progress, so I must read each radical step on the group.

Let me adjoin one prime-degree radical in the form that can be read cleanly on the group. First I make sure the needed $p$-th roots of unity are already among the known quantities; adjoining them is itself a radical preparation, so it does not change the kind of solution I am allowing. Now take $r^p=A$ with $A$ known, and look inside one common splitting field containing both the roots of the original equation and the conjugates of this new radical. If this radical does not shrink the automorphisms when I restrict back to the old root field, the group stays the same. If it does, the restrictions of the automorphisms that fix the enlarged known field form a subgroup $H$ of the old group. The $p$ conjugates $r,\alpha r,\alpha^2r,\dots,\alpha^{p-1}r$ are all present once $\alpha$ is known, and the substitution sending $r$ to $\alpha r$ cycles them. So the old group is cut into $p$ equal cosets
$$H,\ HS,\ HS^2,\dots,HS^{p-1},$$
where $S$ is a substitution that advances the radical conjugates. Because $r^p=A$ now gives a cyclic splitting field, the field fixed by $H$ is normal over the old field; on the group side that means $H$ is normal. The same partition is therefore obtained from left cosets and from right cosets. That is the special feature I want to isolate: a **proper decomposition** is exactly such a normal prime-index step, and the quotient $G/H$ is cyclic of order $p$.

Let me make sure the correspondence runs both ways, because I want a criterion, not just a description. Forward I have it: after the needed root of unity is available, each effective prime-radical step gives a normal subgroup of prime index, and the quotient is cyclic. Now the converse — if the group of the equation has a normal subgroup $H$ of prime index $p$ with cyclic quotient, can I actually lower it by a single $p$-th-root extraction? Yes, and here is the construction. Pick a function $\theta$ of the roots invariant under all substitutions in $H$ but moved by substitutions outside it. Apply a substitution whose coset generates $G/H$; call the result $\theta_1$; apply it again to get $\theta_2$; and so on. Because the quotient has order $p$, this orbit runs $\theta,\theta_1,\dots,\theta_{p-1}$ and then closes, $\theta_p=\theta$. Now form, with $\alpha$ a primitive $p$-th root of unity,
$$\big(\theta + \alpha\,\theta_1 + \alpha^2\,\theta_2 + \cdots + \alpha^{p-1}\,\theta_{p-1}\big)^{p}.$$
Under the stepping substitution each $\theta_i$ advances to $\theta_{i+1}$, which multiplies the inner sum by $\alpha^{-1}$; raising to the $p$-th power kills that factor, so this quantity is invariant under the *whole* group — hence rationally known. I choose $\theta$ generically enough that the inner sum is nonzero and has stabilizer exactly $H$; only finitely many choices fail. Extract its $p$-th root and adjoin the inner sum itself. The substitutions that still fix the enlarged field are then exactly $H$. So a single $p$-th-root extraction lowers the group to the proper piece, and the proper decomposition is necessary and sufficient for that one step. Now repeat on $H$, decompose it properly again, extract another radical, and continue. The equation is solvable by radicals exactly when I can chain proper decompositions all the way down to the identity.

Let me state the whole criterion as cleanly as it now wants to be stated. **An equation is solvable by radicals if and only if its group admits a chain of proper decompositions**
$$G = G_0 \;\rhd\; G_1 \;\rhd\; G_2 \;\rhd\; \cdots \;\rhd\; \{e\},$$
each $G_{i+1}$ a proper normal piece of $G_i$, with each quotient $G_i/G_{i+1}$ cyclic of prime order. If several prime steps are grouped together, this is the same as asking for abelian quotients. Such a group I call **solvable**. After the necessary roots of unity are present, the field chain and the subgroup chain are the same ledger read in opposite directions: each radical extraction gives one normal step with cyclic quotient, and each cyclic quotient can be realized by one radical. The size measure I noticed earlier is now exact along a prime-index chain: $|G| = \prod p_i$.

I should test this against the cases that must come out right, or I have made an error somewhere. The general quartic starts with all $24$ substitutions of four letters. Adjoin a square root first — the natural order, since the classical quartic descends through a resolvent cubic that itself needs a square root. The $24$ split into two blocks of $12$; that block of $12$ splits into three blocks of $4$ by extracting one cube root; that block of $4$ — the four substitutions $\{abcd, badc, cdab, dcba\}$ — splits into two pieces of $2$ by a square root; the piece of $2$, namely $\{abcd, badc\}$, splits to the identity by one more square root. A chain $24 \rhd 12 \rhd 4 \rhd 2 \rhd 1$ with factors $2,3,2,2$, all prime. Solvable, and this is exactly Descartes' and Euler's solution of the quartic, recovered. The cubic similarly gives $6 \rhd 3 \rhd 1$, factors $2,3$. Gauss's cyclotomic equation has a cyclic group, which decomposes properly at every prime dividing its order — solvable, as he found by hand. Every classical success is the criterion's "yes."

Now the case I came for. The general equation of degree $n$, where the coefficients are independent unknowns, has the *full* symmetric group as its group — every permutation of the roots preserves every relation, because the only relations among generic roots are the symmetric ones built from the coefficients. So I must ask: is the full symmetric group on five letters solvable? Can I chain proper decompositions from it down to the identity with prime-index steps?

To answer I look for normal subgroups. The symmetric group on $n$ letters has the alternating group — the even permutations — as a normal subgroup of index $2$; that step is fine, a prime quotient. The question is whether the alternating group on five letters can be properly decomposed any further. I claim it has *no* nontrivial proper decomposition at all — it is simple — and it is not abelian, so neither can it sit at the bottom of an abelian-quotient chain. Let me verify the simplicity by counting, the way Cauchy's divisibility constraints invite. The alternating group on five letters has order $60$. Its elements fall into conjugacy classes by cycle type: the identity ($1$ element); the products of two disjoint transpositions, $(ab)(cd)$ ($15$ elements); the $3$-cycles ($20$ elements); and the $5$-cycles, which in this group split into two classes of $12$ each ($12$ and $12$). A normal subgroup is a union of conjugacy classes (normality forces it to contain whole classes), must contain the identity, and must have order dividing $60$ by the index counting. So I need a sub-collection of $\{1, 15, 20, 12, 12\}$ that includes the $1$ and sums to a divisor of $60$. Run through them: $1$; $1+15=16$; $1+20=21$; $1+12=13$; $1+15+20=36$; $1+15+12=28$; $1+20+12=33$; $1+12+12=25$; $1+15+20+12=48$; $1+15+12+12=40$; $1+20+12+12=45$; and the total $1+15+20+12+12=60$. Of all these, only $1$ and $60$ divide $60$. So the only normal subgroups are the trivial one and the whole group: the alternating group on five letters is simple. And it is plainly not abelian (two $3$-cycles need not commute). Therefore the chain cannot continue past it; there is no proper decomposition with prime quotient that gets below it. The symmetric group on five letters is **not solvable**.

So the criterion delivers its verdict: the general quintic is not solvable by radicals, because its group is the full symmetric group on five letters, which is not solvable. And — this is the part Abel's argument could not give me — the *reason* is now a single structural fact about a finite object: a non-abelian simple group blocks the chain. The same obstruction appears for every $n\ge 5$, since the alternating group on $n$ letters is simple for all $n\ge 5$ (a nontrivial normal subgroup would have to contain a $3$-cycle, and the $3$-cycles generate the whole alternating group, by the conjugation argument $(a_3a_4a_5)$ on a long cycle producing a shorter overlap). The general equation of every degree five and above is unsolvable, in one breath.

And the criterion is not only about the *general* equation — that was Abel's limit, and it is the limit I wanted to break. For a *given* equation of prime degree $n$, I can push the analysis to a concrete shape. The smallest group above the identity in any solvable chain for an irreducible equation of prime degree must have exactly $n$ permutations, and a transitive group on $n$ letters with only $n$ elements must be the cyclic group generated by a single $n$-cycle $x_k \mapsto x_{k+1}$ (this uses Cauchy's result on permutation groups of prime-letter count). The group just above it must consist of substitutions all sharing this same cyclic substitution; working out which substitutions commute with the $n$-cycle in the right way forces them to the affine form $x_k \mapsto x_{ak+b}$ with $a,b$ constants modulo $n$. Reasoning up the chain, the *whole* group of a solvable irreducible prime-degree equation can contain only substitutions $x_k \mapsto x_{ak+b}$. Conversely, if every substitution of the group has this affine form, the function $\prod (X_a - X)$ built from the Lagrange-style resolvents $X_a = (x_0 + \alpha x_a + \alpha^2 x_{2a} + \cdots)^n$ is rationally known, and the equation solves by Gauss's method for binomials. So an irreducible equation of prime degree is solvable by radicals **if and only if** its group consists only of affine substitutions $x_k\mapsto x_{ak+b}$.

That affine condition has a strikingly concrete restatement, and it is the one I will put forward as the headline because it requires no group theory to check. The affine substitution $x_k \mapsto x_{ak+b}$ never fixes two letters at once unless it is the identity (two fixed points would force $a=1, b=0$). So adjoining *any two* of the roots collapses the group to the identity — every other root becomes rational. And conversely, if any two roots being known makes the rest rational, then no nontrivial substitution can fix two letters, the group can hold at most $n(n-1)$ permutations and only a single cyclic substitution, which pins it to the affine form. Hence: **an irreducible equation of prime degree is solvable by radicals if and only if every one of its roots is a rational function of any two of them.** That is a condition on the equation itself, checkable in principle, and it is the single application I would stake the whole theory on.

I attach to an equation, over a chosen field of known quantities, the automorphism group of the root field fixing that base field pointwise, viewed as permutations of the roots. "Rationally known" and "fixed by the group" are the same property, and intermediate fields correspond inclusion-reversingly to subgroups. Solving means shrinking this group to the identity. Each prime-degree radical extraction, after the matching root of unity is in hand, shrinks the group across a proper decomposition — a normal step whose quotient is cyclic of prime order — and conversely every such proper decomposition can be realized by one radical. So the equation is solvable by radicals exactly when its group is solvable: a chain of normal subgroups with abelian quotients down to the identity. The general equation of degree $n$ has the full symmetric group as its group; for $n\ge 5$ that group is not solvable, because its alternating subgroup is simple and non-abelian and so blocks the chain — and that, finally, is why the general quintic cannot be solved by radicals, stated as a property of a finite object rather than as the failure of any particular formula.
