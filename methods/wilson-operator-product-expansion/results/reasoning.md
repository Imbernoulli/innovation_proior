Let me start from the thing that actually bothers me, which is that current algebra works beautifully for one kind of question and is silent on a neighboring kind, and I want to understand why the line falls where it does.

The kind it handles: Gell-Mann's currents close into an algebra. The charges $I_\alpha=\int d^3x\,J_{0,\alpha}(x)$ satisfy $[I_\alpha,I_\beta]=c_{\alpha\beta\gamma}I_\gamma$, and localizing this gives the equal-time commutator of the densities, $[J_{0,\alpha}(\mathbf x),J_{0,\beta}(\mathbf y)]|_{x_0=y_0}=c_{\alpha\beta\gamma}J_{0,\gamma}(\mathbf x)\,\delta^3(\mathbf x-\mathbf y)$. This is genuinely model-independent — no Lagrangian, no fundamental fields, it holds whether the proton is elementary or a bound state. And because it is bilinear in the currents it fixes things linear dispersion relations cannot: it pins $G_A/G_V$, where a homogeneous linear relation can never fix the overall scale of a matrix element. So far so good.

The kind it cannot touch: anything that lives in the short-distance behavior of a *product* of two currents. The Weinberg sum rules ask whether the vector-minus-axial propagator is less singular than $x^{-4}$, or than $x^{-2}$, as $x\to0$ — a question about the strength of the singularity of $j(x)j(0)$ at small $x$. Radiative corrections to strong interactions come from $\int T j_\mu(x) j_\nu(0)\,D^{\mu\nu}(x)$ and diverge according to how singular the current product is at $x\to0$. $\eta\to3\pi$, $\pi^0\to2\gamma$ run into the same wall. And the Bjorken limit — the large-$q_0$ behavior of $\int e^{iq\cdot x}\,T j(x)j(0)$, the thing that controls the deep-inelastic electron data — is a statement about that product near the light cone, Fourier-transformed.

So why exactly is the equal-time commutator powerless here? Let me look hard at what it actually is, as an object. It is a distribution supported on the single hypersurface $x_0=y_0$. Its coefficients are $\delta^3(\mathbf x-\mathbf y)$ and derivatives of $\delta^3$, functions of a *three*-vector. That is the whole problem in one sentence: it knows the product only on the equal-time slice, and only as a $\delta$-function in the spatial separation. The genuine short-distance structure I care about is how $j(x)j(y)$ behaves as the *four*-vector $y\to x$ — along the light cone, off the equal-time surface, in the timelike and spacelike directions. The equal-time commutator has integrated all of that away.

And there are two further symptoms that the equal-time commutator is the wrong tool, not just a coarse one. First, it can carry an *infinite* c-number constant. Schwinger pointed this out (Phys. Rev. Lett. 3, 296): take the naive canonical commutator of the time and a space component of a current and you generate a divergent constant times a derivative of $\delta^3$ — a Schwinger term. A quantity that contains an infinite constant cannot constrain a finite physical number; the infinity has to be subtracted by hand and the predictive content leaks out with it. Second, Bjorken's high-energy theorem, derived *from* the equal-time commutator, says the Fourier transform of the $T$-product goes as a power series in $q_0^{-1}$ at large $q_0$, with the leading coefficient set by the equal-time commutator. But I know an exactly solved case — the Thirring model — where the behavior is not of that form. So the equal-time commutator is not merely losing resolution; it is giving the wrong functional form for the high-energy limit.

Put those together and the move is forced on me by what the equal-time commutator throws away. It throws away everything off $x_0=y_0$. So I should stop discarding it. Instead of the commutator on a slice, work with the *ordinary product* $A(x)B(y)$ and ask how it behaves as the full four-vector $y\to x$. Whatever I write must contain the equal-time commutator as a degenerate special case — collapse to the slice and I should recover Gell-Mann — but it must keep the off-surface, near-light-cone information that the slice destroyed.

What could such a description even look like? In every field theory where I *can* compute — free scalar, free spinor, and renormalized interacting fields order by order in perturbation theory — the short-distance product of two local operators is not some wild new object. It reorganizes into a sum of *local* operators sitting at one point, each multiplied by a c-number function of the separation that carries all the singularity. The product's singular structure factorizes: the operator content is local and finite, the singularity is a number. Let me write the hypothesis as

$$A(x)\,B(y)=\sum_n C_n(x-y)\,O_n(x),\qquad y\to x,$$

with $\{O_n(x)\}$ a complete countable set of local fields including the unit operator $O_0=I$, and $C_n(x-y)$ ordinary c-number functions of the *four*-vector $x-y$. This is the generalization I was after: it lives in four dimensions, not on a slice.

Let me immediately check it against the things that broke the equal-time commutator, because if it inherits the same diseases it is pointless. The coefficients $C_n(x-y)$ are functions of the four-vector, and they are *distributions* in that four-vector — and a distribution cannot be an infinite constant. The Schwinger-term infinity arose precisely because the equal-time commutator squeezed a four-dimensional singularity onto a three-dimensional surface and the squeezing produced a divergent constant. Keeping the four-vector dependence, the singularity stays a singular *function* of $x-y$, finite away from $x=y$, integrable in the distributional sense — no infinite constants. That is the first symptom cured, and it is cured for a structural reason, not by hand.

Second symptom: the Bjorken limit. If the $C_n(x-y)$ can be arbitrary singular functions of the four-vector — powers of $(x-y)^2$ on the light cone, possibly *non-integer* powers, possibly with logarithms — then their Fourier transforms need not be a power series in integer powers of $q_0^{-1}$. Fractional powers of $q_0$ become possible. And fractional powers of $q_0$ are exactly what is found in the Thirring model. So the ordinary-product expansion has the room the equal-time commutator lacked. Good. And it is manifestly covariant — $x-y$ is a four-vector — it exists for $T$-products, and I can discuss symmetry breaking inside it to all orders. The equal-time commutator had none of these.

Now I have to be honest about the form of the $C_n$. What are they, near the light cone? The natural singular structure is a power of the invariant interval, $[(x-y)^2-i\epsilon(x_0-y_0)]^{-p}$ for some power $p$, possibly times a polynomial in the components of $x-y$ for tensor structure. Write $z=x-y$, with time component $z_0$ and spatial part $\mathbf z$, and let $z^2=z_0^2-\mathbf z^2$, so that $-z^2=\mathbf z^2-z_0^2$ is positive for spacelike $z$. The $i\epsilon$ is not decoration; it encodes the physics. Only intermediate states of very large energy — much larger than the fixed initial- and final-state energies — contribute to the light-cone singularity, and high-energy states are exponentially damped when $x_0$ is given a negative imaginary part. Pushing $z_0\to z_0-i\epsilon$ in the timelike piece, the singular base $-z^2$ acquires an imaginary part proportional to $z_0$, so the leading coefficient of the *product* $A(x)B(y)$ behaves as $(-z^2+i\epsilon z_0)^{-p}$. That prescription is what makes the difference between the product and its reverse vanish for spacelike separation, which is what microcausality demands.

I should verify the claim that this thing *reduces* to the equal-time commutator — otherwise it is not a generalization, it is a replacement, and I lose Gell-Mann. Take the commutator $[A(x),B(y)]=\sum_n E_n(x-y)O_n(x)$ for $y$ near $x$ but not equal-time. The leading c-number coefficient $E_0$, from the difference of the product and its reverse, is

$$E_0(z)=E\big[(-z^2+i\epsilon z_0)^{-p}-(-z^2-i\epsilon z_0)^{-p}\big],$$

$E$ a constant. The two terms differ only in the sign of the $i\epsilon z_0$, so for *spacelike* $z$ (where $-z^2>0$ and the bracket has no branch cut to cross) the two pieces are equal and $E_0$ vanishes — exactly the spacelike vanishing a commutator must have. For nonzero but small $z_0$, $E_0(z)$ is supported near $\mathbf z=0$, and I can convert it into a sum of $\delta$-functions of $\mathbf z$. The reason I am *allowed* to do this is that $E_0(z)$ vanishes for $|\mathbf z|>|z_0|$: the support in $\mathbf z$ shrinks to a point as $z_0\to0$, so integrating $E_0$ against a smooth test function $\rho(\mathbf z)$ only ever samples $\rho$ and its derivatives at $\mathbf z=0$. Concretely, using $\int_{\mathbf z}\delta^3(\mathbf z)\rho(\mathbf z)=\rho(0)$ and $\int_{\mathbf z}[\nabla\delta^3(\mathbf z)]\rho(\mathbf z)=-\nabla\rho(0)$, and Taylor-expanding $\rho(\mathbf z)=\rho(0)+\mathbf z\cdot\nabla\rho(0)+\cdots$ inside $\int_{\mathbf z}\rho(\mathbf z)E_0(z)$, I get

$$E_0(z)=F_0(z_0)\,\delta^3(\mathbf z)+\mathbf F_1(z_0)\cdot\nabla\delta^3(\mathbf z)+\cdots,$$

with $F_0(z_0)=\int_{\mathbf z}E_0(z_0,\mathbf z)$ and $\mathbf F_1(z_0)=-\int_{\mathbf z}E_0(z_0,\mathbf z)\,\mathbf z$. Now read off the $z_0$-dependence by dimension counting. $E_0\sim (z^2)^{-p}$ has mass dimension $2p$; integrating over $d^3\mathbf z$ removes dimension $3$, so $F_0(z_0)=f_0\,z_0^{-2p+3}$, and the extra factor of $\mathbf z$ in $\mathbf F_1$ gives $\mathbf F_1(z_0)=\mathbf f_1\,z_0^{-2p+4}$, with $f_0,\mathbf f_1$ constants. (By rotational invariance $\mathbf f_1$ itself vanishes, but the tensor analogues $f_{2ij}$ for the second-derivative term need not.)

Now take the equal-time limit $z_0\to0$. The coefficient of $\delta^3(\mathbf x-\mathbf y)\,I$ in the commutator is $F_0(0)=f_0\,z_0^{-2p+3}\big|_{z_0\to0}$, which is

$$F_0(0)=\begin{cases}0,& p<3/2,\\ f_0,& p=3/2,\\ \infty,& p>3/2,\end{cases}$$

and the $\nabla\nabla\delta^3$ coefficient, carrying $z_0^{-2p+5}$, is nonzero for $p\ge5/2$ and infinite for $p>5/2$. There it is, in one formula. When the light-cone singularity is exactly $p=3/2$, the equal-time commutator gets a finite $\delta^3$ coefficient — the Gell-Mann case, recovered cleanly. When $p>3/2$, the equal-time commutator coefficient is *infinite* — that is precisely the Schwinger-term divergence, now seen as the artifact of forcing a too-singular four-dimensional function onto the three-dimensional slice. And when $p<3/2$ the equal-time coefficient is *zero* even though the product itself is perfectly singular off the surface — information the equal-time commutator simply cannot see. So the ordinary-product expansion strictly contains the equal-time commutator, and it explains *why* the latter sometimes carries infinite constants: those are exactly the $p>3/2$ cases. Unless one is willing to allow infinite coefficients, the operator-product expansion is the more flexible object. This is the cleanest possible confirmation that I am generalizing in the right direction.

So I have a candidate framework: the structure $A(x)B(y)=\sum_n C_n(x-y)O_n(x)$ exists, and I need rules for how the $C_n$ behave. The obvious guess — translating the old large-momentum Feynman-diagram analysis into position space — is that the operators carry their *canonical* dimensions, a scalar counting as dimension 1, a spinor as dimension $3/2$, and the singularity of each $C_n$ following from adding up those canonical dimensions. Let me test that guess on the one strong-coupling case I can actually solve, the Thirring model. And it breaks. There *is* a short-distance expansion in the Thirring model — the structure $A(x)B(y)=\sum C_n O_n$ holds fine — but the canonical-dimension rules for the coefficient functions come out *all wrong* in the strong-coupling regime. The powers are not what canonical counting predicts.

Why does it break? Because in the Thirring model the dimension of the spinor field is not the canonical $1/2$. It slides *continuously* with the coupling constant — a renormalization effect, exact and computable in that solvable model. The canonical dimension is a free-field accident; renormalization shifts it. So the whole premise — that the singularity of $C_n$ is fixed by canonical dimension counting — is wrong the moment interactions are strong. That is the crack, and it is the key to everything.

Where does the right structure come from, then? I keep noticing the same pattern in three different places. The Thirring model is *exactly scale-invariant* for every coupling, and its operators carry non-canonical, coupling-dependent dimensions. Kastrup and Mack argue that scale invariance is a broken symmetry of strong interactions — exact in the short-distance regime, broken by the masses. And the scaling theory of critical phenomena — Kadanoff's block spins, Widom's equation of state — has matrix elements scaling with distance by *non-trivial exponents* that are nothing like the naive engineering dimensions. Three faces of one idea: at short distances there is a scale symmetry, and under it field operators carry genuine, renormalization-determined, possibly non-integer dimensions that are the field-theory analogue of critical exponents. That is what I should build the coefficient functions on — not canonical counting.

Let me make scale invariance do the work. Suppose at short distances the theory is invariant under a one-parameter dilatation group $U(s)$, and each local field transforms with a definite dimension $d(n)$:

$$U^\dagger(s)\,O_n(x)\,U(s)=s^{d(n)}\,O_n(sx).$$

For a free field $d(n)$ is the engineering dimension in mass units — the free spinor's $d=3/2$ is forced by the canonical commutation rule (which carries no dimensional constant, so $\psi\to s^{3/2}\psi(sx)$; multiplying $\psi$ by a power of a mass or a pure constant would just rescale it, it doesn't touch the $U(s)$ law). But for an interacting field $d(n)$ is whatever renormalization makes it, and need not be an integer or half-integer — the Thirring lesson, now built in from the start rather than assumed away.

Apply $U(s)$ to both sides of the expansion. The left side: $A,B$ each pick up $s^{d_A}$ and $s^{d_B}$ and have their arguments scaled, so $U^\dagger(s)A(x)B(y)U(s)=s^{d_A+d_B}A(sx)B(sy)$. The right side: each $O_n(x)\to s^{d(n)}O_n(sx)$. Setting them equal,

$$s^{d_A+d_B}\sum_n C_n(sx-sy)\,O_n(sx)=\sum_n C_n(x-y)\,s^{d(n)}\,O_n(sx),$$

where on the left I have also rescaled the argument of the expansion $A(sx)B(sy)=\sum_n C_n(sx-sy)O_n(sx)$. Now the operators $O_n(sx)$ on both sides are the *same* set, and they are linearly independent — that is the whole point of choosing a complete linearly independent basis. So I can match coefficients term by term:

$$\boxed{\,C_n(sx-sy)=s^{-d_A-d_B+d(n)}\,C_n(x-y)\,.}$$

This is the engine. It says each coefficient function is *homogeneous of degree* $-d_A-d_B+d(n)$ in its argument. Lorentz invariance plus homogeneity then fixes $C_n$ up to a finite number of constants — a single constant if $C_n$ is a Lorentz scalar. For a scalar that means $C_n(x-y)\propto |x-y|^{-d_A-d_B+d(n)}$ outright. The singularity strength of the $n$-th term is governed *entirely* by $d_A+d_B-d(n)$. The term is singular only when $d_A+d_B\ge d(n)$, and more singular the larger $d_A+d_B$ is relative to $d(n)$.

And this hands me, for free, the organizing principle that makes the expansion *usable*. Order the operators by dimension. The identity $O_0=I$ has $d=0$, so its coefficient $C_0\sim|x-y|^{-d_A-d_B}$ is the most singular — the leading light-cone singularity. As $d(n)$ increases the coefficients get less singular, and only finitely many $O_n$, those of low enough dimension, contribute at any given order of singularity. So to control the short-distance behavior to a fixed power, I only need finitely many terms. The expansion is an honest asymptotic organization, not a formal infinite sum I can't use. (It converges in the weak sense — sandwiched between fixed states $\langle\alpha|\cdots|\beta\rangle$ for $y$ close enough to $x$; as an operator statement to finite order in $x-y$ only finitely many terms matter.)

Let me make sure this isn't a fantasy by computing a case where I know every step: free fields, Wick's theorem. Take a free scalar $\phi$ with $d=1$, and the product of two normal-ordered squares, $:\phi^2(x):\,:\phi^2(y):$. Wick's theorem gives

$$:\phi^2(x):\,:\phi^2(y):=2\,[D(x-y)]^2\,I+4\,D(x-y)\,:\phi(x)\phi(y):+\,:\phi^2(x)\phi^2(y):,$$

where $D(x-y)$ is the free singular two-point function, which in four dimensions scales as $(x-y)^{-2}$. Now read the dimensions. The leading term is $2[D]^2 I$: it goes as $(x-y)^{-4}$, and the identity has $d_C=0$, so the predicted power is $-d_A-d_B+d_C=-(2)-(2)+0=-4$. Exactly $(x-y)^{-4}$. The scaling formula is right on the nose. The middle term $4D(x-y):\phi(x)\phi(y):$ has $D\sim(x-y)^{-2}$; expand the bilocal operator in a Taylor series in $y-x$ to bring it to the point $x$,

$$:\phi(x)\phi(y):=:\phi^2(x):+(y-x)_\mu:\phi(x)\nabla^\mu\phi(x):+\cdots,$$

and the leading piece is $4D(x-y):\phi^2(x):$ — coefficient $\sim(x-y)^{-2}$, operator $:\phi^2:$ of dimension $2$, predicted power $-2-2+2=-2$. Again exact. Reduce all the Wick products to a linearly independent operator basis at $x$ and every coefficient comes out homogeneous of the dimension the scaling law demands. The framework is not just consistent; in the one regime I can fully solve, it reproduces itself term by term.

Now, what about the currents — does current algebra survive this, or does scaling distort the very thing I was trying to extend? This worried me, because if the current dimensions also slid with the coupling, the whole equal-time algebra would shift and Gell-Mann's results would be at the mercy of dynamics. So let me check what scale invariance does to a *conserved* current. Take a symmetry generator $Q$ with $[A,Q]=qA$ for the fields it acts on. For this to be consistent with scale invariance, $Q$ must commute with the dilatations, $U^\dagger(s)QU(s)=Q$ — $Q$ is scale-invariant, dimension zero. If $Q=\int j_0$ is the space integral of a current, then the integral $\int d^3x\,j_0$ being dimensionless forces

$$U^\dagger(s)\,j_0(x)\,U(s)=s^3\,j_0(sx),$$

i.e. a conserved current has dimension $3$ in four dimensions. Now I want to know whether interactions can shift this. Consider the unequal-time commutator $[A(x),j_\mu(y)]=\sum_n K_{n\mu}(x-y)O_n(x)$. Because $j_\mu$ is conserved, the coefficient tensors $K_{n\mu}$ are conserved, and so the integrals $k_n=\int_{\mathbf z}K_{n0}(z_0,\mathbf z)$ are *independent of $z_0$* — current conservation makes them time-independent. A time-independent quantity cannot diverge as $z_0\to0$. So $k_n$ stays finite, and at equal times

$$[A(x),j_0(y)]=\sum_n k_n\,\delta^3(\mathbf x-\mathbf y)\,O_n(x)+\text{(Schwinger terms)}.$$

Consistency with $[A,Q]=qA$ (integrate over $\mathbf y$) gives $qA(x)=\sum_n k_n O_n(x)$, and since $q$ is a pure number this forces every $O_n$ appearing with nonzero $k_n$ to have the *same dimension as $A$*. In particular $k_n$ for the current term is a nonzero constant only when its $O_n$ matches the current's dimension, and it cannot diverge — so the conserved current's dimension is pinned at $3$ even with interactions. The same argument on the translation generator $P_\mu=\int\theta_{\mu0}$ gives $P_\mu$ dimension $1$ and the stress tensor $\theta_{\mu\nu}$ dimension $4$. The conserved currents keep their canonical dimensions while everything non-conserved is free to shift. That is exactly the Thirring pattern — conserved current rigid, spinor sliding — and it is *why* current algebra can survive intact inside a theory whose other operator dimensions are renormalized away from canonical.

There is a tension I have to face: a theory with exact scale invariance has no finite-mass particles — masses break scaling — so it is not physical on its own. The way out is to treat the scale-invariant theory as a *skeleton* and reintroduce masses as broken-scale interactions. Write the mass and interaction terms as generalized operators of dimension $d_i$, with dimensionful couplings $\lambda_i$. A term of dimension $d_i<4$ is super-renormalizable, $d_i=4$ renormalizable, $d_i>4$ non-renormalizable, and the $n$-th order contribution in $\lambda_i$ scales relative to the skeleton as $z^{n(4-d_i)}$ — so the broken-scale pieces are *less* singular at short distance and the skeleton's scaling controls the leading light-cone behavior. The skeleton has a stress tensor that is traceless, symmetric, conserved — so it sits in an irreducible Lorentz representation, which is what makes scale and Lorentz invariance automatic once translation invariance holds (this is clean in the quark model). The currents and the baryon current sit in the skeleton at dimension $3$; the stress tensor at $4$. There must also be a multiplet containing the pion field. For PCAC to work — for the symmetry-breaking axial divergence to be a relevant perturbation — the pion-field dimension $\Delta$ must satisfy $\Delta<4$; and the Källén–Lehmann representation forces $\Delta\ge1$. So $1\le\Delta<4$, the actual value being a consequence of renormalization (it is $1$ in the $\sigma$-model, $6$ in the naive quark model — and the framework says the truth is fixed by the dynamics, not by which Lagrangian you happened to write). This is the cash value of refusing to pick a Lagrangian: instead of choosing a canonical dimension I get a *constraint* on the real one.

Now feed all of this into the Bjorken limit, the question that started everything. The amplitude is

$$T_{\alpha\beta}(q)=\langle\alpha|\int d^4x\,e^{iq\cdot x}\,T A(x)B(0)|\beta\rangle.$$

Bjorken takes $q_0\to\infty$ at fixed $\mathbf q$. With the operator-product expansion I don't even need to fix $\mathbf q$ — I just need $q^2$ large, because large $q^2$ means the $x$-integral is dominated by small $x$, where the expansion holds. Substitute it:

$$T_{\alpha\beta}(q)\simeq\sum_n\langle\alpha|O_n(0)|\beta\rangle\int d^4x\,e^{iq\cdot x}\,C_n(x)=\sum_n R_n(q)\,\langle\alpha|O_n(0)|\beta\rangle,$$

with $R_n(q)$ the Fourier transform of the coefficient function. Each $C_n$ is homogeneous of degree $-d_A-d_B+d(n)$ in a four-dimensional $x$, so its transform is homogeneous of the complementary degree: Fourier transform in $d=4$ dimensions trades $x^{-\alpha}$ for $q^{\alpha-4}$, giving

$$R_n(q)\sim q^{\,d_A+d_B-d(n)-4}$$

for large $q$ (the skeleton term; finite-mass corrections add less-singular pieces, and Lorentz invariance lets each term be a polynomial in $q$ times this). And here is the thing the equal-time commutator could never produce: if some operator dimension $d(n)$ is *non-integer* — which it is, generically, once renormalization shifts it — then $R_n(q)$ carries a *fractional* power of $q$. To get the Bjorken limit specifically, set $q^2\to q_0^2$ to leading order and expand in $q_0^{-1}$; one gets a series, but it may contain fractional powers of $q_0^{-1}$. Bjorken's original theorem, tied to the equal-time commutator, allowed only integer powers. The operator-product expansion permits the fractional powers — and the fractional powers are exactly what the Thirring model exhibits, which is the case that broke the equal-time treatment in the first place. The crack in the canonical-dimension guess and the headline result close on the same fact: dimensions are real numbers fixed by renormalization, not canonical integers. (Such fractional powers are seldom important when $A$ and $B$ are both currents — those keep dimension $3$ — but they matter when $A$ and $B$ both come from the pion-field multiplet, whose $\Delta$ is non-integer, and $O_n$ is a current.)

Let me say back the whole chain to be sure it stands. The pain was a class of short-distance/high-energy questions — Weinberg sum-rule convergence, radiative-correction divergences, $\eta\to3\pi$, $\pi^0\to2\gamma$, the Bjorken limit — that current algebra cannot reach, because the equal-time commutator lives on a slice, carries $\delta$-functions of a three-vector, can hide infinite Schwinger constants, and gives only integer powers of $q_0^{-1}$. The fix is to keep the off-slice information: expand the *ordinary* four-vector product $A(x)B(y)=\sum_n C_n(x-y)O_n(x)$ over a complete set of local operators with c-number coefficient functions of the four-vector. That object reduces to the equal-time commutator by the $\delta$-function reduction (and explains the Schwinger infinities as the $p>3/2$ cases), and it has the room for fractional high-energy powers. Then the singularities of the $C_n$ are not free: assuming short-distance scale invariance, scale covariance forces $C_n$ homogeneous of degree $-d_A-d_B+d(n)$, so each coefficient is fixed up to constants by the dimensions, the leading singularity belongs to the identity, the expansion orders by dimension, conserved currents stay pinned at dimension $3$ (so current algebra survives), and the pion field is constrained to $1\le\Delta<4$. Feed this into the Bjorken amplitude and the high-energy behavior is $R_n(q)\sim q^{\,d_A+d_B-d(n)-4}$, with fractional powers whenever a dimension is non-integer. The decisive correction over the canonical-dimension guess is that the operator dimensions are genuine renormalization-determined real numbers — critical exponents of the field theory — not the canonical integers, and that single correction is what makes the coefficient-function rules come out right where canonical counting came out wrong.

The final form the whole construction lands on, then, is the pair of statements — the expansion and the scaling law that fixes it:

$$A(x)\,B(y)=\sum_n C_n(x-y)\,O_n(x),\qquad
C_n(\lambda x-\lambda y)=\lambda^{-d_A-d_B+d(n)}\,C_n(x-y),$$

so that for scalar coefficients $C_n(x-y)\propto|x-y|^{-d_A-d_B+d(n)}$, the singularities are ordered by operator dimension, the equal-time commutator and the Bjorken limit drop out as special cases, and the conserved currents keep dimension $3$.
