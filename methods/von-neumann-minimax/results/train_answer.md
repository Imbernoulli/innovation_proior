Two people sit at a table playing a game of pure opposition: player 1 picks a row $x\in\{1,\dots,m\}$, player 2 picks a column $y\in\{1,\dots,n\}$, simultaneously and in ignorance of each other, and a fixed matrix pays player 1 the amount $g(x,y)$ and player 2 the amount $-g(x,y)$. The phrase everyone reaches for — "play as advantageously as possible" — dissolves on inspection, because player 1 wants $g$ large but does not own $g$: it hangs on $y$, which his exactly-opposed opponent controls, whose own best move hangs back on $x$. The situation is circular, and a sentence that points in a circle is not a definition. The honest thing a single player can compute is what he can guarantee against the worst case. If player 1 commits to $x$ and is then driven down to $\min_y g(x,y)$, the most he can secure is the floor $v_{\text{low}}=\max_x\min_y g(x,y)$; dually, the least player 2 can hold him to is the ceiling $v_{\text{up}}=\min_y\max_x g(x,y)$. A one-line argument — $\min_{y'}g(x,y')\le g(x,y)\le\max_{x'}g(x',y)$, then take outer max over $x$ and outer min over $y$ — shows $v_{\text{low}}\le v_{\text{up}}$ for every matrix: whoever is found out first is never better off, and the gap is precisely the worth of being found out. The dream is equality, $v_{\text{low}}=v_{\text{up}}$, which happens exactly when there is a saddle $(x_0,y_0)$ with $g(x,y_0)\le g(x_0,y_0)\le g(x_0,y)$; then the value is determinate and play is deterministic. But saddles are the exception. Matching Pennies $\bigl(\begin{smallmatrix}+1&-1\\-1&+1\end{smallmatrix}\bigr)$ has $v_{\text{low}}=-1<+1=v_{\text{up}}$, and Stone–Paper–Scissors shows the same wider hole; in pure strategies the generic game has no value, and the real enemy one feels while playing is not insufficient cleverness but *being found out* — any deterministic pattern is read and beaten. Borel (1921) named the right language — randomize over plans, evaluate by expectation — and proved existence for symmetric games up to three strategies, but then conjectured that for $n>3$ no fair solution generally exists, and Brouwer's fixed-point theorem stood by as an unaimed hammer for "each player optimal against the other." The central question stayed open: does the floor always meet the ceiling once mixing is allowed?

I propose the Minimax Theorem for finite zero-sum games: for *every* real $m\times n$ matrix $A=[g(i,j)]$, once each player chooses a mixed strategy — a probability vector $\xi\in\Delta_m$ over rows, $\eta\in\Delta_n$ over columns — and is scored by the expected payoff $\xi^\top A\eta$, the floor and ceiling coincide at a single value $v$, and there exist optimal mixes $\xi^\*,\eta^\*$ forming a saddle. The first move that makes this possible is itself the choice of randomization, and it is a genuine enlargement of freedom, not a restriction — a definite row is recovered by setting $\xi_x=1$. What randomizing buys is twofold. It defeats being found out, since against $\xi=(\tfrac12,\tfrac12)$ in Matching Pennies nobody, not even the player, knows the draw in advance, so there is nothing to read. And, quietly but decisively, it replaces the *arbitrary* function $g$ on a finite grid by the *bilinear* form $h(\xi,\eta)=\sum_{i,j}g(i,j)\xi_i\eta_j=\xi^\top A\eta$, linear in each argument separately. Enlarging the strategies made the payoff vastly more special: it now carries linearity, hence convexity, and convexity is the entire lever. Expectation is the right criterion precisely because it is the unique *linear* functional of the mix; that linearity is what converts the intractable $g$ into the tractable $\xi^\top A\eta$ (and Borel justified it directly, taking each payoff to be a win-probability so the expectation is one's ex-ante chance of winning). A first simplification falls out immediately: $\min_{\eta\in\Delta_n}\xi^\top A\eta$ is a linear function minimized over a simplex, so it is attained at a vertex, $\min_j(\xi^\top A)_j$ — the opponent, against a mix, never needs to mix back, a pure best response suffices. Hence $v_1'=\max_\xi\min_j(\xi^\top A)_j$ and $v_2'=\min_\eta\max_i(A\eta)_i$, and only one side ever randomizes at a time.

The load-bearing step is to see past Borel's wall. In a symmetric game $A$ is skew-symmetric and the value, if it exists, is $0$; Borel demanded a full-support $\xi$ with $\xi^\top A=0$ as a vector — a kernel vector — and since a skew-symmetric matrix of even order is generically nonsingular, no such vector exists for $n=4$, whence his pessimism. But that conflates two different demands. I do not need a mix that ties everything dead ($\xi^\top A\equiv 0$); I need only a mix that does no worse than zero against everything ($\xi^\top A\ge 0$ componentwise), which already guarantees me $\ge 0$. Nonnegativity is one-sided and weak where constancy is two-sided and strong, and the kernel obstruction bites only the strong version. So the right target, stripped of symmetry, is an alternative: for any $A$, *either* there is $\xi\in\Delta_m$ with $\xi^\top A\ge 0$ (so $v_1'\ge 0$), *or* there is $\eta\in\Delta_n$ with $A\eta\le 0$ (so $v_2'\le 0$) — in particular the bad straddle $v_1'<0<v_2'$ never occurs. This "either … or" about two oppositely-signed linear systems is exactly the shape of a theorem of the alternative, and such theorems come from separating a point from a convex set. So I first build the separation from the ground: for a closed convex $C\subseteq\mathbb R^N$ and $y\notin C$, let $z\in C$ be nearest to $y$; for any $u\in C$ and $t\in[0,1]$, $tu+(1-t)z\in C$, so $\|(z-y)+t(u-z)\|^2\ge\|z-y\|^2$, and expanding, dividing by $t>0$, and letting $t\to 0^+$ gives $(z-y)\cdot(u-z)\ge 0$; with $a=z-y\neq 0$ and $b=a\cdot z$ this reads $a\cdot u\ge b$ for all $u\in C$ while $a\cdot y=b-\|a\|^2<b$, a clean separating hyperplane. I deploy it with $C=\operatorname{conv}\{A_{\cdot 1},\dots,A_{\cdot n},e_1,\dots,e_m\}\subset\mathbb R^m$, the columns together with the coordinate vectors (the $e_i$ are slack that turns an equality with $0$ into a componentwise inequality), and ask whether the origin lies in $C$. If $0\in C$, then $\sum_j\alpha_j A_{\cdot j}+\sum_i\beta_i e_i=0$ with nonnegative weights summing to one; the column weight $\alpha=\sum_j\alpha_j$ must be positive, since a nonzero convex combination of coordinate vectors can never be zero, so setting $\eta_j=\alpha_j/\alpha$ gives $A\eta=-\alpha^{-1}\sum_i\beta_i e_i\le 0$, hence $v_2'\le 0$. If $0\notin C$, separation against $y=0$ gives $a$ with $a\cdot u\ge b$ on $C$ and $0<b$; then $a_i=a\cdot e_i\ge b>0$ and $a^\top A_{\cdot j}\ge b>0$ for every $j$, so $\xi_i=a_i/\sum_k a_k$ satisfies $\xi^\top A\ge 0$ and $v_1'\ge 0$. Either way, never $v_1'<0<v_2'$.

That is only the zero threshold, an accident of where the origin sits — so I slide it. Subtract a constant $w$ from every entry of $A$. Because $\xi$ and $\eta$ are distributions, $\sum_{i,j}\xi_i\eta_j=1$, so $\xi^\top(A-w)\eta=\xi^\top A\eta-w$ for *every* pair; the whole payoff surface drops by $w$ and both one-sided optima drop by $w$. Applying the alternative to the shifted game gives "never $v_1'-w<0<v_2'-w$," that is, never $v_1'<w<v_2'$, and $w$ was arbitrary. If $v_1'<v_2'$ held I could pick a $w$ strictly between them, which is forbidden; combined with the universal $v_1'\le v_2'$, this forces

$$\max_{\xi\in\Delta_m}\min_{\eta\in\Delta_n}\xi^\top A\eta \;=\; \min_{\eta\in\Delta_n}\max_{\xi\in\Delta_m}\xi^\top A\eta \;=\; v.$$

The floor meets the ceiling for every matrix, at all sizes; Borel's obstruction was simply too strong a demand. The strategies come with it: $v_1'=v$ supplies $\xi^\*$ with $\xi^{*\top}A\ge v\mathbf 1$ (player 1 secures $\ge v$ against every column, hence every mix), $v_2'=v$ supplies $\eta^\*$ with $A\eta^\*\le v\mathbf 1$ (player 2 holds him to $\le v$ against every row), and together

$$\xi^\top A\eta^\* \;\le\; v \;=\; \xi^{*\top}A\eta^\* \;\le\; \xi^{*\top}A\eta\qquad\text{for all }\xi,\eta,$$

a genuine saddle of $\xi^\top A\eta$: the circular "each optimal against the other" has a consistent solution. What this dictates about actual play is sharp. Since $v=\xi^{*\top}(A\eta^\*)$ is a weighted average of row-payoffs $(A\eta^\*)_i$ all $\le v$, an average equal to $v$ forces every positively-weighted term to equal $v$; thus $\xi^*_i>0\Rightarrow(A\eta^\*)_i=v$, and by mirror $\eta^*_j>0\Rightarrow(\xi^{*\top}A)_j=v$. Every pure strategy in the support pays exactly the value, so a player is indifferent across the rows he uses — and he randomizes not to help himself directly but to make the *opponent* indifferent across the columns the opponent uses, leaving him no lever. That is bluffing made exact, and it is complementary slackness: support of one player matches tight inequalities for the other. Indeed $v_1'=\max_\xi\min_j(\xi^\top A)_j$ is the linear program $\max t$ s.t. $A^\top\xi\ge t\mathbf 1,\ \xi\in\Delta_m$, and for any feasible $(\xi,t)$ and any $\eta$, $t\le\sum_j\eta_j(\xi^\top A)_j=\xi^\top A\eta\le\max_i(A\eta)_i$, so the tightest column bound is the LP $\min u$ s.t. $A\eta\le u\mathbf 1,\ \eta\in\Delta_n$, which is exactly $v_2'$. The minimax theorem is strong duality for this primal–dual pair; the separating hyperplane, the theorem of the alternative, the existence of a game value, and LP duality are four faces of one convexity fact. To compute the value and a saddle pair I therefore shift $A$ to a strictly positive game — using the very shift-by-$w$ invariance the proof rests on — and solve the two LPs, undoing the shift at the end.

```python
import numpy as np
from scipy.optimize import linprog

def solve_zero_sum_game(A):
    """Value v and optimal mixed strategies (xi, eta) of the finite zero-sum game A.
    Player 1 maximizes xi^T A eta; player 2 minimizes."""
    A = np.asarray(A, dtype=float); m, n = A.shape
    shift = A.min() - 1.0; B = A - shift          # shift to a positive game; value shifts by 'shift'

    # Player 1:  max v  s.t.  xi^T B >= v 1,  xi in simplex.  Vars [xi, v], maximize v.
    c = np.r_[np.zeros(m), -1.0]
    A_ub = np.hstack([-B.T, np.ones((n, 1))]); b_ub = np.zeros(n)        # v - (xi^T B)_j <= 0
    A_eq = np.hstack([np.ones((1, m)), [[0.0]]]); b_eq = [1.0]
    r1 = linprog(c, A_ub, b_ub, A_eq, b_eq, bounds=[(0, None)]*m + [(None, None)], method="highs")
    if not r1.success:
        raise RuntimeError(f"row-player LP failed: {r1.message}")
    xi, v = r1.x[:m], r1.x[-1]

    # Player 2 (dual):  min u  s.t.  B eta <= u 1,  eta in simplex.
    c2 = np.r_[np.zeros(n), 1.0]
    A_ub2 = np.hstack([B, -np.ones((m, 1))]); b_ub2 = np.zeros(m)        # (B eta)_i - u <= 0
    A_eq2 = np.hstack([np.ones((1, n)), [[0.0]]]); b_eq2 = [1.0]
    r2 = linprog(c2, A_ub2, b_ub2, A_eq2, b_eq2, bounds=[(0, None)]*n + [(None, None)], method="highs")
    if not r2.success:
        raise RuntimeError(f"column-player LP failed: {r2.message}")
    eta = r2.x[:n]
    return v + shift, xi, eta

if __name__ == "__main__":
    RPS = np.array([[0., -1., 1.], [1., 0., -1.], [-1., 1., 0.]])
    v, xi, eta = solve_zero_sum_game(RPS)
    assert abs(v) < 1e-9 and np.allclose(xi, [1/3, 1/3, 1/3], atol=1e-6)
    assert np.min(xi @ RPS) >= v - 1e-9 and np.max(RPS @ eta) <= v + 1e-9   # saddle guarantees
```
