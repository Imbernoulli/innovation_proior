I start with the discomfort that Valiant later names directly: learning looks reproducible enough to deserve a quantitative theory, but statistics alone does not explain artificial intelligence and computation alone does not explain learning from experience. I need one theory that combines both. Computability becomes a science when "calculation" receives a precise model, so I want the analogous object for acquiring a recognizer without being handed its program.

The object I can hope to make precise is not every meaning of learning. I narrow the question to recognition: there is a hidden concept, and learning means deducing a program that recognizes it. Propositional concepts are a reasonable first arena because actual expert systems already use propositional representations heavily, and because Boolean expressions let me ask a computational-complexity question about whole classes of recognizers.

The nearest rigorous neighboring work, as Valiant frames it, is inductive inference: examples are used to induce recursive functions or formal grammars. That gives me formality, but not the Boolean recognition setting and not a polynomial-time deduction guarantee for whole concept classes. Statistical pattern recognition gives me distributions and error, but it does not tell me which classes of programs can be deduced efficiently. AI concept-learning work gives examples and heuristics, but not class-wide guarantees. So the missing model has to keep the distributional idea from statistics, add explicit computational resources, and stop demanding exact recovery from feasible samples.

The first real wall is exact recovery. If I ask for certainty after only polynomially many examples, I already fail on a single monomial. Nature may draw positive examples from a distribution that hides a variable whose presence or absence matters only on rarely sampled inputs. A feasible sample cannot force the learner to distinguish all such cases. So exact recovery is not merely inconvenient; it asks for information the protocol cannot supply.

That suggests the first relaxation. I should not require the output program to be right everywhere. I should require it to be right on almost all inputs that matter under the distribution producing the examples. The same distribution that supplies natural examples should measure error. If a region has tiny probability mass, uncertainty there should count only tiny error. This does not make the theorem distribution-specific in a weak sense, because the learner must work for every fixed distribution. It makes the guarantee meaningful: the learner succeeds on the world it actually samples from, without knowing that world in advance.

The second wall is randomness itself. Even when a good approximation is possible, a finite sample can be unlucky. A guarantee with probability one is too strong. I therefore need two knobs: an accuracy tolerance, epsilon, for how much distributional error remains, and a confidence tolerance, delta, for how often the random sample may fail to produce such a hypothesis. Valiant's original notation folds these into a single parameter h, but conceptually they are separate.

Now feasibility must be part of the definition rather than an afterthought. The learner should use polynomially many examples and polynomial time, with the polynomial depending on the target size, the relevant number of variables, and the inverse tolerances. This is the bridge to complexity theory. I am not just asking whether a rule can be inferred eventually; I am asking whether a whole class of recognizers admits a feasible deduction procedure with quantified sample guarantees.

I still have to calibrate what information the learner can get. If a teacher can choose a prearranged sequence of examples, that sequence can encode the target program, and I have smuggled programming into learning. If the learner gets no typical positive examples, a concept true on one obscure vector may require exponential search. The compromise is a source of random positive examples drawn from an arbitrary fixed distribution, plus a membership oracle when a deduction procedure needs to test a specific vector. The examples supply typicality; the oracle supplies targeted checks without letting the teacher transmit the program by a code.

This gives the success criterion. A class is learnable when one deduction algorithm, for every target in the class and every distribution over its positive examples, runs in polynomial time and with probability at least 1-delta outputs a hypothesis whose error under that same distribution is at most epsilon. In the classes I can prove first, I can keep one-sided soundness: the hypothesis never accepts a vector outside the target, and its only mistakes are rejected true positives of small probability mass.

The important step is to see how random examples can drive a proof. I need an abstract progress counter. Suppose at any stage there are at most S structural repairs that can ever be made. While the current hypothesis still has large error, at least a 1/h fraction of the positive-example distribution exposes one of those repairs. Then each random example is a Bernoulli trial with success probability at least 1/h, even though what counts as success changes with the history. If I draw enough examples to make fewer than S successes unlikely, then either I have exhausted the repairs or the error has dropped below 1/h.

This is exactly what the function L(h,S) measures. It is the number of trials needed so that, when each trial succeeds with probability at least 1/h, the probability of seeing fewer than S successes is below 1/h. The Chernoff lower-tail calculation gives L(h,S) <= 2h(S+ln h). This is the engine: a linear-in-S, polynomial-in-h sample bound for any learning procedure that turns high current error into a chance of concrete progress.

Now I test the engine on conjunctions, the simplest recognizers. I start with the safest possible hypothesis: the conjunction containing every literal. This accepts essentially nothing, so it cannot make false-positive errors. Each positive example tells me which literals cannot be in the target. If the example sets x_i to 1, then not-x_i is impossible; if it sets x_i to 0, then x_i is impossible; if it leaves x_i undetermined, neither literal is forced. I delete every literal the positive example does not make true. True target literals survive forever, so the hypothesis stays more restrictive than the target and remains one-sided sound. The only remaining error comes from extra literals, and each time the rejected positive mass is large, a random positive example is likely to delete one.

The same idea scales to bounded CNF. Instead of literals, the removable objects are clauses of at most k literals. For fixed k there are fewer than (2t)^(k+1) such clauses. I initialize the hypothesis as the conjunction of all of them. A positive example cannot violate any clause that truly belongs to the target, so every violated clause is safe to delete. If the current hypothesis still rejects more than a 1/h mass of true positives, then the next example lands in that rejected region with probability greater than 1/h and deletes at least one clause. Since fewer than (2t)^(k+1) deletions are possible, L(h,(2t)^(k+1)) examples suffice with failure probability below 1/h. For fixed k this is polynomial. A conjunction is just the k=1 case.

I can cross-check this with the modern finite-hypothesis argument. If a finite hypothesis class H is searched for a hypothesis consistent with the sample, then a bad hypothesis with error greater than epsilon survives m independent examples with probability at most (1-epsilon)^m <= exp(-epsilon m). Union-bound over H and choose m >= (1/epsilon)(ln |H| + ln(1/delta)). For monotone conjunctions |H|=2^n; for general conjunctions |H|=3^n. This gives the same basic scaling and confirms that the deletion algorithm is doing the expected sample-complexity work.

DNF shows where the structure matters. Human-readable concepts often look like disjunctions of conjunctions, but unrestricted DNF over partial vectors hides a hard test: asking whether an undetermined vector forces a DNF formula includes the tautology problem. Cook's theorem-proving paper is the background for treating tautology and DNF tautology as hard under polynomial reductions. So I should not pretend the general concept version is easy.

For monotone DNF, however, I can run the progress engine in the opposite direction. I start with the false hypothesis, again safely one-sided because it accepts nothing. When a positive example is not yet accepted, it points to missing mass. I use the oracle to strip away inessential positive coordinates until the remaining vector is a prime implicant of the target. I add that monomial to the hypothesis. Each added monomial is sound, each is new, and there are at most d prime implicants in the monotone prime DNF. While the hypothesis misses more than a 1/h mass of positives, a fresh example reveals a missing implicant with probability greater than 1/h. Thus L(h,d) examples and at most dt oracle calls give the guarantee.

The definition and the algorithms now fit together. The model is "probably" because finite random samples can be unlucky. It is "approximately correct" because exact recovery is information-theoretically too much to ask from feasible samples under arbitrary distributions. It is computational learning because both examples and deduction time are polynomially bounded. The deepest move is that learning becomes a progress-counting theorem over random examples: high error is not just a defect, it is probability mass that makes the next example useful. That is what turns vague induction into a complexity-theoretic boundary, with simple Boolean classes inside the boundary and cryptographic or tautology-based obstacles showing why the boundary is real.
