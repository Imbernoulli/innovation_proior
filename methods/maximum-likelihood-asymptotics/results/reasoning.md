I start by refusing to treat a likelihood maximizer as automatically trustworthy. A global maximum can chase a single-observation spike, a shifted support endpoint, a false faraway peak, or an incidental nuisance feature. It may even fail to exist as an attained value, depend on the chosen version of the density, or improve after information is deliberately discarded. If I want a regular estimator, I first need a reason that the optimizer enters the right neighborhood. I get that reason from the population log-likelihood contrast: under correct specification and identifiability, the expected log density is separated from its value at the truth by a Kullback-Leibler loss. A uniform law of large numbers then transfers this separation to the sample criterion, so a well-behaved maximizer has to localize near `theta_0`.

Once I have localization, I stop thinking of the estimator as a hard global optimizer and begin thinking of the log likelihood as a local stochastic surface. I write nearby parameters as `theta_0 + h / sqrt(n)`, because the derivative of a sum fluctuates on the `sqrt(n)` scale and a stable curvature term grows on the `n` scale. In that coordinate, the local expansion I need is `ell_n(theta_0 + h / sqrt(n)) - ell_n(theta_0) = h^T Delta_n - (1/2) h^T I(theta_0) h + o_p(1)`, uniformly for `h` in each fixed compact set, where `Delta_n = n^{-1/2} dot ell_n(theta_0)`. This is the point where the distinctive structure appears: the local likelihood is not merely smooth; it is a random linear tilt plus a deterministic quadratic penalty.

I then identify the two parts of that quadratic picture. The linear term is the normalized score. At the true parameter, the individual score has mean zero, and its covariance is Fisher information, so the central limit theorem gives `Delta_n -> N(0, I(theta_0))`. The quadratic term is curvature. Under the same regularity that lets me differentiate and average the Hessian, `n^{-1} ddot ell_n(theta_0)` converges to `-I(theta_0)`. Fisher information therefore has two jobs at once: it is the covariance of the random tangent force, and it is the positive curvature scale that appears after I put the minus sign on the Hessian.

Now the estimator's limit is forced by geometry, provided the local maximizer is tight. The limiting local objective is `h^T Delta - (1/2) h^T I h`, and I maximize it by solving `Delta - I h = 0`. The local optimizer is `h = I^{-1} Delta`. Since `Delta` is Gaussian with covariance `I`, the curvature-scaled displacement is Gaussian with covariance `I^{-1}`. Translating from `h` back to `theta`, I obtain `sqrt(n)(hat theta_n - theta_0) -> N(0, I(theta_0)^{-1})`.

This also explains why the usual score-equation proof works. I expand `dot ell_n(hat theta_n)` around `theta_0`; the left side is zero at an interior optimizer, the first term is the random score, and the derivative term is the Hessian multiplying `hat theta_n - theta_0`. After scaling, the equation reads `0 = Delta_n - I(theta_0) sqrt(n)(hat theta_n - theta_0) + o_p(1)`. The proof is therefore not a separate trick; it is the differential form of the same local quadratic approximation.

I use the local Gaussian-shift viewpoint to interpret efficiency. Once likelihood ratios in shrinking neighborhoods behave like those of a Gaussian shift experiment, lower bounds from the limit experiment apply back to the original sequence of experiments. A regular estimator cannot improve on the inverse-information covariance in that local first-order sense, while the localized likelihood maximizer attains it. The word `regular` matters here: the conclusion is inherited from the Gaussian local experiment, not from the act of maximizing alone.

I keep the caveats inside the result rather than after it. If the optimizer is not localized, the quadratic neighborhood may be irrelevant. If the score has no Gaussian limit, the linear term changes. If the information matrix is singular or nearly flat, the curvature no longer converts score noise into a stable displacement. If parameters sit on a boundary, if dimension grows through nuisance parameters, or if the model is singular, the local shape can be one-sided, nonquadratic, or centered at the wrong target. If a coordinate choice makes an ordinary Wald quadratic misleading, the apparent uncertainty can be an artifact; the likelihood-ratio or Hellinger geometry is the safer object. The scientific insight is therefore conditional and geometric: maximizing likelihood becomes a regular estimator when global separation gets me to the truth and local likelihood ratios become a curvature-controlled Gaussian quadratic.
