Saying that a language $L$ is in NP is the same as saying $L$ has an efficient proof system: an unbounded prover writes a string $\alpha$, and a verifier running in time polynomial in $|x|$ checks $x$ against $\alpha$ and accepts iff $x \in L$. That is the standard picture of communicating a proof — send a satisfying assignment to prove a formula satisfiable, send a tour to prove a graph Hamiltonian. But these proofs over-deliver. I asked to be convinced of a single bit, "$x \in L$", and I walked away holding the witness — an actual satisfying assignment I might not have been able to find myself in polynomial time, the very thing that was hard to know. The proof leaks exactly the secret. And a static NP proof cannot do otherwise: whatever string the verifier checks and accepts is, by construction, a re-checkable certificate, so it cannot be hidden. The applications are cryptographic — a party must often convince another of a fact ("this number is a product of two primes," "I followed the protocol") without surrendering the secret that makes the fact true — so the question is whether a proof can convey only that the statement is true and nothing more.

The first obstacle is that I cannot even say "nothing more." Shannon information is the wrong yardstick: if I simply tell you a true bit you receive a bit and that is exactly what you came for, while a gigabyte of random noise carries enormous information and teaches nothing. The model-theoretic notion is also wrong: an agent that knows every logical consequence of what it hears "already knew" $x \in L$, yet that agent has unbounded deductive power, and the danger I worry about — being handed a satisfying assignment — is dangerous precisely because the real verifier is computationally limited and could not have found that assignment itself. That limitation is the hinge. Knowledge must be computational and resource-bounded: the verifier learns nothing extra if everything it sees in the interaction is something it could have produced for itself, in polynomial time, given only the bare fact $x \in L$. The prior probabilistic generalizations of proof do not reach this. Arthur–Merlin games add interaction and randomness but publish the verifier's coins to the prover, which is enough to recognize hard languages but leaves the verifier no secret and offers no handle on how much the prover reveals. Blum's and Goldwasser–Micali's membership protocols already convince a verifier that an integer has special structure without sending its factorization, and under the hardness of factoring are believed not to give it away — but "does not reveal the factorization, in any obvious way" is informal, with no quantification over cheating verifiers and no proof that nothing beyond membership is released. Semantic security for encryption supplies the right template — "whatever the adversary computes from the ciphertext it could compute without it," made rigorous by indistinguishability of distributions to a bounded judge — but it is built for a one-way transfer of a single hidden message against a passive line-tapper, not a multi-round interaction with an actively misbehaving party.

I propose zero-knowledge interactive proofs, built on the simulation paradigm. The setting is an interactive proof system $(A,B)$: an unbounded prover $A$ and a verifier $B$ that is probabilistic and polynomial-time in $|x|$, sharing the input $x$, each reading a private random tape, exchanging messages in turns until $B$ accepts or rejects. It is a proof system for $L$ if it is complete — for $x \in L$ the honest prover makes $B$ accept with probability at least $1 - |x|^{-k}$ for every constant $k$ and all long $x$ — and sound — for $x \notin L$ every prover strategy $A'$ of arbitrary power makes $B$ accept with probability at most $|x|^{-k}$, an error driven down to $2^{-|x|}$ by repetition. Convincing the verifier without leaking is possible only because of interaction plus secret randomness: the verifier becomes convinced not by holding a certificate but by watching the prover pass random challenges that a false statement would let it survive only by luck, so no single transcript is a re-checkable proof.

The definition of releasing no knowledge is the real contribution. After a run, the complete record of the verifier's experience is its view — its own coin tosses together with every message it received, in order — and because the parties flip coins this view is a probability distribution, not a fixed string. I define $(A,B)$ to be zero-knowledge if for every polynomial-time, possibly cheating verifier $B'$, holding any auxiliary input $H$, there is an efficient simulator $M$ that, with no prover in the room, outputs a distribution indistinguishable from $B'$'s real view $\mathrm{View}_{A,B'}(x,H)$. If the view is reproducible without the prover, then whatever the verifier could compute after the interaction it could already compute before — first simulate the view, then compute — so the interaction is informationally idle. Several design choices are load-bearing and each beats the obvious alternative. The quantification must range over every cheating $B'$, not merely the honest verifier, because a real adversary picks its challenges to maximize what it extracts; this makes zero-knowledge a property of the prover $A$ alone against all $B'$, the mirror image of soundness, which depends only on $B$. Each $B'$ must be handed its side information $H$ and the simulator must receive $H$ too, demanding that $M(x,H)$ approximate $\mathrm{View}_{A,B'}(x,H)$ — without this the definition would not compose, since running the protocol twice gives the second verifier the first transcript as history. "Indistinguishable" comes in three graded strengths, borrowed wholesale from semantic-secure encryption: the simulator's distribution may be literally equal to the view (perfect), within total variation distance $\sum_\alpha |\Pr[U(x){=}\alpha] - \Pr[V(x){=}\alpha]| < |x|^{-c}$ for every $c$ (statistical, against an infinite-time judge with polynomially many samples), or indistinguishable to every nonuniform poly-size circuit family with $|\Pr[C_x(U(x)){=}1] - \Pr[C_x(V(x)){=}1]| < |x|^{-c}$ (computational). The judge is nonuniform on purpose, so that arbitrary side information about $x$ or protocol history can be wired in and "indistinguishable" survives it. The view must include the verifier's coins, not just the message text, or the definition misses real leaks: for $L$ = composites, $B$ picks random $x \in Z^*_n$ and sends $a = x^2 \bmod n$, and $A$ returns a random square root $y$ of $a$; the text $(a,y)$ is trivially simulatable by picking $y$ and setting $a = y^2$, but with $x$ in the view, $y \neq \pm x$ with probability at least $\tfrac{1}{2}$, and then $\gcd(x+y,n)$ is a nontrivial factor of $n$ — the view leaks the factorization, which the text-only version would falsely hide. Finally the simulator is allowed expected rather than strict polynomial time, because reproducing a rare-event message forces rejection sampling.

To show the definition is not vacuous I prove it on quadratic residuosity, where hiding has teeth. With $Z^*_x = \{y : 1 \le y < x,\ \gcd(x,y)=1\}$ and $Q_x(y) = 0$ when $y$ is a quadratic residue (QR) and $1$ otherwise, the Jacobi symbol $(y/x)$ is poly-time computable without the factorization and settles the easy case $(y/x) = -1 \Rightarrow Q_x(y) = 1$, but the case $(y/x) = +1$ is the quadratic residuosity problem, conjectured as hard as factoring. So $QR = \{(x,y) : Q_x(y)=0\}$ and $QNR = \{(x,y) : y \in Z^*_x,\ (y/x){=}{+}1,\ Q_x(y){=}1\}$ are in $\mathrm{NP} \cap \mathrm{co\text{-}NP}$ but not known in BPP, and the obvious NP proof would send the factorization or a square root — exactly the secrets to protect. Two algebraic facts carry the protocol: every residue has equally many square roots, and residues are closed under multiplication and inverse so that a residue times a nonresidue is a nonresidue. To prove $(x,y) \in QR$, repeat $m = |x|$ times: $A$ sends a random residue $u = s^2$ (knowing the root $s$); $B$ sends a random bit; if $\mathit{bit}=0$, $A$ returns a random root of $u$, and if $\mathit{bit}=1$, a random root of $uy$ (which exists because $y$ is a residue so $uy$ is); $B$ accepts the round iff $w^2 \equiv u$ or $w^2 \equiv uy$ respectively, and accepts iff all $m$ rounds pass. Completeness holds with probability $1$, since $u$ and $uy$ are both residues so $A$ always has the demanded root. Soundness is at most $2^{-m}$: if $y$ is a nonresidue then $u$ and $uy$ cannot both be residues — otherwise $y = (uy)u^{-1}$ would be one — so at most one challenge is answerable, and since $A'$ must commit to $u$ before seeing the fair coin, the unanswerable challenge is hit with probability at least $\tfrac{1}{2}$ per round.

What makes the protocol zero-knowledge is rewinding, and the proof here is perfect — the simulator reproduces the view identically. The real prover sends $u$ first and answers after the challenge, which it can do because it secretly knows a root of $y$; the simulator has no such root, so it reverses the order, choosing the answer first and deriving a matching question. For each round it guesses a bit $\mathit{bit}'$ and a root $w'$, then sets $u' = (w')^2$ if $\mathit{bit}'=0$ and $u' = (w')^2 y^{-1}$ if $\mathit{bit}'=1$ — so that $w'$ is genuinely a root of $u'$ in the first branch and of $u'y$ in the second — feeds $u'$ to $B'$, and keeps the round only if $B'$'s actual challenge $f(x,y,H,\text{history},u')$ equals the guess, otherwise rewinding $B'$ and retrying. This is correct because in both branches $u'$ is a uniformly random residue: $w' \mapsto (w')^2$ is uniform over residues since every residue has equally many roots, and multiplying by the fixed unit $y^{-1}$ — which permutes residues bijectively, as $y$ is itself a residue here — keeps it uniform. Since $u'$ has the same distribution regardless of the guessed branch, $B'$'s challenge $f(\dots,u')$ is independent of $\mathit{bit}'$; the challenge need not be balanced, but the independent fair guess matches it with probability exactly $\tfrac{1}{2}$, so the loop is a geometric trial halting in two iterations in expectation, and conditioned on a match the triple $(u',\mathit{bit}',w')$ has exactly the real round distribution. The same coin shows two faces: a fresh challenge the prover cannot anticipate forces soundness, while a challenge the simulator can guess-and-retry — because it controls $B'$'s coins in its head and may take unlimited do-overs — gives zero-knowledge.

The companion language $QNR$ asks the prover to certify a nonresidue, which has no root to reveal, so I switch the prover's job from revealing a root to classifying samples. $B$ generates elements $w$ of two types — type $1$, $w = r^2$, a residue, and type $2$, $w = r^2 y$ — and the unbounded $A$ tells the type by testing residuosity; if $(x,y) \notin QNR$ then $y$ is a residue, both types are residues and identically distributed, and $A$ guesses correctly with probability at most $\tfrac{1}{2}$ per round, giving soundness $2^{-m}$. The danger is that an arbitrary $w$ would turn $A$ into a residuosity oracle of $B$'s choosing, so $B$ must prove for each $w$ that it knows the type — a root of $w$ (type $1$) or of $wy^{-1}$ (type $2$) — without revealing which, via a cut-and-choose over randomly ordered pairs each containing one value $r_{j1}^2$ and one value $r_{j2}^2 y$: on challenge $i_j=0$, $B$ opens the pair to show well-formedness, and on $i_j=1$, $B$ reveals one root showing that $w$ times one component is a residue, the random ordering hiding the type bit. The simulator must output the correct $\mathit{answer} = Q_x(w)$ without unbounded power, which it does by finding a second special string $i' \neq i$ that $B'$ also answers: at a coordinate where they differ, the $i_j=0$ opening exposes roots witnessing the pair's contents and the $i'_j=1$ opening exposes a value $v'$ with $(v')^2 w^{-1}$ equal to one component, so either $(v'/s)^2 = w$ proving $w$ a residue or $(v'/t)^2 = wy$ proving $w = (v'/t)^2 y^{-1}$ a nonresidue — $Q_x(w)$ pinned down in polynomial time without factoring. $M$ rejection-samples for $i'$; with $k$ special strings the second-string search takes expected $2^m/(k-1)$ tries when $k \ge 2$, with unconditional contribution polynomial because the first $i$ is special with probability $k/2^m$, and the only failure is the unique-special case $k=1$ of probability at most $1/2^m$, giving statistical distance at most $2m/2^m$ over $m$ iterations — statistical zero-knowledge, made perfect by spending the already-budgeted $\approx 2^m$ time on factoring in that lone case. Together $QR$ (perfectly) and $QNR$ (statistically) are the first zero-knowledge proofs for languages not known to be efficiently recognizable, witnessing that a proof can convey only the validity of its statement.

The core artifact is the rewinding simulator that establishes perfect zero-knowledge for $QR$. For any poly-time $B'$ whose challenge is a deterministic $\mathit{bit} = f(x,y,H,\text{history},u)$, it reproduces the view identically in expected polynomial time:

```
for each of the m rounds:
    DO FOREVER:                       # rewinding; expected 2 iterations
        bit' := random in {0,1}       # guess the challenge first
        w'   := random in Z*_x        # the root to reveal first
        if bit' = 0: u' := w'^2  mod x            # u' a random residue, w' a root of u'
        else:        u' := w'^2 * y^{-1} mod x    # u'*y = w'^2, w' a root of u'y; u' a random residue
        if f(x,y,H,view,u') = bit':   # B''s real challenge match the guess?
            append (u', bit', w'); break          # keep round; discard+rewind otherwise
```
