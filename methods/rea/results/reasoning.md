Let me start from what actually hurts. I want to discover a convolutional image classifier automatically — one that's as good as the ones humans have spent years hand-tuning, ResNet and Inception and the rest. There's a method that already gets the best numbers: reinforcement learning, an LSTM controller that emits an architecture token by token and gets nudged by policy gradient toward the architectures that scored well. It works. But every time I look at it I see the same thing — it's heavy. I'm training a whole second neural network, the controller, by backprop; I'm carrying a learning-rate schedule for it, a greediness knob, batching, a replay buffer, on top of all the hyperparameters of the classifiers I'm actually searching for. People have shown that two implementations of the same RL algorithm give different answers. And the controller update ties my workers together, which fights the one thing I desperately need here: clean parallelism, because every single candidate has to be trained from scratch before I even know if it's any good, and that training is the entire cost of the enterprise. So I keep coming back to the simplest possible alternative: evolution. Keep a population of trained models, copy the good ones, mutate them, throw away the bad ones. No controller, no backprop-through-anything, nothing to tie the workers together. The catch is the field's standing belief — evolved image classifiers have never actually beaten hand-designs on real images. People who tried it got close on CIFAR and stalled. So the question isn't "should I use evolution," it's "what is the *one thing* wrong with plain evolution that keeps it just short, and can I fix it without piling on complexity."

So let me build plain evolution and watch where it breaks. The skeleton is the steady-state tournament loop. I keep P trained models. Each cycle I run a tournament: sample S of them at random, take the highest-accuracy one as the parent, mutate it into a child, train and evaluate the child, add it to the population. To keep the population at size P I have to remove someone, and the obvious, textbook thing — the thing the large-scale-evolution work just before this did — is to remove the *worst* model in the tournament sample. Survival of the fittest, literally: the loser of each little tournament dies, the population's average quality ratchets up. It's clean, it's asynchronous (one model in, one model out, workers never wait on a generation boundary the way NEAT does), and the parent selection gives me a tunable amount of greed.

Let me make that greed precise, because S is the only real knob and I want to know what it controls. There's the old population-dynamics calculation: write the birth-life-death equation for the count of the single best class, m_{t+1} = m_t + births − deaths, and ask how long until that best individual, starting from one copy, takes over the whole population of size n. For an s-ary tournament with s > 1, the takeover time comes out as roughly t* ≈ (1/ln s)·[ln n + ln(ln n)]. The dependence on s is entirely through the 1/ln s prefactor; everything else in the bracket is fixed by n. So let me just put numbers in for, say, n = 100. t*(s=2) = (1/ln 2)(ln 100 + ln ln 100) = (1/0.693)(4.605 + 1.527) ≈ 8.85 generations; t*(s=4) = (1/ln 4)(same bracket) = (1/1.386)(6.132) ≈ 4.42. The ratio is 4.42/8.85 = 0.500 — bang on a half, and it's a half for any n, because the bracket cancels and ln 4 / ln 2 = 2 exactly. So doubling the tournament from 2 to 4 literally halves the takeover time: the best model floods the population twice as fast. S is therefore a clean dial on selective pressure — S=1 is no selection at all, pure random search, and as S climbs toward P the search gets greedier and converges faster. Good. I have a simple, parallel loop with one interpretable knob. And it doesn't beat hand-designs. Why not?

I can't ignore that train_and_eval is *noisy*. I train a network with random initialization, random data ordering, dropout-style stochastic regularization — re-train the *exact same architecture* and I get a different validation accuracy, with a spread of around a percent in these experiments. So the "accuracy" I'm selecting on isn't the architecture's true quality; it's the true quality plus a noise term. And now I see the trap, and it's specifically a trap of the kill-the-worst rule. Suppose some mediocre architecture gets trained on a lucky day and posts an accuracy a full noise-width above where it deserves. Under kill-the-worst, what happens to it? It's *high* accuracy, so it never loses a tournament — it's never the worst. It just sits there. And because it sits there at a high (lucky) score, it keeps getting picked as the parent, so it spawns child after child, and the whole search zooms in around this one model that was never actually that good. The population collapses onto a lucky fluctuation. That's premature convergence, and it's *caused* by the very rule that's supposed to help: rewarding high observed accuracy with permanent residency means rewarding noise with permanent residency. The longer a model can live by being good-looking, the more the search overfits to the luck in its single evaluation.

So the disease has a name now: a model that scores high *once* can persist indefinitely and dominate, and persistence-by-observed-score is persistence-by-luck. Let me try the within-frame patches first, because I'd rather not invent anything. Patch one: lower S, less greedy, more exploration. But that just slows the takeover; the lucky model still never dies, it just takes longer to flood. I'm trading speed for the same eventual collapse, and I lose the early-search quality I care about. Patch two: re-evaluate models, average out the noise. But every evaluation is a full training run — the entire budget *is* evaluations — so re-evaluating to denoise means roughly halving how many distinct architectures I can afford to try. Far too expensive. Patch three: keep the kill-the-worst rule but cap how many children a model may produce. Now I've added a meta-parameter (the cap) and a counter per model, and I'm back to complexity, which is the thing I'm trying to escape. Each patch either doesn't address the root cause — *a good-looking model lives forever* — or buys the fix with complexity I won't pay.

Let me look straight at the root cause instead of patching around it. The problem is the coupling between *observed score* and *lifespan*: score high, live long, reproduce a lot. What if I just cut that coupling entirely? What if a model's lifespan had *nothing to do with its accuracy*? Concretely: instead of removing the worst model each cycle, remove the *oldest* one — the model that was trained earliest. Give every model an age; the newest child goes in, the most senior model comes out, no matter how good either one is. Now every model has the same fixed maximum lifespan, on the order of P cycles, and then it's gone. The best model in the population doesn't get to homestead. It will be evicted by age in a bounded number of steps regardless of how spectacular its score was.

My first reaction is that this should be *worse*. I'm deliberately throwing away good models on a schedule — that sounds like throwing away the whole point of selection. But let me actually trace what happens to a high-accuracy *architecture* under this rule, not a high-accuracy *model*, because the distinction is suddenly load-bearing. A model is one trained instance, an architecture plus the luck of its one training run. Under kill-the-oldest, that instance is doomed — it ages out soon. So how can a *good architecture* survive in the population for any length of time? Only one way: it has to be re-created. A model carrying that architecture has to be selected as a parent, and produce a child that *also* carries (most of) that architecture — and that child is a *fresh* model, freshly trained, with its own independent noise draw. For the architecture to persist across the generations, it has to keep winning tournaments as it gets re-instantiated, which means it has to keep posting good accuracy *on independent re-trainings*.

A model that was lucky once cannot ride that single lucky score to immortality anymore, because age evicts it; to leave descendants it must be re-trained, and on the re-training the luck is gone — a one-time fluctuation re-trains back down to its true mediocre level, its child doesn't win the next tournament, and the lineage dies out. The *only* architectures that can stay in the population for a long time are the ones that score well *repeatedly*, on independent training runs — the ones whose high accuracy is a property of the architecture and not of one lucky evaluation. Kill-the-oldest doesn't throw away signal; it throws away the *noise-riders* and keeps the architectures that survive re-evaluation. The forced turnover is a filter for robustness to the training noise.

Now I can see why "remove the worst" felt safe and was actually the leak. Under kill-the-worst, the way to persist is to have a high *observed* score — which a lucky model has — so the population accumulates whatever was lucky when it trained. Under kill-the-oldest, the way to persist is to be re-discoverable — to re-train well, repeatedly — so the population accumulates whatever is *genuinely* good. I'm injecting a piece of prior knowledge into the search: I *want* architectures that re-train well, and I'm constraining the survivors to exactly those. Constraining the solution set to suppress fitting to noise — that's regularization, in the plain mathematical sense of the word. So I'll call this aging evolution, or regularized evolution: the age-based eviction *is* the regularizer.

I want to convince myself it's not just a story, so let me strip the architecture search away entirely and keep only the part I claim matters — the noise — and see if aging still wins. I'll build a toy where evaluation is trivial except for noise and actually run it, because right now this is a plausible argument and I've talked myself into plausible arguments before. Take the vertices of a D-dimensional unit cube; a "model" is just a bitstring of D bits, and its "accuracy" is the fraction of coordinates matching one fixed target corner plus a small Gaussian, mean 0, standard deviation 0.01 — tuned to match the noise I actually see training neural nets. There's a single optimum, no local minima, no real landscape — the *only* difficulty is that the fitness you read off is corrupted by noise. Mutation flips one random bit. I run both rules identically — same P = 100, same S = 10, same budget of 1000 evaluations, same kill-the-worst meaning Real's "remove the worst of the tournament sample" — and the only difference is whether each cycle evicts the leftmost (oldest) or that worst model.

The first thing I have to get right is *what to measure*, and I nearly fooled myself. My instinct was "mean accuracy of the final population," but accuracy is the noisy observed score, and that's circular — kill-the-worst is precisely the rule that hoards high *observed* scores, so of course its surviving population reads high. When I scored it that way the numbers actually favored kill-the-worst at small D, which for a second looked like my whole thesis was wrong. But that's measuring the symptom as if it were the cure. What I care about is the *true* quality of the architecture the search hands me — so I score the single best-observed model in history by its *denoised* fitness (the exact bit-fraction, no noise), which is what its accuracy would converge to if I could re-train it forever. With that honest metric, the run (50 repeats per cell) gives:

```
D=  10   kill-oldest 1.000   kill-worst 1.000   gap +0.000
D=  50   kill-oldest 0.982   kill-worst 0.989   gap -0.007
D= 100   kill-oldest 0.892   kill-worst 0.889   gap +0.003
D= 250   kill-oldest 0.679   kill-worst 0.667   gap +0.012
D= 500   kill-oldest 0.590   kill-worst 0.571   gap +0.019
```

So the prediction holds, and not because I assumed it: at D=10 both rules just solve the problem (the signal is so strong per bit that noise of 0.01 can't mislead anyone — they tie at the optimum). Around D=50 they're within a noise-width of each other and it even flickers the wrong way once. Then from D≈100 onward the gap turns positive and *grows* with D — exactly where I argued it should, because as the dimension rises the per-bit signal (1/D) shrinks toward the fixed 0.01 noise floor, a single lucky evaluation gets more dangerous, and that is precisely the regime aging is built for. The gap widening as the problem gets noisier-relative-to-signal is the fingerprint I wanted: this mechanism is about noise, not about convnets, and the toy has nothing to do with convnets. The two near-ties at the easy end are reassuring too — aging isn't a free lunch I'm imagining everywhere; it pays off specifically when the noise is the bottleneck, which is the only thing I claimed for it.

I should also make sure I'm not sneaking in complexity through the back door, because simplicity is half the point. There's an older idea that uses "age" too — assigning ages to genes and splitting the population into protected age-layers so new genes aren't instantly out-competed. But that needs two extra meta-parameters, the age-gap and the number of layers, and a layered population structure. My version needs none of that. Age here attaches to *individuals*, and it's used for exactly one thing: identifying who is oldest so I can evict them. The population stays exactly size P by the trivial rule "append the new one on the right, drop the oldest on the left." No new meta-parameter at all — I still only have P and S, the same two knobs as plain tournament evolution. That's the version I want: it's *less* machinery than the alternatives, not more, and it stays nature-like — the young are less likely to die than the very old.

Let me also pin down why the rest of the loop stays as bare as it is, because every piece I *don't* add is a piece I don't have to tune. No recombination, no crossover — mutation alone, because there's no obviously meaningful way to splice two of these cell graphs together, and single mutations already reach the entire space, so crossover buys complexity for no clear gain. No fitness sharing or explicit diversity machinery — the age-based turnover already supplies the diversity, so I don't bolt on a second mechanism. Tournament selection rather than picking the global best each cycle, because the tournament is O(1) on a small sample and reads the population *locally* — perfect for asynchronous workers each grabbing a random handful — whereas "scan the whole population for the global best" forces a global read and is maximally greedy. I sample the S candidates *with* replacement and leave them in the population; it's simpler and matches how a distributed worker would just read S random entries. And the loop is steady-state, one-in-one-out, not generational: in a real run each model trains on its own machine and they finish at wildly different times, so a generational barrier would leave fast machines idle waiting for the slow ones — the queue keeps every worker busy.

Now the mutations, which have to match whatever search space I'm in. The space I care about fixes the outer network — a stack of cells, normal cells that preserve resolution and reduction cells that halve it — and asks me to design only the cell. A cell takes two input hidden states, "0" and "1" (the outputs of the previous two cells), and then builds up through exactly five *pairwise combinations*. Each combination picks two existing hidden states, applies an op to each, and *adds* the two results to make a new hidden state, which later combinations can then use; whatever hidden states are never consumed get concatenated into the cell's output. So an architecture is fully described by ten such combinations — five for the normal cell, five for the reduction cell — each combination being (hidden-state, op) for its two inputs. I want the *minimal* set of mutations that can still reach every point in this space. Two suffice. A hidden-state mutation: pick the normal or the reduction cell, pick one of its five combinations, pick one of the two inputs of that combination, and rewire it to a different existing hidden state in the cell — with the one constraint that I can't create a loop, since the net must stay feed-forward. An op mutation: same choices of cell / combination / input, but instead of the wiring I swap the op for a different one from the fixed menu. Any architecture is reachable from any other by a chain of single rewires and single relabels, so these two cover the whole space. I'll also allow an identity mutation — change nothing — at a small fixed probability, untuned, mostly so a parent occasionally gets re-evaluated unchanged (a free re-draw of the training noise on the same architecture). Each cycle applies exactly one mutation: identity with that small fixed probability, otherwise hidden-state and op mutations split the remaining probability uniformly. That's the entire exploration mechanism: random mutation provides exploration, parent selection provides exploitation, and the two are completely decoupled.

Let me write the whole thing out, because it has to be trivially droppable into the search harness I already have, where train_and_eval is the black box that builds, trains, and evaluates an architecture and hands back a noisy validation accuracy. The population wants to be a structure where "append newest" and "pop oldest" are both cheap and where age is just position — a FIFO queue. A double-ended queue gives me exactly that: append on the right is the newest, pop on the left is the oldest, so age is encoded for free by position and I never store an age field at all.

```python
import collections
import random


class Model:
    """One evaluated candidate: its genome and its noisy fitness.
    'Age' is not stored — it's just the model's position in the FIFO queue."""
    def __init__(self):
        self.arch = None        # the cell genome (5 normal-cell + 5 reduction-cell blocks)
        self.accuracy = None    # noisy validation accuracy from train_and_eval


def regularized_evolution(cycles, population_size, sample_size):
    """Aging / regularized evolution.

    population: a FIFO queue; rightmost = newest, leftmost = oldest.
    cycles:          total models to evaluate (the whole budget).
    population_size: P, models kept alive at once.
    sample_size:     S, tournament size; the selective-pressure knob.
    """
    population = collections.deque()
    history = []                                    # every model ever made (for reporting)

    # Initialize: fill the population with random, evaluated models.
    while len(population) < population_size:
        model = Model()
        model.arch = random_architecture()          # uniform over the search space
        model.accuracy = train_and_eval(model.arch) # the expensive, noisy step
        population.append(model)
        history.append(model)

    # Evolve in steady state: each cycle makes one model and evicts one.
    while len(history) < cycles:
        # Tournament: S random members (with replacement); the population is untouched.
        sample = [random.choice(list(population)) for _ in range(sample_size)]
        parent = max(sample, key=lambda m: m.accuracy)   # exploitation: best of the sample

        child = Model()
        child.arch = mutate(parent.arch)            # exploration: one random change
        child.accuracy = train_and_eval(child.arch) # fresh, independent noise draw
        population.append(child)                     # newest goes on the right
        history.append(child)

        population.popleft()                         # REGULARIZER: evict the OLDEST,
                                                     # not the worst — lifespan is fixed,
                                                     # independent of accuracy.

    return max(history, key=lambda m: m.accuracy)    # best architecture ever seen


def mutate(parent_arch):
    """One random change to the cell genome: rewire a hidden-state input,
    relabel an op, or do nothing — chosen uniformly each cycle."""
    r = random.random()
    if r < 0.05:
        return clone(parent_arch)                    # identity: re-evaluate unchanged
    elif random.random() < 0.5:
        return hidden_state_mutation(parent_arch)    # rewire one input (no loops)
    else:
        return op_mutation(parent_arch)              # swap one op from the fixed menu
```

The one line that carries the whole idea is `population.popleft()` — evict by age, not by score — and everything else is deliberately the plainest tournament-evolution loop I could write. Let me re-trace the causal chain to be sure it holds. I want a simple, parallel search that beats hand-designs; RL gets the numbers but is complex and hard to parallelize, so I go to evolution; plain tournament evolution is simple and parallel but stalls short of hand-designs. The reason it stalls is that training accuracy is noisy, and the standard kill-the-worst rule rewards high *observed* accuracy with long life, so a model that got lucky once persists, reproduces, and the search collapses onto a fluctuation — overfitting to the evaluation noise. The within-frame patches (smaller S, re-evaluation, child caps) either don't touch that root coupling or pay for it with cost or extra knobs. So I cut the coupling between score and lifespan directly: evict the oldest model every cycle, giving every model a fixed bounded lifespan. Now an architecture can only persist by being re-instantiated through parent→child inheritance, and each re-instantiation is a fresh training with fresh noise, so only architectures that re-train well repeatedly survive — the noise-riders are filtered out. That constraint, "survivors must re-train well," is prior information injected to suppress fitting to noise, i.e. regularization, which is why it's *regularized* evolution. It needs no new meta-parameter (age is just queue position), keeps the same two knobs P and S with S controlling selective pressure through the takeover time, and stays steady-state and asynchronous so workers never idle. The mutations are the minimal pair — rewire a hidden state, relabel an op — that reach the whole cell space. And the toy unit-cube experiment, where the only difficulty is noise, came out as the mechanism predicts when I actually ran it: the two rules tie when the signal is strong (D=10), and aging pulls measurably ahead — a gap that widens with D — once the per-bit signal shrinks toward the noise floor and lucky evaluations turn dangerous. That's the cleanest confirmation I can get that the eviction-by-age rule is doing what I think it's doing, with no convnets anywhere in sight.
