I approached this as a case study in the comparative method with register tonogenesis. The task gives a reconstructed ancestor, Proto-Chamic, and two daughter languages, Phan Rang Cham and Tsat, and asks me to push the ancestor forward rather than reconstruct it backward. That means every law I write has to be a clean function of the starred Proto-Chamic form, and the same engine has to explain both the given data and the missing blanks.

The first thing that stands out is that Phan Rang Cham is the conservative daughter. Words stay mostly disyllabic, the segmental shape is preserved, and the main drama is a set of regular consonant changes plus the birth of a single low tone. Tsat, by contrast, has reduced most words to their final syllable, merged final stops into a glottal stop, dropped final *h, and built a full register tone system out of the wreckage. Both languages, however, were shaped by the same voiced obstruents: *b, *d, and *j.

In Phan Rang Cham, the voiced obstruents simply devoice: *b becomes p, *d becomes t, and *j becomes c. At the same time, the pitch-lowering effect of those voiced consonants on the following vowel was phonetic and automatic. Once the voicing contrast collapsed, the only thing left that distinguished an old *d from an old *t was that low pitch, so the pitch became a tone. That is tonogenesis in action. A root-final vowel gets low tone exactly when the consonant before it was a voiced obstruent, or when that consonant is a liquid whose own preceding consonant was voiced. For example, *paday becomes patày because the old *d lowered the following vowel, while *hatay stays hatay because the *t was voiceless and left no pitch trace. The same principle explains *jalaːn becoming calàn, with the low tone passing through the liquid that followed *j.

Several other segmental rules are needed for Phan Rang Cham. Word-initial *ʔ drops, so *ʔikaːn becomes ikan. Long vowels shorten, so *labaːt becomes lipàʔ. The vowel *a raises to ɨ after a nasal, giving limɨ from *lima and mɨta from *mata. Word-initial *lV becomes li, so *lanah becomes linɨh and *labaːt becomes lipàʔ. A special cluster of shapes, initial *b followed by a vowel and then a liquid, collapses to pi plus the liquid, which is why *bara becomes pirà, *bala becomes pilà, *bahrow becomes piròw, and *bulow becomes pilòw. The sibilant *s strengthens to th, and final stops *p, *t, *k all merge to the glottal stop ʔ. With these rules in hand, the entire Phan Rang Cham column falls into place.

Tsat requires the same voiced obstruents to do even more work, because it has lost so much segmental information. The first syllable is deleted almost everywhere, leaving a monosyllable built from the final syllable of the ancestor. The exception is the same stop-plus-vowel-plus-liquid shape that produced pi in Phan Rang Cham; in Tsat the penult survives as phi or pi, the liquid drops, and the old *b is not merely devoiced but aspirated. Thus *bara becomes phia, *bahrow becomes phiə, and *paley becomes piai, while ordinary words like *lima and *mata lose their first syllable entirely and become ma³³ and ta³³.

For the surviving final syllable in Tsat, voiced stop onsets devoice and aspirate, so *b becomes ph and *d becomes th, while *r becomes z and *s stays s. Final stops merge to ʔ, final *h disappears, long vowels stay long, and the diphthongs *ow and *ey become ə and ai respectively. The tone system is then organized by two things: whether the ancestor contained a voiced obstruent anywhere, and whether the surviving syllable ends in a stop or in something open or sonorant-final. After setting aside three special rhyme overrides, the grid is simple. If a voiced obstruent was present, stop-final words get the falling tone 42 and open or sonorant-final words get the low tone 11. If no voiced obstruent was present, stop-final words get the rising tone 24 and open or sonorant-final words get the mid tone 33. The three overrides are that final *h always gives high tone 55, final short *a plus a nasal gives aːnʔ with tone 42, and final *ay gives aːiʔ with tone 42.
