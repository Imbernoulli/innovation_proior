# The double-helix structure of DNA — distilled

## The problem

DNA carries heredity (Avery's transforming principle is DNA, not protein). A correct atomic structure must therefore reconcile the X-ray fiber-diffraction data with stereochemistry, explain Chargaff's base-composition ratios, and ideally show how a molecule can both store arbitrary information and be copied.

## The key idea

DNA is a **double helix**: two sugar-phosphate backbones wound around a common axis with the charged phosphates on the outside and the bases stacked flat on the inside. Each rung is one **purine hydrogen-bonded to one pyrimidine**, which keeps the diameter constant. With the bases in their correct **keto/amino** tautomeric forms, the hydrogen-bond donors and acceptors line up for exactly two regular same-shape pairs — **adenine–thymine** and **guanine–cytosine**. That single fact forces three conclusions at once: a constant-diameter regular helix; Chargaff's A=T, G=C as a necessity while A+T versus G+C remains free; and, because each base specifies its partner, a built-in copying mechanism with complementary template strands.

## The structure, stated precisely

- **Two** antiparallel right-handed helical chains around a common axis; the sugar-phosphate backbones are related by a dyad axis perpendicular to the fiber axis, so they run in opposite chemical directions. Backbone = β-D-deoxyribofuranose joined by 3′,5′ phosphodiester bonds.
- **Backbone outside** (phosphates hydrated, accessible to cations); **bases inside**, in planes perpendicular to the axis.
- **Geometry:** 3.4 Å rise per base, 36° twist per base ⇒ 10 residues/turn, **34 Å pitch**; phosphorus ~10 Å from the axis (≈20 Å diameter). All read from the B-form fiber pattern (helical cross + layer lines, per Cochran–Crick–Vand).
- **Base pairing:** each base on one chain hydrogen-bonds to one base on the other, side by side at the same z. One purine + one pyrimidine per pair. In the keto/amino tautomers, the regular same-shape pairs that fit the backbone are **A·T** and **G·C**; both present identical glycosidic-attachment geometry. A·T has two H-bonds: A N6-H to T O4, and T N3-H to A N1. G·C has three H-bonds: C N4-H to G O6, G N1-H to C N3, and G N2-H to C O2.
- **Consequences:** given one chain the other is determined (A demands T, G demands C); sequence along a chain is unrestricted (arbitrary information); %A=%T and %G=%C follow necessarily while the AT:GC mixture can vary (Chargaff).

## Why the alternatives fail

- **Backbone-inside models** (magnesium-bridged core; or Pauling–Corey's triple helix with un-ionized phosphates) bury the charged, hydrophilic backbone where it cannot be hydrated; ionized phosphates at the core repel and the structure cannot exist, and Pauling's un-ionized phosphate would not even be an acid.
- **Like-with-like pairing** (A·A, T·T, …) gives non-constant diameter (purine-purine too wide, pyrimidine-pyrimidine too narrow) and predicts no relation between base amounts, so it cannot explain Chargaff. Purine-with-pyrimidine repairs both.
- **Enol tautomers** (as drawn in contemporary textbooks) put a hydrogen on the wrong atom, scrambling the donor/acceptor pattern; the correct keto forms are what make A·T and G·C the regular, same-shape pairs.

## The replication implication

Because pairing is specific (A–T, G–C), the two strands are complementary: each is the negative of the other and a template for it. Separating the strands and building the complementary partner against each yields two identical daughter molecules, each conserving one original strand and adding one newly made partner. The specific pairing immediately suggests the semiconservative copying mechanism for the genetic material.

## The final model

The atomic model and mechanism are: two antiparallel right-handed sugar-phosphate helices on the outside; bases inside, stacked every 3.4 Å and rotated 36° per residue; ten residues per turn, 34 Å pitch, about 20 Å diameter; keto/amino A·T and G·C pairs with identical glycosidic geometry; Chargaff's A=T and G=C as a structural consequence with variable AT:GC composition; complementary strands as direct semiconservative templates for copying.
