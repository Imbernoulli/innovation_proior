# Context — the structure of the gene-carrying molecule

## Research question

What is the three-dimensional structure of deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA)? The question matters because, by the early 1950s, the weight of evidence points to DNA — not protein — as the material that carries hereditary information. If a molecule is the gene, then its structure must somehow do two jobs at once: it must be able to *store* an essentially unlimited, arbitrary amount of information (every organism's heredity differs), and it must be able to be *copied* faithfully when a cell divides. A structure that explained how one molecule could hold arbitrary information *and* template an exact replica of itself would not just be a chemical result; it would be the physical basis of heredity. So the goal is a stereochemically valid atomic model of DNA — bond lengths, bond angles, and van der Waals contacts all correct — that is consistent with the X-ray fiber-diffraction data and that, ideally, makes the molecule's biological role intelligible.

## Background

**DNA is the genetic material.** The prevailing wisdom through the 1940s held that genes were made of protein — proteins had twenty amino acids and seemed rich enough to encode heredity, while DNA, with only four bases, was dismissed as a monotonous "tetranucleotide" structural scaffold. Avery, MacLeod, and McCarty (1944) showed that the "transforming principle" that carries a heritable trait from one pneumococcus strain to another is DNA, not protein. This reframed DNA from a dull polymer into the prize: solve its structure and you may be looking at the gene itself.

**The chemical units.** DNA is a polymer of nucleotides. Each nucleotide is a sugar (deoxyribose) linked to a phosphate and to one of four nitrogenous bases. The bases come in two sizes: the **purines** adenine (A) and guanine (G) are large, double-ring molecules; the **pyrimidines** cytosine (C) and thymine (T) are small, single-ring molecules. The sugars and phosphates link head-to-tail (3′,5′ phosphodiester bonds) into a long backbone; the bases hang off the backbone. Furberg (1952) had built a single-chain nucleotide model establishing that the base sits roughly perpendicular to the sugar — a fixed local geometry any larger model must respect.

**Tautomers — the same molecule, a movable hydrogen.** Each base can in principle exist in alternate tautomeric forms that differ only in the position of one hydrogen atom and the placement of a double bond: a **keto** (lactam) form versus an **enol** (lactim) form for guanine and thymine, an **amino** versus **imino** form for adenine and cytosine. This is not a cosmetic detail: which tautomer a base adopts decides which of its atoms are hydrogen-bond *donors* (an –NH) and which are *acceptors* (a =O or ring N). Two bases will only hydrogen-bond to each other if a donor on one faces an acceptor on the other. Many chemistry textbooks of the time drew guanine and thymine in the enol form — placing a crucial hydrogen in the wrong place.

**Chargaff's regularities (≈1950).** Erwin Chargaff measured the base composition of DNA from many species and found a striking pattern: within any one species the amount of adenine equals the amount of thymine, and the amount of guanine equals the amount of cytosine — %A ≈ %T and %G ≈ %C, so the purines together equal the pyrimidines together. Yet the overall composition (the A+T to G+C ratio) varies widely between species. These 1:1 ratios are an unexplained empirical fact: any correct structure ought to make the pairwise equalities inevitable while still leaving the overall A+T versus G+C mixture free to vary.

**X-ray fiber diffraction and the theory of helical diffraction.** DNA drawn into fibers gives X-ray diffraction patterns. The patterns come in two states: a crystalline "A" form (low humidity) and a wetter, simpler "B" form. Cochran, Crick, and Vand (1952) worked out what the diffraction pattern of a *helix* must look like: the Fourier transform of atoms arranged on a helix produces layer lines whose intensities are governed by Bessel functions, and the meridian (the vertical line of the pattern) is largely empty, so that the strong reflections fan out into a characteristic **X-shaped cross**. The practical upshot: a cross in the photograph is the signature of a helix; the spacing of the layer lines gives the helix pitch, and a strong meridional reflection gives the rise per repeating unit. The clearest such pattern available was Franklin and Gosling's B-form fiber photograph at King's College in 1952: an unmistakable bold cross, a 34 Å repeat along the fiber, a strong 3.4 Å meridional reflection (so ten units per turn), and an overall intensity distribution consistent with the dense phosphate backbone lying on the *outside* of the molecule.

**Crystal symmetry and chain direction.** The A-form crystalline data point to monoclinic C2 symmetry, which brings in a twofold dyad. For a helical molecule, a dyad perpendicular to the fiber axis naturally relates one sugar-phosphate backbone to a second one; because the dyad reverses top and bottom along a directed chain, the two backbones must run in opposite chemical directions if they are to superpose. This makes an antiparallel two-chain model the symmetry target.

**The diffraction data underdetermine the structure.** A fiber pattern does not hand you atomic coordinates; it constrains them. The missing constraint is stereochemistry — the fixed lengths and angles of chemical bonds and the hard-sphere van der Waals radii that forbid atoms from overlapping. Pauling had just shown, with the alpha-helix of proteins, that *physical model building* — constructing accurate scale models of the atoms and letting the geometric constraints rule out the impossible — is the way to combine diffraction evidence with chemical law. A central lesson of that success was that the chemistry of the repeating unit must be exactly right (the peptide bond is planar and rigid because of its double-bond character); get the unit wrong and the whole model is worthless.

## Baselines

**Pauling & Corey's triple helix (early 1953).** The most formidable competing attempt. Pauling and Corey proposed three DNA chains wound together with the phosphate backbones packed at the *center* of the structure and the bases pointing outward, like spokes. The fatal flaw is chemical: to fit three phosphate backbones into the core without them flying apart electrostatically, the model treated the phosphate groups as *un-ionized* — each phosphate kept a bound hydrogen, giving it no net charge. But phosphoric acid is a strong acid; at the near-neutral conditions of the experiments it gives up that hydrogen and the phosphate carries a negative charge. Un-ionized, the proposed substance would not even be an acid (and DNA plainly is); ionized, three negatively charged backbones crushed together at the axis would repel violently and the structure could not hold. The model also left no room for the counterions that must be present and made no contact with Chargaff's 1:1 ratios. It demonstrates the standard everything must clear: the ionization state and electrostatics of the phosphates must be right, and the bases must be accounted for.

**An earlier three-chain, backbone-inside model.** A still earlier model attempt placed the sugar-phosphate backbone at the center, bases outward, with magnesium ions bridging adjacent phosphates to hold the core together. It failed for two reasons: it assumed far too little water (DNA fibers in fact hold a great deal of water, which would surround any Mg²⁺ and stop it from bridging), and it again buried the charged backbone where it cannot be hydrated. The lesson carried forward: the hydrophilic, charged backbone wants to face the water on the *outside*, not be packed into the dry core.

**Like-with-like base pairing (a pairing scheme to be tested).** Given that the bases must somehow meet across the molecule's interior, the most natural first guess is that each base pairs with its own kind — adenine with adenine, thymine with thymine, and so on — held by hydrogen bonds. This scheme is the obvious baseline for "how the bases hold the chains together," and any correct pairing rule must beat it. It has two weaknesses a successful scheme must repair: a purine-with-purine pair is far wider than a pyrimidine-with-pyrimidine pair, so a like-with-like molecule would bulge and pinch rather than keep a constant diameter; and like-with-like pairing implies no relation between the amounts of different bases, so it cannot explain Chargaff's A=T and G=C.

## Evaluation settings

The yardsticks that exist for judging a proposed structure:
- **X-ray fiber-diffraction patterns** of DNA, in both the crystalline A form and the hydrated B form (the B-form photograph being the cleaner helical signature): the model's predicted diffraction — layer-line spacing, the cross pattern, the meridional reflections, the helix pitch (34 Å), the rise per residue (3.4 Å), ten residues per turn, and the radial mass distribution — must match what is observed.
- **Stereochemical validity**: every bond length and angle must match measured values; no two non-bonded atoms may sit closer than their van der Waals contact distance; the phosphates must be in the correct ionization state for the conditions.
- **Chargaff's base-composition ratios**: A=T and G=C within a species, with composition varying across species — a structure should make these a consequence, not a coincidence.
- **Biological adequacy**: the structure should be able to hold arbitrary sequence information and should be intelligible as something a cell could copy.

## Code framework

The working framework is a physical scale model constrained by a stereochemical ledger. The fixed entries are the nucleotide bond lengths and angles, the van der Waals radii, the donor/acceptor pattern implied by each tautomer, the B-form helical repeat of 34 Å, the 3.4 Å stacking interval, ten residues per turn, the roughly 20 Å diameter, and the radial mass clues from the fiber pattern.
