OK, let me think about what I'm actually trying to do here. The thing that matters is this: DNA is the gene. Avery showed it — the transforming principle that carries a trait from one strain of pneumococcus to another, surviving everything that destroys protein, is the deoxyribonucleic acid. So whatever the molecule looks like, its shape has to be the shape of heredity. A gene has to do two impossible-sounding things at once: hold an arbitrary, essentially unlimited amount of information — every organism is different — and then be copied, exactly, when a cell divides. If I can find the structure, maybe the structure itself tells me how those two things are possible. That's the prize. Not a chemical curiosity — the physical basis of inheritance. So I'm not going to be satisfied with "a plausible polymer." I want the structure that makes the biology fall out.

Now, how do you actually find a molecular structure this size? The X-ray people at King's have fiber diffraction patterns, but a diffraction pattern doesn't hand you the atomic coordinates — it constrains them, badly underdetermines them. You can't just invert the photograph and read off where every atom sits; the phase information is gone. So I need a second source of constraint, and the only one available is chemistry itself: bond lengths are fixed, bond angles are fixed, two atoms can't sit closer than their van der Waals radii let them. Pauling just proved how powerful this is — he got the alpha-helix of proteins by *building a model*, a literal scale model of the atoms, and letting the geometry forbid everything that's stereochemically impossible until only a few candidates survive, then checking those against the X-ray data. That's the method. Build the molecule with your hands, obeying the chemistry exactly, and see what's left standing. And the lesson buried in Pauling's success — Crick keeps hammering this — is that you have to get the chemistry of the repeating unit *exactly* right first. The peptide bond is planar and rigid because it has double-bond character; if you'd let it rotate freely you'd never have found the helix. Get the unit wrong and every model you build on it is garbage.

So the units. DNA is a chain of nucleotides: a deoxyribose sugar, a phosphate, and a base. The backbone is sugar-phosphate-sugar-phosphate, linked 3′ to 5′, running the length of the molecule, and the bases hang off it. Four bases. Two of them, adenine and guanine, are purines — big, two fused rings. Two of them, cytosine and thymine, are pyrimidines — small, one ring. Furberg already showed the base sits roughly perpendicular to its sugar, so that local geometry is pinned. Fine. The freedom is: how many chains, where does the backbone go, where do the bases go, and how is the whole thing held together.

Let me just try the obvious thing first, because that's how I find out why it's wrong. Put the backbone in the middle. Three chains, sugar-phosphate cores wound together down the axis, bases sticking outward like bristles, and bridge the phosphates with magnesium ions to glue the core. I build it. And it dies almost immediately, for two reasons that I should have seen. First, water. I'd assumed the molecule was fairly dry, but DNA fibers actually hold an enormous amount of water — I had the water content badly wrong — and if there's that much water around, it surrounds the Mg²⁺ ions and they can't bridge anything; the glue dissolves. Second, and this is the deeper one: the phosphates are *charged*. Each phosphate is the conjugate base of a strong acid; at the conditions we're in, it's ionized, it carries a negative charge. A charged, hydrophilic group does not want to be buried in a dry core away from water. It wants to face the solvent. Burying the backbone is fighting the molecule's own chemistry. Bragg orders us off DNA after this fiasco, and rightly — it was sloppy.

But the failure teaches me the single most useful constraint I now have: the charged phosphate backbone belongs on the *outside*, where it can be hydrated and where counterions can sit next to it. And if the backbone is outside, the bases are on the inside, facing each other across the axis. Hold that.

Then Pauling's manuscript arrives, and my stomach drops, because he has a structure in hand and he's the best chemist alive. I read it. Three chains — and the phosphates are at the *center*, bases out. Exactly the mistake I just made. And then I see how he held the core together, and I can hardly believe it: the phosphate groups in his model are *not ionized*. Each one has a bound hydrogen atom, so it has no net charge, and that's the only way three backbones could be packed at the axis without repelling each other to pieces. But that's chemically wrong. If the phosphates keep their hydrogens, the substance isn't an acid at all — and DNA is plainly an acid, that's what the "acid" in nucleic acid means. Give the hydrogens up, as they must at neutral pH, and you have three negatively charged backbones jammed together at the core, repelling each other so hard the structure can't exist. He's made the same error I made, in a more sophisticated form, and his eminence didn't save him. There's no counterion room either, and Chargaff's ratios are nowhere in it. So Pauling is wrong — but he won't stay wrong for long once someone points it out to him. The clock is running.

Now Wilkins shows me Franklin and Gosling's B-form photograph. And it stops me cold — it's so much simpler than the crystalline A-form pictures, just clean and bold, and right there in the middle is a black cross of reflections. A cross. I know exactly what a cross means, because Crick and Cochran and Vand worked out the diffraction of a helix: the Fourier transform of atoms on a helix gives layer lines whose intensities are Bessel functions, the meridian goes nearly empty, and the strong reflections splay out into precisely this X shape. The photograph is not an atomic model, but it removes the argument about the gross form: the molecule is helical. And the geometry is in the photo if I read it: the pattern repeats every 34 Å up the fiber, there's a strong reflection at 3.4 Å on the meridian — so the stacked units are 3.4 Å apart, like a pile of plates, and ten of them make one turn of 34 Å. The intensity distribution is compatible with the dense phosphates sitting at large radius, and the chemistry already says the charged groups belong near water. Helix; backbone outside; bases stacked flat on the inside.

How many chains? The crystal symmetry of the A form is monoclinic, space group C2 — there's a dyad axis, a two-fold rotation that maps the molecule onto itself. A dyad fits two sugar-phosphate backbones beautifully: one backbone carried onto the other by a 180° turn. But for a rotation perpendicular to the helix axis to superpose directed backbones, the chains can't both run the same way — the dyad flips top-to-bottom, so the two backbones must run in *opposite* chemical directions. Antiparallel. Two chains, antiparallel, backbone outside, bases stacked inside, 3.4 Å rise, ten per turn, ~20 Å across. That's the cage. The only thing left undetermined is what happens in the middle — how the bases from the two chains meet across the axis.

The backbone is a regular helix — same radius at every level, smooth. That means whatever pairs up in the middle, at every single rung, has to span the *same distance* and hold the two backbones at the *same* separation. The diameter has to be constant all the way up. If different rungs were different widths, the backbone would bulge here and pinch there, and it wouldn't be the regular helix the photograph demands. So I'm not looking for "some way the bases can touch." I'm looking for a way they touch that is *the same width every time*, regardless of which bases are at that rung — because the sequence varies, that's the whole point of carrying information.

So how do the bases meet? They have hydrogen-bonding groups — amino groups, ring nitrogens, keto oxygens — so they should hold together by hydrogen bonds. The most natural guess: like pairs with like. Adenine opposite adenine, thymine opposite thymine, guanine opposite guanine, cytosine opposite cytosine. Each base recognizes its own kind. I start trying to build it with the base shapes I have from the textbooks.

It won't behave. Two problems, and they're both fatal. The geometric one first: a purine is big, two rings; a pyrimidine is small, one ring. An adenine-adenine pair is two big purines side by side — fat. A thymine-thymine pair is two small pyrimidines — thin. So a rung where two purines meet is much wider than a rung where two pyrimidines meet, and the backbone would have to bulge out at the purine-purine rungs and squeeze in at the pyrimidine-pyrimidine ones. That's exactly the non-constant diameter the helix forbids. Like-with-like can't keep the width regular.

And there's a second problem that nags worse, because it's about the biology. Like-with-like makes no demand whatsoever on how much of each base there is. A could be paired with A wherever it likes; nothing forces the amount of adenine to relate to the amount of anything else. But Chargaff measured, carefully, across species: the amount of adenine always equals the amount of thymine, and the amount of guanine always equals the amount of cytosine. A equals T. G equals C. That is a *fact about the molecule* screaming that adenine and thymine are somehow yoked together, and guanine and cytosine are yoked together — not A to A. Like-with-like is deaf to the loudest clue I have. So like-with-like is wrong on geometry and wrong on Chargaff. Throw it out.

The fix for the width problem is staring at me the moment I state it: pair a big one with a small one. One purine and one pyrimidine at every rung — big + small — and the total width is always one purine-width plus one pyrimidine-width, the same at every rung no matter which purine and which pyrimidine. That's the constant diameter, automatically. And look — purine-with-pyrimidine is also exactly the shape of Chargaff's clue, because if every rung is one purine paired to one pyrimidine, the purines as a class equal the pyrimidines as a class. It's pointing the right way. So the pairs are purine-to-pyrimidine. But *which* purine with which pyrimidine? A with C and G with T? A with T and G with C? I need the hydrogen bonds to actually decide it.

So I start pushing the cardboard cutouts of the bases around on my desk, trying to get the hydrogen-bond donors of one base to line up with the acceptors of another. And it keeps not working cleanly. Using the forms of the bases I've got, I try to make adenine bond to thymine and it's awkward; I keep getting drawn to pairing like with like again because at least those seem to make two nice bonds, even though I know like-with-like is geometrically dead. Something about the shapes is fighting me, and I can't tell what.

Then Jerry Donohue — he's sharing our office, and he learned his chemistry from Pauling — looks at what I'm doing and tells me I've got the bases drawn wrong. The textbooks I'm copying from put guanine and thymine in the enol form — they've got a hydrogen sitting on an oxygen, an –OH, with the ring double bonds arranged to match. But that's the wrong tautomer. In solution these bases sit overwhelmingly in the *keto* form: the oxygen is a double-bonded =O, and the hydrogen that was on it has moved onto a ring nitrogen instead. It's one hydrogen atom in a different place. And I almost wave it off as a quibble — but it isn't a quibble at all, because where that hydrogen sits is exactly what determines whether a given atom is a hydrogen-bond *donor* or an *acceptor*. In the enol form, that oxygen is an –OH, a donor. In the keto form, that same oxygen is a =O, an acceptor, and there's now a donor –NH on the ring where the hydrogen moved to. I've been trying to mate the bases using the wrong donor/acceptor pattern. No wonder nothing fit. Move the hydrogen. Redraw guanine and thymine in the keto form, put adenine and cytosine in their proper amino forms.

On the morning of February 28, 1953, I cut out the corrected shapes and start shuffling them again. And then — adenine and thymine. With thymine in the keto form, adenine's amino N6-H donates a hydrogen bond to thymine's O4 oxygen, and thymine's N3-H donates to adenine's ring N1. Two hydrogen bonds, and they form without any forcing, the geometry just falls into place — the two bases lie flat, side by side, and the bonds that join them to their sugars come off at the right places. Then guanine and cytosine, both in their keto/amino forms: cytosine's N4-H donates to guanine's O6, guanine's N1-H donates to cytosine's N3, and guanine's N2-H donates to cytosine's O2. Three hydrogen bonds, again with no fudging.

And now the thing that makes me sit up. I lay the adenine-thymine pair next to the guanine-cytosine pair, and they are the *same shape*. Not similar — the same. The two glycosidic bonds, the bonds that attach each base to its sugar backbone, come off in the same two positions and at the same distance apart and at the same angle, for the A-T pair and for the G-C pair. The two pairs are interchangeable. You could swap an A-T rung for a G-C rung, or flip A-T to T-A, or G-C to C-G, and the backbone never notices — same width, same attachment geometry, every single time. That's the constant diameter I needed, and it isn't something I imposed; it dropped out of putting the correct keto bases together with the right hydrogen bonds. Two hydrogen bonds for A-T, three for G-C, and yet the overall pair shapes match. I didn't have to force a thing.

And the instant the shapes match, everything I've been carrying around clicks into one structure. In this same-width geometry, the hydrogen-bonding pattern lets adenine pair with thymine and guanine pair with cytosine; the crossed alternatives don't put the donors and acceptors in the same matched positions while keeping the glycosidic bonds where the backbone needs them. So across the molecule, every adenine on one chain faces a thymine on the other, every guanine faces a cytosine. Which means the amount of adenine *must* equal the amount of thymine, and guanine must equal cytosine — Chargaff's rules. The relative number of A-T rungs and G-C rungs can still vary, so the composition can differ from species to species without breaking the pairwise equalities. The thing I couldn't explain, the 1:1 ratios that like-with-like was deaf to, are now not a coincidence to be explained but an inescapable consequence of the structure. A equals T because every A is bonded to a T. Chargaff's rules suddenly stand out as a consequence of a double helix. That's the moment I know it's right — when the structure forces the one fact I'd been unable to account for.

So the structure assembles itself: two sugar-phosphate chains, both right-handed helices coiled around a common axis, running in opposite directions because the dyad demands it, the charged phosphates on the outside facing the water, the bases turned in and stacked flat 3.4 Å apart, ten to a turn, every rung a purine hydrogen-bonded to a pyrimidine — A to T, G to C — each pair the same width so the backbone stays a smooth helix ~20 Å across. The sequence of bases along one chain can be anything at all; that's how it stores arbitrary information. There's no restriction on the order, so any message can be written.

And then the second job of a gene answers itself, and I almost don't dare say it out loud. Because the pairing is specific — A only with T, G only with C — the sequence on one chain completely determines the sequence on the other. They're not two independent strands; they're complementary, each one the negative of the other. If one chain reads A-G-C-T, the other must read T-C-G-A across from it. So the molecule carries its information twice, once in each strand, each a template for the other. Pull the two chains apart, and each original chain specifies, base by base, exactly the partner that must be built against it — an A calls for a T, a G calls for a C — and you get two daughter molecules each identical to the original, each keeping one old strand and adding one new complementary strand. The structure is its own copying machine. It has not escaped me that the specific pairing we've found immediately suggests how the genetic material is copied. That's the whole point — the structure doesn't just permit heredity, it explains it.

Now I can write the model as a stereochemical object, not as a hunch. There are two helical chains, each a sugar-phosphate backbone of β-D-deoxyribofuranose residues joined by 3′,5′ phosphodiester bonds, coiled around one common axis. Both helices are right-handed. The sugar-phosphate backbones are related by a dyad axis perpendicular to the fiber axis, so they run in opposite chemical directions: antiparallel. The phosphate-sugar backbones are on the outside, hydrated and available to cations; the bases are on the inside. The bases lie in planes nearly perpendicular to the axis and stack 3.4 Å apart. Adjacent residues turn 36° from each other, so ten residues make one complete turn and the pitch is 34 Å. The phosphorus atoms sit about 10 Å from the axis, giving the molecule a diameter of about 20 Å.

At every level, one base on one chain hydrogen-bonds to one base on the other at the same height. One member of each pair is a purine and the other is a pyrimidine, so the diameter stays constant. With the bases in their ordinary keto/amino forms, adenine and thymine make two hydrogen bonds, guanine and cytosine make three, and both pairs put the glycosidic attachments in the same relative positions. That is the crucial geometric fact: A-T and G-C are interchangeable rungs of one regular helix. Therefore if adenine sits at a position on one chain, thymine must sit opposite it; if guanine sits there, cytosine must sit opposite it. The base sequence along a single chain is unrestricted, and the balance between A-T and G-C rungs is unrestricted, but once one chain is chosen, the other is fixed.

So the whole path is now closed. DNA is the gene, so its structure must store arbitrary information and be copyable; a diffraction pattern alone underdetermines the atoms, so I build stereochemically exact models the way Pauling built the alpha-helix; burying the charged phosphate backbone in the core fails — for me with magnesium and too little water, for Pauling with un-ionized phosphates that can't really exist — so the backbone goes outside and the bases inside; the B-form cross says helix, the layer lines say 34 Å pitch / 3.4 Å rise / ten per turn, and the C2 dyad says two antiparallel backbones; a regular helix demands a constant diameter at every rung, which kills like-with-like pairing because purine-purine is too fat, pyrimidine-pyrimidine is too thin, and the rule ignores Chargaff; with Donohue's correction to the keto tautomers the hydrogen bonds line up for adenine-thymine and guanine-cytosine, and those two pairs turn out to be the same shape. The diameter stays constant, Chargaff's A=T and G=C become inevitable while A+T versus G+C remains free, and because each base specifies its partner, separating the complementary strands gives the gene a direct copying mechanism with one old strand in each copy.
