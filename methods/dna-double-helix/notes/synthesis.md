# Synthesis — DNA Double Helix discovery trace

## Self-account (backbone of the trace)
- James D. Watson, *The Double Helix: A Personal Account of the Discovery of the Structure of DNA* (1968). FREE archive borrow: https://archive.org/details/doublehelixpers00wats_0
- Francis Crick, *What Mad Pursuit* (1988) — corroborating self-account; stresses lessons from the alpha-helix model-building errors (rigidity from double bonds), the importance of complementarity.
- Verbatim Watson quotes obtained (LitCharts, which quotes the memoir with chapter/page):
  - "Suddenly I became aware that an adenine-thymine pair held together by two hydrogen bonds was identical in shape to a guanine-cytosine pair held together by at least two hydrogen bonds." (Ch.26, p.194)
  - "Chargaff's rules then suddenly stood out as a consequence of a double-helical structure for DNA." (Ch.26, p.196)
  - "It has not escaped our notice that the specific pairing we have postulated immediately suggests a possible copying mechanism for the genetic material." (Ch.29, p.220) — also in the Nature paper.
  - "I realized that the phosphate groups in Linus' model were not ionized, but that each group contained a bound hydrogen atom and so had no net charge." (Ch.22, p.160)
  - On Photo 51: "The pattern was unbelievably simpler than those obtained previously ('A' form)... the black cross of reflections which dominated the picture could arise only from a helical structure." (Ch.23, p.167)

## Discovery beats as Watson tells it (chronological, first-person backbone)
1. **Model-building method** borrowed from Pauling's alpha-helix triumph: build physical scale models that obey stereochemistry (bond lengths/angles, van der Waals contacts), let the constraints do the work. Crick's lesson from the alpha helix: the peptide bond is planar/rigid (double-bond character) — get the chemistry of the unit right or the model is junk.
2. **First model (late 1951): a disaster.** Three chains, sugar-phosphate backbone in the CENTER, bases pointing out, held by Mg²⁺ bridging the phosphates. Watson had misremembered Franklin's water-content figure. Franklin's critique: DNA in fibers holds far MORE water than the model allowed; the Mg²⁺ would be surrounded by water and not bridge anything. Bragg ordered them off DNA.
3. **Cochran–Crick–Vand helical diffraction theory (1952):** Fourier transform of a helix gives layer lines whose intensities are squared Bessel functions; the signature is an X-shaped ("Maltese cross") pattern, with the meridian largely absent. So a cross in the photo ⇒ helix; layer-line spacing ⇒ pitch.
4. **Pauling's model (Feb 1953):** three chains, phosphates packed at the core, bases outside. Watson spotted the fatal chemistry error: the central phosphates were NOT ionized — Pauling had added bound H atoms to neutralize them. But at neutral pH the phosphate is a strong acid; it gives up that H. Un-ionized, his structure was not even an acid; ionized, the three negative backbones at the core repel and blow apart. (Also no room for counterions; ignored Chargaff.) Same error class as W&C's own first model. The race was on.
5. **Photo 51 / B-form:** Wilkins showed Watson Franklin's B-form photo. Far simpler than the A-form; a bold black cross of reflections ⇒ helix. Layer lines: repeat 34 Å, strong meridional 3.4 Å ⇒ 10 residues per turn, 3.4 Å rise between stacked bases. The strong outer/low-angle reflections and the overall mass distribution argued the phosphate backbone is on the OUTSIDE (where it can be hydrated and meet counterions), bases on the inside. Symmetry (monoclinic C2) ⇒ a dyad axis ⇒ two chains running in OPPOSITE directions (antiparallel).
6. **Decide: two chains, backbone outside, bases inside.** Now the only freedom left is how the bases meet across the axis. The diameter must be CONSTANT (≈20 Å) at every level so the backbone stays a smooth regular helix.
7. **Like-with-like (the wrong turn):** Watson first tries pairing each base with its own kind (A·A, T·T, G·G, C·C) bonded by hydrogen bonds, using the base shapes from the textbook (enol/imino tautomers). Two failures: (a) **geometry** — a purine (two rings, big) paired with a purine is fat; a pyrimidine (one ring, small) paired with a pyrimidine is thin; the diameter would bulge and pinch, not constant. (b) **It does NOT predict Chargaff** — like-with-like makes no demand that A=T or G=C.
8. **Donohue's correction:** Jerry Donohue (Pauling-trained, sharing their office) tells Watson the textbook keto/enol forms are wrong: guanine and thymine occur in the **keto** (not enol) tautomer; the textbook had a hydrogen in the wrong place. Move that H.
9. **Feb 28, 1953 — the aha:** shuffling cardboard cutouts in the corrected keto forms, Watson finds A·T (two H-bonds) has the SAME shape/size as G·C (three H-bonds): in each, one purine + one pyrimidine, the glycosidic bonds in the same positions and the same distance apart. Both pairs are interchangeable in the backbone — constant diameter automatically. And purine↔pyrimidine pairing forces A with T, G with C: **Chargaff's A=T, G=C falls out for free.**
10. **The copying mechanism:** each chain's sequence dictates the other's (A demands T, G demands C). The two strands are complementary; separate them and each templates a new partner. "It has not escaped our notice..." — the structure carries its own replication scheme.

## Primary source (the landing artifact, grounded)
Watson & Crick, *Nature* 171:737 (1953). Verbatim structural claims captured from the paper text in refs/wc1953-alt.txt:
- Two helical chains coiled round the same axis; right-handed; related by a dyad ⊥ to the fibre axis ⇒ the two chains run in opposite directions.
- Phosphate-sugar backbone OUTSIDE, bases inside (resembles Furberg). β-D-deoxyribofuranose, 3′,5′ phosphodiester links.
- Residue every 3.4 Å along z; 36° between adjacent residues ⇒ repeat every 10 residues = 34 Å; P atom 10 Å from axis (≈20 Å diameter).
- Bases perpendicular to axis, joined in pairs by H-bonds; one purine + one pyrimidine per pair. "purine position 1 to pyrimidine position 1; purine position 6 to pyrimidine position 6."
- Assuming keto (not enol) tautomers, only specific pairs bond: **adenine–thymine, guanine–cytosine.**
- If A on one chain ⇒ T opposite; G ⇒ C. Sequence on one chain unrestricted; given one chain, the other is determined.
- Experimentally A:T and G:C ratios ≈ unity (Chargaff). Explained.
- Probably impossible with ribose (extra O ⇒ van der Waals clash).
- Closing: "It has not escaped our notice that the specific pairing we have postulated immediately suggests a possible copying mechanism for the genetic material."

## Antecedents (load-bearing)
- **Avery, MacLeod, McCarty (1944):** transforming principle is DNA ⇒ DNA is the genetic material (against the dominant "protein is the gene" view). Makes DNA's structure the prize.
- **Chargaff (≈1950):** base-composition ratios — in any species %A=%T, %G=%C, and Σpurines=Σpyrimidines; but base composition varies by species. Watson/Crick must explain the 1:1's.
- **Cochran–Crick–Vand (1952):** helical diffraction theory (above).
- **Franklin & Gosling / Wilkins (King's):** X-ray fiber diffraction; A and B forms; Photo 51 (B-form, May 1952) — clear helix, dimensions, backbone-outside.
- **Pauling & Corey (Feb 1953):** triple-helix wrong model (above).
- **Furberg (1952):** single-chain nucleotide model with base ⊥ to sugar — W&C's chains "loosely resemble" Furberg's standard configuration.
- **Griffith (calc) & Crick:** quantum-chemical hint that A attracts T and G attracts C (complementary, not like-with-like) — predates the shape argument.
- **Tautomers:** keto (lactam) vs enol (lactim) / amino vs imino — the H-position that decides which H-bond donors/acceptors a base presents.

## Design-decision → why
- Model building (not pure math from the photos): the diffraction data alone underdetermine the structure; stereochemistry (fixed bond geometry, vdW contacts) is the extra constraint that pins it. Pauling proved the method on the alpha helix.
- Backbone outside: charged phosphates need water + counterions (hydrophilic); burying them at the core (Pauling) causes electrostatic self-repulsion and no hydration. Photo 51 mass distribution agrees.
- Two chains (not three): C2 dyad symmetry from the crystallography; density/diameter; and two makes complementary templating clean.
- Antiparallel: the dyad ⊥ to the axis maps one chain onto the other only if they run opposite ways.
- Bases inside, paired: only way to fill the core and keep a constant diameter while hydrating the backbone.
- Purine–pyrimidine pairing (one big + one small): constant diameter; purine-purine too fat, pyrimidine-pyrimidine too thin.
- Keto tautomer: decides the H-bond donor/acceptor pattern; with keto, A·T and G·C are the same-shape complementary pairs and self-pairing is excluded.
- 3.4 Å rise / 36° twist / 34 Å pitch / 10 residues: read directly from B-form layer lines + base-stacking vdW thickness (~3.4 Å).
