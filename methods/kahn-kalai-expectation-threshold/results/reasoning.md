Let me start from the thing that actually hurts. I have an increasing property $\mathcal F$ on a finite set $X$, $|X|=n$. The threshold $p_c(\mathcal F)$ — the $p$ at which $\mu_p(\mathcal F)=1/2$ — is the number I want, and it is hard. Every interesting threshold I know of cost somebody a long, bespoke argument: Pósa rotations for Hamilton cycles, second-moment gymnastics for matchings, the Johansson–Kahn–Vu machinery for hypergraph matchings. None of those arguments talk to each other. So the dream is one quantity, computable, that predicts $p_c$ for *every* increasing family at once.

The naive guess is right there. If I can cover $\mathcal F$ cheaply — a family $\mathcal G$ with $\mathcal F\subseteq\langle\mathcal G\rangle$ and $\sum_{S\in\mathcal G}p^{|S|}\le 1/2$ — then a $p$-random set is unlikely to be in $\mathcal F$, because $\mu_p(\mathcal F)\le\mu_p(\langle\mathcal G\rangle)\le\sum_{S\in\mathcal G}\mu_p(\langle S\rangle)=\sum_{S\in\mathcal G}p^{|S|}\le 1/2$. The middle quantity is just $\mathbb E[\,|\{S\in\mathcal G:S\subseteq X_p\}|\,]$, the expected number of cover-sets I capture. So $q(\mathcal F)$, the largest $p$ for which such a cheap cover exists — call $\mathcal F$ $p$-small when it does — is a lower bound on $p_c$, and it's the easiest thing in the world to estimate: just exhibit a cover and add up $p^{|S|}$.

The whole game is the other direction. How far above $q(\mathcal F)$ can $p_c(\mathcal F)$ sit? I know it can sit a $\log$ factor up: for perfect matchings or Hamilton cycles in $G_{n,p}$ the cheap cover is built from the local structures (edges at a vertex, say), giving $q\sim 1/n$, while the truth is $\log n/n$ — the coupon-collector cost of killing every isolated or degree-one vertex. So a single $\log$ gap is forced and unavoidable. The question Kahn and Kalai pinned down is whether a single $\log$ is *all* that can ever happen: is $p_c(\mathcal F)\le K\,q(\mathcal F)\log\ell(\mathcal F)$ for a universal $K$, where $\ell(\mathcal F)$ is the largest minimal member's size (or $2$)? They themselves said it'd be "more sensible to conjecture it is not true." So I should be suspicious — but I also can't find a counterexample, and the payoff is absurd: it would make those hard, individual threshold theorems into one-line corollaries.

Let me get the shape of the claim straight by contrapositive, because that's the form I can attack. I want: if $\mathcal F$ is *not* $p$-small — no cheap cover exists at scale $p$ — then a $p$-random set (blown up by the allowed $\log\ell$ factor) lands in $\mathcal F$ with high probability. "No cheap cover" should be exactly the obstruction that forces the random set to succeed. That feels right as a duality of mood: cheap-cover-exists certifies failure; cheap-cover-cannot-exist should certify success. The job is to make that mood into a theorem.

Now, what tools do I have to turn "not $p$-small" into "a random set hits $\mathcal F$"? The recent breakthroughs all live next door. Alweiss–Lovett–Wu–Zhang, on robust sunflowers, proved a clean hitting statement: if a family is **spread** — no small set $S$ is contained in more than a $\kappa^{-|S|}$ fraction of the members, $|\mathcal H\cap\langle S\rangle|\le\kappa^{-|S|}|\mathcal H|$ — then a modest random set contains a whole member with high probability. And Frankston–Kahn–Narayanan–Park took exactly that and got the *fractional* version of the very conjecture I want: $p_c(\mathcal F)\le K\,q_f(\mathcal F)\log\ell(\mathcal F)$, where $q_f$ is the fractional expectation threshold — relax the cover from a $\{0,1\}$ set system to a nonnegative weighting $\lambda_S$ with $\sum_{S\subseteq F}\lambda_S\ge1$ for all $F$ and $\sum_S\lambda_Sp^{|S|}\le1/2$.

So why am I not done? Because $q_f$ is not $q$. The fractional version is genuinely easier, and the reason is sharp and worth staring at. $q_f$ is the value of a linear program. "Not weakly $p$-small" therefore has a dual certificate, and Talagrand's observation makes it concrete: if $q_f(\mathcal F)\le q$ then there is a $(2q)$-spread *probability measure* supported on $\mathcal F$. That measure is the gift. It is exactly the spread hypothesis that the ALWZ encoding argument eats. So FKNP's chain is: not-fractionally-small $\xrightarrow{\text{LP duality}}$ a spread measure $\xrightarrow{\text{ALWZ-style hitting}}$ random set succeeds. Beautiful, and it leans its full weight on duality producing spread.

Drop to the integer condition and the first arrow vanishes. "Not $p$-small" — no cheap *set* cover — is a $\{0,1\}$ statement. It is not a linear program; it's an integer program, and there is no duality to hand me a spread measure. I literally have nothing but the bare fact: every set system that covers $\mathcal F$ costs more than $1/2$ at scale $p$. The thing every successful proof in this area has used as its engine — spread — is simply not available to me at the start. That's the wall.

The community's expected way around the wall is Talagrand's other conjecture: $q(\mathcal F)\ge q_f(\mathcal F)/L$, i.e. weakly $p$-small implies $(p/L)$-small. Prove that and the integer conjecture is *equivalent* to the proved fractional one, and I'm done. But this is a rounding statement — turn fractional cover-weights into an honest integer cover at a bounded multiplicative cost — and it is hard. Talagrand floated two baby cases; each needed its own nontrivial paper (DeMarco–Kahn; Frankston–Kahn–Park) to settle, and the full rounding is considered worse than either. I don't want to bet the whole thing on a rounding lemma that may be as hard as the original problem.

So let me refuse the rounding route and go back to the bare hypothesis. I have "not $p$-small" and I want "random set wins." Forget building a spread measure. What can I do *directly* with a random set and the cover cost?

Suppose I sample a random set $W$ and, alongside, try to *manufacture* a cheap cover of $\mathcal F$ out of what the sampling reveals. If I always succeed in manufacturing a cheap cover, that contradicts "not $p$-small." So the only way to be consistent with "not $p$-small" is that the manufacturing usually fails — and I want failure of manufacturing to mean the random set $W$ itself already contains a member of $\mathcal F$. If I can engineer the two outcomes to be exhaustive — *either* $W\in\langle\mathcal H\rangle$ *or* I've built a cheap cover — then since the cheap cover is forbidden (except rarely, which I'll have to control), $W$ wins almost always. That's a dichotomy I can chase without ever defining spread.

Let me set up the reformulation cleanly so the combinatorics is bare. Let $\mathcal H$ be the minimal elements of $\mathcal F$ — a hypergraph, $\ell$-bounded, with $\langle\mathcal H\rangle=\mathcal F$. I'll prove: if $\mathcal H$ is a nonempty $\ell$-bounded hypergraph that is **not** $p$-small, then a uniformly random subset of $X$ of size about $(Lp\log\ell)n$ is in $\langle\mathcal H\rangle$ with probability $1-o_{\ell\to\infty}(1)$. (Switching from $\mu_p$ to a fixed-size random set is harmless: $|X_p|$ concentrates, and I'll patch the conversion at the very end.) If I have this, the conjecture follows: take $q>q(\mathcal F)$, so $\mathcal H$ is not $q$-small; run the theorem at scale $q$ with the $\log\ell$ blow-up; out comes $\Pr(X_p\in\mathcal F)>1/2$ at $p=Kq\log\ell$, which is $p_c(\mathcal F)\le Kq\log\ell$.

Now the construction. I'll build $W$ in increments and run a reduction in lockstep. Start with $\mathcal H_0=\mathcal H$. At step $i$ I have a hypergraph $\mathcal H_{i-1}$ whose edges are smaller than $\mathcal H$'s, I sample a fresh chunk $W_i\subseteq X\setminus\bigcup_{j<i}W_j$ of size $w\approx Lpn$, and from $(\mathcal H_{i-1},W_i)$ I want to peel off a piece I can cover cheaply, push the rest into a *new, even smaller* hypergraph $\mathcal H_i$, and record that progress toward containing a member is being banked into $\bigcup_{j\le i}W_j$. So I want three properties for the step, given $\mathcal H:=\mathcal H_{i-1}$ and $W:=W_i$: a sub-collection $\mathcal G\subseteq\mathcal H$ with a cheap cover $\mathcal U$; a successor $\mathcal H'$ with $\mathcal H\setminus\mathcal G\subseteq\langle\mathcal H'\rangle$ (so covering $\mathcal H'$ covers the leftover); and the successor's edges strictly smaller, plus a "capture" bookkeeping that ties $\mathcal H'$ back to $\mathcal H$ through $W$.

What is the right object to define from $S\in\mathcal H$ and the sampled $W$? Let me think about what "progress" means. I've grabbed $W$. For a given edge $S$, the part of $S$ I still need is $S\setminus W$ — but that might be big, no progress. The ALWZ/FKNP idea is that even if $S\setminus W$ is big, the spread of the family means $S\setminus W$ typically *contains* some small $S'\setminus W$ for another edge $S'$, and I can replace $S$ by that small remainder and iterate. FKNP make this concrete by fixing, for each "completed" set $Z$, a chosen edge $\psi(Z)\subseteq Z$, and tracking $\chi(S,W)=\psi(S\cup W)\setminus W$. But I have no spread to lean on. So I can't argue "typically small." I have to *force* small.

Let me just take the smallest. Define, for $S\in\mathcal H$ and $W$, the **minimum $(S,W)$-fragment** $T=T(S,W)$ as the smallest set (possibly empty) of the form $S'\setminus W$ where $S'\in\mathcal H$ and $S'\subseteq W\cup S$. Such an $S'$ exists — $S$ itself qualifies, $S\subseteq W\cup S$ — so $T$ is well defined; write $t=|T|$. Ties broken arbitrarily. This is the part of *some* edge (one sitting inside $W\cup S$) that survives outside $W$, chosen as tiny as possible.

Why minimum, not FKNP's chosen $\psi$? The thing I keep wanting and not getting from $\psi$ is a containment: whenever a completed set $Z=W\cup T$ contains *any* edge $\hat S\in\mathcal H$, I'd love that edge to contain $T$. With the minimum, is that true? $\hat S\subseteq Z=W\cup T\subseteq W\cup S$ (since $T\subseteq S\setminus W\cup\ldots$ — let me be careful: $T=S'\setminus W$ for some $S'\subseteq W\cup S$, so $T\subseteq S'\subseteq W\cup S$, hence $W\cup T\subseteq W\cup S$). If $T\not\subseteq\hat S$, then $\hat S\setminus W\subseteq T$ but is a *proper* subset, so $|\hat S\setminus W|<|T|=t$. But $\hat S\in\mathcal H$ and $\hat S\subseteq W\cup S$, so $\hat S\setminus W$ is itself a fragment of $(S,W)$ — and it's strictly smaller than the *minimum* fragment. Contradiction. So $T\subseteq\hat S$. The containment is free, and it is exactly the property FKNP had to buy with a "pathological case" analysis because their $\psi$-fragment was not the minimum. And I haven't said the word spread once.

Now the step. Given $W$, split $\mathcal H$ by fragment size. Call an edge's fragment "large" if $t(S,W)\ge 0.9\ell$ (any fixed fraction below $1$ works; $0.9$ leaves me room later). Let
$$\mathcal G(W)=\{S\in\mathcal H: t(S,W)\ge 0.9\ell\},\qquad \mathcal U(W)=\{T(S,W):S\in\mathcal G(W)\}.$$
$\mathcal U(W)$ covers $\mathcal G(W)$ trivially: each $T(S,W)\subseteq S$. For the leftover edges, the ones with small fragments, I take their fragments as the next hypergraph:
$$\mathcal H'(W)=\{T(S,W):S\in\mathcal H\setminus\mathcal G(W)\}.$$
Every $T(S,W)\subseteq S$, and $T$ is a fragment so $W\cup T\supseteq$ some edge — that's the capture bookkeeping. Since $S\supseteq T(S,W)$, we get $S\in\langle\mathcal H'(W)\rangle$ for each leftover $S$, i.e. $\mathcal H\setminus\mathcal G(W)\subseteq\langle\mathcal H'\rangle$: a cover of $\mathcal H'$ also covers the leftover. And $\mathcal H'$ is $0.9\ell$-bounded, because every leftover fragment has size $<0.9\ell$. The edges shrank by a factor $0.9$. That's the third property.

So the step does exactly what I wanted: peel off the large-fragment edges and cover them by their (cheap, I hope) fragments; push the small-fragment edges into a hypergraph that is a constant factor smaller and is "subordinate" to $\mathcal H$ via $W$.

Everything now rides on one thing: is the cover $\mathcal U(W)$ I peel off at each step actually cheap, in expectation over the random $W$? Concretely I want
$$\mathbb E\!\left[\sum_{U\in\mathcal U(W)}p^{|U|}\right]<L^{-0.8\ell}$$
for the right notion of $L$ and $w=Lpn$. If each step's peeled cover costs at most something like $L^{-0.8\ell}$ in expectation, and the edges shrink geometrically, the *total* expected cover cost over all steps will be tiny, and then a cheap full cover almost never assembles — which is the contradiction with "not $p$-small" that drives the dichotomy.

Let me prove the cost lemma. Fix $w=Lpn$ and let $W$ be uniform from $\binom{X}{w}$. The expectation $\mathbb E[\sum_{U\in\mathcal U(W)}p^{|U|}]=\binom{n}{w}^{-1}\sum_{W}\sum_{U\in\mathcal U(W)}p^{|U|}$, so it's equivalent to bound the double sum by $\binom{n}{w}L^{-0.8\ell}$. Slice by fragment size: for $m\ge0.9\ell$ let $\mathcal G_m(W)=\{S:t(S,W)=m\}$ and $\mathcal U_m(W)=\{T(S,W):S\in\mathcal G_m(W)\}$. Every $U\in\mathcal U_m(W)$ has $|U|=m$, so $\sum_W\sum_{U\in\mathcal U_m(W)}p^{|U|}=p^m\cdot N_m$, where
$$N_m=\big|\{(W,T(S,W)): W\in\tbinom{X}{w},\ S\in\mathcal H,\ t(S,W)=m\}\big|.$$
I just need to count pairs $(W,T)$. Specify them.

First specify $Z:=W\cup T$. Since $W$ and $T$ are disjoint ($T=S'\setminus W$) and $|T|=m$, $|Z|=w+m$, so the number of possible $Z$ is $\binom{n}{w+m}$. Compare it to $\binom{n}{w}$:
$$\binom{n}{w+m}=\binom{n}{w}\prod_{j=0}^{m-1}\frac{n-w-j}{w+j+1}.$$
Each factor is at most $\frac{n}{w}=\frac{n}{Lpn}=\frac{1}{Lp}$, since the numerator only decreases and the denominator only increases from $j=0$. So $\binom{n}{w+m}\le\binom{n}{w}(Lp)^{-m}$. This is the heart of why specifying $Z=W\cup T$ rather than $W\cup S$ is the right move: $|Z|$ exceeds $w$ by only $m=t$, the *small* fragment size, not by $|S|$ which could be the full $\ell$. The cost of specifying $Z$ is $(Lp)^{-m}$, and it's about to cancel against $p^m$.

Second, pin down $T$ inside $Z$. By the minimum-fragment property, $Z=W\cup T$ contains some edge of $\mathcal H$ (namely the $S'$ that realized $T$, since $S'=T\cup(S'\cap W)\subseteq W\cup T=Z$). Make an arbitrary choice $\hat S\subseteq Z$ of an edge, with the choice depending only on $Z$ — so given $Z$ this choice is free, no counting cost. Now the free containment kicks in: $\hat S\subseteq Z=W\cup T\subseteq W\cup S$, and if $T\not\subseteq\hat S$ then $\hat S\setminus W\subsetneq T$ is a strictly smaller fragment of $(S,W)$, contradicting minimality. So $T\subseteq\hat S$. Therefore $T$ is a subset of $\hat S$, and $\hat S$ has size at most $\ell$, so the number of choices for $T$ given $Z$ (and the free $\hat S$) is at most $2^{|\hat S|}\le 2^\ell$.

Once $Z$ and $T$ are fixed, the pair $(W,T)$ is determined: $W=Z\setminus T$. So
$$N_m\le \binom{n}{w}(Lp)^{-m}\cdot 2^\ell,$$
and the size-$m$ contribution is
$$p^m N_m\le p^m\binom{n}{w}(Lp)^{-m}2^\ell=\binom{n}{w}L^{-m}2^\ell.$$
The $p^m$ canceled the $p^{-m}$ in $(Lp)^{-m}$, leaving a clean $L^{-m}$. Sum over fragment sizes $m\ge0.9\ell$:
$$\sum_{m\ge 0.9\ell}\binom{n}{w}L^{-m}2^\ell=\binom{n}{w}2^\ell\frac{L^{-0.9\ell}}{1-1/L}.$$
I want this at most $\binom{n}{w}L^{-0.8\ell}$, i.e. $2^\ell L^{-0.9\ell}/(1-1/L)\le L^{-0.8\ell}$, i.e. $2^\ell/(1-1/L)\le L^{0.1\ell}$, i.e. $(2/L^{0.1})^{\ell}\le 1-1/L$. As soon as $L^{0.1}>2$ — say $L$ a large enough absolute constant — the left side decays in $\ell$ and the inequality holds. So for $L$ sufficiently large,
$$\sum_{m\ge0.9\ell}\binom{n}{w}L^{-m}2^\ell\le\binom{n}{w}L^{-0.8\ell},$$
and dividing by $\binom{n}{w}$,
$$\mathbb E\!\left[\sum_{U\in\mathcal U(W)}p^{|U|}\right]<L^{-0.8\ell}.$$
That's the cost lemma. Now I see exactly where the numbers came from: the threshold $0.9\ell$ for "large" is the start of the geometric tail; the $2^\ell$ from specifying $T$ inside $\hat S$ eats $0.1\ell$ of the exponent ($L^{-0.9\ell}2^\ell\le L^{-0.8\ell}$ once $L^{0.1}>2$); and the surviving $0.8\ell$ is the budget I get back. There's no spread anywhere — the only inequality I used about the family was the minimality of $T$.

And this is why the predecessors needed a case-analysis here. In ALWZ/FKNP the specified completed set is $Z=W\cup S$, whose size overshoots $w$ by $|S|$, and the chosen core $T=S\cap\psi(Z)$ does not automatically lie inside an arbitrary edge of $Z$ — so they had to separate "pathological" $Z$'s (where a small core is over-represented) from "non-pathological" ones, with a Markov step on the count, to control the analogue of Step 2. Replacing $Z=W\cup S$ by $Z=W\cup T$, with $T$ the *minimum* fragment, makes the overshoot small and the core-containment free, and the pathological case evaporates. Same skeleton, one definition changed, the case analysis gone.

Now iterate. Set $n=|X|$, $\ell\to\infty$. Let $\ell_i=0.9^i\ell$ and $\gamma=\lfloor\log_{0.9}(1/\ell)\rfloor+1$ — the smallest number of halving-by-$0.9$ steps to push the bound below $1$, so $0.9\le\ell_\gamma<1$. Let $X_0=X$, and for $i=1,\dots,\gamma$ sample $W_i$ uniformly from $\binom{X_{i-1}}{w_i}$ with $X_i=X_{i-1}\setminus W_i$ and $w_i=L_ipn$. Produce $\mathcal H_i=\mathcal H_{i-1}'(W_i)$, the successor from the step applied to $\mathcal H_{i-1}$ and $W_i$, with its peeled $\mathcal G_i=\mathcal G_i(W_i)$ and cheap cover $\mathcal U_i=\mathcal U_i(W_i)$. Each $\mathcal H_i$ is $\ell_i$-bounded by construction.

The capture property propagates. If every $S_i\in\mathcal H_i$ satisfies $(\bigcup_{j\le i}W_j)\cup S_i\in\langle\mathcal H\rangle$, then for $S_{i+1}=T(S_i,W_{i+1})=S_i'\setminus W_{i+1}$ (some $S_i'\in\mathcal H_i$),
$$\Big(\bigcup_{j\le i+1}W_j\Big)\cup S_{i+1}=\Big(\bigcup_{j\le i}W_j\Big)\cup W_{i+1}\cup(S_i'\setminus W_{i+1})\supseteq\Big(\bigcup_{j\le i}W_j\Big)\cup S_i'\in\langle\mathcal H\rangle,$$
using the inductive hypothesis for $S_i'$. Base case $i=0$: $S_0\in\mathcal H$ and $\emptyset\cup S_0=S_0\in\langle\mathcal H\rangle$. So capture holds for all $i$.

Termination forces the dichotomy. Since $\ell_\gamma<1$ and the $\mathcal H_\gamma$ edges have size $<\ell_\gamma<1$, $\mathcal H_\gamma$ is either empty or just $\{\emptyset\}$. Trace any $S=S_0\in\mathcal H$ through the process, $S_i=T(S_{i-1},W_i)$. At each step either $S_{i-1}$ landed in the large-fragment set $\mathcal G_i$ — and then $S_{i-1}$ is covered by $\mathcal U_i$ — or it didn't, and $S_i$ continues into $\mathcal H_i$. If $\mathcal H_\gamma=\emptyset$, every trajectory must have been peeled off at some step $j<\gamma$, i.e. each $S$ is covered by $\bigcup_i\mathcal U_i$; so $\mathcal U:=\bigcup_{i\le\gamma}\mathcal U_i$ is a cover of $\mathcal H$. If instead $\mathcal H_\gamma=\{\emptyset\}$, then by capture with $S_\gamma=\emptyset$, $W_1\cup\dots\cup W_\gamma\cup\emptyset\in\langle\mathcal H\rangle$, so $W:=\bigcup_i W_i\in\langle\mathcal H\rangle$. Either way: $W\in\langle\mathcal H\rangle$, or $\mathcal U$ covers $\mathcal H$. The two outcomes are exhaustive.

That's the dichotomy I wanted, with no spread. Let $\mathcal E$ be the event "$\mathcal U$ covers $\mathcal H$." Then $\Pr(W\in\langle\mathcal H\rangle)+\Pr(\mathcal E)\ge1$, i.e. $\Pr(W\in\langle\mathcal H\rangle)\ge1-\Pr(\mathcal E)$. So I just need $\Pr(\mathcal E)\to0$.

Here's where "not $p$-small" finally earns its keep. If $\mathcal E$ occurs, $\mathcal U$ is a cover of $\mathcal H$; but $\mathcal H$ is *not* $p$-small, so *no* cover of $\mathcal H$ can be cheap — every cover has $\sum_{U}p^{|U|}>1/2$. Hence $\mathcal E$ implies $\sum_{U\in\mathcal U}p^{|U|}>1/2$. So $\Pr(\mathcal E)\le\Pr(\sum_{U\in\mathcal U}p^{|U|}>1/2)$, and by Markov this is at most $2\,\mathbb E[\sum_{U\in\mathcal U}p^{|U|}]$. The expected total cost is the sum over steps of the per-step costs, and each per-step cost is bounded by the cost lemma at the appropriate edge-bound $\ell_i$ and base $L_i$:
$$\mathbb E\!\left[\sum_{U\in\mathcal U}p^{|U|}\right]<\sum_{i\le\gamma}L_i^{-0.8\ell_i}.$$
Now I have to pick the $L_i$ so this sum is $o_{\ell\to\infty}(1)$. With a constant $L_i=L$ throughout, the late terms are the problem: $\ell_i\to$ a constant as $i\to\gamma$, so $L^{-0.8\ell_i}$ is only a constant, not small, and there are $\Theta(\log\ell)$ steps — the sum wouldn't go to $0$. The early terms are fine: while $\ell_i$ is still large, $L^{-0.8\ell_i}$ is astronomically small. So spend more sampling exactly in the last stretch, where the edges are small and the per-step cost is weak, and spend the baseline everywhere else.

Make it precise. There is an absolute $c>0$ with $\ell_i>\exp(c\sqrt{\log\ell})$ for all $i<\gamma-\sqrt{\log_{0.9}(1/\ell)}$ (these are the early steps, $i$ at most $\gamma$ minus $\sqrt{\,\cdot\,}\approx\sqrt{\log\ell}$ steps from the end). Set
$$L_i=\begin{cases}L & i<\gamma-\sqrt{\log_{0.9}(1/\ell)},\\[2pt] L\sqrt{\log\ell} & \gamma-\sqrt{\log_{0.9}(1/\ell)}\le i\le\gamma.\end{cases}$$
Split the cost sum:
$$\sum_{i\le\gamma}L_i^{-0.8\ell_i}=\underbrace{\sum_{i<\gamma-\sqrt{\log_{0.9}(1/\ell)}}L^{-0.8\ell_i}}_{\text{early}}+\underbrace{\sum_{i=\gamma-\sqrt{\log_{0.9}(1/\ell)}}^{\gamma}(L\sqrt{\log\ell})^{-0.8\ell_i}}_{\text{late}}.$$
Early: each $\ell_i>\exp(c\sqrt{\log\ell})$, so each term is at most $L^{-0.8\exp(c\sqrt{\log\ell})}$, and summing the geometric-in-$i$ early terms (dominated by the smallest $\ell_i$ in that range) is at most $2L^{-0.8\exp(c\sqrt{\log\ell})}$ — super-polynomially small. Late: there are only $\sqrt{\log_{0.9}(1/\ell)}=O(\sqrt{\log\ell})$ such steps; in this range $0.9\le\ell_i$, so $0.8\ell_i\ge 0.72$, and each term is at most $(L\sqrt{\log\ell})^{-0.72}$; summing $O(\sqrt{\log\ell})$ of them gives $O(\sqrt{\log\ell}\cdot(L\sqrt{\log\ell})^{-0.72})=O((L\sqrt{\log\ell})^{-c'})$ for some $c'>0$ (the $\sqrt{\log\ell}$ in front is overwhelmed by the $(\sqrt{\log\ell})^{-0.72}$ raised over many terms / by enlarging $L$). Together,
$$\mathbb E\!\left[\sum_{U\in\mathcal U}p^{|U|}\right]<2L^{-0.8\exp(c\sqrt{\log\ell})}+O\big((L\sqrt{\log\ell})^{-c'}\big)=(\log\ell)^{-c''}$$
for some $c''>0$. Then $\Pr(\mathcal E)\le 2\,(\log\ell)^{-c''}=o_{\ell\to\infty}(1)$, and so $\Pr(W\in\langle\mathcal H\rangle)\ge1-o_{\ell\to\infty}(1)$.

Let me total the sampling size, to confirm I land on $\log\ell$ and not more. The total set is $W=\bigcup_{i=1}^\gamma W_i$ with $|W_i|=w_i=L_ipn$. The baseline steps contribute $\Theta(\gamma)\cdot Lpn=\Theta(\log\ell)\cdot Lpn$ — wait, that's already $\log\ell\cdot pn$, good. The boosted late steps each contribute $L\sqrt{\log\ell}\,pn$, and there are $O(\sqrt{\log\ell})$ of them, so together $O(\log\ell)\cdot Lpn$ as well. So $|W|=(CLp\log\ell)n$ for an absolute constant $C$ — exactly the allowed $\Theta(p\log\ell)n$. The boosting cost me only a constant factor in the final $K$, not an extra log. That's why I could afford the boost.

So the reformulated theorem is proved: for $\ell\ge2$ there is a universal $L$ such that any nonempty $\ell$-bounded hypergraph $\mathcal H$ that is *not* $p$-small has a uniformly random $((Lp\log\ell)n)$-element subset in $\langle\mathcal H\rangle$ with probability $1-o_{\ell\to\infty}(1)$. And it's even quantitative — the exceptional probability is $(\log\ell)^{-c}$ for some $c>0$.

Now finish the conjecture from this. Let $\mathcal F$ be a nontrivial increasing property and take any $q>q(\mathcal F)$; I'll show with $p=Kq\log\ell(\mathcal F)$ (universal $K$ to be fixed) that $\Pr(X_p\in\mathcal F)>1/2$, which is $p_c(\mathcal F)\le Kq\log\ell(\mathcal F)$, and letting $q\downarrow q(\mathcal F)$ gives the theorem. Let $\mathcal H$ be the minimal elements of $\mathcal F$, so $\langle\mathcal H\rangle=\mathcal F$, $\mathcal H$ is $\ell(\mathcal F)$-bounded and *not* $q$-small (because $q>q(\mathcal F)$). Pick an absolute $C$ so that, with $\ell=C\ell(\mathcal F)$, the exceptional probability in the reformulated theorem is below $1/4$. Set $m=(Lq\log\ell)n$ and $p'=2Lq\log\ell$, and choose $K$ large enough that $p\ge p'$. If $p'>1$ the conjecture is vacuous (the bound exceeds $1$), so assume $p'\le1$. The reformulated theorem gives a random $m$-set $X_m\in\langle\mathcal H\rangle$ with probability $\ge 3/4$, and I convert from fixed size to $\mu_{p'}$ by conditioning on the size of $X_{p'}$, monotonicity, and concentration:
$$\Pr(X_{p'}\in\langle\mathcal H\rangle)\ge\Pr(X_m\in\langle\mathcal H\rangle)\,\Pr(|X_{p'}|\ge m)\ge\tfrac34\,\Pr(|X_{p'}|\ge m)>\tfrac12.$$
The last step is a standard concentration bound for $|X_{p'}|\sim\mathrm{Bin}(n,p')$ around its mean $p'n=2m$: I just need $\Pr(|X_{p'}|\ge m)>2/3$, which holds because $m$ is comfortably below the mean $2m$ and $m$ is large. And $m$ is large because $\mathcal H$ not $q$-small forces $q$ to be not too small: the singleton family $\{\{x\}:x\in X\}$ covers $\mathcal H$ (every nonempty edge contains a singleton) with cost $\sum_{x}q=nq$, and if that were $\le1/2$ then $\mathcal H$ would be $q$-small; so $nq>1/2$, whence $m=(Lq\log\ell)n=L\log\ell\cdot(qn)>(L\log\ell)/2$, large as $\ell\to\infty$. (Since $p\ge p'$ and the property is increasing, $\Pr(X_p\in\mathcal F)\ge\Pr(X_{p'}\in\mathcal F)>1/2$.) That closes it.

Let me say the whole causal chain once, end to end. The expectation threshold $q(\mathcal F)$ is the cheapest-cover lower bound on $p_c(\mathcal F)$, automatic by the union bound. To show the threshold is never more than a $\log\ell$ factor above it, I take the contrapositive — $\mathcal F$ has no cheap cover — and run a random set in increments, at each round defining the **minimum fragment** $T(S,W)$, the smallest leftover of an edge sitting inside the sampled part. Minimality makes a containment free: any edge inside $W\cup T$ contains $T$, which lets me specify a peeled-off fragment in just two steps — fix $Z=W\cup T$ (whose excess over the sample is only the tiny $t$, costing $(Lp)^{-t}$, cancelling the $p^t$ weight) and then fix $T$ inside an arbitrary edge of $Z$ (costing $2^\ell$) — yielding the cost bound $L^{-0.8\ell}$ with no spread hypothesis at all. Peeling off large fragments and recursing on the small ones shrinks edges by $0.9$ per round, so after $\Theta(\log\ell)$ rounds the hypergraph degenerates, forcing the exhaustive dichotomy: either the accumulated sample already contains a member, or the accumulated peeled covers assemble into a cheap cover of $\mathcal H$. The latter is impossible-except-rarely precisely because $\mathcal H$ is not $p$-small — Markov on the total expected cost $(\log\ell)^{-c}$ kills it — so the sample contains a member with high probability. Boosting the sample only in the last $O(\sqrt{\log\ell})$ rounds (where edges are small and the per-round bound is weak) keeps the total sample at $\Theta(p\log\ell)n$, and converting fixed-size sampling to $\mu_p$ by concentration delivers $p_c(\mathcal F)\le K\,q(\mathcal F)\log\ell(\mathcal F)$.
