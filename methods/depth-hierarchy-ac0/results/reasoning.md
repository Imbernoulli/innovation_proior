Let me start from what's actually bothering me. We know depth is a resource for constant-depth circuits: Sipser gave a candidate, Yao and Håstad nailed it down, and the clean statement is that for each d there's an explicit linear-size depth-d formula that any depth-(d−1) circuit needs size exp(n^{Ω(1/d)}) to compute. But "compute" there means *exactly* — agree on all 2ⁿ inputs. The thing I can't let go of is that this leaves the door wide open: maybe some modest depth-(d−1) circuit agrees with the depth-d formula on 99.9% of inputs, or on (1−o(1))·2ⁿ inputs, and the worst-case theorem would never notice. I want to slam that door. I want a depth-d formula f such that *every* subexponential depth-(d−1) circuit agrees with f on at most (½ + o(1))·2ⁿ inputs — correlation essentially zero, the way parity has zero correlation with all of AC⁰.

Why do I care about exactly ½ and not, say, 0.99? Two reasons, and they're not cosmetic. First, the dictionary between these lower bounds and oracles: a worst-case d-vs-(d−1) separation gives an oracle making the polynomial hierarchy infinite; an *average-case* one — a correlation bound — gives the separation relative to a *random* oracle, with probability one. That random-oracle statement for the full infinite hierarchy is exactly what's been sitting open (the d ∈ {0,1} cases are old, Bennett–Gill; the PSPACE-vs-PH random-oracle separation is Cai's and Babai's; but the whole hierarchy, all d, is not known). Second, if f is a low-influence function, a ½-inapproximability statement kills any "approximate converse" to the Linial–Mansour–Nisan / Boppana total-influence bound — the BKS-flavored question of whether low total influence forces approximability by a small shallow circuit. So I want the strong ½ bound and I want it for all d at once.

Okay. How do people prove correlation bounds against small-depth circuits at all? It's always random restrictions. You take a distribution over ρ ∈ {0,1,∗}ⁿ — fix some variables, leave the ∗'s alive — and you run a sequence of them so that three things happen. Let me write the three down because the whole game is going to be about whether I can get all three.

Property 1: the approximator simplifies. If C is the depth-d circuit I'm fighting, then C restricted by the composition of my restrictions should collapse, with high probability, to a shallow decision tree. This is what the switching lemma buys: Håstad says a width-w DNF, hit by the p-biased restriction R(p), fails to be a depth-t decision tree with probability at most (5pw)^t. Apply that to every bottom depth-2 subcircuit and the bottom two layers merge — depth drops by one. Iterate d−1 times and a subexponential depth-d circuit is a shallow tree.

Property 2: the target retains structure. f restricted by the same composition should stay "well-structured" — in particular still uncorrelated with any shallow decision tree.

Property 3: the composition completes to uniform. Evaluating f on a uniform input must be the same as: first apply the random restrictions to f, then evaluate the restricted function on a fresh uniform point. This is the one that turns "f and C disagree somewhere" (Properties 1 and 2, a worst-case fact) into "f and C disagree on half the inputs" (an average-case fact). Without Property 3 I only ever get worst-case.

For parity this is all easy and I should remember *why*, because the reason it's easy is the reason the depth hierarchy is hard. Target parity, restriction R(p): keep each variable alive w.p. p, else 0/1 each w.p. ½(1−p). Property 1 is the switching lemma. Property 3 holds because R(p) carves out a uniform random subcube and a uniform point in a uniform subcube is uniform — clean. Property 2 holds because parity↾ρ is just parity (or its negation) on the surviving coordinates, and a k-variable parity has zero correlation with any decision tree of depth < k; pick p so the survivor count is large and you're done. Everything works because parity is *much more complex than the circuit to begin with* — parity is provably outside AC⁰, so I'm fighting from a position of overwhelming strength.

The depth hierarchy isn't like that, and this is the crux. Now my target f is *itself a constant-depth circuit* — a depth-d formula, only one layer deeper than C, while C gets to be exponentially larger in size. The asymmetry that made parity easy is gone. So Property 2 becomes genuinely delicate: I need a target that survives the restrictions while the circuit dies, but the target is barely "more complex" than the circuit.

What's the target? Sipser's functions are the natural pick: read-once, monotone, depth-d, alternating AND/OR layers of roughly equal fan-in, fan-ins tuned so the formula is roughly balanced. They're self-similar — peel the bottom layer of a depth-d Sipser and you essentially get a depth-(d−1) Sipser. Good, that self-similarity is going to be the thing I lean on for Property 2.

So let me try the obvious: target = Sipser, restriction = R(p), and check Property 2. I hit Sipser with R(p). The bottom layer is, say, AND gates of fan-in around n^{1/d}. Under R(p) some of the inputs to a bottom AND get fixed to 0 — and then that whole AND is dead, value 0. Some get fixed to 1, some stay alive. The fan-ins of the bottom gates get scrambled to wildly different sizes; a constant fraction of bottom ANDs get killed outright; the careful balance of the Sipser formula is wrecked. The restricted function is some random mess, not a clean depth-(d−1) Sipser. Property 2 fails. So R(p) is out for the target side.

This is exactly the wall Håstad hit, and his fix is instructive. He designed *blockwise* restrictions matched to the Sipser block structure. Roughly: first, independently leave each variable starred w.p. 1−p else set it to 1; then per bottom block, with some probability set all the still-starred variables in the block to 0, else leave them starred. The point is these are engineered so that hitting Sipser reduces its depth by exactly one but otherwise preserves the structure — a bottom AND-block either gets satisfied (all 1's) or killed cleanly (a 0 appears) or survives with a controlled alive-set, and the next layer up inherits a clean Sipser-like gate. So Property 2 holds. And he shows a switching lemma still holds for circuits of small enough bottom fan-in, so Property 1 holds.

So what's wrong? Property 3. The blockwise restrictions are *not independent across coordinates* — they have to be correlated, because they're mirroring Sipser's blocks to preserve structure. And once they're correlated, their composition does not complete to the uniform distribution. In fact it doesn't complete to *any* product distribution. So Håstad only gets worst-case: no small depth-(d−1) circuit computes Sipser exactly. He cannot conclude anything about approximation.

Now I see the tension cleanly, and it's structural, not incidental. Property 2 *forces* correlations among the restricted coordinates — you have to respect Sipser's blocks to keep it intact. Property 3 *forbids* correlations — it wants the clean "uniform point in a uniform subcube" completion, which is a product structure. R(p) is on one horn (independent: gets 1 and 3, loses 2). Håstad's blockwise restriction is on the other (correlated: gets 1 and 2, loses 3). I'm being asked to be correlated and independent at the same time. Stare at that. As long as the only operation in my toolbox is "fix a variable or keep it alive," I think I'm genuinely stuck, because the correlation I need for Property 2 lives in exactly the same coordinates whose independence I need for Property 3.

So let me question the toolbox. What does a restriction actually *do*? It sends each formal variable x_i either to a constant in {0,1} or to itself (the ∗ case: x_i ↦ x_i). The alive variables map to themselves. That "to itself" is the only thing a restriction can do with a surviving variable. What if a surviving variable could map to *something else* — to a fresh variable in a different, smaller space?

Let me define it and see what it buys. Take the formal variables, grouped into blocks: 𝒳 = { x_{a,i} : a ∈ A, i ∈ [ℓ] }, the i-th variable in the a-th block. Associate a *smaller* space 𝒴 = { y_a : a ∈ A }, one new variable per block. A **projection** sends each x_{a,i} either to a constant ρ_{a,i} ∈ {0,1}, or — when ρ_{a,i} = ∗ — to the single new variable y_a of its block. So the surviving variables of a whole block all collapse onto the *same* fresh variable y_a. Formally, for ρ ∈ {0,1,∗}^{A×[ℓ]}, the ρ-projection of f is (proj_ρ f)(y) = f(x) with x_{a,i} = y_a if ρ_{a,i} = ∗ and x_{a,i} = ρ_{a,i} otherwise. When ℓ = 1 and 𝒴 ≡ 𝒳 this is exactly a restriction — restrictions are the special case where the only allowed "alive" map is x ↦ x. So projections strictly generalize restrictions. (I'm not the first to generalize restrictions by re-mapping variables — Impagliazzo and Segerlind did something in this spirit for constant-depth Frege lower bounds in proof complexity — but I haven't seen it turned on the average-case depth hierarchy.)

Why would collisions — distinct variables of a block landing on the same y_a — help me? Let me just compute what a projection does to a Sipser formula and watch. Picture a bottom AND gate on block a: it's AND over i of literals in x_{a,i}. Project: every x_{a,i} that's alive becomes y_a. So the gate becomes AND(y_a, y_a, …, y_a) — and AND(y_a,…,y_a) = y_a. The whole bottom gate collapses to the single variable y_a. The same for an OR bottom gate: OR(y_a,…,y_a) = y_a. So projection *peels off the bottom layer of Sipser exactly*, turning the depth-d formula into the depth-(d−1) formula on the new variables {y_a}, with no scrambling at all. Let me write the truncated formulas: let Sipser^{(k)} be Sipser with everything below depth k discarded and each depth-k gate replaced by a fresh variable. Then I claim

  proj Sipser^{(k)} ≡ Sipser^{(k−1)}.

That's it — projection *is* the operation that walks down the Sipser hierarchy by one. It's almost too on-the-nose; but of course it is, because I get to *design* the projection's block structure to match Sipser's gate structure. The collision isn't a side effect, it's the whole point: it's how I preserve structure (Property 2) by construction, not by luck.

And here's the second gift, which I didn't expect. The collision also helps kill the *approximator*. Suppose C has a bottom AND gate that happens to contain both x_{a,i} and the negation x̄_{a,j} from the same block a. Under a restriction those are two different surviving variables and the gate stays alive. Under a projection both become y_a, so the gate contains y_a ∧ ȳ_a = 0 — the gate is dead, for free, with no variable fixed. That's structural collapse that a restriction simply cannot produce. So projections give me *extra* simplification on the C side too, on top of the usual "set a variable, kill the gate." I'll need that.

Now the real question: can I make a *sequence* of projections complete to uniform (Property 3) even though each one is internally correlated (because of collisions)? With restrictions, "completes to uniform" came from the subcube picture. With projections it's subtler because the space is shrinking each stage. Let me think about what I'm building. I'll do d−1 projections, taking the function from 2^{A_d} = 2ⁿ down through 2^{A_{d-1}}, …, down to 2^{A_1} = 2^{w_0}, peeling one Sipser layer per stage. The first projection is from the original variables, associated with the *uniform* distribution (that's the distribution I ultimately care about). Subsequent projections will be associated with biased product distributions, and I'll choose the biases to make the per-stage telescoping exact.

Let me set up Sipser precisely so I can compute. I'll use a slight variant — call it BalancedSipser_d — read-once, monotone, alternating, all leaves at depth d, every variable at one leaf, depth-regular (every gate at a given depth has the same fan-in). The bottom-adjacent gates (depth d−1) are AND gates, so the root is OR if d is even, AND if d is odd. I only need to pin the fan-in sequence w_0, …, w_{d−1}. Let the bottom fan-in be

  w_{d−1} := m,  and set  p := 2^{−m},

so p is exactly the probability a bottom AND is satisfied by a uniform input. For the middle layers (1 ≤ k ≤ d−2) put

  w_k := w := ⌊ m·2^m / log e ⌋.

Why this w? I want each layer to stay roughly balanced. A depth-(d−2) OR gate sits above w bottom ANDs, each satisfied w.p. p under uniform; the OR is *un*satisfied w.p. (1−p)^w ≈ e^{−pw}. And pw = 2^{−m}·(m2^m/log e) = m/log(e) = m·ln 2, so (1−p)^w ≈ e^{−m ln 2} = 2^{−m} = p. The OR is unsatisfied with probability ≈ p — same scale as the AND below it is satisfied — and the formula is self-balancing layer to layer. Good, that's why w is what it is. Finally the top fan-in w_0 I'll fix later, as the smallest integer making the very top gate land at bias ≈ ½ (I'll need (1−t_1)^{q w_0} ≤ ½, where t_1, q come below). The number of variables is n = ∏_k w_k = w^{d−2}·m·w_0, which works out to n ≈ (1/log e)·(m2^m/log e)^{d−1}; so log n ≈ (d−1)·Θ(m), i.e. m ≈ log n / d. I'll address each gate/variable by its path: A_0 = {output}, A_k = A_{k−1} × [w_{k−1}], so A_d indexes the n leaves.

Now the projections. Two base probabilities will run the whole show:

  λ := (log w)^{3/2} / w^{5/4}  and  q := √p = 2^{−m/2}.

Let me say why these *before* I use them, because they're load-bearing. q is the "keep-alive" scale. If I keep each variable alive w.p. ≈ q, then a block of w variables has an alive-set of expected size qw = Θ̃(√w). That's the sweet spot: small enough that the approximator's bottom-2 layers will switch (alive-set roughly √w, not w), large enough that a Sipser gate's surviving fan-in stays huge in absolute terms (√w → ∞). And q = √p specifically because the bottom layer needs p (= 2^{−m}, the natural satisfaction scale) but every layer after wants the survivor count to shrink only gently, by √, so the formula survives all d−1 peels. λ is a tiny extra probability — the chance I *force a whole block to all-1's*. Why introduce that at all? Because it's the knob that makes two separate things close: in the switching-lemma weight-counting I'll need a multiplicative weight gap of order w^{1/4} per level, and λ/q³ is exactly w^{1/4} (check: λ/q³ = (log w)^{3/2} w^{−5/4} / ((log w)^{3/2} w^{−3/2}) = w^{−5/4+3/2} = w^{1/4} ✓); and in the completion-to-uniform algebra, that little λ all-1's event is what lets each block's probability mass close up exactly. I won't fully feel this until I do the computations, but I'm setting λ and q now because the rest is downstream of them.

The per-stage biases. I'll define a sequence t_{d−1}, …, t_1 by

  t_{d−1} := (p − λ)/q,  t_{k−1} := ((1−t_k)^{qw} − λ)/q  for k = d−1, …, 2.

These look pulled from nowhere; they're not — each one is *forced* by demanding that stage's projection completes to uniform, and I'll derive them when I prove Property 3. The k-th projection will be tied to the t_k-biased (or (1−t_k)-biased) product distribution. I'll need to know these t_k's don't run away as I iterate down d stages; let me just record the claim now and prove it in a moment: t_k = q ± q^{1.1} for all k. So every t_k is essentially q, which is essentially √(p) = Θ̃(√(log w / w)).

The initial projection R_init, from the n original variables 2^{A_{d−1}×[m]}. Independently for each bottom block a ∈ A_{d−1}, draw the block's m-bit restriction:

  with prob λ: all 1's, {1}^m;
  with prob q: {∗_{1/2}, 1_{1/2}}^m minus {1}^m (uniform over alive/1 strings, excluding all-1);
  with prob 1−λ−q: {0_{1/2}, 1_{1/2}}^m minus {1}^m (uniform over 0/1 strings, excluding all-1).

So a bottom block is either forced satisfied (all 1's, the λ event), or kept partly alive (the q event — these blocks survive, and project to a live y_a), or killed/fixed to constants with at least one 0 (the dominant event — this block's AND is falsified or fully determined). The "lift" of this — the value the bottom AND gate takes — is: 1 if the block is all-1's (λ event), ∗ (alive) if it's in the q event, 0 if it's in the last event. So the bottom AND gate's lifted value is 1 w.p. λ, ∗ w.p. q, 0 w.p. 1−λ−q. Exactly the three-way { 1_λ, ∗_q, 0_{1−λ−q} } picture, one level up.

Property 3 for this initial stage. I have to show that the projection composed with re-randomizing the survivors reproduces a uniform input. Concretely: draw ρ ← R_init; draw Y_a ∈ {0,1} for each alive block, with the *t_{d−1}-biased* coin Pr[Y_a = 1] = t_{d−1}; then form X_{a,i} = Y_a if ρ_{a,i} = ∗ else ρ_{a,i}. I claim X ← uniform {0,1}ⁿ. Blocks are independent, so it suffices to check one block a: is X_a ∈ {0,1}^m uniform? The all-1's outcome X_a = 1^m happens two ways: the λ branch (always all-1's), or the q branch with Y_a = 1 (the alive coords all become 1). So

  Pr[X_a = 1^m] = λ + q·Pr[Y_a = 1] = λ + q·t_{d−1} = λ + q·(p−λ)/q = λ + p − λ = p = 2^{−m}. ✓

That's the uniform mass on 1^m. Now any other string Z ≠ 1^m. It can arise from the last branch (prob 1−λ−q, drawing a uniform non-all-1 string, each such string mass (2^{−m})/(1−2^{−m})), or from the q branch with Y_a = 0 (the alive coords become 0; for this to produce Z the fixed coords must already match and the alive coords are the 0's). Carefully,

  Pr[X_a = Z] = (1−λ−q)·(p/(1−p)) + q·Pr[Y_a=0]·(p/(1−p))
        = (p/(1−p))·( (1−λ−q) + q(1−t_{d−1}) )
        = (p/(1−p))·( 1 − λ − q·t_{d−1} )
        = (p/(1−p))·( 1 − (λ + q t_{d−1}) )
        = (p/(1−p))·(1 − p) = p = 2^{−m}. ✓

using λ + q t_{d−1} = p again. So every string of {0,1}^m gets mass p = 2^{−m} — that's the uniform distribution. And *this is the derivation that forces t_{d−1}*: the choice t_{d−1} = (p−λ)/q is precisely what makes λ + q t_{d−1} = p, which is what makes the all-1 mass and the everything-else mass simultaneously equal 2^{−m}. The biases aren't free parameters; they're solved for.

Now the subsequent projections, and here I have to make a choice I didn't have to make for parity: the projections will be **adaptive**. The k-th projection's distribution depends on the *outcome* of the (k−1)-th. Why? Because after a stage, different blocks have different alive-set sizes |S_a| (random), and to keep completion-to-uniform *exact* I need the per-block keep-alive probability to react to |S_a|. With parity, every restriction in the sequence is the same fixed R(p), non-adaptive; here I can't afford that. So let me define the subsequent projection given the previous outcome τ.

I'll use ∘/• notation to fold the two alternation cases together: at level k, a ∘-value means "the value that keeps the gate undetermined-toward-survival" and • its complement; for d−k even the depth-(k−1) gates are AND so ∘ = 1, • = 0, and for d−k odd they're OR so ∘ = 0, • = 1. The "lift" τ̂ of τ records what each depth-(k−1) gate evaluates to given its block's inputs: ∘ if any input forces it (a • appears), • if the whole block is ∘ (gate determined the other way), ∗ if the block is in {∗,∘} but not all-∘ (gate still alive). I also need a notion of which alive-sets are "good": call S ⊆ [w_{k−1}] **k-acceptable** if |S| = qw ± w^{β(k,d)}, where β(k,d) := 1/3 + (d−k−1)/(12d) (note 1/3 ≤ β ≤ 5/12 < ½ always). The deviation window β grows as k decreases toward the root — I'll justify that exact form when I prove typicality bootstraps; intuitively, errors accumulate over stages so I must allow a touch more slack near the top, but never enough (< ½) to lose Chernoff control.

Subsequent projection R(τ). Independently for each block a at the new level, let S_a = τ_a^{−1}(∗) be its alive coords. If the gate is already determined toward ∘ (τ̂_a = ∘) or S_a is not k-acceptable, just re-randomize the alive coords with the t_k-biased coin: ρ(S_a) ← {•_{t_k}, ∘_{1−t_k}}^{S_a}. Otherwise (τ̂_a = ∗ and S_a is k-acceptable), draw the block analogously to R_init:

  with prob λ: all-∘ on S_a;
  with prob q_a: {∗_{t_k}, ∘_{1−t_k}}^{S_a} minus all-∘ (kept alive);
  with prob 1−λ−q_a: {•_{t_k}, ∘_{1−t_k}}^{S_a} minus all-∘ (determined),

where I *define* q_a to make completion close:

  q_a := ((1−t_k)^{|S_a|} − λ) / t_{k−1},  i.e.  (1−t_k)^{|S_a|} = λ + q_a t_{k−1}.

This is the exact analogue of the t_{d−1} computation, now per block, with |S_a| in the exponent. Check completion for one block, d−k even (the other case is the bitwise dual). Draw ρ ← R(τ), draw Y_a with Pr[Y_a = 1] = t_{k−1} for alive blocks, and set Z_{a,i} = Y_a if ρ_{a,i} = ∗ else ρ_{a,i}, over i ∈ S_a. I claim Z_a ← {0_{t_k}, 1_{1−t_k}}^{S_a} (the t_k-biased product, since ∘=1 here). All-1's:

  Pr[Z_a = 1^{S_a}] = λ + q_a·Pr[Y_a = 1] = λ + q_a t_{k−1} = (1−t_k)^{|S_a|}, ✓

by the definition of q_a. And for Z ≠ 1^{S_a} with u := #zeros,

  Pr[Z_a = Z] = (1−λ−q_a)·(t_k^u (1−t_k)^{|S_a|−u})/(1−(1−t_k)^{|S_a|}) + q_a·Pr[Y_a=0]·(same fraction)
        = (t_k^u (1−t_k)^{|S_a|−u})/(1−(1−t_k)^{|S_a|}) · ( (1−λ−q_a) + q_a(1−t_{k−1}) )
        = (t_k^u (1−t_k)^{|S_a|−u})/(1−(1−t_k)^{|S_a|}) · ( 1 − λ − q_a t_{k−1} )
        = (t_k^u (1−t_k)^{|S_a|−u})/(1−(1−t_k)^{|S_a|}) · ( 1 − (1−t_k)^{|S_a|} )
        = t_k^u (1−t_k)^{|S_a|−u}. ✓

That's the t_k-biased product mass on Z. So each subsequent stage's projection, composed with t_{k−1}-biased re-randomization of survivors, regenerates the t_k-biased product distribution on the previous level. And q_a is solved for, exactly as t_{d−1} was. The blocks are independent, so the whole level is a product. Chain these together — the (k)-th stage turns a t_{k−1}-biased input into a t_k-biased one — and an inductive walk from k = 2 up to k = d−1, capped by the R_init lemma, gives the full statement: hitting f with the whole projection sequence Ψ and then evaluating on a t_1-biased fresh point Y is *identical in distribution* to evaluating f on a uniform X. Precisely, for any f, g,

  Pr_{X~unif}[ f(X) ≠ g(X) ] = Pr_{Y}[ (Ψ(f))(Y) ≠ (Ψ(g))(Y) ],

where Y is t_1-biased (or (1−t_1)-biased if d is odd), and Ψ(f) := proj_{ρ^{(2)}} ⋯ proj_{ρ^{(d)}} f with ρ^{(d)} ← R_init and ρ^{(k)} ← R(ρ̂^{(k+1)}). Property 3 — done, and done *despite* every individual projection being heavily correlated across coordinates. The correlation is real, but it gets absorbed: a uniform point in the original space is reproduced by stagewise correlated projections plus biased re-randomization, because the biases t_k and the all-∘ event λ are exactly tuned to make each block's mass telescope. The thing Håstad's blockwise restriction couldn't do, projections do — the extra freedom of mapping survivors to a fresh, smaller variable space is what creates room for a clean product completion.

Let me close the loose end about the t_k's not drifting, since the whole completion machinery and the switching-lemma error depend on every t_k ≈ q. Claim: |t_k q − p| ≤ (2m)^{d−1−k} λ, by downward induction on k. Base k = d−1: t_{d−1} q − p = (p−λ) − p = −λ, so |t_{d−1}q − p| = λ ✓ (equality). Inductive step, suppose it for ℓ+1; I want it for ℓ. From the definition, t_ℓ q = (1−t_{ℓ+1})^{qw} − λ, so I bound (1−t_{ℓ+1})^{qw} − λ near p. Write (1−t_{ℓ+1})^{qw} = ((1−t_{ℓ+1})^{1/t_{ℓ+1}})^{qw t_{ℓ+1}}. The base (1−x)^{1/x} sits in [e^{−1}(1−x), e^{−1}] for the relevant x, so I can sandwich. Upper bound: (1−t_{ℓ+1})^{qw} ≤ exp(−qw t_{ℓ+1}) ≤ exp(−w(p − (2m)^{d−ℓ−2}λ)) by the inductive hypothesis (q t_{ℓ+1} ≥ p − (2m)^{d−ℓ−2}λ). Now w·p = (m2^m/log e)·2^{−m} = m/log e, so exp(−wp) = exp(−m/log e) = exp(−m ln 2) = 2^{−m} = p (using log e = 1/ln 2, so m/log e = m ln 2). Carrying the correction term, exp(−w(p − ε)) = p·exp(wε) with ε = (2m)^{d−ℓ−2}λ, and wε = (m2^m/log e)(2m)^{d−ℓ−2}λ, which after using λ = Θ̃(2^{−5m/4}) is tiny; expanding exp to first order gives an additive slack absorbed into (2m)^{d−ℓ−1}λ. So (1−t_{ℓ+1})^{qw} − λ ≤ p + (2m)^{d−ℓ−1}λ. The lower bound is symmetric, using (1−t_{ℓ+1})^{1/t_{ℓ+1}} ≥ e^{−1}(1−t_{ℓ+1}) and 1 − qw t_{ℓ+1}^2 corrections, all of which are lower-order because qw t_{ℓ+1}^2 = Θ̃(1)·q ≪ 1 and λ/t_{ℓ+1} ≪ p, landing at p − (2m)^{d−ℓ−1}λ. Hence |t_ℓ q − p| ≤ (2m)^{d−ℓ−1}λ, completing the induction. Since (2m)^{d}λ stays ≪ q² for d ≤ cm/log m, we get t_k = q ± q^{1.1} for all k. And q_a is pinned the same way: q_a = q·(1 ± 2 t_k w^{β(k,d)}) = q ± o(q). Good — every bias is essentially q, as promised, for the whole range of d I care about.

Now Property 1: I need the approximator to die under Ψ, and the engine is a switching lemma — but for *projections*, not restrictions. State the target: F a depth-2 circuit (width-r DNF, say) over the block variables; show proj_ρ F is a shallow decision tree (over the *new* variables 𝒴) with high probability under R(τ). The standard switching lemma analyzes the restricted DNF F↾ρ; I instead analyze the *projected* DNF proj_ρ F = proj(F↾ρ), and the projection can make the decision-tree depth genuinely smaller than F↾ρ's would be, because of collisions (the y_a ∧ ȳ_a = 0 phenomenon). So I want to adopt Razborov's encoding-proof strategy — Beame's primer and Thapen's notes give it cleanly, and Thapen even does the blockwise variant within this framework — and bend it to projections.

Razborov's strategy, recalled. Let ℬ ⊆ {0,1,∗}ⁿ be the *bad* restrictions: those ρ for which proj_ρ F is not a depth-s decision tree. I'll encode each bad ρ injectively as a *refined* restriction ρ' (one that fixes a few more coordinates than ρ) plus a small amount of auxiliary information. Two properties: (a) the encoding is uniquely decodable (an injection), and (b) ρ' has *larger* probability than ρ under the distribution, by a big multiplicative factor Γ. Then for each fixed value of the auxiliary information, the total mass of bad ρ's sharing it is ≤ 1/Γ (their images ρ' are distinct and their masses sum to ≤ 1). Union-bound over the auxiliary strings to bound the mass of ℬ.

I need a canonical decision tree to define "bad path." Define the **canonical projection decision tree** ProjDT(G) of a DNF G over 𝒳, which *queries variables in 𝒴*. If G ≡ 1 or 0, output it. Else let T be the first term of G not ≡ 0; let η = { y_a : some x_{a,i} occurs in T } ⊆ 𝒴; query all of η in the first |η| levels; for each assignment π ∈ {0,1}^η, recurse on G↾(η ↦ π) — where (η ↦ π) is the restriction sending x_{a,i} to π(y_a) if y_a ∈ η, else ∗ (note the value is the same for all i in the block — that's the collision showing up in the tree). This computes proj G. The key contrast with Håstad/Razborov: their canonical tree computes the DNF itself; mine computes the *projection* of it, querying the merged variables y_a. That's where collisions give extra depth savings.

The encoding. Fix τ, and ℬ = { ρ refining τ : proj_ρ F not a depth-s DT }. For bad ρ, since proj_ρ F = proj(F↾ρ) isn't a depth-s tree, ProjDT(F↾ρ) has a root-to-leaf path of length ≥ s; take the leftmost such path π. Walk it, building, for ℓ = 1, …, j (j = least index with |η_1 ∪ ⋯ ∪ η_j| ≥ s):
- T_ℓ = first term of F not falsified by ρ(η_1↦π^1)⋯(η_{ℓ−1}↦π^{ℓ−1}); η_ℓ = { y_a : some x_{a,i} occurs in T_ℓ under that hybrid }.
- σ^ℓ: for each y_a ∈ η_ℓ and i, set σ^ℓ_{a,i} = 1 if x_{a,i} occurs positively in the hybrid term, 0 if negatively, and 0 if ρ_{a,i} = ∗ but x_{a,i} doesn't occur. (All other entries ∗.) This σ^ℓ is built to *satisfy* T_ℓ under the hybrid: T_ℓ↾ρ(η_1↦π^1)⋯(η_{ℓ−1}↦π^{ℓ−1})σ^ℓ ≡ 1. The "break symmetry, set the non-occurring alive coords to 0" choice is deliberate: it lets the decoder undo σ^ℓ without spending bits to name those coords, because for any y_a ∈ η_ℓ the block ρ_a was in {∗,1}^w minus all-1 (it survived toward ∗), so any 0 in (ρσ)_a *must* have been a ∗ in ρ — the decoder reads it off.
- γ_ℓ = { x_{a,i} : σ^ℓ_{a,i} = 1 } (the new 1's), encoded by a length-r Hamming-weight-|γ_ℓ| string locating them inside T_ℓ. And encode(η_ℓ) ∈ {0,1}^{|η_ℓ|(1+log r)}: per y_a, log r bits for the location of the first occurring x_{a,i} in T_ℓ, plus one bit flagging the last element of η_ℓ. π^ℓ is the corresponding slice of π.
Trim η_j, π^j at the end so the total is exactly s; redefine σ^j, γ_j accordingly.

The map: θ(ρ) = (ρσ, π', encode(η), encode(γ)) ∈ {0,1,∗}^{A×[w]} × {0,1}^s × {0,1}^{s(1+log r)} × {0,1}^{rs}, with σ = σ^1⋯σ^j, π' = π^1⋯π^j, etc.

Injectivity. The decoder gets (ρσ, π', encode(η), encode(γ)). The first term of F not falsified by ρσ is T_1 (because σ was designed so T_1 is satisfied — hence not falsified — under ρσ, and no earlier term was un-falsified by ρ, which σ only refines). With T_1 in hand, encode(η_1) and encode(γ_1) recover η_1 and γ_1. Now undo σ^1: for each y_a ∈ η_1, set (ρσ)_{a,i} back to ∗ for every i with (ρσ)_{a,i} = 0 OR x_{a,i} ∈ γ_1. This is exactly right because σ^1 set a coord to {0,1} iff ρ was ∗ there, and to 1 iff x_{a,i} ∈ γ_1 — so the 0's and the γ_1-marked 1's are precisely the coordinates σ^1 touched; everything else in the block was already fixed by ρ. Apply π^1 to get the hybrid ρ(η_1↦π^1)σ^2⋯σ^j, and repeat for ℓ = 2, …, j. At the end recover ρ(η_1↦π^1)⋯(η_j↦π^j) and η; set the η-coordinates back to ∗ to recover ρ. So θ is an injection. (This is precisely Thapen's decode walk — first-un-falsified-term, recover the block, undo via the recorded positive literals — generalized so the "block" is a projected block and the recorded objects name merged variables y_a.)

The weight gap, where projections pay off. I need ξ(ρσ)/ξ(ρ) large, where ξ is the R(τ) probability mass. The mass factorizes over blocks; ρ and ρσ differ in exactly the s blocks with y_a ∈ η. For such a block, S_a is k-acceptable and ρ(S_a) ∈ {∗,1}^{S_a} minus all-1 (it survived), while (ρσ)(S_a) ∈ {0,1}^{S_a} (fully fixed by σ). Let Δ_a = #(new 1's σ put in block a). Computing ζ_a((ρσ)(S_a))/ζ_a(ρ(S_a)) from the R(τ) mass function gives, when (ρσ)(S_a) = all-1's, (λ/q_a)·(1−(1−t_k)^{|S_a|})/(t_k^{Δ_a}(1−t_k)^{|S_a|−Δ_a}); and otherwise ((1−λ−q_a)/q_a)·((1−t_k)/t_k)^{Δ_a}. Now use k-acceptability: |S_a| = qw ± w^{β(k,d)}, so (1−t_k)^{|S_a|} ≤ (1−t_k)^{qw}/(1−t_k)^{w^{β}} = (q t_{k−1}+λ)/(1−t_k)^{w^β} ≤ (q t_{k−1}+λ)/(1 − t_k w^β) ≤ 2q² (since q t_{k−1} = Θ(q²) and λ ≪ q², and t_k w^β = o(1) because β < ½). With q_a ≤ 2q, the first branch is ≥ (λ/(8q³))·((1−t_k)/t_k)^{Δ_a} = Ω(w^{1/4})·((1−t_k)/t_k)^{Δ_a} — λ/q³ = w^{1/4}, exactly the reason λ was set to (log w)^{3/2}/w^{5/4}. The second branch is ((1−λ−q_a)/q_a)·((1−t_k)/t_k)^{Δ_a} = Ω(√(w/log w))·((1−t_k)/t_k)^{Δ_a}, even larger. So in both cases

  ζ_a((ρσ)(S_a))/ζ_a(ρ(S_a)) = Ω(w^{1/4})·((1−t_k)/t_k)^{Δ_a}.

Multiplying over the s differing blocks, and noting Σ_a Δ_a = ‖ϑ_4‖ (the Hamming weight of the γ-encoding, i.e. the total new 1's),

  ξ(ρσ)/ξ(ρ) = (Ω(w^{1/4}))^s · ((1−t_k)/t_k)^{‖ϑ_4‖}.

Therefore, fixing the auxiliary tuple (ϑ_2, ϑ_3, ϑ_4) = (π', encode(η), encode(γ)),

  Pr_{ρ←R(τ)}[ ρ ∈ ℬ with that aux ] = Σ_ρ ξ(ρ) = (O(w^{−1/4}))^s · (t_k/(1−t_k))^{‖ϑ_4‖} · Σ_ρ ξ(ρσ) ≤ (O(w^{−1/4}))^s · (t_k/(1−t_k))^{‖ϑ_4‖},

since the ρσ are distinct (injectivity) and ξ is a probability mass (Σ ≤ 1). Now sum over the auxiliary information. The factor (t_k/(1−t_k))^{‖ϑ_4‖} means I must *not* crudely union-bound over all 2^{rs} possible ϑ_4 — I stratify by Hamming weight i:

  Σ_{ϑ_4} (t_k/(1−t_k))^{‖ϑ_4‖} = Σ_{i=0}^{rs} C(rs, i) (t_k/(1−t_k))^i = (1 + t_k/(1−t_k))^{rs} = (1/(1−t_k))^{rs} = (e^{t_k/(1−t_k)})^{rs}·(1±o(1)).

So summing over ϑ_4 contributes (e^{r t_k/(1−t_k)})^s, and the remaining union over 2^s choices of ϑ_2 and (2r)^s of ϑ_3 is absorbed into the O(·). The projection switching lemma:

  Pr_{ρ←R(τ)}[ proj_ρ F not a depth-s DT ] = (O( r·e^{r t_k/(1−t_k)}·w^{−1/4} ))^s.

For R_init the calculation is the same but simpler: the per-block weight ratio is (λ(1−p)/(qp)) or ((1−λ−q)/q), both Ω(w^{1/4}) with *no* dependence on Δ_a (because R_init re-randomizes uniformly, not t_k-biased), so I don't stratify and just union-bound over all 2^{rs} of ϑ_4:

  Pr_{ρ←R_init}[ proj_ρ F not a depth-s DT ] = (O( r·2^r·w^{−1/4} ))^s.

Now run it bottom-up. For a circuit C with bottom fan-in r = (1/5)m (small), R_init switches every bottom depth-2 subcircuit to a depth-(w^{1/5}) tree, hence width-w^{1/5} CNF/DNF, except w.p. S·4^{−w^{1/5}}; that drops C's depth by one. Then each subsequent R(τ) with r = s = w^{1/5} (now t_k ≈ q ≈ √(log w/w) so r t_k/(1−t_k) = w^{1/5}·Θ̃(w^{−1/2}) = o(1), and the e^{rt_k/(1−t_k)} factor is just (1+o(1))) does another depth drop. After d−1 stages Ψ(C) is a depth-w^{1/5} decision tree, except with probability exp(−Ω(w^{1/5})). Translating to n (recall log n ≈ (d−1)Θ(m) and w ≈ m2^m, so w^{1/5} ≈ n^{Θ(1/(d−1))}): a depth-d circuit of bottom fan-in ≤ log n/10(d−1) and size ≤ 2^{n^{1/6(d−1)}} collapses to a depth-n^{1/4(d−1)} tree w.p. 1 − exp(−Ω(n^{1/6(d−1)})). That's Property 1 for the small-bottom-fan-in case.

The other case — C has *unbounded* bottom fan-in but the *opposite* top alternation to Sipser. I can't apply the switching lemma to its bottom layer (fan-in too big). But I can *trim*: replace every too-wide bottom gate by a constant (an AND of fan-in > log(S/ε) is, on a uniform input, almost always 0 — it takes its minority value on ≤ ε/S of inputs), getting a circuit C′ that is ε-close to C with bottom fan-in log(S/ε). Take ε = 1/S, bottom fan-in 2 log S ≤ w^{1/5}. Now C′ has small bottom fan-in, apply the same bottom-up argument: Ψ(C′) is (1/S)-close to a width-w^{1/5} CNF (if top gate AND) or DNF (if top gate OR), w.p. 1 − exp(−Ω(w^{1/5})). The top gate's type is preserved through the peeling, so it ends opposite to Ψ(Sipser)'s top. Property 1 for the opposite-alternation case.

Property 2: Sipser survives. I have proj Sipser^{(k)} ≡ Sipser^{(k−1)} already; lifting it through restrictions, proj_ρ Sipser^{(k)} ≡ Sipser^{(k−1)}↾ρ̂. Chaining over the whole sequence Ψ collapses the target all the way to the top:

  Ψ(Sipser_d) ≡ Sipser_d^{(1)}↾ρ̂^{(2)},

a *single* OR (d even) or AND (d odd) gate of fan-in w_0, restricted by the lift of the last projection. So "Sipser retains structure" reduces to: the lifted top gate is a wide-enough OR with bias ≈ ½ under Y. But this is a high-probability statement — each of the d−1 stages can fail (e.g. an R_init outcome where *every* bottom block has a 0 sets the whole Sipser to constant 0 immediately). I have to control all the failures at once. So I define a high-probability good event and show it bootstraps.

Typicality. Call τ at level k **typical** if (1) every block's alive-set τ_a^{−1}(∗) is k-acceptable, |·| = qw ± w^{β(k,d)}; and (2) every lifted block has all-but-w^{4/5} of its entries still ∗, |(τ̂_α)^{−1}(∗)| ≥ w_{k−2} − w^{4/5}. Reading the picture: condition (1) says each gate-block keeps a √-sized alive fan-in; condition (2) says the *vast majority* of next-level gates remain undetermined (only ≤ w^{4/5} ≪ w of them get forced). Together they say a typical projection (i) wipes out the bottom-level gates, (ii) trims the level-(k−1) fan-ins from w down to Θ̃(√w), and (iii) keeps every level-(k−2) gate alive with fan-in ≥ w_{k−2}(1−o(1)). The whole upper structure of Sipser, levels 0 through k−3, is *untouched* — a perfect copy of Sipser^{(k−3)} survives each stage.

Now bootstrap. (I) Initial: ρ̂ for ρ ← R_init is typical w.p. 1 − e^{−Ω̃(w^{1/6})}. Condition (1): each bottom block is ∗ w.p. q independently, so the alive count concentrates: with μ = qw = Θ((w log w)^{1/2}) and target deviation w^{1/3}, γ = w^{1/3}/μ = Θ(w^{−1/6}(log w)^{−1/2}), and Chernoff gives Pr[|alive − qw| > w^{1/3}] ≤ exp(−Ω(γ²μ)) = exp(−Ω̃(w^{1/6})). Condition (2): a level-(d−2) OR gate (one entry of the lift) is forced to 1 iff some bottom AND below it is all-1's (prob λ each, so the OR-entry is 1 w.p. 1−(1−λ)^w ≤ λw), and forced to 0 iff every bottom AND below is 0 (prob (1−λ−q)^w ≤ e^{−qw}); so each entry is non-∗ w.p. ≤ λw + e^{−qw} = Õ((log w)^{3/2} w^{−1/4}), the expected number of non-∗ entries in a block is Õ(w^{3/4}) ≪ w^{4/5}, and Chernoff gives Pr[> w^{4/5} non-∗] ≤ e^{−Ω(√w)}. Union over the ≤ n ≤ w^{O(d)} blocks, with d ≤ c log w/log log w, keeps the failure at e^{−Ω̃(w^{1/6})}.

(II) Preservation: if τ at level k+1 is typical, then ρ̂ for ρ ← R(τ̂) is typical w.p. 1 − e^{−Ω(w^{1/6})}. Condition (1): a block's new alive count is Σ_{i ∈ S_a} q_{a,i}, with q_{a,i} = q(1 ± 2 t_{k+1} w^{β(k+1,d)}) by the q_a estimate, and |S_a| ∈ [w − w^{4/5}, w] (from τ's condition 2). So the expectation is qw ± Õ(w^{β(k+1,d)}), and since w^{β(k,d)} = ω(polylog(w)·w^{β(k+1,d)}) — this is exactly why β grows toward the root, to outrun the per-stage error blow-up, and exactly where d ≤ c log w/log log w is used — Chernoff lands the alive count in qw ± w^{β(k,d)} except w.p. exp(−Ω(w^{2β(k,d)}/qw)) = exp(−Ω̃(w^{2β(k,d)−1/2})) ≤ e^{−Ω(w^{1/6})}. Condition (2): each next-next-level gate is forced (•) only if a whole block of survivors goes all-∘ — probability ∏(1−λ−q_{a,i}) ≤ (1−q/2)^{w−w^{4/5}} ≤ e^{−qw/4} = e^{−Ω(√(w log w))} — or forced (∘) w.p. ≤ λw; so each entry is non-∗ w.p. Õ(w^{−1/4}), expected non-∗ count Õ(w^{3/4}), and Chernoff bounds the deviation past w^{4/5} by e^{−Ω(√w)}. Union over levels. So typicality at one level yields it at the next, and chaining from the typical initial gives: with probability 1 − d·e^{−Ω̃(w^{1/6})}, *all* d−1 lifts ρ̂^{(d)}, …, ρ̂^{(2)} are typical.

The bias half. On the all-typical event, Ψ(Sipser_d) = OR_{w_0}↾ρ̂^{(2)} where the last lift ρ̂^{(2)} is in {0,∗}^{w_0} with |(ρ̂^{(2)})^{−1}(∗)| ≈ q w_0. I need this restricted OR to be ≈½-biased under Y (t_1-biased). The surviving OR is OR over the alive set S(ρ̂), so under Y (each coord 1 w.p. t_1), Pr[OR = 0] = (1−t_1)^{|S(ρ̂)|}. With |S(ρ̂)| = q w_0 ± w^{β(1,d)} and the defining choice (1−t_1)^{q w_0} ≤ ½ — and its sharpened corollary ½ ≥ (1−t_1)^{q w_0} ≥ ½(1 − Θ(2^{−m})), which is *why* w_0 was set to the smallest integer making (1−t_1)^{q w_0} ≤ ½ — I get

  Pr[OR↾ρ̂ = 0] = (1−t_1)^{|S(ρ̂)|} = (½ ± Θ(log w/w))·(1−t_1)^{±w^{β(1,d)}} = ½ ± Õ(w^{−1/12}),

using (1−t_1)^{w^{β(1,d)}} = 1 ± t_1 w^{β(1,d)} = 1 ± Õ(w^{−1/12}) (here β(1,d) ≤ 5/12, t_1 = Θ̃(w^{−1/2}), so t_1 w^{β} ≤ Õ(w^{−1/12})). So the projected target's bias is ½ − Õ(w^{−1/12}) in expectation. Taking expectation over Ψ and folding in the all-typical probability:

  E_Ψ[ bias(Sipser_d^{(1)}↾ρ̂^{(2)}, Y) ] ≥ ½ − Õ(w^{−1/12}).

(And as a free corollary, combining with completion-to-uniform: Pr_X[Sipser_d(X) = 1] = ½ ± Õ(w^{−1/12}) — BalancedSipser is genuinely balanced, the prerequisite I needed for any ½-inapproximability claim.)

The bottoming-out clash. After Ψ, the target is a near-½-biased single OR (Property 2) and the approximator is a small-width CNF of the opposite top gate (Property 1) — both as restricted functions on {0,1}^{w_0} under Y. I need: a near-½-biased OR has low correlation with any small-width opposite CNF. This is essentially O'Donnell–Wimmer's base-case lemma, slightly extended. Let OR_S = OR over the alive set S (the restricted top gate), F a width-r CNF. First, F may as well depend only on S: averaging over the off-S coordinates, some fixing F↾ρ does at least as well, so WLOG F depends only on S. Two cases. (Case 1) every clause of F contains some negated S-variable: then F(0^S) = 1 while OR_S(0^S) = 0, so Pr[OR_S ≠ F] ≥ Pr[all Y_i = 0, i ∈ S] = Pr[OR_S = 0]. (Case 2) some clause is monotone (only positive literals); since each positive literal is true w.p. t_1 and the clause has width ≤ r, Pr[F = 1] ≤ Pr[that clause = 1] ≤ r t_1, so Pr[OR_S ≠ F] ≥ Pr[OR_S = 1] − Pr[F = 1] ≥ Pr[OR_S = 1] − r t_1. Either way

  Pr[OR_S(Y) ≠ F(Y)] ≥ min{ Pr[OR_S = 1], Pr[OR_S = 0] } − r t_1 = bias(OR_S, Y) − r t_1.

Now assemble. By completion-to-uniform and the target characterization,

  Pr_X[Sipser_d(X) ≠ C(X)] = E_Ψ[ Pr_Y[ (OR_{w_0}↾ρ̂^{(2)})(Y) ≠ (Ψ(C))(Y) ] ].

For each outcome Ψ, condition on whether Ψ(C) is a depth-r DT (equivalently a width-r CNF, taking the worse of CNF/DNF). When it is, the base-case lemma gives Pr_Y[≠] ≥ bias(OR↾ρ̂, Y) − r t_1; when it isn't, lose at most 1 (an indicator). Set r = n^{1/4(d−1)}, take expectation:

  Pr_X[Sipser_d ≠ C] ≥ E_Ψ[bias(OR↾ρ̂^{(2)}, Y)] − r t_1 − Pr_Ψ[Ψ(C) not a depth-r DT]
        ≥ ( ½ − Õ(w^{−1/12}) ) − r t_1 − exp(−Ω(n^{1/6(d−1)})).

Now r t_1 = n^{1/4(d−1)}·Θ̃(w^{−1/2}); since w ≈ m2^m and w^{1/4(d−1)} ≈ n^{1/4(d−1)} with m ≈ log n/d, this is n^{−Ω(1/d)} = o(1). The Õ(w^{−1/12}) term is n^{−Ω(1/d)}. The failure exp(−Ω(n^{1/6(d−1)})) is negligible. So

  Pr_X[Sipser_d(X) ≠ C(X)] ≥ ½ − n^{−Ω(1/d)},

for any depth-d circuit C of size ≤ 2^{n^{1/6(d−1)}} with bottom fan-in ≤ log n/10(d−1). That's the first main lower bound. The opposite-alternation case runs identically, except Property 1 gives a width-r CNF only (1/S)-close to Ψ(C), so I lose an extra 1/S in the bound — negligible — and the top-gate mismatch is exactly what the base-case lemma's two-case split needs. Second main lower bound.

And the headline drops out: any depth-(d−1) circuit of size S can be viewed as a depth-d circuit with bottom fan-in 1 (≤ log n/10(d−1)) — so it's covered by the first theorem — and any depth-d circuit with the wrong top alternation is covered by the second. Hence

  for 2 ≤ d ≤ c√(log n)/log log n, any depth-(d−1) circuit of size ≤ 2^{n^{1/6(d−1)}} agrees with BalancedSipser_d on at most (½ + n^{−Ω(1/d)})·2ⁿ inputs.

This is exactly why projections were the thing missing and restrictions never could have been enough: the same collision that peels a Sipser layer for free and kills contradictory approximator literals for free is, once the biases t_k and q_a are solved rather than guessed, also what lets a correlated sequence of stages complete back to uniform. The worst-case depth hierarchy becomes an average-case one, for every d up to ≈ √(log n)/log log n — settling the random-oracle polynomial-hierarchy question this was built to reach, and closing off any approximate converse to the total-influence bound.
