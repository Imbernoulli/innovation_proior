Let me start from what the layer actually has to do, because the constraint is unusually tight and I think it does most of the work. The backbone hands me a tensor `X` of shape `[B, C, H, W]`. The classifier head wants `[B, C]`. So for each image, for each of the `C` channels, I must turn an `H × W` grid of activations into one number. The optimizer, the schedule, the loss, the augmentation — all fixed, none of mine to touch. I have no labels and no data inside this layer; all I see at run time is one channel's grid of numbers. And the grid sizes vary wildly across the backbones I have to serve: `8×8` for ResNet on CIFAR, all the way down to `1×1` after VGG's max-pool stack. Whatever I build has to be a single rule, parameter-light if I can manage it, that turns `N = H·W` activations into one scalar and does it well for ResNet, VGG, and MobileNet at once. So the whole question is: which scalar summary of a feature map is most discriminative, given that I have to commit to one rule for everything.

The two obvious summaries are sitting right there. Average the grid, or take its max. Global average pooling is the modern default — average each map, get a `C`-vector, no parameters, robust to translation, works at any spatial size. Global max pooling reads off the single strongest activation per channel. The folklore says max usually beats average inside CNNs, and there is a clean empirical demonstration of that on a fixed architecture. But "usually" is doing a lot of work in that sentence, and I don't want folklore; I want to know *why* one beats the other and *when*, because if the answer is "it depends," then committing to either one is leaving accuracy on the table for the cases where I picked wrong.

So let me actually reason about it rather than trust the ranking. Take one channel and think about what its `N` activations look like, and what makes the pooled scalar useful — useful meaning: the pooled value should be different for an image where this channel's concept is present versus absent, and reliably so. That is a separability question. Treat it as signal-to-noise: how far apart are the pooled-value distributions for the two cases, relative to how wide each distribution is. Big mean gap, small spread, easy to classify.

Let me get concrete with a tractable model so I can compute, not hand-wave. Say each location of the channel either fires or doesn't, a Bernoulli with activation rate `α` — `α` is high for images where the concept is present, low where it's absent. Pool over `P = N` locations, treat them as independent for the calculation (they're not, really, neighboring locations are correlated, but the independent model is the one I can compute and it tends to predict the right qualitative behavior).

Average pooling first. `f_a = (1/P) Σ vᵢ`, a sum of `P` i.i.d. Bernoulli(`α`) scaled by `1/P`. The mean is `µ_a = α`. The variance is `Var[(1/P)Σ vᵢ] = (1/P²)·P·α(1-α) = α(1-α)/P`. Now stare at this. The mean `µ_a = α` does not depend on `P` at all — average pooling just estimates the activation rate, and the rate is what it is no matter how many locations I average. But the variance shrinks like `1/P`. So if I have two classes with rates `α₁` and `α₂`, the gap between the means is `|α₁ - α₂|`, *fixed*, while the standard deviations shrink like `1/√P`. The separation ratio — mean gap over spread — therefore *grows* like `√P`. Averaging over more locations gives me a cleaner read on the rate. That makes sense: average pooling is in the business of estimating a mean, and more samples means a better mean estimate. Good in the large-pool, dense-feature regime.

Now max pooling, and this is where it gets interesting. `f_m = maxᵢ vᵢ` over `P` Bernoullis. The max is 1 unless every one of the `P` is 0, so `f_m` is itself Bernoulli with mean `µ_m = 1 - (1-α)^P` and variance `σ_m² = (1 - (1-α)^P)(1-α)^P`. Look at the mean: at `P = 1` it's just `α`, and as `P` grows `(1-α)^P → 0`, so `µ_m → 1`. The max-pooled value climbs from `α` toward 1 as the pool gets bigger — eventually, with enough locations, *some* location fires almost surely and the max saturates at 1 for both classes. So the mean separation for max pooling is `φ(P) = |(1-α₂)^P - (1-α₁)^P|`. At `P = 1`, `φ = |α₁ - α₂|`, same as average. For large `P`, both `(1-α)^P → 0`, so `φ → 0` — the separation *vanishes* because both classes saturate at 1. If I extend the cardinality variable continuously, the curve starts from zero at `P = 0`; for the sparse cases I care about, it grows past the `P = 1` gap, reaches a peak, and then falls. For dense features the peak can slide back toward `P = 1` or below, which is the same warning in another form: max separability is not something I can make monotone just by taking more locations. Let me sanity-check the peak depends on the rate: differentiate `φ`, the maximum of `|(1-α₂)^P - (1-α₁)^P|` sits where the two decaying exponentials are maximally spread, which for sparse features (small `α`) happens at large `P` — a rare feature needs a big pool before its max even reliably lights up — and for dense features at small `P`. So max-pool separability is good only in a *window* of cardinalities, and where that window sits depends on `α`.

Here's the thing that stops me. Compare the two regimes. Average pooling: separability monotone increasing in `P`, governed by the rate gap, best when features are dense and pools are big. Max pooling: separability non-monotone, peaking at an intermediate `P` that depends on the feature rate, and — crucially — in the sparse regime, especially when a feature is rare overall but much rarer in one class than the other (`α₂ ≪ α₁ ≪ 1`), the max-pool separation can *exceed* the average-pool one over a whole range of cardinalities. There the max, which keeps the rare strong response undiluted, beats the average, which divides that lone spike by `P` and buries it under the dead field. But in the dense regime the average wins, because it's the better mean estimator and the max has saturated to 1 for everybody.

Neither dominates. Which pooling rule separates the classes better is a function of the channel's activation rate and the effective pool size. And now I look at what I actually have to serve: `C` channels — 64 of them in ResNet, 512 in VGG, 1280 in MobileNet — across three datasets, at spatial sizes from `8×8` down to `1×1`. Different channels respond to different things at different rates; some encode a sparse, localized part (max's regime), some a diffuse texture spread across the map (average's regime). The pool size itself varies by an order of magnitude across my backbones. So the regime that decides max-versus-average is not fixed — it's all over the place, channel by channel, layer by layer, dataset by dataset. If I pick global average pooling I'm aligned with the dense, broadly-firing channels and wrong for the sparse, peaky ones. If I pick global max I'm aligned with the sparse peaks and throwing away the broad ones. Either single choice becomes the wrong statistic whenever the channel falls in the other regime. Wall.

The obvious escape is "just learn which one per channel." But look at the constraint: no labels, no data inside the layer, and I'd love zero parameters to avoid overfitting at exactly this bottleneck — that's the whole point of global pooling as a head, NIN got rid of the FC stack precisely because it overfit. I could in principle learn a per-channel selector, but that's the parametric road, and before I pay for parameters I want to ask whether I even need to *choose*.

Why am I choosing at all? The two summaries are not redundant. They are lossy in opposite directions. The average knows how broadly and consistently the channel fired — its prevalence — but divides away the peak. The max knows the peak intensity — the single strongest evidence — but is blind to everything else, it reads one location and discards `N-1`. A diffuse pattern at moderate strength everywhere and a one-pixel spike in a dead field can produce the *same* max; they produce very different averages. A faint signal spread over the whole map and a strong signal at one spot can produce the same average; they produce very different maxes. Each statistic resolves exactly the ambiguity the other is blind to. So a channel summary should keep a contribution from both instead of pretending one ambiguity is harmless.

I don't have to pick. I can keep both. The question becomes how to fold the two scalars back into one number per channel so the channel dimension stays `C` and the head interface is untouched. Concatenating them would give `2C` and break the fixed-`C` contract — and the backbones hand me a fixed channel count the head expects, so doubling is out. I want one number per channel that carries information from both. The cleanest object that does that and reduces exactly to each endpoint is a combination of the two,

  `f(x) = a · max(x) + (1 - a) · avg(x)`,   with `a ∈ [0, 1]`.

Let me check this is the right *kind* of combination before I fix `a`. Why linear, why a convex blend, and not, say, `max(avg, max)` or a product or the larger SNR of the two? A convex linear blend has the properties I want and the alternatives break. It's an honest interpolation of the two endpoints: `a = 0` is pure average, `a = 1` is pure max, and everything between is a controlled mixture along the average-to-max continuum. It keeps the output on the same units and the same scale as a single pooled value, so the downstream classifier, which was trained to expect activations of the usual magnitude, sees nothing strange. It's trivially differentiable, and — let me look — the gradient splits cleanly into the two branches, which I'll want for training. A product or a `max`-of-the-two would entangle the two statistics nonlinearly, lose the units, and lose the clean reduction to the endpoints. Linear it is.

Now `a`. The fully general thing is to learn `a` — one mixing proportion per layer, say, which is two extra scalars for a two-pool network, almost free. And I can see how I'd train it: with `f = a·max + (1-a)·avg`,

  `∂f/∂a = max(x) - avg(x)`,

so backprop can move `a` according to the loss and the gap between the max and average branches — the network discovers, per layer, how peaky-versus-diffuse its channels are. That's the rich version, and it's a genuinely good idea: one knob per layer, learned, no real overfitting risk.

But step back to my actual constraints. I want a parameter-free rule — nothing to learn at this layer, the strongest guard against overfitting the bottleneck — so in this variant I am not going to introduce an `a` that backprop can tune. If I fix `a` once and for all, what value do I set? I have just argued that neither endpoint dominates; the better one depends on a per-channel regime I cannot observe from inside this layer. I have no information that tells me to lean toward the peak or toward the prevalence for a given channel. The value that encodes "I don't know which regime this channel is in, and I refuse to commit to either" is the symmetric one, `a = 1/2`. It treats the two complementary summaries as equally trustworthy, and it is the unique choice that is invariant if I relabel which pooler I call "first." Any `a ≠ 1/2` is a bet that the channels lean one way, and I have no evidence for such a bet that holds across 64-, 512-, and 1280-channel backbones on three datasets. So `a = 1/2`:

  `f(x) = ½ · max(x) + ½ · avg(x) = (max(x) + avg(x)) / 2`.

The `/2` is not decoration. Without it, `max + avg` would sit outside the scale of either endpoint; with non-negative activations, `(max + avg)/2` stays between `avg` and `max`, so swapping the pooler in doesn't silently rescale the inputs to the classifier. And `(max + avg)/2` is exactly the `a = 1/2` convex combination, so it inherits everything I argued for the blend: reduces to neither extreme, carries both contributions, same units, differentiable.

Let me make sure the gradient does the sensible thing, because that's where I'll find out if this is actually using both branches during learning. Let `δ = ∂E/∂f` be the error arriving from the head. For one location `xᵢ` in a map of `N` locations,

  `∂f/∂xᵢ = ½ · ∂max/∂xᵢ + ½ · ∂avg/∂xᵢ = ½ · 1[xᵢ = max] + ½ · (1/N)`,

so

  `∂E/∂xᵢ = δ · ( ½ · 1[xᵢ = max] + ½/N )`.

Read that. Every location gets `δ·½/N` from the average branch — the field is credited uniformly, the way GAP credits it, so a broadly-firing channel still gets a learning signal spread over where it fired. And the single argmax location gets an *extra* `δ·½` from the max branch — the peak is credited on top, the way GMP credits it, so the strongest evidence is reinforced. Pure GAP would have sent `δ/N` everywhere and never singled out the peak; pure GMP would have sent `δ` to one location and left the rest of the map with no gradient at all. The blend does both: it learns from the prevalence and from the peak in the same step. That is exactly the complementary-supervision behavior the forward pass promised, now showing up in the backward pass for free. Good — both branches are live.

One more check on robustness, since I'm shipping this across backbones. Numerically there's nothing to floor: average and max of finite, ReLU-nonnegative activations are benign — no powers, no roots, no division by a possibly-tiny quantity (unlike a generalized-mean pooler with its `x^p` and `(·)^{1/p}` that needs a stability epsilon). So no `eps`, nothing to overflow. And the spatial-size robustness is handled by computing each pooled scalar with an adaptive reduction to output size 1: `adaptive_avg_pool2d(x, 1)` and `adaptive_max_pool2d(x, 1)` each turn any `[B, C, H, W]` — `8×8` or `1×1`, doesn't matter — into `[B, C, 1, 1]`, which I flatten to `[B, C]`. Channel dimension `C` untouched, any `H, W` accepted, zero parameters.

So the artifact writes itself, filling the empty `CustomPool` slot — compute the two complementary global summaries with the size-agnostic adaptive reductions, take their equal-weight blend so the output carries both at the usual scale, flatten to the channel vector:

```python
import torch.nn as nn
import torch.nn.functional as F


class CustomPool(nn.Module):
    """Equal-weight blend of global average and global max pooling.

    Average pooling reads the channel's prevalence (the broad/dense statistic);
    max pooling reads its peak (the sparse/localized statistic). Neither
    dominates across channels and datasets, so keep both at equal weight.
    """

    def __init__(self):
        super().__init__()
        # parameter-free: no mixing proportion to learn at this bottleneck

    def forward(self, x):                       # x: [B, C, H, W], activations >= 0
        avg = F.adaptive_avg_pool2d(x, 1)       # [B, C, 1, 1]  prevalence / mean rate
        mx = F.adaptive_max_pool2d(x, 1)        # [B, C, 1, 1]  peak / strongest evidence
        return ((avg + mx) / 2).view(x.size(0), -1)   # a=1/2 convex blend -> [B, C]
```

Tracing the chain back: I had to collapse each `H × W` feature map to one scalar, one fixed rule for backbones whose channels, pool sizes, and feature statistics all differ. Modeling separability as signal-to-noise showed average pooling estimates the activation rate and improves monotonically with pool size — best for dense, broadly-firing channels — while max pooling keeps the peak undiluted and wins in the sparse, localized regime, but its separability is non-monotone and saturates away for large pools. Neither dominates, and the deciding regime varies channel-by-channel and dataset-by-dataset in a way I cannot observe from inside the layer. The two summaries aren't redundant, they're lossy in opposite directions, so instead of choosing I keep both contributions and fold them into one number per channel with a convex linear blend `a·max + (1-a)·avg`, which interpolates the two endpoints, preserves the scale and the `C` interface, and is cleanly differentiable. With a hard preference for zero parameters at the bottleneck, the symmetric fixed value is `a = 1/2`, giving `(max + avg)/2` — the `/2` keeping the output at single-pooled scale — whose gradient credits both the whole field (`½/N` everywhere) and the peak (`½` at the argmax), so it learns from prevalence and intensity at once.
