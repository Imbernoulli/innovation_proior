Let me start from what actually goes wrong when I train this policy. I sample a batch of responses from `π_θ`, a reward source hands me one scalar per response — for math reasoning it's basically a correctness flag, 1.0 if the final answer is right and 0.0 if it isn't, maybe with a little format bonus — and that scalar gets parked at the last valid token and turned into a parameter update by a policy-gradient estimator. The estimator is the thing I actually care about, and it's Monte-Carlo: it averages `R · ∇log π` over the responses in the batch. So whatever I do, I'm averaging a product of a reward number and a log-prob gradient, over a finite, smallish set of samples. The reward numbers come in fixed; the question I get to decide is what numbers I feed the estimator. I only see the rewards and which tokens are valid, I have to hand back a same-shape tensor, and it's a pure data transform — no gradient flows through it. So the whole game is: take the batch of reward scalars and reshape them so the resulting update is better-behaved.

What does "better-behaved" mean concretely? Two things keep biting me. The first is variance. The per-sample term `R·∇log π` is noisy, and with only a handful of rollouts per prompt the average over the batch is a jumpy estimate of the true gradient — each step is partly chasing sampling noise. The second is scale and offset: the raw reward's magnitude and where it sits relative to zero feed straight into the size and the sign-pattern of the gradient, and the learning rate is otherwise just a fixed constant I picked, so the reward scale is silently acting as a second, drifting learning rate. Let me take these one at a time, because I suspect they want different fixes and I shouldn't conflate them.

Variance first. The estimator is the score-function form, `∇_θ E[R] = E[R · ∇_θ log π_θ(a)]`. It's unbiased, that's its whole appeal, but the variance is the well-known pain of the family and has been since the start. Where does the variance come from? Two factors in the product: the spread of `R` across sampled responses, and the spread of `∇log π`. I can't touch `∇log π` from inside a reward transform. But `R` I can. So is there anything I can do to the reward, sample by sample, that shrinks the spread of the term I'm averaging without changing what it estimates on average? If I changed the gradient's expectation I'd be cheating — I'd be optimizing the wrong thing. So I need a transform that's variance-reducing but expectation-preserving.

Here's the fact that makes this possible, and I want to actually verify it rather than wave at it, because everything downstream leans on it. Suppose I subtract some reference level `b` from the reward before forming the gradient, and suppose `b` does not depend on the sampled action. What happens to the expectation? `E[b · ∇log π] = b · E[∇log π] = b · ∑_a π(a) ∇log π(a)`. Now `π ∇log π = π · (∇π / π) = ∇π`, so that sum is `b · ∑_a ∇π(a) = b · ∇ ∑_a π(a) = b · ∇1 = 0`. The probabilities sum to one, the gradient of a constant is zero, the whole subtracted term vanishes in expectation. So `E[(R−b)∇log π] = E[R∇log π]` exactly — subtracting any action-independent reference leaves the gradient untouched. But the *variance* of the per-sample term `(R−b)∇log π` absolutely does depend on `b`. So I have a free knob: I can shift the reward by any constant I like, pay nothing in bias, and spend that freedom on shrinking variance. That's the lever.

So what `b` shrinks variance? Intuitively I want the term `(R−b)∇log π` to be small in spread, which means I want `R−b` to be small on average — I want `b` to sit in the middle of the rewards so that `R−b` is sometimes positive, sometimes negative, and small in magnitude, rather than `R` itself which (for my 0/1 reward) is a big lump of all-positive numbers. Let me make "shrink variance" precise and actually minimize over `b`. Treat `b` as a constant and look at the variance of the vector per-sample term; since subtracting an action-independent `b` doesn't change the mean, minimizing variance is minimizing the second moment `E[(R−b)^2 ||∇log π||²]`. Differentiate with respect to `b`: `d/db E[(R−b)^2 ||∇log π||²] = E[−2(R−b)||∇log π||²] = 0`, which gives `b* = E[R ||∇log π||²] / E[||∇log π||²]`. So the variance-minimizing reference is a squared-score-norm-weighted average of the reward. That's the optimal constant baseline, and it's a real, principled object — not just "subtract something reasonable."

But look at what `b*` needs: per-sample score-norm weights, inside a thing that's only supposed to see the rewards. I don't have the log-prob gradients in here, and even if I did, `||∇log π||²` is itself a high-variance quantity to estimate from a few samples, so I'd be building my variance-reduction reference out of a noisy estimate. That's self-defeating. Wall. Let me back off from the optimal weighted mean and ask what it degrades to if I drop the weights. If I pretend `||∇log π||²` is roughly constant across the samples, then `b*` collapses to `E[R ||∇log π||²]/E[||∇log π||²] ≈ E[R]` — the plain, unweighted mean of the reward. The exact no-bias identity applies when that mean is fixed with respect to the sampled action. If I estimate the mean from the same finite batch and include each sample in its own baseline, the centered Monte-Carlo estimator is shrunk by the familiar positive `(N−1)/N` factor before any additional random rescaling, not rotated into a different direction; a leave-one-out mean would remove that finite-sample shrinkage but costs more bookkeeping. So the cheapest sensible reference, the one that requires nothing but the rewards themselves and preserves the intended direction up to a batch-level scale, is the empirical mean of the rewards in the batch. That's the "subtract the mean" half, and now I know *why* the mean and not some other constant — it's the weight-free shadow of the optimal baseline.

There's a second, sharper reason to center that's specific to my reward, and once I see it I won't want to skip centering even if variance were already fine. My reward is bounded and one-signed — correctness is never negative. So every single sampled response carries a non-negative weight into `R·∇log π`. That means the gradient pushes *up* the log-probability of *every* response I sampled, the right ones and the wrong ones alike; they differ only in how hard they're pushed up. Picture a batch where some responses got the answer right (reward 1) and some got it wrong (reward 0): with raw rewards, the wrong ones get weight 0 — they're ignored, but never *suppressed*. The policy can never explicitly learn "don't do that"; it can only learn "do this other thing slightly more." Learning has to crawl entirely through differences in magnitude between all-positive pushes, which is slow and rattly, and it's worst exactly for sparse 0/1 outcome rewards like mine. Subtract the batch mean and the picture flips: a wrong response, reward 0, becomes `0 − mean < 0`, so it gets a *negative* weight and is actively pushed down; a right response, `1 − mean > 0`, is pushed up. Now the batch contains both reinforcement and suppression, anchored at the average response. Centering isn't just variance cosmetics — for a bounded reward it's what lets the policy push bad samples *down* at all.

Good, so centering is settled, and doubly motivated: it's the weight-free optimal baseline, and it cures the all-same-sign pathology. But centering does nothing about my second problem — scale. After subtracting the mean, the rewards are centered, but their *spread* is still whatever the reward source happened to produce. If today's batch has rewards spread over a range of 0.5 and tomorrow's over a range of 2, the centered signal is twice as big tomorrow, the gradient is twice as big, and since the learning rate is a fixed constant, I've effectively doubled my learning rate without touching it. That coupling is the well-documented thing where the magnitude of the signal behaves like an extra multiplier on the step size — feed a signal on scale `σ` and the effective learning rate scales with `σ`. And `σ` is *arbitrary*: it depends on whether a bonus is 0.1 or 1.0, on what fraction of the batch is correct (which sets the spread of a 0/1 reward — a batch that's half-right has the biggest spread, a near-all-right or near-all-wrong batch has tiny spread), on incidental choices in the reward source. I don't want my real learning rate to lurch around with the difficulty mix of each batch. So after centering, I want to also pin the scale.

The natural way to pin the scale of a centered quantity is to divide by its spread — its standard deviation. If I take the centered reward `R − mean` and divide by the batch standard deviation `std`, then the transformed reward has, by construction, mean ≈ 0 and spread ≈ 1, every batch, regardless of the reward source's units or the batch's difficulty mix. This is not trying to preserve the original gradient magnitude; it deliberately replaces a hand-tuned reward-scale multiplier with a positive batch-level normalization. Let me double check the scale-invariance claim explicitly, the way I'd check Adam's `m̂/√v̂`: if I multiplied every reward by a positive constant `c` — say the reward source changed units — then the mean scales by `c`, the numerator `R − mean` scales by `c`, and the std scales by `c`, so `(R − mean)/std` is unchanged. If `c` were negative, the sign would flip, which is correct because the preference ordering has been reversed. Away from the tiny epsilon floor, the transformed signal doesn't care about the absolute positive scale of the reward at all, only its relative structure. That's precisely the property I want: a learning rate decoupled from the reward scale.

So the transform is taking shape as: standardize the batch of reward scalars — subtract the batch mean, divide by the batch standard deviation. Center for the baseline / variance / sign reasons, rescale for the learning-rate-decoupling reason. This is whitening of the reward signal. Before I commit, let me sanity-check the degenerate cases, because a divide-by-std is begging to blow up. What if the std is zero? It is zero exactly when all the reward scalars in the batch are equal — and for a 0/1 reward that happens whenever the batch is *all* correct or *all* wrong. Then `R − mean` is identically zero, so the numerator is zero too, and I'm at `0/0`. I need to floor the denominator so it never detonates: divide by `std + ε` with a small `ε`, so an all-equal multi-sample batch gives `0/(0+ε) = 0` — no relative signal, which is the honest answer (a batch with no spread carries no relative information to learn from). And the truly degenerate case of a single sample, batch size one: there's no spread to speak of and no baseline to subtract a sample from meaningfully, so I return the original masked reward tensor unchanged rather than secretly scaling it by `1/(1+ε)`. `ε` should be small — small enough that in any normal batch, where `std` is a real fraction, it's negligible and the positive-scale invariance holds; just nonzero so zero-spread batches are tamed. Something like `1e-6` sits well below any healthy reward spread.

Now a subtlety on the standard deviation itself: do I divide variance by `N` or by `N−1`? The maximum-likelihood variance from `N` samples divides by `N` and is biased low; Bessel's correction multiplies by `N/(N−1)` to make it unbiased. The canonical masked whitening utility defaults to the Bessel-corrected variance, because it is a general masked statistic. Here I am not estimating a population variance for inference; I am rescaling the reward numbers in this batch, and the edit I want is the population std, `std(unbiased=False)`, divide by `N`. That gives the empirical spread of the numbers in front of me and leaves the single-sample case to the explicit early return.

Let me also be honest about *which* statistics I'm computing the mean and std over, because there's a choice I've been gliding past. I could compute the mean and std per *prompt* — over just the responses that share a prompt — or over the *whole batch*. Per-prompt is tempting because prompts differ wildly in difficulty, so the "right" reference for a response is arguably the typical reward of other responses to the *same* prompt. But two things push me to the whole-batch statistic here. First, a per-prompt mean and std are computed from only the handful of rollouts I have per prompt, so they're themselves very noisy estimates — I'd be referencing each response to a baseline estimated from maybe sixteen points, and the std especially is jumpy at that sample size. The batch-spanning statistic is estimated from far more samples and is correspondingly stable. Second — and this is the decisive one — the downstream advantage estimator I'm feeding is GRPO-style, and *it already* subtracts a per-prompt group mean and divides by a per-prompt group std. If I did the per-prompt normalization here too, I'd be applying essentially the same per-group statistic twice, which collapses the same information twice and adds nothing. Whereas a single batch-spanning whitening is a *different*, complementary transform: a global affine rescale of the whole batch's reward signal, sitting upstream of the per-group normalizer. So I'll deliberately ignore the grouping and normalize against one batch-wide `(mean, std)` pair — the simplest possible global baseline, and the one that doesn't fight the downstream per-group step. I should hold the interaction in mind: my global whitening shrinks the overall spread before GRPO recomputes per-prompt contrasts, but since GRPO re-centers and re-scales within each group anyway, a global affine transform upstream mostly sets the overall signal scale and leaves the per-prompt contrasts intact for GRPO to extract.

One more implementation point about preserving the convention. The reward arrived as a per-token tensor with the scalar sitting at the last valid token and zeros elsewhere; the downstream machinery expects that "outcome reward at the last valid token" layout. So I can't just hand back a flat per-response vector — I recover the scalar per response by summing over tokens (the sum picks out that single last-token value), do my whitening on those scalars, then scatter each normalized scalar back to its response's last valid token and zero everywhere else, and multiply by the response mask to be safe. And the whole thing runs under no-grad: it's a transform on the reward data, not a differentiable part of the model, so nothing here should be on the autograd graph.

Let me assemble it. Recover scalars, return unchanged for the degenerate single-sample case, compute the batch mean and population std, whiten with the `+ε` floor, scatter back to the last valid token:

```python
import torch
import numpy as np
from typing import Optional


@torch.no_grad()                                    # pure data transform on the reward tensor
def normalize_rewards(
    token_level_scores: torch.Tensor,              # (bs, response_length); scalar at last valid token
    response_mask: torch.Tensor,                   # (bs, response_length); 1 on valid response tokens
    index: np.ndarray = None,                      # (bs,) prompt id — deliberately unused (global, not per-prompt)
    epsilon: float = 1e-6,
    config: Optional[object] = None,
    **kwargs,
) -> torch.Tensor:
    bsz, seq_len = token_level_scores.shape
    scores = token_level_scores.sum(dim=-1)        # (bs,) one outcome scalar per response

    if bsz <= 1:
        # one sample: no spread, no baseline
        return token_level_scores * response_mask

    mean = scores.mean()                           # weight-free plug-in baseline (subtract to center)
    std = scores.std(unbiased=False)               # population spread (divide to pin the scale)

    # whiten: zero-mean, ~unit-spread; +epsilon floors the all-equal-batch / zero-std case
    scores = (scores - mean) / (std + epsilon)

    # re-place each normalized scalar at its response's last valid token (preserve the convention)
    out = torch.zeros_like(token_level_scores)
    last_idx = (response_mask.long().sum(dim=-1) - 1).clamp(min=0)   # (bs,)
    out[torch.arange(bsz, device=out.device), last_idx] = scores
    return out * response_mask
```

Let me trace the causal chain back. I started from a Monte-Carlo policy-gradient estimator that averages `R·∇log π` over a small batch, with two diseases: high variance, and an effective learning rate that drifts with the reward's arbitrary scale. The variance disease has a classical lever — subtracting any action-independent reference leaves the gradient's expectation untouched (because `E[∇log π] = ∑∇π = ∇1 = 0`) while changing its variance; the variance-minimizing reference is a `||∇log π||²`-weighted mean of the reward, which I can't compute in here, so its weight-free shadow, the plain batch mean, is the cheap plug-in reference — and centering on the mean also cures the all-same-sign pathology of my bounded 0/1 reward, letting bad samples be pushed down instead of merely not-up. The scale disease wants the centered signal's spread pinned, so I divide by the batch standard deviation, which makes the transformed reward zero-mean unit-spread and invariant to positive reward-unit rescalings away from the epsilon floor — moving per-batch scale tuning out of the hand-set learning rate and making it automatic. A small `ε` floor on the denominator handles the all-equal / zero-spread batch, the single-sample case returns the masked input unchanged, and I normalize against one batch-wide statistic rather than per-prompt — both because the per-prompt statistic is noisier from few rollouts and because the downstream GRPO estimator already does the per-prompt normalization, so a global whitening is the complementary, non-redundant move. The result is batch-level reward whitening: standardize the batch of per-response scalars, then scatter them back to the last valid token under no-grad.
