OK, let me sit with the thing that doesn't add up. Pruning works: I can train a dense network, delete more than 90% of its weights by magnitude, fine-tune the survivors, and the resulting tiny network keeps essentially the full accuracy. So a network with a tenth of the parameters can *represent* this function perfectly well. Then the natural question — the one that would save me an enormous amount of compute — is: if the small network suffices, why not just train *it* from the start? Skip the big network entirely, train the sparse one, save both inference cost and training cost.

And the answer, every time I or anyone else tries it, is that it doesn't work. Take the sparse architecture that pruning left behind, re-initialize it randomly, train it from scratch — and it learns slower and tops out at lower accuracy than the dense network did. Let me make that precise as a measurement, because the shape of the curve is the clue. Randomly sample sparse subnetworks at a range of sparsity levels (random sampling is a fair stand-in for the connectivity unstructured pruning leaves), train each from a fresh random init, and record two things: the iteration where validation loss bottoms out (how *fast* it learns) and the test accuracy there. The trend is monotone and unforgiving — the sparser the network, the slower it learns and the lower its final accuracy. Sparse-from-scratch is just bad, and it gets worse the sparser you go.

So here's the precise tension. I have a proof-by-construction that a small trainable network exists — I literally produced one by pruning. Yet SGD, starting from a fresh random initialization of that very same sparse architecture, cannot find a solution of comparable quality. It's not that no good sparse solution exists; one provably does. It's that the optimizer can't *get there* from a random start. That gap — solution exists, optimizer can't reach it from scratch — is the whole mystery, and the standard reading of it is "small-capacity networks are inherently hard to train." But that reading is suspiciously incurious. Let me not accept it yet and instead ask what's actually *different* between the sparse network that trains fine (the pruned-and-fine-tuned one) and the sparse network that doesn't (the same architecture, freshly re-initialized).

Two things differ, and I've been sloppily lumping them together. One is the *architecture* — which connections survive, the sparse wiring pattern. The other is the *values* the surviving weights hold at the moment training resumes. In the pruned-and-fine-tuned case, the survivors don't start from a fresh random draw; they start from the values the original dense training gave them. In the train-from-scratch case, the survivors start from a brand-new random init. I've been blaming the architecture. But the architecture is identical in both cases. The only thing that changed is the *starting values* of the surviving weights. So maybe the architecture was never the problem. Maybe the missing ingredient is the *initialization* — that these particular surviving connections are trainable only when they start from particular initial values, and a fresh random draw throws those values away.

And there's a fact from the pruning literature that suddenly looks like evidence rather than folklore: practitioners report that when you prune, it's better to *keep* the surviving connections' weights from the initial training phase than to re-initialize them — that gradient descent finds a good solution when the network is first trained but not after re-initializing the pruned layers. People said this and moved on. But it's pointing right at initialization being the active ingredient.

Now, "keep the weights from initial training" in the fine-tuning recipe means keeping the *post-training* values — the weights as they were at the end of training the dense net, θ_j. That's the obvious thing, and it works for fine-tuning. But it conflates two hypotheses again. If keeping θ_j works, is it because those values are *near the final answer already* (so we're basically starting trained), or because the surviving connections + their *original random* init θ₀ form a special trainable combination that the dense training merely *revealed*? Those are very different claims. The first is boring — "fine-tuning works because you're almost done." The second is the interesting one: that buried inside the random initialization of the dense network is a sparse subnetwork whose *initial* weights are already a winning configuration, and dense training is just a search procedure that surfaces *which* connections those are.

Let me design the experiment that separates them. I'll go back not to the trained values θ_j, but all the way to the *original* initialization θ₀ — the random draw, before any training. The procedure:

1. Randomly initialize a network f(x; θ₀), θ₀ ~ D_θ.
2. Train it for j iterations, arriving at θ_j.
3. Prune p% of θ_j by magnitude — delete the smallest-magnitude weights, producing a binary mask m ∈ {0,1}^{|θ|}. The surviving connections are the architecture.
4. *Reset* each surviving weight to its value in θ₀ — its original random initialization, from *before* training — which is where this departs from all prior pruning. This gives f(x; m ⊙ θ₀).

Then train f(x; m ⊙ θ₀) in isolation, with the mask m held fixed (pruned weights stay zero, no gradient flows to them), and see whether it matches the dense network.

Notice exactly what this isolates. The architecture m came from training (I needed the dense training to discover *which* connections matter — magnitude after training tells me that). But the *values* I put back are the pre-training random ones, θ₀. So if this trains well, it can't be because "the weights are already trained" — they're at their random starting point. It can only be because the *combination* of this sparse architecture with these specific initial values is trainable. I'd call such a subnetwork a winning ticket: it won the initialization lottery — it drew, by chance, a set of connections whose initial weights are jointly capable of learning.

This suggests a hypothesis I can state sharply. A randomly-initialized dense network contains a subnetwork — picked out by a mask m — that, *initialized at its original values m ⊙ θ₀ and trained in isolation*, matches the full network's test accuracy in at most the same number of iterations. Concretely: the dense net reaches minimum validation loss at iteration j with test accuracy a; the masked net f(x; m ⊙ θ₀) reaches minimum validation loss at iteration j' with accuracy a'; the claim is that there exists m with j' ≤ j (it learns at least as fast — commensurate training time), a' ≥ a (it's at least as accurate — commensurate accuracy), and ‖m‖₀ ≪ |θ| (it's much smaller — fewer parameters). The dense network isn't a monolith I have to train as one piece; it's a bag of overlapping subnetworks, and at least one of them already holds a winning hand at initialization.

I need to be careful that I don't fool myself, so the control is essential and it's the thing that turns "I found a small trainable net" into "the initialization is the cause." Keep the *structure* — the very same mask m — but draw a *fresh* random initialization θ'₀ ~ D_θ for the survivors, and train f(x; m ⊙ θ'₀). Same architecture, different starting values. If this trains just as well, then structure was all that mattered and my "winning ticket" story is wrong. If it trains *worse* — slower, lower accuracy — then the specific init θ₀ is the active ingredient, exactly as the hypothesis says. (My expectation, given the whole motivation, is that random reinitialization breaks it: the ticket should learn progressively slower and lose accuracy as it's pruned, while the original-init ticket holds up. That's what would distinguish "won the lottery" from "any subnetwork of this shape works.")

Now the mechanics of finding the ticket. How do I prune? I'll reach for the simplest proven thing — magnitude pruning, à la Han et al.: after training, remove the weights with the smallest absolute value, on the reasoning that small-magnitude weights contribute least to the function. I'll do it *layer-wise* — remove a fixed fraction of the lowest-magnitude weights *within each layer* — rather than globally, because a single global threshold could wipe out an entire layer whose weights happen to be small in scale, severing the network; pruning per layer guarantees every layer keeps a proportional share. One refinement: the connections to the *output* layer. The output layer is comparatively tiny and each of its weights is doing disproportionate work mapping features to class logits; pruning it as hard as the rest would gut the classifier. So prune output connections at *half* the rate of the rest of the network. And I'll keep it unstructured — prune individual weights, not whole units or filters — because per-weight pruning can reach far higher sparsity (the survivors can be scattered arbitrarily), and right now I care about finding the smallest possible winning ticket, not about whether the result runs fast on current hardware.

There's a choice in *how much* to prune at once that turns out to matter. The simplest version is one-shot: train once, prune p% in a single cut, reset to θ₀, done. That does find winning tickets. But think about what a single large cut does — it ranks weights by magnitude *once*, after one training run, and removes a big chunk in one go. Some weights that look small after that single run might have become important had the network kept training in a slightly different sparse regime; one big cut commits to a ranking that was never re-examined. So instead, prune *iteratively*. Over n rounds, each round: train the (currently masked) network to completion, prune a small fraction of the weights that *survived* the previous round (say 20% of survivors), reset the remaining survivors back to their θ₀ values, and repeat. Each round the surviving subnetwork is re-trained and re-evaluated before the next small cut, so the magnitude ranking is refreshed at every sparsity level instead of trusted once. The per-round fraction compounds geometrically — keep 80% each round and after k rounds the fraction remaining is 0.8^k — so I can march down to extreme sparsity in controlled steps. I expect iterative pruning to find winning tickets at *smaller* sizes than one-shot, precisely because it never commits to a stale ranking; the cost is that it trains the network many times over, which is the practical price of the search.

One subtlety I have to respect in the reset: at every round I reset survivors to θ₀ — the *same* original initialization, every time — not to the values from the most recent training run. The whole hypothesis is about θ₀ being special, so each round's ticket must be re-anchored to those original values; if I reset to the last round's trained weights I'd be doing iterative fine-tuning, a different (and uninteresting, for this question) thing. So: the mask accumulates across rounds (connections only ever get removed), but the surviving weights are always rewound to θ₀.

Let me think about whether this can possibly scale up, because I have a worry about deeper, larger networks. On a small fully-connected net or a shallow conv net, I expect the reset-to-θ₀ ticket to train cleanly. But deeper networks (think Resnet-18, VGG-19) are trained with larger learning rates and step schedules. A large learning rate early in training takes big, aggressive steps — and a sparse subnetwork rewound to its raw initialization is fragile early on; those big early steps can knock it out of the favorable region before it settles, so the reset-to-θ₀ ticket fails to materialize at the higher learning rates these nets need. The fix that suggests itself is learning-rate *warmup*: start the learning rate near zero and ramp it up over the first stretch of training, so the ticket isn't blasted by full-magnitude steps from iteration zero and gets a chance to find its footing. I'd predict that with warmup the iterative procedure recovers winning tickets in the deep nets where, without it, it comes up empty. (Why warmup is *necessary* — what exactly the big early steps destroy — I can't fully explain yet; I'm noting it as the patch that makes the deep case work.)

Step back and ask *why* a winning ticket's initialization should matter at all, because the answer shapes how I interpret everything. The lazy explanation is that the winning-ticket weights are already close to their trained values — basically pre-trained — so of course they train well. I can test that directly: track how far each weight moves during training. If the lazy story were right, winning-ticket weights would barely move. The opposite is what I'd expect to find — the winning-ticket weights move *farther* than other weights, not less. So it's not that they're already trained. The benefit of the initialization must instead be about *where in the loss landscape* m ⊙ θ₀ sits — a starting point and a sparse connectivity that together land in a region the optimizer can descend effectively, given this dataset and this model. The structure is carrying an inductive bias too: I found these connections by heavy use of the training data, so the surviving wiring encodes something task-specific about which computations matter. Initialization and structure are jointly the ticket; neither alone is enough, which is exactly why the random-reinit control (structure without the init) breaks.

There's a generalization angle worth holding onto. As I prune, I'd expect test accuracy to first *rise* above the dense network's and then eventually *fall* — an Occam's-hill shape: the original overparameterized model has more capacity than it needs (room to overfit), moderate pruning trims that excess toward a simpler hypothesis that generalizes better, and extreme pruning finally removes capacity the task genuinely needs. That fits the old intuition that compact hypotheses generalize better (minimum description length), but with a twist specific to this picture: the large network isn't just compressible after the fact — it *explicitly contains* the simpler, better-generalizing representation as a subnetwork from the very beginning.

And that closes back to the original puzzle with a conjecture I can't prove but that the whole construction points at. Why are overparameterized networks easier to train than sparse ones? Because a dense network is a superposition of enormously many candidate subnetworks, so the chance that *at least one* of them is a winning ticket — drew a trainable init — is high; SGD, in effect, seeks out and trains such a well-initialized subnetwork. Shrink the network and you shrink the number of candidate tickets, so the odds that any of them won the lottery collapse — which is exactly why a sparse-from-scratch net, with far fewer subnetworks to draw from, usually loses. Overparameterization buys lottery tickets.

Let me write the procedure as real code. The heart is three pieces: the magnitude-prune step (grounded in the standard implementation — sort the surviving weights by absolute value, find the cutoff at the requested percentile, zero everything below it), the reset-to-θ₀ step, and the iterative loop that composes train → prune → reset.

```python
import numpy as np
import copy

# --- magnitude pruning: zero out the smallest-magnitude SURVIVING weights ---
def prune_by_percent_once(percent, mask, final_weight):
    # rank only the weights that are still alive (mask == 1) by |value|
    sorted_weights = np.sort(np.abs(final_weight[mask == 1]))
    cutoff_index = int(np.round(percent * sorted_weights.size))   # the percent-th smallest survivor
    cutoff = sorted_weights[cutoff_index]
    # prune every weight at or below that magnitude; survivors keep mask 1
    return np.where(np.abs(final_weight) <= cutoff, np.zeros(mask.shape), mask)

def prune(percents, masks, final_weights):
    # output layer is pruned at HALF the rate of the rest (set in `percents`)
    return {k: prune_by_percent_once(percents[k], masks[k], final_weights[k]) for k in masks}

# --- the iterative lottery-ticket procedure ---
def find_winning_ticket(model_init, train_fn, percents, num_rounds):
    theta_0 = model_init()                                        # random init, drawn once
    masks   = {k: np.ones_like(v) for k, v in theta_0.items()}    # start fully dense
    weights = copy.deepcopy(theta_0)

    for _ in range(num_rounds):
        # train the current masked subnetwork to completion (mask fixed: pruned weights stay 0)
        trained, metrics = train_fn(weights, masks)
        # prune a fraction of the survivors by magnitude -> accumulate into the mask
        masks = prune(percents, masks, trained)
        # RESET every surviving weight to its ORIGINAL init theta_0 (not the trained value)
        weights = {k: masks[k] * theta_0[k] for k in masks}       # m ⊙ θ₀  = the winning ticket

    return masks, weights                                         # f(x; m ⊙ θ₀)

# --- control: keep the structure, throw away the special init ---
def random_reinit_control(model_init, masks):
    theta_prime = model_init()                                    # fresh random draw θ'₀
    return {k: masks[k] * theta_prime[k] for k in masks}          # m ⊙ θ'₀  — should train worse
```

And the training step applies the mask on every forward pass so the pruned weights are held at zero and never receive gradient:

```python
def masked_forward(x, weights, masks):
    # each layer uses (mask * weight); zeros stay zero, gradients flow only to survivors
    h = x
    for k in layer_order:
        h = layer_op(h, masks[k] * weights[k])
    return h
# trained with Adam (e.g. Lenet-300-100 on MNIST) or SGD+momentum with a stepped LR
# schedule and learning-rate warmup for deeper nets (Resnet-18 / VGG-19).
```

The causal chain, start to finish: pruning proves a tiny network can represent the function, but training that sparse architecture from a fresh random init learns slower and worse the sparser it gets — a good solution provably exists yet SGD can't reach it from scratch. The only thing that differs between the sparse net that trains (pruned-and-kept) and the one that doesn't (re-initialized) is the surviving weights' *starting values*, so the missing ingredient is the initialization, not the architecture. Rewinding the survivors all the way to their *original* random values θ₀ — not the trained ones — isolates that: if f(x; m ⊙ θ₀) trains well it's because this sparse architecture plus these specific initial weights is a winning ticket the dense init happened to contain, and dense training merely revealed which connections it occupies. Magnitude pruning identifies the survivors; doing it iteratively, always rewinding to θ₀, finds smaller tickets than one cut; layer-wise pruning with output weights at half rate keeps the structure intact; warmup rescues the procedure on deep nets; and the random-reinitialization control (same mask, fresh init) is what confirms the init, not the structure, is doing the work — which in turn suggests why dense nets are easier to train at all: they contain vastly more candidate tickets, so one of them is far more likely to have won the lottery.
