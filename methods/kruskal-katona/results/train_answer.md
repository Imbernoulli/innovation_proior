Fix a uniformity $k$ and a count $m$, take any family $\mathcal{F}$ of $m$ distinct $k$-element sets, and form its lower shadow $\partial\mathcal{F} = \{F\setminus\{x\} : F\in\mathcal{F},\ x\in F\}$, the $(k-1)$-uniform family of all $(k-1)$-subsets sitting inside some member of $\mathcal{F}$. The question I want answered exactly — not up to order of magnitude — is how small $|\partial\mathcal{F}|$ can be once $|\mathcal{F}|=m$ is pinned, and which families attain that minimum. The maximisation direction is trivial: each set throws $k$ subsets into the shadow, so $|\partial\mathcal{F}|\le km$, met by pairwise-disjoint sets. Minimisation is the one with teeth, because making the shadow small means forcing the sets to *share* their subsets, and how much sharing is achievable is not obvious. The tools on the table fall short. Double counting gives only $|\partial\mathcal{F}|\ge\frac{k}{n-k+1}|\mathcal{F}|$ — linear, dependent on the ambient $n$, loose by a wide margin, and blind to the extremal configuration; the true answer is sublinear, $n$-free, and binomial. The natural guess that the minimiser is a clique $[a]^{(k)}$ (maximal subset-sharing, giving $\binom{a}{k-1}$) only speaks to the round counts $m=\binom{a}{k}$, says nothing for an $m$ in between, and "it looks optimal" is no proof in a subject littered with plausible false conjectures. Sperner and Erdős–Ko–Rado are landmarks of the same arena but each is a single statement that a sharp shadow inequality ought to subsume rather than rely on. What is needed is an exact bound valid for *every* $(m,k)$, an explicit extremal family for each, and a proof that no cleverer configuration beats it.

I propose the Kruskal–Katona theorem. Its first ingredient is the right bookkeeping for an arbitrary $m$. Greedily stacking cliques — fill the largest $[a_k]^{(k)}$ with $\binom{a_k}{k}\le m$, then place the remaining sets through a fresh element $a_k+1$ as a $(k-1)$-clique on the largest sub-ground-set that fits, and recurse — forces $m$ into a unique base-binomial expansion, the **$k$-cascade representation**, $$m=\binom{a_k}{k}+\binom{a_{k-1}}{k-1}+\cdots+\binom{a_s}{s},\qquad a_k>a_{k-1}>\cdots>a_s\ge s\ge1,$$ where the strict decrease of the top arguments is exactly the statement that no larger clique fit at the previous stage. Telescoping the shadow of this greedy stack — the big clique contributes all of $[a_k]^{(k-1)}$, each later block's removals either land back in an already-counted lower layer or produce a genuinely new appended set — collapses to the same cascade shape with every index pushed down by one, which I name $$\partial^{(k)}(m):=\binom{a_k}{k-1}+\binom{a_{k-1}}{k-2}+\cdots+\binom{a_s}{s-1}.$$ The claim is then $|\partial\mathcal{F}|\ge\partial^{(k)}(|\mathcal{F}|)$ for every family, with equality at the first $m$ sets in **colexicographic order**, $A<B\iff\max(A\triangle B)\in B$, the order that avoids large elements as long as possible. Colex is the privileged order, not lex, because of a self-similarity I can make exact: the colex initial segment of length $m$ with cascade $(a_k,\dots,a_s)$ has, as its shadow, the colex initial segment one layer down with the *same* parameters, so the shadow of an initial segment never leaks out into later sets and equality is visible by construction. Colex initial segments are also intrinsic — independent of the ambient $n$ — mirroring the fact that the shadow itself is intrinsic, whereas lex initial segments and their shadows depend on $n$ and are not themselves initial segments.

What makes the lower bound true, rather than merely guessed, is a two-stage argument: a shift that reduces an arbitrary family to a structured one without hurting the shadow, then a double induction on the structured family. The **shift** $S_i$ ($i\ge2$) swaps a large element for $1$: for each $F$, if $i\in F$, $1\notin F$, and $F\setminus\{i\}\cup\{1\}\notin\mathcal{F}$, replace $F$ by $F\setminus\{i\}\cup\{1\}$; otherwise leave $F$ (call it *blocked*). The "not already in" clause is forced — a moved set cannot land on an occupied slot without losing a member — and it makes $S_i$ injective, so $|S_i(\mathcal{F})|=|\mathcal{F}|$. The load-bearing fact is the subcommutativity $\partial S_i(\mathcal{F})\subseteq S_i(\partial\mathcal{F})$, proved by casing on how $\{1,i\}$ meets $S_i(F)$; since $S_i$ also preserves size on the $(k-1)$-layer, the inclusion yields $|\partial S_i(\mathcal{F})|\le|\partial\mathcal{F}|$. Shifting therefore never raises the shadow, and because every successful shift strictly increases the count of sets containing $1$, iterating terminates at a **stable** family $\mathcal{G}$ (fixed by every $S_i$) of the same size and no larger shadow. Stability is weaker than being a colex initial segment — $\{1,3\},\{2,3\}$ is stable but not colex — so I do not need stable to *equal* colex; I only need stable families to obey the cascade bound, and stability supplies the leverage. Split $\mathcal{F}=\mathcal{F}_0\sqcup\mathcal{F}_1$ by membership of $1$ and strip the $1$ from $\mathcal{F}_1$ to get a $(k-1)$-family $\mathcal{F}_1'$ with $|\mathcal{F}_1'|=|\mathcal{F}_1|$. Any $E=F\setminus\{x\}\in\partial\mathcal{F}_0$ has $x\ge2$, and stability means $F$ was blocked, so $F\setminus\{x\}\cup\{1\}\in\mathcal{F}_1$ and hence $E\in\mathcal{F}_1'$: the boundary of the $1$-free part is *paid for* inside the stripped $1$-containing part, giving $|\mathcal{F}_1'|\ge|\partial\mathcal{F}_0|$. Meanwhile $\partial\mathcal{F}_1=\mathcal{F}_1'\sqcup\{1\}\!\cdot\!\partial\mathcal{F}_1'$ (disjoint, by presence of $1$), and since $\partial\mathcal{F}_0$ is already swallowed by $\mathcal{F}_1'$, the whole shadow collapses to the engine $$|\partial\mathcal{F}|=|\mathcal{F}_1'|+|\partial\mathcal{F}_1'|.$$ Now run a double induction — outer on $k$, inner on $m$ — with $m$ of cascade $(a_k,\dots,a_s)$. If $|\mathcal{F}_1'|<\sum_j\binom{a_j-1}{j-1}$, then because $|\mathcal{F}_0|=m-|\mathcal{F}_1'|$ and Pascal's rule $\binom{a}{i}-\binom{a-1}{i-1}=\binom{a-1}{i}$ shaves one off each top argument, $|\mathcal{F}_0|>\sum_j\binom{a_j-1}{j}$; dropping any zero term $\binom{s-1}{s}$, monotonicity of $\partial^{(k)}$ and the inner induction on $\mathcal{F}_0$ (which has size $<m$, and is nonempty-complemented because a nonempty stable family must contain a set through $1$) give $|\partial\mathcal{F}_0|\ge\sum_j\binom{a_j-1}{j-1}$, contradicting the supposed failure via $|\mathcal{F}_1'|\ge|\partial\mathcal{F}_0|$. So the lower bound on $|\mathcal{F}_1'|$ holds. The outer induction on $k$ bounds its shadow, $|\partial\mathcal{F}_1'|\ge\sum_j\binom{a_j-1}{j-2}$, and pairing terms with Pascal once more, $\binom{a_j-1}{j-1}+\binom{a_j-1}{j-2}=\binom{a_j}{j-1}$, plugs into the engine to yield $|\partial\mathcal{F}|\ge\sum_j\binom{a_j}{j-1}=\partial^{(k)}(m)$. The base cases ground both inductions: $k=1$ gives $|\partial\mathcal{F}|=|\{\emptyset\}|=1=\binom{m}{0}$, and $m=1$ gives $|\partial\mathcal{F}|=k=\binom{k}{k-1}$. At a round count $m=\binom{a}{k}$ the equality version leaves no slack, so the only minimisers are cliques $[a]^{(k)}$ after relabelling. An ordered **compression** $C_{ij}$ (replace $j$ by $i$ where possible) gives a parallel geometric route all the way to the colex segment, but only when the compressions are ordered to keep the shadow from rising — the blanket $\partial C_{ij}(\mathcal{F})\subseteq C_{ij}(\partial\mathcal{F})$ fails for arbitrary steps, and fully compressed families of a fixed size are not uniquely colex, so compression is no shortcut to "every compressed family is the colex segment."

For downstream use the cascade form is exact but awkward inside other proofs, so I extend $\binom{x}{k}=x(x-1)\cdots(x-k+1)/k!$ to real $x\ge k$, where it is strictly increasing from $1$ to $\infty$, and carry the bracketed real-$x$ calculation through the same induction using the polynomial Pascal identity $\binom{x}{k}=\binom{x-1}{k}+\binom{x-1}{k-1}$. This gives the **Lovász form**: if $|\mathcal{F}|=\binom{x}{k}$ then $|\partial\mathcal{F}|\ge\binom{x}{k-1}$ — weaker than the cascade bound strictly between integer points, exact at $m=\binom{n}{k}$, and the version I actually plug into arguments. (The one careful spot is $x\in[k,k+1)$, where $x-1<k$ blocks the outer induction; but then $|\mathcal{F}_0|>\binom{x-1}{k}\ge0$ makes $\mathcal{F}_0$ nonempty and a single $k$-set already contributes $k=\binom{k}{k-1}\ge\binom{x-1}{k-1}$.) These corollaries are why the bound matters: passing to complements and iterating the shadow $n-2r$ times keeps an intersecting $r$-family disjoint from itself, forcing $|\mathcal{A}|\le\binom{n-1}{r-1}$ with the star as the equality case — exactly Erdős–Ko–Rado; and downward closure makes $(f_0,\dots,f_{d-1})$ a realisable face-count vector of a simplicial complex iff $f_{i-1}\ge\partial^{(i+1)}(f_i)$ for every $i\ge1$, the shadow bound being the whole content of the characterisation.

```python
from math import comb
from itertools import combinations

def k_cascade(m, k):
    """Unique greedy k-cascade: m = sum_i C(a_i, i), a_k > a_{k-1} > ... ."""
    rep, i = [], k
    while i >= 1 and m > 0:
        a = i
        while comb(a + 1, i) <= m:        # largest a with C(a, i) <= m
            a += 1
        rep.append((a, i))
        m -= comb(a, i)
        i -= 1
    return rep

def min_shadow_size(m, k):
    """Kruskal-Katona minimum: push every cascade index down by one."""
    return sum(comb(a, i - 1) for (a, i) in k_cascade(m, k))

def colex_key(S):
    """Colex order: A < B iff max(A symdiff B) in B; equiv. sum of 2^element."""
    return sum(1 << x for x in S)

def extremal_family(m, k, ground=None):
    """First m k-sets in colex order — the minimiser of the shadow."""
    if ground is None:
        ground = m + k
    sets = sorted((frozenset(c) for c in combinations(range(1, ground + 1), k)),
                  key=colex_key)
    return sets[:m]

def shadow(family):
    out = set()
    for S in family:
        for x in S:
            out.add(frozenset(S - {x}))
    return out
```
