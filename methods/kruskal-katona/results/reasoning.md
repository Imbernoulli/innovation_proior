Let me get the question stated cleanly before I reach for anything. I have a uniformity $k$ and a number $m$. I take some family $\mathcal{F}$ of $m$ distinct $k$-element sets, and I look at its shadow: every $k$-set $F$ has $k$ subsets of size $k-1$, and the shadow $\partial\mathcal{F}$ is the collection of all of those, over the whole family. So $\partial\mathcal{F} = \{E : E = F\setminus\{x\},\ F\in\mathcal{F},\ x\in F\}$, a $(k-1)$-uniform family. And the thing I actually care about is: with $|\mathcal{F}|$ pinned at $m$, how small can $|\partial\mathcal{F}|$ be?

The upper question dies immediately. Each of the $m$ sets throws $k$ subsets into the shadow, so $|\partial\mathcal{F}| \le km$, and if I spread the sets out so they share nothing — pairwise disjoint $k$-sets — every one of those $km$ subsets is distinct and the bound is met. Boring. The minimisation is the one with teeth, because to make the shadow small I want the sets to *share* their subsets as much as possible, and "how much sharing is possible" is not obvious at all.

Let me feel out the extremal shape with my hands. Suppose I want a $(k-1)$-set $E$ to be reused a lot. $E$ is the shadow of $F$ whenever $F = E\cup\{x\}$ for some $x\notin E$. So the more of those extensions $E\cup\{x\}$ I actually put in $\mathcal{F}$, the more "value" I extract per shadow-set. That pushes me toward families that are *closed up* and *concentrated*: keep everything on a small ground set, so that the same small pool of $(k-1)$-sets gets recycled. The most concentrated thing I can write down is a clique: take *all* $k$-subsets of a small ground set $[a]$. Then $\mathcal{F} = [a]^{(k)}$, of size $\binom{a}{k}$, and its shadow is *all* $(k-1)$-subsets of $[a]$ — nothing else is reachable — so $|\partial\mathcal{F}| = \binom{a}{k-1}$. Every $(k-1)$-subset of $[a]$ is shared among the $a-(k-1)$ different $k$-sets that contain it. That is maximal sharing. It really feels like the clique should be the minimiser when $m = \binom{a}{k}$.

But "feels like" has buried a lot of false conjectures in this subject, so I distrust it, and anyway it only answers the question for the special counts $m = \binom{a}{k}$. What do I do for an $m$ strictly between $\binom{a}{k}$ and $\binom{a+1}{k}$? The honest greedy move: fill the biggest clique I can, $[a]^{(k)}$ with $a$ as large as possible so that $\binom{a}{k} \le m$, and then I have $m - \binom{a}{k}$ sets left to place. Where do they go? They have to use the element $a+1$ (everything not using $a+1$ already lives inside $[a]^{(k)}$, which I've exhausted). A $k$-set through $a+1$ is $\{a+1\}\cup T$ with $T$ a $(k-1)$-subset of $[a]$. To keep *those* concentrated, I should again take a clique among the $T$'s: let the remaining sets be $\{a+1\}\cup T$ with $T$ ranging over $[b]^{(k-1)}$ for the largest $b$ with $\binom{b}{k-1} \le m - \binom{a}{k}$. And recurse on what's left.

Unrolling that recursion, the number $m$ has been written as
$$ m = \binom{a_k}{k} + \binom{a_{k-1}}{k-1} + \binom{a_{k-2}}{k-2} + \cdots + \binom{a_s}{s}, \qquad a_k > a_{k-1} > \cdots > a_s \ge s \ge 1. $$
This is forced and unique if at each stage I take the largest binomial coefficient that still fits: pick $a_k$ maximal with $\binom{a_k}{k}\le m$, subtract, drop to index $k-1$, repeat. The strict decrease $a_k>a_{k-1}>\cdots$ is exactly the statement that I genuinely couldn't have fit a bigger clique at the previous stage. It's a base-binomial expansion of $m$ — the binomial analogue of writing a number in binary. Call it the $k$-cascade representation. Good: this is the bookkeeping that lets me talk about *every* $m$, not just the round ones.

Now what is the shadow of this greedy stack? The biggest clique $[a_k]^{(k)}$ contributes its full lower layer $[a_k]^{(k-1)}$, size $\binom{a_k}{k-1}$. The next block is $\{a_k+1\}$ appended to $[a_{k-1}]^{(k-1)}$; removing an element from such a set either drops the appended $a_k+1$ — giving a $(k-1)$-set inside $[a_{k-1}]^{(k-1)}\subseteq[a_k]^{(k-1)}$, which I've *already counted* in the first block's shadow — or drops something from the $[a_{k-1}]^{(k-1)}$ part, giving $\{a_k+1\}$ appended to a $(k-2)$-set from $[a_{k-1}]^{(k-2)}$, which is genuinely new, $\binom{a_{k-1}}{k-2}$ of them. And so on down the cascade. So the new contributions telescope to
$$ \binom{a_k}{k-1} + \binom{a_{k-1}}{k-2} + \cdots + \binom{a_s}{s-1}. $$
That's a beautiful pattern: the shadow of the greedy stack has the *same cascade shape*, with every index pushed down by one. Write $\partial^{(k)}(m) := \binom{a_k}{k-1}+\binom{a_{k-1}}{k-2}+\cdots+\binom{a_s}{s-1}$ for it. Let me sanity-check the arithmetic on a small case so I'm not fooling myself: $k=3$, $m=14$. Greedy: $\binom{5}{3}=10\le 14$, remainder $4$; $\binom{3}{2}=3\le 4$, remainder $1$; $\binom{1}{1}=1$. So $14 = \binom{5}{3}+\binom{3}{2}+\binom{1}{1}$ and the predicted shadow is $\binom{5}{2}+\binom{3}{1}+\binom{1}{0} = 10+3+1 = 14$. If I actually list the first fourteen $3$-sets greedily and grind out their shadow, I get fourteen $(k-1)$-sets. The pattern holds.

So my conjecture sharpens to: for every $\mathcal{F}$ of size $m$,
$$ |\partial\mathcal{F}| \ge \partial^{(k)}(|\mathcal{F}|), $$
with equality for the greedy stack. Now I need a clean *description* of the extremal family, because "greedy stack" is a procedure, not an order, and I want an order so I can say "the first $m$ sets" and reason about its initial segments uniformly.

Let me look at what the stack is, set by set. The blocks are: all $k$-sets inside $[a_k]$; then $k$-sets using $a_k+1$ and otherwise inside $[a_{k-1}]$; then using $\{a_{k-1}+1, a_k+1\}$ and otherwise inside $[a_{k-2}]$; and so on. The organising principle is: **avoid large elements for as long as possible.** A set comes earlier exactly when its largest "distinguishing" element is smaller. That is an order. Concretely: order $k$-sets by $A < B \iff \max(A\triangle B) \in B$. Sets whose largest elements are small come first; you only introduce a big element when you've used up everything below it. This is the colexicographic order. Let me list the first few $3$-sets to make sure it agrees with the greedy stack: $123,124,134,234,125,135,235,145,245,345,\dots$ — yes, that's exactly first-the-clique-on-$\{1,2,3,4\}$, then start using $5$, smallest companions first. And a nice bonus I notice: this order doesn't care about the ambient $n$ at all — the initial segment of length $m$ is the same whether I'm working in $[10]$ or $[1000]$. The shadow was intrinsic; so is the candidate extremal family. (Lexicographic order, by contrast — order by the *smallest* differing element — would give initial segments that depend on $n$ and whose shadows aren't themselves initial segments. Colex is the right order precisely because of the self-similarity I just found.)

Let me make that self-similarity exact, because it's the cleanest form of the equality case. If $C$ is the colex initial segment of length $m$ with cascade $(a_k,\dots,a_s)$, then I claim its shadow $\partial C$ is *itself* the colex initial segment one layer down with the *same* parameters $(a_k,\dots,a_s)$, of length $\partial^{(k)}(m)$. I basically just showed this when I telescoped: the shadow block-by-block is all of $[a_k]^{(k-1)}$, then $\{a_k+1\}$ on $[a_{k-1}]^{(k-2)}$, etc., which is exactly the colex initial segment on $(k-1)$-sets with cascade $(a_k,\dots,a_s)$ read in indices $k-1,k-2,\dots,s-1$. So the colex shadow stays an initial segment — it never leaks out into "later" sets. That's the structural fact that makes colex the privileged order, and it gives equality in my conjectured bound for free.

Now the actual content: proving that *no* family does better than the colex initial segment. The clique guess gives me a target but not an argument, and direct counting over all families is hopeless. I need a transformation that takes an arbitrary $\mathcal{F}$ and pushes it toward looking like the colex initial segment, while never increasing the shadow and never changing the size. If I can do that, then the worst case is achieved by something colex-like and I'm done.

What should the transformation be? Colex-extremal sets *avoid large elements*. So the move should be: take a large element and replace it with a smaller one. The smallest target is $1$. Define, for $i\ge 2$, the shift $S_i$: for each set $F\in\mathcal{F}$, if $i\in F$ and $1\notin F$ and the swapped set $F\setminus\{i\}\cup\{1\}$ is *not already in* $\mathcal{F}$, then replace $F$ by $F\setminus\{i\}\cup\{1\}$; otherwise leave $F$ alone. The "not already in" clause is forced — if the target is occupied I can't move there without losing a set — and when it's occupied I'll say $F$ is *blocked*.

First I need this to preserve size. Could two different sets $F, F'$ collide to the same image under $S_i$? Suppose $F$ moved, so its image is $F\setminus\{i\}\cup\{1\}$, which by the rule was not already in $\mathcal{F}$ — in particular $F'$, if it stayed put, can't equal that image, and if $F'$ also moved then $F'\setminus\{i\}\cup\{1\}$ equals $F\setminus\{i\}\cup\{1\}$ forces $F=F'$ after swapping $1$ back to $i$. So $S_i$ is injective on $\mathcal{F}$, and $|S_i(\mathcal{F})| = |\mathcal{F}|$. Good.

The crucial thing is whether shifting can *hurt* the shadow. I want $|\partial S_i(\mathcal{F})| \le |\partial\mathcal{F}|$. The clean way to get this is to show $\partial S_i(\mathcal{F}) \subseteq S_i(\partial\mathcal{F})$ — the shadow of the shifted family sits inside the shift of the shadow — because $S_i$ on the $(k-1)$-layer also preserves size, so a subset relation gives the size inequality immediately. This is a "subcommutativity": shifting and taking the shadow almost commute, with $\partial S_i \subseteq S_i \partial$.

Let me actually check it. Take $E \in \partial S_i(\mathcal{F})$, so $E = S_i(F)\setminus\{x\}$ for some $F\in\mathcal{F}$ and $x\in S_i(F)$. I want $E\in S_i(\partial\mathcal{F})$, and I'll case on how $1$ and $i$ sit relative to $S_i(F)$.

If neither $1$ nor $i$ is in $S_i(F)$: then $F$ didn't move (a move would have put $1$ in), so $S_i(F)=F$ and $E\subset F$, hence $E\in\partial\mathcal{F}$; and since $i\notin E$, $S_i(E)=E$, so $E\in S_i(\partial\mathcal{F})$.

If both $1$ and $i$ are in $S_i(F)$: $i$ being present means $F$ didn't move, $S_i(F)=F$, so $E\in\partial\mathcal{F}$. If $x\ne 1$ then $1\in E$, so $E$ can't shift (its target slot would need $1$ removed) and $S_i(E)=E\in S_i(\partial\mathcal{F})$. If $x=1$, then $E' := E\setminus\{i\}\cup\{1\}\subset F$ as well, so $E'\in\partial\mathcal{F}$, which blocks $E$ from shifting, so again $S_i(E)=E\in S_i(\partial\mathcal{F})$.

If $S_i(F)$ contains $i$ but not $1$: then $F$ didn't move and $S_i(F)=F$, but $i\in F$, $1\notin F$ means $F$ *was* blocked, i.e. $F':=F\setminus\{i\}\cup\{1\}\in\mathcal{F}$. Now $E\subset F$ so $E\in\partial\mathcal{F}$. If $x=i$ then $i\notin E$, $S_i(E)=E$, done. If $x\ne i$, then $E':=F'\setminus\{x\}\in\partial\mathcal{F}$ is the blocker for $E$ (it's $E$ with $i\to1$), so $E$ doesn't shift and $S_i(E)=E\in S_i(\partial\mathcal{F})$.

If $S_i(F)$ contains $1$ but not $i$: then $i\notin E$ so $S_i(E)=E$. If $F$ didn't move, $E\subset F$ gives $E\in\partial\mathcal{F}=S_i(\partial\mathcal{F})$ here. If $F$ moved, $F=S_i(F)\setminus\{1\}\cup\{i\}$. If $x=1$ then $E\subset F$, so $E\in\partial\mathcal{F}$. If $x\ne 1$, let $E':=E\setminus\{1\}\cup\{i\}\subset F$, so $E'\in\partial\mathcal{F}$; then either $E$ is also in $\partial\mathcal{F}$, or $E'$ isn't blocked and $E=S_i(E')\in S_i(\partial\mathcal{F})$. Either way $E\in S_i(\partial\mathcal{F})$.

That exhausts the cases, so $\partial S_i(\mathcal{F})\subseteq S_i(\partial\mathcal{F})$ and therefore $|\partial S_i(\mathcal{F})| \le |\partial\mathcal{F}|$. Shifting never hurts.

So I iterate: as long as some $S_i$ actually changes $\mathcal{F}$, apply it; each application strictly increases the total number of sets containing $1$ (a successful shift converts a $1$-free set into a $1$-containing one and never the reverse), so the process can't run forever and stops at a family $\mathcal{G}$ with $S_i(\mathcal{G})=\mathcal{G}$ for every $i\ge 2$ — call it *stable* — with $|\mathcal{G}|=m$ and $|\partial\mathcal{G}|\le|\partial\mathcal{F}|$. I've reduced to stable families.

Now I'd love to say "the only stable families are the colex initial segments, so we're done." Let me test that hope. Stable means I can't push any large element down to $1$. But consider $\{1,3\}$ and $\{2,3\}$ as a $2$-uniform family: I can't shift $\{2,3\}$ to $\{1,3\}$ because $\{1,3\}$ is occupied, and there's no other shift available — it's stable, yet it's not a colex initial segment (the colex initial segment of size $2$ on $2$-sets is $\{1,2\},\{1,3\}$). So stability is weaker than being a colex initial segment. Shifting alone doesn't pin down the extremal family. I don't get the theorem for free from "stable = colex."

But I don't actually need stable to *equal* colex; I only need stable families to satisfy the cascade bound, and stability gives me real structural leverage. Here's the leverage. Partition the stable $\mathcal{F}$ by membership of the element $1$: $\mathcal{F}_0 = \{F : 1\notin F\}$ and $\mathcal{F}_1 = \{F : 1\in F\}$. The sets in $\mathcal{F}_1$ all contain $1$, so strip it off: $\mathcal{F}_1' = \{F\setminus\{1\} : F\in\mathcal{F}_1\}$, a $(k-1)$-uniform family with $|\mathcal{F}_1'| = |\mathcal{F}_1|$.

Stability forces $\mathcal{F}_1'$ to be large relative to the boundary of $\mathcal{F}_0$. Take $E\in\partial\mathcal{F}_0$, so $E = F\setminus\{x\}$ for some $F\in\mathcal{F}_0$ and $x\in F$; since $1\notin F$, we have $x\ge 2$. Because $\mathcal{F}$ is stable, $S_x(\mathcal{F})=\mathcal{F}$, and in particular $F$ couldn't shift via $x$ — but $x\in F$ and $1\notin F$, so the only reason it's stuck is that it was *blocked*: $F\setminus\{x\}\cup\{1\}\in\mathcal{F}$, hence in $\mathcal{F}_1$. Stripping the $1$, $E = (F\setminus\{x\}\cup\{1\})\setminus\{1\} \in \mathcal{F}_1'$. So $\partial\mathcal{F}_0\subseteq\mathcal{F}_1'$ and therefore $|\mathcal{F}_1'|\ge|\partial\mathcal{F}_0|$. Stability has converted a boundary of the $1$-free part into actual membership in the (stripped) $1$-containing part.

And the shadow of the whole family is governed by $\mathcal{F}_1'$. Compute $\partial\mathcal{F} = \partial\mathcal{F}_0\cup\partial\mathcal{F}_1$. I just showed $\partial\mathcal{F}_0\subseteq\mathcal{F}_1'$. What is $\partial\mathcal{F}_1$? Removing an element from a set $F = \{1\}\cup F'$ with $F'\in\mathcal{F}_1'$ either removes $1$, yielding $F'\in\mathcal{F}_1'$, or removes some $x\in F'$, yielding $\{1\}\cup(F'\setminus\{x\})$ — that is, $\{1\}$ glued onto a member of $\partial\mathcal{F}_1'$. So $\partial\mathcal{F}_1 = \mathcal{F}_1' \ \sqcup\ \big(\{1\}\cdot\partial\mathcal{F}_1'\big)$, and these two parts are disjoint because the first never contains $1$ and the second always does. Since $\partial\mathcal{F}_0\subseteq\mathcal{F}_1'$ is already swallowed by the first part, the entire shadow is exactly
$$ |\partial\mathcal{F}| = |\mathcal{F}_1'| + |\partial\mathcal{F}_1'|. $$
This is the engine. The $k$-layer shadow problem has collapsed onto a $(k-1)$-layer family $\mathcal{F}_1'$: I just need $\mathcal{F}_1'$ to be big (lower bound on $|\mathcal{F}_1'|$) and then to bound *its* shadow (which is a smaller instance of the very theorem I'm proving). Two inductions are about to fall out — one on $k$ for the shadow of $\mathcal{F}_1'$, one on $m$ for the size of $\mathcal{F}_1'$.

Set it up as induction on $k$, and within that, induction on $m$. Take $\mathcal{F}$ stable, of size $m$ with cascade $\binom{a_k}{k}+\binom{a_{k-1}}{k-1}+\cdots+\binom{a_s}{s}$, minimising the shadow. I need a lower bound on $|\mathcal{F}_1'|$, and the natural target — the one that makes the final sum telescope — is
$$ |\mathcal{F}_1'| \ge \binom{a_k-1}{k-1} + \binom{a_{k-1}-1}{k-2} + \cdots + \binom{a_s-1}{s-1}. $$
Why this exact target? Because the cascade of $\mathcal{F}_1'$ should be the cascade of $\mathcal{F}$ with every top argument knocked down by one — if $\mathcal{F}$ is the greedy stack, then $\mathcal{F}_1'$, the sets through $1$ with $1$ stripped, is itself a greedy stack on $\{2,3,\dots\}$ shrunk by one. So this is the right quantity to aim for.

Suppose it fails: $|\mathcal{F}_1'| < \binom{a_k-1}{k-1}+\cdots+\binom{a_s-1}{s-1}$. Then since $|\mathcal{F}_0| = m - |\mathcal{F}_1'| = m - |\mathcal{F}_1|$,
$$ |\mathcal{F}_0| > \Big[\binom{a_k}{k}+\cdots+\binom{a_s}{s}\Big] - \Big[\binom{a_k-1}{k-1}+\cdots+\binom{a_s-1}{s-1}\Big]. $$
Pascal's rule says $\binom{a}{i} - \binom{a-1}{i-1} = \binom{a-1}{i}$ — let me confirm the direction, $\binom{a-1}{i}+\binom{a-1}{i-1}=\binom{a}{i}$, yes, so $\binom{a}{i}-\binom{a-1}{i-1}=\binom{a-1}{i}$. Applying it termwise,
$$ |\mathcal{F}_0| > \binom{a_k-1}{k}+\binom{a_{k-1}-1}{k-1}+\cdots+\binom{a_s-1}{s}. $$
This right-hand side is the threshold I get by shaving one from every top argument; if the last shaved term becomes $\binom{s-1}{s}=0$, I drop that zero term. Since $|\mathcal{F}_0|$ is an integer strictly above the threshold, and since colex initial segments are nested so $\partial^{(k)}(r)$ is nondecreasing in $r$, the next possible size already has shifted shadow at least the sum I need. Also $\mathcal{F}_0$ has size strictly below $m$: a nonempty stable family must contain a set through $1$, because otherwise any set could shift one of its elements down to $1$ without being blocked. So the inner induction hypothesis on $m$ applies to $\mathcal{F}_0$:
$$ |\partial\mathcal{F}_0| \ge \binom{a_k-1}{k-1}+\binom{a_{k-1}-1}{k-2}+\cdots+\binom{a_s-1}{s-1}, $$
with the zero-term case covered by that monotonicity step. But I proved $|\mathcal{F}_1'|\ge|\partial\mathcal{F}_0|$, so $|\mathcal{F}_1'|$ is at least that very sum — contradicting the supposed failure. Hence the lower bound on $|\mathcal{F}_1'|$ holds.

Now finish. $\mathcal{F}_1'$ is a $(k-1)$-uniform family, so by the *outer* induction on $k$ its shadow obeys the theorem:
$$ |\partial\mathcal{F}_1'| \ge \binom{a_k-1}{k-2}+\binom{a_{k-1}-1}{k-3}+\cdots+\binom{a_s-1}{s-2}. $$
Plug both into $|\partial\mathcal{F}| = |\mathcal{F}_1'| + |\partial\mathcal{F}_1'|$ and pair the $j$-th terms:
$$ \binom{a_j-1}{j-1} + \binom{a_j-1}{j-2} = \binom{a_j}{j-1} $$
by Pascal again. Summing,
$$ |\partial\mathcal{F}| \ge \binom{a_k}{k-1}+\binom{a_{k-1}}{k-2}+\cdots+\binom{a_s}{s-1} = \partial^{(k)}(m). $$
That is exactly the bound, and the colex initial segment achieves it, so it's sharp. The base cases are clean: for $k=1$ the shadow of any nonempty family of singletons is $\{\emptyset\}$ of size $1 = \binom{m}{0}$; and for $m=1$ a single $k$-set has shadow of size $k = \binom{k}{k-1}$. Both inductions are grounded.

Let me re-read the structure, because it's worth seeing why it works at all. Shifting was only ever a *reduction*: it let me assume stability without loss, since it never raised the shadow and preserved the count. Stability then did the real work — it forced the boundary of the $1$-free sets to be paid for inside the $1$-containing sets, which is what makes $|\mathcal{F}_1'|\ge|\partial\mathcal{F}_0|$ true and what lets the shadow decompose as $|\mathcal{F}_1'| + |\partial\mathcal{F}_1'|$. After that it's two nested inductions: $m$ to keep $\mathcal{F}_1'$ from being too small, $k$ to control its shadow, with Pascal's identity stitching the cascade back together at the end. The cascade representation was the right language precisely because each induction step shaves a binomial coefficient cleanly.

There is a second, more geometric way to see *why colex*: shifting-to-$1$ stops at stable families rather than driving all the way to the colex initial segment, and it bugs me that the endpoint of the reduction isn't the extremal family itself. Generalise the shift to a compression. For $i<j$ define $C_{ij}$ on a set: replace $j$ by $i$ when $j\in A$, $i\notin A$, else leave it. On a family take the images together with the sets that map onto already-present sets:
$$ C_{ij}(\mathcal{F}) = \{C_{ij}(A) : A\in\mathcal{F}\}\cup\{A\in\mathcal{F} : C_{ij}(A)\in\mathcal{F}\}. $$
This preserves cardinality by the same blocking-bijection argument, and a nontrivial $i<j$ compression lowers the weight $w(A)=\sum_{x\in A}2^x$ on the moved sets, so any controlled sequence of successful compressions terminates. But I have to be careful: the shift proof does not license the blanket statement $\partial C_{ij}(\mathcal{F})\subseteq C_{ij}(\partial\mathcal{F})$ for arbitrary compressions in arbitrary order. Compressions can increase the shadow if I use them carelessly. The usable compression variant is ordered: choose the compressions so the shadow is not increased at each step, and with that correct order the family can be compressed all the way to the colex initial segment. Along the way, once the family is compressed enough for the same split by the element $1$, every $E\in\partial\mathcal{F}_0$ is paid for inside $\mathcal{F}_1'$: if $E=F\setminus\{x\}$ with $F\in\mathcal{F}_0$, the compression replacing $x$ by $1$ forces $F\setminus\{x\}\cup\{1\}$ into the family, so $E\in\mathcal{F}_1'$. From there the same $|\mathcal{F}_1'|\ge|\partial\mathcal{F}_0|$ and $|\partial\mathcal{F}|=|\mathcal{F}_1'|+|\partial\mathcal{F}_1'|$ induction runs. So compression gives the geometric route toward small elements and colex, but it is not a shortcut saying that every fully compressed family of size $m$ is the colex initial segment. The colex segment is the explicit nested family where equality is visible, because its shadow is the one-layer-down initial segment.

At a round count $m=\binom{a}{k}$ the cascade is a single term and the colex equality example is the clique $[a]^{(k)}$. If another family has the same shadow size, the equality version of the induction leaves no slack: after relabelling the $a$ elements supporting the clique, it is the same complete $k$-uniform family. So the round-count minimisers are exactly the cliques on $a$ vertices, not some more scattered configuration.

One more thing I want for downstream use. The cascade bound is exact but a pain to compute with — manipulating $\binom{a_k}{k}+\cdots$ inside another proof is unpleasant. Let me extend binomial coefficients to real arguments, $\binom{x}{k} = x(x-1)\cdots(x-k+1)/k!$. On $x\in[k,\infty)$ this is strictly increasing from $1$ to $\infty$, so for any $m\ge1$ there's a unique real $x$ with $\binom{x}{k}=m$. Run the same induction but carry the bracketed real-$x$ calculations instead of the cascade: at the stable step I need $|\mathcal{F}_1'|\ge\binom{x-1}{k-1}$, and if it failed then $|\mathcal{F}_0| > \binom{x}{k}-\binom{x-1}{k-1} = \binom{x-1}{k}$ — the real-argument Pascal identity $\binom{x}{k}=\binom{x-1}{k}+\binom{x-1}{k-1}$ holds as a polynomial identity, so the same contradiction via the inner induction goes through; then the outer induction gives $|\partial\mathcal{F}_1'|\ge\binom{x-1}{k-2}$, and $\binom{x-1}{k-1}+\binom{x-1}{k-2}=\binom{x}{k-1}$ finishes it. (The one careful spot: if $x\in[k,k+1)$ then $x-1<k$ and I can't invoke the $(k-1)$-shadow induction directly — but there $|\mathcal{F}_0|>\binom{x-1}{k}\ge0$ makes $\mathcal{F}_0$ nonempty, and a single $k$-set already contributes $k=\binom{k}{k-1}\ge\binom{x-1}{k-1}$ to the shadow, so the bound holds without the hypothesis.) This is the Lovász form: if $|\mathcal{F}|=\binom{x}{k}$ then $|\partial\mathcal{F}|\ge\binom{x}{k-1}$. It's weaker than the cascade bound strictly between integer points but agrees at $m=\binom{n}{k}$, and it's the version I'd actually plug into other arguments.

And those other arguments are why I cared. The bound subsumes Erdős–Ko–Rado: an intersecting family $\mathcal{A}$ of $r$-sets means no member contains the complement of another, so passing to complements $\overline{\mathcal{A}}$ on the $(n-r)$-layer and iterating the shadow $n-2r$ times keeps it disjoint from $\mathcal{A}$; the shadow bound then forces $|\mathcal{A}|\le\binom{n-1}{r-1}$, with the star as the equality case — exactly EKR. And it answers the original motivation: with the usual convention that $f_i$ counts $i$-dimensional faces, a vector $(f_0,f_1,\dots,f_{d-1})$ is the face-count vector of a simplicial complex iff $f_{i-1}\ge\partial^{(i+1)}(f_i)$ for every $i\ge1$, because downward closure means the $(i-1)$-faces must include the shadow of the $i$-faces, whose vertex-size is $i+1$, and the colex/compressed complexes show the inequalities are simultaneously achievable. The shadow bound is the whole content of the characterisation.

The shadow of a $k$-uniform family $\mathcal{F}$ is $\partial\mathcal{F} = \{F\setminus\{x\} : F\in\mathcal{F},\, x\in F\}$. Write $m=|\mathcal{F}|$ in its $k$-cascade form $\binom{a_k}{k}+\binom{a_{k-1}}{k-1}+\cdots+\binom{a_s}{s}$ with $a_k>\cdots>a_s\ge s\ge1$. Then
$$ |\partial\mathcal{F}|\ \ge\ \binom{a_k}{k-1}+\binom{a_{k-1}}{k-2}+\cdots+\binom{a_s}{s-1}, $$
with equality for the first $m$ sets in colexicographic order — and at $m=\binom{a}{k}$ every minimiser is a clique on some $a$-element ground set, which is $[a]^{(k)}$ after relabelling. In the smoother real form: if $|\mathcal{F}|=\binom{x}{k}$ then $|\partial\mathcal{F}|\ge\binom{x}{k-1}$.

The causal chain, end to end: I wanted the smallest possible boundary of a fixed-size $k$-uniform family; maximal subset-sharing pointed at concentrated cliques, and greedily stacking cliques to hit an arbitrary $m$ produced the $k$-cascade representation and its index-shifted shadow $\partial^{(k)}(m)$; the colex order packaged those stacks as initial segments whose shadows are again initial segments; a shift that swaps large elements for $1$ never increased the shadow and let me assume stability, where the boundary of the $1$-free sets is forced inside the $1$-containing sets, collapsing the shadow to $|\mathcal{F}_1'|+|\partial\mathcal{F}_1'|$ and yielding the cascade bound by a double induction stitched with Pascal's rule; ordered compressions give the parallel small-element route without the false promise that arbitrary compression is harmless; and extending binomials to the reals gave the Lovász $\binom{x}{k}\to\binom{x}{k-1}$ form, from which Erdős–Ko–Rado and the $f$-vector characterisation follow.

```python
from math import comb

def k_cascade(m, k):
    """Greedy k-cascade representation: m = sum_i C(a_i, i), a_k > a_{k-1} > ... .
    Returns list of (a_i, i) with strictly decreasing a_i and i = k, k-1, ... ."""
    rep, i = [], k
    while i >= 1 and m > 0:
        a = i
        while comb(a + 1, i) <= m:        # largest a with C(a, i) <= m
            a += 1
        rep.append((a, i))
        m -= comb(a, i)
        i -= 1
    return rep                            # cliques stacked largest-first

def min_shadow_size(m, k):
    """Kruskal-Katona minimum |shadow(F)| over families F of m k-sets:
    push every cascade index down by one."""
    return sum(comb(a, i - 1) for (a, i) in k_cascade(m, k))

def colex_key(S):
    """Colex order on finite sets: A < B iff max(A symdiff B) in B.
    Equivalently order by sum of 2^element; initial segments are the extremal families."""
    return sum(1 << x for x in S)

def extremal_family(m, k, ground=None):
    """First m k-sets in colex order — the minimiser of the shadow."""
    from itertools import combinations
    if ground is None:
        ground = m + k                    # enough elements for the first m k-sets
    sets = sorted((frozenset(c) for c in combinations(range(1, ground + 1), k)),
                  key=colex_key)
    return sets[:m]

def shadow(family):
    out = set()
    for S in family:
        for x in S:
            out.add(frozenset(S - {x}))
    return out
```
