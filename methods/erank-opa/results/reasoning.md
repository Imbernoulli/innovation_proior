Let me start from what actually goes wrong when I fit a scene with millions of 3D Gaussians. I optimize each Gaussian's mean, rotation, scales, opacity, and color by gradient descent against a photometric loss — something like 0.8 times L1 plus 0.2 times one-minus-SSIM, comparing my rendered image to the ground truth from each training camera. Real-time rendering, gorgeous training-view reconstruction. But the held-out views are full of thin spiky artifacts, semi-transparent floaters, and the geometry — normals, surface coverage — is bad. The photometric loss is the whole story here, and it only ever sees pixels from the training cameras. It says nothing about what an individual primitive should look like. So the optimizer, doing exactly what I asked, finds whatever pile of Gaussians reproduces the training pixels — and a great many of those Gaussians come out extremely elongated, one variance enormous, the other two collapsed almost to zero. Needles. From a training camera a forest of needles can tile an image fine; rotate to a new view and each needle is a spike sticking out of the surface. The problem is under-constrained, and the missing constraint is on shape.

So I want to add a scalar penalty to the loss that pushes each Gaussian toward a shape that generalizes. It has to be computed only from the parameters I already have — no depth maps, no normal supervision, that would be cheating against the setup — differentiable so it rides the same backward pass, and cheap, because it runs at every one of tens of thousands of steps over millions of primitives, so anything worse than linear in the number of Gaussians is out. And it can't be a sledgehammer: thin, genuinely slender structures — a wire, a leaf edge — do exist in scenes and need to stay representable.

What's already on the table? The closest existing pressure is the compactness idea from recasting the Gaussians as samples — penalize each Gaussian's *extent*, by putting an L1 on its opacity and on its scale. With log-scales and logit-opacities, that's an L1 on exp(scale) and on sigmoid(opacity), small weight on each. It does something real: it shrinks Gaussians that aren't pulling their weight toward disappearing, keeps the set compact and prunable, and that helps. But stare at it for a second — it controls *how big* and *how many*, not *what shape*. Penalizing total size says nothing about the ratio of the three axes. A needle and a disk of the same total extent get penalized identically. So compactness alone will happily hand me a smaller needle. I'll keep the compactness term — it's genuinely useful, a clean prior on parsimony, and it keeps the primitive set tidy so a shape prior has clean material to work on — but it is not the thing that fixes needles. I need a second term that is *about shape*.

Other people have tried to attack shape directly and it's instructive where each stalls. The flatten-it approaches add a regularizer that drives one scale small so the Gaussian lies flat against the surface — which is the right instinct, density should concentrate near the surface, that's well known from surface reconstruction. But "one scale small" is exactly the ambiguity I'm fighting: a Gaussian with one small scale can be a *disk* (two big axes, one tiny — good, it covers area) or a *needle* (one big axis, two tiny — bad). Flatness doesn't separate them, and empirically those methods drift right back into needles. Even the approach that switches the primitive to a 2D disk so it *starts* perfectly planar — every primitive begins at the ideal shape — still watches the optimization collapse one in-plane axis and slide back to needle-shaped 2D Gaussians. So the degeneracy isn't about where you start; it's a basin the photometric loss actively prefers, and you have to push against it the whole time. And the aspect-ratio cap — bound max-scale over min-scale — is a two-axis comparison; it can't tell a disk from a needle either, and it over-punishes legitimately thin objects. The common failure across all of them is the same: they look at one or two scales at a time, and a Gaussian's shape is a three-way thing. I need a single number that looks at all three scales jointly and says, cleanly, where on the sphere-disk-needle spectrum this Gaussian sits.

Let me try to build that number from scratch. What I want is "how many axes effectively matter" for this Gaussian. The honest version of that is the rank of the covariance — a sphere is rank 3, a disk rank 2, a needle rank 1. But integer rank is useless to me on two counts. It's discrete, so it has no gradient, and I need to backprop through this. And it's brittle: a disk has one *exactly*-zero scale and so does a... no wait, a needle has *two* near-zero scales. Rank would call a perfect disk rank-2 and a perfect needle rank-1, which is actually the distinction I want — but in floating point with two tiny-but-nonzero scales everything is numerically rank 3, and there's no gradient to push a near-needle toward a disk. So I need a real-valued, differentiable surrogate for rank that varies *smoothly* as the scales change, sitting near 1 for a needle, near 2 for a disk, near 3 for a sphere.

How do I turn "spread of the three scales" into a smooth scalar between 1 and 3? The three scales describe how the Gaussian's energy is distributed across its three principal directions. If I normalize them into a probability distribution — what fraction of the "size" lives on each axis — then a measure of how *spread out* that distribution is would give me exactly the count I want: concentrated on one axis means low spread (needle), spread evenly over two means medium (disk), over three means high (sphere). The canonical measure of spread of a distribution is Shannon entropy. And there's a beautiful fact about entropy that makes it the right choice and not just *a* choice: the entropy of a distribution concentrated on `n` equal atoms is exactly `log n`. So if I take `exp(entropy)`, a distribution on one atom gives `exp(0) = 1`, two equal atoms give `exp(log 2) = 2`, three equal give `exp(log 3) = 3`. The exponential-of-entropy of a normalized spectrum is a continuous, differentiable number that equals the integer rank at the clean cases and interpolates smoothly between. That's precisely the effective rank from signal processing — the real-valued extension of rank, defined as `exp(H)` of the normalized singular values. It was built to answer "effective dimensionality," which is my question, primitive by primitive.

Now I have to be careful about *which* normalized quantity I feed the entropy, because the covariance and the scales are related by a square. The covariance is `Sigma = R S S^T R^T` with `S = diag(s_1, s_2, s_3)`. The rotation only orients it; the singular values of `Sigma` are the eigenvalues of `S S^T`, which are `s_1^2, s_2^2, s_3^2` — the *squared* scales, not the scales. So when the effective-rank recipe says "normalize the singular values," for a Gaussian that means normalize the squared scales. Define `S_sum = s_1^2 + s_2^2 + s_3^2`, the distribution `q_i = s_i^2 / S_sum`, the entropy `H(q) = -sum_i q_i log q_i`, and `erank = exp(H(q))`. Let me sanity-check this is the geometry I think it is, with concrete numbers in my head. Scales `(1, 0.01, 0.01)` — one axis dominant, two tiny: `q ≈ (1, ~0, ~0)`, entropy near 0, `erank ≈ 1`. Needle. Scales `(1, 1, 0.01)` — two equal, one tiny: `q ≈ (0.5, 0.5, ~0)`, entropy near `log 2`, `erank ≈ 2`. Disk. Scales `(1,1,1)`: `q = (1/3,1/3,1/3)`, entropy `log 3`, `erank = 3`. Sphere. It lands exactly where I want, and it's smooth in between — `(1, 0.01, 0.01)` gives about 1.002, `(1, 1, 0.01)` about 2.001. So `erank` is my shape coordinate. And it's strictly better than the pairwise-variance comparisons the other methods use, because it folds all three axes into one number and so never confuses a disk with a needle — the failure that sank flatness.

One thing this analysis hands me for free, before I even write a regularizer: I can just *measure* this on a trained scene and see what's happening. If I histogram `erank` over all Gaussians as 3DGS converges, the prediction is that a growing mass piles up near `erank = 1` — and that's exactly the overfitting story, more and more needles while PSNR and Chamfer stop improving. It would also tell me the flatten-it and 2D-disk methods, despite their different constraints, share the same needle mass near 1. The effective rank isn't just the basis for the fix; it's the diagnostic that proves the disease is shape-degeneracy and not something else. And it suggests the target: I'd like the mass to sit near 2 — disks — not pushed all the way to 3.

So now: how do I turn `erank` into a penalty? The naive move is to add a term that drives `erank` toward 2, like `(2 - erank)^2`. Let me think about whether that's right before I commit, because the requirements are subtle and a symmetric quadratic gets two of them wrong. First requirement: I must *not* punish `erank > 2`. A Gaussian that's between disk and sphere is fine — it's not a needle — and I have no reason to force everything to be exactly planar; over-flattening would fight the natural slight thickness scenes need. A quadratic in `(2 - erank)` punishes `erank = 2.5` as much as `erank = 1.5`, which is wrong; I want zero penalty above 2. Second requirement: thin but legitimate structures live at `erank` somewhere in `(1, 2)` — a slender disk, a wire — and I shouldn't crush them to exactly 2 either; I want only a *gentle* discouragement of being too needle-like, with the pressure ramping up *hard* only as I approach the genuine pathology at `erank = 1`. A quadratic ramps up linearly in the gap and treats `erank = 1.9` and `erank = 1.1` as merely a bit different; I want `erank → 1` to feel like hitting a wall, and `erank` near 2 to feel like almost nothing.

A one-sided barrier that explodes at `erank = 1` and is exactly zero for `erank ≥ 2` is what those two requirements describe. What function explodes as its argument goes to zero and is gentle as the argument grows? The negative logarithm. `-log(x)` goes to `+∞` as `x → 0+` and crosses zero at `x = 1`, going negative beyond. So if I let the argument be the distance from the needle limit, `x = erank - 1`, then `-log(erank - 1)` blows up as `erank → 1` (the needle) and equals exactly 0 when `erank - 1 = 1`, i.e. at `erank = 2` (the disk). That's almost magically aligned: the barrier wall sits at the needle, and the barrier *naturally* hits zero precisely at the disk. The only problem is that for `erank > 2` the log goes negative — `-log(erank - 1) < 0` — which would *reward* erank between 2 and 3 and pull Gaussians toward sphericity. I don't want that. So I clamp it at zero: `max(-log(erank - 1), 0)`. Now the penalty is a steep wall as `erank → 1`, decreasing through `(1, 2)`, exactly 0 at `erank = 2`, and flat zero for everything above. Let me check the shape numerically: at `erank = 1.001` the penalty is about 6.9, at 1.1 about 2.3, at 1.5 about 0.69, at 2.0 it's 0, at 2.5 the raw value is negative so the clamp gives 0. That's exactly the asymmetric soft barrier I argued for — punish needles hard, leave disks-and-beyond alone, and keep the pressure small only when an `(1,2)` shape is already close to a disk.

There's a numerical landmine: at a true needle `erank = 1` exactly, `-log(0)` is `+∞`, infinite loss and a NaN gradient. The standard barrier fix is to add a small floor inside the log: `max(-log(erank - 1 + eps), 0)` with `eps = 1e-5`. This does two jobs at once. It keeps the penalty finite at the needle — the value there becomes `-log(eps) = -log(1e-5) ≈ 11.5`, a large-but-finite wall height — and it keeps the gradient finite so a primitive sitting at the needle still gets a real, large push outward instead of a NaN. At the disk, `erank = 2`, the raw value is now `-log(1 + eps)`, slightly negative, and the clamp returns exactly 0; everything beyond the disk is clamped to zero too. So my per-Gaussian barrier is `max(-log(erank(G_k) - 1 + eps), 0)`, multiplied by a small weight.

Now I think I'm done, but let me actually trace what the optimizer will *do* with this barrier, because there's a loophole. The penalty is a function of `erank`, and `erank` is scale-*invariant*: it depends only on the ratios `q_i = s_i^2 / S_sum`, not on the overall size. Multiply all three scales by any constant and `erank` doesn't change at all. So the barrier tells the optimizer "make the two smaller axes more comparable to the largest" — but it expresses no preference about *how*. The optimizer can raise `erank` from 1 toward 2 by *growing the two small axes* to match the big one, inflating the needle into a fat disk or even a blob, instead of by *flattening* — keeping the third axis genuinely thin while letting the second grow. A bloated disk is bad: it pulls density off the surface, the exact opposite of the concentrate-near-the-surface prior I'm chasing. The barrier alone is shape-correct but size-blind, and the loophole is real.

I need to break the scale-invariance with an absolute downward pressure on the *smallest* axis, so that the cheapest way to satisfy the barrier is to flatten rather than inflate. Add the smallest scale itself to the penalty: `+ s_3`, where `s_3` is the smallest of the three scales. Now consider the two routes from a needle `(big, tiny, tiny)`. Route one, inflate: grow the two tiny axes up toward `big` — this raises `erank` and *increases* `s_3` (the smallest axis is now large), so the new `+ s_3` term punishes it. Route two, flatten: grow one tiny axis to match `big` and keep the third axis small — this also raises `erank` toward 2 (two comparable axes, one small) but keeps `s_3` small, so the `+ s_3` term stays cheap. The combination prefers route two: a *flat* disk near the surface, which is exactly what I wanted and what the barrier alone couldn't pin down. So the shape penalty for one Gaussian is `lambda * max(-log(erank - 1 + eps), 0) + s_3` — the barrier sets the shape, and the smallest-scale term sets the realization of that shape as flat rather than bloated.

There's a timing question I have to get right, because applying this from step zero would be a mistake. At initialization the Gaussians come from sparse structure-from-motion points and are roughly isotropic — `erank` near 3 — and early training is when densification is busy cloning and splitting to grow capacity and discover where the geometry even is. If I clamp shapes toward disks immediately, I'm imposing a strong structural prior on a scene whose structure hasn't formed yet, fighting the exploratory growth that's supposed to happen first. The natural fix is coarse-to-fine: let the early phase run free, let the geometry and the primitive count settle, and switch the shape barrier on partway through, once there's real structure for it to refine. Concretely, schedule the barrier to begin at iteration 7000 — well into the run, after the bulk of densification — and leave the compactness L1 on throughout. Early on the Gaussians sit at `erank > 2` and train stably; from 7000 the barrier starts pulling the needles that have formed back toward disks. The `step` argument is there precisely so I can gate the term on this schedule.

Let me also confront a second-order interaction I'd otherwise miss, because it nearly defeats the whole thing. The densification rule splits a Gaussian when its accumulated view-space positional gradient norm exceeds a threshold. For a needle, moving it perpendicular to its long axis changes pixels sharply, so it gets big gradients and gets split — needles densify happily. For a *disk* — which is what I'm now trying to create — the primitive covers many pixels, and the per-pixel positional gradients point in many different directions and substantially *cancel* when you take the norm of their vector sum. So a disk often fails the split test even when it should be subdivided, because the standard rule measures the norm of the summed gradient. The fix is to accumulate the *sum of the norms* instead of the *norm of the sum* — add up gradient magnitudes per pixel so they can't cancel — and then disks split properly; this is the revised densification criterion Bulò et al. and the ray-Gaussian-intersection line use. This isn't part of my penalty term; it's a change to the densification strategy that lives elsewhere in the trainer and is held fixed in my setup. But it's the reason the shape prior is even compatible with densification: without it, the moment I succeed in making disks, the densifier would stop subdividing them. With it, the disk-favoring penalty and the densifier pull the same way.

So I can now assemble the full regularizer that drops into the one empty slot — the function the trainer calls every step and adds to the photometric loss. Two pieces. The compactness L1 on `exp(scales)` and `sigmoid(opacities)`, always on, small weight each, inherited because it keeps the set parsimonious. The effective-rank shape prior — the log-barrier plus the smallest-scale flattening term — gated on after step 7000. Let me write the effective rank exactly as derived: square the (exponentiated) scales, normalize to a distribution over the three axes, take entropy, exponentiate. Guard every numerical edge: a tiny constant in the normalization denominator so a degenerate Gaussian doesn't divide by zero, a tiny constant inside the `log` of the entropy so `0 * log 0` is `0 * log(tiny)` and stays finite, and the `eps = 1e-5` inside the barrier's log. Everything is elementwise over the `N` Gaussians and reduced with a mean, so it's `O(N)`, exactly as required.

```python
import torch

# Per-Gaussian regularizer: compactness L1 (always) + effective-rank shape
# barrier (after warmup). Added to the photometric loss unscaled, so it
# carries its own weights. O(N), numerically guarded for autograd.

SCALE_REG   = 1e-2     # L1 on exp(scales): keep Gaussians compact
OPACITY_REG = 1e-2     # L1 on sigmoid(opacities): keep opacity sparse / prunable
ERANK_REG   = 1e-2     # weight on the log-barrier
ERANK_WARMUP = 7000    # coarse-to-fine: shape prior switched on only after this step
ERANK_EPS    = 1e-5    # floor inside the barrier's log: finite wall at the needle


def compute_regularizer(splats, step, scene_scale):
    s = torch.exp(splats["scales"])           # [N, 3]  actual scales from log-scales
    a = torch.sigmoid(splats["opacities"])    # [N]     actual opacities from logits

    # Compactness: shrink size and opacity of unneeded Gaussians (parsimony).
    loss = SCALE_REG * s.mean() + OPACITY_REG * a.mean()

    if step >= ERANK_WARMUP:
        # Effective rank of each Gaussian: singular values of Sigma = R diag(s^2) R^T
        # are the squared scales, so normalize s^2 into a distribution q over the 3 axes.
        s_sq = s * s                                          # [N, 3]
        q = s_sq / (s_sq.sum(dim=-1, keepdim=True) + 1e-12)   # normalize -> distribution
        H = -(q * (q + 1e-12).log()).sum(dim=-1)             # Shannon entropy per Gaussian
        erank = H.exp()                                       # exp(H): ~1 needle, ~2 disk, ~3 sphere

        # One-sided log-barrier: finite wall near erank = 1, exactly 0 at erank = 2
        # and above, with pressure on erank in (1, 2) that grows sharply near the needle.
        barrier = torch.clamp(-torch.log(erank - 1.0 + ERANK_EPS), min=0.0)

        # Smallest scale: break the scale-invariance of erank so the barrier is
        # satisfied by flattening (thin third axis) rather than inflating into a blob.
        s_min = s.min(dim=-1).values

        loss = loss + ERANK_REG * barrier.mean() + s_min.mean()

    return loss
```

Let me retrace the whole causal chain so I'm sure each piece earned its place. The photometric loss is shape-blind, so the optimizer overfits training views with needle Gaussians that spike in novel views — that's the disease. Compactness L1 controls size and count but not shape, so it leaves the needles, hence I need a shape term. Shape is a three-axis property, and the pairwise comparisons prior methods use confuse disks with needles; integer rank captures the right distinction but is non-differentiable, so I take its real-valued surrogate, the effective rank `exp(entropy of the normalized squared scales)`, which equals 1, 2, 3 at needle, disk, sphere and is smooth in between — and which, measured on a trained scene, both diagnoses the needle pile-up and names the target (`erank ≈ 2`). To penalize toward disks I need an asymmetric soft barrier — a finite numerical wall at the needle, zero at the disk and beyond, gentle near 2 but steep near 1 — and `max(-log(erank - 1 + eps), 0)` is exactly that, with `eps` keeping the needle finite and the clamp making `erank = 2` free. The barrier is scale-invariant and so admits a bloating loophole, which the `+ s_3` smallest-scale term closes by making *flattening* the cheap way to raise `erank`. Applying it from the start would fight early densification on unformed geometry, so I gate it on at step 7000, coarse-to-fine. And the densifier had to switch from norm-of-sum to sum-of-norms so it would keep splitting the disks I'm now creating. The result drops into the per-step slot as an `O(N)` parameter-only penalty, compactness always on and the effective-rank shape prior after warmup — pushing each Gaussian off the needle and onto a flat disk that covers surface and survives a change of viewpoint.
