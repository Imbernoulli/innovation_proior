We want a reinforcement-learning agent that is no longer judged on reward alone. Each policy induces, besides its discounted reward return $J_0(\pi)$, one or more expected discounted cost returns $J_i(\pi)$, and those must stay under prescribed budgets. The problem is

$$\max_\pi\; J_0(\pi)\quad\text{subject to}\quad J_i(\pi)\le d_i,\ \ i=1,\dots,p,$$

and the target is the reward-maximizing policy inside the feasible set $\Omega_C=\{\pi: J_i(\pi)\le d_i\ \forall i\}$. In a parameterized policy class both the objective and the constraints can be nonconcave, even though the occupancy-measure view is a linear program. The standard machinery turns safety into a saddle point: introduce nonnegative multipliers and optimize the Lagrangian $J_0(\pi)-\sum_i\lambda_i\,(J_i(\pi)-d_i)$, updating the policy by a policy-gradient, natural-gradient, or trust-region step while the multipliers are pushed up by observed violation and projected back to the nonnegative orthant. This is principled at the saddle-point level, and CMDP duality even gives zero duality gap under suitable representations. But the multiplier is the source of the practical lag. The policy step I actually execute at a given iteration is controlled by a dual variable that remembers past violations, so it is a slow actuator. If the policy is newly unsafe, the multiplier may still be too small to push back; if the policy is already back under budget, a large multiplier can keep suppressing reward while it decays. On top of that, the implementation inherits dual learning rates, multiplier initialization, and projection thresholds, all of which change behavior substantially. What is missing is a primal rule that keeps the simplicity of an ordinary policy-optimization step but reacts immediately to current constraint information.

I propose constraint-rectified policy optimization, CRPO. The key observation is that I do not actually need a weighted blend of reward and cost. I should ask what the next policy step is for. If every estimated cost is within budget, the only unsolved objective is reward, so spending the step on cost is wasteful; the step should maximize reward. If at least one estimated cost exceeds its budget by the allowed margin, then improving reward is the wrong priority because the policy sits outside the acceptable region; the step should reduce a violated cost. This converts the apparent reward-versus-cost trade-off into a choice of target for a single unconstrained policy-optimization step, decided from the *current* policy estimate rather than from accumulated dual history. There are no multipliers to tune, initialize, or project.

Concretely, at iteration $t$ under the current policy $\pi_{w_t}$ I estimate reward and cost action-values $\bar Q_t^i$ for $i=0,\dots,p$, reusing the same policy-evaluation rollouts. I then estimate each constraint return as a weighted average over the batch $B_t$,

$$\bar J_{i,B_t}=\sum_{j\in B_t}\rho_{j,t}\,\bar Q_t^i(s_j,a_j),$$

which costs nothing extra because it reuses the distributional information policy evaluation already produces. The switch is then a simple feasibility test with tolerance $\eta$: if $\bar J_{i,B_t}\le d_i+\eta$ for every constraint $i\ge 1$, I declare $w_t$ approximately feasible, add it to the set $N_0$, and take one unconstrained step maximizing $J_0$; otherwise I pick any one violated constraint $i_t$ with $\bar J_{i_t,B_t}>d_{i_t}+\eta$ and take one unconstrained step minimizing $J_{i_t}$. In natural-gradient form, with the tabular identity $\bar\Delta_t(s,a)=(1-\gamma)^{-1}\bar Q_t^i(s,a)$ for the chosen objective, this is a plus step for reward and a minus step for the selected cost,

$$w_{t+1}=w_t+\alpha\,\bar\Delta_t\quad\text{(reward)},\qquad w_{t+1}=w_t-\alpha\,\bar\Delta_t\quad\text{(violated cost)}.$$

The signs are exactly what the objectives demand: maximizing $J_0$ ascends the reward direction, so the sign is positive; minimizing a violated cost descends that cost, so the sign is negative, equivalently a policy-gradient implementation ascends on the negative cost advantage $-A_i$. Selecting only one violated constraint — random choice is a clean default, but any currently violated one works — is what keeps the update a single unconstrained optimization step rather than a combined multi-constraint subproblem.

The tolerance $\eta$ is load-bearing, not decorative, and it should not be mistaken for the whole feasibility guarantee. It defines the approximately feasible set used both by the switch and by the output rule. Too large and the method ignores meaningful violation; too small and the analysis has too little room around the constraint boundary, which is exactly where the optimum tends to live and where estimation error perturbs the switch. The analysis therefore sets $\eta$ on the same scale as the target error. In the tabular softmax setting, $\eta=\Theta\!\big(\sqrt{|S||A|}/((1-\gamma)^{1.5}\sqrt{T})\big)$ with natural-gradient stepsize $\alpha=(1-\gamma)^{1.5}/\sqrt{|S||A|\,T}$. The reported policy $w_{\text{out}}$ is not the last iterate but a sample drawn uniformly from $N_0$, the iterations whose estimated constraints passed the $d_i+\eta$ test; this is precisely what lets the feasibility proof bound true violation by the tolerance plus estimation error.

The convergence argument has to confront the fact that the optimizer is not following one fixed scalar objective — standard NPG analyses assume a single fixed function and so do not apply directly. The fix is to condition on the high-probability event that policy evaluation is accurate enough. On that event the switch partitions the iterations into the approximately feasible set $N_0$ and the per-constraint repair sets $N_i$. The reward argument then shows a dichotomy: either there are enough approximately feasible iterations to recover the usual NPG reward-progress bound, or the average reward over them is already no worse than the optimal feasible policy. For constraints, because the output is sampled from $N_0$, the violation is controlled by $\eta$ plus the critic or sampling error. In the tabular softmax setting this yields, with probability at least $1-\delta$, both

$$J_0(\pi^*)-\mathbb{E}[J_0(w_{\text{out}})]\le\Theta\!\Big(\tfrac{\sqrt{|S||A|}}{(1-\gamma)^{1.5}\sqrt{T}}\Big),\qquad \mathbb{E}[J_i(w_{\text{out}})]-d_i\le\Theta\!\Big(\tfrac{\sqrt{|S||A|}}{(1-\gamma)^{1.5}\sqrt{T}}\Big)$$

for every $i=1,\dots,p$, with inner-loop length $K_{\text{in}}=\Theta\!\big(T^{1/\sigma}(1-\gamma)^{-2/\sigma}\log^{2/\sigma}(T^{1+2/\sigma}/\delta)\big)$. The same switch and the same plus/minus signs carry over to the neural softmax setting, where the update is applied to the temperature-scaled parameter $\tau_t W_t$ with $\tau_{t+1}=\tau_t+\alpha$; there $\alpha=\Theta(1/\sqrt{T})$, $K_{\text{in}}=\Theta((1-\gamma)^2\sqrt{m})$, and $\eta=\Theta\!\big(m/((1-\gamma)\sqrt{T})+1/((1-\gamma)^{2.5}m^{1/8})\big)$, and the objective and every constraint violation are bounded by a $1/((1-\gamma)\sqrt{T})$ term plus a finite-width term of order $(1-\gamma)^{-2.5}m^{-1/8}\log^{1/4}\!\big((1-\gamma)^2 T\sqrt{m}/\delta\big)$.

So CRPO removes dual variables not by relaxing the constraint but by replacing the dual-weighted blend with a current-feasibility switch: approximately feasible policies spend the next step on reward, and policies with an estimated violated cost spend it reducing one violated cost. The proof is built around exactly that switch — the same signs, the same tolerance scale, and the same output-from-$N_0$ rule. The mechanism reduces to a tiny amount of code; the following is OmniSafe's upstream `OnCRPO`, a single-constraint, on-policy TRPO-backed realization of the same switch, where the cost estimate comes from logged episodic cost and the tolerance is named `distance` (default `2.0`):

```python
Jc = self._logger.get_stats('Metrics/EpCost')[0]
if Jc <= self._cfgs.algo_cfgs.cost_limit + self._cfgs.algo_cfgs.distance:
    return adv_r
return -adv_c
```

This is faithful to the theoretical sign and threshold logic — return the reward advantage when approximately feasible, and the negated cost advantage $-A_c$ when the logged cost exceeds $d+\eta$ — while the theoretical algorithm is more general: it allows multiple constraints, chooses one violated constraint to repair, and samples the final output from the approximately feasible set $N_0$.
