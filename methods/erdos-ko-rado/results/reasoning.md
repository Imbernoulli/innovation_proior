Let me state the thing I actually want and then refuse to be satisfied until I understand it. I have a ground set `[n] = {1,…,n}`, I look at families `F` of `k`-element subsets where every two members share a point, and I want the largest such `F`. I already know one good family: fix a point `x` and take every `k`-set through `x`. Any two of them meet at `x`, and there are `\binom{n−1}{k−1}` of them. I am fairly sure this is best possible when `n ≥ 2k`, because every small case I check by hand stops there, and below `2k` the question collapses — any two `k`-sets in `[n]` with `n < 2k` are forced to meet, so then *all* `\binom{n}{k}` sets form an intersecting family and there's nothing to prove. So the live conjecture is: for `n ≥ 2k`, `|F| ≤ \binom{n−1}{k−1}`, with the star attaining it.

The proof I have on the table for this is an induction, and I find it unsatisfying. You fix the parameters, pick the intersecting family that minimizes some potential like the total sum of all elements over all its sets, argue it must sit in a compressed canonical position, then invoke a Sperner-type covering lemma — count, two ways, the incidences between the small sets and the slightly larger sets covering them, get an inequality like `n_0(m − \ell_0) ≤ n_1(\ell_0+1)` — and use it to trade the family down to a smaller ground set or smaller subset size, splitting into several sub-cases at every step, until base cases finish it. It is correct. But at the end I have *verified* `\binom{n−1}{k−1}` without *understanding* it. Two things in particular bother me. Why is the answer exactly `\binom{n−1}{k−1}` and not some other binomial expression? And why is the hypothesis exactly `n ≥ 2k` — where does the number `2` come from? The induction produces both as outputs of bookkeeping; it doesn't tell me their meaning. I want a proof where the constant and the threshold are *forced and visible*, not extracted.

Let me look at the number itself for a clue. `\binom{n−1}{k−1} = \frac{k}{n}\binom{n}{k}`. So the bound is exactly a `\frac{k}{n}` fraction of *all* the `k`-sets. That's suggestive. It says: an intersecting family can occupy at most the fraction `k/n` of the whole space of `k`-sets. A fraction smells like an averaging statement. If I could show that, *on average*, an intersecting family takes up at most a `k/n` share of some structured slice of the `k`-sets, and if the slices tile the whole space symmetrically, I'd be done — and the `k/n` would *be* the answer instead of being reverse-engineered. So I should hunt for a structured collection of `k`-sets, repeated symmetrically across the whole space, inside which "intersecting" forces the family to be small in a clean ratio.

What does Sperner's world give me to imitate? Sperner bounds the largest antichain in `2^{[m]}`, and the two proofs I trust both work by *normalizing and slicing*. The LYM proof weights each set by `1/\binom{m}{\ell}` so that each full level contributes total weight `1`, and an antichain's weights sum to at most `1`; the bound drops out. The chain-decomposition proof cuts the cube into saturated chains, symmetric about the middle, and notes an antichain hits each chain at most once, so its size is at most the number of chains. In both, a hard *global* extremal count is converted into an easy *local* statement — per level, or per chain — by exploiting symmetry. That is the move I want to copy. The obstruction is that "antichain" and "intersecting" are different constraints, so Sperner's chains and weights don't transfer mechanically. I need slices adapted to *intersection*, not to *containment*.

So: what structured slice of `k`-sets interacts cleanly with intersection? Here's a generic version of the same idea that might tell me what to look for. Make the graph `G` on the `\binom{n}{k}` `k`-sets, joining two when they intersect. An intersecting family is a clique. And `G` is wildly symmetric: any permutation of `[n]` is an automorphism, and it acts transitively on the vertices — every `k`-set looks like every other. Vertex-transitive graphs are exactly where averaging over the automorphism group turns a local clique bound into a global one. Concretely, suppose I can find a *small* set `Y` of vertices (a slice of `k`-sets) with the property that any clique restricted to `Y` is at most `|Y|/m` for some `m`. Then I claim every clique `C` in the whole graph is at most `|X|/m`, where `X` is the full vertex set. Let me actually prove that, because it's the averaging engine and I want it airtight.

Let `N` be the order of the automorphism group. By vertex-transitivity, for any two vertices `u, v`, the number of automorphisms `g` with `u^g = v` is the same for all pairs, namely `N/|X|` (the orbit of `u` is all of `X`, so the stabilizer has index `|X|`, hence size `N/|X|`, and each target `v` is hit by exactly one coset's worth). Now count pairs `(u, g)` with `u ∈ C` and `u^g ∈ Y`. Fixing `u ∈ C`: for each of the `|Y|` targets `v ∈ Y`, there are `N/|X|` automorphisms sending `u` to `v`, so `|Y|·N/|X|` automorphisms put `u` into `Y`; summing over `u ∈ C` gives `|C|·|Y|·N/|X|` pairs. Now fix `g` instead: the image `C^g` is again a clique (automorphisms preserve adjacency), so `C^g ∩ Y` is a clique inside `Y`, of size at most `|Y|/m` by hypothesis, and `u ∈ C` with `u^g ∈ Y` is the same as `u^g ∈ C^g ∩ Y`, so each `g` contributes at most `|Y|/m` pairs; over all `N` automorphisms that's at most `N·|Y|/m`. Equate the two counts: `|C|·|Y|·N/|X| ≤ N·|Y|/m`, and the `|Y|`, `N` cancel, leaving `|C| ≤ |X|/m`. Clean. So the whole problem reduces to finding a *small symmetric slice* `Y` of `k`-sets in which intersecting subfamilies are provably at most `|Y|/m`, with `|X|/m = \binom{n−1}{k−1}`. Since `|X| = \binom{n}{k}` and I want `|X|/m = \frac{k}{n}\binom{n}{k}`, I need `m = n/k`. So the target is: a slice `Y` with intersecting subfamilies of size at most `|Y|·k/n`.

What should `Y` be? It has to be a family of `k`-sets that (a) is symmetric enough to be moved around by the group to cover everything, and (b) has the property that geometry forces intersecting subfamilies to be tiny — at most a `k/n` fraction of it. The induction proof never gave me such a slice; it pushed sets *down* toward a fixed point. Let me instead think about *position*. Right now my `k`-sets are abstract — `{2,5,9}` has no shape. What if I give the ground set a shape, an arrangement, and let `Y` be the `k`-sets that are "nice" with respect to that arrangement? The nicest possible `k`-sets relative to an arrangement of `[n]` are the *consecutive blocks*.

Try the obvious arrangement first: lay `1,…,n` out on a line and let `Y` be the `n−k+1` windows of `k` consecutive points, `{1,…,k}, {2,…,k+1}, …`. Two such windows intersect iff they overlap, i.e. their starting points differ by less than `k`. So an intersecting subfamily of these windows is a set of windows pairwise within `k−1` of each other in starting position. How big can that be? Windows `1,…,k` and `k,…,2k−1` already share the point `k` and start `k−1` apart — fine — but `1,…,k` and `k+1,…,2k` are disjoint. So intersecting windows are those whose starts lie in an interval of length `< k`; up to `k` of them. That's the right *number*, `k`. But look at the size of the slice: `|Y| = n−k+1`, and `n−k+1` is not `n`, so it doesn't tile the space symmetrically — and worse, the line has *ends*: the window `{1,…,k}` has no neighbor on its left, the windows near the boundary behave differently from the windows in the middle, and the automorphism group of `[n]` does not act transitively on linear windows (a cyclic shift would push `{n−k+2,…,n}` off the end). The boundary breaks the symmetry. I hit a wall: the line gives the right local bound `k` but the wrong, asymmetric, undersized slice.

The fix is to kill the boundary. Bend the line into a circle. Put `1,…,n` around a circle, and let the slice be the `n` **arcs** — the `n` sets of `k` consecutive points, one starting at each position. Now there are exactly `n` arcs, no special ones; a rotation of the circle permutes the arcs cyclically and is an automorphism of `[n]`, so this slice is genuinely symmetric — and `|Y| = n` is exactly the `m = n/k` I needed, since then `|Y|/m = n·k/n = k`. Everything lines up if I can show the local fact: on a single circular arrangement, an intersecting family contains at most `k` of the `n` arcs. Let me try to prove that, and watch where `n ≥ 2k` has to come in, because that's the threshold I'm trying to understand.

Suppose some arc `A` of this circle is in `F`; if none is, the count is `0 ≤ k` trivially. Write `A = \{a_1, a_2, …, a_k\}` going around, `k` consecutive points. Take any *other* arc `B` of the same circle that's also in `F`. Since `F` is intersecting, `B ∩ A ≠ ∅`, and `B ≠ A`. An arc is a contiguous block; if `B` overlaps `A` but isn't equal to it, then `B` slides off `A` at one end — that is, exactly one of `B`'s two boundaries falls strictly inside `A`. Concretely, there's an internal gap of `A`, between two consecutive points `a_i` and `a_{i+1}` (for some `i` from `1` to `k−1`), such that `B` contains exactly one of `a_i, a_{i+1}` — `B` "separates" that adjacent pair. So every arc of `F` other than `A` is pinned to one of the `k−1` internal gaps of `A`. Now, for a fixed gap between `a_i` and `a_{i+1}`, how many arcs separate it? An arc of length `k` whose block straddles this gap with exactly one of the two points inside it is determined by which side it extends to: there's the arc that ends at `a_i` (covering `a_i` and `k−1` points to the left of `A`), and the arc that starts at `a_{i+1}` (covering `a_{i+1}` and `k−1` points to the right of `A`). Call them `L_i` and `R_i`. These two are the only arcs separating that gap, and here is the crux: `L_i` and `R_i` are *disjoint*. `L_i` lives on one side, `R_i` on the other, each is a block of `k` points, and between them they occupy `2k` distinct points — which they can only do without colliding around the back of the circle if `2k ≤ n`. **There it is — `n ≥ 2k` is exactly the condition that makes `L_i` and `R_i` disjoint.** If `n < 2k`, the two blocks of length `k` wrap around and overlap, they are *not* disjoint, the argument breaks — and that is no accident, because for `n < 2k` the theorem is false anyway (everything intersects). So the hypothesis isn't a technical convenience; it is precisely the geometric fact that two opposite length-`k` arcs around the same gap don't touch.

Now finish the local count. For each of the `k−1` gaps, `L_i` and `R_i` are disjoint, so they cannot both be in the intersecting family `F` — at most one is. Therefore `F` contains at most `k−1` arcs other than `A`, hence at most `k` arcs in total on this circle. The local bound is exactly `k`. And it's tight: take `k` arcs all containing one common point — `\{a, …\}` sliding so they all still cover the fixed point — they pairwise intersect and there are `k` of them. So `k` is the truth, not slack.

Let me re-derive this same local bound a second way to be sure I haven't fooled myself with the pairing, because miscounting the gaps or the wrap-around is exactly the kind of slip that hides here. Suppose for contradiction the circle held `k+1` pairwise-intersecting arcs. Among arcs on a circle, "every two overlap" forces them to pile up: walk the starting points around the circle. If I had a set of arcs that pairwise intersect, consider the three arcs whose starting points are spread out the most. If those three together covered the entire circle, then some two of them would be disjoint — picture three blocks covering all `n` points with total length `3k`; the overlaps among consecutive blocks sum to `3k − n`, and a clique of arcs whose union is the whole circle has size at most (the spread of starts) which works out to at most `3k − n`. Since `n ≥ 2k`, `3k − n ≤ 3k − 2k = k`. So you can't get more than `k` whose union wraps all the way around. And if the arcs *don't* cover the whole circle, then their union is contained in an arc-shaped gap-free stretch, all starts lie within a window of length `< k`, and again there are at most `k` of them. Either way at most `k`. Good — same answer by a different route; the pairing wasn't an artifact. (I like the pairing argument better because it shows *where* `n ≥ 2k` enters, point-blank.)

Now I assemble the global bound, and I'll do it by direct averaging rather than threading it through the vertex-transitive lemma, because the circle makes the averaging completely concrete and I want the `k/n` to appear in the open. Pick a circular arrangement of `[n]` uniformly at random — equivalently, drop the `n` points onto the circle in random order. For a *fixed* `k`-set `A`, what's the chance it becomes an arc, i.e. lands as `k` consecutive points? Count linear orderings of all `n` points: there are `n!`. `A` is contiguous around the circle when its `k` points form one consecutive block: there are `n` rotational positions for where the block sits, `k!` internal orders of `A`'s points, and `(n−k)!` orders of the rest, giving `n·k!·(n−k)!` favorable arrangements. So
`P(A is an arc) = \frac{n·k!·(n−k)!}{n!} = \frac{n}{\binom{n}{k}}.`
By linearity of expectation, the expected number of members of `F` that are arcs of the random circle is
`E[\#\{A ∈ F : A \text{ is an arc}\}] = |F|·\frac{n}{\binom{n}{k}}.`
But I just proved that *for every* circular arrangement, the number of members of `F` that are arcs is at most `k`. An expectation can't exceed a bound that holds for every outcome, so
`|F|·\frac{n}{\binom{n}{k}} ≤ k,`
and therefore
`|F| ≤ \frac{k}{n}\binom{n}{k} = \binom{n−1}{k−1}.`
That last equality is the whole point made flesh: `\frac{k}{n}\binom{n}{k} = \frac{k}{n}·\frac{n!}{k!(n−k)!} = \frac{(n−1)!}{(k−1)!(n−k)!} = \binom{n−1}{k−1}`. The answer is exactly the `k/n` fraction I spotted at the start, and now I see *why* — a circle has `n` arcs, an intersecting family eats at most `k` of them, so it occupies at most a `k/n` share of the symmetric slice, and averaging over all circles spreads that share evenly over all `\binom{n}{k}` sets. The constant is forced, the threshold `n ≥ 2k` is forced, and there is not a single induction or sub-case in sight.

Let me restate the bound the dual way too, the pure double-count, because it's the same fact and it's reassuring to see the integers line up. There are `(n−1)!` distinct circular arrangements (fix one point to kill rotation, order the rest). Each `k`-set is an arc in exactly `k!·(n−k)!` of them — once a circle is given, a `k`-set being an arc is the event "its points are consecutive," and the number of circular arrangements realizing a *specific* `k`-set as a block is `k!·(n−k)!` (order the block internally, splice it into the circle of the rest). Count incidences `(\text{circle}, A∈F \text{ that is an arc})`: from the family side it's `|F|·k!·(n−k)!`; from the circle side it's at most `k` per circle over `(n−1)!` circles, so at most `k·(n−1)!`. Hence `|F|·k!·(n−k)! ≤ k·(n−1)!`, i.e. `|F| ≤ \frac{k·(n−1)!}{k!·(n−k)!} = \frac{(n−1)!}{(k−1)!·(n−k)!} = \binom{n−1}{k−1}`. Same number, same mechanism — the circle is the slice, the group of rotations-and-relabelings is the symmetry, and double counting is the averaging.

Now I want the extremal families, because a tight bound without "who attains it" is half a theorem, and I've already seen the boundary `n = 2k` must behave differently. The star `S_x` attains the bound — `|S_x| = \binom{n−1}{k−1}` and it's intersecting. The question is whether anything else does. First, at `n = 2k`, I know it does: each `k`-set has a unique disjoint complement, the `\binom{2k}{k}` sets pair off into `\tfrac12\binom{2k}{k} = \binom{2k−1}{k−1}` complementary pairs, and choosing one set out of each pair gives an intersecting family of exactly the extremal size that is, in general, nowhere near a star. So I should expect to prove uniqueness *only* for `n > 2k`, and the proof had better use `n > 2k` strictly, not just `n ≥ 2k` — that's a built-in check on correctness.

So suppose `n > 2k` and `F` is intersecting with `|F| = \binom{n−1}{k−1}`, and suppose toward contradiction `F` is *not* a star. Let me first nail down what "not a star" forces. Claim: if there are two points `x, y` such that *every* `k`-set containing `x` but not `y` is in `F`, then `F` is exactly the full star `S_x`. To see it, take any `k`-set `K` with `x ∉ K`; since `n > 2k`, there are at least `2k` points other than `y` available (in fact `n−1 ≥ 2k` points besides `y`), so I can pick a `k`-set `L` with `x ∈ L`, `y ∉ L`, and `L ∩ K = ∅` — I just need `k−1` points besides `x`, none of them `y`, all outside `K`, and there are `n − 1 − k ≥ k` such points, enough. By assumption `L ∈ F`. But `L ∩ K = ∅`, so `K ∉ F` (it would clash with `L`). Hence every set of `F` contains `x`, so `F ⊆ S_x`, and since `|F| = |S_x| = \binom{n−1}{k−1}` we get `F = S_x` — a star, contradicting "not a star." So: **a non-star extremal `F` has, for every pair `x, y`, at least one `k`-set containing `x` but not `y` that is missing from `F`.**

Second structural fact: there exist two `k`-sets `K, K'` differing in a single element (`|K ∩ K'| = k − 1`) with `K ∈ F` but `K' ∉ F`. If not, then for every `K ∈ F`, every `k`-set obtained by swapping one element is also in `F`; but single-element swaps connect all of `\binom{[n]}{k}` (the Johnson graph is connected), so `F` would be *all* `k`-sets, which isn't intersecting for `n ≥ 2k > 1`. So such a "boundary swap" pair `K, K'` exists.

Now I run the same circle machinery, but I steer the labeling to manufacture a contradiction. Label the unique element of `K \ K'` as `0` and the unique element of `K' \ K` as `k`, so that `K = (K \ \{0\}) ∪ \{0\}` and `K' = (K \ \{0\}) ∪ \{k\}`. By the first fact (applied to `x = 0`, `y = k`: not every `k`-set with `0` and without `k` is in `F`), pick a `k`-set `K''` with `0 ∈ K''`, `k ∉ K''`, and `K'' ∉ F`. Let `K ∩ K'' = \{0, 1, …, \ell − 1\}` with `1 ≤ \ell < k` (both contain `0`, so `\ell ≥ 1`); number the remaining points of `K` as `1, …, k−1` so that `K = \{0, 1, …, k−1\}`, place the element `k` (the one in `K' \ K`) at position `k` so it sits just clockwise past `K`, put the remaining points of `K''` at positions `n−k+\ell, …, n−1` going the other way around, and fill the rest of the circle with the leftover labels — all of this just choosing a circular arrangement of `[n]`. With this circle, `K = \{0, 1, …, k−1\}` is an arc, and so is its immediate clockwise neighbor `\{1, …, k\}`, which is exactly `(K \ \{0\}) ∪ \{k\} = K'`; on the other side, `K'' = \{0, …, \ell−1\} ∪ \{n−k+\ell, …, n−1\}` is the arc `[n−k+\ell, \ell−1]` (a block of `\ell + (k−\ell) = k` consecutive points wrapping through `0`). Apply the local lemma to this circle: `F` contains at most `k` arcs of it, and by the structure of the local bound (the only way to get `k` pairwise-intersecting arcs on a circle is to take `k` consecutive arcs all sharing a common point) those arcs in `F` would have to be `k` *consecutive* arcs sliding through `K`. There are exactly `k` such runs — one for each position `K` can occupy inside a length-`k` block. All but one of them contain the immediate clockwise neighbor `[1, k]`; the single exception is the run in which `K` is the clockwise *end*, namely the `k` arcs with starting points `n−k+1, n−k+2, …, n−1, 0` (ending at `K = [0, k−1]`), and that run contains `K''`, whose starting point `n−k+\ell` lies in `\{n−k+1, …, n−1\}` since `1 ≤ \ell ≤ k−1`. But `[1, k] = K' ∉ F` (the boundary-swap fact) and `K'' ∉ F` (chosen so), so each of the `k` runs has a forbidden arc and none of them can lie inside `F`. Hence the `k` arcs of `F` cannot be `k` consecutive ones through `K` — there's no run of `k` consecutive arcs available that stays inside `F`. That contradicts the only configuration that realizes the local maximum while keeping `K`. The contradiction means no non-star extremal `F` exists when `n > 2k`: the star is unique. And the argument genuinely used `n > 2k` (in the first fact, where I needed `n − 1 − k ≥ k`, i.e. `n ≥ 2k + 1`); at `n = 2k` exactly, that slack vanishes and the complementary-pair families slip through, which is exactly the boundary behavior I expected. Both halves agree.

Let me make sure I haven't quietly assumed something false in the boundary count. At `n = 2k`, the complementary-pair construction: there are `\binom{2k}{k}` sets, each with a unique complement also of size `k`, the complement is distinct from the set (`A ≠ [n]\A` since they're disjoint and nonempty), so the sets pair up perfectly into `\tfrac12\binom{2k}{k}` pairs. A family that picks exactly one from each pair is intersecting (it never contains both a set and its disjoint complement, and any two sets that aren't complementary already intersect because two `k`-subsets of a `2k`-set are disjoint only if they're complementary). Its size is `\tfrac12\binom{2k}{k} = \binom{2k−1}{k−1} = \binom{n−1}{k−1}`. So at the boundary the extremal families are exactly these "transversals" of the complementary-pair matching — a star is just one special such choice (pick, from each pair, the member containing a fixed `x`; every pair has exactly one member with `x`, since a set and its complement split `x` between them), but most transversals are not stars. The picture is consistent: tight bound for all `n ≥ 2k`; unique star for `n > 2k`; many extremal families at `n = 2k`.

Let me also confirm the bound can't be improved and that I haven't mixed up which direction `n ≥ 2k` cuts. The star gives `\binom{n−1}{k−1}` and the averaging gives `|F| ≤ \binom{n−1}{k−1}`, so the maximum is *exactly* `\binom{n−1}{k−1}` for `n ≥ 2k` — construction and bound meet. For `n < 2k` the averaging breaks at the disjointness step (`L_i, R_i` overlap), and rightly so, because the true maximum there is the trivial `\binom{n}{k}` (all sets intersect). So `n = 2k` is the exact phase boundary: the bound formula `\binom{n−1}{k−1}` is correct for all `n ≥ 2k` and tight, the extremal structure is rigid (only stars) strictly above `2k`, and at `2k` it loosens into the complementary-pair transversals. Everything the answer needs is now pinned down, and pinned to *reasons*: the value `\binom{n−1}{k−1}` is the `k/n` share of `\binom{n}{k}`; the threshold `2k` is the disjointness of opposite arcs around a gap; the rigidity above `2k` is the failure of complementary pairing to give freedom.

To say the whole causal chain in one breath: I wanted the maximum intersecting `k`-family and noticed the conjectured answer `\binom{n−1}{k−1}` is exactly a `k/n` fraction of all `k`-sets, which screamed "average over a symmetric slice"; antichain-style normalization pointed me to slicing-and-averaging but its slices fit containment, not intersection, so I looked for a slice fitted to *position*; consecutive blocks on a line had the right local bound `k` but a boundary that wrecked the symmetry, so I bent the line into a circle, getting exactly `n` arcs with full rotational symmetry; on one circle an intersecting family can hold at most `k` arcs because the arcs flanking a chosen arc pair off into disjoint opposites — and those opposites are disjoint precisely when `n ≥ 2k`, which is where and why the threshold lives; averaging that local `≤ k` over all circles, where each `k`-set is an arc with probability `n/\binom{n}{k}`, turns `k` into `|F| ≤ \frac{k}{n}\binom{n}{k} = \binom{n−1}{k−1}`; the star attains it, uniquely when `n > 2k`, while at `n = 2k` the complementary-pair transversals tie it.

I'll close by writing the result down as the clean theorem it has become, together with the two-line core of its proof.

```
Theorem (intersecting k-uniform families).
  Let 1 ≤ k and n ≥ 2k, and let F be a family of k-element subsets of [n]
  such that A ∩ B ≠ ∅ for all A, B ∈ F. Then
        |F| ≤ C(n-1, k-1).
  The star S_x = { A : x ∈ A } attains equality.
  If n > 2k, every extremal F equals some star S_x (uniqueness).
  If n = 2k, the extremal families are exactly the transversals of the
  complementary-pair matching (pick one set from each pair {A, [n]\A});
  these include the stars but are not only stars.

Proof of the bound (the cycle / circular-arrangement argument).
  Local: arrange [n] on a circle; among its n arcs (blocks of k consecutive
  points) F contains at most k. [Fix an arc A ∈ F; any other arc of F shares
  exactly one of some adjacent pair a_i, a_{i+1} of A (i = 1..k-1); the two
  arcs doing so are disjoint because each has length k and 2k ≤ n, so F holds
  at most one of them; total ≤ (k-1) + 1 = k.]
  Global: for a uniformly random circular arrangement, a fixed k-set is an arc
  with probability n / C(n,k); so E[# arcs of F on the circle] = |F|·n/C(n,k).
  Since this is ≤ k for every arrangement,
        |F|·n/C(n,k) ≤ k  ⟹  |F| ≤ (k/n)·C(n,k) = C(n-1, k-1).            ∎
```
