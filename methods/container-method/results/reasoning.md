I keep running into the same shape of question in completely different corners of combinatorics. A graph with no triangle. A subset of the integers with no 3-term arithmetic progression. A sum-free subset of an abelian group. A graph with no 4-cycle. A set of points in the plane with no four on a line. Every one of these is "avoid a fixed local pattern," and every one of them rewrites for free: take the basic objects as vertices of a hypergraph, take every occurrence of the forbidden pattern as a hyperedge, and the legal objects are exactly the independent sets. That rewriting costs nothing. The real difficulty starts right after it, because an independent-set family is typically of size close to 2^|V|, and I want its count, its typical member, and its behavior when I only get to see a random sparse slice of the ground set — not just the size of its largest member, which classical extremal theorems already pin down.

Some instances of this question are already solved by a trick that has nothing to do with independent sets as such. Erdős, Kleitman and Rothschild worked out the case where the forbidden graph H is a complete graph, and Erdős, Frankl and Rödl pushed the same idea to every non-bipartite H: the extremal number ex(n,H) is Θ(n²), and — this is the part that matters — every H-free graph turns out to be close, edge by edge, to one of a bounded number of extremal Turán-type graphs. So I can count H-free graphs almost for free: pick one of O(1) extremal skeletons, choose which o(n²) edges to flip relative to it, and the total comes to 2^{(1+o(1))ex(n,H)}, matching the trivial lower bound (every subgraph of an extremal graph is automatically H-free). This works because a non-bipartite forbidden pattern forces a stability phenomenon: near-extremal density already implies near-identical structure.

I try to reuse exactly this trick on a bipartite forbidden pattern, say H = C4. Now ex(n,C4) = Θ(n^{3/2}) — genuinely sparse, not a positive fraction of all possible edges — and the moment I ask whether every C4-free graph is close to one of a few canonical extremal graphs, the answer is no. Incidence graphs of projective planes, polarity graphs, and countless perturbations of each all achieve the same edge count while sharing almost none of their edges with one another. There is no small family of attractors to be close to, so the stability shortcut that just finished the dense case is unavailable here. This is exactly the question Kleitman and Winston set for themselves in the early 1980s — sharpened later into the still-open conjecture that the number of H-free graphs is at most 2^{C·ex(n,H)} for every bipartite H containing a cycle — and it is the same wall that sum-free sets, progression-free sets, and sparse-random extremal problems all hit: none of these forbidden patterns are dense enough for stability alone to finish the job.

If I can't describe a small number of attractor structures, I need something weaker: not a structural theorem about my mystery independent set I, but an invertible encoding of I using few bits, whatever I actually is. If such an encoding exists, the number of possible I is at most the number of possible encoded outputs — I never have to argue that I looks like anything in particular.

Here is a first attempt, worked out in the graph language (vertices of the encoding hypergraph are edges of K_n, hyperedges are copies of the forbidden pattern). Keep a "still alive" set A, starting as everything. In each round, sort what's left of A by degree measured only within A, and sweep through that order: every vertex that is not in I gets deleted on the spot and I move to the next one, until I hit the first vertex that is in I. That vertex goes into a fingerprint set S, and I delete its entire neighborhood from A too, since none of those neighbors could possibly also be in I — placing them alongside the vertex I just committed to S would recreate the forbidden pattern. Run this for q rounds.

Why interrogate vertices in degree order specifically, rather than some arbitrary fixed order? Because it turns an average-case density statement into a worst-case guarantee. If the graph induced on the current A still has at least β·C(|A|,2) edges, its average degree is β(|A|-1), and the very next vertex I commit to S — because I always pick along the current maximum-degree order — has degree at least that average, |N(v)∩A| ≥ 2e(A)/|A| ≥ β(|A|-1). So finishing a round doesn't just cost one deleted vertex for every "no" along the way; the final "yes" wipes out a β-fraction of whatever was still alive. That means density can't survive many rounds: after q ~ (1/β)·log(n/R) rounds, A has shrunk to at most e^{-βq}n ≤ R. Both halves of the bookkeeping are now controlled at once — I've committed exactly q vertices to the fingerprint (so there are only C(n,q) possible fingerprints), and whatever remains of A has collapsed to at most R elements (so there are only 2^R possibilities for the rest of I inside it). Multiplying these gives an honest bound on the number of independent sets of size m, roughly C(n,q)·C(R,m-q) — and every I is recoverable from its own (fingerprint, leftover) pair, because the whole peeling trace only ever depended on membership-in-I questions I already asked. I never showed any single I resembles a canonical shape; I showed that the deterministic process of building it, one degree-ordered question at a time, can only produce few distinguishable traces.

This machine settles C4-free graphs and, with the same peeling argument, the count of finite lattices — Kleitman and Winston's own original targets. Extending it further, case by case, is slow: it takes roughly three decades before the analogous graph-level statement is worked out for H = K_{s,t} and H = C_{2k}, and Sapozhenko needs a variant of the same peeling idea to count independent sets in regular graphs and sum-free sets in abelian groups (Green and Ruzsa get the sum-free case a different way, through Fourier analysis). Meanwhile Conlon–Gowers and, independently, Friedgut–Rödl–Schacht and Schacht are proving sparse-random extremal and Ramsey theorems — bounding ex(G(n,p),H), forcing monochromatic patterns inside random colorings — by working directly with independent sets of k-uniform encoding hypergraphs, with no container theorem at that level of generality to lean on. That is enough pressure to stop re-deriving the peeling argument by hand for every new forbidden pattern and instead lift it, once, to general k-uniform hypergraphs.

The direct port looks like this: at each round, find the (k-1)-subset of the still-alive vertices contained in the most hyperedges — the maximum-codegree small set — decide whether it belongs to the fingerprint under construction, and if so kill every vertex that would complete one of those hyperedges together with it. Try to control the whole process with a single top-level degree bound, Δ_k(H), the largest number of hyperedges through any (k-1)-set. If a fingerprint S of size b behaved like a uniformly random b-subset, a union bound over hyperedges says the expected number of vertices forced out, X(S), is at most k·τ^{k-1}·e(H), where τ = b/v(H): for each hyperedge, the chance that S already contains k-1 of its k vertices is about τ^{k-1}, and there are k ways to be missing one. Demanding this bound hold for every S, not just on average, is essentially a statement about Δ_k(H) alone.

That top-level bound is not enough, and the reason is structural, not a matter of loose constants. Suppose H contains a hidden ℓ-uniform skeleton G, for some ℓ < k, such that every hyperedge of H contains an edge of G — G can have as few as e(H)/Δ_ℓ(H) edges and still touch every hyperedge of H. Run the identical union-bound argument on G instead of H: the forced-out set can be pushed down to as little as ℓ·τ^{ℓ-1}·e(G) = ℓ·τ^{ℓ-1}·e(H)/Δ_ℓ(H), and if Δ_ℓ(H) happens to be large this collapses toward zero even while Δ_k(H) looks perfectly healthy. In that situation the peeling process stalls: a fingerprint of the size I planned for no longer guarantees any real shrinkage, because I only policed the top level and left a lower-uniformity bottleneck free to sabotage the compression from underneath. It's the exact same failure mode the graph algorithm's degree-ordering step was built to rule out — controlling total density while ignoring that a handful of vertices carry disproportionate degree — just recurring one level down inside the hypergraph.

So the degree bound has to hold at every level simultaneously: for every ℓ = 1,...,k, Δ_ℓ(H) ≤ (b/v(H))^{ℓ-1}·e(H)/r, for a scale parameter r that measures how much guaranteed shrinkage I'm demanding per fingerprint element. With that full ladder of conditions in hand, no matter which level a would-be bottleneck lives at, the forced-out set at that level is provably large, so a fingerprint of size at most (k-1)b really does buy a container smaller than V(H) by a definite margin r. This ladder — not one inequality about the top degree, but conditions running from single vertices up through (k-1)-sets — is the actual hypothesis I need, and it's exactly what closes the loophole the naive top-level attempt left open.

What one round of this lemma buys, by itself, is modest: every independent set now sits inside a container that is smaller than the whole vertex set by a fixed fraction, but not yet actually sparse. To get something usable I bootstrap: whenever a container is still edge-dense, I re-run the identical lemma on the hypergraph induced by that container alone, treating it as a smaller instance of the same problem. Each round multiplies the number of containers by a controlled factor while cutting either their size or their edge count by a constant factor, so after boundedly many rounds every surviving container is provably sparse, or close to some extremal or bipartite structure — whichever downstream fact the particular application needs. From there the same collection of containers plugs into a supersaturation lemma to bound extremal numbers inside a random subset G(n,p) (union-bounding over containers instead of over exponentially many independent sets directly), into a stability lemma to recover typical-structure results, or straight into a union bound for Ramsey-type and sparse-threshold statements. One peeling algorithm, generalized correctly by insisting on a full ladder of codegree conditions instead of a single top-level one, is what lets a single container lemma stand in for the bespoke construction that used to have to be reinvented for every new forbidden-pattern problem.
