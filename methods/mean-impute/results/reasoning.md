I have a table of numbers with holes in it. Some entries are `NaN`, and almost nothing I want to run downstream will tolerate that — the regression routine quietly drops any row that has a missing cell, the gradient-based learner can't even put a `NaN` into a tensor, and a distance between two rows is undefined the moment one coordinate is absent. So before I can do anything I have to turn this `(n, d)` table with holes into an `(n, d)` table of finite numbers. That's the whole job: a map from table-with-holes to table-without-holes. Let me not over-think it yet — let me think about what the constraints actually force.

First constraint: I have no oracle for the missing values. The only thing I have is the entries that *are* present, so whatever I write into a hole has to be manufactured out of the observed entries. Second: this isn't a one-shot cleanup of a single fixed table. A new row will arrive later, at test time, possibly with its own holes, and I'll have to fill *that* too — and if I fill the test row by some rule I re-derived from the test set, I don't have a well-defined predictor anymore, I have two different procedures. Whatever I do at training time I have to be able to freeze and replay on unseen rows. Third, and this is the subtle one: every number I write into a hole is a guess that I'm about to hand to the next stage *as if it were data*. So I can't treat the choice as free. The filler changes the statistics of the column it lands in, and I'd better understand how, because the next stage might be fooled by whatever structure I accidentally inject.

Why not just refuse to fill — keep only the rows that are complete and run on those? That's the reflex, and it's even unbiased in the nicest case. But let me actually count what it costs. Say each of the `d` columns is independently missing with probability `p`. The chance a given row has no hole anywhere is `(1 − p)^d`. Put in the kind of numbers I'm staring at — `p` around 0.2 — and watch it fall off a cliff: at `d = 8` it's about 0.17, at `d = 13` about 0.055, at `d = 30` about 0.0012. Thirty columns at twenty percent missing and I've thrown away 998 rows in a thousand. The width kills me. And even if I could afford the loss, dropping rows does nothing for the test-time problem — a new row with a hole still can't be scored. So discarding isn't a completion at all; it dodges the actual requirement. I need to fill.

Before I pick a filler I should pin down when filling can even be innocent, because that controls everything. The relevant fact is about *why* the entries are missing. Call `M` the indicator of which cells are absent. If the probability of a cell being missing doesn't depend on any value at all — missingness completely at random — then the entries I *do* see are an unbiased sample of the population, and a hole is just a coin flip that happened to land tails; nothing about the value made it disappear. If the missingness depends only on the *observed* part of the row — missing at random — then conditioning on what I can see, the holes are again uninformative about themselves. But if the probability of being missing depends on the value that's missing — high earners declining to state income, say — then the very fact of a hole carries information that no observed value can reconstruct, and no value-level filling can recover the truth. So the clean regime, where putting a number in a hole can be harmless, is when missingness carries no signal beyond what I already observe. I'll work in that regime and come back to what breaks when I leave it, because I suspect it'll bite.

Now, the simplest possible completion: one fixed number per column, write that number into every hole in the column. Call it `c_j` for column `j`. The question is which constant. Let me think about what I want it not to wreck. The most basic statistic anyone computes on a column is its mean. If the downstream stage cares about the column's central tendency — and a regression that includes the column certainly does — then I'd like the filler to leave the mean where it should be. In the completely-at-random regime the observed entries are an unbiased sample, so their mean `ȳ_j` is the honest estimate of the column mean. What constant, written into the holes, leaves the completed column's mean equal to `ȳ_j`? If I fill `n − n_obs` holes with `c` (where `n_obs` is the count of observed entries), the completed mean is `(n_obs · ȳ_j + (n − n_obs) · c) / n`, and that equals `ȳ_j` exactly when `c = ȳ_j`. Any other constant drags the completed mean toward `c`. So among all single constants, the observed mean is the unique mean-preserving one.

There's a second way to see it that I like better because it tells me the mean is *optimal* for a reason, not just convenient. Ask: what single constant `c` is closest, in squared error, to the observed values of the column? Minimize `Σ_obs (y_ij − c)²` over `c`. Differentiate, set to zero: `−2 Σ_obs (y_ij − c) = 0`, so `c = (1/n_obs) Σ_obs y_ij = ȳ_j`. The mean is the least-squares constant predictor of the column — the best you can do at guessing an entry if you refuse to look at any other variable. That's exactly the situation a hole puts me in: I want a number for `y_ij` and I'm choosing to use no covariates. So the no-covariate optimal guess is the mean, and that's what I'll write into the holes. `c_j = ȳ_j`, the mean over the observed entries of column `j`.

So the rule is: `fit` computes, for each column, the mean of its non-`NaN` entries; `transform` replaces each `NaN` in column `j` with that stored mean. Cheap — one pass to compute `d` means, one pass to scatter them. And it satisfies the freeze-and-replay requirement automatically: the means are learned on the training rows, stored, and the *same* stored means fill the test rows. Good. But I promised myself I'd understand the damage before trusting it, so let me actually compute what this does to the column, because "it leaves the mean alone" is only half the story.

Take a column with true variance I'll estimate by `s²_obs`, the sample variance of its `n_obs` observed values: `s²_obs = (1/(n_obs − 1)) Σ_obs (y_ij − ȳ_j)²`. Now I fill the `n − n_obs` holes with `ȳ_j` and recompute the variance over the whole completed column of `n` entries. Here's the thing: every filled entry equals `ȳ_j` exactly, and `ȳ_j` is the mean of the observed part — but is it the mean of the *completed* part? Yes, I just showed the completed mean is still `ȳ_j`. So when I form `Σ_all (y_ij − ȳ_j)²` over all `n` completed entries, each filled entry contributes `(ȳ_j − ȳ_j)² = 0`. The sum of squared deviations doesn't change at all from the observed part: it's still `Σ_obs (y_ij − ȳ_j)² = (n_obs − 1) s²_obs`. But the variance divides that fixed sum by `n − 1` now instead of `n_obs − 1`. So the completed-column variance is

  `s²_completed = (n_obs − 1)/(n − 1) · s²_obs`.

And `n_obs < n`, so that factor is strictly below one. I've *deflated* the variance, by exactly the ratio of degrees of freedom — which, for a decent sample, is essentially the fraction of the column that was observed. Twenty percent missing and the variance comes out at about eighty percent of the truth. That's not a rounding error; that's me stuffing a fifth of the column with a value that has zero deviation, and the variance dutifully reporting that the column is tighter than it is. The mean I preserved; the spread I crushed.

It gets worse when I look across columns. Suppose two columns are genuinely correlated. The observed pairs trace out a tilted cloud. But the entries I fill in one column all sit at that column's mean, `ȳ_j`, *regardless* of what the other column is doing in those rows. So I'm laying down a flat horizontal band of points at height `ȳ_j`, spread across the whole range of the other variable — a band with no tilt at all. Mix that flat band into the tilted cloud and the overall tilt gets diluted: the Pearson correlation is pulled toward zero. The filler, by construction, asserts "in these rows this variable is exactly average and tells you nothing about its partner," which is precisely the no-correlation hypothesis, smeared over a fraction of the data. So mean filling systematically attenuates correlations and covariances toward zero. The more I fill, the flatter everything looks.

So now I'm uneasy. I've got a method that preserves the one statistic I aimed at — the mean — and visibly corrupts two others I care about — the variance and the inter-variable structure. If the goal were to estimate those statistics, this would be a genuinely bad method, and the right verdict would be "don't do this; do something that puts the scatter back." Stochastic filling — predict each hole from the other variables *and* add a random residual matched to the noise — would restore the variance, because now the filled points aren't pinned to a surface. Conditional/regression filling would at least respect the tilt. There's a whole ladder of better-behaved methods above me. And on top of all that, any *single* filling, mine included, commits a deeper sin: it writes one number into the hole and proceeds as if that number were known with certainty, so every standard error computed downstream comes out too small — the analysis never accounts for the fact that the hole could have been many values. By the inferential yardstick I'm at the bottom of the ladder.

But wait — what is the yardstick, actually? Let me re-read my own job description. I'm not being asked to report an unbiased estimate of the column variance or the correlation matrix. The downstream stage is a *predictor*: it gets fit on the completed table and then has to do well on out-of-sample rows. The objective is prediction risk, `E[ℓ(f(X), Y)]`, not fidelity of the data's summary statistics. That's a genuinely different target, and I shouldn't assume the inferential indictment carries over. The predictor is going to be re-fit *on the completed table* — so it sees whatever I did to the columns and can adapt to it. The question isn't "did I reproduce the column's variance," it's "after I fill the holes my way and let a learner fit the filled table, can it still make good predictions." Let me chase that, because it might rescue the method or might condemn it for a different reason.

Suppose I knew the perfect predictor on fully observed data, `f⋆(x) = E[Y | X = x]`. When a coordinate is missing, I fill it with the column mean and apply `f⋆` to the completed row. Is that the right prediction? Let me test it on the smallest nontrivial case I can write down. One feature, `X ~ Uniform(0,1)`, target `Y = X² + ε` with mean-zero noise, and `X` missing completely at random half the time. The honest best prediction when `X` is missing is `E[Y | X missing] = E[X²]`, the average of `X²` over the distribution, which is `∫₀¹ x² dx = 1/3`. Now what does "fill the mean, then apply `f⋆`" give me? The column mean is `E[X] = 1/2`, and `f⋆(x) = x²`, so I predict `(1/2)² = 1/4`. That's `1/4` against the correct `1/3` — wrong, and not by a little. I've hit a wall, and I can see exactly which brick: `f⋆` is nonlinear, and `E[X²] ≠ (E[X])²`. Pushing the average *through* a nonlinear function is not the same as averaging the function. Plugging the mean into a fixed clean-data predictor systematically misfires whenever that predictor is curved. So the naive "impute then apply the pre-trained `f⋆`" is not consistent. If I'd stopped at "fill the mean and reuse my old model," I'd be quietly biased forever.

Don't abandon the method yet — diagnose the wall precisely, because the fix might be free. The failure was that I *froze* the predictor `f⋆`, the one trained as if data were complete, and then fed it a doctored input. But in my actual pipeline I don't freeze anything: I fill the holes *and then fit the learner on the filled table*. So the learner doesn't have to be `x ↦ x²`. It gets to see that, in the rows where `X` was filled, `X` takes the special value `1/2` — every single time, because I always fill the same constant. The filled value `1/2` isn't a typical draw of `X`; it's a point mass that only ever appears in formerly-missing rows. A flexible learner can notice that. It can fit one response curve for the rows where `X` was genuinely observed and a *separate* value for the spike at `X = 1/2` — and at that spike, what should it learn? The average `Y` among the rows sitting there, which are exactly the rows where `X` was missing, whose average target is `E[Y | X missing] = 1/3`. So the learner, re-fit on my filled table, can output `1/3` at the imputed location even though `1/3 ≠ (1/2)²`. The constant I filled doesn't have to be a *plausible value of `X`* — it only has to be a *consistent marker* of "this was missing," distinct enough that the learner can give that region its own prediction. The mean serves perfectly well as that marker; so would any other fixed constant. What rescues me is not that the mean is the right value — it manifestly isn't, `1/4 ≠ 1/3` — but that I impute the *same* constant in train and test and let the learner adapt around it.

Let me make sure I believe this is general and not a fluke of one example. The argument: I fill column one with a fixed `α` wherever it's missing, in both train and test, and then fit a universally consistent learner — one that, given enough data, recovers `E[Y | filled X]` for whatever the filled distribution is. The filled feature is a mixture: with some probability it's the genuine `X` (when observed), and with the rest it's the spike at `α` (when missing). There are three cases to keep straight. If I look at a completed point with first coordinate not equal to `α`, it cannot have come from an imputed missing value, so the learner is just estimating the usual conditional mean on observed rows. If the first coordinate is `α` but the missingness probability there is zero, then the point is an ordinary observed point and nothing special happens. If the first coordinate is `α` and missingness has positive probability, then the mass created by imputation sits exactly on that spike; a flexible learner can estimate the average target for the rows that landed there, which is the optimal prediction to make when that coordinate is absent. The condition I'm leaning on is the clean-regime one from before: missingness at random with a smooth dependence on the observed covariates, so the missing region is stable and the spike carries the right conditional mean. Under that, impute-a-constant-then-learn is consistent. The variance deflation and correlation attenuation I computed are real, and they'd be fatal if I were reporting statistics — but the learner doesn't consume those statistics; it consumes the filled table and re-fits, and the re-fitting is what launders the "wrong" constant into a correct prediction. The inferential sin and the predictive adequacy are simply about different objectives.

This also tells me, sharply, the one thing I must not get wrong operationally: I have to fill train and test with the *same* constant. If I computed the column mean separately on the test set, the spike would land at a different location than the one the learner was trained to recognize, and the whole adaptation argument collapses — the learner would be looking for the imputed point at the training mean and finding the test rows somewhere else. So `fit` learns the means on training data and stores them, and `transform` replays *those stored means* on whatever table it's given, training or test. That's not an implementation nicety; it's what makes the method a consistent predictor. And of course `fit` must not look at the target `Y` at all when computing the means — the means come purely from the observed feature entries, or I'd be leaking the label into the features.

Now let me push on the regime boundary, because I flagged earlier that informative missingness might bite, and I want to know exactly which claim survives. Suppose the fact of being missing itself shifts the response. Concretely, `X ~ Uniform(0,1)`, `M ~ Bernoulli(1/2)` independent of `X`, and `Y = X` when `M = 0` but `Y = 3X` when `M = 1`. If I train a clean-data predictor only on observed rows, it sees `Y = X`, so its regression function is `x ↦ x`. When `X` is missing and I plug in the mean `E[X] = 1/2`, it predicts `1/2`. But the right prediction for a missing row is `E[3X | M = 1] = 3/2`. That is the same averaging mistake in a sharper form: the missingness indicator changed the response law, and a predictor trained without that indicator cannot recover it by receiving a single filled value at test time.

If I instead impute the training rows too and re-fit a very flexible learner, the continuous one-dimensional example gives the spike a chance to carry the missingness pattern: away from `α` I see observed rows, at `α` with positive missingness probability the imputed mass dominates the point, and the learner can assign the spike the missing-row mean. So I should not state the boundary as "any constant filling always fails." The real boundary is representation. When the target depends on the mask and the filled value does not keep the mask distinguishable — because the feature is discrete and the filler collides with a real category, because the learner is too smooth or too rigid to isolate the spike, or because several missingness patterns collapse to the same completed vector — the information lives in `M` and value-only completion has thrown it away. The robust escape hatch is to keep `M`: add a missingness-indicator column so the learner can read "this row was missing" directly. That's a different method, and I note it as the escape hatch; plain mean filling is the small tool for the non-informative regime where a stable constant spike and a re-fit learner are enough.

So where have I landed. The honest constant for a no-covariate guess is the column mean, derived two ways — it's the unique mean-preserving constant and it's the least-squares constant predictor of the column. It deflates the column variance by exactly `(n_obs − 1)/(n − 1)` and pulls correlations toward zero, which damns it as an inferential tool but is irrelevant to a re-fit predictor. Plugging the mean into a frozen nonlinear predictor is biased because `E[g(X)] ≠ g(E[X])`, but imputing the same constant in train and test and *re-fitting* a flexible learner is consistent under non-informative missingness, because the learner treats the imputed spike as its own region and assigns it the right conditional mean. When the mask itself is predictive and a value-only representation does not keep it distinguishable, I need to add the mask rather than pretend the filled number contains that information. That's the complete picture; the method is the least, done cleanly.

Let me write it into the transformer slot. `fit` stores the per-column mean over observed entries — the masked mean over rows; `transform` copies the input and writes each column's stored mean into that column's holes. One guard I owe the same-shape contract: a column that is entirely missing has no observed entries. The library default can drop such a feature, while `keep_empty_features=True` uses `0`; this harness wants a finite table of the same shape, so I use `0.0` as the stored marker for that column. The two stubs from the harness map one to one: the `fit` slot becomes the masked column mean, the `transform` slot becomes the scatter of stored means into the holes.

```python
import numpy as np
from sklearn.base import BaseEstimator, TransformerMixin


class CustomImputer(BaseEstimator, TransformerMixin):
    """Unconditional mean imputation: replace each missing entry of a column
    with the mean of that column's observed (training) entries. The least-
    squares constant predictor of the column; the same stored means are
    replayed on any table so train and test share one imputation map."""

    def __init__(self, random_state=42, max_iter=10):
        self.random_state = random_state
        self.max_iter = max_iter

    def fit(self, X, y=None):
        X = np.asarray(X, dtype=float)
        # c_j = mean over the OBSERVED entries of column j (no target, no test data).
        # argmin_c sum_obs (x_ij - c)^2 ; also the unique mean-preserving constant.
        missing_mask = np.isnan(X)
        mean_masked = np.ma.mean(np.ma.masked_array(X, mask=missing_mask), axis=0)
        self.statistics_ = np.ma.getdata(mean_masked).astype(float)
        # Same-shape contract for all-missing columns: mirror keep_empty_features=True.
        self.statistics_[np.ma.getmaskarray(mean_masked)] = 0.0
        return self

    def transform(self, X):
        X_imputed = np.array(X, dtype=float, copy=True)
        # Write the SAME learned column means into the holes of whatever table
        # this is (train or test): the imputed value is a fixed per-column spike
        # the downstream learner can re-fit around.
        nan_mask = np.isnan(X_imputed)
        col_idx = np.where(nan_mask)[1]                 # column of each missing cell
        X_imputed[nan_mask] = np.take(self.statistics_, col_idx)
        return X_imputed

    def fit_transform(self, X, y=None):
        return self.fit(X, y).transform(X)
```

The causal chain, end to end: the table has holes that downstream tools won't accept, and discarding incomplete rows collapses to almost nothing as the width grows and still leaves test rows unscorable, so I must fill. Working in the regime where missingness carries no signal beyond the observed data, the best no-covariate guess for a hole is the column's observed mean — it minimizes squared error among constants and is the only constant that leaves the column mean intact. That constant deflates the column variance by `(n_obs − 1)/(n − 1)` and attenuates correlations, which would be disqualifying if I were estimating statistics, but the downstream stage is a re-fit predictor, not a statistic. Plugging the mean into a frozen nonlinear predictor is biased because averaging doesn't commute with a curved function, yet imputing one fixed constant in both train and test and re-fitting a flexible learner is consistent, because the learner treats the imputed value as a distinct region and learns the right conditional mean there — the constant only has to be a stable marker, not a plausible value. The same-constant-train-and-test requirement is what makes it a well-defined predictor, so `fit` stores the observed-column means and `transform` replays exactly those, never peeking at the target. When the missingness mask itself is predictive and the completed values do not keep it readable, the mask has to be added as a separate feature; this small method deliberately stops before that step. It lands as a few lines: one masked column mean in `fit`, one scatter in `transform`, and one `keep_empty_features=True`-style guard for columns with no observations.
