Key passage (Rabin's account of the genesis of the probabilistic primality test):

In 1975, having finished his term as Rector of the Hebrew University of
Jerusalem, Rabin came to MIT as a visiting professor. Gary Miller was at MIT
and had a polynomial-time test for primality that was based on the *extended
(generalized) Riemann hypothesis*. With the idea of using probability and
allowing the possibility of error, Rabin took Miller's test and turned it into
what is now called a randomized algorithm — today the most efficient test for
primality. The randomized algorithm produces an answer where, depending on how
many times you run the probabilistic procedure, you have a very small
probability of error.

Cross-reference (ACM A.M. Turing Award profile, https://amturing.acm.org/award_winners/rabin_9681074.cfm):

"Miller had earlier developed a primality test that was based on the unproven
Riemann hypothesis. That bothered Rabin, because if the Riemann hypothesis was
eventually shown to be false it would bring into question any methods based on
it. Michael had earlier worked on probabilistic automata, theoretical machines
for which a random number is used to determine which transition to take from
each state. While not deterministic in the sense that it always provides a
provable result, if run a number of times the chance of it being incorrect can
be made vanishingly small. Rabin used this concept to develop a primality
testing algorithm called the Miller-Rabin test."
