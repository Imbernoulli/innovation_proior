A diffusion model corrupts a clean image $x$ into a noisy latent $z_t = \alpha_t x + \sigma_t \varepsilon$ along a variance-preserving schedule with $\alpha_t^2 + \sigma_t^2 = 1$, and trains a single network to undo the corruption under a squared-error loss; a sampler then runs the trained denoiser backward from noise to data. The sampler never consumes the raw network output — at every step it consumes a clean-image estimate $\hat x$, which is a fixed linear function of the output, of $z_t$, and of the schedule. The free design choice at the center of the whole construction is what quantity the network should predict, and I had been treating it as a cosmetic relabeling because noise, clean image, and latent are all linearly interconvertible. At infinite precision and infinite sampling steps that is true, but few-step samples degrade in a way that depends on exactly this choice, so the relabeling is not free and the reason has to be found.

The standard choice is to predict the added noise $\hat\varepsilon_\theta(z_t)$ and recover the clean image by inverting the forward equation, $\hat x = (z_t - \sigma_t \hat\varepsilon_\theta)/\alpha_t$. There is the divide by $\alpha_t$. For most of the schedule $\alpha_t$ is order one and this is harmless, but the schedule's whole purpose is to drive $\alpha_t \to 0$ at the noisy end. A small output error $\delta$, $\hat\varepsilon = \varepsilon + \delta$, induces a clean-image error $-(\sigma_t/\alpha_t)\,\delta$, and the factor $\sigma_t/\alpha_t \to \infty$ as $\alpha_t \to 0$, $\sigma_t \to 1$. At the literal limit $\alpha_t = 0$, $\sigma_t = 1$, the latent $z_t = \varepsilon$ is pure noise carrying zero information about $x$; "predict the noise" becomes "given pure noise, name the noise," a degenerate task, and the noise-space loss $\|\varepsilon - \hat\varepsilon\|^2 = (\alpha_t^2/\sigma_t^2)\|x - \hat x\|^2$ has weight exactly zero there — the loss stops caring about clean-image error precisely where I most need it to. With hundreds of sampling steps this pathology is masked: each step clips the latent back into range and later steps correct early missteps. The moment the sampler is cut to a few steps — or one step starting from pure noise — there is no later step to clean up, and the $1/\alpha_t$ amplification lands directly in the sample. The obvious alternative, predicting the clean image directly with loss $\|x - \hat x\|^2$, cures the noisy end (the target is exactly what the sampler wants, no division) but breaks symmetrically at the clean end: there $z_t \approx x$, so the network merely copies its input and wastes capacity, and the implied noise $\hat\varepsilon = (z_t - \alpha_t \hat x)/\sigma_t$ divides by $\sigma_t \to 0$. The two natural choices are well-conditioned at opposite ends and fail at opposite ends — that opposition is not a coincidence to shrug at; it is the shape of a solution.

I propose v-prediction: train the network to output the velocity $v = \alpha_t \varepsilon - \sigma_t x$, the tangent to the noising trajectory viewed as rotation on a circle. The starting point is the constraint $\alpha_t^2 + \sigma_t^2 = 1$, which says $(\alpha_t, \sigma_t)$ is a point on the unit circle. Introduce the angle $\phi_t = \arctan(\sigma_t/\alpha_t)$, so $\alpha_t = \cos\phi_t$, $\sigma_t = \sin\phi_t$, and the latent becomes $z_\phi = \cos\phi\, x + \sin\phi\, \varepsilon$. As $\phi$ runs from $0$ to $\pi/2$, $z_\phi$ rotates from pure signal $x$ to pure noise $\varepsilon$: the entire forward process is rotation in the $(x, \varepsilon)$ plane. Noise prediction reads off the fixed $\varepsilon$ axis and clean-image prediction off the fixed $x$ axis, and recovering $x$ by projecting onto a fixed axis is exactly what forces a division by $\cos\phi$ or $\sin\phi$, one of which vanishes. Fixed axes are the disease. The natural direction that moves *with* $z_\phi$ is its velocity along the arc,
$$v = \frac{d z_\phi}{d\phi} = -\sin\phi\, x + \cos\phi\, \varepsilon = \alpha_t \varepsilon - \sigma_t x.$$
What makes $v$ special is that $\{z_\phi, v\}$ is an orthonormal rotation of $\{x, \varepsilon\}$,
$$\begin{pmatrix} z_\phi \\ v \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \cos\phi & \sin\phi \\ -\sin\phi & \cos\phi \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} x \\ \varepsilon \end{pmatrix}.$$
A rotation is orthogonal, so its inverse is its transpose — no determinant, no division, the inverse coefficients are again $\cos$ and $\sin$, all bounded in $[-1, 1]$. Inverting gives
$$x = \cos\phi\, z_\phi - \sin\phi\, v = \alpha_t z_t - \sigma_t v, \qquad \varepsilon = \sin\phi\, z_\phi + \cos\phi\, v = \sigma_t z_t + \alpha_t v.$$
So if the network predicts $v$, the clean-image estimate the sampler needs is $\hat x = \alpha_t z_t - \sigma_t \hat v$, with both coefficients in $[0,1]$: an error $\delta$ in $\hat v$ induces an error $-\sigma_t \delta$ in $\hat x$, bounded by $\delta$ everywhere. The $\sigma_t/\alpha_t \to \infty$ amplification is gone, and it fell straight out of choosing the moving tangent direction over a fixed axis.

The smooth handoff I wanted is automatic from the definition. At the clean end $\alpha_t \to 1$, $\sigma_t \to 0$, so $v \to \varepsilon$: predicting $v$ is essentially predicting the noise, the regime where noise prediction was good. At the noisy end $\alpha_t \to 0$, $\sigma_t \to 1$, so $v \to -x$: predicting $v$ is essentially predicting minus the clean image, the regime where clean-image prediction was good. The two parameterizations with cliffs are recovered as the two *limits* of this one, each used where it belongs, with a continuous crossover and no division by zero at either end. The construction is also coherent with the sampler I actually run. A deterministic DDIM step from level $t$ to a lower level $s$ rebuilds the latent as $z_s = \alpha_s \hat x + \sigma_s \hat\varepsilon$; substituting the rotation expressions for $\hat x$ and $\hat\varepsilon$ and collecting terms with the angle-difference identities $\cos(A-B) = \cos A \cos B + \sin A \sin B$ and $\sin(A-B) = \sin A \cos B - \cos A \sin B$ gives
$$z_s = \cos(\phi_s - \phi_t)\, z_t + \sin(\phi_s - \phi_t)\, \hat v = \cos\delta\, z_t - \sin\delta\, \hat v, \qquad \delta = \phi_t - \phi_s > 0.$$
A DDIM step is a pure rotation by the angle $\delta$ in the $(z, \hat v)$ plane, and its coefficients depend only on the angular step size, not on where on the circle I am — not on the SNR. With noise prediction the effective step coefficients are tangled up with $\alpha_t, \sigma_t$ and behave very differently across noise levels; here every step is the same kind of rotation. That SNR-independence is the deep reason $v$ stays stable for large, few steps: the angular step adds no schedule-endpoint amplification on top of the model error.

The loss weighting cannot simply be inherited, since the standard signal-to-noise weighting was part of the problem. The simplest choice is plain mean-squared error on $v$, and it is worth working out what clean-image weighting that secretly is. Using $\varepsilon - \hat\varepsilon = -(\alpha_t/\sigma_t)(x - \hat x)$,
$$v - \hat v = \alpha_t(\varepsilon - \hat\varepsilon) - \sigma_t(x - \hat x) = -\Big[\tfrac{\alpha_t^2}{\sigma_t} + \sigma_t\Big](x - \hat x) = -\frac{\alpha_t^2 + \sigma_t^2}{\sigma_t}(x - \hat x) = -\frac{1}{\sigma_t}(x - \hat x),$$
so that
$$\|v - \hat v\|^2 = \frac{1}{\sigma_t^2}\|x - \hat x\|^2 = \Big(1 + \frac{\alpha_t^2}{\sigma_t^2}\Big)\|x - \hat x\|^2.$$
Plain MSE on $v$ is therefore a clean-image loss with weight $w(\lambda_t) = 1 + \exp(\lambda_t)$ — "SNR + 1." At high SNR the $+1$ is negligible and this agrees with the proven noise-prediction weight $\exp(\lambda_t)$, so the good high-SNR emphasis is kept; at SNR zero, where plain SNR gives weight zero and abandons the end the few-step sampler depends on, SNR + 1 floors the weight at one. The $+1$ is exactly the floor that keeps the noisy end contributing to the gradient. Finally, the recovery is the exact algebraic inverse of the target, not an approximation: plugging the true $v = \alpha_t \varepsilon - \sigma_t x$ into $\hat x = \alpha_t z_t - \sigma_t v$ gives $\alpha_t(\alpha_t x + \sigma_t \varepsilon) - \sigma_t(\alpha_t \varepsilon - \sigma_t x) = (\alpha_t^2 + \sigma_t^2)x = x$, so training target and sampling recovery are consistent. In the schedule tensors at hand, $\texttt{sqrt\_alpha} = \sqrt{\overline\alpha} = \alpha_t$ and $\texttt{sqrt\_one\_minus\_alpha} = \sqrt{1 - \overline\alpha} = \sigma_t$, the whole method is one definition and its inverse dropped into the existing noising-plus-MSE training step and the existing DDIM step, leaving the backbone, cosine schedule, optimizer and sampler untouched.

```python
import torch


def compute_training_target(x_0, noise, timesteps, schedule):
    # v = alpha_t * eps - sigma_t * x_0
    sqrt_alpha = schedule["sqrt_alpha"][timesteps].view(-1, 1, 1, 1)
    sqrt_one_minus_alpha = schedule["sqrt_one_minus_alpha"][timesteps].view(-1, 1, 1, 1)
    return sqrt_alpha * noise - sqrt_one_minus_alpha * x_0


def predict_x0(model_output, x_t, timesteps, schedule):
    # x_0 = alpha_t * x_t - sigma_t * v   (inverse rotation; no division by alpha_t)
    sqrt_alpha = schedule["sqrt_alpha"][timesteps].view(-1, 1, 1, 1)
    sqrt_one_minus_alpha = schedule["sqrt_one_minus_alpha"][timesteps].view(-1, 1, 1, 1)
    return sqrt_alpha * x_t - sqrt_one_minus_alpha * model_output
```
