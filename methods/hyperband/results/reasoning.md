Let me start from what actually hurts. I have a search space `X` of hyperparameter configurations and a fixed compute budget `B`, and I want the configuration with the lowest validation error. The one operation I can do is pick a configuration, train it for some amount of resource — iterations, or a data subsample, or a number of features — and read off a validation loss. That operation is the entire cost; everything I do is measured in units of it. The objective from configuration to error is a black box: non-convex, high-dimensional, I don't know its smoothness, and I only see it through noisy, expensive samples. So the game is to spend `B` so that the configuration I return is as close to the best in `X` as I can get.

What do I do today? The honest baseline is random search: sample `n` configurations i.i.d. from the space, train each one all the way to the maximum resource `R`, take the best. I actually respect this more than it first looks. Bergstra and Bengio showed that across datasets only a few hyperparameters matter and which few changes by dataset, so random sampling spreads its trials over the axes that matter far better than a grid, which burns trials on combinations of axes that don't. And it converges to the optimum asymptotically by a plain covering argument, no smoothness assumptions needed. So it's a clean foundation. But here's the thing that bothers me: every single configuration is trained to the full `R` before I look at it. With a total budget `B`, that means I see only `B/R` configurations, full stop. And I *know* — I can see it in the loss curves of real models — that most randomly drawn configurations declare themselves hopeless after a tiny fraction of `R`. A learning rate that's an order of magnitude too high diverges in the first few epochs. Random search pays the full price `R` for that information when a sliver would have sufficed. The waste is structural.

The Bayesian-optimization crowd — TPE with its Parzen densities, SMAC with random forests, Spearmint with a GP — attacks a different half of the problem. They build a model `p(y|x)` of validation performance and use it to pick the next configuration cleverly. Adaptive *selection*. And empirically they beat random search on low-dimensional benchmarks. But they're fighting an uphill battle: fitting and then optimizing a high-dimensional, non-convex, unknown-smoothness, noisy surface is genuinely hard, and in high dimensions they slide back toward random search; the GP posterior costs `O(n^3)` to fit. More to the point for me: they're still black boxes on the *evaluation* side. Each configuration they pick gets trained to completion. So they inherit exactly the cost I'm trying to kill — every configuration pays full price. They optimize *which* configuration; I'm increasingly convinced the bigger lever is *how much* I spend on each one before I throw it out.

So let me commit to the evaluation side and think about adaptive resource allocation. The cleanest way to reason about it is to borrow the bandit framing: a configuration is an *arm*, training it one more unit is a *pull*, and pulling arm `i` a total of `j` times gives me a loss `ell_{i,j}`. The standing fact about iteratively trained models is that `ell_{i,j}` converges to a terminal value `nu_i = lim_{j->inf} ell_{i,j}` as `j` grows. I want the arm with the smallest `nu_i`. The trouble is that I have no idea how *fast* each curve converges, the curves can be non-monotone and jagged, and a cheap evaluation `ell_{i,j}` is only a rough proxy for the thing I actually care about, `nu_i`. This is best-arm identification, but in a *non-stochastic* setting — the loss sequence isn't i.i.d. noise around a mean, it's an arbitrary sequence converging to a limit at an unknown rate. The classic stochastic best-arm algorithms lean on concentration inequalities for i.i.d. samples, which I can't invoke here. I need something that works against an arbitrary converging sequence.

Let me make the convergence behavior precise so I can actually reason. Define `gamma(j)` to be the smallest non-increasing function such that `sup_i |ell_{i,j} - nu_i| <= gamma(j)` for all `j`. It exists because the limits exist, and it bounds how far any partially trained loss can be from its terminal value after `j` units. Think of it as an *envelope*: at resource `j`, every curve is pinned within `gamma(j)` of where it will end up. Now suppose I have the best arm with terminal value `nu_1` and another arm with `nu_i > nu_1`. When can I tell them apart from their partial losses? Their envelopes are `[nu_1 - gamma(j), nu_1 + gamma(j)]` and `[nu_i - gamma(j), nu_i + gamma(j)]`. These stop overlapping exactly when `2 gamma(j) <= nu_i - nu_1`, i.e. `gamma(j) <= (nu_i - nu_1)/2`. Let me name that threshold resource `tau_i := gamma^{-1}((nu_i - nu_1)/2)`, where `gamma^{-1}(y) = min{j : gamma(j) <= y}`. Below `tau_i` the two arms might still cross in their empirical losses; at or above `tau_i` the ordering of their *partial* losses is guaranteed to match the ordering of their terminal losses. So `tau_i` is the resource I need to separate arm `i` from the best — small when curves converge fast or the gap `nu_i - nu_1` is large, large when curves crawl or the gap is tiny.

There's a second unknown lurking, separate from `gamma`. Even if I could separate arms perfectly, I still need a *good* arm to be among the ones I drew. If terminal values are i.i.d. with CDF `F` and infimum `nu_*`, then the chance that all `n` draws miss the near-optimal region by at least `Delta` is `(1 - F(nu_* + Delta))^n ≈ exp(-n F(nu_* + Delta))`, so `E[min_{i<=n} nu_i - nu_*] ≈ F^{-1}(1/n) - nu_*`. To have a hope of a near-optimal arm in my pool I need `n` large enough that good arms are sampled at all, and how large depends on how rare good arms are under `F`. So two things are unknown and both bite: `gamma` (how fast curves converge) and `F` (how rare good arms are).

Now, the naive adaptive baseline. Suppose I fix `n` arms and just spread the budget uniformly: give each arm `B/n`, look at the loss, pick the smallest. When does that work? I'd need `B/n >= tau_i` for *every* sub-optimal arm I want to rule out, because uniform allocation means I can't tell them apart any sooner than the hardest one allows. The hardest sub-optimal arm to separate is the one closest to the best, `nu_2`, needing `tau_2 = gamma^{-1}((nu_2 - nu_1)/2)`. So uniform needs, in the worst case, `B >= n · gamma^{-1}((nu_2 - nu_1)/2)` — it pays the *hardest* arm's separation cost on *every single arm*. That's a lot of waste: the arm with a learning rate ten times too high needed almost nothing to rule out, but uniform allocation gave it the same `B/n` as everyone else.

The fix is sequential elimination, and I think Karnin, Koren and Somekh had the right shape of it for the stochastic fixed-budget setting: proceed in rounds; within a round pull every surviving arm the same number of times; at the end of the round throw out a fraction of the worst; repeat until one remains. Jamieson and Talwalkar showed this same procedure works in the non-stochastic setting I'm in. Let me make it concrete with halving: given budget `B` and `n` arms, set `S_0 = [n]`. For round `k = 0, 1, ..., ceil(log2 n) - 1`, pull each arm in `S_k` for `r_k = floor( B / (|S_k| ceil(log2 n)) )` times, then keep the best `floor(|S_k|/2)`. The arms halve each round and there are `ceil(log2 n)` rounds, so I drain down to one survivor.

Why is this so much better than uniform? Because the resource per arm *escalates*. The bad arms get cut after round 0 having received only `r_0`, a tiny slice — exactly what they deserve, since they were easy to rule out. The resource concentrates on the arms that survive, which are the ones genuinely competitive with the best and so the ones that *needed* more resource to separate. Let me first sanity-check that this never overspends the budget. The total pulls are
`sum_{k=0}^{ceil(log2 n)-1} |S_k| · floor( B / (|S_k| ceil(log2 n)) ) <= sum_k B/ceil(log2 n) = B`.
Each round costs at most `B/ceil(log2 n)`, and there are `ceil(log2 n)` rounds, so the total is at most `B`. Good — the budget is split evenly across rounds, which is the same as saying each round gets `B/ceil(log2 n)` and within a round the arms share it equally.

The budget threshold is the thing that matters, so I should derive it rather than wave at it. Order the arms so `nu_1 <= nu_2 <= ... <= nu_n`. The separation statement I need is this: for any arm `i` and any resource `t >= tau_i`,
`ell_{i,t} - ell_{1,t} = (ell_{i,t} - nu_i) - (ell_{1,t} - nu_1) + (nu_i - nu_1) >= -gamma(t) - gamma(t) + (nu_i - nu_1) = -2 gamma(t) + (nu_i - nu_1) >= -2 · (nu_i - nu_1)/2 + (nu_i - nu_1) = 0`,
using `gamma(t) <= (nu_i - nu_1)/2` for `t >= tau_i`. So `t >= tau_i` forces `ell_{i,t} >= ell_{1,t}`: at any resource past `tau_i`, arm `i` *empirically* looks no better than arm 1, so it will be eliminated no later than arm 1. Note `tau_i` is non-increasing in `i` (bigger gap, smaller `tau`), since `nu_i` is sorted and `gamma^{-1}` is non-increasing.

Now I trace arm 1 through the rounds. Suppose `1 in S_k` but `1 not in S_{k+1}` — the best arm got eliminated at round `k`. Halving keeps the better `floor(|S_k|/2)`, so for arm 1 to be cut, at least `floor(|S_k|/2)` other arms in `S_k` must have *beaten* it empirically at resource `r_k`, i.e. had `ell_{i,r_k} < ell_{1,r_k}`. But I just showed `t >= tau_i => ell_{i,t} >= ell_{1,t}`; contrapositively `ell_{i,r_k} < ell_{1,r_k} => r_k < tau_i`. So at least `floor(|S_k|/2)` arms have `r_k < tau_i`. Since `tau_i` is non-increasing in `i`, the arms with the *largest* `tau_i` are the ones with the *smallest* index (closest to the best), so the first `floor(|S_k|/2)` indices `i = 2, ..., floor(|S_k|/2)+1` are the ones most likely to satisfy `r_k < tau_i`. Hence `floor(|S_k|/2)` arms with `r_k < tau_i` forces `r_k < tau_{floor(|S_k|/2)+1}`. Read the contrapositive of the whole chain: if `1 in S_k` and `r_k >= tau_{floor(|S_k|/2)+1}`, then `1 in S_{k+1}`. So arm 1 survives round `k` as long as the resource that round clears the separation threshold of the arm sitting at position `floor(|S_k|/2)+1`.

So what do I need `r_k` to be? I need `r_k >= tau_{floor(|S_k|/2)+1} = gamma^{-1}((nu_{floor(|S_k|/2)+1} - nu_1)/2)`. From the algorithm, `r_k >= B/(|S_k| ceil(log2 n)) - 1`. Let me see what `B` makes this hold for every round. Try
`z_SH = 2 ceil(log2 n) · max_{i=2,...,n} i · (1 + gamma^{-1}((nu_i - nu_1)/2))`
and assume `B > z_SH`. Then for round `k`, with `i = floor(|S_k|/2)+1`,
`r_k >= B/(|S_k| ceil(log2 n)) - 1 > (2/|S_k|) · max_i i (1 + gamma^{-1}((nu_i-nu_1)/2)) - 1`.
Plug in `i = floor(|S_k|/2)+1 >= |S_k|/2`, so `(2/|S_k|) · (floor(|S_k|/2)+1) >= (2/|S_k|)·(|S_k|/2) = 1`; the factor in front of `(1 + gamma^{-1}(...))` is at least 1. So `r_k >= (1 + gamma^{-1}((nu_{floor(|S_k|/2)+1}-nu_1)/2)) - 1 = gamma^{-1}((nu_{floor(|S_k|/2)+1}-nu_1)/2) = tau_{floor(|S_k|/2)+1}`. Exactly the threshold I needed. The `+1` inside `z_SH` is there precisely to absorb the `-1` from the floor in `r_k`, and the leading `2 ceil(log2 n)` covers the `1/|S_k|` shrinkage and the per-round budget split. So with `B > z_SH`, arm 1 clears the threshold every round and is never eliminated — halving returns the best arm.

I should be honest about an `epsilon` slack, because in practice I won't separate arms whose terminal gaps are below my resolution, and that's fine — I only need to return an arm within `epsilon/2` of the best. Replace `tau_i` by `gamma^{-1}(max{epsilon/4, (nu_i - nu_1)/2})`: I demand separation only down to gaps of order `epsilon`. The argument splits into cases. If arm 1 is still present and `(nu_{floor(|S_k|/2)+1} - nu_1)/2 >= epsilon/4`, the `max` picks the gap term, `r_k >= tau_{floor(|S_k|/2)+1}`, and arm 1 survives as before. If arm 1 is still present and `(nu_{floor(|S_k|/2)+1} - nu_1)/2 < epsilon/4`, the `max` picks `epsilon/4` and arm 1 *might* be cut — but then look at who's left: let `p = min{i : (nu_i - nu_1)/2 >= epsilon/4}`. Since `r_k >= gamma^{-1}(epsilon/4) >= gamma^{-1}((nu_i-nu_1)/2) = tau_i` for all `i >= p`, every arm `i >= p` looks empirically no better than arm 1, so it is eliminated no later than arm 1. Thus if arm 1 is cut in this case, all the survivors have index `< p`, meaning `nu_i < nu_1 + epsilon/2` for every survivor. And if arm 1 is already absent at some later round, then there was an earlier first round where it moved from present to absent; that earlier transition could only have been the second case, because the first case would have kept it, so from that point onward every survivor already has terminal loss below `nu_1 + epsilon/2`. In all three cases the final survivor is either arm 1 itself or an arm whose terminal value is within `epsilon/2` of the best. So
`z_SH = 2 ceil(log2 n) max_{i>=2} i (1 + gamma^{-1}(max{epsilon/4, (nu_i-nu_1)/2}))`
and `B > z_SH` guarantees `nu_hat - nu_1 <= epsilon/2`. A looser but more readable form: since `max_i i · g_i <= sum_i g_i` for non-negative terms,
`z_SH <= 2 ceil(log2 n) ( n + sum_{i} gamma^{-1}(max{epsilon/4, (nu_i-nu_1)/2}) )`.

Now stare at that `z_SH` next to uniform's `n · gamma^{-1}((nu_2-nu_1)/2)`. Uniform pays `n` copies of the *hardest* arm's separation cost (the `max` over arms, since uniform can't stop early). Halving pays, up to a `log` factor, a *sum* of each arm's *own* separation cost — and most arms have small `gamma^{-1}` because they were easy to rule out. The difference between paying `n × max` and paying `sum` is the whole win, and it can be enormous when most arms are easy and only a few are close to the best.

Let me make the gap quantitative with a parameterization, just to see the exponents, even though I won't actually know the parameters. Say `gamma(j) ≈ (1/j)^{1/alpha}` so `gamma^{-1}(y) ≈ y^{-alpha}` — large `alpha` means slow convergence — and `F(x) ≈ (x - nu_*)^beta` for `x >= nu_*` — large `beta` means good arms are rare. To get within `Delta` of optimal I need `n ≈ Delta^{-beta} log(1/delta)` arms so a `Delta`-good arm is sampled. Then uniform allocation needs budget `B ≈ Delta^{-(alpha+beta)} log(1/delta)`: the `Delta^{-beta}` arms times the `Delta^{-alpha}` resource to separate them. Halving, plugging the parameterization into the sum, needs `B ≈ polylog · Delta^{-max{alpha,beta}} log(1/delta)`. The exponent drops from `alpha + beta` to `max{alpha, beta}` — a polynomial saving in `1/Delta` whose exponent is the smaller of `alpha` and `beta`. That's the payoff for stopping early.

But here's the wall, and it's the wall that kills halving as a standalone method. To run it I have to fix `n` up front. For a fixed total budget `B`, the average resource per arm is `B/n`, and the right `n` depends entirely on `gamma` and `F`, which I cannot see. If curves separate slowly or candidates have very similar terminal losses, I want *small* `n` — few arms, lots of resource each, conservative. If good arms are rare, or if bad arms reveal themselves fast, I want *large* `n` — many arms, little resource each, aggressive early stopping. Look at the parameterized budget: halving is efficient at `Delta^{-max{alpha,beta}}`, but to actually hit accuracy `Delta` I have to set `n ≈ Delta^{-beta}` and `B` to match, and both depend on the unknown `alpha, beta`. Set `n` too small and I never sample a good arm; set it too large and I starve each arm of the resource it needs to be told apart, and a good arm gets eliminated early because at a tiny resource its partial loss is still noise. There's no `n` that's safe across unknown `gamma` and `F`. This is the "`n` versus `B/n`" problem, and it's not a tuning nuisance — it's the same unknown-rate obstacle that the whole non-stochastic framing was supposed to handle, resurfacing as a single scalar I'm forced to guess.

So how do I pick `n` without knowing `gamma` or `F`? My first instinct is to *learn* `n` — estimate the convergence rate from the early loss curves, or fit `F` from the arms I've drawn, and set `n` accordingly. But that's precisely the parametric-convergence-model trap I rejected when I dismissed the learning-curve-extrapolation hybrids: those assumptions are hard to verify and fail badly when wrong, and the whole point of the envelope framing was to make *no* assumption on the shape of `gamma`. If I now sneak a model of `gamma` back in to choose `n`, I've reintroduced the fragility through the back door. Estimating `F` is just as bad: it's the distribution of terminal values, and I only ever see partial losses. Dead end.

Let me back up. I don't need to *find* the optimal `n`; I need to not be badly hurt by *not* finding it. And I already used a trick of exactly this flavor on `B` itself: when I didn't know the right budget, I could double `B` — `B, 2B, 4B, ...` — and pay at most twice the budget I'd have paid if I'd known the right one, because the geometric series of all the smaller attempts sums to less than the last attempt. A doubling trick turns "I don't know the right value" into "I lose only a constant factor." The unknown here, though, isn't a budget on a line; it's a *tradeoff* between two coupled quantities, `n` and `B/n`. So what I want is the same idea but in two dimensions: a hedge across the whole spectrum of `n`-versus-`B/n` tradeoffs, so that whichever one is right, I've tried something close to it and lost only a small factor.

That reframes everything. Stop trying to choose `n`. Instead, fix the budget per attempt and run halving for *several* values of `n`, spaced out geometrically so a handful of them cover the whole useful range, and just hedge across all of them. The most aggressive value of `n` starts with a huge pool trained at tiny resource (bets that bad arms die fast); the least aggressive value of `n` is a small pool trained to full resource — which is exactly plain random search, my safe fallback. Everything in between interpolates. If the problem rewards aggression, an aggressive bracket finds the answer; if it punishes aggression, the conservative end protects me. I never had to know which.

Now the bookkeeping, and it has to be done carefully so each value of `n` consumes roughly the same budget — otherwise the hedge is biased. Fix two inputs: `R`, the maximum resource any one configuration can get, and `eta >= 2`, the factor by which I cut the pool each round (and, correspondingly, multiply the resource each round). Index the brackets by `s`, where `s` is how many rounds of elimination the bracket will perform. The most aggressive bracket has the most rounds. How many distinct brackets are there? A bracket that does `s` rounds, each multiplying resource by `eta`, starting from a minimum resource `r` and ending at `R`, has `r · eta^s = R`, so `r = R eta^{-s}`. For `r >= 1` I need `s <= log_eta R`, so `s` ranges over `0, 1, ..., s_max` with `s_max = floor(log_eta R)`. That's `s_max + 1` brackets — and crucially, that count is *logarithmic* in `R`, so hedging across all of them is cheap.

For bracket `s`, the minimum (starting) resource is `r = R eta^{-s}` and I want it to do `s+1` elimination rounds (rounds `i = 0, ..., s`), where round `i` runs `n_i = floor(n eta^{-i})` configurations at resource `r_i = r eta^i`, then keeps the top `floor(n_i / eta)` for the next round. The pool shrinks by `eta` each round and the resource grows by `eta` each round, so the survivor in round `s` is trained at `r eta^s = R`, exactly the max. Good — every bracket ends with at least one configuration trained to the full `R`. Now I need to choose the starting pool size `n` so that bracket `s` spends about a fixed budget `B` per bracket. Per round `i`, the cost is `n_i · r_i ≈ n eta^{-i} · r eta^i = n r`, independent of `i`. There are `s+1` rounds, so the bracket costs about `(s+1) n r = (s+1) n R eta^{-s}`. Setting that equal to `B` gives `n = (B/R) eta^s / (s+1)`, and rounding up, `n = ceil( (B/R) · eta^s / (s+1) )`. There's the formula, and now I can read off what each piece does: the `eta^s` makes the aggressive (large `s`) brackets start with exponentially many cheap configurations; the `1/(s+1)` divides by the number of rounds so that brackets with more rounds don't overspend; the `B/R` sets the overall scale. Pick `B = (s_max + 1) R` so one full sweep over all brackets costs `(s_max + 1) B`, a fixed, known amount.

Let me trace it concretely to make sure the brackets are what I think. Take `R = 81`, `eta = 3`, so `s_max = floor(log_3 81) = 4`, and five brackets. Bracket `s = 4`: `r = 81/81 = 1`, `n = ceil((B/81) · 81/5) = ceil(B/5)`, with `B = 5·81 = 405`, so `n = 81`. It runs 81 configs at 1 unit, keeps 27 at 3, keeps 9 at 9, keeps 3 at 27, keeps 1 at 81. Maximal exploration: 81 configurations seen, but most killed after a single unit of resource. Bracket `s = 0`: `r = 81`, `n = ceil((B/81)·1/1) = 5`, runs 5 configs at 81 units each, no elimination — that's pure random search over 5 configurations trained to completion. My safe fallback, exactly as designed. The middle brackets `s = 3, 2, 1` interpolate the aggression. Each uses about `B = 405` units; the whole sweep uses `5 · 405`.

Why `eta = 3`? It's a knob with a clean meaning: each round keeps `1/eta` of the pool, so a bracket with `n` configurations does `floor(log_eta n) + 1` rounds. Larger `eta` means more aggressive cuts and fewer rounds; smaller `eta` means gentler cuts and more rounds. If I optimize the constant in the bracket-budget overhead — the factor I pay for hedging instead of knowing the right `n` — it comes out minimized near `eta = e ≈ 2.718`, so rounding to 3 (or 4) is the natural choice, and the results aren't sensitive to it anyway. And `eta` also sets the number of brackets through `s_max = floor(log_eta R)`; I'd pick it to give a handful — around five — distinct tradeoffs, which is plenty to cover the `n`-versus-`B/n` spectrum without excessive overhead.

Now I should check that hedging across brackets doesn't cost me more than the doubling intuition promised. The claim is that this two-dimensional hedge loses only a `log` factor over running halving with the *best* `n` for the problem. Heuristically: for a fixed budget I non-adaptively try a geometric grid of `n` values, and the grid is fine enough that one of them is within a constant factor of the unknown optimal `n`; since there are only `s_max + 1 = O(log R)` brackets and I run all of them, the overhead of trying the others is at most an `O(log)` factor on the budget. Then, if I also want to grow the budget over time (the infinite-horizon view), I double `B` so the cumulative budget to reach the necessary `B` is at most `2B`. Two doubling tricks stacked — one over `n`, one over `B`. Pushing this through the parameterized model, the returned arm satisfies `nu_hat - nu_* <= c (polylog(T) / T)^{1/max{alpha,beta}}` after total budget `T`, where the exponent `1/max{alpha,beta}` is exactly halving-with-known-`n`'s rate and the only price is the polylog. And in the stochastic special case (`alpha = 2`) this matches the known infinite-armed and finite-`K`-armed best-arm lower bounds up to log factors — so the hedge is essentially optimal, not just convenient. The thing I was most worried about, the overhead of not knowing `n`, is provably just a log factor.

One more design point I want to be deliberate about: where do the configurations in each bracket come from? The theory only needs that they're drawn i.i.d. from some stationary distribution `F` over the space — it never uses any structure of the configurations, only their loss sequences. So the assumption-free default is uniform sampling over the hyperparameter ranges, which immediately gives the covering/consistency guarantee. A smarter prior (or a model-based sampler) would only help, but it's strictly optional and orthogonal — the resource-allocation skeleton stands on its own with plain random sampling, which keeps the method free of the very convergence-model assumptions I rejected.

And the finite- versus infinite-horizon choice. In the infinite-horizon view I'd grow `B` over time by doubling and let the number of brackets grow — natural for the theory and for the case where I don't know `R`. But in practice I almost always *do* know a sensible `R` (max epochs, full dataset size, memory-limited feature count), and I'd rather have a fixed, predictable cost per sweep than an exponentially growing time between completed configurations. So the practical form fixes the number of brackets at `s_max + 1 = floor(log_eta R) + 1` and sets `B = (s_max + 1) R` per outer loop, repeating the whole sweep as long as I have budget. Constant time between full evaluations, same hedge.

Let me assemble the whole thing as the algorithm I'd actually run, filling the empty allocation slot in the search harness. Two nested loops: an outer loop over brackets `s` from most to least aggressive, and an inner halving loop over rounds `i`. The inner loop is the successive-elimination subroutine; the outer loop is the hedge over `n`-versus-`B/n`.

```python
import numpy as np
from math import log, ceil


def successive_halving(space, n, r, s, eta, rng,
                       sample_configuration,
                       run_then_return_val_loss, top_k):
    """One bracket: start with n configs at minimum resource r, and over s+1 rounds
    keep the top 1/eta while multiplying the resource by eta each round, so the
    survivor is trained to r*eta^s. This is the early-stopping inner loop."""
    T = [sample_configuration(space, rng) for _ in range(n)]  # n i.i.d. configs
    results = []
    for i in range(s + 1):                                 # rounds 0..s
        n_i = int(n * eta ** (-i))                         # configs alive this round: floor(n eta^-i)
        r_i = r * eta ** i                                 # resource each gets:        r eta^i
        losses = [run_then_return_val_loss(t, r_i) for t in T]   # the one expensive operation
        results.extend(zip(T, losses, [r_i] * len(T)))
        T = top_k(T, losses, int(n_i / eta))               # promote the best floor(n_i/eta)
    return results


def hyperband(space, R, eta, rng,
              sample_configuration,
              run_then_return_val_loss, top_k):
    """Hedge over the n-versus-B/n tradeoff: run successive halving for a geometric
    grid of bracket sizes s, from maximal early-stopping (s=s_max) down to plain
    random search (s=0). Each bracket uses ~B resource; no need to choose n."""
    s_max = int(log(R) / log(eta))                         # number of distinct brackets - 1
    B = (s_max + 1) * R                                    # total budget per bracket
    all_results = []
    for s in reversed(range(s_max + 1)):                   # s = s_max, s_max-1, ..., 0
        # starting pool: eta^s makes aggressive brackets large; 1/(s+1) equalizes
        # per-bracket budget across the number of elimination rounds.
        n = int(ceil((B / R) * eta ** s / (s + 1)))        # n = ceil((B/R) eta^s/(s+1))
        r = R * eta ** (-s)                                # minimum resource so r*eta^s = R
        all_results.extend(
            successive_halving(space, n, r, s, eta, rng,
                               sample_configuration,
                               run_then_return_val_loss, top_k)
        )
    # return the configuration with the smallest validation loss seen anywhere
    return min(all_results, key=lambda cfg_loss_r: cfg_loss_r[1])[0]
```

That is the form I want to preserve in code: the outer loop is the bracket hedge, the inner loop is plain successive halving, every ranking is by validation loss with smaller better, and the returned configuration is the best intermediate loss seen anywhere in the sweep. A class wrapper can add bookkeeping such as result dictionaries, counters, and optional early-stop flags, but those are implementation details around the same two loops, not a different allocation policy.

Let me retrace the causal chain. I started with random search wasting the full resource `R` on every configuration even though most declare themselves hopeless almost immediately, and with Bayesian methods that fix *which* configuration cleverly but still train each to completion — both pay full price per configuration. Casting configurations as bandit arms with converging loss sequences and an unknown envelope `gamma` made the waste precise: two arms become distinguishable only once the envelope shrinks below half their terminal gap, so each arm has its own separation cost `tau_i`, and uniform allocation foolishly pays the *hardest* arm's `tau` on every arm. Successive halving fixes that by eliminating in rounds and escalating resource onto survivors; pushing the proof through showed `B > z_SH ≈ 2 ceil(log2 n)(n + sum_i tau_i)` returns a near-best arm — a *sum* of per-arm costs instead of `n × max`, with exponent `max{alpha,beta}` instead of `alpha + beta` in the parameterized model. But halving needs `n` as an input, and the right `n` depends on the unobservable `gamma` and `F` — and trying to estimate them reintroduces exactly the fragile convergence assumptions I rejected. The escape was a doubling-trick-in-two-dimensions: don't choose `n`, hedge over a geometric grid of brackets `s = 0, ..., s_max` with `s_max = floor(log_eta R)`, each running halving with `n = ceil((B/R) eta^s/(s+1))` configurations at minimum resource `r = R eta^{-s}`, where the most aggressive bracket maximizes early stopping and the least aggressive is plain random search. Because there are only `O(log R)` brackets, hedging costs only a log factor over knowing the best `n`, which the parameterized rate confirms — and matches best-arm lower bounds in the stochastic special case. With `eta = 3`, a fixed `B = (s_max+1)R` per sweep, and assumption-free uniform sampling, it drops into the search harness as the two nested loops above.
