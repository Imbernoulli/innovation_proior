The thing I pay for over and over in a GPT-style decoder is the transformer block. The same block runs once per layer, and I am stacking a couple dozen of them at the small end and well over a hundred at the large end, and the block is also the unit I replicate across accelerators. So every little thing inside the block — an extra reduction over the channel dimension, an extra place where a sharded result has to be summed back into the full-width residual, an extra step where one sublayer has to wait for the other — gets multiplied by the layer count and by the device count and lands directly on my wall-clock. When the models were small this micro-structure was effectively free; it is not free anymore. The question I actually care about is therefore which parts of the block are load-bearing and which are convention I am paying for. Two regions are open to me and fixed by nothing fundamental: the normalization rule, and how the two sublayers plus the residual are wired together. Everything else — the data, the optimizer, the schedule, what attention and the feed-forward compute internally — I hold fixed.

Take the normalization first. LayerNorm, for the summed inputs $a \in \mathbb{R}^n$ to a layer, computes a mean and a standard deviation across the $n$ channels and standardizes, $\bar a_i = \frac{a_i - \mu}{\sigma} g_i$ with $\mu = \frac1n \sum_i a_i$ and $\sigma = \sqrt{\frac1n \sum_i (a_i - \mu)^2}$, learned per-channel gain $g$ and optional bias $b$. Its benefit is usually credited to two invariances: re-centering — shift the inputs or weights by a constant, the mean shifts with them, and the subtraction $a - \mu$ cancels it — and re-scaling — scale everything by a constant, $\sigma$ scales the same way, and the division cancels it. But look at the arithmetic per call, since that is what I multiply by the layer count: two reductions over the channel dimension, one for $\mu$ and one for the variance, plus an elementwise subtraction of $\mu$. Now ask which invariance is actually doing the stabilizing. Subtracting $\mu$ moves the activation vector so it has zero channel-mean, but the variance of $a - \mu$ is identical to the variance of $a$ — subtracting a constant from every entry does not touch the spread at all, and does nothing to the dispersion of the gradients flowing back, which is controlled by the division by $\sigma$. The mechanism I believe in is that normalization keeps the scale of activations and gradients under control as they pass through a deep stack, and that scale control lives entirely in the rescaling. So re-centering is the dispensable half, and dropping it lets me delete one of the two reductions and the subtraction.

The method that results is RMSNorm, used as the single shared normalizer of a parallel attention/feed-forward block. For the normalization, drop the mean and divide by a pure scale statistic, the root mean square:
$$\bar a_i = \frac{a_i}{\mathrm{RMS}(a)} \, g_i, \qquad \mathrm{RMS}(a) = \sqrt{\frac1n \sum_{i=1}^n a_i^2},$$
with learned per-channel gain $g$ initialized to ones and no bias. Note that if the activation vector already has zero channel-mean then $\mathrm{RMS}(a) = \sigma$ and this is exactly LayerNorm — I am not inventing a different beast, I am keeping the scale-control half and discarding the centering half, and in the zero-mean case they coincide. Re-scaling invariance, the property the whole bet rests on, survives because RMS is homogeneous: $\mathrm{RMS}(\alpha a) = |\alpha|\,\mathrm{RMS}(a)$, equal to $\alpha\,\mathrm{RMS}(a)$ for positive $\alpha$, so scaling the inputs by a positive $\delta$ scales numerator and RMS together and they cancel, and scaling the whole weight matrix $W \to \delta W$ gives $a \to \delta a$ with the same cancellation. What I give up is exactly what I bet was dispensable: there is no mean, so a constant added to $a$ does not cancel — no re-centering invariance — and because the statistic pools all $n$ channels into one scalar, per-weight-vector re-scaling with different factors also breaks. The bias goes too: a bias exists to re-introduce a shift after the mean was removed, and since I remove no mean there is no re-centering being enforced that a bias would need to restore, so the gain $g$ is the only learned parameter. The backward pass is well-behaved: the gradient with respect to $g$ is proportional to the normalized inputs $Wx/\mathrm{RMS}(a)$, not the raw ones, so the scale of $g$ stays controlled regardless of how $Wx$ is scaled; the weight gradient carries a factor $R = \frac{1}{\mathrm{RMS}(a)}\big(I - \frac{(Wx)(Wx)^\top}{n\,\mathrm{RMS}(a)^2}\big)$, and under $x$ or $W$ scaled by $\delta$ the input scaling cancels while $\partial L/\partial W \propto 1/\delta$, so a layer whose weights have grown large gets smaller gradients — an implicit per-layer learning-rate adaptor, a bonus. One numerical point I cannot skate past: the statistic is a sum of squares across a wide channel dimension, and in low precision squaring then summing loses significance fast, so I compute the reduction in fp32 — cast up, form $\mathrm{mean}(x^2)$, add a small $\mathrm{eps} = 10^{-5}$ inside the reciprocal-sqrt so it floors a degenerate all-zero row rather than being bolted on afterward, take $\mathrm{rsqrt}$, multiply, cast back, then apply the gain.

That settles the op, but it does nothing about the two things actually scaling my cost: the block has two of these norms, two places where a sharded result is summed into the residual, and a hard sequential dependency between attention and the MLP. Write the serial pre-normalized block out fully and the dependency is visible — $x \leftarrow x + \mathrm{Attn}(\mathrm{LN}(x))$ then $x \leftarrow x + \mathrm{MLP}(\mathrm{LN}(x))$, so the MLP's input is $\mathrm{LN}(x + \mathrm{Attn}(\mathrm{LN}(x)))$: the feed-forward sublayer literally cannot start until attention's contribution has landed in the stream and been normalized again. Why does that cost so much under sharding? When the model is too big for one device I shard the matmuls inside attention and the MLP across accelerators; each sublayer then computes a partial result per device, and adding it back into the full-width residual needs an all-reduce to sum the shards — one in the forward pass and one in the backward — at each rejoin point. Collectives are synchronization barriers and at large device counts a leading term in step time, and the serial block has two rejoin points, so two forward and two backward all-reduces per block, multiplied by every layer. So I ask the dangerous question: is the ordering necessary? The serial form buys the MLP one extra step of composition — it conditions on what attention just did — but that is one mixing step, and I have dozens to a hundred-plus layers already stacking exactly this kind of composition. Both sublayers are, by construction, additive perturbations of the same residual highway. So let them read one shared normalized input and run independently, summing both into the stream at once:
$$x \leftarrow x + \mathrm{Attn}(\mathrm{LN}(x)) + \mathrm{MLP}(\mathrm{LN}(x)).$$
The residual $x$ is still a clean un-normalized identity path — I am adding the two branches to it, not normalizing the stream — so the pre-normalization gradient story, the very thing pre-norm was bought for, stays intact, and I avoid the large output-layer gradients at initialization that the post-norm placement forces a warmup to tame. The payoff is structural: one normalized input means one norm per block instead of two, halving the normalization layers in the whole model, and because the two branches are summed before rejoining the residual, a sharded implementation can sum the shards locally on each device and then do a single all-reduce for the combined contribution — one rejoin point, one forward and one backward all-reduce per block instead of two of each. In a large-scale implementation that owns the attention and MLP kernels, both branches consuming the identical $\mathrm{LN}(x)$ also lets the two input projections fuse into one wider matmul and the two output projections co-schedule, which together with the single norm and the local branch sum is what yields the roughly 15% throughput win. The only thing surrendered is that one composition step, which costs a little quality at small scale and washes out by large scale — a small degradation around 8B parameters and no measurable degradation by around 62B.

One subtlety I want exactly right: "one norm" has two readings. The tied version, $x + \mathrm{Attn}(\mathrm{LN}_1(x)) + \mathrm{MLP}(\mathrm{LN}_1(x))$, computes a single $\mathrm{LN}_1(x)$ once and hands it to both branches; the untied version, $x + \mathrm{Attn}(\mathrm{LN}_1(x)) + \mathrm{MLP}(\mathrm{LN}_2(x))$, computes two independent norms with separate gains, both fed $x$. The tied version is the one that buys the savings: one norm computed, one normalized tensor, which is the precondition for fused projections and is already enough to halve the norm count. The untie is strictly more parameters and could a priori help a hair, but the evidence is that tied versus untied makes no measurable quality difference at the scales checked, so the tied single-norm is the clear choice — same quality, and it delivers the halved norm count and keeps the fused-kernel path open. One depth check: stacking two additive branches per block means the residual accumulates $\mathrm{Attn} + \mathrm{MLP}$ together each layer, so activations could grow with depth; the fix lives in initialization, not the forward equation — a residual-output init on the order of $2/(L\sqrt{d})$ keeps the stream from growing, the factor of two compensating for the parallel organization, which the existing GPT-2-style $0.02/\sqrt{2L}$ scaling on residual `c_proj.weight` parameters already matches since the block still has two residual-producing projections. The two changes are independent and composable: RMSNorm is a swap of the normalization op, the parallel block a rewiring of how norm, attention, MLP and residual connect, and I use one RMSNorm as the single shared norm of the parallel block — a cheaper op inside a cheaper structure. In the local code I keep `CausalSelfAttention` and `MLP` unchanged, so it realizes the single-norm parallel wiring but not the literal fused-matmul rewrite, which is the systems-level optimization the architecture enables rather than something the narrow patch performs.

```python
import torch
import torch.nn as nn


class LayerNorm(nn.Module):
    """RMSNorm under the scaffold's LayerNorm name. Normalize by RMS over the
    channel dimension; learned per-channel gain; no bias. The bias argument is
    intentionally ignored."""

    def __init__(self, ndim, bias):
        super().__init__()
        self.weight = nn.Parameter(torch.ones(ndim))   # gain g, init 1
        self.eps = 1e-5

    def forward(self, input):
        # rms_inv = 1 / sqrt(mean(x^2) + eps), reduction in fp32 for stability
        rms = input.float().pow(2).mean(-1, keepdim=True).add(self.eps).rsqrt()
        return (input * rms).type_as(input) * self.weight   # x / RMS(x) * g


class Block(nn.Module):
    """Parallel attention + MLP block. One shared norm feeds both sublayers, which
    run on the same normalized input; their outputs are summed into the residual."""

    def __init__(self, config):
        super().__init__()
        self.ln = LayerNorm(config.n_embd, bias=config.bias) # single shared (tied) RMSNorm
        self.attn = CausalSelfAttention(config)              # unchanged
        self.mlp = MLP(config)                               # unchanged

    def forward(self, x):
        h = self.ln(x)                       # normalize once
        x = x + self.attn(h) + self.mlp(h)   # parallel branches, summed into the residual
        return x
```
