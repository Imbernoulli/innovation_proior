Let me start from what actually costs me in one of these decoder stacks, because the block is the thing I pay for over and over. I have a residual stream `x`, and the block does two perturbations of it: attention and a feed-forward. In the pre-normalized form I'm using, that's `x ← x + Attn(LN(x))` then `x ← x + MLP(LN(x))`. The same block runs once per layer, and I'm stacking a couple dozen of them at the smaller end and well over a hundred at the large end, and the whole block is the unit I replicate across accelerators. So every little thing inside the block — an extra reduction over the channel dimension, an extra place where a sharded result has to be summed back into the full residual, an extra step where one sublayer has to wait for the other — gets multiplied by the layer count and by the device count and lands directly on my wall-clock. When the models were small none of this mattered; the block's micro-structure was free. It isn't free anymore. So the question I actually care about is: what inside this block is load-bearing, and what is just convention I'm paying for?

There are two regions I'm allowed to touch and that aren't fixed by anything fundamental: the normalization rule, and how the two sublayers plus the residual are wired together. Everything else — the dataset, the optimizer, the schedule, what attention and the MLP compute internally — I hold fixed. Let me take the normalization first, because it's the smaller, cleaner question, and then come back to the wiring.

LayerNorm. For the summed inputs `a ∈ R^n` to a layer it computes a mean and a standard deviation across the `n` channels and standardizes: `ā_i = (a_i − μ)/σ · g_i`, with `μ = (1/n)Σ a_i`, `σ = sqrt((1/n)Σ(a_i − μ)^2)`, and a learned per-channel gain `g`. Why does this help training? The story I've absorbed is that it confers two invariances. Re-centering: if I shift all the inputs (or the weights producing them) by a constant, the mean shifts with them and the subtraction `a − μ` cancels the shift, so the output is unchanged. Re-scaling: if I scale everything by a constant, `σ` scales the same way and the division cancels it, so the output is again unchanged. And there's the landscape-smoothing reading too — that the real benefit is a better-conditioned optimization surface rather than the invariances themselves. Fine. But let me look at the *arithmetic* of what it does per call, because that's what I'm multiplying by the layer count: it's two reductions over the channel dimension — one to form `μ`, a second to form the variance — plus an elementwise subtraction of `μ`, and then the divide and the gain.

Now, which of those two invariances is actually doing the stabilizing? Take a concrete vector, `a = [3, −1, 4, 1, −2, 5]` across `n = 6` channels. Its mean is `μ = 10/6 = 1.6667` and its variance is `(1/6)Σ(a_i − μ)^2 = 6.5556`. Now subtract the mean: `a − μ = [1.333, −2.667, 2.333, −0.667, −3.667, 3.333]`. That vector has mean zero by construction. What's its variance? `(1/6)Σ(a_i − μ)^2` over the centered entries — but the centered entries are exactly `a_i − μ`, so this is the same sum I just computed: `6.5556`, identical. And if I instead *add* an arbitrary constant, say `a + 7.3`, the variance comes out `6.5556` again. So mean-subtraction — and any shift — moves the activation vector around but leaves its spread untouched, exactly. That's the thing I wanted to know and now I've actually seen it on numbers: the dispersion of the hidden states, and by the same token the dispersion of the gradients flowing back, is controlled entirely by the *division*, by `σ`. The re-centering step contributes nothing to scale. If the working mechanism of normalization is keeping the scale of activations and gradients under control as they pass through a deep stack — and that's the mechanism I believe in — then the part that controls scale is the rescaling, and the part that only slides the vector around is the re-centering. That makes re-centering the candidate I can try to drop: one of the two reductions, plus the subtraction, gone.

What do I normalize by, then, if I drop the mean? I want a pure scale statistic. The natural one is the root mean square: `RMS(a) = sqrt((1/n) Σ a_i^2)`. So the rule becomes `ā_i = a_i / RMS(a) · g_i`. No mean removed, just divide by the RMS and apply the gain. How far does this sit from LayerNorm? For my vector, `RMS(a) = sqrt((9+1+16+1+4+25)/6) = sqrt(56/6) = 3.055`, which is *not* `σ = 2.560` — they differ because `a` has a nonzero mean. But now compute `RMS` of the *centered* vector `a − μ`: `sqrt((1/6)Σ(a_i − μ)^2)`, and that sum is the variance times `n` over `n`, i.e. exactly `σ`. Numerically `RMS(a − μ) = 2.560 = σ`. So `RMS = σ` precisely when the mean is zero, and `RMS ≠ σ` otherwise. That tells me I'm not inventing a different beast: in the zero-mean case RMSNorm and LayerNorm are the *same* operation, and away from it RMSNorm just declines to center first. I'm keeping the scale-control half and dropping the centering half.

Do I keep re-scaling invariance? That was the whole point, so check it directly. Algebraically RMS is homogeneous: `RMS(αa) = sqrt((1/n)Σ(αa_i)^2) = |α|·RMS(a)`, which is `α·RMS(a)` for positive `α`, so scaling the input by positive `δ` scales the numerator by `δ` and the denominator by `δ` and they cancel. Let me watch it on numbers with a gain `g = [1.2, 0.8, 1.0, 1.5, 0.9, 1.1]` so I'm not testing a degenerate `g = 1` case. RMSNorm of `a` gives `[1.178, −0.262, 1.309, 0.491, −0.589, 1.800]`. RMSNorm of `4.7·a` gives `[1.178, −0.262, 1.309, 0.491, −0.589, 1.800]` — identical to all the digits I'm printing. So positive re-scaling invariance is real, and the same cancellation covers scaling the whole weight matrix `W → δW`, since that takes `a = Wx → δa`. Now the thing I *gave up*: shift invariance. RMSNorm of `a + 2` gives `[1.342, 0.179, 1.342, 1.006, 0.000, 1.722]`, which is nowhere near RMSNorm of `a` — so a constant added to the input does *not* cancel. That's the property I deliberately bet was dispensable, and the numbers confirm it's genuinely gone, not merely weakened. One more loss to be honest about: if I scale *individual* channels by different factors, the homogeneity argument needs a single scalar shared across the whole vector, so per-weight-vector re-scaling breaks — RMSNorm pools all `n` channels into one statistic and can't be invariant to that. I'm comfortable with both losses given the bet.

The bias. LayerNorm carries an optional bias `b` added after the gain. Do I want one? The bias is there to let the layer re-introduce a shift after it has removed the mean — it's the restoration knob for the re-centering it did. But I'm not removing any mean; there's no re-centering invariance being enforced that a bias would need to restore. So a bias is unmotivated here; I'll default it off. The only learned parameter is the per-channel gain `g`, initialized to ones so the layer starts as a pure normalizer.

Let me think about the backward pass, since the forward simplification might hide a problem there. I can't run a full training curve in my head, but I can reason about scaling. The gradient with respect to the gain is `∂L/∂g = ∂L/∂v ⊙ (Wx)/RMS(a)` where `v` is the pre-activation — it's proportional to the *normalized* inputs, not the raw ones, so I'd expect the scale of `g`'s gradient to stay well-behaved regardless of how `Wx` is scaled, by the same cancellation I just verified for the forward output. The gradient with respect to the weight matrix carries a factor `R = (1/RMS(a))·(I − (Wx)(Wx)^T/(n·RMS(a)^2))`; under `x → δx` or `W → δW` the `1/RMS` makes `R → R/δ`. Combined with the explicit `x` sitting in `∂L/∂W`, the input-scaling `δ` should cancel (input-scale invariant gradient — smooth learning) while weight scaling leaves `∂L/∂W ∝ 1/δ`, so a layer whose weights have grown large gets *smaller* gradients — an implicit per-layer learning-rate adaptor that keeps weight norms in check. That last property I'm inferring from the `1/RMS` factor rather than having measured a training run, so I'd flag it as expected-but-unverified-here; it's a plausible bonus, not something I've watched happen. What I'm confident about is the per-call arithmetic: I go from two reductions plus a subtraction to one reduction (sum of squares) and a reciprocal-sqrt, and the saving is biggest precisely where the normalization runs many times per step, which is my deep stack.

A numerical point I shouldn't skate past: the statistic is a sum of squares across the channel dimension. In low precision, squaring then summing a thousand-wide vector loses significance fast — the squares span orders of magnitude and the accumulation eats the small ones. So I'll compute the reduction in fp32: cast `x` up, form `mean(x^2)`, add a small `eps` for the zero-row floor, take the reciprocal square root, multiply, then cast back to the working precision before applying the gain. The `eps` goes *inside* the reciprocal-sqrt, `rsqrt(mean(x^2) + eps)`, so it floors the reciprocal against a degenerate all-zero row rather than being bolted on afterward. I should check that floor actually behaves: a row of all zeros has `mean(x^2) = 0`, so `rsqrt(0 + 1e-5) ≈ 316`, finite, and `0 · 316 = 0` — the row passes through as zeros with no NaN, which is the behavior I want. That's the form I'll write.

That settles the normalization op: drop the mean, normalize by RMS, learned gain, no bias, fp32 reduction. But this is just the *op*. It does nothing about the two things that are actually scaling my cost: the block has two of these norms, two places where a sharded result gets summed into the residual, and a hard sequential dependency between attention and the MLP. RMSNorm makes each norm cheaper; it doesn't change how many there are or how the block is wired. So now the structural question.

Write the pre-normalized block out fully, substituting the actual data dependencies:

  `x ← x + Attn(LN1(x))`
  `x ← x + MLP(LN2(x))`

so unrolled, the MLP's input is `LN2(x + Attn(LN1(x)))`. The feed-forward sublayer takes as its input the residual stream *after* attention has already been added to it. The two sublayers are strictly ordered: the MLP literally cannot start until attention's contribution has landed in the stream and been normalized again. And there are two norms per block, and — this is the part that bites at scale — two separate points where a sublayer's output rejoins the residual.

Why does that last point cost so much? When the model is too big for one device I shard the matmuls inside attention and the MLP across accelerators (op-sharding). Each sublayer then computes a *partial* result on each device, and to add it back into the full-width residual stream I need an all-reduce to sum the shards — one in the forward pass and one in the backward pass, at *each* such rejoin point. Collectives are synchronization barriers; at large device counts they're a leading term in step time. The serial block has two rejoin points, so two forward and two backward all-reduces per block, multiplied by every layer. That's a lot of blocking communication for a structure I inherited rather than derived.

So let me ask whether the sequential ordering is necessary. What does the MLP actually gain from reading the post-attention stream rather than the pre-attention stream? In the serial form the MLP gets to condition on what attention just did — there's one extra step of mixing, a little more effective depth, the MLP composing on top of attention's output. That's real. But it's *one* step of composition, and I have dozens to a hundred-plus layers stacking exactly this kind of composition already. Both attention and the MLP are, by construction, just additive perturbations to the same residual highway — `x + Attn(·) + MLP(·)` is what the stream accumulates either way. What if I let them both read the *same* normalized input and run independently, summing their outputs into the residual together?

  `x ← x + Attn(LN(x)) + MLP(LN(x))`.

One normalized input feeds both branches; the branches don't see each other; their outputs are summed and added to the stream in one shot. Two things need checking before this is a speed story: does it break the gradient behavior pre-norm was bought for, and does it actually do fewer norms and fewer rejoins.

Gradients first. I'm in a pre-normalized world specifically because the residual stream stays an un-normalized identity highway, which is what keeps gradients well-behaved at initialization and lets me train without a warmup crutch; if I'd normalized the residual itself (the post-norm placement) I'd get those large output-layer gradients at init that force warmup and make everything delicate. In the parallel form the residual `x` is still a clean identity path — the update is `x + (Attn(LN(x)) + MLP(LN(x)))`, and `∂x_out/∂x` still has that `I + (branch jacobians)` shape with the `I` coming from the untouched residual, exactly as in the serial pre-norm block. I'm adding to the stream, not normalizing the stream. So the pre-norm gradient story should be intact; I haven't broken the thing pre-norm was bought for.

Now the cost. Write both forms out at the tensor level: the parallel block computes `h = ln(x)` once and reuses `h` for both `attn(h)` and `mlp(h)`; the serial block needs a second, distinct norm module `ln2` to produce `mlp(ln2(x1))`, and `ln2(x1)` is a different tensor from `h` — the MLP's input genuinely changes from the post-attention stream to the pre-attention one, nothing else. So the parallel form really is one-norm-feeds-both, and the only thing it surrenders is that one composition step.

With that confirmed, the cost win follows. One normalized input means **one** norm per block instead of two — half the normalization layers in the whole model. And both branches now consume the identical `LN(x)`: in a large-scale implementation that owns the attention and MLP kernels, the attention input projection and the MLP input projection can be combined into one wider input matmul, and the two residual-producing output projections can be scheduled as one combined write path. I need to keep that separate from the narrower local edit I'll actually write, though: there, `CausalSelfAttention` and `MLP` stay unchanged, so the code realizes the shared-norm parallel residual structure but does not literally rewrite those internals into fused matmuls. The sharding payoff is the architectural one: because the two branches are summed *before* rejoining the residual, I can sum the shards locally on each device first and then do a *single* all-reduce for the combined `Attn + MLP` contribution — one rejoin point instead of two, so one forward and one backward all-reduce per block instead of two of each. The blocking communication per block roughly halves. In a large-scale op-sharded implementation, that combination of one norm, local branch summation, and fusible projections is what should drive a throughput win; I'd put it in the low tens of percent from the halved collectives and norm count, but the exact figure is implementation-dependent and I'd want to measure it on the real sharded kernels rather than promise a number here.

So what's the catch — what did I pay for that speed? Only the one step of composition the MLP gave up, which the tensor comparison above showed explicitly: the parallel MLP reads `LN(x)`, the same tensor attention reads, instead of the post-attention stream. So I'd expect a small quality hit, and I'd expect it to *shrink with scale*, because the lost composition is one mixing step and a deeper, wider model has many other layers to recover the same effect — at large enough depth I'd be surprised if it remained measurable. That's a prediction I can't settle from this scaffold; I'd want to train both wirings at a couple of model sizes and read off validation loss to see where the gap closes. At my GPT-2-Medium scale I should expect to be in the regime where there's a little composition cost; the structural win is the point of the design, and the cost is the thing I'm trading it against.

Now a subtlety I want to get exactly right, because there are two readings of "one norm" and they're not the same object. Do attention and the MLP read the *same* normalized tensor (one shared norm), or do they each get their *own* norm applied to the same `x` (two norms, both reading `x`)? The tied version is `x + Attn(LN1(x)) + MLP(LN1(x))` — a single `LN1(x)` computed once and handed to both. The untied version is `x + Attn(LN1(x)) + MLP(LN2(x))` — two independent norms, each with its own gain, both fed `x`. The tied version is the one that actually buys me the savings: one norm computed, one normalized tensor, which is the precondition for fused projections in systems that implement that fusion and is already enough to halve the norm count in the local edit. The untied version computes `LN(x)` twice with different gains — it costs an extra norm and the inputs to the two branches differ, so it loses that clean shared-input property. Does the untie *buy* anything in quality? Each branch getting its own learned per-channel scale is strictly more parameters and more flexibility, so a priori it could help a hair. I can't decide that from arithmetic — it's an empirical question — but my expectation is the effect is tiny, since the gain is a single per-channel vector and the branches' own projection weights already give them independent linear freedom on top of `LN(x)`. If tied and untied come out indistinguishable in quality, then the tied single-norm is the clear choice: it's the version that delivers the halved norm count and keeps the fused-kernel path open at no quality cost. So I'll commit to one shared norm: `h = LN(x); x ← x + Attn(h) + MLP(h)`, and treat "untie helps" as the thing I'd disconfirm before bothering. (Worth keeping in mind that an "accidental" untie — two norms where you meant one — is a real and easy mistake to make in code; the reassuring part, if my expectation holds, is that it's harmless to quality, just slower.)

One thing about depth I should sanity-check: stacking two additive branches per block means the residual stream now accumulates `Attn + MLP` together each layer, and if I'm not careful the activations can grow with depth. The clean fix lives in initialization, not in the forward equation. A residual-output init on the order of `2/(L·sqrt(d))` for the residual-producing projections keeps the stream from growing with depth, the factor of two compensating for the parallel organization. In the local scaffold the existing GPT-2-style rule already scales every residual projection ending in `c_proj.weight` by `0.02/sqrt(2L)`, matching the fact that the block still has two residual-producing projections. I don't need a new forward branch for this; I just need not pretend the parallel equation alone solves depth scaling.

Let me also be precise that the two pieces I'm changing are *independent and composable*. RMSNorm is a swap of the normalization op — it slots in wherever a norm goes. The parallel block is a rewiring of how the norm, attention, MLP and residual connect. I can do both: use one RMSNorm as the single shared norm in the parallel block. The RMSNorm makes that one norm cheap (one reduction, no subtraction); the parallel wiring makes there be one of it instead of two and gives sharded implementations the local-sum and fused-projection opportunity. They stack cleanly — the cheaper op inside the cheaper structure.

So let me write the actual code I'd ship, against the decoder I already have. The normalization module keeps the same `(ndim, bias)` constructor signature the block expects so it's a drop-in — the `bias` argument is simply ignored, since RMSNorm enforces no re-centering to restore. The forward does the fp32 sum-of-squares, the `eps`-floored reciprocal-sqrt (which I checked keeps an all-zero row finite), the multiply, the cast back, and the gain:

```python
import torch
import torch.nn as nn


class LayerNorm(nn.Module):
    """RMSNorm under the scaffold's LayerNorm name: normalize by root-mean-square
    over channels; learned per-channel gain; no bias (no mean is removed, so
    there is no shift-invariance to restore)."""

    def __init__(self, ndim, bias):
        super().__init__()
        self.weight = nn.Parameter(torch.ones(ndim))   # gain g, init 1; bias intentionally absent
        self.eps = 1e-5

    def forward(self, input):
        # sum-of-squares reduction in fp32 for numerical stability, then reciprocal-sqrt:
        #   rms_inv = 1 / sqrt(mean(x^2) + eps)
        rms = input.float().pow(2).mean(-1, keepdim=True).add(self.eps).rsqrt()
        # x / RMS(x), cast back to the working dtype, then the learned per-channel gain
        return (input * rms).type_as(input) * self.weight


class Block(nn.Module):
    """Parallel transformer block: ONE shared norm feeds attention and the MLP, which
    run independently on the same normalized input; their outputs are summed into the
    residual together."""

    def __init__(self, config):
        super().__init__()
        self.ln = LayerNorm(config.n_embd, bias=config.bias) # single shared (tied) RMSNorm
        self.attn = CausalSelfAttention(config)              # unchanged sublayer
        self.mlp = MLP(config)                               # unchanged sublayer

    def forward(self, x):
        h = self.ln(x)                       # normalize once
        x = x + self.attn(h) + self.mlp(h)   # parallel branches on the same h, summed into the residual
        return x
```

That's it — the scaffold's `LayerNorm` class now implements RMSNorm, so the final norm and the block norm use the same rule, and `Block` replaces the two-norm serial wiring with a one-norm parallel wiring while `CausalSelfAttention` and `MLP` are untouched.

The design that falls out: RMSNorm as the single shared norm of a parallel block, `x ← x + Attn(LN(x)) + MLP(LN(x))`. The residual stays a clean identity path, so the pre-norm gradient behavior survives; one shared norm halves the per-block norm count and lets a sharded implementation reduce the summed branch output once instead of twice. The only thing surrendered is the one composition step the serial MLP used to get from reading the post-attention stream, which I'd expect to cost a little quality at small scale and wash out as depth grows — a claim to settle by training both wirings, not by asserting it.
