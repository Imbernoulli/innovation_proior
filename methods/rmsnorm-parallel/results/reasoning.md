Let me start from what actually costs me in one of these decoder stacks, because the block is the thing I pay for over and over. I have a residual stream `x`, and the block does two perturbations of it: attention and a feed-forward. In the pre-normalized form I'm using, that's `x ← x + Attn(LN(x))` then `x ← x + MLP(LN(x))`. The same block runs once per layer, and I'm stacking a couple dozen of them at the smaller end and well over a hundred at the large end, and the whole block is the unit I replicate across accelerators. So every little thing inside the block — an extra reduction over the channel dimension, an extra place where a sharded result has to be summed back into the full residual, an extra step where one sublayer has to wait for the other — gets multiplied by the layer count and by the device count and lands directly on my wall-clock. When the models were small none of this mattered; the block's micro-structure was free. It isn't free anymore. So the question I actually care about is: what inside this block is load-bearing, and what is just convention I'm paying for?

There are two regions I'm allowed to touch and that aren't fixed by anything fundamental: the normalization rule, and how the two sublayers plus the residual are wired together. Everything else — the dataset, the optimizer, the schedule, what attention and the MLP compute internally — I hold fixed. Let me take the normalization first, because it's the smaller, cleaner question, and then the wiring, which is where the real structural win is hiding.

LayerNorm. For the summed inputs `a ∈ R^n` to a layer it computes a mean and a standard deviation across the `n` channels and standardizes: `ā_i = (a_i − μ)/σ · g_i`, with `μ = (1/n)Σ a_i`, `σ = sqrt((1/n)Σ(a_i − μ)^2)`, and a learned per-channel gain `g`. Why does this help training? The story I've absorbed is that it confers two invariances. Re-centering: if I shift all the inputs (or the weights producing them) by a constant, the mean shifts with them and the subtraction `a − μ` cancels the shift, so the output is unchanged. Re-scaling: if I scale everything by a constant, `σ` scales the same way and the division cancels it, so the output is again unchanged. And there's the landscape-smoothing reading too — that the real benefit is a better-conditioned optimization surface rather than the invariances themselves. Fine. But let me look at the *arithmetic* of what it does per call, because that's what I'm multiplying by the layer count: it's two reductions over the channel dimension — one to form `μ`, a second to form the variance — plus an elementwise subtraction of `μ`, and then the divide and the gain.

Now I want to ask which of those two invariances is actually doing the stabilizing. Stare at mean-subtraction on its own. Subtracting `μ` shifts the activation vector so it has zero mean across channels — it moves the vector, it recenters it. But does it change the *spread*? The variance of `a − μ` is identical to the variance of `a`; subtracting a constant from every entry doesn't touch the variance at all. So the re-centering step does nothing to the magnitude or the dispersion of the hidden states, and nothing to the dispersion of the gradients flowing back — those are controlled by the *division*, by `σ`. If the working mechanism of normalization is keeping the scale of activations and gradients under control as they pass through a deep stack — and that's the mechanism I believe in — then the part that controls scale is the rescaling, and the part that only slides the vector around without changing its spread is the re-centering. So my hypothesis is that re-scaling invariance is what makes LayerNorm work and re-centering is dispensable. If that's right, I can throw away the mean entirely: one of the two reductions, plus the subtraction, gone.

What do I normalize by, then, if I drop the mean? I want a pure scale statistic. The natural one is the root mean square: `RMS(a) = sqrt((1/n) Σ a_i^2)`. So the rule becomes `ā_i = a_i / RMS(a) · g_i`. No mean removed, just divide by the RMS and apply the gain. Notice immediately that if the activation vector already has zero mean across channels, then `RMS(a)` equals `σ` and this is *exactly* LayerNorm — so I'm not inventing a different beast, I'm keeping the scale-control half and dropping the centering half, and in the zero-mean case they coincide.

Do I keep re-scaling invariance? That was the whole point, so let me check it actually survives. RMS is linear: `RMS(αa) = sqrt((1/n)Σ(αa_i)^2) = |α|·sqrt((1/n)Σ a_i^2) = α·RMS(a)` for positive `α`. So if I scale the inputs by `δ`, the numerator scales by `δ` and `RMS` scales by `δ` and they cancel; if I scale the whole weight matrix `W → δW`, then `a = Wx → δa`, same cancellation. So `y' = f(δWx / RMS(δa) · g + b) = f(Wx / RMS(a) · g + b) = y`. Invariant to input re-scaling and to whole-weight-matrix re-scaling. What did I give up? Shifts: there's no mean anymore, so a constant added to `a` does *not* cancel — RMSNorm is not invariant to re-centering. That's the property I deliberately bet was dispensable. And one more I should be honest about: if I scale *individual* weight vectors by different factors, the linearity `RMS(αa)=α·RMS(a)` needs a single scalar `α`, so per-weight-vector re-scaling breaks it — RMSNorm isn't invariant to that, the way it pools all `n` channels into one statistic. I'm comfortable with both losses given the bet.

The bias. LayerNorm carries an optional bias `b` added after the gain. Do I want one? The bias is there to let the layer re-introduce a shift after it has removed the mean — it's the restoration knob for the re-centering it did. But I'm not removing any mean; there's no re-centering invariance being enforced that a bias would need to restore. So a bias is unmotivated here; I'll default it off. The only learned parameter is the per-channel gain `g`, initialized to ones so the layer starts as a pure normalizer.

Let me make sure I'm not breaking the backward pass in some way that the forward simplification hides. The gradient with respect to the gain is `∂L/∂g = ∂L/∂v ⊙ (Wx)/RMS(a)` where `v` is the pre-activation — it's proportional to the *normalized* inputs, not the raw ones, so the scale of `g` stays well-behaved regardless of how `Wx` is scaled. The gradient with respect to the weight matrix carries a factor `R = (1/RMS(a))·(I − (Wx)(Wx)^T/(n·RMS(a)^2))`; if I scale `x` or `W` by `δ`, then `R → R/δ`. Combined with the explicit `x` sitting in `∂L/∂W`, the input-scaling `δ` cancels (input-scale invariant gradient, good — smooth learning) while weight scaling leaves `∂L/∂W ∝ 1/δ`, so a layer whose weights have grown large gets *smaller* gradients. That's an implicit per-layer learning-rate adaptor that keeps weight norms in check — a bonus, not a bug. So dropping the mean costs me re-centering invariance and buys me one fewer reduction, no subtraction, and these clean gradient properties. The per-call cost goes from two reductions plus a subtraction to one reduction (sum of squares) and a reciprocal-sqrt — and the saving is biggest precisely where the normalization runs many times per step, which is exactly my deep stack.

A numerical point I shouldn't skate past: the statistic is a sum of squares across the channel dimension. In low precision, squaring then summing a thousand-wide vector loses significance fast — the squares span orders of magnitude and the accumulation eats the small ones. So I'll compute the reduction in fp32: cast `x` up, form `mean(x^2)`, add a small `eps` for the zero-row floor, take the reciprocal square root, multiply, then cast back to the working precision before applying the gain. The `eps` goes *inside* the reciprocal-sqrt, `rsqrt(mean(x^2) + eps)`, so it floors the reciprocal against a degenerate all-zero row rather than being bolted on afterward. That's the form I'll write.

Good — that's the normalization op settled: drop the mean, normalize by RMS, learned gain, no bias, fp32 reduction. But this is just the *op*. It does nothing about the two things that are actually scaling my cost: that the block has two of these norms, two places where a sharded result gets summed into the residual, and a hard sequential dependency between attention and the MLP. RMSNorm makes each norm cheaper; it doesn't change how many there are or how the block is wired. So now the structural question, which is the bigger lever.

Write the pre-normalized block out fully, substituting the actual data dependencies:

  `x ← x + Attn(LN1(x))`
  `x ← x + MLP(LN2(x))`

so unrolled, the MLP's input is `LN2(x + Attn(LN1(x)))`. The feed-forward sublayer takes as its input the residual stream *after* attention has already been added to it. The two sublayers are strictly ordered: the MLP literally cannot start until attention's contribution has landed in the stream and been normalized again. And there are two norms per block, and — this is the part that bites at scale — two separate points where a sublayer's output rejoins the residual.

Why does that last point cost so much? When the model is too big for one device I shard the matmuls inside attention and the MLP across accelerators (op-sharding). Each sublayer then computes a *partial* result on each device, and to add it back into the full-width residual stream I need an all-reduce to sum the shards — one in the forward pass and one in the backward pass, at *each* such rejoin point. Collectives are synchronization barriers; at large device counts they're a leading term in step time. The serial block has two rejoin points, so two forward and two backward all-reduces per block, multiplied by every layer. That's a lot of blocking communication for a structure I inherited rather than derived.

So let me ask the dangerous question: is the sequential ordering necessary? What does the MLP actually gain from reading the post-attention stream rather than the pre-attention stream? In the serial form the MLP gets to condition on what attention just did — there's one extra step of mixing, a little more effective depth, the MLP composing on top of attention's output. That's real. But it's *one* step of composition, and I have dozens to a hundred-plus layers stacking exactly this kind of composition already. Both attention and the MLP are, by construction, just additive perturbations to the same residual highway — `x + Attn(·) + MLP(·)` is what the stream accumulates either way. What if I let them both read the *same* normalized input and run independently, summing their outputs into the residual together?

  `x ← x + Attn(LN(x)) + MLP(LN(x))`.

One normalized input feeds both branches; the branches don't see each other; their outputs are summed and added to the stream in one shot. Let me think hard about whether this is sane before I get excited about the speed.

Gradients first — I'm in a pre-normalized world specifically because the residual stream stays an un-normalized identity highway, which is what keeps gradients well-behaved at initialization and lets me train without a warmup crutch; if I'd normalized the residual itself (the post-norm placement) I'd get those large output-layer gradients at init that force warmup and make everything delicate. In the parallel form the residual `x` is still a clean identity path — I'm adding `Attn(LN(x)) + MLP(LN(x))` to it, I'm not normalizing the stream — so the pre-norm gradient story is intact. Good, I haven't broken the thing pre-norm was bought for.

Now the cost, which is where this pays off. One normalized input means **one** norm per block instead of two — half the normalization layers in the whole model. And both branches now consume the identical `LN(x)`: attention's input projection and the MLP's input projection are two big matmuls reading the *same* tensor, so they fuse into one wide input matmul; and at the output, attention's output projection and the MLP's down-projection both write into the residual, so they fuse into one wide output matmul. Two fused matmuls instead of four separate stages. And the sharding payoff: because the two branches are summed *before* rejoining the residual, I can sum the shards locally on each device first and then do a *single* all-reduce for the combined `Attn + MLP` contribution — one rejoin point instead of two, so one forward and one backward all-reduce per block instead of two of each. The blocking communication per block roughly halves. That's where the throughput comes from: the input matmuls fuse, the norm count halves, and the all-reduce count halves. This is reported to land around a 15% throughput increase at large scale, and that's exactly the arithmetic that produces it.

So what's the catch — what did I pay for that speed? Only the one step of composition the MLP gave up: it no longer reads the post-attention stream, only the same `LN(x)` attention reads. So I'd expect a small quality hit, and I'd expect it to *shrink with scale*, because the lost composition is one mixing step and a deeper, wider model has many other layers to recover the same effect. That matches what the parallel formulation actually does: a small quality degradation at the ~8B scale, and no measurable degradation by ~62B — at which point I get the throughput for free. At my GPT-2-Medium scale I should expect to be in the regime where there's a little composition cost; the structural win is the point of the design, and the cost is the thing I'm trading it against.

Now a subtlety I want to get exactly right, because there are two readings of "one norm" and they're not the same object. Do attention and the MLP read the *same* normalized tensor (one shared norm), or do they each get their *own* norm applied to the same `x` (two norms, both reading `x`)? The tied version is `x + Attn(LN1(x)) + MLP(LN1(x))` — a single `LN1(x)` computed once and handed to both. The untied version is `x + Attn(LN1(x)) + MLP(LN2(x))` — two independent norms, each with its own gain, both fed `x`. The tied version is the one that actually buys me the savings: one norm computed, one normalized tensor, which is precisely what lets the input projections fuse and the norm count halve. The untied version computes `LN(x)` twice with different gains — it costs an extra norm and the inputs to the two branches differ, so the input matmuls no longer share a tensor to fuse over. Does the untie *buy* anything in quality? Each branch getting its own learned per-channel scale is strictly more parameters and more flexibility, so a priori it could help a hair — but the evidence is that tied versus untied makes no measurable difference at the scales people have checked. Given that, the tied single-norm is the clear choice: same quality, and it's the version that delivers the fusion and the halved norm count. So I commit to one shared norm: `h = LN(x); x ← x + Attn(h) + MLP(h)`. (Worth keeping in mind that an "accidental" untie — two norms where you meant one — is a real and easy mistake to make in code, and the reassuring part is it's harmless to quality; but I want the tied form on purpose, for the cost.)

One thing about depth I should sanity-check: stacking two additive branches per block means the residual stream now accumulates `Attn + MLP` together each layer, and if I'm not careful the activations can grow with depth. The clean fix lives in initialization — scale the output projections down so the per-layer contribution doesn't compound; an output-projection init on the order of `1/(L·sqrt(d))`-ish keeps the residual from blowing up with depth, with the constant adjusted because two branches now feed the same residual addition rather than one. That's an initialization knob, not a change to the block's forward, and the harness already owns initialization; I just note that the parallel sum wants its output scale chosen with the doubled per-block contribution in mind.

Let me also be precise that the two pieces I'm changing are *independent and composable*. RMSNorm is a swap of the normalization op — it slots in wherever a norm goes. The parallel block is a rewiring of how the norm, attention, MLP and residual connect. I can do both: use one RMSNorm as the single shared norm in the parallel block. The RMSNorm makes that one norm cheap (one reduction, no subtraction); the parallel wiring makes there be one of it instead of two and fuses the matmuls around it. They stack cleanly — the cheaper op inside the cheaper structure.

So let me write the actual code I'd ship, against the decoder I already have. The normalization module keeps the same `(ndim, bias)` constructor signature the block expects so it's a drop-in — the `bias` argument is simply ignored, since RMSNorm enforces no re-centering to restore. The forward does the fp32 sum-of-squares, the `eps`-floored reciprocal-sqrt, the multiply, the cast back, and the gain:

```python
import torch
import torch.nn as nn


class RMSNorm(nn.Module):
    """RMSNorm: normalize by the root-mean-square over channels; learned per-channel
    gain, no bias (no mean is removed, so there is no shift-invariance to restore).
    Keeps the (ndim, bias) signature so it drops into the block unchanged."""

    def __init__(self, ndim, bias):
        super().__init__()
        self.weight = nn.Parameter(torch.ones(ndim))   # gain g, init 1; bias intentionally absent
        self.eps = 1e-5

    def forward(self, input):
        # sum-of-squares reduction in fp32 for numerical stability, then reciprocal-sqrt:
        #   rms_inv = 1 / sqrt(mean(x^2) + eps)
        rms = input.float().pow(2).mean(-1, keepdim=True).add(self.eps).rsqrt()
        # x / RMS(x), cast back to the working dtype, then the learned per-channel gain
        return (input * rms).type_as(input) * self.weight


class Block(nn.Module):
    """Parallel transformer block: ONE shared norm feeds attention and the MLP, which
    run independently on the same normalized input; their outputs are summed into the
    residual together (one rejoin point, fusible input/output matmuls)."""

    def __init__(self, config):
        super().__init__()
        self.ln = RMSNorm(config.n_embd, bias=config.bias)   # single shared (tied) norm
        self.attn = CausalSelfAttention(config)              # unchanged sublayer
        self.mlp = MLP(config)                               # unchanged sublayer

    def forward(self, x):
        h = self.ln(x)                       # normalize once
        x = x + self.attn(h) + self.mlp(h)   # parallel branches on the same h, summed into the residual
        return x
```

That's it — `RMSNorm` replaces the normalization op, and `Block` replaces the two-norm serial wiring with a one-norm parallel wiring, while `CausalSelfAttention` and `MLP` are untouched.

The causal chain, start to finish. I'm paying for the block's micro-structure once per layer and once per device, so per-block waste scales with depth and device count. Looking at LayerNorm's two invariances, I noticed that mean-subtraction only shifts the activations and leaves their spread — and the gradients' spread — unchanged, so the scale control I actually rely on comes entirely from the division by the spread statistic; that made re-centering the dispensable half, and dropping it gives RMSNorm: divide by the root-mean-square, keep a learned gain, no bias, one reduction instead of two, with re-scaling invariance preserved (RMS is linear) and a gradient that's input-scale invariant and self-damping in weight scale. Then, looking at the block's wiring, the strict serial order — MLP reading the post-attention stream — buys exactly one step of composition but costs two norms, two matmul stages, and, under op-sharding, two forward and two backward all-reduces per block. Since both sublayers are just additive perturbations of the same identity residual, I let them read one shared normalized input and run in parallel, `x + Attn(LN(x)) + MLP(LN(x))`: the residual stays a clean identity path so the pre-norm gradient behavior survives, the norm count halves, the input projections and output projections fuse into single wide matmuls, and the two rejoin points collapse into one all-reduce — about 15% faster at scale. The only thing surrendered is that one composition step, which costs a little quality at small scale and washes out by large scale. Tied (single shared norm) beats untied because it's the version that actually delivers the fusion and the halved count while quality is the same either way. Compose the two: a single cheap RMSNorm as the shared norm of a parallel block — a cheaper op inside a cheaper structure.
