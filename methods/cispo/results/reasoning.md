Let me start from the thing that bothers me about the clip I inherited. I am training an LLM with RL: roll out responses from a frozen `π_old`, score them with a verifier, partition the batch into mini-batches, and take several gradient steps on `π_θ`. Because the data is off-policy, each token carries an importance ratio `r_{i,t} = π_θ(y_{i,t}|x,y_{i,<t}) / π_old(y_{i,t}|x,y_{i,<t})`, and the PPO/GRPO surrogate combines the unclipped and clipped terms pessimistically, `min( r_{i,t} Â_{i,t}, clip(r_{i,t}, 1−ε, 1+ε) Â_{i,t} )`. The clip is there to stop the optimizer from chasing surrogate value by driving a single stale token's ratio far from 1. That part I believe. What I want to interrogate is the *price* the clip charges, because in reasoning RL the price looks like it is being paid by exactly the tokens I least want to silence.

Look at what the clip does to the gradient, mechanically. Take a positive-advantage token. While `r_{i,t}` is inside the band the objective is `r_{i,t} Â`, with the usual gradient `r_{i,t} Â ∇_θ log π_θ(y_{i,t}|·)`. The instant the policy raises that token's probability enough that `r_{i,t} > 1+ε`, the pessimistic `min` switches to the clipped branch `(1+ε) Â`, which has *no dependence on θ at all*: its gradient is identically zero. The token is not down-weighted, it is *deleted* from the update. The clip is a hard gate — in-band the token contributes fully, out-of-band it contributes nothing. So the question becomes: which tokens get gated out, and do I care about them?

I do care, and this is the crux. The tokens whose ratio moves the most in a single update are the ones whose probability changes the most in *relative* terms, and that is dominated by the tokens that were *rare under `π_old`*. A token the old policy assigned probability 0.02 that the new policy now likes at 0.10 has a ratio of 5 — wildly outside any sane band — even though in absolute terms it moved only eight points. A token the old policy already assigned 0.6 can barely produce a large ratio no matter how the policy shifts. So the clip preferentially fires on low-probability tokens. And in a reasoning trace, the low-probability, high-entropy tokens are not noise — they are the *fork* tokens: the "However", the "Wait", the "Let me recheck" that begins a self-correction or switches the line of reasoning. These are the tokens that determine whether a long chain recovers from a mistake or commits to it. They are rare precisely because branching is rare, and they are high-leverage precisely because they redirect everything downstream. The standard clip, by construction, zeroes the gradient on the tokens that move the reasoning. Every update, it throws away the signal I most need. That is the wall.

Let me make sure I am not misdiagnosing this as "the clip band is too tight." Widening `ε` does not fix it. If I widen the band the fork tokens stay in for a while longer, but I have also loosened the leash on *every* token, including the genuinely-stale ones the clip exists to catch, so I trade reasoning signal for instability. There is no single `ε` that keeps the rare high-leverage tokens while still bounding the runaway ones, because "rare" and "stale" are not the same axis — a fork token can be perfectly trustworthy and still produce a huge ratio. The problem is not the *width* of the gate. The problem is that there is a *gate at all*: a clip that responds to a large ratio by removing the gradient cannot distinguish "this token is informative and its ratio is large because it was rare" from "this token is stale and its ratio is large because it drifted." So I should stop trying to tune the gate and ask whether I need a gate.

Step back to first principles about *why* a large ratio is dangerous. The danger is variance: an importance weight is an unbiased correction only in aggregate, and a heavy-tailed weight makes the gradient estimate wander. The PPO clip controls that variance by *capping the influence of a large-ratio token at zero* — it caps influence by deletion. But capping influence and deleting the gradient are not the same thing. What I actually want is for a large-ratio token to contribute a *bounded* amount to the gradient, not *zero*. If I could keep every token's gradient direction `∇_θ log π_θ` but bound the *magnitude* of its coefficient, I would control the variance without ever silencing a token. That reframes the whole objective: the surrogate's `min`/`clip` machinery exists to bound influence, but it bounds it by gating; I want to bound influence by *bounding the coefficient*.

So let me write the gradient I am actually after, from the off-policy policy gradient itself rather than from the clipped surrogate. The score-function estimator for the off-policy gradient is `E[ w_{i,t} · Â_{i,t} · ∇_θ log π_θ(y_{i,t}|·) ]`, where `w_{i,t}` is the importance weight that corrects the `π_old`→`π_θ` mismatch. This is plain REINFORCE with an IS weight: the weight multiplies the gradient as a *scalar coefficient*, and the gradient flows through `log π_θ` directly. Crucially, in this form there is no clipped surrogate, so there is nothing whose slope goes to zero — every token contributes `w_{i,t} Â_{i,t} ∇ log π_θ` no matter how large `w_{i,t}` is. The variance problem is now isolated cleanly: it lives entirely in the magnitude of the coefficient `w_{i,t}`, not in any gating decision. And that is a quantity I can bound directly.

The fix writes itself once I see it that way. Bound the coefficient: replace `w_{i,t}` with `clip(w_{i,t}, 1−ε_low, 1+ε_high)`. Now the coefficient on every token's score-function gradient is at most `1+ε_high` in magnitude, so the variance contributed by any single stale, large-ratio token is bounded — exactly the property the PPO clip was trying to buy. But here is the difference that makes it work: the clip acts on the *coefficient*, and the gradient still flows through `∇ log π_θ`. A fork token with `w = 5` now contributes `(1+ε_high) Â ∇ log π_θ` — a bounded but *nonzero* gradient in the right direction — instead of the zero the PPO clip gave it. No token is ever dropped. I keep the rare reasoning tokens *and* I bound the variance, because I stopped conflating "bound influence" with "delete gradient."

But I have to be careful, because if I literally put `clip(w_{i,t})` into the loss and let autograd differentiate it, the gradient is no longer the clean bounded-coefficient REINFORCE gradient I want. Differentiating `clip(w) · Â · log π_θ` produces *two* terms by the product rule: the one I want, `clip(w) · Â · ∇ log π_θ`, plus an extra `Â · log π_θ · ∇ clip(w)`. In the clipped (flat) region `∇ clip(w) = 0`, so out-of-band tokens lose the very coefficient-weighted score-function term I was trying to keep; in-band `∇ clip(w) = ∇ w = w ∇ log π_θ`, so I get a spurious extra contribution that has nothing to do with the estimator. Either way it is wrong: out-of-band it re-creates the gating I am trying to avoid, in-band it double-counts. The whole point is that the *value* of the coefficient should be the clipped weight while *no gradient* should flow through it. That is precisely what a stop-gradient does. Write the coefficient as `sg[ clip(w_{i,t}, 1−ε_low, 1+ε_high) ]`, where `sg` is detach. In the forward pass it is the clipped weight, so it bounds each token's coefficient as intended. In the backward pass it is a constant — the optimizer cannot move `θ` to change it, so there is no incentive to game the clip and no flat region to kill the gradient. The gradient flows only through the live `log π_θ` factor I attach. So the loss to *minimize* (negative of the objective to maximize) is

  `L = − sg[ clip(w_{i,t}, 1−ε_low, 1+ε_high) ] · Â_{i,t} · log π_θ(y_{i,t}|·)`,

aggregated over the masked tokens. Let me verify the gradient is exactly the bounded-coefficient REINFORCE gradient I wanted. The only θ-dependent factor is `log π_θ(y_{i,t}|·)`, so

  `∇_θ L = − sg[clip(w)] · Â · ∇_θ log π_θ(y_{i,t}|·)`,

which is `−` (bounded coefficient) × (advantage) × (score function) — the off-policy policy gradient with the IS weight clipped to a bounded range, every token present. That is the object. The clip caps the variance; the stop-gradient keeps the gate from ever deleting a gradient.

Now I should pin down what the clip band should be, because it is *not* the same band as PPO's even though I am reusing the symbol. In PPO the clip sits on the surrogate and its job is to define a trust region around `π_old`; the natural width is symmetric and modest, `ε ≈ 0.2`. Here the clip sits on the IS *weight* and its job is purely variance control of the coefficient. The asymmetry matters: the dangerous direction is `w` becoming *large* (a rare token the new policy now loves), which inflates the coefficient and the variance; `w` becoming small just shrinks a coefficient toward zero, which is harmless. So the upper edge `1+ε_high` is the load-bearing one — it is what actually caps the variance — and it can be set fairly generously, because I am no longer paying for a high edge by dropping tokens; a token whose `w` exceeds the high edge is *kept*, just with its coefficient pinned. The lower edge `1−ε_low` matters much less. I will read both from config (`clip_ratio_low`, `clip_ratio_high`, each falling back to `clip_ratio`) rather than hardcode, so the band can be tuned for the variance/signal trade-off without touching the loss. The reference design even leaves the low edge effectively wide open and tunes only the high edge; the implementation I land on will clamp both sides for safety but the high edge is the knob that does the work.

Two details to get right so the thing trains. First, numerics: `w = exp(log π_θ − log π_old)` can overflow if a single token's log-ratio is large, so I clamp the log-ratio to `[−20, 20]` before exponentiating — `exp(20)` is already astronomically outside any band that matters, so the clamp only defuses overflow and never distorts a real update. Second, diagnostics: the trainer expects `actor/ppo_kl` and `actor/pg_clipfrac`. The KL diagnostic is the cheap `k1` estimate, `masked_mean(−(log π_θ − log π_old))`, i.e. `masked_mean(old_log_prob − log_prob)` over the response mask. The clip-fraction here means something slightly different from PPO's: PPO reports where the *surrogate* clipped branch was active; here I report where the IS *weight* was actually clipped, `masked_mean( (w != clip(w)) )` over the mask — the fraction of tokens whose coefficient was pinned. There is no asymmetric "lower clip" pathology to track because I am not using the dual-clip floor (that floor existed in token-level PPO to bound the large-ratio-negative-advantage surrogate; here the coefficient is already bounded by the clip on `w`, so the runaway it guarded against cannot happen). I will return `actor/pg_clipfrac_lower` as `0.0` for interface compatibility.

Let me also be explicit about the aggregation, because it is a genuine design choice and not just plumbing. The objective is a sum of per-token terms, each a bounded coefficient times a score function, so the natural aggregation is the flat token-mean: sum the per-token losses over the response mask and divide by the token count, every valid token weighted equally. This is *not* the sequence-level `seq-mean-token-mean` aggregation that a sequence-ratio method would use — that aggregation exists to mirror a length-normalized per-sequence ratio, and there is no per-sequence ratio here. My ratio is per-token, my clip is per-token, my gradient is per-token; the token-mean is the aggregation that matches. I pass `loss_agg_mode` straight through to `agg_loss` with the trainer's `global_batch_info` so the normalization is invariant to the data-parallel sharding, exactly as the vanilla loss does. If the trainer hands me `rollout_is_weights` — an outer correction for the rollout-engine-vs-training-engine likelihood mismatch — I multiply them onto the per-token losses before aggregation, since that correction is orthogonal to the IS clipping I am doing.

Now let me retrace the whole chain to be sure it holds, because the move is subtle and easy to get backwards. The PPO/GRPO clip bounds the influence of a large-ratio token by *zeroing its gradient* once the ratio leaves the band; the tokens that leave the band are disproportionately the rare, high-entropy fork tokens that drive reasoning, so the clip systematically deletes the signal I most need, and widening the band does not fix it because "rare" and "stale" are different axes. The danger a large ratio poses is variance, and variance is a property of the *magnitude of the coefficient*, not of whether the gradient is gated — so I want to bound the coefficient while keeping the gradient on every token. Rewriting the objective as IS-weighted REINFORCE, `E[ w Â ∇ log π_θ ]`, isolates the variance in the scalar `w` and removes any clipped surrogate that could zero a gradient. Bounding `w` by `clip(w, 1−ε_low, 1+ε_high)` caps the per-token coefficient, but to keep the cap from re-introducing a flat region I put the clipped weight inside a stop-gradient, so it bounds the forward coefficient while the gradient flows only through the live `log π_θ`. The resulting gradient is exactly bounded-coefficient REINFORCE — every token present, every coefficient capped — which keeps the rare reasoning tokens and controls the variance at the same time. The high clip edge is the variance knob (set generously, since exceeding it no longer drops the token); the low edge is nearly inert; numerics are guarded by a `[−20,20]` log-ratio clamp; diagnostics report the `k1` KL and the fraction of tokens whose weight was pinned; aggregation is the per-token token-mean that matches the per-token structure. The single design decision — clip the importance-sampling *weight* under a stop-gradient instead of clipping the *surrogate* — is what lets a clipped, off-policy objective keep every token's gradient. That is the contribution.
