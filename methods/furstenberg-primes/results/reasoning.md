I want infinitely many primes, and I already know one way to get them. Suppose only finitely many, $p_1,\dots,p_n$; form $N=p_1p_2\cdots p_n+1$; then $N>1$, so $N$ has a prime divisor, but dividing $N$ by any $p_i$ leaves remainder $1$, so that prime divisor is none of the $p_i$ — a new prime, contradiction. Clean, and it's been clean for two thousand years. So why am I sitting here. Because that argument is *arithmetic to the bone*: it manufactures a specific new number and inspects its divisibility. It tells me nothing about *why* the primes are inexhaustible beyond "you can always cook up one more." I want to know whether the infinitude is forced by some softer, more structural fact — something that doesn't depend on the clever choice of $N$ at all.

Let me look at the contrapositive shape of the problem. The primes are infinite iff... what's the cheapest restatement? An integer is divisible by *no* prime exactly when it's $\pm 1$. Every other integer — every $n$ with $|n|\ge 2$ — has a prime factor (take the least divisor of $|n|$ that exceeds $1$; it can't factor further, so it's prime), and $0$ is divisible by every prime. So the integers split as: the two units $\{-1,1\}$, which dodge all primes, and everyone else, who is caught by at least one prime. Write the "caught" set as a union over primes of the multiples of that prime:
$$\mathbb{Z}\setminus\{-1,1\}=\bigcup_{p\ \text{prime}}\, p\mathbb{Z}.$$
This is just bookkeeping so far, true whether there are finitely or infinitely many primes. But stare at it under the *assumption I want to refute*. If there were only finitely many primes, the right-hand side would be a **finite** union of sets of the form $p\mathbb{Z}$. And the left-hand side is the whole integer line with just two points poked out.

So the question becomes: is there something a *finite union* cannot do that the set $\mathbb{Z}\setminus\{-1,1\}$ nonetheless does? I'm hunting for a property $P$ such that (i) each $p\mathbb{Z}$ has $P$, (ii) finite unions of sets with $P$ still have $P$, and (iii) $\mathbb{Z}\setminus\{-1,1\}$ does *not* have $P$. If I can find such a $P$, then assuming finitely many primes makes the union have $P$ while equalling a set that lacks it — done, no number-cooking anywhere.

What kind of property is preserved by finite unions but not arbitrary ones, and is sensitive to the difference between "missing two points" and "missing infinitely many"? "Finite union preserves it, infinite union may break it" — that asymmetry is exactly the signature of **closedness** in a topology. Closed sets are closed under finite unions (and arbitrary intersections); the failure of the union over *all* primes to be closed, while each finite sub-union is, is precisely the kind of gap I'm describing. So let me gamble that $P$ = "is a closed set," for some topology on $\mathbb{Z}$ I get to design.

Now I'm a topology engineer with a wish list. I need a topology on $\mathbb{Z}$ in which each $p\mathbb{Z}$ is closed, so that the finite-primes union is closed, but $\mathbb{Z}\setminus\{-1,1\}$ is not closed.

That second condition is the same as saying its complement $\{-1,1\}$ is **not open**. So I need a topology where each $p\mathbb{Z}$ is closed and the particular two-point set $\{-1,1\}$ fails to be open. There's nothing special about $-1,1$ to the topology though; the cleanest way to forbid $\{-1,1\}$ from being open is to forbid *all* nonempty finite sets from being open. If I can arrange that **every nonempty open set is infinite**, then $\{-1,1\}$, having two elements, can't be open, and I'm past condition (iii) for free.

How do I force every nonempty open set to be infinite? An open set is a union of basic open sets, so it suffices that every *basic* open set is infinite — then any nonempty open set contains an infinite basic piece and is itself infinite. So I should build the topology from a basis of infinite sets. And I also want the $p\mathbb{Z}$, and more generally the multiple-type sets, to come out closed. The obvious infinite subsets of $\mathbb{Z}$ that are "made of multiples" are the arithmetic progressions. Let me just try declaring the arithmetic progressions to be the basic open sets and see if everything I need falls out — or breaks.

Define, for an integer $a\neq 0$ and any integer $b$,
$$S(a,b)=\{\,an+b : n\in\mathbb{Z}\,\}=a\mathbb{Z}+b,$$
the two-sided arithmetic progression through $b$ with common gap $a$. I'll call these the basic sets. Note right away $S(a,b)=S(-a,b)$, since $n\mapsto -n$ runs over $\mathbb{Z}$ either way — so I lose nothing by taking $a>0$ whenever it's convenient, and I'll do that to talk about "the modulus" $a$ without sign worries. Each $S(a,b)$ with $a\ne0$ is genuinely infinite: it's $\dots,b-a,\,b,\,b+a,\,b+2a,\dots$, running off to $\pm\infty$. Good, that's the infinitude-of-basics I wanted.

Before I get excited I have to actually check these form a basis — that the topology generated by calling every union of $S(a,b)$'s "open" really is a topology. The clause that bites is intersections: a topology must be closed under finite intersections, and a *union* of basic sets is closed under finite intersection only if the basic sets themselves interlock properly. The precise basis condition is: whenever a point $x$ lies in $S(a_1,b_1)\cap S(a_2,b_2)$, I must be able to slip a whole basic set through $x$ staying inside that intersection — find $a\ne0$ with $x\in S(a,x)\subseteq S(a_1,b_1)\cap S(a_2,b_2)$. (And the basis must cover $\mathbb{Z}$, which is immediate: any $x$ sits in $S(1,x)=\mathbb{Z}$, or for that matter in $S(a,x)$ for any $a$.)

So suppose $x\in S(a_1,b_1)\cap S(a_2,b_2)$. Saying $x\in S(a_i,b_i)$ just says $x\equiv b_i \pmod{a_i}$, i.e. $S(a_i,b_i)=S(a_i,x)$ — the progression with gap $a_i$ through $x$ is the same set whichever of its members I name it by. So I need $a$ with $S(a,x)\subseteq S(a_1,x)\cap S(a_2,x)$. The members of $S(a,x)$ are the integers $\equiv x \pmod a$; I want all of them to be $\equiv x \pmod{a_1}$ *and* $\equiv x\pmod{a_2}$. That holds exactly when $a_1\mid a$ and $a_2\mid a$. The cheapest such $a$ is $a=\operatorname{lcm}(|a_1|,|a_2|)$ — a positive integer, well-defined and nonzero because $a_1,a_2\ne0$. Then any $an+x$ is $a_1\!\cdot\!(n\,a/a_1)+x\in S(a_1,x)$, and likewise lands in $S(a_2,x)$, so $S(a,x)\subseteq S(a_1,x)\cap S(a_2,x)=S(a_1,b_1)\cap S(a_2,b_2)$, with $x\in S(a,x)$. The basis condition holds. So there's a genuine topology here whose open sets are exactly the unions of arithmetic progressions (with $\varnothing$ the empty union and $\mathbb{Z}=S(1,0)$ itself open).

Now condition (iii), which I'd already lined up: every nonempty open set, being a union of $S(a,b)$'s, contains at least one of them, which is infinite, so the open set is infinite. Hence no nonempty finite set is open. In particular $\{-1,1\}$ is not open, so $\mathbb{Z}\setminus\{-1,1\}$ is not closed. That's exactly the lack-of-$P$ I needed on the left-hand side.

The whole thing now hinges on the part I haven't checked: is each $p\mathbb{Z}=S(p,0)$ **closed**? This is where it could die. The basis was built to make these sets *open*; I deliberately put the arithmetic progressions in as open sets. So I'm asking whether a basic *open* set is also closed. That sounds greedy — in a typical topology open and closed are opposite flavors. Let me just compute the complement of $S(a,b)$ and see whether it's open.

Take $a>0$ (allowed, by $S(a,b)=S(-a,b)$). What's *not* in $S(a,b)$? An integer is in $S(a,b)$ iff it's $\equiv b\pmod a$. The residues mod $a$ are $b, b+1, b+2,\dots, b+(a-1)$ — that's a complete set of representatives, because as $j$ runs $0,1,\dots,a-1$ the value $b+j$ runs through every residue class exactly once (division algorithm: every integer is $\equiv b+j\pmod a$ for one and only one $j$ in that range). So the $a$ progressions
$$S(a,b),\,S(a,b+1),\,\dots,\,S(a,b+a-1)$$
are pairwise disjoint and together cover all of $\mathbb{Z}$ — they partition it. The ones other than $S(a,b)$ are exactly the integers *not* $\equiv b$, i.e. exactly the complement:
$$\mathbb{Z}\setminus S(a,b)=\bigcup_{j=1}^{a-1} S(a,b+j).$$
And that is a union of $(a-1)$ basic sets — an open set. (When $a=1$ the union is empty: $S(1,b)=\mathbb{Z}$, whose complement $\varnothing$ is indeed open. Consistent.) So the complement of every $S(a,b)$ is open, which means **every $S(a,b)$ is closed as well as open** — it's clopen. The greedy hope pays off: an arithmetic progression is simultaneously open and closed in this topology. The reason it doesn't feel paradoxical once I see it is that the complement of one residue class is just the *other* residue classes glued together, and those are progressions too.

That clears the last obstacle. Each $p\mathbb{Z}=S(p,0)$ is clopen, hence closed. So now assemble it. I have, as plain bookkeeping,
$$\mathbb{Z}\setminus\{-1,1\}=\bigcup_{p\ \text{prime}} S(p,0),$$
and I'll suppose, for contradiction, that there are only finitely many primes. Then the right-hand side is a *finite* union of closed sets, and a finite union of closed sets is closed — so $\mathbb{Z}\setminus\{-1,1\}$ is closed. But then its complement $\{-1,1\}$ would be open; and $\{-1,1\}$ is a nonempty finite set, while every nonempty open set in this topology is infinite. A nonempty finite set that is open cannot exist here. Contradiction. The only thing I assumed was finiteness of the primes, so that's false: there are infinitely many primes.

Let me walk back over the joints to make sure none of them quietly used what I was proving. The bookkeeping identity $\mathbb{Z}\setminus\{-1,1\}=\bigcup_p S(p,0)$ used only that every integer of absolute value $\ge 2$ has a prime factor and that $\pm1$ have none — no count of primes. The basis check used $\operatorname{lcm}$ of two fixed gaps, fine. "Nonempty open $\Rightarrow$ infinite" used only that the basics are infinite. "$S(a,b)$ closed" used only that the complement is the union of the *other* residue classes mod $a$ — a fixed finite count $a-1$ of progressions, for a single $a$, again independent of how many primes exist. The *only* place finiteness-of-primes enters is the single step "a finite union of closed sets is closed," applied to the union over all primes — and that step is exactly the one that breaks if the union is infinite. So the proof isolates the infinitude of primes as precisely the obstruction to that union being closed. No clever $N$, no manufactured integer: the primes have to be infinite because if they weren't, two innocent points would become an open set in a topology where openness demands infinitely many.

I topologize $\mathbb{Z}$ by taking the arithmetic progressions $S(a,b)=a\mathbb{Z}+b$ ($a\neq0$) as a basis; this is a basis because they cover $\mathbb{Z}$ and any point in the intersection of two of them carries a third progression, with gap $\operatorname{lcm}$ of the two gaps, inside the intersection. In the resulting topology every nonempty open set is infinite because it contains a progression, so no nonempty finite set is open. Each $S(a,b)$ is clopen, since its complement is the union of the remaining $a-1$ residue classes mod $a$, hence open. Now $\mathbb{Z}\setminus\{-1,1\}=\bigcup_{p}S(p,0)$. Were the primes finite, this would exhibit $\mathbb{Z}\setminus\{-1,1\}$ as a finite union of closed sets, hence closed, forcing $\{-1,1\}$ to be open, impossible for a nonempty finite set. Hence the primes are infinite.
