I start with the treated aggregate unit and ask what I actually need to know after the intervention begins. The observed outcome is available, but the untreated outcome for that same unit is missing. Calling the gap a treatment effect does not solve the problem, because the main work is to construct a credible version of the path that would have appeared without treatment.

I first try the familiar comparative case-study move. I look for another city, state, country, or region that resembles the treated one, and I compare their paths before and after the event. This is attractive because it is concrete, but it breaks down as soon as every possible comparison unit is wrong in a different way. One candidate may have the right industrial structure but the wrong pre-treatment trend. Another may have the right trend but the wrong demographics. Choosing one of them asks too much from a single imperfect control.

I then consider averaging untreated units. That gives me a more stable comparison, but an ordinary average treats all donors as equally relevant. Equal weighting is a design assumption, not a discovered fact, and it is rarely believable when the treated unit is distinctive. I can also turn to regression, but unrestricted regression worries me for a reason I should pin down rather than wave at. The fear is that a good numerical fit becomes a bad counterfactual when it is built from extrapolation. Let me make that concrete before I trust the intuition. Suppose I have a single predictor and three donors with values 2, 5, and 9, and a treated unit at 12. If I let weights be arbitrary real numbers, I can put weight 12/9 = 1.333 on the donor at 9 and reproduce 12 exactly, with zero pre-fit error. But 1.333 is not a weight any real mixture of donors can carry; nothing in my data sits at 12, and I have invented a comparison out of thin air. The perfect fit is the symptom, not the cure.

So I ask what restriction would have stopped that. If I require the weights to be nonnegative and sum to one, the synthetic predictor becomes a convex combination of the donor values, and a convex combination of {2, 5, 9} is a linear function over the simplex, which is maximized at a vertex. The most I can reach is 9. The treated value 12 is then unreachable, and the best I can do leaves an irreducible pre-fit gap of 12 - 9 = 3. That is exactly the behavior I want: the restriction cannot manufacture a fit that the donor pool does not support, and when the treated unit lies outside the donor range, the failure shows up as a visible gap rather than as a confident-looking coefficient. The convex restriction turns extrapolation from a hidden risk into an observable diagnostic.

That reframes the whole problem. I am no longer choosing a control or fitting a coefficient; I am composing the counterfactual out of actual untreated units. I keep the untreated units as the raw material, assign each donor a weight, require every weight to be nonnegative, and require the weights to sum to one. The weights are no longer just regression coefficients. They describe the mixture of real donors that defines the comparison object, and I can read them off and report them.

I choose the composition using only pre-treatment information. I collect predictors and selected pre-treatment outcomes for the treated unit in `X1`, collect the same quantities for the donor units in `X0`, and search for weights `W` that make `X0 W` close to `X1`. A predictor-weight matrix `V` decides which discrepancies matter most. For a given `V`, I minimize `(X1 - X0 W)' V (X1 - X0 W)` subject to `w_j >= 0` and `sum_j w_j = 1`. I can choose `V` by asking which predictor weighting gives the best pre-treatment outcome fit, either on the same pre-treatment outcome window or on a held-out validation part of the pre-treatment history.

When I turn the inner problem into an algorithm, I have to keep the signs straight, so I expand the objective by hand. With `V` symmetric, `(X1 - X0 W)' V (X1 - X0 W) = X1' V X1 - 2 X1' V X0 W + W' X0' V X0 W`. The first term is a constant in `W` and can be dropped. The quadratic term has matrix `H = X0' V X0`, and the linear term has coefficient `-X1' V X0`; the minus sign is the part I am most likely to get wrong, because it is the term that pulls the synthetic predictor toward `X1` rather than away from it. Let me check the expansion numerically before I commit to it: with random `X1`, `X0`, a diagonal `V`, and an arbitrary `W` on the simplex, the assembled `X1'VX1 - 2 X1'VX0 W + W'X0'VX0 W` matches the raw `(X1 - X0 W)'V(X1 - X0 W)` to machine precision, and rebuilding it from `H` and `c = -X1'VX0` reproduces the same value. So `H = X0' V X0` and the linear term `c = -X1' V X0` are the right pieces, the constant is safe to discard, and a positive rescaling of the objective leaves the minimizer alone — which I also confirm by multiplying the loss by 7 and recovering the identical argmin. For a diagonal `V`, this is a constrained quadratic program over donor weights. The outcome-fit loss that judges a candidate `V` is the mean squared pre-treatment gap between the treated outcome matrix `Z1` and the donor outcome matrix `Z0 W`.

Before I trust any of this, I want to watch it run on a case where I know the answer. I build a treated unit that is, by construction, an exact convex mix of three donors over four pre-treatment periods with weights `(0.5, 0.3, 0.2)`, then add a known post-treatment shock of `(+4.0, -1.5)` to its two post periods. Solving the constrained QP on the pre-treatment outcomes recovers the weights `(0.5, 0.3, 0.2)` with a pre-fit MSPE on the order of `1e-17`, and the post-treatment gaps, computed as treated minus weighted donors, come back as `(4.0, -1.5)`. So when the treated unit really does sit inside the donor hull, the procedure finds the composition that put it there and then reads the injected effect straight off the post-treatment gap. That is the behavior the design promises, and now I have seen it rather than supposed it.

The procedure makes the counterfactual visible, because I can report exactly which donors enter and with what weights, and it keeps the counterfactual inside the convex hull of the donor pool, which is the property I checked above. If the treated unit cannot be approximated before treatment, the bad fit is visible before I estimate the effect, the way the value 12 outside `[2, 9]` left a gap of 3 that no choice of convex weights could close. The method does not hide that failure behind a coefficient table.

A subtler reason to lean on long pre-treatment outcome histories is that observed covariates are not enough. Untreated outcomes may depend on unobserved unit features whose effects change over time. A simple parallel-trends design handles only a narrower version of that problem. If the weighted donors reproduce the treated unit's pre-treatment outcome path for many periods — not just one or two, which a flexible enough mixture could match by accident — they are also indirectly matching the latent features that drive that path. The pre-treatment trajectory becomes evidence about hidden determinants, not just a diagnostic plot.

Once I have the weights, the post-treatment estimate is direct. For each period after the intervention, I compute the treated outcome minus the weighted donor outcome. A positive gap means the treated outcome is above its estimated no-treatment path, and a negative gap means it is below that path. I then ask whether this gap is large relative to placebo gaps obtained by pretending each donor was treated in turn, and I judge the result together with pre-treatment fit. The logic stays design-based: the claim is credible only if the donor pool is valid, the pre-treatment approximation is strong, and the post-treatment gap is unusual compared with comparable placebo exercises.
