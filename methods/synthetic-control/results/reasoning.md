I start with the treated aggregate unit and ask what I actually need to know after the intervention begins. The observed outcome is available, but the untreated outcome for that same unit is missing. Calling the gap a treatment effect does not solve the problem, because the main work is to construct a credible version of the path that would have appeared without treatment.

I first try the familiar comparative case-study move. I look for another city, state, country, or region that resembles the treated one, and I compare their paths before and after the event. This is attractive because it is concrete, but it breaks down as soon as every possible comparison unit is wrong in a different way. One candidate may have the right industrial structure but the wrong pre-treatment trend. Another may have the right trend but the wrong demographics. Choosing one of them asks too much from a single imperfect control.

I then consider averaging untreated units. That gives me a more stable comparison, but an ordinary average treats all donors as equally relevant. Equal weighting is a design assumption, not a discovered fact, and it is rarely believable when the treated unit is distinctive. I can also turn to regression, but unrestricted regression is too free for the task. It may fit the treated unit by assigning negative weights, large positive weights, or an intercept that shifts the comparison outside the support of the actual untreated cases. A good numerical fit can become a bad counterfactual if it is built from extrapolation that no real combination of donors represents.

The useful step is to treat the counterfactual as a composed case. I keep the untreated units as the raw material, but I search for a donor composition that looks like the treated unit before treatment. I assign each donor a weight, require every weight to be nonnegative, and require the weights to sum to one. Those restrictions make the comparison a convex combination of actual untreated units. The weights are no longer just regression coefficients. They describe the mixture of real donors that defines the comparison object.

I choose the composition using only pre-treatment information. I collect predictors and selected pre-treatment outcomes for the treated unit in `X1`, collect the same quantities for the donor units in `X0`, and search for weights `W` that make `X0 W` close to `X1`. A predictor-weight matrix `V` decides which discrepancies matter most. For a given `V`, I minimize `(X1 - X0 W)' V (X1 - X0 W)` subject to `w_j >= 0` and `sum_j w_j = 1`. I can choose `V` by asking which predictor weighting gives the best pre-treatment outcome fit, either on the same pre-treatment outcome window or on a held-out validation part of the pre-treatment history.

When I turn this into an algorithm, I have to keep the signs straight. Expanding the squared predictor distance gives a constant in `X1`, a quadratic term in `W`, and a negative linear term from matching `X0 W` to `X1`. The constant can be dropped, and multiplying the quadratic objective by a positive factor does not change the minimizer, but the minus sign in the linear term is essential. For a diagonal `V`, the computation is a constrained quadratic program over donor weights. The outcome-fit loss that judges a candidate `V` is the mean squared pre-treatment gap between the treated outcome matrix `Z1` and the donor outcome matrix `Z0 W`.

This solves two problems at once. It makes the counterfactual visible, because I can report exactly which donors enter and with what weights. It also blocks the most troubling extrapolation, because the counterfactual stays inside the convex hull of the donor pool. If the treated unit cannot be approximated before treatment, the bad fit is visible before I estimate the effect. The method does not hide that failure behind a coefficient table.

The deeper reason to use long pre-treatment outcome histories is that observed covariates are not enough. Untreated outcomes may depend on unobserved unit features whose effects change over time. A simple parallel-trends design handles only a narrower version of that problem. If the weighted donors reproduce the treated unit's pre-treatment outcome path for many periods, they are also indirectly matching the latent features that drive that path. The pre-treatment trajectory becomes evidence about hidden determinants, not just a diagnostic plot.

Once I have the weights, the post-treatment estimate is direct. For each period after the intervention, I compute the treated outcome minus the weighted donor outcome. A positive gap means the treated outcome is above its estimated no-treatment path, and a negative gap means it is below that path. I then ask whether this gap is large relative to placebo gaps obtained by pretending each donor was treated in turn, and I judge the result together with pre-treatment fit. The logic stays design-based: the claim is credible only if the donor pool is valid, the pre-treatment approximation is strong, and the post-treatment gap is unusual compared with comparable placebo exercises.
