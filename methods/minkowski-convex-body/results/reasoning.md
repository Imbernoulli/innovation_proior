Let me start from the thing that keeps coming up. I keep proving the same kind of statement by different tricks: a positive-definite quadratic form `Q` in `n` variables always takes a value below a determinant-only bound at some nonzero integer point. Hermite has this — in his letters to Jacobi in Crelle's volume 40 he shows you can substitute integers, not all zero, so that `Q(x)` stays under a bound depending only on `det Q`. It's a workhorse: reduction of forms, finiteness of class numbers, the whole business leans on it. And I have my own proof of it now, cleaner than the reduction-theory route. But the proof is *algebraic*. I keep grinding the Gram matrix, reducing the form, and out drops a constant `cₙ` that I don't even believe is the best possible. It feels like I'm computing my way to an answer that ought to have a reason.

So let me look at what Hermite's statement *is*, geometrically. `Q(x) ≤ c` is the inside of an ellipsoid. "There is a nonzero integer point with `Q(x)` below the bound" is just: *this ellipsoid, once it's big enough, swallows an integer point other than the origin.* That's a picture, not an algebra problem. Dirichlet was already thinking this way about ternary forms in that same Crelle 40 — reduction as a geometric act. Let me take the picture seriously and ask: an ellipsoid of large enough volume contains a nonzero integer point. Why?

Now I want to stare at my own proof of Hermite's bound and ask which properties of the ellipsoid it actually uses. I go through it line by line. Where do I use that the boundary is a *quadric*? Where do I use the *algebra* of `Q`? And the honest answer, when I track it, is: I don't. What I use is that the region `{Q ≤ c}` is convex — it has no dents, segments between its points stay inside — and that it is symmetric about its center, `x` in means `−x` in. The quadratic-ness is scaffolding I can kick away. Hermite's theorem isn't really about ellipsoids. It's about *any* region that is convex and centrally symmetric.

That reframing is worth everything, because it does two opposite-feeling things at once. It generalizes — now I have one statement covering an infinite family of shapes, ellipsoids and cubes and octahedra and anything convex with a center — and it *simplifies*, because the moment I stop insisting on the quadratic form I stop fighting its algebra. The shape is now described by two soft geometric words, convex and symmetric, and a number, its volume. Let me chase that: a convex, centrally-symmetric body `C` with center at the origin and large enough volume must contain a nonzero lattice point. How large is "large enough," and what's the argument that has nothing to do with quadratics?

First let me make sure the soft hypotheses are both needed, so I know I'm not carrying dead weight. Drop convexity: a thin star or a needle of huge area can thread between the lattice lines and miss every integer point but the origin — volume alone can't force anything for a wild shape. So convexity is load-bearing. Drop central symmetry: I can take a convex region of large volume and shove it off-center so its only lattice point is the origin sitting at a corner — symmetry has to matter too. Good. So the right object really is *convex and centrally symmetric*, and I should expect the proof to *use both*, each in an essential place. If a proof uses only one of them, I've either over- or under-assumed.

Now the volume threshold. Let me get a feel for it on the cleanest case, `Zⁿ` and the cube. The cube `{max|xᵢ| < 1}`, centered at the origin, has edge `2` and volume `2ⁿ`. Does it contain a nonzero integer point? Every nonzero integer vector has some coordinate of absolute value at least `1`, so it sits on the boundary or outside — *not strictly inside*. So the open cube of volume `2ⁿ` contains no nonzero lattice point. And if I shrink it a hair, edge `2 − ε`, volume `(2−ε)ⁿ`, still nothing inside. That's a hard fact about the design space: whatever the threshold is, for `Zⁿ` it cannot be below `2ⁿ`, because here is a convex symmetric body of volume just under `2ⁿ` that dodges the lattice. So the threshold, if there is one, is `2ⁿ` — and the cube shows it's sharp. For a general lattice `L` with covolume `d(L)`, the same example scaled says the threshold should be `2ⁿ d(L)`.

So my conjecture is: **`vol(C) > 2ⁿ d(L)` forces a nonzero lattice point of `L` inside `C`.** Now I need the reason, and it must use volume, convexity, and symmetry, and it must produce the `2ⁿ` honestly.

Where could `2ⁿ` come from? It's `2ⁿ` per dimension, a factor of `2` in each coordinate. A factor of `2`... what if I *halve* the body. Consider `½C = {½x : x ∈ C}`. Its volume is `2⁻ⁿ vol(C)`. If `vol(C) > 2ⁿ d(L)`, then `vol(½C) > d(L)`. So `½C` has more volume than one fundamental cell of the lattice. That number — "more volume than a single cell" — is exactly the smell of pigeonhole. Dirichlet's box principle: too much stuff for the boxes available, two things collide.

Let me try to run a pigeonhole on `½C` against the lattice. The translates `{u + F : u ∈ L}` of the fundamental cell `F` tile all of space and are disjoint. So I can cut `½C` into the pieces it has in each cell: write `S = ½C`, and `Sᵤ = S ∩ (u + F)` for each `u ∈ L`. These pieces are disjoint and their union is `S`, so `Σᵤ vol(Sᵤ) = vol(S) > d(L) = vol(F)`. Now here's the fold: translate each piece *back into the home cell* by `−u`. The piece `Sᵤ − u` sits inside `F` (because `Sᵤ ⊆ u + F`), and translation doesn't change volume, so `Σᵤ vol(Sᵤ − u) = Σᵤ vol(Sᵤ) > vol(F)`. But all these translated pieces live inside one cell `F`, whose total volume is `d(L)`, and they sum to *more* than `d(L)`. They cannot be pairwise disjoint — there isn't room. So two distinct cells, say `u ≠ v`, have overlapping folded images: there's a point `a` in `(Sᵤ − u) ∩ (Sᵥ − v)`.

Unfold: `a + u =: x` is in `Sᵤ ⊆ S`, and `a + v =: y` is in `Sᵥ ⊆ S`. And their difference is `x − y = (a + u) − (a + v) = u − v`, which is a *nonzero* lattice vector since `u ≠ v` are both in `L`. So I've produced two distinct points `x, y ∈ S = ½C` whose difference is a nonzero lattice point. This is the continuous pigeonhole: more area than one cell can hold forces two points of `S` onto the same residue mod `L`, and the lattice vector is hiding in their *difference*. Notice I get the difference, not `x` or `y` individually — `x` and `y` are generic points, but `x − y ∈ L`. The lattice information is entirely in the collision.

I have `x − y ∈ L \ {0}` with `x, y ∈ ½C`. But I wanted a nonzero lattice point *inside `C`*, and `x − y` is so far just a lattice vector — why is it in `C`? `x ∈ ½C` means `2x ∈ C`. `y ∈ ½C` means `2y ∈ C`. Now I want to manufacture `x − y` out of points of `C`. Write `x − y = ½(2x) + ½(−2y)`. For this to be a convex combination of points of `C`, I need `2x ∈ C` (yes) and `−2y ∈ C`. And `−2y ∈ C` is exactly **central symmetry**: `2y ∈ C ⇒ −2y ∈ C`. There it is — symmetry is what supplies the second endpoint. Then `x − y = ½(2x) + ½(−2y)` is the *midpoint* of `2x` and `−2y`, two points of `C`, and **convexity** says the midpoint of two points of `C` is in `C`. So `x − y ∈ C`. Both hypotheses used, each exactly once, each irreplaceable: symmetry to get `−2y` into `C`, convexity to put the midpoint in `C`. And the halving is what married them — I scaled by `½` precisely so that `2x, 2y` land back in `C` and the difference comes out as a midpoint rather than something at scale `2` that would overshoot `C`.

So: `vol(C) > 2ⁿ d(L)` gives a nonzero lattice point `x − y ∈ C`. The `2ⁿ` is no longer mysterious. It's the cost of halving: I had to shrink the body by `2` in each of `n` directions so that `½C` could overflow one fundamental cell, and overflowing the cell is what the pigeonhole needs. The fold demands `vol(½C) > d(L)`, i.e. `2⁻ⁿ vol(C) > d(L)`, i.e. `vol(C) > 2ⁿ d(L)`. And the cube example already told me this `2ⁿ` is exactly the right constant, not improvable. The two ends meet.

Let me write the fold cleanly as its own lemma, because it stands on its own and uses *neither* convexity nor symmetry — it's pure measure and lattice. **If `S` is any measurable set with `vol(S) > d(L)`, then there exist distinct `x, y ∈ S` with `x − y ∈ L`.** Proof exactly as above: partition `S` by the tiling, fold each piece into `F`, sum of volumes exceeds `vol(F)`, so two folded pieces overlap, and the difference of the two unfolded preimages is a nonzero lattice vector. This is the engine. The convex-body theorem is then the corollary I just derived: apply the fold to `S = ½C`, then use symmetry + convexity to land the difference back in `C`.

Let me double check the threshold direction in the lemma, because off-by-one in a strict inequality would be fatal. The folded pieces total volume `> vol(F)`. If they were disjoint they'd fit in `F` with total volume `≤ vol(F)` — contradiction. So they overlap. Yes, the `>` is what I need, and it's strict. Good. And I should be honest that the lemma gives a *strict* threshold; equality is a separate question I'll come back to.

Now I'm slightly bothered. The fold gives me existence, but it throws away quantitative control — I get *a* nonzero lattice point, but not how *deep* inside `C` it sits, and not the sharp relationship between the lattice's shortest vector and the body's volume. Hermite's whole point was a numerical bound, `min Q ≤ cₙ (det)^{1/n}`. Can I get the sharp constant out of this circle of ideas, not just existence? Let me try a second route that I think will be more quantitative, built directly on packing instead of folding, and see if it gives the threshold *and* the boundary case for free.

Here is the packing idea. Forget cutting `C`; instead reason about the minimum distance of the lattice *measured in the body's own gauge*. A convex symmetric body `C` is the unit ball of a norm — call it `f`, the gauge: `f(x) = min{λ ≥ 0 : x ∈ λC}`, so `{f < 1}` is the interior of `C` and `vol{f < 1} = vol(C) =: J`. The gauge has exactly the three properties I keep needing: `f(tx) = t f(x)` for `t > 0` (homogeneity, from dilation), `f(x + y) ≤ f(x) + f(y)` (the triangle inequality — this *is* convexity), and `f(−x) = f(x)` (central symmetry). Everything about `C` is in `f`. Let `M = min_{l ∈ L\{0}} f(l)` be the smallest gauge-length of a nonzero lattice vector. (It's attained: nonzero gauge-distances from a point are bounded below by a positive constant times Euclidean distance, so only finitely many lattice points lie within any gauge-radius, and the minimum over that finite set is achieved. Existence of a positive lower bound on the distance coefficients is what makes the lattice discrete in this gauge in the first place.)

Now around every lattice point `a` put the body of gauge-radius `M/2`: `Bₐ = {x : f(x − a) < M/2}`. These are pairwise disjoint. Suppose `b` were interior to two of them, `b ∈ Bₐ ∩ B_c` with `a ≠ c`. Then by the triangle inequality `f(a − c) ≤ f(a − b) + f(b − c) < M/2 + M/2 = M`. But `a − c` is a nonzero lattice vector, so `f(a − c) ≥ M` by minimality. Contradiction. So the `M/2`-bodies are disjoint — they *pack*. (This used the triangle inequality, i.e. convexity, and that `M` is the minimum.)

Now count volume in a big box. Take an even integer `Ω` and all lattice points whose coordinates are among `0, ±1, …, ±Ω/2` — there are `(Ω + 1)ⁿ` of them. Each `M/2`-body around such a point is contained, after accounting for its own gauge-radius, in a fixed cube centered at the origin whose edge is `Ω` plus a bounded correction that does not grow with `Ω`. The `(Ω+1)ⁿ` bodies are disjoint, so the volume of that cube is at least the sum of their volumes:

`(Ω + correction)ⁿ ≥ (Ω + 1)ⁿ · vol(M/2-body) = (Ω + 1)ⁿ · (M/2)ⁿ · J`,

since `vol{f < M/2} = (M/2)ⁿ vol{f < 1} = (M/2)ⁿ J` by homogeneity of the gauge. Divide by `Ωⁿ` and let `Ω → ∞` through even integers. The left side `(Ω + correction)ⁿ / Ωⁿ → 1`; the factor `(Ω + 1)ⁿ / Ωⁿ → 1`. So in the limit

`1 ≥ (M/2)ⁿ J`, i.e. `Mⁿ J ≤ 2ⁿ`.

That's the sharp inequality, and it's *quantitative*: `M ≤ 2 / J^{1/n} = 2 (vol C)^{−1/n}` (for `Zⁿ`; with `d(L)` it's `M ≤ 2 (d(L)/J)^{1/n}`). Read it back as existence: if `J = vol(C) = 2ⁿ`, then `M ≤ 1`, so there is a nonzero lattice point with gauge-distance `≤ 1`, i.e. in the closed body `C`. If `J > 2ⁿ`, then `M < 1` strictly, so a nonzero lattice point sits strictly inside `C`. This is the same `2ⁿ` again, now falling out of a packing volume count, and it hands me the *equality* case directly: the closed (compact) body of volume *exactly* `2ⁿ d(L)` already contains a nonzero lattice point, because `Mⁿ J ≤ 2ⁿ` gives `M ≤ 1` at `J = 2ⁿ`, not `M < 1`. The fold lemma only gave me strict `>`; the packing argument is sharper at the boundary.

Let me reconcile the two proofs on the boundary, because the open cube is nagging me. The open cube of volume exactly `2ⁿ` contains *no* nonzero lattice point. So the statement "`vol(C) = 2ⁿ ⇒ nonzero lattice point inside`" is false for an *open* body. How does the packing argument escape this? It gives `M ≤ 1`, i.e. a lattice point at gauge-distance `≤ 1` — that point lies in the *closed* body `{f ≤ 1}`, possibly on the boundary. For the open cube, the nonzero lattice points sit exactly *on* the boundary (a coordinate equal to `1`), at gauge-distance exactly `1`, consistent with `M = 1`; they're in the closed cube but not the open one. So the correct equality statement needs `C` closed: **for `C` compact (closed and bounded) convex symmetric, `vol(C) ≥ 2ⁿ d(L)` forces a nonzero lattice point of `L` in `C`.** The open version needs strict `>`. The cube is the witness that you can't have both equality and openness.

I can also get the equality case from the fold lemma by a limiting argument, which is reassuring and uses only closedness, so let me do it. Suppose `vol(C) = 2ⁿ d(L)` and `C` is closed, and suppose for contradiction `C` contains no nonzero lattice point. For each integer `m ≥ 1`, the dilated body `(1 + 1/m)C` has volume `(1 + 1/m)ⁿ · 2ⁿ d(L) > 2ⁿ d(L)`, so by the strict theorem it contains a nonzero lattice point `xₘ`. All the `xₘ` lie in `2C` (since `1 + 1/m ≤ 2`), and `(2C) ∩ L` is a *finite* set (it's bounded and `L` is discrete). So infinitely many of the `xₘ` coincide: there's a single nonzero `x ∈ L` with `x ∈ (1 + 1/m)C` for infinitely many `m`, i.e. `(1 + 1/m)⁻¹ x ∈ C` for infinitely many `m`. Let `m → ∞`: `(1 + 1/m)⁻¹ x → x`, and since `C` is closed it contains the limit `x`. So `x ∈ C` is a nonzero lattice point — contradiction. Hence the equality case holds for closed `C`. Both routes agree, and both pin closedness as exactly the hypothesis that licenses equality.

Let me sanity-check the sharp constant on a known case, the plane with the Euclidean disk, against the cube bound, because I want to be sure the `2ⁿ` is the right *shape-independent* constant and to see how much the shape buys. For the cube `{max|xᵢ| < ℓ}` of volume `(2ℓ)ⁿ`: applying the theorem, if `(2ℓ)ⁿ > 2ⁿ d(L)` there's a nonzero lattice point inside, so the shortest lattice vector in the `ℓ∞`-norm satisfies `‖x‖_∞ ≤ d(L)^{1/n}`. That's a clean determinant-only bound, the geometry-of-numbers form of Hermite, for *every* lattice and with the cube as the test body — no quadratic algebra anywhere. Converting to Euclidean length, `‖x‖₂ ≤ √n ‖x‖_∞ ≤ √n · d(L)^{1/n}`, so the Hermite constant satisfies `γₙ ≤ n`. In 2D the cube argument gives `γ₂ ≤ n = 2`. Now use a disk instead of a square in the plane: if the open disk `{‖x‖₂ < λ}` contains no nonzero lattice point, its area `πλ²` can be at most `2² d(L) = 4 d(L)` by the theorem, so `λ² ≤ 4 d(L)/π` and `γ₂ = (λ/d(L)^{1/2})² ≤ 4/π ≈ 1.27`, better than the square's `2`. So the body's shape sharpens the constant — using a ball instead of a cube gains a dimension-independent factor — but the *threshold* `2ⁿ d(L)` is universal across all convex symmetric shapes. That's the right division of labor: the theorem provides the universal `2ⁿ`, and choosing the test body tunes the constant for a particular quantity. (The true `γ₂ = 2/√3 ≈ 1.15`, achieved by the hexagonal lattice, is sharper still, but `4/π` is what the disk gives directly.)

Now let me cash this out as the analytic statement I started from, to make sure I've actually recaptured and surpassed Hermite. Phrase everything through the gauge `f`. The body `{f < r} = rC` has volume `rⁿ J`. It contains a nonzero lattice point as soon as `rⁿ J > 2ⁿ d(L)`, i.e. `r > 2 (d(L)/J)^{1/n}`. So the minimum gauge-value over nonzero lattice points obeys `min_{l ∈ L\0} f(l) ≤ 2 (d(L)/J)^{1/n}`. For a positive-definite quadratic form take `f = √Q`; then `{f < 1} = {Q < 1}` is an ellipsoid, `J = vol{Q < 1}`, and the bound becomes `min_{Zⁿ\0} Q = (min f)² ≤ 4 (J)^{−2/n}` — Hermite's "value below a determinant-only bound" with a *clean geometric constant* tied to the volume of the unit-`Q` ellipsoid, recovered as the special case `C =` ellipsoid. So the abstraction didn't lose Hermite; it *contains* Hermite as one shape among infinitely many, with a transparent constant, and it works for cubes, octahedra, anything convex and symmetric. That's exactly the fruitfulness I was hoping the geometric picture would buy.

Let me also make sure the theorem actually delivers on the Diophantine problems, because existence-of-a-lattice-point is only useful if I can dress the problems as bodies. The linear-forms problem: I want a nonzero integer `x` with `|Lᵢ(x)| ≤ Aᵢ` for forms `Lᵢ(x) = Σⱼ bᵢⱼ xⱼ`, `det(B) ≠ 0`, and `∏ Aᵢ ≥ |det B|`. The region `P = {x : |Lᵢ(x)| ≤ Aᵢ for all i}` is `B⁻¹R` where `R = {|yᵢ| ≤ Aᵢ}` is a box of volume `2ⁿ ∏ Aᵢ`. So `vol(P) = |det B|⁻¹ · 2ⁿ ∏ Aᵢ ≥ 2ⁿ`. `P` is convex (linear preimage of a box) and centrally symmetric, so by the theorem (compact version, `≥ 2ⁿ`) it contains a nonzero integer point — there is the simultaneous bound. From this, Dirichlet's simultaneous approximation falls out: apply it to the forms `Xᵢ − αᵢ X_{n+1}` and `X_{n+1}` (determinant `1`) with bounds `Q^{−1/n}` and `Q`, getting integers with `|xᵢ − αᵢ q| ≤ Q^{−1/n}` and `0 < |q| ≤ Q`, hence `|αᵢ − xᵢ/q| ≤ q^{−1−1/n}`, for every `Q`, and letting `Q → ∞` gives infinitely many. And the sum-of-squares facts: for a prime `p ≡ 1 (mod 4)` pick `r` with `r² ≡ −1 (mod p)`, and apply the theorem to the ellipse `{(pz₁ + rz₂)² + z₂² < R²}` of area `πR²/p`; choosing `R² = 2p/π · (something > 1)` — concretely area `> 4` — forces a nonzero integer `(z₁, z₂)` with `0 < (pz₁ + rz₂)² + z₂² < 2p`, and the congruence `(pz₁ + rz₂)² + z₂² ≡ (r² + 1) z₂² ≡ 0 (mod p)` pins the value to exactly `p`, so `p` is a sum of two squares. The same machine, with a four-dimensional region and `r² + s² + 1 ≡ 0 (mod p)`, gives Lagrange's four-square theorem. Every one of these was a separate clever construction before; now each is "write the region, check its volume passes `2ⁿ d(L)`, read off the integer point."

Let me state the result cleanly and assemble the full proof, both routes, since I want the conceptual fold *and* the sharp packing constant in the record.

**Theorem (convex body theorem).** Let `L ⊂ Rⁿ` be a full lattice with covolume `d(L)`, and let `C ⊂ Rⁿ` be convex and symmetric about the origin.
(i) If `vol(C) > 2ⁿ d(L)`, then `C` contains a nonzero point of `L`.
(ii) If in addition `C` is compact, then `vol(C) ≥ 2ⁿ d(L)` already suffices.
The constant `2ⁿ d(L)` is best possible: the open cube of edge `2` (volume `2ⁿ`, taking `L = Zⁿ`) contains no nonzero lattice point, and shrinking it to volume `(2 − ε)ⁿ` still contains none.

**Proof.**
*Fold lemma.* If `S ⊆ Rⁿ` is measurable with `vol(S) > d(L)`, there are distinct `x, y ∈ S` with `x − y ∈ L`. The translates `{u + F : u ∈ L}` of the fundamental cell `F` tile `Rⁿ` disjointly. Cut `S` into `Sᵤ = S ∩ (u + F)`; these are disjoint with union `S`, so `Σᵤ vol(Sᵤ) = vol(S) > vol(F) = d(L)`. Translate each into the home cell: `Sᵤ − u ⊆ F`, same volume, total `> vol(F)`. As they can't be disjoint inside `F`, some `a ∈ (Sᵤ − u) ∩ (Sᵥ − v)` with `u ≠ v`. Then `x := a + u, y := a + v ∈ S` are distinct and `x − y = u − v ∈ L \ {0}`. ∎

*Part (i).* Let `S = ½C`. Then `vol(S) = 2⁻ⁿ vol(C) > d(L)`, so the fold gives distinct `x, y ∈ ½C` with `x − y ∈ L \ {0}`. Now `2x ∈ C`; by central symmetry `2y ∈ C ⇒ −2y ∈ C`; by convexity the midpoint `x − y = ½(2x) + ½(−2y) ∈ C`. So `x − y` is a nonzero lattice point in `C`. ∎

*Part (ii).* Suppose `C` is compact with `vol(C) = 2ⁿ d(L)` and contains no nonzero lattice point. For each `m ≥ 1`, `vol((1 + 1/m)C) = (1 + 1/m)ⁿ · 2ⁿ d(L) > 2ⁿ d(L)`, so by (i) there is a nonzero lattice point `xₘ ∈ (1 + 1/m)C ⊆ 2C`. Since `(2C) ∩ L` is finite, some nonzero `x ∈ L` equals `xₘ` for infinitely many `m`, so `(1 + 1/m)⁻¹ x ∈ C` for infinitely many `m`; letting `m → ∞` and using that `C` is closed, `x ∈ C` — contradiction. ∎

*Sharp packing form (with the gauge).* Let `f` be the gauge of `C`, so `vol{f < r} = rⁿ vol(C)`, and `M = min_{l ∈ L\0} f(l)` (attained, by discreteness). The bodies `Bₐ = {x : f(x − a) < M/2}`, `a ∈ L`, are pairwise disjoint: if `b ∈ Bₐ ∩ B_c` with `a ≠ c`, then `f(a − c) ≤ f(a − b) + f(b − c) < M`, impossible. Packing `(Ω + 1)ⁿ` of them (lattice points with coordinates in `{0, ±1, …, ±Ω/2}`, `Ω` even) into a cube of edge `Ω + O(1)` and letting `Ω → ∞` gives `(M/2)ⁿ vol(C) ≤ d(L)`, i.e. `Mⁿ vol(C) ≤ 2ⁿ d(L)`. Hence `vol(C) ≥ 2ⁿ d(L) ⇒ M ≤ 1` (nonzero lattice point in the closed `C`), and `vol(C) > 2ⁿ d(L) ⇒ M < 1` (one strictly inside). This is parts (ii) and (i) again, with the sharp quantitative bound `min_{l ∈ L\0} f(l) ≤ 2 (d(L)/vol(C))^{1/n}`. ∎

To recap the chain in one breath: I started from Hermite's determinant-only bound on quadratic forms, read it as "a big enough ellipsoid catches a lattice point," noticed the proof never used the quadratic shape — only convexity and central symmetry — and so generalized to any convex symmetric body. The cube of volume `2ⁿ` fixed the threshold at `2ⁿ d(L)` and showed it sharp. A factor `2` per dimension said *halve the body*; halving made `½C` overflow one fundamental cell; overflow is pigeonhole, which folds two points of `½C` onto the same lattice residue, and the lattice vector is their difference; central symmetry supplies `−2y ∈ C` and convexity lands the midpoint `x − y` back in `C`. A packing of `M/2`-bodies in the body's own gauge gives the same `2ⁿ` quantitatively and handles the compact equality case, with closedness exactly the hypothesis that licenses equality. The whole of Hermite returns as the ellipsoid special case `f = √Q`, and the linear-forms theorem, Dirichlet's simultaneous approximation, and the sum-of-squares theorems all become "write the region, check its volume clears `2ⁿ d(L)`, read off the integer point."
