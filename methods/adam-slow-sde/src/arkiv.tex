\begin{abstract}
    Despite the popularity of the Adam optimizer in practice, most theoretical analyses study Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) as a proxy for Adam, and little is known about how the solutions found by Adam differ. In this paper, we show that Adam implicitly reduces a unique form of sharpness measure shaped by its adaptive updates, leading to qualitatively different solutions from SGD.
    More specifically, when the training loss is small, Adam wanders around the manifold of minimizers and takes semi-gradients to minimize this sharpness measure in an adaptive manner, a behavior we rigorously characterize through a continuous-time approximation using stochastic differential equations.
    We further demonstrate how this behavior differs from that of SGD in a well-studied setting: when training overparameterized models with label noise, SGD has been shown to minimize the trace of the Hessian matrix, $\tr(\mH)$, whereas we prove that Adam minimizes $\tr(\Diag(\mH)^{1/2})$ instead.
    In solving sparse linear regression with diagonal linear networks, this distinction enables Adam to achieve better sparsity and generalization than SGD.
    Finally, our analysis framework extends beyond Adam to a broad class of adaptive gradient methods, including RMSProp, Adam-mini, Adalayer and Shampoo, and provides a unified perspective on how these adaptive optimizers reduce sharpness, which we hope will offer insights for future optimizer design.

In solving sparse linear regression with diagonal linear networks, we show that this distinction leads to better sparsity and generalization for Adam. This allows us to compare and contrast the sharpness reduction behaviors of different adaptive gradient methods, which may provide insights for future design of adaptive optimizers.

%The implicit bias of an optimizer, which characterizes its tendency leading towards an optimum with specific properties, is crucial for understanding the generalizability of the model trained by that optimizer. 
%Adam, though being widely used in practice for providing stable and efficient convergence, is relatively underexplored in terms of implicit bias. In this work, we derive a special kind of SDE that drops off the fast mixing dynamics and captures the near-manifold dynamics of a framework of adaptive gradient methods including Adam, concluding that Adam implicitly reduces a unique form of sharpness. We further prove that Adam's implicit regularizer reduces to $\tr(\tr(\mH)^{1/2})$ with label noise. We argue that this target aligns better with sparsity than the $\tr(\tr(\mH))$ of SGD, and verified our theoretical predictions on a diagonal-net toy model. We also conducted real-world experiments to demonstrate the difference of the form of sharpness regularized by Adam and SGD. Our results bridge the gap between theory and practice for adaptive optimizers and provide actionable guidelines for their design and hyperparameter tuning. 
\end{abstract}
