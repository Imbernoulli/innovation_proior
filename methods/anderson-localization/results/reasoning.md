I start with the spin packet that refuses to smear out. If the usual random-walk picture is right, a packet selected at one resonance frequency should not keep its identity for seconds. Neighboring spins precess against one another, occasional energy-conserving flip-flops move the excitation, and after enough of those microscopic exchanges the packet should equilibrate across the inhomogeneous line. That is the Bloembergen-Portis picture: broadening slows diffusion, but it does not abolish the idea that diffusion is the long-time fate. The numbers are the warning sign. The estimated packet lifetime can be fractions of a second down to microseconds, while the selected packets persist for tens of seconds. Charge motion can be pinned by Coulomb effects in compensated donor systems, but spin diffusion is still staring at me. Something in the quantum hopping problem is more severe than a short mean free path.

Let me remove every complication that is not needed for that question. I do not need a detailed semiconductor band structure. I need localized sites, random local energies, and hopping between sites. So I write amplitudes `a_j(t)` in a site basis and let

`i d a_j/dt = E_j a_j + sum_k V_jk a_k`.

The onsite energies `E_j` are random over a width `W`; the hopping matrix elements `V_jk` are the only way amplitude moves. If every `E_j` is the same, the hopping term makes ordinary band states, and a localized packet spreads. If `W` is small compared with the band width, I can start with plane waves and let the random part scatter them. That gives a self-energy with an imaginary part, a lifetime, and then a diffusion constant. But that route assumes the extended wave basis is still the right unperturbed problem, and in the donor packet problem the local frequencies are the large objects. So let me reverse the perturbation theory and start from the sites instead, and see what the resolvent on the starting site actually does.

Take the Laplace transform `f_j(s)=int_0^infty exp(-s t) a_j(t) dt`, with `Re s >= 0`. For a particle initially on site `0`, the transformed equations let me eliminate the other `f_j`'s into a self-energy for `f_0`:

`f_0(s) = i / (i s - E_0 - V_c(s))`,

where the locator self-energy begins as

`V_c(s) = sum_k |V_0k|^2/(i s - E_k) + sum_{k,l} V_0k V_kl V_l0/[(i s - E_k)(i s - E_l)] + ...`.

In resolvent variables this is `Sigma_0(z) = sum_k |V_0k|^2/(z-E_k) + ...` with `z = E + i*eta`, and the question is what `Sigma_0` does as `eta -> 0`. This expression is superficially the same kind of object as the propagator self-energy in scattering theory, but the starting states are different. In the propagator expansion, the denominators come from a continuum of plane-wave energies, and the imaginary part is a stable averaged decay rate. Here the denominators are random site energies, and whether a term is large is not a smooth density-of-states question; it is a question about whether this particular site has a near-resonant neighbor.

I want to see this concretely rather than argue it, so let me build the smallest version where I can hold the locator series in my hand and check it against the exact resolvent. Put site `0` at `E_0 = 0`, couple it with hopping `V = 1` to `K = 4` neighbor sites whose energies are drawn uniformly on `[-W/2, W/2]`, and leave the neighbors uncoupled from each other. On that star there are no paths of length greater than two, so the locator series terminates after the first term: `Sigma_0(z)` should equal `sum_k V^2/(z - E_k)` exactly. With `W = 12`, one draw gives neighbor energies near `1.50, 4.77, 3.31, -3.30` — none of them close to `E = 0`. Inverting `(z - H)` numerically at `E = 0` and reading off `Sigma_exact = z - E_0 - 1/G_00`, I get, as I push `eta` down,

```
eta = 1e-1 : Im Sigma_exact = -6.69e-2 ,  Im Sigma_1hop = -6.69e-2
eta = 1e-2 : Im Sigma_exact = -6.71e-3 ,  Im Sigma_1hop = -6.71e-3
eta = 1e-3 : Im Sigma_exact = -6.71e-4 ,  Im Sigma_1hop = -6.71e-4
eta = 1e-4 : Im Sigma_exact = -6.71e-5 ,  Im Sigma_1hop = -6.71e-5
```

Two things land at once. The single-hop locator reproduces the full self-energy to every digit, so the expansion is doing what I derived it to do. And `Im Sigma` is not approaching a finite limit — it is falling off linearly in `eta`, tracking `eta` down by exactly a factor of ten each time. There is no resonant neighbor inside the window, so the imaginary part is a pure artifact of the regulator and vanishes on the real axis. The pole of `G_00` therefore stays sharp. When I diagonalize the same star, the eigenstate nearest `E = 0` carries weight `|<0|psi>|^2 = 0.57` on site `0`; the four eigenstates together exhaust the unit sum but the central one keeps more than half. So the residue on the starting site survives at a finite value: the amplitude has bled a little onto off-resonant neighbors but has not relaxed away.

That is the distinction I was reaching for, now made on the page. If `Im Sigma_0(E + i0)` stays finite, the pole of `f_0` is broadened, the initial amplitude decays, and transport has a chance. If `Im Sigma_0` vanishes as `eta -> 0` while the real virtual admixture stays finite, `f_0` keeps a pole with nonzero residue. The wavefunction has spread virtually, not diffusively, and a local object can dress itself with off-resonant tails and still keep memory forever. The numerics just exhibited the second case for one strongly disordered cluster.

But one cluster proves nothing about a macroscopic sample, and the obvious temptation is to average `V_c` over the random energies to get a clean answer. Let me follow that road far enough to see where it ends. The mean of a single term involves `<1/(E - E_k)>` over the distribution of `E_k`. With `E_k` uniform near `E`, the integrand `1/|E - E_k|` is non-integrable at the resonance, so a thin sliver of accidentally resonant sites dominates the mean and the averaged decay rate looks finite — exactly the old diffusive answer. The trouble is that a real sample is not an ensemble, and a selected donor is not the average donor. Quantifying it: for `W = 1` and `E = 0`, the *typical* size of `1/|E - E_k|`, taken as `exp(<ln|E - E_k|>)^{-1}`, comes out to `5.44`, matching the analytic `(2/W) e = 5.437`. That typical denominator is finite and modest, while the *mean* of `1/|E - E_k|` diverges. The average is being set by events that almost never happen, so it answers the wrong question. I should track the probability distribution of the locator, or its typical value, not its mean.

So the real-decay channel from a single hop needs two coincidences in the same site: the mismatch `|E - E_k|` small enough to probe, and the hopping `V_0k` large enough to matter on that same scale. If the hopping falls with distance faster than `1/r^3`, or exponentially as overlapping localized orbitals do, distant sites cannot rescue the situation. The number of candidate sites grows as volume, but the direct matrix element falls faster than the probability of finding a useful far resonance grows. Rare near-resonances still exist, and they are why the mean is treacherous, but the typical locator stays finite as `eta -> 0`, and a finite typical virtual admixture is not a diffusion constant.

That only handles one hop, and the star geometry I checked deliberately had no longer paths. The real objection is that maybe no single neighbor is resonant, yet a long chain of off-resonant virtual hops eventually finds a way out. A path of length `L` carries a product of hoppings divided by random energy denominators, and there are roughly `K^L` such paths if `K` is the effective connectivity. This is where the problem becomes a quantum version of percolation: geometry supplies path proliferation, the denominators supply resonance rarity. The typical magnitude of a length-`L` path is `(V * (typical 1/|E - E_k|))^L`, and the count is `K^L`, so the typical total scales like `[K V (typical 1/|E-E_k|)]^L`. Using the number I just computed, the naive convergence demand `K V (2/W) e < 1`, i.e. `W/2V > e K`, would say the series converges for strong enough disorder.

I do not fully trust that naive factor, because the sum over `K^L` paths is dominated by the *largest* terms, not the typical ones, and the largest are governed by the tail of the denominator distribution rather than its geometric mean. Pushing the path-counting estimate with the tail in place turns the bare `e K` into a log-enhanced threshold: solving `e K ln(W/2V) = W/2V` for the critical disorder gives `W/2V ≈ 14.6` at `K = 2`, `40.2` at `K = 4`, `69.1` at `K = 6`, each well above the naive `e K` values of `5.4, 10.9, 16.3`. The logarithm is not decoration; it is the room the rare resonances buy back, and it is why the critical disorder is several times the typical-denominator estimate. The criterion can be written as `F(K, W/V) < 1`: when `F < 1` the probability that the sum of all length-`L` paths stays large falls off in `L`, the full series converges with probability one, and `G_00` keeps its real-axis poles. When `F` crosses one, long paths can no longer be dismissed and the expansion around localized sites breaks down. The threshold is not a sharp universal number in this crude treatment, but its meaning is definite: large disorder relative to hopping and connectivity prevents a resonant network from percolating through Hilbert space.

Now the physical picture is settled by what the computations showed. The local state is not trapped in an ordinary potential well. It is surrounded by possible sites, most of which have the wrong energy; hopping to them is allowed, but because the energy mismatch is real, those hops mostly stay virtual. A finite cluster of nearly resonant sites can share amplitude, so the eigenfunction is not a delta function — the star already had its central weight drop from one to 0.57. Yet if the resonant clusters do not connect across the lattice, the exact eigenstate has a localized envelope and the initial excitation keeps a finite long-time overlap with it. Disorder has not merely shortened the mean free path; the coherent sum over paths has removed the diffusive channel.

It is worth checking the opposite corner the same way, so the localized verdict is not an accident of small `K`. Take a long one-dimensional ring, `N = 2001` sites, hopping `V = 1`, and watch `Im Sigma` at the middle site as `eta -> 0`. At weak disorder `W/V = 1`, I get `Im Sigma ≈ -2.50, -2.05, -1.15` at `eta = 0.1, 0.01, 0.001` — it is still order unity as the regulator shrinks, which is the finite decay rate that produces transport. At strong disorder `W/V = 30`, the same quantity reads `-2.52e-2, -2.52e-3, -2.53e-4` — falling linearly in `eta`, vanishing on the real axis exactly as the star did. The site keeps a sharp pole and never acquires a real width. So the same diagnostic separates the two fates on a genuinely extended lattice: weak disorder leaves a finite `Im Sigma` and diffusion, strong disorder kills it and leaves a localized, memory-keeping state.

This also tells me what failure of the localized picture should look like. Decrease `W/V`, increase connectivity, or move to energies where the effective denominators are more favorable, and the high-order terms stretch the effective hopping to longer scales. The localized envelope grows, then at some energy or parameter value `F(K, W/V)` reaches one and the locator expansion ceases to converge. That is the natural place for extended states to appear, and there need not be a single answer for the whole spectrum: some energies can have localized eigenstates while others support transport, with a mobility boundary in energy between them.

I should be cautious about dimension. Counting nonrepeating paths by a single `K` is only a rough stand-in for lattice geometry, and loops are exactly the structures crude path counting handles least cleanly. Still, the direction is right. In low dimensions there are fewer independent escape routes and returning paths matter strongly; in higher dimensions path proliferation has a better chance to defeat localization. The later conductance-scaling language says the same thing in a different variable: in a localized regime the dimensionless conductance decreases exponentially with system size, `g(L) ~ exp(-L/xi)`, so `beta(g) = d ln g / d ln L < 0`, while in an ordinary conductor `g` grows like the ohmic power of size and `beta(g) ~ d - 2` at large `g`. A three-dimensional sample can therefore have a genuine critical point — an unstable fixed point separating the two flows — while a one- or two-dimensional sample is much more vulnerable to localization in the infinite-size limit.

The mechanism this leaves me with is therefore: write the random tight-binding problem in the site basis, expand the local resolvent in hopping rather than expanding plane waves in disorder, and judge the series by the distribution of sample-specific resonant paths rather than its ensemble average. If the disorder is large enough relative to hopping and connectivity, the locator series converges, `G_00` has isolated real-axis poles with finite residues instead of a transport-producing branch cut, and exact eigenfunctions remain spatially localized. An initially local excitation then does not diffuse away in the closed quantum system. It keeps a finite memory of where it began because interference among all those off-resonant paths blocks the conversion of virtual spreading into transport — which is exactly the long-lived spin packet I started from.
