I start with the spin packet that refuses to smear out. If the usual random-walk picture is right, a packet selected at one resonance frequency should not keep its identity for seconds. Neighboring spins precess against one another, occasional energy-conserving flip-flops move the excitation, and after enough of those microscopic exchanges the packet should equilibrate across the inhomogeneous line. That is the Bloembergen-Portis picture: broadening slows diffusion, but it does not abolish the idea that diffusion is the long-time fate. The numbers are the warning sign. The estimated packet lifetime can be fractions of a second down to microseconds, while the selected packets persist for tens of seconds. Charge motion can be pinned by Coulomb effects in compensated donor systems, but spin diffusion is still staring at me. Something in the quantum hopping problem is more severe than a short mean free path.

Let me remove every complication that is not needed for that question. I do not need a detailed semiconductor band structure. I need localized sites, random local energies, and hopping between sites. So I write amplitudes `a_j(t)` in a site basis and let

`i d a_j/dt = E_j a_j + sum_k V_jk a_k`.

The onsite energies `E_j` are random over a width `W`; the hopping matrix elements `V_jk` are the only way amplitude moves. If every `E_j` is the same, the hopping term makes ordinary band states, and a localized packet spreads. If `W` is small compared with the band width, I can start with plane waves and let the random part scatter them. That gives a self-energy with an imaginary part, a lifetime, and then a diffusion constant. But that route assumes too much: it assumes the extended wave basis is still the right unperturbed problem. In the donor packet problem, the local frequencies are the large objects. I should reverse the perturbation theory and start from the sites.

Take the Laplace transform `f_j(s)=int_0^infty exp(-s t) a_j(t) dt`, with `Re s >= 0`. For a particle initially on site `0`, the transformed equations let me eliminate the other `f_j`'s into a self-energy for `f_0`. In the first round this looks like

`f_0(s) = i / (i s - E_0 - V_c(s))`,

where the locator self-energy begins as

`V_c(s) = sum_k |V_0k|^2/(i s - E_k) + sum_{k,l} V_0k V_kl V_l0/[(i s - E_k)(i s - E_l)] + ...`.

This expression is superficially the same kind of object as the propagator self-energy in scattering theory, but the starting states are different. In the propagator expansion, the denominators come from a continuum of plane-wave energies, and the imaginary part is a stable averaged decay rate. Here the denominators are random site energies. Whether a term is large is not a smooth density-of-states question; it is a question about whether this particular site has a near-resonant neighbor.

Suppose the imaginary part of `V_c(E+i0)` stays finite. Then the pole of `f_0` is broadened, the initial amplitude decays, and transport has a chance. But if the imaginary part vanishes while the real virtual admixture remains finite, then `f_0` keeps a pole with nonzero residue. The amplitude on site `0` is reduced because the eigenstate borrows weight from neighbors, but it does not disappear. The wavefunction has spread virtually, not diffusively. That distinction is exactly what the packet data demand: a local object can dress itself with off-resonant tails and still keep memory forever.

The tempting move is to average `V_c` over the random energies. That immediately pulls me back to the old answer, because a tiny fraction of sites are accidentally resonant and their huge denominators dominate the mean. But a real sample is not an ensemble average, and a selected atom is not an average atom. If the average diverges because of rare events, the average is answering the wrong question. I need the probability distribution of the locator, or at least its typical behavior.

Look at the first term. A site `k` contributes a real decay channel near energy `E` only if two things happen at once: the energy mismatch `|E-E_k|` is within the small width being probed, and the hopping to that site is large enough to matter on that same scale. If the hopping falls with distance faster than `1/r^3`, or exponentially as overlapping localized orbitals do, then distant sites do not rescue the situation. The number of possible sites grows as volume, but the direct matrix element falls faster than the probability of finding a useful far resonance grows. Rare near-resonances still exist, and they are why the mean is treacherous, but the typical locator remains finite as `s -> 0`. Finite typical virtual spreading is not diffusion.

That only handles one hop. The objection is obvious: maybe no single neighbor is resonant, but a long chain of off-resonant virtual hops eventually finds a way out. A path of length `L` carries a product of hoppings divided by random energy denominators. There are many such paths, roughly `K^L` of them if `K` is the effective connectivity. This is where the problem becomes a quantum version of percolation. Geometry supplies path proliferation; the denominators supply resonance rarity. If the distribution of length-`L` path amplitudes has a typical scale that shrinks faster than the number of paths grows, the locator series converges. If path proliferation wins, the expansion around localized sites breaks down.

For a nearest-neighbor model with fixed hopping `V`, the path-counting criterion can be phrased as a function `F(K,W/V)`: the number distribution of large length-`L` terms has the form `[F(K,W/V)]^L` times a slowly varying tail. When `F<1`, the probability that the sum of all length-`L` paths stays large falls with `L`, and the full series converges with probability one. When `F` crosses one, long paths can no longer be dismissed. The threshold is not a precise universal number in this crude treatment, but its meaning is sharp: large disorder compared with hopping and connectivity prevents a resonant network from percolating through Hilbert space.

Now the physical picture is clearer. The local state is not trapped in an ordinary potential well. It is surrounded by possible sites, most of which have the wrong energy. Hopping to them is allowed by quantum mechanics, but because the energy mismatch is real, those hops mostly remain virtual. A finite cluster of nearly resonant sites can share amplitude, so the eigenfunction is not a delta function. Yet if the resonant clusters do not connect across the lattice, the exact eigenstate has a localized envelope and the initial excitation keeps a finite long-time overlap with it. Disorder has not merely shortened the mean free path; the coherent sum over paths has removed the diffusive channel.

This also tells me what failure of the localized picture should look like. Decrease `W/V`, increase connectivity, or move to energies where the effective denominators are more favorable, and the high-order terms stretch the effective hopping to longer scales. The localized envelope grows, then at some energy or parameter value the locator expansion ceases to converge. That is the natural place for extended states to appear. There need not be a single answer for the whole spectrum: some energies can have localized eigenstates while others support transport, with a boundary in energy between them.

I should be cautious about dimension. Counting nonrepeating paths by a single `K` is only a rough stand-in for lattice geometry, and loops are exactly the structures that crude path counting handles least cleanly. Still, the direction is right. In low dimensions there are fewer independent escape routes and returning paths matter strongly; in higher dimensions path proliferation has a better chance to defeat localization. The later conductance-scaling language says the same thing in a different variable: in a localized regime the dimensionless conductance decreases exponentially with system size, while in an ordinary conductor it grows like the ohmic power of size. A three-dimensional sample can therefore have a genuine critical point, while a one- or two-dimensional sample is much more vulnerable to localization in the infinite-size limit.

The mechanism I land on is therefore this: write the random tight-binding problem in the site basis, expand the local resolvent in hopping rather than expanding plane waves in disorder, and judge the series by the distribution of sample-specific resonant paths. If the disorder is large enough relative to hopping and connectivity, the locator series converges, `G_00` has isolated real-axis poles with finite residues instead of a transport-producing branch cut, and exact eigenfunctions remain spatially localized. An initially local excitation then does not diffuse away in the closed quantum system. It keeps a finite memory of where it began because interference among all those off-resonant paths blocks the conversion of virtual spreading into transport.
