A new upper bound for the Heilbronn triangle problem

arXiv:2305.18253v1 [math.CO] 29 May 2023

Alex Cohen∗

Cosmin Pohoata†

Dmitrii Zakharov‡

Abstract
For sufficiently large n, we show that in every configuration of n points chosen inside the unit
square there exists a triangle of area less than n−8/7−1/2000 . This improves upon a result of Komlós,
Pintz and Szemerédi from 1982. Our approach establishes new connections between the Heilbronn
triangle problem and various themes in incidence geometry and projection theory which are closely
related to the discretized sum-product phenomenon.

Introduction

Given an integer n ⩾ 3, the Heilbronn triangle problem asks for the smallest number ∆ = ∆(n) such
that in every configuration of n points in the unit square [0, 1]2 one can always find three among them

the simple observation that one can always triangulate a set of n points in [0, 1]2 and obtain at least
n − 2 triangles whose interiors are disjoint and are all contained in the unit square. By the pigeonhole

Lower bounds and upper bounds for incidences. Roth developed his inductive step to prove
lower bounds for incidences. Guth, Solomon, and Wang [8] introduced the high-low method to prove
upper bounds for incidences. Theorem 3.1 unifies these two perspectives: the same inequality can be
used to prove upper bounds or lower bounds. Lots of famous problems in combinatorics and analysis—
for example, the unit distance problem and the Kakeya problem—have to do with upper bounds for
incidences, and there is a huge amount of research on this topic. On the other hand, incidence lower
bounds have not been studied much. We hope to draw attention that these lower bounds are the key
in Heilbronn’s problem, and to encourage more research on lower bounds for incidences in general.

6.2

s-regular subsets relative to a rectangle

Recall that a finite set of points P ⊂ [0, 1]2 is s-regular above scale δ if for any square Q ⊂ [0, 1]2 of
width w ⩾ δ we have |P ∩ Q| ⩽ Cws |P |. It is convenient to generalize this definition to sets contained
in an arbitrary rectangle R ⊂ R2 instead of the square [0, 1]2 . Namely, for a rectangle R let P ⊂ R be
a finite set and let ψ : [0, 1]2 → R be an affine isomorphism between the unit square and the rectangle
R. We say that P ⊂ R is s-regular above scale δ relative to R if ψ −1 (P ) ⊂ [0, 1]2 is s-regular above
scale δ in the usual sense.
Let R be a rectangle and ψ : [0, 1]2 → R an affine isomorphism between the unit square and R. Let
T ⊂ R2 be a strip. We say that T has width w relative to R if the strip ψ −1 (T ) has (the usual) width
equal to w.
Using these notions we will be able to apply the discretized projection theorems, Theorem 4.1 and
Theorem 4.2. For example, given a finite set P ⊂ [0, 1]2 and a number 0 < s < 2, we can construct an
s-regular subset in P as follows. Among all squares Q ⊂ [0, 1]2 with side w ⩾ w0 for some fixed w0 ,
choose one maximizing the expression
|P ∩ Q|w−s .
(6.4)
Let PQ = P ∩ Q and observe that by definition, the set PQ ⊂ Q is s-regular relative to Q above scale
w0 /w. Indeed, if Q′ ⊂ Q is a square with side w′ ∈ [w0 , w] then by the maximality of Q we have
|P ∩ Q′ |(w′ )−s ⩽ |P ∩ Q|w−s ,
19

i.e. |PQ ∩ Q′ | ⩽ (w′ /w)s |PQ |. In the coordinate system of Q the square Q′ has side w′ /w so this
condition means precisely that PQ ⊂ Q is s-regular above scale w0 /w.
In the actual proof of Theorem 1.1 will use a slightly more complicated way to construct s-regular
sets. Namely, by restricting more carefully the set of squares over which we maximize (6.4), we can
get more information on w and |PQ |.
Furthermore, in order to apply Theorem 4.2 to an s-regular set P we also need to check that any strip
of width τ = τ (σ, s) does not contain too many points of P . However, this property does not follow
from the construction above and we need the following modification. Given a finite set of points P
and 0 < s < 1 we consider all rectangles R with sides a ⩽ b such that ab ⩾ A and choose a rectangle
R maximizing the expression
|P ∩ R|a−ε b−s+ε .
Here A and ε are parameters of thepconstruction. It can be easily checked that the resulting set
PR = P ∩ R is s-regular above scale A/ab relative to R and satisfies the strip property needed for
Theorem 4.2: any strip T of width w relative to R satisfies |T ∩ PR | ≲ wε |PR |.

Final remarks

In this paper we showed that in every set of n points chosen inside a unit square there exists a triangle
of area less than n−8/7−1/2000 , thereby improving upon the classical result of Komlós, Pintz and
Szemerédi from [11] by a polynomial factor. We also gave a further improved bound of n−7/6 , in the
case when P is homogeneous. Perhaps most importantly, our approach established new connections
34

between the Heilbronn triangle and various themes in incidence geometry and projection theory that
are closely related to the discretized sum-product theorem of Bourgain [4].
Apart from the problem of further improving the bounds for the Heilbronn triangle problem, there
are several other related open questions which unfortunately are a bit less known. We would like to
use this section as an opportunity to draw attention towards a few of our favorites. We hope that the
ideas introduced in this paper will also lead to progress on some of these.
The first one is a problem due to Motzkin and Schmidt (cf. [5] and [3]). For every set of points
P ⊂ [0, 1]2 , let Φ(P ) denote the smallest real number w ⩾ 0 for which there is a strip of width w
containing at least three points from P . Let
Φ(n) := max Φ(P ),
where the maximum is taken over all sets of n points P ⊂ [0, 1]2 .
Conjecture 1.
Φ(n) = o(1/n).
A simple pigeonholing argument shows that Φ(n) ⩽ 3/n. Using an analytic technique in the style
of Roth’s method discussed in §2, Beck [3] proved this conjecture in the special case when the set is
uniformly distributed in the sense that it contains precisely one point from each n−1/2 × n−1/2 square
(note that this is a stronger condition than being homogeneous). No nontrivial upper bound is known
in general.
It is worth emphasizing that Conjecture 2 is directly related to the Heilbronn triangle problem. Clearly,
∆(n) ⩽ Φ(n),
but the inequality goes only one way: a triangle of area o(1/n) is not necessarily be contained in a
strip of width o(1/n).
The next problem is a natural generalization of the Heilbronn triangle problem, which was also proposed by Schmidt in [23]. Let ∆k (n) be the smallest number ∆ = ∆(n) such that in every configuration
of n points in the unit square [0, 1]2 one can always find k points among them whose convex hull has
area at most ∆k (n).
Conjecture 2. For every k ⩾ 3,
∆k (n) = o(1/n).
Clearly, ∆(n) = ∆3 (n) ⩽ ∆4 (n) ⩽ . . . ⩽ ∆k (n) holds for al k ⩾ 3, so Conjecture 2 holds for k = 3.
However, rather surprisingly, the question remains open for every k ⩾ 4. The best known lower bound
is due to Leffman [13] and comes from a generalization of the semi-random construction from [11]: for

Question 3. Is it possible to find a set of n points P ⊂ [0, 1]2 and an absolute constant C > 0 such
that any tube of width 1/n contains at most C points from P ?
As Varjú writes in [Remembering Jean Bourgain (1954-2018), AMS Notices June 2021, p. 957], the
motivation for this problem comes from a possible construction of spherical harmonics as a combination
of Gaussian beams, which would have L∞ norm bounded by a constant independently of the degree.
If one could show that no such constant existed, this would immediately imply that Φk (n) = o(1/n)
holds for every k ⩾ 3.
