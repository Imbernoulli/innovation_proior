The first move is to stop thinking of an isometric embedding as a picture and write down what it has to satisfy. If `f:M -> R^N` is the embedding and `e` is the Euclidean metric, the condition is `f^*e=g`. In coordinates this is `g_ij=sum_alpha partial_i f^alpha partial_j f^alpha`. That formula is the whole change of viewpoint: an abstract metric is no longer something to visualize directly, but right-hand-side data in a nonlinear first-order PDE for the coordinate functions of `f`.

Once the problem is written this way, the dimension of the ambient Euclidean space becomes analytical room. The metric has `m(m+1)/2` independent components on an `m`-manifold. The embedding has `N` coordinate functions. In the high-dimensional smooth theorem, Nash chooses `N` large enough that the system is strongly underdetermined. That does not make the PDE easy, because the equations are quadratic in first derivatives and global embedding constraints remain. But it changes the nature of the difficulty: instead of a rigid matching problem with too few variables, it becomes a flexible correction problem with many possible directions in which to move.

The natural baseline would be the implicit function theorem. Define `P(f)=f^*e`; to correct an approximate embedding, solve the linearized equation `DP_f(u)=h` for a small metric error `h`, then iterate. The obstruction is derivative loss. The operator depends on derivatives of `f`, and solving the linearized first-order system asks more regularity from the data than the correction returns. In a Banach scale, the inverse is not bounded at one fixed differentiability level. A standard Newton scheme therefore cannot simply be repeated: each step would degrade regularity until the argument has nothing left to use.

Nash's smooth proof is brilliant because it does not try to wish this loss away. It changes the iteration. The correction step is paired with smoothing, so the next approximate solution regains enough differentiability before the nonlinear error is attacked again. Smoothing introduces its own error, but Newton-type contraction can be arranged to dominate it. This is the seed of the Nash-Moser method: solve an implicit equation in a setting where the inverse loses derivatives by working across a scale of norms instead of one fixed Banach space.

The `C^1` theorem shows the same flexibility in a more geometric form. Start with a short embedding, meaning the induced metric is smaller than the desired one. The gap `g-f^*e` is a positive metric defect. Nash's twist adds high-frequency oscillations, usually in normal directions, whose derivatives have a nontrivial average quadratic contribution to the metric. Because the oscillation is rapid, the map itself can stay close in `C^0`; because its derivative oscillates, the induced metric can change substantially. Repeating these small corrugations reduces the metric defect until the limit is isometric.

That distinction between `C^0` size and derivative effect is the trick. A perturbation can be visually tiny while its derivative carries enough energy to repair the metric. The price is low regularity and wild geometry, which is why the `C^1` theorem feels counterintuitive compared with classical rigidity. But for the purpose of existence, the oscillatory freedom is exactly what the underdetermined PDE permits.

The deeper lesson is that Nash did not produce a closed-form embedding. He produced a convergence mechanism. Each step fixes part of the defect while creating manageable new errors. In the `C^1` version, high-frequency twists keep positional error small and metric correction large. In the smooth version, smoothing keeps derivative loss from destroying the iteration. Both arguments trade explicit construction for flexible analysis: the final Euclidean submanifold is the limit of controlled approximations.

That is why the result is a breakthrough beyond the statement that every Riemannian manifold embeds isometrically. It changed what counts as a geometric existence proof. Instead of deriving the embedded shape from intrinsic formulas, Nash reframed the problem as an underdetermined nonlinear PDE and then exploited the underdetermination by iteration. Geometry becomes solvable not because the right shape is visible, but because the space of possible corrections is rich enough and the iteration is designed to converge despite derivative loss.
