I begin with the communication problem, because the betting rule is too easy to misunderstand if I start with a casino example. Shannon gives me a rate for a noisy channel, `R = H(X) - H(X|Y)`, and he gives it force through coding: below capacity I can transmit messages with arbitrarily small error. But I am trying to value a signal when no such coding operation is the object. The receiver observes something imperfect about an event and must act before the event is public. If I attach an arbitrary cost to every possible mistake, I can make a decision problem, but I have not explained why Shannon's rate itself should be the value. The cost table comes from outside the channel.

So I look for a receiver whose payoff is generated only by probabilities, odds, and the signal. A gambler with a private wire has exactly that shape. The channel input is the event outcome, the channel output is side information, and the market still offers odds before the outcome is common knowledge. At first this feels like a metaphor, but it is more than that: it makes information spendable. If the wire is noiseless for a fair even-money binary event, the receiver can stake everything every time and double capital every time. One bit per round becomes one doubling per round.

The first wall appears as soon as the wire has errors. If I maximize expected terminal capital, I am pushed toward betting everything whenever the expected gain is favorable. Kelly's primary example makes the problem stark: an all-in policy can have a large expected value and still leave the gambler broke with probability one over indefinite play. Expected capital is being dominated by rare surviving paths. It is not measuring the typical reinvested path. I need a quantity that follows the single path generated by repeated multiplication.

Current capital after many rounds is a product of round-by-round multipliers. That is the structural fact that decides the criterion. I take the logarithm not because I like logarithmic utility, and not because a cautious person has a special taste for it, but because the logarithm turns the product into a sum. Once I have a sum, empirical frequencies enter through the law of large numbers. The per-round quantity `G = lim (1/N) log(V_N/V_0)` is the growth rate that almost every long repeated sequence reveals.

Now the link to information rate becomes exact. Suppose the true outcome is `s`, the received symbol is `r`, the payoff including stake is `alpha_s`, and after seeing `r` I allocate fraction `a(s|r)` to outcome `s`. The capital multiplier on that round is `alpha_s a(s|r)` for the winning `s`, so the long-run rate is `sum_{r,s} p(s,r) log(alpha_s a(s|r))`. Under fair odds, `alpha_s = 1/p(s)`, this becomes `sum p(s,r) log(a(s|r)/p(s))`. The allocation that maximizes it is not the one with the largest one-shot edge and not the one that feels safest. It is the posterior allocation `a(s|r) = p(s|r)`.

This is the distinctive turn: I am matching capital allocation to the conditional distribution supplied by the signal. If I match it exactly, the maximum growth rate is `H(S) - H(S|R)`, the same mutual information that Shannon calls the rate of transmission. If I choose another allocation, I pay a mismatch penalty because the expected log ratio is lower. In modern notation this penalty is a divergence between the true posterior and my betting distribution. That makes the result an information-rate matching problem. The money process is acting like a decoder whose score is not block reconstruction but exponential growth.

I also have to keep the rejected alternatives clear. Von Neumann-Morgenstern utility explains how a numerical utility can represent preferences under axioms, and Bernoulli's logarithmic utility can fit inside that framework. But Kelly's reason is different. He explicitly separates the criterion from the value function attached to money: reinvestment makes log capital additive, and additivity is what lets frequencies settle. If winnings could not be reinvested and the stake were a fixed outside dollar each week, the ordinary expected-value rule would be appropriate instead.

For a binary wager I can now specialize the general idea. Let a win have probability `p`, a loss have probability `q = 1 - p`, and net odds be `b:1`. If I stake fraction `f`, the multipliers are `1 + b f` and `1 - f`. The growth objective is `g(f) = p log(1 + b f) + q log(1 - f)`. I differentiate and get `g'(f) = p b/(1 + b f) - q/(1 - f)`. At an interior optimum these terms balance, so `p b(1 - f) = q(1 + b f)`, and therefore `b p - q = b f`. The candidate fraction is `f = (b p - q)/b = p - q/b`.

I check the curvature because a formula alone is not enough. The second derivative is `-p b^2/(1 + b f)^2 - q/(1 - f)^2`, which is negative whenever both outcomes can occur. So the stationary point is the unique maximum if it lies in the feasible range. If `b p - q <= 0`, the growth-maximizing action is no stake. If the edge is positive, the stake is proportional to the edge but scaled by the odds. The all-in expected-value answer disappears because a total loss is not a finite additive setback in a reinvested product; it is a zero multiplier from which the long-run path cannot recover.
