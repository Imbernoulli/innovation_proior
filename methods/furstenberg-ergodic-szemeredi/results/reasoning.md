Let me start from the thing that actually bothers me, which is not whether the Erdős–Turán conjecture is true — Szemerédi has settled that — but that I cannot *see* why it is true. His proof is a magnificent combinatorial machine, van der Waerden plus a regularity partition plus pages of case analysis, and at the end the arithmetic progressions are there, but I come away unable to say what forced them. So I want a proof that names the mechanism: the small number of situations that actually control whether the progressions appear, and a way to reduce every system to those situations.

So what do I really have in front of me? A set $A \subseteq \mathbb{Z}$ with $\bar d(A) = \limsup_N |A \cap [-N,N]|/(2N+1) > 0$, and I want, for every $k$, some $a$ and some $n \neq 0$ with $a, a+n, \dots, a+(k-1)n$ all in $A$. Stare at the upper density for a second. It is a number in $[0,1]$, it is monotone and shift-invariant, but the limsup is not a measure: it is not countably additive, and I cannot even treat it as a finitely additive set function on all subsets. Still, if I choose intervals on which the density of $A$ tends to a positive limit, and then refine that averaging scheme so the shifts of the functions I care about all have limits, those limits form a positive shift-invariant linear functional. That is the object that is almost a probability measure invariant under a transformation. The defects are now clear: the integers are not compact, the limiting functional lives only as a mean on functions, and there is no $L^2$ structure yet. Those are exactly the defects that a measure-preserving system repairs.

And look at what I am being asked to find, rewritten in this language. The progression $a, a+n, \dots, a+(k-1)n$ lies in $A$ exactly when $a \in A \cap (A-n) \cap (A-2n) \cap \dots \cap (A-(k-1)n)$ — the point $a$ is in $A$, and shifting it forward by $n, 2n, \dots$ keeps landing in $A$. So "$A$ contains a $k$-AP" is "$A \cap (A-n) \cap \dots \cap (A-(k-1)n) \neq \emptyset$ for some $n$." If the shift $S\colon x \mapsto x+1$ really is the discrete shadow of a measure-preserving $T$, and "nonempty" is the shadow of "positive measure," then the statement I want on the dynamical side is: for $\mu(B) > 0$,
$$\mu\big(B \cap T^{-n}B \cap T^{-2n}B \cap \dots \cap T^{-(k-1)n}B\big) > 0 \quad \text{for some } n.$$
This is the analogy I keep returning to. The number-theoretic problem on $\mathbb{Z}$ and this ergodic problem on a probability space look like the same problem in two costumes. So the first thing to do is make the analogy into an actual reduction — turn the density of $A$ into a genuine invariant measure on a genuine compact space.

Why bother leaving $\mathbb{Z}$ at all? Because on $\mathbb{Z}$ the raw datum is only a limsup, and even after I extract an invariant mean it is still not a probability space on which I can use analysis — no $L^2$, no spectral theorem, no conditional expectation, no decomposition of the space into pieces. The entire toolkit I want to use to handle a general set lives on probability spaces. So I should compactify: I want a compact space $X$, a homeomorphism $T$, and an honest $T$-invariant Borel probability measure $\mu$, such that the density behaviour of $A$ is encoded in $\mu$.

Here is the clean way to do it. Identify the set $A$ with its indicator sequence, a point $a \in \{0,1\}^{\mathbb{Z}}$, where $a(m) = 1$ iff $m \in A$. Use the left shift on sequences, $(Tx)(m)=x(m+1)$; on subsets this is $B \mapsto B-1$. The whole space $\{0,1\}^{\mathbb{Z}}$ is compact (Tychonoff) and metrizable. Now I do not want all of it; I want the closed shift-invariant piece carrying $A$, so let $X = \overline{\{T^n a : n \in \mathbb{Z}\}}$, the orbit closure of $a$. And the event "the integer $0$ is in the set" is the cylinder $E = \{x \in X : x(0)=1\}$, which is clopen, hence its indicator is continuous. With this convention the signs are exact: $T^m a \in E$ iff $(T^m a)(0)=a(m)=1$ iff $m \in A$.

Now the measure. I have no invariant measure yet, only the density of $A$, so I build one by averaging the orbit and taking a limit. For each $N$ let
$$\mu_N = \frac{1}{2N+1} \sum_{n=-N}^{N} \delta_{T^n a},$$
the empirical measure that puts equal mass on the first $2N+1$ points of the orbit. Then $\mu_N(E)$ is precisely the fraction of $n \in [-N,N]$ with $T^n a \in E$, which is $|A\cap[-N,N]|/(2N+1)$. Since $\bar d(A) > 0$, I can choose $N_j \to \infty$ along which $\mu_{N_j}(E) \to c > 0$. The space of Borel probability measures on the compact metric $X$ is weak-$*$ compact (Banach–Alaoglu, sequential by metrizability), so passing to a further subsequence $\mu_{N_j} \to \mu$ weak-$*$. Two things are true of this $\mu$. First, $\mu(E) = c > 0$, because $1_E$ is continuous. Second, $\mu$ is $T$-invariant, even though no single $\mu_N$ was. Compute $T\mu_N - \mu_N$: telescoping the shifted empirical sum, the only surviving terms are the two endpoints, $T\mu_N - \mu_N = \frac{1}{2N+1}(\delta_{T^{N+1}a} - \delta_{T^{-N}a})$, total variation $\leq 2/(2N+1) \to 0$. The empirical measures are asymptotically invariant, so any weak-$*$ limit is exactly invariant. There it is: $(X,\mathcal{B},\mu,T)$ is a measure-preserving system, $\mu(E) > 0$, and the density of $A$ has become the measure of a set.

Let me make sure the reduction actually goes the way I need. Suppose I have proved the ergodic statement, that $\mu(E \cap T^{-n}E \cap \dots \cap T^{-(k-1)n}E) > 0$ for some $n > 0$. Then the intersection is non-empty, so there is a point $x \in X$ with $x, T^n x, \dots, T^{(k-1)n}x$ all in $E$. The set $E \cap T^{-n}E \cap \dots \cap T^{-(k-1)n}E$ is clopen, because it is a finite intersection of clopen cylinder pulls. Since $X$ is the orbit closure of $a$, some genuine orbit point $T^m a$ lies in that same clopen set. Unwinding the cylinder condition gives $m, m+n, \dots, m+(k-1)n \in A$. A genuine $k$-term arithmetic progression in $A$, gap $n \neq 0$. So the ergodic statement implies Szemerédi. The analogy has become a theorem-shaped reduction. Now I have to prove the ergodic statement.

Let me name it, because it is the object the rest of the work is about. Call it multiple recurrence: for every measure-preserving system $(X,\mathcal{B},\mu,T)$, every $B$ with $\mu(B) > 0$, and every $k \geq 1$, there is $n > 0$ with $\mu(B \cap T^{-n}B \cap \dots \cap T^{-(k-1)n}B) > 0$. Is this the *right* statement to be chasing? Test it at the bottom. For $k = 1$ it is trivial. For $k = 2$ it says $\mu(B \cap T^{-n}B) > 0$ for some $n$ — and that is exactly Poincaré recurrence, which is essentially free: the sets $B, T^{-1}B, T^{-2}B, \dots$ all have measure $\mu(B)$ in a space of mass $1$, so they cannot be pairwise disjoint, two of them overlap, and invariance turns that into the claim. So multiple recurrence is the honest $k$-fold generalization of Poincaré recurrence, and it bottoms out at exactly the classical fact. That is a strong sign I have the right ergodic statement. The whole difficulty has been displaced into one place: the *simultaneous* recurrence of $x, T^n x, T^{2n}x, \dots$ to the same set, along a *common* gap $n$. Poincaré's pigeonhole gives one return; it says nothing about making $k-1$ returns line up arithmetically.

Before I attack the general system, let me also check the reduction the other way, just to be sure I have not picked a statement that is secretly weaker. If Szemerédi's theorem were assumed, could I recover multiple recurrence? Given $\mu(E) > 0$, consider for each $x$ its return set $R_x = \{n : T^n x \in E\}$. For
$$\delta_N(x)=\frac{1}{2N+1}|\{|n|\le N:T^n x\in E\}|,$$
invariance gives $\int \delta_N\,d\mu=\mu(E)$ for every $N$. Therefore the sets $A_N=\{\delta_N\ge \mu(E)/2\}$ have measure bounded below by a positive constant; otherwise the integral could not stay equal to $\mu(E)$. The limsup $F=\limsup_N A_N$ has positive measure, and every $x\in F$ has $R_x$ of positive upper density. Szemerédi puts a $k$-term progression $a,a+n,\dots,a+(k-1)n$ inside $R_x$ for every $x\in F$. There are only countably many pairs $(a,n)$, so one pair works on a positive-measure subset of $F$; for those $x$, $T^{a+jn}x\in E$ for all $j$. Applying the measure-preserving map $T^a$ to that positive-measure set gives $\mu(E\cap T^{-n}E\cap\dots\cap T^{-(k-1)n}E)>0$. So the two statements are equivalent; multiple recurrence is a faithful translation.

Now, the general system. I cannot say anything about an arbitrary $T$ directly, so I look for the regimes where the count is forced for an obvious reason, and there are exactly two opposite ones.

The first is when $T$ is as *random* as possible. Think of $E$ as an event in the probability space. If the events $E, T^{-n}E, T^{-2n}E, \dots$ were genuinely independent, the intersection would have measure $\mu(E)^k > 0$ outright, for every $n$. They are not exactly independent, but for a sufficiently mixing $T$ they decorrelate. The right notion turns out to be weak mixing, which has several faces: $T \times T$ acting on $X \times X$ is ergodic; equivalently $T$ has no non-constant eigenfunctions; equivalently $\frac{1}{N}\sum_n |\mu(A \cap T^{-n}B) - \mu(A)\mu(B)| \to 0$ for all $A,B$. Let me see if weak mixing really delivers the $k$-fold count.

I will phrase it as a statement about the diagonal in a product. On $X^k = X \times \dots \times X$ consider the transformation $\tau_k = T \times T^2 \times \dots \times T^k$, and the *diagonal measure* $\nu_k$ defined by $\int f_1 \otimes \dots \otimes f_k \, d\nu_k = \int f_1(x)f_2(x)\cdots f_k(x)\, d\mu(x)$ — it lives on the diagonal $\{(x,x,\dots,x)\}$. I want to show that under $\tau_k$ the diagonal measure equidistributes to the product measure $\mu^k$, in the sense that $\nu_k$ is *generic* for $\mu^k$: averaging $f \circ \tau_k^n$ against $\nu_k$ converges to $\int f \, d\mu^k$. If that holds, then taking $f = 1_B \otimes \dots \otimes 1_B$ gives averages of $\mu(T^{-n}B \cap T^{-2n}B \cap \dots \cap T^{-kn}B)$, and applying $T^n$ to the intersection rewrites the summand as $\mu(B \cap T^{-n}B \cap \dots \cap T^{-(k-1)n}B)$. The limit is $\mu(B)^k > 0$, which is multiple recurrence with room to spare.

The base case $k = 2$ is the mean ergodic theorem in disguise. The diagonal measure $\nu_2$ on $X \times X$ is generic for $\mu \times \mu$ under $T \times T^2$: by the mean ergodic theorem $\frac{1}{N}\sum_n T^n f \to \int f\, d\mu$ in $L^2$ for any $f$, and pairing $g(x) f(T^n x)$ and averaging gives $\frac{1}{N}\sum_n \int g(x)f(T^n x)\,d\mu \to \int g\,d\mu \int f\,d\mu$, which is exactly $\nu_2$ becoming the product. Now induct. Suppose $\nu_k$ is generic for $\mu^k$ under $\tau_k$. The crucial leverage of weak mixing is that $T^q$ is again weakly mixing (hence ergodic) for every $q$, so the product system $(X^k, \tau_k)$ is ergodic. That ergodicity is exactly what lets me run a generalized mean ergodic theorem *along the generic measure* $\nu_k$ — even though $\nu_k$ is singular with respect to $\mu^k$, genericity plus ergodicity of the target force $\frac{1}{N}\sum_n \tau_k^n f \to \int f\, d\mu^k$ in $L^2(\nu_k)$. Applying that with $f = f_1 \otimes \dots \otimes f_k$, multiplying by an $f_0$ on the new last coordinate and integrating, the average $\frac{1}{N}\sum_n \int f_0(x) f_1(T^n x)\cdots f_k(T^{kn}x)\,d\mu$ converges to $\prod_{i} \int f_i\,d\mu$, which is precisely $\nu_{k+1}$ generic for $\mu^{k+1}$. So by induction weak mixing gives the full product limit; "weak mixing of all orders" falls right out. In the weak-mixing regime, multiple recurrence holds for all $k$, with positive density of good $n$.

Now the opposite extreme: when $T$ is as *structured* as possible. The cleanest structured system is a Kronecker system — $X = G$ a compact abelian group, $T$ rotation by an element $g_0$ generating a dense subgroup, $\mu$ Haar measure. Here every orbit is almost periodic: $T^n x = g_0^n x$ wanders densely but rigidly around $G$. Let me try $k = 3$ directly on a Kronecker system and see the count come out positive. Take $0 \le f \in L^\infty(G)$, not a.e. zero, and form
$$\phi(z') = \int_G f(z)\, f(z z')\, f(z z'^2)\, dm_G(z).$$
This is the "$3$-AP count starting from displacement $z'$." Three facts about $\phi$. It is continuous in $z'$, because translation $z' \mapsto f(\cdot\, z')$ is continuous from $G$ into $L^2(G)$ — rotations move $L^2$ functions continuously — so the integral, a product of three such, depends continuously on $z'$. It is nowhere negative, since $f \ge 0$. And at the identity $\phi(e) = \int f^3\,dm_G > 0$. Now the quantity I want is $\frac{1}{N}\sum_n \phi(g_0^n)$, the Cesàro average of $\phi$ along the orbit of $g_0$. But a Kronecker system is uniquely ergodic — the orbit of $g_0$ equidistributes for Haar measure — so for continuous $\phi$ this average converges to $\int_G \phi\, dm_G$. And $\int_G \phi\, dm_G > 0$ because $\phi$ is continuous, nonnegative, and strictly positive at $e$ (hence on a neighbourhood). So
$$\lim_N \frac{1}{N}\sum_n \int f(x)\,f(T^n x)\,f(T^{2n}x)\,d\mu > 0$$
on a Kronecker system. The structured regime handles $k = 3$ as cleanly as the random regime did. The two computations could not look more different — one is "independence makes it $\mu(B)^k$," the other is "almost periodicity plus unique ergodicity makes the count a positive continuous average" — but both *force* the progression count to be positive.

So I have two regimes, weak mixing and Kronecker, and a real system is generally neither. The question that decides everything is: given an arbitrary ergodic system, how do I cut it into pieces I can already handle? What is the boundary between the two regimes?

Eigenfunctions. They are the dividing line, and I can feel it from both sides. On the weak-mixing side, what exactly breaks the product computation when $T$ is *not* weakly mixing? Suppose there is a non-trivial eigenfunction $\phi$ with $|\phi| = 1$, $\phi(Tx) = \lambda\phi(x)$, $\lambda = e^{2\pi i \alpha}$, $\alpha \neq 0$. Set $f = \phi$, $g = \phi^{-2}$, $h = \phi$ and track $f(x)\,g(T^n x)\,h(T^{2n}x)$. The base values multiply to $\phi(x)\cdot\phi^{-2}(x)\cdot\phi(x) = 1$. The phases multiply to $\lambda^{-2n}\cdot\lambda^{2n} = 1$, since $g(T^n x) = \phi^{-2}(T^n x) = \lambda^{-2n}\phi^{-2}(x)$ and $h(T^{2n}x) = \phi(T^{2n}x) = \lambda^{2n}\phi(x)$. So $f(x)\,g(T^n x)\,h(T^{2n}x) \equiv 1$, and its average is $1$ — yet $\int f\,d\mu = \int \phi\,d\mu = 0$ for a non-trivial eigenfunction, so the average cannot equal the product $\int f\,\int g\,\int h = 0$. The product computation fails precisely *at* the eigenfunctions, and only there. So eigenfunctions are exactly the obstruction to randomness.

On the structured side, eigenfunctions are exactly the *content* of the Kronecker system: an ergodic system's eigenfunctions, suitably normalized so $\phi_{\lambda_1}\phi_{\lambda_2} = \phi_{\lambda_1\lambda_2}$, assemble into a map $a\colon X \to G$ to the compact dual group of eigenvalues, intertwining $T$ with a rotation $\tau_{g_0}$, and the closed subspace they span is isomorphic to $L^2(G)$. This is the maximal Kronecker factor of the system — the largest "compact" piece sitting inside $X$. So the very thing that obstructs weak mixing *is* a structured (Kronecker) factor. That is the hinge: the obstruction to one regime is an instance of the other. So the natural first cut of an arbitrary ergodic system is along its maximal Kronecker factor.

Let me close $k = 3$ for a general ergodic system using this. I claim the $3$-AP average for $X$ is controlled entirely by its Kronecker factor. Concretely, the limit of $\frac{1}{N}\sum_n \int h(x)\,f(T^n x)\,g(T^{2n}x)\,d\mu$ depends only on the projections $Q_T h, Q_T f, Q_T g$ of $h,f,g$ onto the eigenfunction subspace. Here is why. Look at $X \times X$ with $\tau = T \times T^2$ and the algebra of functions $\sum f_i(x_1)g_i(x_2)$. A $\tau$-invariant product is made from a $T$-eigenfunction on the first coordinate and a $T^2$-eigenfunction with the reciprocal eigenvalue on the second. But every $T^2$-eigenfunction in an ergodic $T$-system is still lifted from the Kronecker factor of $T$: if $T^2 u=\lambda u$, then the nonzero combinations $Tu\pm \lambda^{1/2}u$ are $T$-eigenfunctions, and $u$ lies in their span. So the invariant subspace for $\tau$ sits inside the tensor square of the $T$-eigenfunction space. Running the mean ergodic theorem for this possibly non-ergodic product system along the diagonal measure, $\frac{1}{N}\sum_n f(T^n x)g(T^{2n}x)$ converges to the conditional expectation of $f \otimes g$ onto that invariant subspace, and the projection only sees $Q_T f$ and $Q_T g$. Multiplying by $h$ and integrating, only $Q_T h$ survives too. So the entire $3$-AP average for $X$ equals the corresponding average computed in the Kronecker factor $G$ for the projected functions $P_G h, P_G f, P_G g$. For recurrence I take $h=f=g=1_A$. Its projection is $p=E(1_A\mid G)$, a nonnegative function on the Kronecker factor with $\int p\,dm_G=\mu(A)>0$, so the compact calculation applies to $p$ and gives a positive limit. Therefore $\lim_N \frac{1}{N}\sum_n \mu(A \cap T^{-n}A \cap T^{-2n}A)$ exists and is positive for every ergodic $X$ and every $A$ with $\mu(A) > 0$. Reducing an arbitrary system to the ergodic case is the easy direction — decompose $\mu$ into its ergodic components, apply the ergodic result on the positive-measure set of components where the conditional measure of $A$ is positive, and integrate. So $k = 3$ is completely proved: multiple recurrence for triples holds for every system, which is to say I have reproved Roth's theorem, and I have done it by exactly the dichotomy I wanted — weakly mixing or controlled by the maximal compact factor.

Now $k \ge 4$, where the real work is, and where I have to be honest about what the $k=3$ argument did and did not use. It used a single split: weak mixing versus the Kronecker (eigenfunction) factor. For longer progressions that single split is not enough. A system can be almost periodic only after I look fibre-by-fibre over a base factor: a skew product or group extension may have compact motion in the fibres even though ordinary eigenfunctions over a point do not capture the needed structure. A circle rotation is only the first structured model; I need systems built from rotations in towers. So the move is to relativize: instead of splitting $X$ once into Kronecker-versus-weak-mixing, I build a tower of structured factors, each one a compact extension of the previous, and only at the top do I have weak mixing relative to the tower.

Let me set up the relativization. The relative version of "compact/Kronecker" over a base factor $Y$ is a *compact extension*: an extension $X \to Y$ where, fibrewise, the dynamics is almost periodic — the analogue of eigenfunctions over a point becomes *generalized eigenfunctions over $Y$*. I make that precise. Over an ergodic base $Y$ with $X \to Y$, disintegrate $\mu = \int \mu_y\,d\nu(y)$ into fibre measures, giving a bundle of Hilbert spaces $\mathcal{H}_y = L^2(X,\mu_y)$. A function $\phi \in L^2(X)$ is a *generalized eigenfunction over $Y$* if the closed $Y$-module spanned by $\{T^n\phi\}$ has finite rank — finite-dimensional fibres. Where a scalar eigenfunction satisfied $T\phi = \lambda\phi$, a generalized eigenfunction satisfies $TH = A(y)H$ for a vector $H = (h_1,\dots,h_r)$ of fibrewise-orthonormal functions and a *unitary matrix-valued* eigenvalue $A(y)$ — the fibres are Hilbert spaces, so "eigenvalue" has to become a unitary on each fibre. The span of all generalized eigenfunctions is the relative analogue of the Kronecker subspace; call it $\mathcal{H}_g(X/Y,T)$. When $\mathcal{H}_g(X/Y,T) = L^2(X)$, the extension is *isometric* (compact); a system built from the trivial system by a sequence of isometric extensions is *distal*. The opposite relative regime, $\mathcal{H}_g(X/Y,T) = L^2(Y)$ — no new generalized eigenfunctions over $Y$ — is the *relatively weak-mixing* extension, equivalent to $X \times_Y X$ being ergodic.

What does an isometric extension concretely look like? It is $X = Y \times S$ with $S = G/L$ a homogeneous space of a compact group, and $T(y,s) = (Ty, \gamma(y)s)$ for a cocycle $\gamma\colon Y \to G$ — a group rotation in the fibre, twisted along the base. The component functions of finite-dimensional representations of $G$ are generalized eigenfunctions, so $\mathcal{H}_g = L^2$. And an ergodic isometric extension is a factor of a *strict group extension* $Y \times G$ with $T(y,g) = (Ty,\gamma(y)g)$ and $\mu = \nu \times m_G$ (Haar on the fibre) — after a reparametrization $\gamma(y) = p(Ty)^{-1}\gamma(y)p(y)$ that pushes the cocycle into a closed subgroup, the ergodic extensions are exactly translates of one Haar-fibred measure. This reduction of isometric extensions to strict group extensions is what makes the induction clean: group extensions are the indecomposable structured building blocks.

The engine that makes generalized eigenfunctions behave is a Hilbert–Schmidt argument relativized over $Y$. If $\psi(x',x'')$ on $X \times_Y X$ is symmetric and $T$-invariant, it defines on each fibre a self-adjoint Hilbert–Schmidt operator $A_y$ on $\mathcal{H}_y$ by $A_y h(x) = \int \psi(x,x')h(x')\,d\mu_y(x')$, and $T$ intertwines them: $U_y A_{Ty} = A_y U_y$. A self-adjoint compact operator has a clean spectral decomposition; the eigenspaces $V_{\lambda,y}$ for $\lambda \neq 0$ are finite-dimensional, depend measurably on $y$, and the intertwining makes them into $T$-invariant finite-rank $Y$-modules — i.e. generalized eigenfunctions. This is how I *manufacture* generalized eigenfunctions whenever an extension fails to be relatively weak mixing: relative non-weak-mixing means $X \times_Y X$ carries a non-trivial invariant function $\psi$, which produces a non-zero compact $A_y$, whose eigenspaces are genuine generalized eigenfunctions over $Y$ that are not already in $L^2(Y)$. So every extension is either relatively weak mixing or admits a non-trivial compact sub-extension. (The other half — that orthogonality of $\mathcal{H}_g(X_1/Y) \otimes \mathcal{H}_g(X_2/Y)$ forces vanishing — is the relative version of "a map into Hilbert space with a.e. orthogonal images is zero," and it gives the tensor-product structure $\mathcal{H}_g(X_1 \times_Y X_2/Y) = \mathcal{H}_g(X_1/Y)\otimes\mathcal{H}_g(X_2/Y)$ that I will need to track the diagonal.)

Now the tower. Start with $Z_0(X) = $ trivial system. Let $Z_1(X)$ be the maximal Kronecker factor (the $k=3$ structured factor). Inductively, $Z_{n+1}(X) = Z(X/Z_n(X),T)$, the maximal isometric extension of $Z_n$ inside $X$, formed from the generalized eigenfunctions $\mathcal{H}_g(X/Z_n)$. This is the *distal series*. Each $Z_n(X)$ is a distal system of order $n$ — a finite tower of isometric extensions. The supremum $X_{\max}$ over all $n$ (transfinitely if needed) is the maximal distal factor, and over $X_{\max}$ the system $X$ is relatively weak mixing — because if it were not, the compact-extension engine would produce new generalized eigenfunctions and the tower would keep growing. So every ergodic system has the shape: a tower of compact (isometric) extensions $Z_0 \to Z_1 \to Z_2 \to \dots \to X_{\max}$, with $X$ a relatively weak-mixing extension on top. This is the structure theorem I was after — the analogue of Roth's single split, iterated.

To make this tower prove recurrence I have to track the diagonal averages themselves. The weak-mixing calculation tells me what a relatively weak-mixing top should do: after conditioning on the right base factor, it contributes no new structured obstruction to the average. The compact rungs need a separate positivity argument, because almost-periodic fibre motion is not random and cannot be replaced by a product limit. The diagonal-measure bookkeeping packages this into two finite tasks: identify the finite distal factor that controls a given $k$-fold average, and then prove positivity on every finite-order distal system.

Form on $X^k$ the diagonal measure $\mu^\Delta$ and let $\mu^*_k$ be a generic limit of $\mu^\Delta$ under $\tau_k = T \times T^2 \times \dots \times T^k$. Then $\mu^*_k$ is "defined over" $Z_{k-2}(X)$ — it is a conditional product measure relative to $Z_{k-2}(X)^k$. In words: the $k$-fold ergodic average is controlled by the order-$(k-2)$ distal factor and nothing higher. For $k=3$ that says it is controlled by $Z_1 = $ the Kronecker factor — exactly the $k=3$ fact I proved by hand. The proof is induction on $k$ via the conditional-product-measure machinery: $\mu^*_2 = \mu \times \mu$ is an absolute product, defined over $Z_0$; and the inductive step uses that the ergodic components of a conditional product measure relative to $\prod Y_i$ are themselves conditional product measures relative to $\prod \mathcal{H}_g(X_i/Y_i)$ — pushing the characteristic factor up by exactly one isometric step each time $k$ increases by one. So multiple recurrence for $X$ reduces to multiple recurrence for the finite-order distal factor $Z_{k-2}(X)$, with $f$ replaced by its conditional expectation $E(f \mid Z_{k-2})$. The tower I have to climb is only $k-2$ rungs tall.

So everything comes down to: a finite-order distal system is SZ. Induct on the distal order. Order $0$ is the trivial system, SZ vacuously. For the step, since a distal system of order $n+1$ is a factor of an ergodic group extension of a distal system of order $n$, and an ergodic group extension is a strict group extension (of a possibly smaller group), it suffices to show: if $Y$ is SZ and $X = Y \times G$ is a strict group extension of $Y$, then $X$ is SZ. This is the heart of the matter, the relativized compact case, and the support criterion I need is $\mu^\Delta \prec \mu^*_k$: every $k$-tuple of nonnegative functions whose product integrates to zero against $\mu^*_k$ also integrates to zero against the diagonal $\mu^\Delta$. That criterion forces the positivity I want, because a nonzero nonnegative $f$ has positive diagonal integral $\int f^k\,d\mu$.

Here is the group-extension step. Write $X = Y \times G$, $\widehat G = G^k$, so $X^k = Y^k \times \widehat G$, and let $G$ act on $X^k$ by simultaneous right translation $\rho_g$ in the fibre, commuting with both $T = T\times\dots\times T$ and $\tau_k$. Project to $Y^k$: by the inductive SZ hypothesis on $Y$, $\nu^\Delta \prec \nu^*_k$ on $Y^k$. Lifting, the diagonal $\mu^\Delta$ is dominated by $m_G * \mu^*_k$, the Haar-averaged limit measure: after Haar-averaging each fibre function, the resulting functions depend only on $y$, $\mu^*_k$ projects to $\nu^*_k$, and the SZ property of $Y$ applies. Now I want to un-average: shrink the averaging neighbourhood $V \ni e$ in $G$ down toward the identity while keeping the domination $\mu^\Delta \prec \lambda_V * \mu^*_k$. Decompose Haar measure of $G$ into small pieces $\lambda_{V_i}$; domination by a convex combination passes to one piece (using ergodicity of $\mu^\Delta$ under $T$, and that supporting sets behave well under convex combinations: $\mathcal{S}_0(p\theta' + (1-p)\theta'') = \mathcal{S}_0(\theta') \cup \mathcal{S}_0(\theta'')$). Let $\Sigma$ be the set of $g \in G$ admitting arbitrarily small neighbourhoods $V$ with $\mu^\Delta \prec \lambda_V * \mu^*_k$. Then $\Sigma$ is non-empty and closed, and it is a semigroup: if $\mu^\Delta \prec \lambda_{V_1}*\mu^*_k$ and $\mu^\Delta \prec \lambda_{V_2}*\mu^*_k$, then because $\lambda_{V_2}*\mu^*_k$ is $\tau_k$-invariant, chaining the two dominations gives $\mu^\Delta \prec \lambda_{V_1}*\lambda_{V_2}*\mu^*_k \prec \lambda_{V_1 V_2}*\mu^*_k$. A closed subsemigroup of a compact group is a group, so $\Sigma$ contains the identity: $\mu^\Delta \prec \lambda_V * \mu^*_k$ for every neighbourhood $V$ of $e$.

To finish I push $V \to \{e\}$ and show $\mu^\Delta \prec \mu^*_k$ itself, by contradiction. If not, some nonnegative $f$ (I can reduce to a single $f = f_1 f_2\cdots f_k$) has $\int f^{\otimes k}\,d\mu^*_k = 0$ but $\int f\,d\mu > 0$. View $f\colon Y \times G \to [0,1]$ as a map $\Phi\colon Y \to L^1(G)$, $\Phi_y(g) = f(y,g)$; since $f$ is not a.e. zero, the distribution $\Phi(\nu)$ on $L^1(G)$ has a non-zero point $\phi$ in its support with values in $[0,1]$. On the set $A = \{y : \|\Phi_y - \phi\| < \alpha/4k\}$ (positive measure, where $\alpha = \int \phi\,dm_G > 0$), a uniform-continuity estimate gives, for a small neighbourhood $U$ of $e$ and $(y_i,g_i) \in (A \times U)^k$, that the Haar-averaged product $\int \prod_i \Phi_{y_i}(g_i g)\,dm_G(g) > \alpha/4$. Translating this into the averaging operator $Q f = \int (f \circ \rho_g)\,d\lambda_\Delta(g)$ (Haar over the diagonal subgroup $G_\Delta \subset G^k$, which satisfies $\lambda_\Delta * \mu^*_k = \mu^*_k$, hence $\int Qf\,d\mu^*_k = \int f\,d\mu^*_k$), and using a neighbourhood $V$ with $VV^{-1} \subset U$, I get $\int Q(f^{\otimes k})\,d\mu^*_k \ge c\int (f'^{\otimes k})\,d\lambda_V * \mu^*_k$ for $f' = 1_{A \times V} f$ and some $c > 0$. But the left side equals $\int f^{\otimes k}\,d\mu^*_k = 0$ by the diagonal-subgroup invariance, while the right side is *positive* by $\mu^\Delta \prec \lambda_V * \mu^*_k$ for $V \ni e$ (which I just proved) and $f'$ not a.e. zero. Contradiction. So $\mu^\Delta \prec \mu^*_k$: the strict group extension $X$ is SZ. By the induction on distal order, every finite-order distal system is SZ.

Now the pieces fit. The diagonal-measure structure theorem ($\mu^*_k$ defined over $Z_{k-2}$) reduces multiple recurrence for an arbitrary ergodic $X$ to multiple recurrence for the finite-order distal factor $Z_{k-2}(X)$; that factor is SZ by the group-extension induction; therefore $X$ is SZ. Spelling out positivity, for every ergodic $(X,\mathcal{B},\mu,T)$, every nonnegative $f \in L^\infty$ not a.e. zero,
$$\liminf_{N-M\to\infty}\ \frac{1}{N-M}\sum_{n=M+1}^{N} \int f(x)\,f(T^n x)\cdots f(T^{(k-1)n}x)\,d\mu(x) \;>\; 0.$$
Taking $f = 1_B$ gives multiple recurrence for ergodic systems; ergodic decomposition lifts it to all systems; and the correspondence principle I built at the start turns it into arbitrarily long arithmetic progressions in any set of positive upper density. That is Szemerédi's theorem, and now I can say what forced the progressions: every system has a distal tower with a relatively weak-mixing top, and for a fixed progression length the diagonal average is already controlled by a finite structured piece of that tower. On that finite distal factor, almost-periodicity and the group-extension induction force a positive average; the weak-mixing part supplies no additional obstruction.

On the density side, given $A \subseteq \mathbb{Z}$ with $\bar d(A)>0$, I identify it with $a\in\{0,1\}^{\mathbb{Z}}$, take the orbit closure $X=\overline{\{T^n a:n\in\mathbb{Z}\}}$ under the left shift, take the clopen cylinder $E=\{x:x(0)=1\}$, and take a weak-$*$ limit $\mu$ of $\mu_N=(2N+1)^{-1}\sum_{|n|\le N}\delta_{T^n a}$ along a density-realizing subsequence. Then $\mu(E)>0$, $\mu$ is $T$-invariant, and any nonempty $E\cap T^{-n}E\cap\dots\cap T^{-(k-1)n}E$ contains an orbit point $T^m a$, hence gives $m,m+n,\dots,m+(k-1)n\in A$.

The recurrence statement I have proved is: for every measure-preserving $(X,\mathcal{B},\mu,T)$, every $B$ with $\mu(B)>0$, and every $k$, there is $n>0$ with $\mu(B\cap T^{-n}B\cap\dots\cap T^{-(k-1)n}B)>0$, indeed with positive Cesàro liminf for the corresponding nonnegative-function averages. For $k=2$ this is Poincaré recurrence; for $k=3$ the average is controlled by the maximal Kronecker factor and the compact calculation makes it positive; for general $k$ the generic diagonal measure is defined over $Z_{k-2}$, and the finite distal factor $Z_{k-2}$ is SZ by the strict group-extension induction. Density becomes recurrence, recurrence gives a clopen orbit configuration, and the orbit configuration is the arithmetic progression.
