Let me start from the thing I actually have to produce, because the abstract operator-algebra question has by now been ground down to something completely concrete. Kadison-Singer — unique extension of pure states from the diagonal algebra to all of $B(\ell_2)$ — is equivalent, after Anderson's compactness reduction and then Weaver's discrepancy reformulation, to a finite statement about chopping up a sum of rank-one matrices. The cleanest version I keep coming back to: I have small vectors $v_1,\dots,v_m\in\mathbb{C}^d$ that resolve the identity, $\sum_i v_iv_i^*=I$, with each $\|v_i\|^2$ tiny, and I want to two-color the indices so each color class $\sum_{i\in S_j}v_iv_i^*$ is bounded strictly away from $I$. Weaver showed a universal-constant version of that implies everything. And the partition version is a special case of a more flexible question: independent random vectors $v_i$ with $\sum_i\mathbb{E}\,v_iv_i^*=I$ and $\mathbb{E}\,\|v_i\|^2\le\epsilon$ — how small can $\big\|\sum_i v_iv_i^*\big\|$ be made, over outcomes of the distribution? A two-coloring is just the case where each $v_i$ picks, at random, which color it lands in.

So the real target is: with *nonzero probability*, $\big\|\sum_i v_iv_i^*\big\|$ is small, at a *dimension-free* scale.

The instinct of everyone who has touched this — and my own first instinct — is matrix concentration. These are sums of independent random rank-one matrices; that's exactly what Ahlswede-Winter and Rudelson are for. Run the matrix Chernoff machinery: $\mathbb{E}\,\mathrm{Tr}\,e^{\theta\sum_i v_iv_i^*}$ factors well enough, and you get $\big\|\sum_i v_iv_i^*\big\|\le C(\epsilon)\log n$ with high probability. And I hit the wall immediately, twice over. First, the scale is wrong: there's a $\log n$ in there, and $n$ is the dimension. Paving needs the number of pieces $r$ to be *independent of the size of the matrix*; any bound that grows with $n$ is dead on arrival. Second, even setting the scale aside, "with high probability" is the wrong kind of statement. The outcome I want can be exponentially rare. Picture the signing problem from the Ramanujan side of this same circle of ideas — a union of disjoint cliques has a good signing for only an exponentially small fraction of all signings. Concentration tells me about the *typical* outcome; it is exactly blind to a good-but-rare one. What I need is not concentration but *existence*: some outcome is good, however unlikely.

That reframing is the whole game. I don't want to control the bulk; I want a first-moment existence statement at constant scale. And the first-moment method, done naively on the norm, gives nothing — $\mathbb{E}\,\big\|\sum_i v_iv_i^*\big\|$ could be large even if some outcome is small, and vice versa, because the norm is a max, not an average.

The norm of a positive semidefinite matrix is its largest eigenvalue, which is the largest root of its characteristic polynomial $\chi[M](x)=\det(xI-M)$. So instead of averaging the norm — a max of eigenvalues — let me average the *characteristic polynomial*, coefficient by coefficient, and look at the largest root of that. The hope is: if I can show that the largest root of *some* outcome's characteristic polynomial is at most the largest root of the *expected* characteristic polynomial, then bounding the largest root of one fixed, computable polynomial gives me a good outcome. Existence for free, no probability bound needed.

But that hope is false in general, and I should be honest about why, because the failure is what tells me what structure to demand. Roots of an average of polynomials have, in general, nothing to do with the roots of the summands. Average $(x-1)(x-2)$ and $(x-3)(x-4)$: that's $\tfrac12(2x^2-10x+14)=x^2-5x+7$, with complex roots $2.5\pm\sqrt{3}\,i$. The average of two real-rooted quadratics isn't even real-rooted, let alone with a root controlled by the four input roots. So I can't just average and hope.

When *does* averaging polynomials average their roots? The classical notion is interlacing. Say $g$ of degree $n-1$ interlaces $f$ of degree $n$ if their roots alternate, $\beta_1\le\alpha_1\le\beta_2\le\dots\le\alpha_{n-1}\le\beta_n$. And say $f_1,\dots,f_k$ have a *common interlacing* if a single $g$ slips between the roots of all of them simultaneously — equivalently, there are nested intervals $I_n\le\dots\le I_1$ on the line, the $j$-th roots of every $f_i$ all living in $I_j$. If that holds, here's why the largest root of the sum is controlled. Let $\alpha_{n-1}$ be the largest root of the interlacer $g$. Each $f_i$ has positive leading coefficient, so it's positive far to the right; and each $f_i$ has *exactly one* root $\ge\alpha_{n-1}$, so $f_i(\alpha_{n-1})\le 0$. Then $f_\emptyset=\sum_i f_i$ is also $\le 0$ at $\alpha_{n-1}$ and eventually positive, so it has a root $\beta_n\ge\alpha_{n-1}$ — its largest. Now $f_\emptyset(\beta_n)=0=\sum_i f_i(\beta_n)$, so *some* $f_i$ has $f_i(\beta_n)\ge 0$; but that $f_i$ was $\le 0$ at $\alpha_{n-1}$ and has only one root past $\alpha_{n-1}$, so its largest root lies in $[\alpha_{n-1},\beta_n]$. There it is: some $f_i$ has its largest root $\le$ the largest root of the sum. The average really does dominate a summand, *provided* a common interlacing exists.

One application of this isn't enough, though — I have $m$ independent vectors, each with several possible values, so the outcomes are indexed by a big product set $S_1\times\cdots\times S_m$, and I can't expect all of those leaf polynomials to share one common interlacing. So I do it level by level. Call a collection of real-rooted leaf polynomials $\{f_{s_1,\dots,s_m}\}$ an *interlacing family* if at every internal node of the product tree the children share a common interlacing: for each partial assignment $s_1,\dots,s_k$, the polynomials $\{f_{s_1,\dots,s_k,t}\}_t$ over the next coordinate have a common interlacing. Then I walk down the tree. At the root, the children over $S_1$ have a common interlacing and sum to $f_\emptyset$, so by the lemma some child $f_{s_1}$ has largest root $\le$ largest root of $f_\emptyset$. At $s_1$, its children over $S_2$ have a common interlacing and sum to $f_{s_1}$, so some $f_{s_1,s_2}$ has largest root $\le$ that of $f_{s_1}$. Inductively, I reach a leaf $f_{s_1,\dots,s_m}$ whose largest root is $\le$ the largest root of $f_\emptyset$. Since $f_\emptyset$ is the sum over all outcomes — i.e. (up to the probability weights) the *expected* characteristic polynomial — I've reduced the whole problem to two things: prove the leaf polynomials form an interlacing family, and bound the largest root of the expected characteristic polynomial.

So now I need two structural facts, and both have to survive the fact that my vectors are *not* identically distributed.

I want to pause on that caveat, because it's the crux of why this is hard and the earlier work didn't already do it. There's a baby version of everything I just said that works when the $v_i$ are i.i.d. and isotropic, $\mathbb{E}\,vv^*=cI$. In that world, Cauchy's interlacing theorem already gives me common interlacings: $\chi[A]$ interlaces $\chi[A+vv^*]$ for any rank-one bump. And the expected update is literally a differential operator. Let me check that, because it's the seed of the right idea. By the matrix determinant lemma, $\det(xI-A-vv^*)=\det(xI-A)(1-v^*(xI-A)^{-1}v)$, and $v^*(xI-A)^{-1}v=\mathrm{Tr}((xI-A)^{-1}vv^*)$, so taking expectations with $\mathbb{E}\,vv^*=cI$,
$$\mathbb{E}\,\chi[A+vv^*](x)=\det(xI-A)\big(1-c\,\mathrm{Tr}(xI-A)^{-1}\big)=\chi[A](x)-c\,\chi[A]'(x),$$
because $\chi[A]'(x)=\sum_i\prod_{j\ne i}(x-\lambda_j)=\chi[A](x)\sum_i\frac1{x-\lambda_i}=\chi[A](x)\,\mathrm{Tr}(xI-A)^{-1}$. So $\mathbb{E}\,\chi[A+vv^*]=(1-cD)\chi[A]$, where $D=\frac{d}{dx}$. The operator $1-cD$ preserves real-rootedness — look at $\frac{f-cf'}{f}=1-c\sum_i\frac1{x-\lambda_i}$, a strictly decreasing function between consecutive poles, so by the intermediate value theorem $f-cf'$ picks up a real root in each gap, $n$ of them — and it also preserves common interlacings. Start from $A=0$, $\chi=x^n$, iterate: after $k$ updates the expected polynomial is $(1-cD)^k x^n$, a scaled associated Laguerre polynomial, whose extreme roots are classically known. That recovers Bourgain-Tzafriri restricted invertibility in the isotropic case, and it's *entirely univariate*: every covariance is a multiple of the identity, so all the operators commute and act on a single polynomial $x^n$. Beautiful, but it leans completely on $\mathbb{E}\,vv^*\propto I$.

In my problem the covariances $A_i=\mathbb{E}\,v_iv_i^*$ are general positive semidefinite matrices and they don't commute. So the univariate "one operator, one polynomial" collapse is gone. I need a representation of the expected characteristic polynomial that keeps the $A_i$ separate and still factors through simple operators. Let me try to find it by mimicking the isotropic computation but giving each $A_i$ its own variable.

Take one vector first, with covariance $A=\mathbb{E}\,vv^*$, and a general matrix in place of $xI-A$. I claim
$$\mathbb{E}\,\det(M-vv^*)=(1-\partial_t)\det(M+t\,\mathbb{E}\,vv^*)\big|_{t=0}.$$
Check it for invertible $M$: by the determinant lemma, $\mathbb{E}\,\det(M-vv^*)=\mathbb{E}\,\det(M)(1-v^*M^{-1}v)=\det(M)-\det(M)\,\mathrm{Tr}(M^{-1}\mathbb{E}\,vv^*)$. On the other side, Jacobi's formula gives $\partial_t\det(M+tA)=\det(M+tA)\,\mathrm{Tr}((M+tA)^{-1}A)$, so $(1-\partial_t)\det(M+tA)=\det(M+tA)-\det(M+tA)\,\mathrm{Tr}((M+tA)^{-1}A)$; at $t=0$ that's $\det(M)-\det(M)\,\mathrm{Tr}(M^{-1}A)$. Same thing. For singular $M$, both sides are polynomials in the entries of $M$ and the identity holds on the dense set of invertible matrices, so by continuity everywhere. Good — a single random rank-one subtraction is exactly the operator $1-\partial_t$ acting on a determinant that carries the covariance through a fresh variable.

Now iterate over independent $v_1,\dots,v_m$, one fresh variable $z_i$ per vector. I'll show by induction that for any $M$,
$$\mathbb{E}\,\det\Big(M-\sum_{i=1}^k v_iv_i^*\Big)=\Big(\prod_{i=1}^k(1-\partial_{z_i})\Big)\det\Big(M+\sum_{i=1}^k z_iA_i\Big)\Big|_{z=0}.$$
Base case $k=0$ trivial. For the step, peel off $v_k$ using independence and the one-vector identity I just proved, with $M-\sum_{i<k}v_iv_i^*$ in the role of "$M$":
$$\mathbb{E}\,\det\Big(M-\sum_{i\le k}v_iv_i^*\Big)=\mathbb{E}_{v_1,\dots,v_{k-1}}\,(1-\partial_{z_k})\det\Big(M+z_kA_k-\sum_{i<k}v_iv_i^*\Big)\Big|_{z_k=0}.$$
Pull the $1-\partial_{z_k}$ and the evaluation outside the remaining expectation (linearity), then apply the inductive hypothesis to the inner expectation with $M+z_kA_k$ in place of $M$:
$$=(1-\partial_{z_k})\Big(\prod_{i<k}(1-\partial_{z_i})\Big)\det\Big(M+z_kA_k+\sum_{i<k}z_iA_i\Big)\Big|_{z_1=\dots=z_{k}=0}=\Big(\prod_{i\le k}(1-\partial_{z_i})\Big)\det\Big(M+\sum_{i\le k}z_iA_i\Big)\Big|_{z=0}.$$
Set $M=xI$, $k=m$, and I have the expected characteristic polynomial in closed form:
$$\mathbb{E}\,\chi\Big[\sum_i v_iv_i^*\Big](x)=\Big(\prod_{i=1}^m(1-\partial_{z_i})\Big)\det\Big(xI+\sum_i z_iA_i\Big)\Big|_{z=0}.$$
This object depends only on the covariances $A_i$. I'll name it the *mixed characteristic polynomial* $\mu[A_1,\dots,A_m](x)$ — "mixed" because, when $m=d$, its constant coefficient is the mixed discriminant $\big(\prod_i\partial_{z_i}\big)\det(\sum_i z_iA_i)$ from Gurvits's world. The point is structural: a product of dead-simple operators $1-\partial_{z_i}$ applied to one determinant, with each $A_i$ riding its own variable. The non-commuting covariances no longer have to be reconciled into a single univariate operator; they're held apart by separate variables and only fused at the end by $z_i\mapsto 0$.

Now I need that this thing is real-rooted — that's what makes the interlacing machinery even apply. The variables-apart form is exactly what lets me see it. Univariate real-rootedness has a multivariate cousin: a polynomial $p(z_1,\dots,z_m)$ is *stable* if it never vanishes when every $z_i$ has strictly positive imaginary part, *real stable* if also its coefficients are real; for one variable, real stable means real-rooted. Three facts close the loop. First, $\det(\sum_i z_iA_i)$ — equivalently $\det(xI+\sum_i z_iA_i)$ with $x$ as one more variable — is real stable when the $A_i$ are positive semidefinite (Borcea-Brändén): if all the $z_i$ have positive imaginary part then $\mathrm{Im}(\sum_i z_iA_i)\succ0$, so the matrix is nonsingular and the determinant is nonzero. Second, $1-\partial_{z_i}$ preserves real stability — I want to be able to derive this rather than cite it, so let me see *why*. Fix all the other variables to numbers with positive imaginary part; the restriction $q(z_i)$ is a univariate stable polynomial, meaning all its roots lie in the closed lower half-plane. Factor $q(z)=c\prod_j(z-w_j)$ with $\mathrm{Im}\,w_j\le 0$. Then $q(z)-q'(z)=q(z)\big(1-\sum_j\frac1{z-w_j}\big)$, and if $\mathrm{Im}\,z>0$ then each $\frac1{z-w_j}$ has *negative* imaginary part (since $z-w_j$ has positive imaginary part), so $1-\sum_j\frac1{z-w_j}$ has positive imaginary part and is nonzero, while $q(z)\ne0$; hence $q-q'$ has no root in the upper half-plane either. (Said in classical language, this is the Marden / Gauss-Lucas-type statement that subtracting a multiple of the derivative keeps the zeros inside the half-plane region.) So $1-\partial_{z_i}$ keeps the no-roots-in-the-upper-orthant property. Third, setting a variable to a real number preserves real stability. Apply all $m$ operators $1-\partial_{z_i}$ to the real stable $\det(xI+\sum_i z_iA_i)$, then set every $z_i=0$: still real stable, and now univariate in $x$, hence real-rooted. The mixed characteristic polynomial is real-rooted.

That gives the leaf polynomials a chance, but I still owe the interlacing-family property. Here's where the convex-combination characterization earns its keep. Common interlacing of $\{f_t\}$ is equivalent (Dedieu; Fell; Chudnovsky-Seymour) to: every convex combination $\sum_t\lambda_t f_t$ is real-rooted. So at an internal node with partial assignment $j_1,\dots,j_k$ fixed and the next coordinate $t$ ranging over the support of $v_{k+1}$, I take the children
$$q_{j_1,\dots,j_k,t}(x)=\Big(\prod_{i\le k}p_{i,j_i}\Big)\,\mathbb{E}_{v_{k+2},\dots,v_m}\,\chi\Big[\textstyle\sum_{i\le k}w_{i,j_i}w_{i,j_i}^*+w_{k+1,t}w_{k+1,t}^*+\sum_{i\ge k+2}v_iv_i^*\Big](x),$$
and a convex combination $\sum_t\lambda_t q_{\dots,t}$ is what I get if I let coordinate $k+1$ itself be a fresh random vector $u_{k+1}$ taking value $w_{k+1,t}$ with probability $\lambda_t$. But then the combination is, up to the fixed constant $\prod_{i\le k}p_{i,j_i}$, a *mixed characteristic polynomial* of the covariances $\sum_{i\le k}w_{i,j_i}w_{i,j_i}^*$ (as a degenerate covariance), $\mathbb{E}\,u_{k+1}u_{k+1}^*$, and the $A_i$ for $i\ge k+2$ — and I just proved every mixed characteristic polynomial is real-rooted. So every convex combination is real-rooted, the children have a common interlacing at every node, and the leaf polynomials form an interlacing family. That's the second structural fact, and the only place real stability was needed.

So the entire problem now sits on one quantitative question: bound the largest root of $\mu[A_1,\dots,A_m](x)$ when $\sum_i A_i=I$ and each $\mathrm{Tr}(A_i)\le\epsilon$. The interlacing family hands me a leaf at most as large, and a leaf is the characteristic polynomial of an actual outcome $\sum_i w_{i,j_i}w_{i,j_i}^*$, whose largest root is its norm. Bound the root, and I'm done.

For the bound, I want to redo the BSS barrier argument, but it was univariate and my polynomial is naturally multivariate before I collapse it. Let me first move the evaluation point to make the structure visible. With $\sum_i A_i=I$,
$$\mu[A_1,\dots,A_m](x)=\Big(\prod_i(1-\partial_{y_i})\Big)\det\Big(\sum_i y_iA_i\Big)\Big|_{y_1=\dots=y_m=x}.$$
Why this works: substitute $y_i=z_i+x$. Then $\partial_{y_i}f(y_i)|_{y_i=z_i+x}=\partial_{z_i}f(z_i+x)$, and $\det(\sum_i y_iA_i)=\det(xI+\sum_i z_iA_i)$ because $\sum_i xA_i=xI$. Setting $y_i=x$ is the same as $z_i=0$, so this is exactly my earlier expression. The advantage: now I have a clean multivariate polynomial $Q(y_1,\dots,y_m)=\big(\prod_i(1-\partial_{y_i})\big)\det(\sum_i y_iA_i)$ with $\mu(x)=Q(x,\dots,x)$, built from the seed $P(y)=\det(\sum_i y_iA_i)$ by applying $1-\partial_{y_i}$ once per coordinate, and I want to push a single point up the diagonal until it's past all the roots of $Q$.

I need a multivariate notion of "past the roots." Say $z\in\mathbb{R}^m$ is *above the roots* of $p$ if $p(z+t)>0$ for every nonnegative $t$ — i.e. $p$ is positive on the whole orthant hanging off $z$ in the increasing direction. If I can show $(1+\sqrt\epsilon)^2\cdot\mathbf1$ is above the roots of $Q$, then in particular $Q(x,\dots,x)=\mu(x)>0$ for all $x\ge(1+\sqrt\epsilon)^2$, so the largest root of $\mu$ is at most $(1+\sqrt\epsilon)^2$. So I want to start above the roots of $P$ and stay above the roots as I apply each $1-\partial_{y_i}$, controlling how far the point has to climb.

The control device, copying BSS, is a barrier function — but vector-valued, one per direction. For real stable $p$ and $z$ above its roots, set
$$\Phi^i_p(z)=\frac{\partial_{z_i}p(z)}{p(z)}=\partial_{z_i}\log p(z).$$
Restrict $p$ to the line through $z$ in coordinate $i$: that univariate restriction is real-rooted with roots $\lambda_1,\dots,\lambda_r$ (real, since real stability survives fixing the other coordinates to reals), so $\Phi^i_p(z)=\sum_j\frac1{z_i-\lambda_j}$, the same Stieltjes-type sum as BSS's potential $\sum_j\frac1{u-\lambda_j}$, just one per direction. Above the roots, $z_i>\lambda_j$, so $\Phi^i_p>0$ and it blows up as $z_i$ descends toward a root — it measures how close coordinate $i$ is to crossing a root.

I need two analytic properties of $\Phi^i_p$ above the roots: monotone decreasing and convex in every coordinate. The diagonal direction $i=j$ is immediate: $\Phi^i_p=\sum_j\frac1{z_i-\lambda_j}$ is decreasing in $z_i$, and $\partial^2_{z_i}\frac1{z_i-\lambda_j}=\frac{2}{(z_i-\lambda_j)^3}>0$ since $z_i>\lambda_j$, so convex. The cross direction $i\ne j$ is where I'd get stuck if I only had real-rootedness, because now I'm asking how the barrier in direction $i$ changes as I move in direction $j$, and the univariate restriction tells me nothing across two variables. This is exactly the place the *bivariate* structure of real stability has to be used. Fix all variables but $z_i,z_j$; the bivariate restriction is real stable, and by Helton-Vinnikov (with the Lax-conjecture representation of Lewis-Parrilo-Ramana) every bivariate real stable polynomial of degree $d$ is $\pm\det(z_iB_i+z_jB_j+C)$ for positive semidefinite $B_i,B_j$ and Hermitian $C$. The sign is $+$ here because for large $t$, $t(B_i+B_j)+C\succ0$ has positive determinant and the restriction is positive far up the diagonal. Now Jacobi's formula computes the barrier as a trace:
$$\Phi^i_p=\frac{\partial_{z_i}\det(z_iB_i+z_jB_j+C)}{\det(z_iB_i+z_jB_j+C)}=\mathrm{Tr}\big((z_iB_i+z_jB_j+C)^{-1}B_i\big).$$
Write $M=z_iB_i+z_jB_j+C$, which is positive definite above the roots (if it weren't, some shift $M+t(B_i+B_j)$ would be singular, putting a root above $z$). Bump $z_j$ by $\delta$:
$$\Phi^i_p(z+\delta e_j)=\mathrm{Tr}\big((M+\delta B_j)^{-1}B_i\big)=\mathrm{Tr}\big((I+\delta B_jM^{-1})^{-1}B_iM^{-1}\big),$$
and for small $\delta$ expand $(I+\delta B_jM^{-1})^{-1}=I-\delta B_jM^{-1}+\delta^2(B_jM^{-1})^2-\cdots$. Reading off the linear coefficient,
$$\partial_{z_j}\Phi^i_p=-\,\mathrm{Tr}\big(B_jM^{-1}B_iM^{-1}\big).$$
Both $B_j$ and $M^{-1}B_iM^{-1}$ are positive semidefinite, and the trace of a product of positive semidefinite matrices is nonnegative (decompose each into rank-one PSD pieces and use $\mathrm{Tr}(AB)=\mathrm{Tr}(BA)$; each cross term is $\ge0$), so $\partial_{z_j}\Phi^i_p\le 0$ — monotonicity across directions. The quadratic coefficient gives
$$\partial^2_{z_j}\Phi^i_p=\mathrm{Tr}\big((B_jM^{-1})^2B_iM^{-1}\big)=\mathrm{Tr}\big((B_jM^{-1}B_j)(M^{-1}B_iM^{-1})\big)\ge0,$$
again a trace of a product of PSD matrices, so convex. Both properties hold in every direction. (I should note this is the one heavy ingredient — the determinantal representation — and there are more elementary routes to the same monotonicity-and-convexity, but this one makes the trace identities transparent, so I'll keep it.)

Now the inductive engine. The bare statement I get from monotonicity alone: if $\Phi^i_p(z)<1$ then $z$ is above the roots of $p-\partial_{z_i}p$. Proof: for nonnegative $t$, monotonicity gives $\Phi^i_p(z+t)\le\Phi^i_p(z)<1$, i.e. $\partial_{z_i}p(z+t)<p(z+t)$, so $(p-\partial_{z_i}p)(z+t)>0$. Good — but this alone won't survive $m$ iterations, because each $1-\partial_{z_j}$ operator can push the barrier *up*, and after a few of them the barriers could blow past $1$ and the argument dies. The naive iteration runs straight into that wall. The fix, again from BSS but now in $m$ directions: don't just stay above the roots, keep the barriers *bounded away* from $1$, and pay for each $1-\partial_{z_j}$ by stepping the point up in direction $j$ by a fixed amount $\delta$, using convexity to make the step exactly cancel the barrier's tendency to rise.

Concretely I want: if $\Phi^j_p(z)\le 1-\frac1\delta$, then for every $i$,
$$\Phi^i_{p-\partial_{z_j}p}(z+\delta e_j)\le\Phi^i_p(z).$$
First compute the new barrier in terms of the old ones. Writing $\partial_i$ for $\partial_{z_i}$,
$$\Phi^i_{p-\partial_j p}=\frac{\partial_i(p-\partial_j p)}{p-\partial_j p}=\frac{\partial_i\big((1-\Phi^j_p)p\big)}{(1-\Phi^j_p)p}=\frac{(1-\Phi^j_p)\partial_i p+(\partial_i(1-\Phi^j_p))p}{(1-\Phi^j_p)p}=\Phi^i_p-\frac{\partial_i\Phi^j_p}{1-\Phi^j_p}=\Phi^i_p-\frac{\partial_j\Phi^i_p}{1-\Phi^j_p},$$
using $\partial_i\Phi^j_p=\partial_i\partial_j\log p=\partial_j\Phi^i_p$. So the claim $\Phi^i_{p-\partial_j p}(z+\delta e_j)\le\Phi^i_p(z)$ becomes
$$-\frac{\partial_j\Phi^i_p(z+\delta e_j)}{1-\Phi^j_p(z+\delta e_j)}\le\Phi^i_p(z)-\Phi^i_p(z+\delta e_j).$$
Convexity in direction $j$ gives the lower bound on the right side: $\delta\cdot\big(-\partial_j\Phi^i_p(z+\delta e_j)\big)\le\Phi^i_p(z)-\Phi^i_p(z+\delta e_j)$ (the chord-versus-tangent inequality $f(x)-f(x+\delta)\ge-\delta f'(x+\delta)$ for convex $f$). So it suffices to show
$$-\frac{\partial_j\Phi^i_p(z+\delta e_j)}{1-\Phi^j_p(z+\delta e_j)}\le\delta\cdot\big(-\partial_j\Phi^i_p(z+\delta e_j)\big).$$
By monotonicity $-\partial_j\Phi^i_p(z+\delta e_j)\ge0$; if it's zero the inequality is trivial, otherwise divide it out to reduce to
$$\frac1{1-\Phi^j_p(z+\delta e_j)}\le\delta.$$
And monotonicity once more gives $\Phi^j_p(z+\delta e_j)\le\Phi^j_p(z)$, so this is implied by $\frac1{1-\Phi^j_p(z)}\le\delta$, which is exactly the hypothesis $\Phi^j_p(z)\le1-\frac1\delta$. Done — applying $1-\partial_{z_j}$ and shifting coordinate $j$ up by $\delta$ leaves every barrier no larger than it was. The barriers don't accumulate; they only fall or hold.

Now I just run it with the right numbers. Start from $P(y)=\det(\sum_i y_iA_i)$. Pick a starting height $t$ and check the all-$t$ point is above the roots: $\det\big(\sum_i tA_i\big)=\det(tI)=t^d>0$ since $\sum_i A_i=I$, so $t\mathbf1\in\mathrm{Ab}(P)$. The barrier of the seed is clean — by Jacobi,
$$\Phi^i_P(y)=\frac{\partial_{y_i}\det(\sum_j y_jA_j)}{\det(\sum_j y_jA_j)}=\mathrm{Tr}\Big(\big(\textstyle\sum_j y_jA_j\big)^{-1}A_i\Big),$$
so at the all-$t$ point, $\Phi^i_P(t\mathbf1)=\mathrm{Tr}\big((tI)^{-1}A_i\big)=\mathrm{Tr}(A_i)/t\le\epsilon/t$. Call $\phi=\epsilon/t$. I need this $\le1-\frac1\delta$ so the inductive lemma fires, and I want the final climb $t+\delta$ as small as possible. Set $\delta=\frac1{1-\phi}$, the threshold exactly. Then climbing: define $P_k=\big(\prod_{i\le k}(1-\partial_{y_i})\big)P$, with $P_m=Q$; let $x^k$ be the point that is $t+\delta$ in the first $k$ coordinates and $t$ in the rest. Inductively, $x^k$ is above the roots of $P_k$ and every $\Phi^i_{P_k}(x^k)\le\phi$. The step from $k$ to $k+1$ applies $1-\partial_{y_{k+1}}$ and shifts coordinate $k+1$ from $t$ to $t+\delta$; the barrier lemma (its hypothesis $\Phi^{k+1}_{P_k}(x^k)\le\phi=1-\frac1\delta$ holds) keeps all barriers $\le\phi$, and the "$\Phi<1$ implies above roots" lemma keeps the point above the roots. After $m$ steps the point is $(t+\delta)\mathbf1$, above the roots of $P_m=Q$, so the largest root of $\mu(x)=Q(x,\dots,x)$ is at most $t+\delta$.

Now optimize $t$. I want to minimize $t+\delta=t+\frac1{1-\epsilon/t}=t+\frac{t}{t-\epsilon}$ over $t>\epsilon$. Try $t=\sqrt\epsilon+\epsilon$. Then $\phi=\frac{\epsilon}{\sqrt\epsilon+\epsilon}=\frac{\sqrt\epsilon}{1+\sqrt\epsilon}$, and $1-\phi=\frac1{1+\sqrt\epsilon}$, so $\delta=\frac1{1-\phi}=1+\sqrt\epsilon$. Therefore
$$t+\delta=(\sqrt\epsilon+\epsilon)+(1+\sqrt\epsilon)=1+2\sqrt\epsilon+\epsilon=(1+\sqrt\epsilon)^2.$$
The largest root of the mixed characteristic polynomial is at most $(1+\sqrt\epsilon)^2$. (And this $t$ is the minimizer: $\frac{d}{dt}\big(t+\frac{t}{t-\epsilon}\big)=1-\frac{\epsilon}{(t-\epsilon)^2}=0$ at $(t-\epsilon)^2=\epsilon$, i.e. $t=\epsilon+\sqrt\epsilon$.) The number isn't arbitrary — it's the same shape as the associated-Laguerre / Heilmann-Lieb root bounds these covariances calibrate against, the right tight constant.

Assemble. Given the random $v_i$ with $\sum_i\mathbb{E}\,v_iv_i^*=I$ and $\mathbb{E}\,\|v_i\|^2\le\epsilon$, set $A_i=\mathbb{E}\,v_iv_i^*$; then $\mathrm{Tr}(A_i)=\mathbb{E}\,\mathrm{Tr}(v_iv_i^*)=\mathbb{E}\,\|v_i\|^2\le\epsilon$. The expected characteristic polynomial is $\mu[A_1,\dots,A_m]$, whose largest root is $\le(1+\sqrt\epsilon)^2$ by the barrier climb. The outcome polynomials form an interlacing family, so by the interlacing-family principle some outcome $\sum_i w_{i,j_i}w_{i,j_i}^*$ has characteristic-polynomial largest root $\le(1+\sqrt\epsilon)^2$, hence operator norm $\le(1+\sqrt\epsilon)^2$. That outcome has positive probability. So
$$\Pr\Big[\Big\|\sum_i v_iv_i^*\Big\|\le(1+\sqrt\epsilon)^2\Big]>0,$$
at a dimension-free scale, with the existence (not high-probability) guarantee I needed.

Now I cash this out into the partition statement, because Weaver wants a coloring, not a norm. Suppose $u_1,\dots,u_m\in\mathbb{C}^d$ with $\sum_i u_iu_i^*=I$ and $\|u_i\|^2\le\delta$, and I want to split $[m]$ into $r$ parts each with $\big\|\sum_{i\in S_k}u_iu_i^*\big\|$ small. Encode "which part" as a random choice. For each $i$ and $k\in[r]$, let $w_{i,k}\in\mathbb{C}^{rd}$ be the block vector that is $u_i$ in the $k$-th block of $\mathbb{C}^d$ and zero elsewhere, and let $v_i$ take the value $\sqrt r\,w_{i,k}$ with probability $1/r$. Then $\mathbb{E}\,v_iv_i^*$ is the block-diagonal matrix with $u_iu_i^*$ in each of the $r$ diagonal blocks (since $r\cdot\frac1r=1$ per block), so $\sum_i\mathbb{E}\,v_iv_i^*=I_{rd}$; and $\|v_i\|^2=r\|u_i\|^2\le r\delta$. Apply the theorem with $\epsilon=r\delta$: some assignment of each $v_i$ to a block gives $\big\|\sum_i v_iv_i^*\big\|\le(1+\sqrt{r\delta})^2$. The assignment *is* a partition $S_k=\{i:v_i=\sqrt r\,w_{i,k}\}$, and on block $k$ the matrix is $\sum_{i\in S_k}(\sqrt r\,w_{i,k})(\sqrt r\,w_{i,k})^*=r\sum_{i\in S_k}w_{i,k}w_{i,k}^*$, whose norm equals $r\big\|\sum_{i\in S_k}u_iu_i^*\big\|$ (the $k$-th block is a copy of $\mathbb{C}^d$). Since the full block-diagonal norm is the max over blocks,
$$\Big\|\sum_{i\in S_k}u_iu_i^*\Big\|=\frac1r\Big\|\sum_{i\in S_k}(\sqrt r\,w_{i,k})(\sqrt r\,w_{i,k})^*\Big\|\le\frac1r(1+\sqrt{r\delta})^2=\Big(\frac1{\sqrt r}+\sqrt\delta\Big)^2$$
for every $k$. So any isotropic frame of $\delta$-small vectors splits into $r$ parts each bounded by $(1/\sqrt r+\sqrt\delta)^2$.

Set $r=2$, $\delta=1/18$: each part is $\le(1/\sqrt2+\sqrt{1/18})^2=(1/\sqrt2+1/(3\sqrt2))^2=(4/(3\sqrt2))^2=16/18=8/9<1$, strictly contractive. Translating back to Weaver's normalization: his $w_i$ with $\sum_i|\langle u,w_i\rangle|^2=\eta$ become $u_i=w_i/\sqrt\eta$ with $\sum_i u_iu_i^*=I$ and $\|u_i\|^2\le1/\eta=\delta$; taking $\eta=18$ gives $\delta=1/18$, and multiplying the result back by $\eta=18$ turns $8/9$ into $16=\eta-\theta$, i.e. $\theta=2$. That's Weaver's $KS_2$ with universal $\eta=18$, $\theta=2$ — and $KS_2$ implies Akemann-Anderson projection paving, which implies Kadison-Singer. The conjecture is settled.

And the paving constant comes out explicitly too. Casazza et al. reduced paving an arbitrary zero-diagonal self-adjoint matrix to paving projection matrices: it's enough to $(r,\frac{1+\epsilon}2)$-pave every $2n\times2n$ projection $Q$ with all diagonal entries $1/2$. Such a $Q$ is the Gram matrix of $2n$ vectors $u_i\in\mathbb{C}^n$ with $\|u_i\|^2=1/2$ resolving the identity, so my partition corollary with $\delta=1/2$ gives, on each part, $\|P_kQP_k\|=\big\|\sum_{i\in S_k}u_iu_i^*\big\|\le(1/\sqrt r+1/\sqrt2)^2<1/2+3/\sqrt r$. Choosing $r=36/\epsilon^2$ makes this $\le\frac{1+\epsilon}2$, and feeding that through the Casazza et al. reduction (which costs a square) gives Anderson paving with $r=(6/\epsilon)^4$ — a fixed number of pieces, independent of the matrix size, exactly the dimension-free constant the whole edifice was demanding.

To trace the causal chain once: I couldn't use concentration because it gives the wrong $\log n$ scale and only a high-probability guarantee, but the good outcome can be exponentially rare, so I needed first-moment *existence*; averaging the norm is useless, but averaging the characteristic polynomial works *if* a common interlacing forces some summand's largest root below the average's, which generalizes up a product tree to the interlacing-family principle; the expected characteristic polynomial turns out to be a product of operators $1-\partial_{z_i}$ on $\det(xI+\sum_i z_iA_i)$ — the mixed characteristic polynomial — because a single random rank-one update *is* such an operator; that determinant is real stable and the operators preserve stability, which makes every mixed characteristic polynomial real-rooted and hence makes the outcome polynomials a genuine interlacing family; and a multivariate barrier function, climbing one coordinate at a time while convexity keeps the barriers from accumulating, bounds the largest root of that expected polynomial by $(1+\sqrt\epsilon)^2$; which, via the direct-sum partition trick, gives Weaver's $KS_2$ with $\eta=18,\theta=2$ and Anderson paving with $r=(6/\epsilon)^4$, and so resolves Kadison-Singer.

Stated cleanly, the result I've landed on:

**Theorem.** Let $v_1,\dots,v_m$ be independent random vectors in $\mathbb{C}^d$ with finite support such that $\sum_{i=1}^m\mathbb{E}\,v_iv_i^*=I_d$ and $\mathbb{E}\,\|v_i\|^2\le\epsilon$ for all $i$. Then
$$\Pr\Big[\Big\|\sum_{i=1}^m v_iv_i^*\Big\|\le(1+\sqrt\epsilon)^2\Big]>0.$$
