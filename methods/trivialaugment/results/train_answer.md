We want a training-time image-augmentation rule for classifiers, and the question I keep returning to is whether the policy over augmentations actually has to be searched for every model and dataset, or whether nearly all of the benefit survives a fixed, search-free rule. The cost of searching is the part I cannot explain away. AutoAugment runs a controller RNN trained by PPO over a policy space of roughly $(16 \cdot 10 \cdot 11)^{10} \approx 2.9 \times 10^{32}$ — five sub-policies, two operations each, with a probability and a magnitude per operation — and samples on the order of fifteen thousand child policies, typically on a small CIFAR proxy of 4,000 images that is then transferred to full training. RandAugment strips the controller down to two scalars, the number of operations $N \in \{1,2,3\}$ and a single global magnitude $M \in \{0,\dots,30\}$, but it still grid-searches those on the target task, which can cost many full trainings. UniformAugment removes search in principle by sampling uniformly from an approximate invariant set, but it keeps a per-operation application probability $p \sim U(0,1)$ (sometimes hardened to a fixed leave-out of $0.5$) and a continuous magnitude $\lambda \sim U(0,1)$, so composition depth, a drop mechanism, and a continuous strength range all remain as free design choices. Cutout, by contrast, is a single regularizer that masks one square patch rather than a policy. The shortfall is the same across the searched methods: the search is treated as the active ingredient, yet two clues say otherwise. First, RandAugment's own best magnitude is not a stable property of the data — it grows with model width and with training-set size — so a magnitude calibrated on a cheap proxy points at the wrong strength for the real run. Second, RandAugment's appendix compares constant, random, linearly increasing, and random-with-increasing-upper-bound magnitude schedules, and the random schedules tie the tuned constant; the constant is kept only because it exposes a single knob to search.

The reason removing the search should not remove the benefit comes from the augmentation-orbit view of the objective. Without augmentation we minimize $\frac{1}{n}\sum_i \mathrm{loss}(f_\theta(x_i), y_i)$; with transforms $t$ drawn from an approximately label-preserving set $T$ the empirical objective becomes
$$\hat R_T(\theta) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_i \mathbb{E}_{t \sim T}\big[\mathrm{loss}(f_\theta(t(x_i)), y_i)\big].$$
For each image the optimizer sees an average over the orbit of transformed versions, and when those transforms stay in-class this averaging reduces the variance of the loss and gradient across the orbit. The argument asks for *coverage* of the orbit, not for a finely optimized distribution over it. Made concrete on the finite surface, with operation set $A$ and strength set $M=\{0,\dots,30\}$, the most symmetric thing one can do is the uniform mixture
$$\frac{1}{|A|\,|M|}\sum_{a \in A}\sum_{m \in M} (a_m)_\# \hat P,$$
the empirical dataset $\hat P$ pushed through every operation-strength pair and averaged uniformly. The strength bins are part of the transform surface, so I cannot average over operations and forget magnitudes — that mixture is the target the coverage argument points at, and it is not a learned policy at all.

I propose TrivialAugment. For each training image it samples one operation uniformly from $A$, samples one strength uniformly from $\{0,\dots,30\}$, applies that operation at that strength, and returns the image — $a \sim \mathrm{Uniform}(A)$, $m \sim \mathrm{Uniform}(\{0,\dots,30\})$, return $a(x,m)$ — with no searched policy, no $N$, no application probability, and no global $M$. Every piece of that minimality is forced by removing an unsupported choice rather than tuning it. Composition depth goes first: chaining operations builds richer distributions, but the in-class assumption is fragile, and a stack of large distortions can push an image out of its class where a single mild one would not. Setting $N=1$ is not because one is magical but because one is the only value that *removes* the composition question instead of tuning it, and it makes the per-image distribution exactly the clean uniform single-push mixture above. Once exactly one operation is applied, the application probability becomes redundant: "do not augment" is just the identity operation, and since $\mathrm{Identity} \in A$ the no-op already occurs with probability $1/|A|$ under the uniform draw. Folding the no-op into the same draw is also what keeps the method honest — the probability of doing nothing is pinned by the operation set, not by a separate sampled $p$ or a hidden $0.5$. The magnitude is the one place I deliberately keep discreteness: a continuous range sounds more general, but the surrounding methods already discretize, the RandAugment diagnostics say a few distinct magnitudes suffice, and discretizing limits augmentation variance, which is a feature when the goal is to cover useful orbits rather than inject unlimited jitter. So I keep the inherited 31 bins, and — this is the load-bearing difference from RandAugment-with-$N=1$ — instead of fixing one tuned $M$ for the whole run, I sample a fresh strength bin per image, so the training distribution contains weak and strong perturbations together rather than living on one shell of the orbit.

The constants matter because they define the actual distribution the training loop sees, so I align the landing to torchvision's wide space exactly. The operation set $A$ has 14 entries: identity, auto-contrast, equalize, rotate, solarize, color, posterize, contrast, brightness, sharpness, shear-x, shear-y, translate-x, translate-y, with `num_magnitude_bins` fixed at $31$. Shear ranges over $\mathrm{linspace}(0, 0.99, 31)$, translation over $\mathrm{linspace}(0, 32, 31)$ cast to integer pixels, rotation over $\mathrm{linspace}(0, 135, 31)$ degrees, solarize over a threshold $\mathrm{linspace}(255, 0, 31)$ (so $255$ is essentially a no-op and $0$ inverts almost everything), and posterize over $8 - \mathrm{round}(\mathrm{arange}(31)/5)$, i.e. retained bits from $8$ down to $2$ (fewer bits is the stronger operation). The four enhancers — brightness, color, contrast, sharpness — use magnitudes from $0$ to $0.99$ applied as factor $1 + \text{magnitude}$, so after a fair sign flip the signed factors span $0.01$ to $1.99$ (the wide table rounds this range as $0.01$ to $2.0$, and the original AutoML one-file code uses that direct factor range, but since the scaffold is torchvision I use its signed $0.99$ representation). Identity, auto-contrast, and equalize ignore the sampled strength. Two implementation traps deserve care: shear must not be written as a raw off-diagonal entry in a PIL affine matrix, because torchvision's `F.affine` expects shear *angles* in degrees, so the canonical code passes $\mathrm{degrees}(\mathrm{atan}(\text{magnitude}))$ to make the tangent of the angle equal the intended shear factor; and signed operations (shear, translate, rotate, the enhancers) get a fair-coin sign flip while posterize and solarize do not. Rather than re-derive any of this by hand, the faithful landing simply calls torchvision's `TrivialAugmentWide`, which implements exactly this surface:

```python
from torchvision import transforms
from torchvision.transforms import InterpolationMode


def build_train_transform(config):
    return transforms.Compose([
        transforms.TrivialAugmentWide(
            num_magnitude_bins=31,
            interpolation=InterpolationMode.NEAREST,
        ),
        transforms.RandomCrop(config["img_size"], padding=4),
        transforms.RandomHorizontalFlip(),
        transforms.ToTensor(),
        transforms.Normalize(config["mean"], config["std"]),
    ])
```
