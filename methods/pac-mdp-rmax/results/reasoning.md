Let me start from the thing that actually bothers me. I have an agent in an unknown MDP — finite states, finite actions, it picks an action, gets a reward, gets shoved to a next state by transition probabilities it can't see. And I have two perfectly good families of learning algorithms, and both of them are useless in the precise way I care about.

Q-learning converges to the optimal value function. Beautiful. But read the theorem carefully: it converges *provided every state-action pair is visited infinitely often*. That clause is doing all the work, and it is exactly the part nobody tells me how to satisfy. The theorem assumes the exploration has already happened and then says "given that, I converge." The hard problem — make sure you actually visit everything, and do it without wasting your life — is shoved into an assumption. Same with the model-based version: estimate the transition counts, run value iteration on the estimates, act greedily; converges if you explore enough. Again: *if you explore enough*. Nobody bounds "enough."

So what I really lack is not a convergence proof. It's a finite-resource guarantee. I want a theorem shaped like PAC learning: with probability at least 1−δ, after a number of actions polynomial in the size of the problem, in 1/ε, in 1/δ, my behavior is within ε of optimal. The number of actions, and the computation, both polynomial. No "in the limit."

And the obstacle to that is the exploration–exploitation dilemma, stated bluntly. Every single action does two jobs at once. It collects reward (or fails to), and it reveals information (or fails to). If I always exploit what I currently believe, I might be permanently wrong about some action in some state that's actually the key to a high-reward region — I'll never find the door because I never push on it. If I always explore, I gather information I never cash in, and my average reward stays low forever. The folklore fix is "optimistic initialization" — set your value estimates high, act greedily, and greed itself drags you toward the under-explored stuff because its inflated value hasn't been beaten down yet. Kaelbling's interval estimation does a version of this, Dyna's exploration bonus does it, Schmidhuber's curiosity does it, and Sutton and Barto literally call it "optimistic initial values, a simple trick that can be quite effective." But it's filed under *ad hoc techniques*. There is no theorem. Nobody has said *why* optimism resolves exploration, or how optimistic to be, or for how long, or what it provably buys.

So the question I actually want to answer is whether optimism can be turned from a trick into a theorem — and to do that I have to first pin down what "near-optimal in finite time" can even mean, then figure out what an agent is allowed to plan on when it only half-knows the world. Let me work through those in order and see where they lead.

First, the meaning of the target, because in the undiscounted average-reward case it's subtle. Picture a tiny MDP: state 0, payoff 0, with a self-loop probability 1−ρ and probability ρ of slipping into an absorbing state 1 that pays a big R. The asymptotic average return is R. But starting at 0, it genuinely takes on the order of 1/ρ steps just to fall into state 1. So no learning algorithm, however clever, can approach R in many fewer than 1/ρ steps — the *optimal policy itself* needs that long to mix to its asymptotic value. That's not a weakness of any algorithm; it's intrinsic. So I can't promise to beat "the" optimal policy in arbitrary time. What I *can* promise is to compete, in time polynomial in T, with the best policy whose own return settles within T steps. Let me make that precise: the ε-return mixing time of a policy π is the smallest T such that for all t ≥ T, |U^π(i,t) − U^π| ≤ ε, for every start state i. Let Π^{T,ε} be the policies that mix within T, and opt(Π^{T,ε}) the best asymptotic return among them. My target is opt(Π^{T,ε}) − ε, in time poly(T, N, 1/ε, 1/δ). And T is a legitimate lower bound — I just argued you can't beat it — so this is the strongest thing I could ask for.

In the discounted case it's cleaner. Fix any path; its discounted value is R_1 + γR_2 + γ²R_3 + ··· ≤ Σ_{k=1}^T γ^{k−1}R_k + R_max γ^T (1 + γ + γ² + ···) = (T-step value) + R_max γ^T/(1−γ). Setting R_max γ^T/(1−γ) ≤ ε and solving gives T ≥ (1/(1−γ)) log(R_max/(ε(1−γ))). So beyond this *horizon time* the future is negligible; I only ever need to reason about T-step returns. Good. That tames both cases with one parameter T — mixing time undiscounted, horizon time discounted.

Now the real question. I have an unknown MDP and I'm sampling it. At any moment I have a partial, noisy picture: some state-action pairs I've tried many times (good estimates), some a few times (bad estimates), some never. I want to *plan*. But plan on what? I can't plan on the true MDP — I don't have it. I can't honestly plan on my raw empirical estimates everywhere — for the barely-sampled pairs those estimates are garbage, and value iteration will happily build castles on garbage.

Let me think about what it means for an estimate to be good enough. Suppose I had an approximate model M̂ whose transition probabilities are close to the true M. I should keep the symbols separate: η is the per-transition estimation error, while α will be a value-loss budget later. For the clean R-MAX bound, the immediate payoff attached to an entry is deterministic and is learned directly when the entry is first sampled, so the simulation step keeps rewards identical and spends its concentration budget on transition probabilities. If I insist on stochastic reward means, I need a separate Hoeffding term for those means before I reuse the same simulation argument. How wrong could planning on M̂ be? If I can show "η-accurate model ⟹ T-step returns accurate," and if η-accuracy requires only polynomially many samples, then I'd have a handle. Let me try to prove that — call it the simulation lemma.

Fix a policy π and a start state i. The thing I want to bound is |U^π_M(i,T) − U^π_{M̂}(i,T)| for the T-step average reward. Along any fixed T-path the average payoff is in [0, R_max]. If the two models use the same path reward function, the value gap is controlled by how differently they distribute probability over T-paths:

|U_M − U_{M̂}| ≤ R_max · Σ_p |Pr_M(p) − Pr_{M̂}(p)|,

where p ranges over T-paths. If empirical rewards add another pathwise error term, I just add that term and halve the transition budget. So the transition part is safe if Σ_p |Pr_M(p) − Pr_{M̂}(p)| ≤ ε/R_max.

The trouble is that a path probability is a *product* of T transition probabilities, and a product of nearly-equal factors can drift. I need to be careful. Here's a clean way: hybrid processes. Define h_i to be the process that starts at i, follows the true M for its first i steps, then follows M̂ for the remaining steps. Then h_0 is exactly M̂'s process and h_T is exactly M's process — wait, let me fix the convention: let h_i use M̂ for the first i steps and M for the rest. Then h_0 = "all M" and h_T = "all M̂." By the triangle inequality,

Σ_p |Pr_{h_0}(p) − Pr_{h_T}(p)| ≤ Σ_{i=0}^{T−1} Σ_p |Pr_{h_i}(p) − Pr_{h_{i+1}}(p)|.

The point of the hybrids is that h_i and h_{i+1} differ in *exactly one* step — the (i+1)-th transition, M̂ versus M; everything before and after is the same process. So for a fixed i, factor each path as prefix · (the i→i+1 transition) · suffix. The prefix probability and the suffix probability are identical between h_i and h_{i+1}; only the middle transition differs. Summing: I sum over the state x_i reached after i steps, over prefixes reaching it, over the next state x_{i+1}, over suffixes. The prefix probabilities sum to 1 (it's a probability distribution over where you are after i steps). For each (x_i, x_{i+1}) the transition gap |P_M − P_{M̂}| ≤ η. And the suffix probabilities, for each fixed x_{i+1}, sum to 1 — so summing over the N possible values of x_{i+1} gives a factor of N. Putting it together, each term Σ_p |Pr_{h_i} − Pr_{h_{i+1}}| ≤ 1 · N · η. There are T of them, so Σ_p |Pr_M(p) − Pr_{M̂}(p)| ≤ N·T·η. I want the transition contribution to be at most ε, so I need

η ≤ ε / (N·T·R_max).

That's it — and crucially the required accuracy depends only *polynomially* on 1/T, 1/ε, 1/N. So "good enough to plan on" is a polynomial-sample condition. The value gap is then |U_M − U_{M̂}| ≤ R_max · (NTη) ≤ ε. If I want half the final error reserved for the empirical-model step, I ask for η = ε/(2NTR_max).

I should not trust a telescoping argument I haven't actually watched produce a number, because the place these proofs go wrong is the suffix-summing step where I claimed the N factor. Let me run it on the smallest case that exercises it. Take N = 2 states, one action so it's just a chain, T = 2 steps, R_max = 1, state rewards (0.2, 0.9). True transitions: from 0 go to {0,1} with probability (0.5, 0.5), from 1 stay at 1. Perturb every entry by η = 0.1 to get M̂: from 0 go (0.4, 0.6), from 1 go (0.1, 0.9). Enumerate the four 2-paths from state 0 and their average-of-two-rewards. Under M the T-step value is 0.375, under M̂ it is 0.41, so the actual gap is 0.035. My bound says R_max·N·T·η = 1·2·2·0.1 = 0.4, and indeed 0.035 ≤ 0.4 — the inequality holds, with the bound loose by an order of magnitude, exactly the slack I'd expect from a worst-case path-by-path triangle inequality. And the intermediate quantity, the L1 distance between the two path distributions, comes out 0.2, comfortably under the claimed N·T·η = 0.4. So the telescoping is not just plausible, it actually bounds the right thing on a case where I can see every path. Good — the lemma is real, not wishful.

In the discounted finite-horizon proof the same idea needs more bookkeeping because small transition probabilities make additive errors look large multiplicatively. I separate out β-small transitions, whose total contribution is at most βNTG_max^T, and on the remaining transitions convert additive η-accuracy into multiplicative (1±η/β)-accuracy. Keeping (1±η/β)^T near 1 is enough if η/β ≤ ε/(4TG_max^T); choosing β = √η makes the small-transition and multiplicative-error budget (η+2β)NTG_max^T ≤ 3√η·NTG_max^T ≤ ε/4, so η = O((ε/(NTG_max^T))²). The accuracy demand is uglier here — it scales with G_max^T, which is itself exponential in T — but it is still a fixed polynomial in 1/ε and 1/N for fixed T, so it does not break the sample-complexity story; it just costs more.

Now I know *when* an estimate is good enough. How many samples deliver η-accuracy? This is just concentration. Fix a state-action slot and a target next-state; let X_j be 1 if the j-th trial from that slot landed in the target, p its true probability. For Bernoulli sample means, Hoeffding gives Pr(|K^{-1}Σ_j X_j − p| > b) ≤ 2 exp(−2Kb²). Take b = K^{−1/3}: then the exponent is −2K·K^{−2/3} = −2K^{1/3}, so the failure probability is at most 2 exp(−2K^{1/3}) — shrinking in K but only stretched-exponentially, which is why the cube root shows up in the final cutoff. I want K^{−1/3} < η = ε/(2NTR_max), with margin, and the failure probability small enough to survive the slot union bound used in the transition proof. Let M_slots be Nk² in the stochastic-game version and NA in a single-agent MDP.

Let me pin the transition term by setting b to exactly half of η and solving for K, then sanity-check it on real numbers. b = η/2 = ε/(4NTR_max) and b = K^{−1/3} together give K = (4NTR_max/ε)³. Take N = 10, T = 20, R_max = 1, ε = 0.1: then η = 0.1/(2·10·20) = 0.00025, the cutoff (4NTR_max/ε)³ = (4·10·20/0.1)³ = 512,000,000,000, and at that K the accuracy b = K^{−1/3} = 0.000125, which is exactly η/2 and indeed below η — so the empirical estimate is comfortably inside the tolerance, not at its edge. The Hoeffding failure at this K and b is 2 exp(−2·5.12e11·(1.25e−4)²) = 2 exp(−16000), which underflows to zero in floating point — astronomically small, far more than enough margin for any reasonable slot union bound. So the transition-accuracy requirement alone is satisfied at this K with room to spare; the only thing that can make me need *more* samples is the confidence demand, and that is the second term. With δ = 0.05 and M_slots = 1000 the confidence term −6 ln³(δ/(6M_slots)) ≈ 9600, tiny next to the transition term here, but for small ε the transition term dominates and for tight δ the confidence term does. Taking the max of the two (plus one) covers both regimes:

K_1 = max( ⌈(4NTR_max/ε)³⌉ , ⌈−6 ln³(δ/(6M_slots))⌉ ) + 1,

the same as max((4NTR_max/ε)³, 6[ln(6M_slots/δ)]³)+1. That is polynomial in N, k or A, T, 1/ε, and log(1/δ). So: sample any slot K_1 times and I may *declare its transition model known* — freeze its empirical transition estimate and treat it as real, because with high probability it's η-accurate. This is the cutoff I was missing. It also tells me to use only the first K_1 transition samples and stop; that keeps the frozen estimate from drifting and keeps the count clean. The reward field is separate in the basic construction: it starts at R_max for optimism, is replaced by the observed slot payoff the first time the slot is tried, and does not change this transition cutoff.

For the explicit partial-model argument, it is cleaner to bundle the reliable slots by state. A state is known only after every action from it has enough samples; otherwise it is either visited but still not reliable, or never visited. Take the set S of currently known states. Form the *known-state MDP* M_S: its states are S together with one extra absorbing state s_0; for transitions *between* known states I keep the true probabilities; every transition that would *leave* S — toward an unknown or unvisited state — is redirected into s_0, and s_0 pays 0 and self-loops. So M_S is "the world, restricted to the part I understand, with one black-box drain representing everything I don't." The agent doesn't have M_S exactly but it has its empirical version M̂_S, and by the simulation lemma M̂_S simulates M_S to within ε when the states in S are known. And note M_S can never *over*-promise on real return: on S it agrees with M, and outside S it pays nothing.

Now what is the *value* of that drain state s_0? Here's where I have to make a choice, and the choice is the whole point.

Let me think about what I want to happen. At any moment, either the good policy lives inside the known region — in which case I should find it and run it — or the good policy has to leave the known region — in which case leaving is exactly the exploration I need to do. I'd love an argument that says: planning on my partial model *always* tells me which, and either way I make progress.

Try it. Take the optimal T-step policy π for the real M, with value U\*(i,T). Run that same π but score it on M_S — where leaving S earns nothing. Split π's T-paths from i into two bundles: q-paths that stay entirely in S, and r-paths that touch a state outside S at least once. The q-paths are identical in M and M_S — same probabilities, same rewards — so they contribute the same to both. Therefore U^π_{M_S}(i,T) ≥ Σ_q Pr_M[q] U_M(q). Two cases. Either Σ_q Pr_M[q] U_M(q) is already close to U\*(i,T), say within α — then there's a policy (namely π, or the optimum of M_S) achieving near-optimal return *while staying in known states*, and I can find it by planning on M̂_S and just run it. That's exploitation, and I can detect it because I can compute returns on M̂_S. Or it isn't close — meaning the missing mass, Σ_r Pr_M[r] U_M(r), is at least α. But each r-path's return is at most G_max^T, the maximum possible T-step return, so Σ_r Pr_M[r] ≥ α / G_max^T. That says: the optimal policy leaves S with probability at least α/G_max^T within T steps. And *that* is a policy that explores — it reaches the drain s_0, i.e. reaches an unknown state, with non-trivial probability — and again I can find it by planning on M̂_S, this time rewarding s_0.

So for any known set S the two cases I split into are mutually exhaustive: either Σ_q Pr_M[q] U_M(q) is within α of U\*(i,T), giving a near-optimal policy that exploits within S, or the leftover mass forces a policy that reaches an unknown state with probability ≥ α/G_max^T. There is no third option — the q-paths and r-paths partition all T-paths, so their returns must sum to U\*(i,T), and if the q-part falls short the r-part makes up the difference. And both sides are things I can actually compute by value iteration on my partial model, not just things that exist. That is more than I expected to get: it means I never have to *guess* whether to explore or exploit, I read it off the partial-model value. The reason the loop terminates: in the state-based E³ view, balanced wandering can charge only a finite m_known budget to each state before the state enters S; in the slot-based R-MAX view, each unknown state-action or state-joint-action slot needs only K_1 samples before it stops being optimistic. Pigeonhole is the counting argument in both cases.

This already gives an algorithm — run two offline value iterations on M̂_S, one with the real empirical rewards to get the exploitation policy, one with reward R_max only at s_0 and zero elsewhere to get the exploration policy. If the exploitation policy clears the near-optimal target threshold, execute it; otherwise execute the exploration policy, whose probability of reaching s_0 is then at least the lower bound from the dichotomy. Call it Explicit Explore or Exploit, E³. It works. But to *decide* whether the exploitation policy clears the threshold, I need to compare its value against the unknown optimal opt(Π^{T,ε}). If I don't know that target, I can bias toward exploration: keep exploring while the computed probability of reaching s_0 exceeds the threshold, and when it no longer does, the dichotomy guarantees exploitation must be near-optimal. Fine. But it's a little unsatisfying that I'm carrying an explicit branch and, in the clean version, leaning on knowing the optimal return.

Now let me stare at the structure and see if I can make the branch *disappear*.

The two offline computations differ only in the reward I assign to the unknown region: real rewards (exploit) versus R_max at the drain (explore). What if I don't run two computations at all? What if I bake the optimism *into a single model* and just plan optimally once?

Instead of draining unknown transitions into a zero-reward absorber and then separately rewarding exploration, give the unknown continuation the *maximum possible value* directly. Build one fictitious model: keep the known slots as their empirical estimates, but every unknown transition goes with probability 1 into the fictitious high-value state; the immediate reward on that entry is R_max only until the payoff has been seen, and after that it is the observed payoff while the transition remains optimistic. The fictitious state itself yields R_max and self-loops. So an untrusted entry is, in this model, worth the best continuation that could possibly happen: R_max forever in the average-reward view, or the remaining discounted tail in the discounted view, plus the slot payoff once I know it. Now just compute the single optimal policy of this optimistic model and execute it. No branch. No comparison against the optimal return. No exploration schedule.

Why would that possibly be correct? Watch what the planner does. It's greedy with respect to a model in which every unknown action is gilded with the maximum conceivable value — R_max/(1−γ) discounted, or R_max average. Nothing real can beat that. So if there is *any* unknown state-action reachable reasonably quickly, the optimal policy of this fictitious model will steer toward it, because the fiction says that's where the gold is. The agent is *automatically* drawn to explore — not because I told it to, but because optimism makes the unexplored look maximally attractive and the planner can't tell the difference yet. And if there's no attractive unknown reachable — if the known region's honest value already dominates — then the planner stays and exploits, because now the real rewards are the best thing on offer. Optimism isn't a heuristic add-on; it's the thing that makes "plan optimally" and "explore-or-exploit" the same instruction.

The suspicious part is that the fictitious continuation is a *lie* — the high-value state overestimates whatever waits beyond an unknown transition. So before I believe this single-plan idea I have to check whether the lie can hurt me, and I want to do that on numbers, not just by hand-waving optimism. Let M_L be this optimistic induced model, where L is the set of entries whose transition model is still unknown; entries in L route to the fictitious state, unseen immediate payoffs are R_max, observed immediate payoffs keep their observed value, and the fictitious state pays R_max forever. Let the R-MAX policy be the optimal policy of M_L.

There are two things I need: that M_L never under-values relative to reality (so optimism is genuinely optimistic), and that the over-valuation is exactly accounted for by the chance of hitting an unknown entry (so the slack is the exploration signal). Take a concrete 3-state chain, T = 3, R_max = 1, where only state 0 pays (reward 0.3) and the dynamics march 0→1→2→2. Suppose state 1's outgoing slot is still unknown, so in M_L leaving state 1 routes to the R_max absorber. Starting at 0: in the real M the cumulative 3-step return is 0.3 + 0 + 0 = 0.3. In M_L, the path is 0 (collect 0.3), then at state 1 I hit the unknown slot and get R_max = 1 and absorb, then 1 more step of R_max, so 0.3 + 1 + 1 = 2.3. The fiction is worth 2.3 ≥ 0.3 — it does over-value, in the right direction. Now the gap is |0.3 − 2.3| = 2.0, and the probability under the real dynamics of touching the unknown state within 3 steps is 1 (the chain deterministically passes through state 1). The bound I want to claim, Pr[hit unknown]·R_max·(remaining steps), is 1·1·3 = 3.0, and 2.0 ≤ 3.0 holds; in the per-step-average normalization both sides divide by T, giving |U_M − V_{M_L}|/T = 0.667 ≤ Pr·R_max = 1. So on a case I can compute by hand the over-valuation really is bounded by the hit-probability times R_max, with the right direction of inequality. That is the structural fact the proof needs, and now I trust it.

So let me lay out the general version of what that example showed. First, any policy is worth at least as much in M_L as in M: paths that avoid L are identical, unseen rewards are never smaller than the real bounded payoff, and after the first entry in L the induced model gives the largest payoff any real continuation could give. Therefore V_{M_L}(R-MAX) ≥ U_M(π\*) — optimism over-values, as the example confirmed numerically. Second, compare what the R-MAX policy actually earns in the real M versus in the fiction M_L. Decompose its T-paths from s the same way: q-paths that never touch an unknown entry, r-paths that hit at least one. On q-paths, M and M_L are identical — same probabilities, same rewards — so they contribute *exactly* the same to both values; that difference is zero. On r-paths, the values can differ, but the T-step average payoff on any path sits in [0, R_max], so the loss from replacing the fiction by reality is at most R_max times the probability, under the real process, of hitting L. Hence

|U_M(R-MAX) − V_{M_L}(R-MAX)| ≤ Σ_r Pr_M[r] · R_max,

which is precisely the (hit-probability × R_max) bound the 3-state computation obeyed. Now the two cases. If Σ_r Pr_M[r] ≥ α/R_max, then I'm exploring: with probability at least α/R_max per T-step phase I touch an unknown entry and collect a sample. Otherwise Σ_r Pr_M[r] < α/R_max, which makes |U_M(R-MAX) − V_{M_L}(R-MAX)| < α, and then

U_M(R-MAX) > V_{M_L}(R-MAX) − α ≥ U_M(π\*) − α = opt − α.

Near-optimal exploitation. So it is the explore-or-exploit dichotomy again, but now falling out of a single inequality instead of a branch: either I'm gathering a new sample with decent probability, or I'm already within α of optimal — and I never had to know which, never had to know opt, never ran a second computation. The picture behind the algebra: the fictitious value is high, so the planner chases it; when chasing it keeps me among known states, those states are modeled *correctly*, so the value I truly collect is close to what the fiction promised, which is near-optimal; when chasing it moves me into the unknown, I learn. The exploration probability and the suboptimality are the two readings of that one inequality.

There's a subtlety I should not gloss: I proved the dichotomy as if M_L used the *true* transition probabilities on known slots, but I only have the *empirical* M̂_L. That's exactly what the simulation lemma is for. I run the whole argument on three models at once: the real M, the empirical model M̂_L I actually plan on, and an idealized M'_L that uses true probabilities on known slots. The known-slot estimates are η-close with η = ε/(2NTR_max), so by the simulation lemma the policy I compute on M̂_L is, on the real world, either within ε of optimal or explores efficiently — the ε/2 of model error plus the ε/2 of the induced-model slack give ε. Everything is sized to fit.

Now assemble the algorithm and count. Maintain, for every slot — (s,a) in an MDP, or (state, joint action) in the stochastic-game version — a known/unknown flag, the multiset of next-states observed, and the entry reward. Initialize *everything* unknown, with the optimistic value: an untried entry returns R_max, every unknown transition goes with probability 1 to a fictitious state G_0, and G_0 itself returns R_max and self-loops. Then repeat: compute the optimal T-step policy of the current optimistic model, execute it for T steps or until some entry crosses the known threshold; after each step, record the transition; if this is the first time I have tried the entry, replace its immediate reward by the observed payoff while leaving its transition optimistic; once an entry has accumulated K_1 transition observations, mark it known and replace the transition stub with the empirical next-state frequencies; re-plan. That's the entire algorithm. It is genuinely simple — the optimism *is* the exploration mechanism, the planner *is* the decision-maker.

The complexity falls out of the pieces. There are M_slots slots — Nk² in the stochastic-game notation, NA in a plain MDP — and each becomes known after K_1 = poly(N, k or A, T, 1/ε, log(1/δ)) transition samples. So the total number of useful unknown-slot visits is at most M_slots K_1. When the implicit lemma is in its exploration branch, a T-step attempt hits an unknown slot with probability at least α/R_max. Let X_i mark whether attempt i actually hits one. The expected count after K_2 attempts is at least K_2α/R_max, and Hoeffding keeps the realized count within K_2^{2/3} of that except with probability exponentially small in K_2^{1/3}; choosing K_2 polynomially large so that K_2α/R_max − K_2^{2/3} exceeds M_slots K_1 makes the needed visits arrive with failure probability below δ/3. During those learning phases my average reward may be low. Once a phase does not learn, its expected T-step average return is within the chosen ε-slack of opt(Π^{T,ε}); averaging sufficiently many bounded exploitation phases makes the realized return close to that expectation, again by Hoeffding. Finally I budget the failure probability δ across exactly three ways the analysis can break: (1) a known slot secretly has a bad transition estimate, controlled by the K_1 union bound; (2) attempted explorations are unlucky, controlled by the K_2 bound; (3) realized exploitation return falls below its expectation, controlled by concentration of bounded T-step averages. Union: total failure ≤ δ. I can state the theorem with constant slack as Opt(Π(ε,T))−2ε in polynomial time, and if I want a final ε statement I just run the construction with a smaller internal tolerance.

And I can drop the assumption that I know the mixing time T. The whole bound is some polynomial P(T) in T. So run the algorithm pretending T=1 for P(1) steps, then T=2 for P(2) steps, and so on; the total time to have run the genuine P(T₀) phase, where T₀ is the true mixing time, is Σ_{t≤T₀} P(t) ≤ T₀·P(T₀), still polynomial. Once I've passed T₀ every subsequent guess is an over-estimate and yields near-optimal average reward, which amortizes the early under-estimated losses. Same doubling trick used for the horizon time. (One honest caveat: if I keep running forever, eventually the guessed T is exponentially larger than the truth and a single value-iteration solve becomes expensive — but that only happens after exponentially many steps during which I've already been near-optimal, so it's not a real problem.)

Let me write down exactly what the planner does on the optimistic model, because that's the concrete artifact. T-step value iteration runs backward on the model whose unknown transitions point to the high-reward fictitious state, with an immediate reward of R_max until the slot payoff has been observed:

```
# T-step value iteration on the optimistic induced model M̂_L.
# Unknown transition: w.p. 1 to the fictitious absorbing high-value state s0.
# Reward model Rmodel[s][a]: R_max until first observed, then the learned slot payoff.
# Known transition: empirical next-state distribution P̂ from the first K1 samples.
U_next[s0] = 0
for s in states: U_next[s] = 0
for t in T, T-1, ..., 1:
    U_curr[s0] = R_max + gamma * U_next[s0]
    for s in states:
        for a in actions:
            if known[s][a]:
                Q[s][a] = Rmodel[s][a] + gamma * sum_{s'} Phat[s'|s,a] * U_next[s']
            else:
                Q[s][a] = Rmodel[s][a] + gamma * U_next[s0]
        pi_t[s] = argmax_a Q[s][a]
        U_curr[s] = max_a Q[s][a]
    U_next = U_curr
# For average reward take gamma = 1 and compare cumulative T-step values divided by T.
# For discounted reward use gamma < 1 and the horizon-time T.
```

So the full causal chain, start to finish: I wanted a finite-resource guarantee, not asymptotic convergence, and the obstacle was the exploration–exploitation dilemma with no principled balance. I noticed I can't plan on the true model, only on the part I trust, so I asked how accurate a model has to be before planning on it is safe — the simulation lemma answered "only polynomially accurate," η = ε/(NTR_max) for the average-reward telescoping argument, or η = ε/(2NTR_max) when I reserve half the budget for empirical-model error, and η = O((ε/(NTG_max^T))²) for the discounted finite-horizon variant. Hoeffding then told me that polynomial transition accuracy needs only K_1 = max(⌈(4NTR_max/ε)³⌉, ⌈−6 ln³(δ/(6M_slots))⌉)+1 transition samples per slot, which gave me a hard cutoff for declaring a state-action or joint-action transition model known. Restricting the model to known states with one zero-reward absorbing drain for the unknown gave the explicit explore-or-exploit dichotomy: for any known set, either a near-optimal policy lives inside it or some policy escapes it with non-trivial probability, and both are computable by value iteration. Then I collapsed the explicit branch by painting unknown transitions as routes to a fictitious high-reward absorbing state — optimism under uncertainty — so that a *single* optimal-planning step automatically explores when there's promising unknown territory and exploits otherwise, with the suboptimality and the exploration probability being two readings of the one inequality |U_M − V_{M_L}| ≤ Pr[hit unknown]·R_max. Pigeonhole bounds the stock of samples gathered from unknown entries, the exploration-attempt and realized-return Hoeffding bounds split the δ failure budget with the known-transition estimate bound, and the result is R-MAX: with probability ≥ 1−δ, near-optimal return in a number of actions and amount of computation polynomial in N, k or A, T, 1/ε, and 1/δ — and, as a bonus, a *proof* of why the old optimistic-initialization trick was right all along.
