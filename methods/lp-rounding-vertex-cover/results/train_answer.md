I want the cheapest set of vertices that touches every edge of a weighted graph $G=(V,E)$ with vertex weights $w_v \ge 0$ — a minimum-weight vertex cover. This is NP-complete (Karp put it on the original list, and it stays hard even on planar cubic unit-weight graphs), so unless $\mathrm{P}=\mathrm{NP}$ I cannot compute the optimum exactly in polynomial time. The realistic goal is therefore a polynomial-time algorithm that outputs a valid cover together with a *proof* that its weight is within a fixed factor $\alpha$ of the optimum $\mathrm{OPT}$ on every instance. The whole difficulty lives in that word "prove": a guarantee means bounding $w(\text{output})/\mathrm{OPT}$, and $\mathrm{OPT}$ is exactly the quantity I cannot get my hands on. The only way out is to find some other quantity $\mathrm{LB}$ that I can compute in polynomial time and can prove satisfies $\mathrm{LB}\le\mathrm{OPT}$; then if I also arrange $w(\text{output})\le\alpha\cdot\mathrm{LB}$, I get $w(\text{output})\le\alpha\cdot\mathrm{LB}\le\alpha\cdot\mathrm{OPT}$ without ever knowing $\mathrm{OPT}$. The game is to find a computable lower bound and tie the algorithm's cost to that same bound — two jobs from one quantity.

The existing baselines each fail at this. Gavril's maximal-matching algorithm covers the unweighted case beautifully — a single vertex covers at most one matched edge, so $|M|\le\mathrm{OPT}$, and taking both endpoints of a maximal matching gives a feasible cover of size $2|M|\le 2\cdot\mathrm{OPT}$ — but the lower bound it uses counts edges, not weight. Put weights back and a matched edge between a vertex of weight $1000$ and one of weight $1$ forces me to pay for the $1000$; $2|M|$ has nothing to do with the weight I actually owe, so this argument has no weighted analogue. The set-cover greedy heuristics (take the maximum-degree vertex, or the minimum weight-per-newly-covered ratio) only guarantee a factor $H(d)=1+\tfrac12+\dots+\tfrac1d\approx\ln d$, logarithmic rather than constant. And independent-set complementation — take $V\setminus V_i$ of smallest weight over some partition into independent sets — has $w(V\setminus V_i)/\mathrm{OPT}$ unbounded. What I need is a lower bound expressed in the currency of weights, available for weighted graphs at no extra cost, and a rounding step that charges each vertex's weight against that same bound.

I propose LP-rounding: model vertex cover as a $0/1$ integer program, relax the integrality constraint to get a linear program, solve it in polynomial time, and round the fractional solution at the threshold $\tfrac12$. Writing $x_v\in\{0,1\}$ for "$v$ is in the cover," vertex cover is exactly
$$\min \sum_{v\in V} w_v x_v \quad\text{s.t.}\quad x_u+x_v\ge 1\ \ \forall (u,v)\in E,\quad x_v\in\{0,1\},$$
whose optimum is literally $\mathrm{OPT}$. Every bit of hardness sits in $x_v\in\{0,1\}$: the objective and the edge constraints are linear, only the integrality is nasty. So I drop it, letting each variable range over $[0,1]$, and obtain the linear program
$$\min \sum_{v\in V} w_v x_v \quad\text{s.t.}\quad x_u+x_v\ge 1\ \ \forall (u,v)\in E,\quad 0\le x_v\le 1,$$
with optimum $\mathrm{OPT}_f$. The box bounds $x_v\le 1$ are value-preserving rather than essential — truncating any feasible $x_v>1$ down to $1$ keeps every incident edge constraint satisfied and never increases a nonnegative objective — but keeping the box makes the structure clean. This relaxation does the first job for free. Removing the integrality constraint only enlarges the feasible region: every integral cover is still a feasible point of the LP (the values $0,1$ lie in $[0,1]$ and satisfy the same edge inequalities), so I am minimizing the same objective over a superset, and
$$\mathrm{OPT}_f \le \mathrm{OPT}.$$
This is the computable lower bound I was hunting, and crucially it is stated in weight units, not edge counts — exactly where the matching argument died. The gap is genuine, not a relabeling: on the triangle $K_3$ with unit weights an integral cover needs $2$ vertices, but $x_v=\tfrac12$ everywhere satisfies each edge ($\tfrac12+\tfrac12=1$) at cost $\tfrac32$, so $\mathrm{OPT}_f=\tfrac32<2=\mathrm{OPT}$.

Now the LP hands me a fractional optimum $x^\*$ that is not a cover, and I round it. Which fractions go up? Look at one edge constraint $x^\*_u+x^\*_v\ge 1$: two nonnegative numbers summing to at least $1$ cannot both be small, and by pure averaging the larger of them is at least $\tfrac12$. So *every* edge has at least one endpoint with $x^\*\ge\tfrac12$ — forced by the inequality itself, not a coincidence of the triangle. That tells me exactly whom to keep:
$$C=\{\,v: x^\*_v\ge \tfrac12\,\}.$$
This $C$ is a cover, because every edge has such an endpoint, so no edge is left uncovered — feasibility falls straight out of the edge inequalities, with no appeal to maximality or any greedy process. And it is cheap: comparing vertex by vertex against $\mathrm{OPT}_f$, if $x^\*_v\ge\tfrac12$ then $v\in C$ contributes $w_v$, and $1\le 2x^\*_v$ gives $w_v\le 2\,w_v x^\*_v$; if $x^\*_v<\tfrac12$ then $v$ is dropped and contributes $0\le 2\,w_v x^\*_v$. Either way each vertex's charge is at most twice its fractional weight, so
$$w(C)=\sum_{v\in C} w_v \le \sum_{v\in V} 2\,w_v x^\*_v = 2\cdot\mathrm{OPT}_f \le 2\cdot\mathrm{OPT}.$$
A $2$-approximation for *weighted* vertex cover. The weights that broke the matching argument are handled here precisely because $\mathrm{OPT}_f$ was a weighted lower bound from the start and the rounding charges each vertex against its own fractional weight — both jobs done by the LP.

The threshold $\tfrac12$ is not a tuning knob; it is $1/p$ where $p=2$ is the number of vertices on an edge. A general set-cover constraint has $p$ terms summing to $\ge 1$ (an element lying in $p$ sets), forcing some term $\ge 1/p$, and rounding everything $\ge 1/p$ up to $1$ multiplies costs by at most $p$, giving a $p$-approximation; vertex cover is the case $p=2$. The lazier rule "round up every nonzero coordinate" is also feasible (it is a superset of $C$) but has no clean factor-$2$ bound: a tiny positive $x^\*_v$ would make $1\le 2x^\*_v$ fail completely. The safety of the $\ge\tfrac12$ rule is that the inequality $1\le 2x^\*_v$ is built into the threshold.

There is more structure that makes the rounding even cleaner. The triangle's all-halves optimum is no accident: every extreme-point (basic) solution of the boxed LP is half-integral, $x_v\in\{0,\tfrac12,1\}$. One way to see it is perturbation. Suppose a feasible $x$ is not half-integral; collect the offenders into $V_+=\{v:\tfrac12<x_v<1\}$ and $V_-=\{v:0<x_v<\tfrac12\}$, not both empty, and form $y$ by adding $\varepsilon$ on $V_+$ and subtracting $\varepsilon$ on $V_-$, and $z$ by the opposite. Then $x=(y+z)/2$ with $y\ne x\ne z$ for small $\varepsilon$. The moved coordinates are bounded away from $0,\tfrac12,1$, so small $\varepsilon$ keeps the box. Slack edges survive; a tight edge $x_u+x_v=1$ with both endpoints moved must have one in $V_+$ and one in $V_-$, so the $\pm\varepsilon$ cancel; a tight edge with exactly one moved endpoint is impossible, since a strict value below or above $\tfrac12$ cannot be completed to exactly $1$ by $0,\tfrac12,1$; an untight-only or unmoved edge is unchanged. So $y,z$ are feasible and $x$ is not extreme — contrapositive, extreme implies half-integral. The same fact follows from the constraint matrix: edge rows have two nonzeros and bound rows one, all in $\{0,\pm1\}$; decomposing a basis matrix into nonseparable blocks and inducting, a block where every row and column has exactly two nonzeros is a single cycle, whose determinant expansion has only two surviving permutation products, each $\pm1$, summing to $0$ or $\pm2$, so every nonsingular block has determinant $\pm1$ or $\pm2$, and Cramer's rule blockwise gives denominators at most $2$ — coordinates in $\{0,\tfrac12,1\}$ once boxed. The "$2$" is exactly the cycle block created by two-variables-per-row; a general set-cover LP has no such bound. Consequently simplex (or any basic-solution method) returns a $\{0,\tfrac12,1\}$ vector, and the algorithm is literally "round the halves up," doubling at worst on the half-valued set. The same two-per-row structure even lets a min-cut solve the LP directly: split each $v$ into $a_v,b_v$ of weight $w_v/2$, replace each edge $(i,j)$ by $(a_i,b_j)$ and $(a_j,b_i)$, and map an optimal bipartite cover back to $x_v=1,\tfrac12,0$ as both, one, or neither copy is chosen. And persistency sharpens what the halves mean: with $P=\{x^\*_v=1\}$, $Q=\{x^\*_v=\tfrac12\}$, $R=\{x^\*_v=0\}$, the set $R$ has no edges to $R$ or $Q$, so for any optimal integer cover $S$ the exchange lowering $A=P\setminus S$ from $1$ to $\tfrac12$ and raising $B=R\cap S$ from $0$ to $\tfrac12$ stays feasible and forces $w(A)\le w(B)$; replacing $S$ by $(S\cup P)\setminus R$ keeps optimality, so some optimal integer cover contains $P$ and avoids $R$, and the factor-$2$ doubling occurs solely on $Q$.

```python
import pulp

def vertex_cover_lp_rounding(G, w):
    prob = pulp.LpProblem("vc_relaxation", pulp.LpMinimize)
    x = {v: pulp.LpVariable(f"x_{v}", lowBound=0, upBound=1) for v in G.nodes}
    prob += pulp.lpSum(w[v] * x[v] for v in G.nodes)                  # min sum w[v] x[v]
    for u, v in G.edges:
        prob += x[u] + x[v] >= 1                                      # edge constraints
    status = prob.solve(pulp.PULP_CBC_CMD(msg=False))
    if pulp.LpStatus[status] != "Optimal":
        raise RuntimeError(f"LP solve failed with status {pulp.LpStatus[status]}")
    # every edge has an endpoint with x* >= 1/2; keep those => feasible cover, w(C) <= 2*OPT
    tol = 1e-9
    return {v for v in G.nodes if x[v].value() >= 0.5 - tol}
```
