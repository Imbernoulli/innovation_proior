Let me start from what actually hurts, because the pain here is sharp and specific. I have a big nonlinear ODE system that came from discretizing a PDE in space,
$$\frac{d}{dt}\mathbf{y}(t)=\mathbf{A}\,\mathbf{y}(t)+\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{y}(t)),\qquad \mathbf{y}\in\mathbb{R}^n,$$
with $\mathbf{A}$ the discrete linear operator and $\mathbf{F}$ the nonlinear term — for a scalar reaction–diffusion problem $\mathbf{F}$ acts componentwise, $\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{y})=[F(y_1),\dots,F(y_n)]^\top$. The dimension $n$ is the number of grid points, and it has to be large — thousands, tens of thousands — for the solution to be accurate. I want to run this thing cheaply, many times, for parameter sweeps and control. So I reach for the standard tool.

The standard tool is to notice that the trajectories don't fill $\mathbb{R}^n$; they're sucked onto a low-dimensional manifold. So I gather snapshots of the solution along some trajectories, stack them as columns of $\mathbf{Y}\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times n_s}$, and take the SVD $\mathbf{Y}=\mathbf{V}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{W}^\top$. The leading $k$ left singular vectors are the POD basis $\mathbf{V}_k$ — the orthonormal set whose span best fits the snapshots in the least-squares sense, with the leftover error being exactly the tail of squared singular values $\sum_{i>k}\sigma_i^2$. That's Eckart–Young: no other $k$-dimensional basis fits this data better. Because the singular values decay fast on a low-dimensional manifold, $k\ll n$. I write $\mathbf{y}\approx\mathbf{V}_k\tilde{\mathbf{y}}$, demand the dynamics' residual be orthogonal to $\mathrm{Range}(\mathbf{V}_k)$ — multiply through by $\mathbf{V}_k^\top$ — and get the Galerkin-reduced system
$$\frac{d}{dt}\tilde{\mathbf{y}}=\underbrace{\mathbf{V}_k^\top\mathbf{A}\mathbf{V}_k}_{\tilde{\mathbf{A}}}\,\tilde{\mathbf{y}}+\mathbf{V}_k^\top\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{V}_k\tilde{\mathbf{y}}).$$
The linear part is beautiful: $\tilde{\mathbf{A}}=\mathbf{V}_k^\top\mathbf{A}\mathbf{V}_k$ is a $k\times k$ matrix I form once, offline, and never touch $n$ again. So I declare victory and look at the nonlinear part — and that's where it falls apart.

Look at what $\mathbf{V}_k^\top\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{V}_k\tilde{\mathbf{y}})$ costs me at each step. To evaluate it I have to lift $\tilde{\mathbf{y}}$ back up to the full grid, $\mathbf{V}_k\tilde{\mathbf{y}}\in\mathbb{R}^n$; evaluate $\mathbf{F}$ at all $n$ of those components; then squash the $n$-vector back down with $\mathbf{V}_k^\top$. The middle step alone costs $\alpha(n)$, the cost of evaluating the nonlinearity at $n$ points, and the two matrix products cost $\mathcal{O}(nk)$ each. So the per-step cost is $\mathcal{O}(\alpha(n)+4nk)$ — it still scales with $n$. I reduced the *number of variables* from $n$ to $k$, but I did *not* reduce the *cost of one step*. I'm still walking across the entire original grid every single step, just to assemble a $k$-dimensional right-hand side. And for a steady problem solved by Newton it's worse: the reduced Jacobian carries $\mathbf{V}_k^\top\mathrm{diag}\{F'(\mathbf{V}_k\tilde{\mathbf{y}})\}\mathbf{V}_k$, an $\mathcal{O}(\alpha(n)+4nk+2nk^2)$ object. The killer detail: if $\mathbf{A}$ is sparse — which it is, being a discretized differential operator — then the *full* step is only $\mathcal{O}(n)$, so my "reduced" model at $\mathcal{O}(nk)$ is actually *slower* than the thing I was trying to speed up once $k$ gets past a handful. The reduced model isn't reduced where it counts. This $n$-dependence in the nonlinear term is the whole obstacle, and I have to kill it.

So what would killing it even look like? I need the nonlinear term to cost something like $\mathcal{O}(\alpha(m)+\text{small})$ for some $m\ll n$ — a cost set by a small number, not by the grid. That means I cannot afford to evaluate $\mathbf{F}$ at all $n$ points. I have to get away with evaluating it at only a few.

The first instinct is the same trick that worked for the state: $\mathbf{F}$ along the trajectory also lives near a low-dimensional subspace. Why wouldn't it? $\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{y}(t))$ is a smooth image of a low-dimensional curve, so its snapshots $\{\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{y}_1),\dots,\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{y}_{n_s})\}$ should also be compressible. Take *their* SVD, keep the leading $m$ left singular vectors $\mathbf{U}=[\mathbf{u}_1,\dots,\mathbf{u}_m]\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times m}$ — a POD basis tailored to the nonlinear term — and approximate
$$\mathbf{f}(\tau)\approx\mathbf{U}\mathbf{c}(\tau),$$
writing $\mathbf{f}(\tau):=\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{V}_k\tilde{\mathbf{y}}(\tau))$ for short. Now, the most accurate way to get the coefficients $\mathbf{c}$ would be orthogonal projection, $\mathbf{c}=\mathbf{U}^\top\mathbf{f}$, which gives the best $m$-term fit $\mathbf{U}\mathbf{U}^\top\mathbf{f}$. But — and here's the wall — computing $\mathbf{U}^\top\mathbf{f}$ requires knowing all $n$ entries of $\mathbf{f}=\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{V}_k\tilde{\mathbf{y}})$, which means evaluating $\mathbf{F}$ at all $n$ points again. I've just moved the bottleneck, not removed it. Orthogonal projection of the nonlinear term is exactly as expensive as before; the $\alpha(n)$ is untouched.

Stare at that. The subspace $\mathbf{U}$ is only $m$-dimensional, so $\mathbf{c}$ is only $m$ unknowns. To pin down $m$ unknowns I shouldn't need $n$ equations — I need $m$ equations. The orthogonal projection used all $n$ entries of $\mathbf{f}$ to compute each coefficient as a global inner product. What if instead I just *demand the approximation be exact at $m$ chosen entries* of $\mathbf{f}$, and solve for $\mathbf{c}$ from those? Pick $m$ distinguished rows $\wp_1,\dots,\wp_m$, collect them with the selection matrix
$$\mathbf{P}=[\mathbf{e}_{\wp_1},\dots,\mathbf{e}_{\wp_m}]\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times m},$$
each $\mathbf{e}_{\wp_i}$ a column of the identity. Then $\mathbf{P}^\top\mathbf{f}$ reads off just those $m$ entries, and $\mathbf{P}^\top\mathbf{U}$ is the $m\times m$ submatrix of $\mathbf{U}$ on those rows. Force agreement there:
$$\mathbf{P}^\top\mathbf{f}=(\mathbf{P}^\top\mathbf{U})\,\mathbf{c}.$$
If $\mathbf{P}^\top\mathbf{U}$ is nonsingular, this determines the coefficients from the few sampled entries,
$$\mathbf{c}=(\mathbf{P}^\top\mathbf{U})^{-1}\mathbf{P}^\top\mathbf{f},$$
and the approximation becomes
$$\boxed{\ \mathbf{f}\approx\hat{\mathbf{f}}=\mathbf{U}(\mathbf{P}^\top\mathbf{U})^{-1}\mathbf{P}^\top\mathbf{f}.\ }$$
This is interpolation, not projection: $\mathbf{P}^\top\mathbf{f}$ touches only $m$ components of $\mathbf{f}$. And I recognize the move — it's the discrete cousin of replacing projection by interpolation, the way the empirical interpolation method (Barrault, Maday, Nguyen, Patera 2004) handled non-affine parametrized functions in a continuous domain: build an empirical basis greedily, then fit it not by a global inner product but by matching at a handful of greedily chosen "magic points," so only that handful of function evaluations is ever needed. Their version lived in a function space with its own bespoke greedy basis and a lower-triangular interpolation system. Mine is going to be flatly discrete: a POD basis $\mathbf{U}$ on $\mathbb{R}^n$ and a clean matrix $\mathbf{U}(\mathbf{P}^\top\mathbf{U})^{-1}\mathbf{P}^\top$.

Let me make sure this thing is actually an interpolation. Apply $\mathbf{P}^\top$ to $\hat{\mathbf{f}}$:
$$\mathbf{P}^\top\hat{\mathbf{f}}=\mathbf{P}^\top\mathbf{U}(\mathbf{P}^\top\mathbf{U})^{-1}\mathbf{P}^\top\mathbf{f}=(\mathbf{P}^\top\mathbf{U})(\mathbf{P}^\top\mathbf{U})^{-1}\mathbf{P}^\top\mathbf{f}=\mathbf{P}^\top\mathbf{f}.$$
So $\hat{\mathbf{f}}$ agrees with $\mathbf{f}$ exactly at the $m$ selected indices — it interpolates. And $\mathcal{P}:=\mathbf{U}(\mathbf{P}^\top\mathbf{U})^{-1}\mathbf{P}^\top$ is idempotent, $\mathcal{P}^2=\mathbf{U}(\mathbf{P}^\top\mathbf{U})^{-1}(\mathbf{P}^\top\mathbf{U})(\mathbf{P}^\top\mathbf{U})^{-1}\mathbf{P}^\top=\mathcal{P}$, with range $\mathrm{Range}(\mathbf{U})$ — it's a projector, but an *oblique* one, not the orthogonal $\mathbf{U}\mathbf{U}^\top$. That obliqueness is the price I pay for only looking at $m$ rows. I'll have to keep an eye on whether it costs me accuracy.

But first the payoff, because it's only real if it actually removes the $n$-dependence. For my componentwise $\mathbf{F}$, here is the crucial little identity:
$$\mathbf{P}^\top\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{V}_k\tilde{\mathbf{y}})=\mathbf{F}\big(\mathbf{P}^\top\mathbf{V}_k\tilde{\mathbf{y}}\big).$$
Selecting rows $\wp$ of $\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{z})$ is the same as evaluating $\mathbf{F}$ on rows $\wp$ of $\mathbf{z}$, *because* $\mathbf{F}$ acts entrywise — the $\wp_i$-th output depends only on the $\wp_i$-th input. So I never lift to $\mathbb{R}^n$ at all. I precompute $\mathbf{V}_\wp:=\mathbf{P}^\top\mathbf{V}_k\in\mathbb{R}^{m\times k}$ (just $m$ rows of $\mathbf{V}_k$), and at each step form $\mathbf{V}_\wp\tilde{\mathbf{y}}\in\mathbb{R}^m$, evaluate $\mathbf{F}$ on those $m$ numbers, and multiply by the precomputed constant. The reduced nonlinear term is
$$\mathbf{V}_k^\top\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{V}_k\tilde{\mathbf{y}})\approx\underbrace{\mathbf{V}_k^\top\mathbf{U}(\mathbf{P}^\top\mathbf{U})^{-1}}_{\mathbf{B}\,\in\,\mathbb{R}^{k\times m},\ \text{precomputed}}\,\mathbf{F}\big(\mathbf{V}_\wp\tilde{\mathbf{y}}\big).$$
The matrix $\mathbf{B}=\mathbf{V}_k^\top\mathbf{U}(\mathbf{P}^\top\mathbf{U})^{-1}$ has no $\tau$-dependence — formed once. So each step costs: $\mathbf{V}_\wp\tilde{\mathbf{y}}$ at $2mk$ flops, $\mathbf{F}$ at $m$ points at $\alpha(m)$, and $\mathbf{B}(\cdot)$ at $2mk$, total $\mathcal{O}(\alpha(m)+4km)$ — *independent of $n$*. That's the kill. The Newton Jacobian goes the same way: $\mathbf{B}\,\mathrm{diag}\{F'(\mathbf{V}_\wp\tilde{\mathbf{y}})\}\,\mathbf{V}_\wp$, with cost $\mathcal{O}(\alpha(m)+4mk+2mk^2)$, again no $n$.

I should pause on the componentwise assumption, because real nonlinearities aren't always entrywise. Suppose the $i$-th output of $\mathbf{F}$ depends on a small index set $\mathbf{p}_i$ of inputs (a stencil), $[\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{y})]_i=F_i(\mathbf{y}(\mathbf{p}_i))$. Then to get the selected rows $\mathbf{P}^\top\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{V}_k\tilde{\mathbf{y}})$ I only need, for each selected $\wp_i$, the inputs in $\mathbf{p}_{\wp_i}$: compute $\mathbf{V}_k(\mathbf{p}_{\wp_i},:)\tilde{\mathbf{y}}$ and apply $F_{\wp_i}$. The cost is $\sum_i n_{\wp_i}\cdot k$ plus the scalar evaluations, so this stays $n$-independent exactly when $\sum_i n_{\wp_i}\ll n$ — i.e. when the dependence is local/sparse, which is the typical FD/FEM situation. (If the stencils overlapped the whole grid it would be cheaper to just lift and sample directly, but that's the degenerate case.) Componentwise is just the special case $\mathbf{p}_i=\{i\}$. Good — the trick is general, the entrywise case is the clean illustration.

Now the part I deferred: *which* $m$ indices? This is the whole game. Two things can go wrong. First, $\mathbf{P}^\top\mathbf{U}$ must be invertible — pick rows where the modes are degenerate and the inverse doesn't exist. Second, even if invertible, $(\mathbf{P}^\top\mathbf{U})^{-1}$ might be huge, and since my approximation is $\mathbf{U}(\mathbf{P}^\top\mathbf{U})^{-1}\mathbf{P}^\top\mathbf{f}$, a huge inverse magnifies error catastrophically. So I want indices that keep $\mathbf{P}^\top\mathbf{U}$ well away from singular. Let me first quantify exactly how the inverse enters the error, so I know what I'm fighting.

Let $\mathbf{f}_*=\mathbf{U}\mathbf{U}^\top\mathbf{f}$ be the best (orthogonal) $m$-term approximation of $\mathbf{f}$ from $\mathrm{Range}(\mathbf{U})$, and split $\mathbf{f}=\mathbf{w}+\mathbf{f}_*$ with $\mathbf{w}=(\mathbf{I}-\mathbf{U}\mathbf{U}^\top)\mathbf{f}$ the orthogonal remainder. Apply my oblique projector $\mathcal{P}=\mathbf{U}(\mathbf{P}^\top\mathbf{U})^{-1}\mathbf{P}^\top$:
$$\hat{\mathbf{f}}=\mathcal{P}\mathbf{f}=\mathcal{P}\mathbf{w}+\mathcal{P}\mathbf{f}_*.$$
Since $\mathbf{f}_*\in\mathrm{Range}(\mathbf{U})$ and $\mathcal{P}$ is the identity on its own range, $\mathcal{P}\mathbf{f}_*=\mathbf{f}_*$. So $\hat{\mathbf{f}}=\mathcal{P}\mathbf{w}+\mathbf{f}_*$, and the error is
$$\mathbf{f}-\hat{\mathbf{f}}=(\mathbf{w}+\mathbf{f}_*)-(\mathcal{P}\mathbf{w}+\mathbf{f}_*)=(\mathbf{I}-\mathcal{P})\mathbf{w}.$$
The best-approximation part passes through untouched; only the remainder $\mathbf{w}$ gets distorted. Therefore
$$\|\mathbf{f}-\hat{\mathbf{f}}\|_2=\|(\mathbf{I}-\mathcal{P})\mathbf{w}\|_2\le\|\mathbf{I}-\mathcal{P}\|_2\,\|\mathbf{w}\|_2.$$
Now I need $\|\mathbf{I}-\mathcal{P}\|_2$. There's a clean fact for any projector that's neither $\mathbf{0}$ nor $\mathbf{I}$: $\|\mathbf{I}-\mathcal{P}\|_2=\|\mathcal{P}\|_2$ — the complementary oblique projector has the same norm. And
$$\|\mathcal{P}\|_2=\|\mathbf{U}(\mathbf{P}^\top\mathbf{U})^{-1}\mathbf{P}^\top\|_2\le\|\mathbf{U}\|_2\,\|(\mathbf{P}^\top\mathbf{U})^{-1}\|_2\,\|\mathbf{P}^\top\|_2=\|(\mathbf{P}^\top\mathbf{U})^{-1}\|_2,$$
because $\mathbf{U}$ has orthonormal columns ($\|\mathbf{U}\|_2=1$) and $\mathbf{P}$ is a selection of identity columns ($\|\mathbf{P}^\top\|_2=1$). Putting it together,
$$\boxed{\ \|\mathbf{f}-\hat{\mathbf{f}}\|_2\le\|(\mathbf{P}^\top\mathbf{U})^{-1}\|_2\cdot\underbrace{\|(\mathbf{I}-\mathbf{U}\mathbf{U}^\top)\mathbf{f}\|_2}_{\mathcal{E}_*(\mathbf{f})}.\ }$$
So the interpolation error is the *orthogonal-projection error* $\mathcal{E}_*(\mathbf{f})$ — the unavoidable cost of working in a rank-$m$ subspace — multiplied by a single magnification factor $\|(\mathbf{P}^\top\mathbf{U})^{-1}\|_2$ that depends *only* on which rows I chose. This is exactly what I wanted: the cheap interpolation is "nearly as good as" the expensive orthogonal projection, as long as I keep $\|(\mathbf{P}^\top\mathbf{U})^{-1}\|_2$ small. My index-selection job is now crisp: **choose $\wp_1,\dots,\wp_m$ to keep $\|(\mathbf{P}^\top\mathbf{U})^{-1}\|_2$ small.**

Searching all $\binom{n}{m}$ row subsets to minimize that norm is hopeless. So I'll build the set greedily, one index at a time, and design each step to limit the *growth* of $\|(\mathbf{P}^\top\mathbf{U})^{-1}\|_2$. To see what controls the growth, let me track $\mathbf{M}=\mathbf{P}^\top\mathbf{U}$ as I add one mode and one index. At the first step, $\mathbf{U}=\mathbf{u}_1$, $\mathbf{P}=\mathbf{e}_{\wp_1}$, so $\mathbf{M}=\mathbf{e}_{\wp_1}^\top\mathbf{u}_1$ is a scalar and $\|\mathbf{M}^{-1}\|_2=1/|\mathbf{e}_{\wp_1}^\top\mathbf{u}_1|=\|\mathbf{u}_1\|_\infty^{-1}$ if I pick $\wp_1$ at the largest-magnitude entry of $\mathbf{u}_1$. Picking the max-magnitude entry *minimizes* this — it's the smallest possible $\|\mathbf{M}^{-1}\|_2$ at step one. That's the seed: $\wp_1=\arg\max_i|u_1(i)|$.

Now a general step $\ell\ge 2$. Write $\bar{\mathbf{U}}=[\mathbf{u}_1,\dots,\mathbf{u}_{\ell-1}]$, $\bar{\mathbf{P}}=[\mathbf{e}_{\wp_1},\dots,\mathbf{e}_{\wp_{\ell-1}}]$ for what I already have, $\bar{\mathbf{M}}=\bar{\mathbf{P}}^\top\bar{\mathbf{U}}$, and append $\mathbf{u}=\mathbf{u}_\ell$, $\mathbf{p}=\mathbf{e}_{\wp_\ell}$. Then
$$\mathbf{M}=\mathbf{P}^\top\mathbf{U}=\begin{bmatrix}\bar{\mathbf{M}}&\bar{\mathbf{P}}^\top\mathbf{u}\\[2pt]\mathbf{p}^\top\bar{\mathbf{U}}&\mathbf{p}^\top\mathbf{u}\end{bmatrix}.$$
Set $\mathbf{a}^\top=\mathbf{p}^\top\bar{\mathbf{U}}$ (the new row, restricted to old modes), $\mathbf{c}=\bar{\mathbf{M}}^{-1}\bar{\mathbf{P}}^\top\mathbf{u}$, and $\rho=\mathbf{p}^\top\mathbf{u}-\mathbf{a}^\top\mathbf{c}=\mathbf{p}^\top(\mathbf{u}-\bar{\mathbf{U}}\bar{\mathbf{M}}^{-1}\bar{\mathbf{P}}^\top\mathbf{u})$. That parenthesized vector is the *residual* of mode $\mathbf{u}_\ell$ after I interpolate it through the previous modes at the previous indices — call it $\mathbf{r}=\mathbf{u}_\ell-\bar{\mathbf{U}}\mathbf{c}$ — and $\rho=\mathbf{p}^\top\mathbf{r}=r_{\wp_\ell}$ is just the new-index entry of that residual. With this, $\mathbf{M}$ factors as
$$\mathbf{M}=\begin{bmatrix}\bar{\mathbf{M}}&\mathbf{0}\\\mathbf{a}^\top&\rho\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}\mathbf{I}&\mathbf{c}\\\mathbf{0}&1\end{bmatrix},$$
and inverting,
$$\mathbf{M}^{-1}=\begin{bmatrix}\mathbf{I}&-\mathbf{c}\\\mathbf{0}&1\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}\bar{\mathbf{M}}^{-1}&\mathbf{0}\\-\rho^{-1}\mathbf{a}^\top\bar{\mathbf{M}}^{-1}&\rho^{-1}\end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix}\bar{\mathbf{M}}^{-1}&\mathbf{0}\\\mathbf{0}&0\end{bmatrix}+\rho^{-1}\begin{bmatrix}\mathbf{c}\\-1\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}\mathbf{a}^\top&-1\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}\bar{\mathbf{M}}^{-1}&\mathbf{0}\\\mathbf{0}&1\end{bmatrix}.$$
I need to take this norm without losing a factor. The last display is really
$$\mathbf{M}^{-1}=\left(\begin{bmatrix}\mathbf{I}&\mathbf{0}\\\mathbf{0}&0\end{bmatrix}+\rho^{-1}\begin{bmatrix}\mathbf{c}\\-1\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}\mathbf{a}^\top&-1\end{bmatrix}\right)\begin{bmatrix}\bar{\mathbf{M}}^{-1}&\mathbf{0}\\\mathbf{0}&1\end{bmatrix}.$$
At the first step $\|\mathbf{M}^{-1}\|_2=\|\mathbf{u}_1\|_\infty^{-1}\ge 1$, and the recursion preserves $\|\bar{\mathbf{M}}^{-1}\|_2\ge 1$, so the last block has norm $\|\bar{\mathbf{M}}^{-1}\|_2$. The bracket contributes at most one from the coordinate projector plus the rank-one term. For the rank-one term, reintroduce the orthonormal block $[\bar{\mathbf{U}},\mathbf{u}]$:
$$\Big\|\begin{bmatrix}\mathbf{c}\\-1\end{bmatrix}\Big\|=\Big\|[\bar{\mathbf{U}},\mathbf{u}]\begin{bmatrix}\mathbf{c}\\-1\end{bmatrix}\Big\|=\|\bar{\mathbf{U}}\mathbf{c}-\mathbf{u}\|_2=\|\mathbf{r}\|_2,$$
using that $[\bar{\mathbf{U}},\mathbf{u}]$ has orthonormal columns. Also $\|[\mathbf{a}^\top,-1]\|_2=\sqrt{1+\|\mathbf{a}\|_2^2}\le\sqrt{2}$, since $\mathbf{a}=\bar{\mathbf{U}}^\top\mathbf{p}$ is the coordinate vector of one identity column in an orthonormal basis and therefore $\|\mathbf{a}\|_2\le 1$. Finally, once I pick $\wp_\ell$ at the largest-magnitude entry of $\mathbf{r}$, $|\rho|=\|\mathbf{r}\|_\infty$, so $\|\mathbf{r}\|_2\le\sqrt{n}\|\mathbf{r}\|_\infty=\sqrt{n}|\rho|$. The rank-one norm inside the bracket is therefore at most $|\rho|^{-1}\sqrt{n}|\rho|\sqrt{2}=\sqrt{2n}$, and
$$\|\mathbf{M}^{-1}\|_2\le\big(1+\sqrt{2n}\big)\,\|\bar{\mathbf{M}}^{-1}\|_2.$$
There it is. The factor by which $\|(\mathbf{P}^\top\mathbf{U})^{-1}\|_2$ can grow at step $\ell$ is $(1+\sqrt{2n})$ *only after* I've used $|\rho|=\|\mathbf{r}\|_\infty$ — and the way to keep the growth as small as the analysis allows is to make $|\rho|$ as *large* as possible, i.e. to put the new index where the residual $\mathbf{r}=\mathbf{u}_\ell-\bar{\mathbf{U}}\mathbf{c}$ has its largest magnitude. Maximizing $|\rho|$ minimizes $1/|\rho|$, which is the actual lever on the increment of $\|\mathbf{M}^{-1}\|_2$. **The greedy rule writes itself: at each step, pick the index of the largest-magnitude entry of the residual.** The bound itself unrolls to a constant $C_1=\|\mathbf{u}_1\|_\infty^{-1}$, $C_\ell=(1+\sqrt{2n})C_{\ell-1}$, so $\|(\mathbf{P}^\top\mathbf{U})^{-1}\|_2\le C_m=(1+\sqrt{2n})^{m-1}\|\mathbf{u}_1\|_\infty^{-1}$. That's wildly pessimistic — it grows like $(2n)^{(m-1)/2}$ — but it's only a worst-case ceiling; in practice $\|(\mathbf{P}^\top\mathbf{U})^{-1}\|_2$ stays tiny, and what matters is that the *greedy step* provably attacks the growth.

Let me write the selection cleanly as an algorithm. Seed with $\wp_1=\arg\max_i|u_1(i)|$, $\mathbf{U}\leftarrow[\mathbf{u}_1]$, $\mathbf{P}\leftarrow[\mathbf{e}_{\wp_1}]$. Then for $\ell=2,\dots,m$: solve the small system $(\mathbf{P}^\top\mathbf{U})\mathbf{c}=\mathbf{P}^\top\mathbf{u}_\ell$ for the coefficients that make the current basis interpolate mode $\ell$ at the chosen indices; form the residual $\mathbf{r}=\mathbf{u}_\ell-\mathbf{U}\mathbf{c}$; set $\wp_\ell=\arg\max_i|r(i)|$; append. By construction $r_{\wp_i}=0$ for the previously chosen $i$ (the interpolant already matches there), so the new index is genuinely new, and as long as the modes are linearly independent $\mathbf{r}\ne\mathbf{0}$ at every step, forcing $|\rho|=\|\mathbf{r}\|_\infty>0$ — which is precisely the condition that keeps $\mathbf{P}^\top\mathbf{U}$ nonsingular and makes the whole scheme well-defined. The indices come out hierarchical and non-repeated: the first $\ell$ of them are a valid index set for any $\ell\le m$. And the order of the input modes matters — I feed them ordered by descending singular value, because the error analysis leans on $\mathbf{U}$ being the POD basis and the most-energetic modes being resolved first.

Two clean facts fall out that reassure me the construction is right. First, once the indices are fixed, the approximation doesn't care which basis I used to span $\mathrm{Range}(\mathbf{U})$: if $\mathbf{Q}$ is another basis with $\mathbf{U}=\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{R}$ for nonsingular $\mathbf{R}$, then $\mathbf{U}(\mathbf{P}^\top\mathbf{U})^{-1}\mathbf{P}^\top=\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{R}(\mathbf{P}^\top\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{R})^{-1}\mathbf{P}^\top=\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{R}\mathbf{R}^{-1}(\mathbf{P}^\top\mathbf{Q})^{-1}\mathbf{P}^\top=\mathbf{Q}(\mathbf{P}^\top\mathbf{Q})^{-1}\mathbf{P}^\top$ — the oblique projector is basis-independent. Second, why POD for $\mathbf{U}$ and not some other basis? Because the bound multiplies the magnification factor by $\mathcal{E}_*(\mathbf{f})=\|(\mathbf{I}-\mathbf{U}\mathbf{U}^\top)\mathbf{f}\|_2$, and POD is exactly the basis that minimizes that orthogonal-projection error for the nonlinear snapshots — Eckart–Young again, now applied to $\mathbf{F}$'s snapshots. It also hands me a free *estimate* of $\mathcal{E}_*$: if I SVD the nonlinear-snapshot matrix and keep $m$ modes, then for any $\mathbf{f}$ nearly in the snapshot span, $\mathcal{E}_*(\mathbf{f})\approx\sigma_{m+1}$, the first discarded singular value. So I can read off the expected accuracy and pick $m$ at the knee of the nonlinear spectrum, without computing the full-$n$ projection error at runtime.

I notice this is *not* the same as gappy POD even though both fit coefficients from a few entries. Gappy POD (Everson–Sirovich) fits by least squares over a mask of observed entries, $\hat{\mathbf{c}}=(\boldsymbol{\Theta}^\top\boldsymbol{\Theta})^{-1}\boldsymbol{\Theta}^\top\mathbf{f}$ with $\boldsymbol{\Theta}$ the observed rows — an *over*determined fit — and, crucially, it never said *which* entries to observe; the mask is given or random. Here I take exactly $m$ rows for $m$ modes, so $\mathbf{P}^\top\mathbf{U}$ is square and I *interpolate* rather than least-squares-fit, and the residual-greedy *is* the missing prescription for which rows. The pains are different too: gappy reconstructs a sparsely-sampled field; I'm cheapening a nonlinear *term* buried inside reduced dynamics so the whole reduced model runs in $n$-independent time.

So the full reduced model, assembled offline, is
$$\frac{d}{dt}\tilde{\mathbf{y}}=\tilde{\mathbf{A}}\,\tilde{\mathbf{y}}+\mathbf{B}\,\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{V}_\wp\tilde{\mathbf{y}}),\qquad \tilde{\mathbf{A}}=\mathbf{V}_k^\top\mathbf{A}\mathbf{V}_k,\quad \mathbf{B}=\mathbf{V}_k^\top\mathbf{U}(\mathbf{P}^\top\mathbf{U})^{-1},\quad \mathbf{V}_\wp=\mathbf{P}^\top\mathbf{V}_k,$$
and every per-step quantity is sized by $k$ and $m$, never $n$. Let me write it as code, grounded in the two primitives that already exist — a truncated SVD for the two POD bases, and a small dense solve inside the greedy.

```python
import numpy as np


def pod_basis(S, r):
    """Leading r left singular vectors of snapshots S (n x n_s).
       Eckart-Young => optimal rank-r least-squares fit; order by sing. value."""
    U, sv, _ = np.linalg.svd(S, full_matrices=False)
    return U[:, :r], sv


def select_interpolation_dofs(U):
    """Greedy row selection: at each step put the new row where the residual
       r = u_l - U c (mode l minus its interpolation through previous modes/points)
       is largest in magnitude -- this maximizes |rho|=||r||_inf, the lever on the
       growth of ||(P^T U)^{-1}||_2."""
    n, m = U.shape
    idx = np.empty(m, dtype=int)
    idx[0] = np.argmax(np.abs(U[:, 0]))               # rho_1 = ||u_1||_inf
    for ell in range(1, m):
        Ue, rows = U[:, :ell], idx[:ell]
        c = np.linalg.solve(Ue[rows, :], U[rows, ell])  # (P^T U) c = P^T u_l
        r = U[:, ell] - Ue @ c                         # residual, zero at previous P
        idx[ell] = np.argmax(np.abs(r))                # argmax |r|
    return idx


def galerkin(A, Vk):
    return Vk.T @ A @ Vk


def assemble_reduced_operators(A, Vk, U, idx):
    """Offline assembly of the n-independent reduced operators."""
    At = galerkin(A, Vk)                               # k x k linear part
    interpolation_matrix = U[idx, :]                   # P^T U
    coupling = np.linalg.solve(interpolation_matrix.T, (Vk.T @ U).T).T
    sample_basis = Vk[idx, :]                          # P^T Vk
    return At, coupling, sample_basis


def reduced_rhs(y_red, t, At, coupling, sample_basis, F_sample):
    """dy~/dt: F_sample is evaluated only at the selected rows, never on R^n."""
    sampled_state = sample_basis @ y_red
    return At @ y_red + coupling @ F_sample(sampled_state, t)  # O(alpha(m)+4mk)


def integrate(rhs, y0_red, dt, nt):
    y = y0_red.copy()
    for step in range(nt):
        y = y + dt * rhs(y, step * dt)
    return y
```

The causal chain, end to end: a high-dimensional nonlinear ODE lives near a low-dimensional manifold, so a POD basis $\mathbf{V}_k$ Galerkin-reduces it — but the nonlinear term $\mathbf{V}_k^\top\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{V}_k\tilde{\mathbf{y}})$ still costs $\mathcal{O}(\alpha(n)+nk)$ because $\mathbf{F}$ must be touched at all $n$ grid points; orthogonal projection onto a second POD basis $\mathbf{U}$ for the nonlinearity is the most accurate fix but needs all $n$ entries too, so instead the coefficients are fixed by *interpolation* at $m$ rows, $\mathbf{f}\approx\mathbf{U}(\mathbf{P}^\top\mathbf{U})^{-1}\mathbf{P}^\top\mathbf{f}$; for a (componentwise/local) $\mathbf{F}$ this means evaluating $\mathbf{F}$ at only those $m$ rows, $\mathbf{B}\,\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{V}_\wp\tilde{\mathbf{y}})$, with $\mathbf{B}$ precomputed, so the step is $n$-independent; the error is the orthogonal-projection error $\mathcal{E}_*(\mathbf{f})$ times the single magnification factor $\|(\mathbf{P}^\top\mathbf{U})^{-1}\|_2$, whose stepwise growth is capped by $(1+\sqrt{2n})\|\bar{\mathbf{M}}^{-1}\|$ and minimized by choosing each new index at the residual's largest entry — the greedy that makes the cheap interpolation provably nearly as good as the expensive projection.
