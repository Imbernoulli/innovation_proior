Let me start from the single operation that actually decides everything here, which is how I collapse the spatial grid. The backbone hands me a tensor of activations: `C` feature maps, each an `H × W` grid, all non-negative because the last thing it does is a ReLU. The classifier head only ever sees a fixed-length vector — it can't consume a grid — so somewhere I have to reduce each `H × W` map to one number and hand up a `C`-vector. That reduction is the whole bottleneck between the spatial evidence the convolutions found and the label I have to predict. Whatever it throws away is gone; whatever it inflates, the head inherits. So the question is narrow: given one feature map, a bag of `N = H·W` non-negative activations, what single scalar should I emit?

The old answer was: don't reduce at all, just flatten the whole `C × H × W` tensor into one long vector and run fully-connected layers on top into the softmax. And I can see why people are moving off it. The FC stack holds the overwhelming majority of the parameters and is exactly the part that overfits — it's the reason dropout was bolted onto those layers in the first place. And flattening fixes the geometry: a fully-connected layer needs a fixed-length input, so the whole network is pinned to a single input size, and the spatial arrangement gets memorized into weights instead of being handled translation-invariantly. That last point is the real tell. I *want* translation invariance — an object in the top-left should classify the same as one in the bottom-right — and an FC layer over a flattened grid fights that, because position `(0,0)` and position `(7,7)` go through different weights.

So Lin, Chen and Yan's move is the right one structurally: kill the FC stack and instead pool each final feature map down to a single number globally, producing a `C`-vector straight into the classifier. No parameters at that layer, so no overfitting introduced there; the feature maps become interpretable as per-category confidence maps; and because you've summed the spatial information out, the head is robust to translation and works for any spatial size. Good — global pooling is clearly the head I want. But "global pooling" is not yet an answer, it's a slot. The entire question has just relocated to: *which* pooling rule fills it. Their choice is the average, `f_k = (1/N) Σ x`. Let me take that as the default and figure out whether it's actually the right rule for my setting, because if it is, I'm done, and if it isn't, the reason it fails will tell me what to build.

Let me look hard at what my supervision and my data actually are, because that's what the pooling rule has to serve. The label is *image-level*: it tells me which categories are present in the image and nothing about where. And a real training image is cluttered — the object I care about sits in a few spatial locations, surrounded by a large field of background, texture, and other co-occurring stuff. So for a channel that has learned to detect, say, a class-discriminative part, what does its feature map look like on a positive image? It fires hard at a handful of locations — wherever that part actually is — and is weak or dead across the rest of the grid: the sky, the road, the blur. The discriminative signal is *localized and sparse*: concentrated at a few unknown locations against a large mostly-empty field. That's the structure the collapse has to respect.

Now run the average on that. `f_k = (1/N) Σ x`. Picture the map: five strong locations where the part is, a thousand near-zero locations everywhere else. The average gives each of those thousand dead locations the same per-pixel weight `1/N` as each of the five informative ones, so the five strong responses get divided down and smeared across the thousand. The summary `f_k` ends up reflecting *how much area* responded far more than *how strongly the best location responded*. On a cluttered image, where the object is a small fraction of the frame, that's exactly backwards: the average is dominated by the background simply because the background is most of the pixels. Two images that are both clearly "contains the object" but where the object occupies very different fractions of the frame get very different averaged scores, even though the *presence* is equally certain in both. The mean is stable, yes — but it's stable precisely because it refuses to notice that the field is sparse. Wall: averaging is the wrong rule when the label is "present somewhere" and the evidence is local.

And it's not just the forward summary that's wrong; the backward is wrong in the same way, and that matters because I'm training. Backprop through `f_k = (1/N) Σ x` sends the upstream gradient back to *every* spatial location with equal weight `1/N`. So when I get the error signal for "this class is present," I spread it uniformly over the whole grid — I nudge the background locations toward "object" just as much as the one location where the object actually is. Under image-level labels that's actively harmful: I have no per-location label, so distributing credit uniformly trains the channel to respond to the background too, which is how you overfit to scene context ("motorbikes are on roads, so fire on road"). I want the opposite: when the label says "present," I want to push up the score at the location that is *most likely to be the object*, not at the whole frame.

So what is the location that's most likely to be the object? I don't have its coordinates. But I have the channel's own opinion: the location where it responded most strongly is, by construction, the location it is most confident is the thing it detects. That reframes the whole problem in a way I should have seen immediately given the label semantics. The label is a bag-level bit — "the object is present *somewhere* in this image" — with the per-location truth hidden. That is exactly the Multiple Instance Learning setup: the image is a bag, each spatial location (each window's score) is an instance, the bag is positive iff at least one instance is positive, and I never see instance labels. So let me ask the MIL question literally: given per-location scores and the rule "positive iff *some* location is positive," what's the right way to summarize the bag into one number?

If "present" means "∃ a location with a high score," then the quantity that decides the bag is the *largest* instance score — `max_x`. A bag is positive when its strongest instance is strong; it doesn't matter how many weak instances there are. So under this label model the average is answering the wrong question (it asks "are locations on average high," which background drags down) and I should be reading off the maximum:

  f_k = max_{x ∈ X_k} x.

Let me feel whether that actually fixes the two failures. Forward: on the cluttered image with five strong locations and a thousand dead ones, the max returns the single strongest response and completely ignores how much dead area surrounds it. So the summary now tracks *how strongly the best location responded* — the certainty of the most-confident evidence — independent of how small the object is relative to the frame. The two images where the object occupies very different fractions but is equally clearly present now get the same high score, because both have at least one strongly-firing location. That's the invariance I wanted: the score depends on the peak evidence, not on the object's area. The background, no matter how vast, contributes nothing as long as it stays below the peak. This is the right reading of "present somewhere."

Now the backward, which is where this earns its keep. What is the gradient of `f_k = max_i x_i` with respect to the inputs? If one location wins, call it `j = argmax_i x_i`, then the output is just `x_j`. So the derivative is the selector I want:

  ∂f_k/∂x_i = 1 if i = j, and 0 otherwise.

At exact ties the ordinary derivative is not unique and an implementation chooses a max index or another valid subgradient convention, but away from ties the rule is one-hot. So backprop routes the *entire* upstream gradient to exactly the single highest-scoring location and *zero* to all the others. Stare at what that does under image-level labels. If the image is positive — the object is present — the error signal raises the score at the one location the channel already thinks is most object-like, and (because the convolution weights are shared across all spatial positions) raising it there raises it at every other location that looks similar across the dataset. I'm not telling the channel "the object is everywhere"; I'm telling it "whatever you were most confident about, be more confident about that and things like it." And if the image is negative — the object is absent — the same routing sends the error to the highest-scoring location and *lowers* it: I'm suppressing the channel's single most tempting false alarm, the patch that most looked like the object but wasn't. That is hard-negative mining, falling straight out of the max's backward — I automatically train against the most confusing background patch on every negative image, which is exactly the patch worth training against.

And here's the thing that makes me sure this is the right object and not just a convenient one: the network is given only a presence/absence bit, yet because the max picks a *location* every step, it has a concrete location hypothesis to update. The forward max says, "if this class is present, the best current candidate is the argmax." The backward then strengthens that candidate on positive images and weakens the most tempting false candidate on negative images. There is no guarantee that the chosen location is correct on every update, especially early on, but wrong peaks should be inconsistent across images while true object evidence is repeatable. The average has no comparable selector: spreading gradient uniformly gives the network no reason to make one spatial response explain the label. The max's all-or-nothing routing is the mechanism that makes a localized response pay during training even though no location label is provided.

Let me make sure this isn't just a re-skin of the old soft aggregators, because Keeler, Rumelhart and Leow already did "recover location from location-free labels" — their integrated segmentation-and-recognition net puts a fixed forward model on top of a grid of per-location scores and backpropagates a non-specific error from presence targets alone. The difference is precisely the aggregator. They summed exponential, softmax-like units — a *smooth* combination of all the locations. A soft sum mixes every location's contribution into the output, so the background still leaks in, and the error signal is shared across the whole field, weighted but never zeroed. That's strictly gentler than what I want: it doesn't *isolate* the most-confident location, it blends it with everyone else. The hard counterpart is to commit fully to the single best location, send the gradient there and nowhere else. The soft version recovers location through a blended response; the hard version commits to a location every step, which is what gives the clean argmax router and the clean hard-negative mining.

Now let me check the practical contract, because the head has to drop into a fixed harness and survive the architectures it'll see. It must take `[B, C, H, W]` and return `[B, C]` with the channel dimension untouched and any spatial size. The max over the `H × W` axes per channel does exactly this by construction: I reduce over the two spatial dimensions and keep `B` and `C`, so `[B, C, H, W] → [B, C]` for any spatial grid — a `1×1` map's max is just its single value, no special case. Size-independence is automatic, same as it was for the average; it's a property of reducing over the spatial axes, not of which reduction I pick. And like the average, the max has *no parameters* — it's a pure reduction — so I introduce nothing to overfit at this layer and nothing to optimize, which keeps the only-the-pooling-rule-changes promise.

I should be honest with myself about what I'm giving up, because the same sharpness that buys the localization is also a cost, and I want to know its shape even if I'm keeping the rule. By committing to a single location per channel per image, I discard every *other* location that also fired — the corroborating evidence. If the object genuinely lit up five windows, four of them are thrown on the floor; only the single peak survives into the descriptor, and only that one location gets any gradient. So the descriptor is at the mercy of one value: a single noisy or spurious peak — a background patch that happens to spike — can hijack the whole component, and the channel's other true responses contribute nothing to either the forward score or the learning signal. The average had the opposite vice (it heard everyone, so it drowned the signal in background); the max has this one (it hears exactly one voice, so it's brittle to which voice and deaf to the rest). That's the genuine trade, and it's the right trade for the regime I'm in — image-level labels, localized sparse evidence, clutter — because there the cost of diluting the signal is worse than the cost of betting on the single peak.

So let me write it as the layer that fills the pooling slot. The operation is: for each channel, take the maximum activation over the whole `H × W` grid. The framework gives me an adaptive max-pool primitive that reduces any spatial size to a target output size, so asking it for a `1×1` output is exactly the global max, and it's fused and GPU-friendly. I reshape the `[B, C, 1, 1]` result to `[B, C]`.

```python
import torch
import torch.nn as nn
import torch.nn.functional as F


class CustomPool(nn.Module):
    """Global max pooling.

    f_k = max over the H x W grid of channel k.  No parameters.
    Reads off the single strongest activation per channel -- the most-confident
    location -- so the per-channel summary tracks the peak evidence, not the
    field average, which matches an image-level "present somewhere" label.
    """

    def __init__(self):
        super().__init__()

    def forward(self, x):                       # x: [B, C, H, W], x >= 0 after ReLU
        # collapse the whole H x W grid of each channel to its maximum -> [B, C]
        # adaptive_max_pool2d(., 1) handles any spatial size, including 1x1
        return F.adaptive_max_pool2d(x, 1).view(x.size(0), -1)
```

For the forward values, this is equivalent to `torch.amax(x, dim=(-2, -1))`, a max reduction over the two spatial axes — same result, same `[B, C]` shape, and it makes the "one number per channel, the strongest" reading explicit.

So the whole chain, end to end: the head is bottlenecked by one spatial collapse, and the FC-then-flatten default pays a heavy parametric, fixed-size, translation-fragile price, which pushes me to global pooling; the default global rule, the average, weights every location equally and so dilutes a localized sparse response against the large background field and spreads the learning signal uniformly over the grid — wrong when the label is only "present somewhere" and the object is a small cluttered fraction of the frame; reading the label as a Multiple Instance Learning bag — positive iff some location is positive — makes the *maximum* over locations the natural summary, since the bag is decided by its strongest instance; the max's forward tracks the peak evidence and ignores how much dead area surrounds it, and its backward is a hard selector that routes the entire gradient to the single argmax location and zero elsewhere for a unique argmax, which on positive images sharpens the most-confident location (and similar ones, via weight sharing) and on negative images suppresses the most-tempting false alarm — automatic hard-negative mining — so a network given only presence/absence labels gets a usable location hypothesis without location labels; it is parameter-free and size-independent because it's a pure reduction over the spatial axes; its one real cost is brittleness to a single peak and discarding the other locations that also fired, which is the acceptable price for matching the image-level, localized-evidence regime; and it drops into the head as a single global max reduction, `F.adaptive_max_pool2d(x, 1)` reshaped to `[B, C]`.
