The problem is to turn a 2D molecular graph — atoms, bonds, bond orders, stereochemistry, but no coordinates — into a useful ensemble of 3D conformers. Downstream tasks like docking, pharmacophore matching, shape comparison, and 3D descriptor calculation all need real Cartesian coordinates, and because flexible molecules populate many low-energy shapes, a single structure is rarely enough. The ensemble must be realistic, diverse, and cheap enough to run on millions of molecules without a starting geometry or human supervision. These three requirements pull against one another: methods that are geometrically correct often produce physically implausible torsions, while methods that clean up structures with a full force field tend to collapse diversity and become expensive.

The classic distance-geometry pipeline builds a bounds matrix of allowed interatomic distances, smooths it with triangle inequalities, samples a distance matrix, and converts it to coordinates through metric-matrix embedding. This works for bonds, angles, and steric clashes, but it has two serious weaknesses. First, the bounds encode torsions only as broad 1–4 distance ranges, so rotatable bonds come out roughly uniformly distributed instead of clustered in the sharp basins seen in experimental crystal structures. Second, a pairwise van der Waals lower bound cannot enforce planarity: aromatic rings pucker, sp2 centers bend out of plane, and triple bonds lose their linearity. The usual remedy is to minimize every conformer with a classical force field such as MMFF94 or UFF, but that is slow per conformer, drags structures toward the same few minima, and replaces experimental torsion preferences with whatever the force-field parameters happen to prescribe.

The method I propose is Experimental-Torsion Knowledge Distance Geometry, abbreviated ETKDG. It keeps the efficient distance-geometry core but replaces the expensive generic force-field cleanup with targeted, experimentally derived knowledge. The pipeline starts from the 2D graph and builds the N×N bounds matrix: 1–2 distances are fixed by standard bond lengths, 1–3 distances are fixed by bond angles through the law of cosines, 1–4 distances are bounded by the full torsion sweep, and all longer distances are bounded below by van der Waals radii. The bounds are then tightened by the triangle inequality, which is equivalent to an all-pairs shortest-paths computation and also detects inconsistent bounds. A conformer is generated by sampling one distance matrix from the smoothed bounds and embedding it in closed form via classical multidimensional scaling: squared distances are turned into a centroid-centered Gram matrix, and the top three eigenvectors give the 3D coordinates. Negative eigenvalues from an unphysical draw are handled by jittering the corresponding axis or resampling. The embedded coordinates are refined against distance and chirality violations, often in four dimensions first so that wrong-handed structures can untangle before the extra dimension is squeezed away.

What makes ETKDG different is the short post-embedding minimization that injects experimental knowledge directly. Instead of relying on a full generic force field, the method adds two small targeted terms. The first is an experimental-torsion potential derived from Cambridge Structural Database histograms: each rotatable-bond motif, identified by a SMARTS pattern, is assigned a sixfold cosine series whose minima align with the peaks of the observed experimental distribution. Because these wells come from crystallography rather than a transferable force field, conformers naturally fall into the basins real molecules occupy. The second is basic-knowledge planarity and linearity enforcement: improper torsion terms keep sp2 centers and small all-sp2 rings flat, and triple bonds are kept linear. The minimization runs for only a few hundred steps, so it is cheap, preserves the diversity created by different random seeds, and usually removes the need for a final MMFF/UFF cleanup.

```python
from rdkit import Chem
from rdkit.Chem import AllChem


def make_embedding_parameters(seed=0xF00D):
    params = AllChem.ETKDG()
    params.randomSeed = seed
    params.enforceChirality = True
    params.randNegEig = True
    params.numZeroFail = 1
    params.optimizerForceTol = 1e-3
    # Enable experimental torsion preferences and basic planarity/linearity rules.
    params.useExpTorsionAnglePrefs = True
    params.useBasicKnowledge = True
    return params


def embed_ensemble_from_graph(smiles, n_confs=50, seed=0xF00D,
                              do_forcefield_cleanup=False):
    mol = Chem.AddHs(Chem.MolFromSmiles(smiles))
    params = make_embedding_parameters(seed)
    cids = list(AllChem.EmbedMultipleConfs(mol, numConfs=n_confs,
                                           params=params))
    if do_forcefield_cleanup:
        AllChem.MMFFOptimizeMoleculeConfs(mol)
    return mol, cids


# Example: generate 50 conformers for a flexible drug-like molecule.
mol, conf_ids = embed_ensemble_from_graph(
    "CN1CCC(O)(CC1)c1ccccc1Cl",
    n_confs=50
)
print(f"Generated {len(conf_ids)} conformers.")
```

Under the hood, the ETKDG parameter object tells the embedder to build the bounds matrix, triangle-smooth it, sample and embed one distance matrix per seed, clean up distance and chirality violations, and finally minimize the experimental-torsion and basic-knowledge terms. The result is a fast, graph-to-ensemble generator that respects real molecular geometry without sacrificing conformational diversity.
