  22         Abstract. Multi-echelon inventory optimization (MEIO) plays a key role in a supply chain seeking to
  23
  24         achieve specified customer service levels with a minimum capital in inventory. This is especially true
  25         in real supply chains, which face supply and demand uncertainty. In this work, we propose a generalized
  26
  27         MEIO model based on the Guaranteed Service approach to allocate safety stock levels across the
  28
  29         network at the lowest holding cost. This model simultaneously accounts for several features that are
  30         usually present in real multi-echelon supply chains: review periods, manufacturing facilities, hybrid
  31
  32         nodes (nodes with both internal and external demand), minimum order quantities (MOQ), and different
  33
  34         service level performance indicators (fill rate and cycle service levels). We propose an integrated
  35         nonlinear programming model to support decision making, which can be reformulated as                    a
  36
  37         Quadratically Constrained Program (QCP), which leads to order of magnitude reductions in
  38
  39         computational time. The performance of the model is evaluated by solving illustrative and real-world
  40         cases, and is validated with simulation.

  43               1. INTRODUCTION
  44
  45                 On-time fulfilment of customer demand is critical in today’s customer-centric supply chains.
  46
  47         Achieving this goal depends in great part on the inventory levels and policies that are set along a supply
  48
  49         chain. However, efficient inventory control is particularly challenging when customer demand is
  50         uncertain and retailers may not know the exact size of an order in advance. Other sources of uncertainty
  51
  52         may increase the problem complexity, such as lead time variability. Moreover, the decision at one stage
  53
  54         impacts inventory decisions at other stages. To overcome these challenges, having a safety stock serves
  55         to mitigate the risk of stock-outs in the system. The purpose of multi-echelon inventory optimization
  56
  57         (MEIO) is to allocate safety stocks to meet customer service levels, while minimizing the total capital
  58
  59         tied up in inventory throughout the supply chain, in contrast to single-echelon inventory optimization
  60
  61
  62
                     1
  63
  64
  65
     (SEIO), which seeks to independently minimize cost at each echelon. MEIO has enabled companies to
 1
 2   reduce their inventories up to 30% and improve item availability up to 5% by supporting supply chain
 3   segmentation, and providing a better balance between lead time, inventory and service under
 4
 5   uncertainty (Payne, 2016). From an optimization perspective, making decisions about inventory in
 6
 7   multi-echelon systems is a challenging task because the objective functions usually involve non-
 8   linearities, and decision variables affect more than one echelon.
 9
10         MEIO approaches have been studied in the literature for allocating safety stock in supply chains.
11
12   The intent of safety stock allocation is to determine an overall strategy for deploying inventory levels
13   across the supply chain in order to buffer it against sources of uncertainty (Graves & Willems, 2003).
14
15   De Kok et al. (2018) present a general typology and review stochastic MEIO models in which they
16
17   classify the extensive research on multi-echelon inventory management under model assumptions,
18   research goals, and different applied methodologies. They state that multi-echelon inventory systems
19
20   are still a very active area of research because of their complexity and practical relevance. More
21
22   recently, Gonçalves et al. (2020) present a systematic literature review describing the history and trends
23   regarding the safety stock determination from an operations research perspective. They also highlight
24
25   that the number of contributions to MEIO has seen a significant increase from the year 2005 onwards,
26
27   and they list many potential directions and trends for future research.
28         There are two widely known approaches in MEIO to determine safety stock levels: the stochastic-
29
30   service model (SSM) and the guaranteed-service model (GSM), which were introduced by Clark and
31
32   Scarf (1960) and Simpson (1958), respectively. These two approaches differ in terms of general
33   characteristics and provide an interesting contrast on how a supply chain is represented for the purpose
34
35   of setting safety stocks. Detailed comparisons can be found in De Smet et al. (2019), Graves and
36
37   Willems (2003), and Simchi-Levi and Zhao (2012).
38         The objective of this work is to develop a guaranteed-service optimization model to address the
39
40   problem of optimizing multi-echelon supply chain network inventory management for complex
41
42   systems. However, the GSM for multi-echelon supply chains developed in literature does not fully
43
     account for all the issues and characteristics arising in industrial practice. Many authors have developed
44
45   some extensions to the GSM, but to the best of our knowledge, nobody has developed a model that can
46
47   achieve optimum safety stocks on complex supply chains while integrating all the features typical of
48
     industrial environments presented in this work. We integrate them into a single model that enables an
49
50   improved real-world supply chain representation in order to provide support to strategic decision-
51
52   makers.
53
           This paper presents a model for multi-echelon inventory systems of a multi-product supply chain
54
55   with both demand and lead time uncertainty. The novelty in the proposed model is that it combines
56
57   several features. First, demand can occur at any node in the network. This can result in hybrid nodes
58
59
     that have both dependent and independent demands. To the best of our knowledge, these characteristics,
60   which represent the common operation mode of many real multi-echelon systems, has not been
61
62
           2
63
64
65
     addressed before, as most of the literature on supply chain inventory management considers only
 1
 2   external demand at the final nodes of the network. Our proposed formulation also captures risk-pooling
 3   effects by consolidating the safety stock inventory of downstream nodes to the upstream nodes in the
 4
 5   multi-echelon supply chain. Second, manufacturing plants can be placed at any location in the network,
 6
 7   enabling the manufacture of any desired good at those locations. This feature allows generalizing and
 8   managing larger supply chains that have grown in their vertical integration. Capturing wider networks
 9
10   can significantly improve the inventory decision-making process across the process supply chains as is
11
12   seen in those industries that produce both raw materials and finished goods. Third, fill rates can be used
13   as an alternative customer service key performance indicator when setting safety stock levels. Fill rates
14
15   are not considered in the standard GSM, which relies on cycle service levels instead. However, fill rate
16
17   is the most widely applied service level measure in industry (Teunter et al., 2017).. We thus allow the
18   modeler the flexibility of specifying the customer service metric to be used. In order to do this, we
19
20   propose a quadratic regression to estimate the equivalent Cycle Service Level (CSL) when fill rates are
21
22   used in the model as the desired customer service measure. In addition, minimum order quantities
23   (MOQ) for replenishment orders are explicitly modelled. To the best of our knowledge, literature on
24
25   the impact of MOQ on safety stock levels is scarce and mostly focuses on continuous-review inventory
26
27   policies. Depending on the size of the order, using MOQ can cause overshoot in the inventory levels,
28   impacting service levels and costs. This is frequently observed in almost all supply chains. Finally, the
29
30   resulting nonconvex Nonlinear Programming (NLP) model is reformulated as a Quadratically
31
32   Constrained Problem (QCP) by exploiting the structure of the constraints of the base model. Several
33   computational examples for illustrative and real industrial systems are presented to illustrate the
34
35   application of the proposed model and its resulting improved computational performance.
36
37         The outline of the paper is as follows. The literature review and background with the basic
38   concepts of the GSM are presented in the following subsection. The problem statement is given in
39
40   Section 2, followed by the model formulation in Section 3. Section 4 details the application of the model
41
42   on illustrative and real-world case studies. We conclude this article in Section 5. A Nomenclature
43
     section is presented at the end to facilitate the model understanding. A Supporting Information Section
44
45   is included to provide the data input used in the real case study and detail additional discoveries relating
46
47   to the impact of MOQ on service metrics.

         1.1 Literature and Background of the Guaranteed-service Model
51
52         The present paper relies on the GSM to optimize safety stocks. Although this approach was
53   developed more than 50 years ago, 80% of the existing works on this topic have been published in the
54
55   last 2 decades (Eruguz et al., 2016). The first multi-echelon serial system for the GSM model was
56
57   proposed by Simpson (1958), and then it was extended to deal with different network topologies
58
     (Graves & Willems, 2000; Inderfurth, 1993; Inderfurth & Minner, 1998; Minner, 1998). Later,
59
60   Magnanti et al. (2006) developed a guaranteed-service approach for general acyclic networks. The main
61
62
           3
63
64
65
     idea of the classic guaranteed service approach is that if the customer places an order of size dj(t) on
 1
 2   node j at time t, this order will be fulfilled by time t + Sj (Graves & Willems, 2000), with Sj being the
 3   guaranteed-service time of node j. Moreover, each node j receives a service commitment from its
 4
 5   upstream node i ∈ J, called inbound service time SIj (SIj = Si), and has an order processing time or lead
 6
 7   time of LTj. This lead time represents the time until the outputs are available to serve the demand,
 8   including material handling and transportation times (You & Grossmann, 2009). Both SIj and LTj are
 9
10   times that must be taken into account to define Sj. The Net Lead Time (NLT) is a concept that links
11
12   them and represents the period of exposure that is not covered within the guaranteed service time and
13   must be covered with safety stock. The NLT for node j is defined as NLTj = SIj + LTj - Sj. Figure 1
14
15   displays examples for different values of Sj. The first example (1), also called “pull” or “full delay”
16
17   scenario, is the case where node j promises to its customer a guaranteed service time equal to the worst-
18   case replenishment time (Sj = SIj + LTj). This node places an order to its predecessor every time it
19
20   receives an order from its customer, then it waits for the upstream node to process its order before
21
22   processing the order without storing any inventory. In this case, NLTj = 0. On the other hand, if the
23   customer bounds the maximum possible service time (Sj ≤ maxSj) and this maximum is less than the
24
25   worst-case replenishment time (maxSj ≤ SIj + LTj), node j should satisfy customer demand in less time
26
27   that the required to place an order on the supplier and process it. Therefore, NLTj > 0, meaning that
28   there is a period of time that should be covered with safety stock, as shown in cases (2) and (3) in Figure
29

36
37         A key assumption of the basic GSM is that demand is bounded. If demand in a certain period
38   exceeds the bound, it is assumed that other extraordinary measures such as overtime production are
39
40   used to satisfy excess demand. Moreover, it is assumed that each stage of the supply chain operates
41
42   under a periodic review inventory policy with a base-stock level, with the review period being common
43   for all echelons. The demand is independent and identically distributed at each demand node, following
44
45   a normal distribution. Lead times are constant, and independent demand only occurs at final nodes in
46
47   the network. In addition, the service times at the initial and the final nodes are inputs. Finally, each plant
48
     has a coefficient that represents the bill of materials for product transformation and depends on location-
49
50   location relationships (network arcs).
51
52         Over the years, many authors have worked on extending the original GSM assumptions to enable
53
     real-world supply chain characteristics to be captured, as presented in the survey by Eruguz et al. (2016).
54
55   The authors in this survey summarize several extensions made to the basic model of Graves and
56
57   Willems (2000). The main assumptions that were relaxed are related to the external demand, lead time
58
     variability, capacity constraints, service time customization, alternative replenishment policies, review
59
60   periods, and extraordinary measures. Moreover, other authors have presented works about integrating
61
62
           5
63
64
65
     the classic GSM with other activities or approaches. You and Grossmann (2008) develop models and
 1
 2   algorithms that simultaneously consider inventory optimization and supply chain network design under
 3   demand uncertainty. In a subsequent work, these authors present an integrated multi-echelon supply
 4
 5   chain design and inventory management model under uncertainty using the GSM (You & Grossmann,
 6
 7   2009). Klosterhalfen et al. (2013) propose an integrated hybrid guaranteed-service and stochastic-
 8   service approach for inventory optimization, that allows selecting the approach that minimizes costs.
 9
10   Recently, the work by Ghadimi (2020) presents a model for joint optimization of production capacity
11
12   and safety stocks under the GSM approach. Bendadou et al. (2021) analyze the impact of merging
13   activities in a supply chain under the GSM.
14
15         In addition to the extensions mentioned in (Eruguz et al., 2016), the inclusion of MOQ and fill
16
17   rate as a service level measure have significant importance for representing real-world supply chain
18   dynamics and must be accounted for. Chopra and Meindl (2013) define the Cycle Service Level (CSL)
19
20   as the fraction of replenishment cycles that end with all the customer demand being met, where the
21
22   replenishment cycle is the interval between two successive replenishment deliveries. In other words, it
23   is the probability that an order is fulfilled “on time in full” (OTIF). On the other hand, the product fill
24
25   rate (fr) is the fraction of product demand that is fulfilled on time from the product in inventory. The
26
27   latter one is known as the most widely applied service level measure in industry (Teunter et al., 2017).
28   However, CSL is the measure required by the GSM. Chopra and Meindl (2013) describe how to
29
30   introduce the fill rate given a continuous review inventory policy with a formula that links both
31
32   indicators to obtain the equivalent CSL. They also describe how a large MOQ yields larger fill rates.
33   Silver and Bischak (2011) present an exact fill rate in a periodic review base stock system under
34
35   normally distributed demand, and they state that the fill rate depends on four parameters, safety factor,
36
37   coefficient of variation, review period, and lead time, but not on the minimum order quantity. Other
38   works (Park et al., 2018; Shen et al., 2019; Zhou et al., 2007; Zhu et al., 2015) focus on the impact of
39
40   the MOQ on inventory control rather than on safety inventory allocation. De Smet et al. (2019) combine
41
42   stochastic lead times with batching decisions for a distribution network based on the work of Humair
43
     et al. (2013) and calculate fill rates with an iterative procedure. More recently, Peeters (2020) accounts
44
45   for MOQ to set safety stock levels and review periods integrated with stochastic lead times, based on
46
47   the approach proposed by Humair et al. (2013), and using the Cycle Service Level (CSL) as a customer
48
     service measure. The present work is based on another approach proposed by Inderfurth (1993) to
49
50   integrating stochastic lead times, and is flexible in the sense that it enables specifying either CSL or fill
51
52   rates for each material at each location.

55       2. PROBLEM STATEMENT
56
57         Given a supply chain with a fixed design for a set of materials p ∈ P that can be either raw
58
     materials or finished goods. The locations j ∈ J belong to a set of plants, distribution centers, and
59
60   retailers that can store different materials. Stock holding costs are incurred at all nodes; their unit costs
61
62
           6
63
64
65
     are given. We assume uncertain demand and lead times. The objective is to determine the guaranteed-
 1
 2   service times for each material at each location, and consequently how much safety stock to maintain
 3   at each location to minimize the total holding costs and satisfy a specified customer service level. The
 4
 5   guaranteed-service time is a variable that affects more than one location, as discussed in the background
 6
 7   section (see Figure 1). Thus, the entire multi-echelon system must be considered for optimal decision-
 8   making.
 9
10          Unlike most literature on the topic, this work does not assume there is a final customer demand
11
12   zone. One the other hand, we assume that external or independent demand for any material p can be
13   placed at any node j in the network. Each node j can have an uncorrelated independent normally
14
15   distributed demand of material p with mean µIjp and variance σIjp. In addition, location j has an internal
16
17   or dependent demand if it is required to satisfy replenishment orders from downstream nodes, which is
18   normally distributed with mean µDjp and variance σDjp. Demand is propagated upstream considering the
19
20   risk pooling assumptions described in You and Grossmann (2009). A node that satisfies both dependent
21
22   and independent demands is called a hybrid node, and an example of it is presented in Figure 3. . If a
23   node is a hybrid one, demand is propagated upstream by pooling independent and dependent demand
24
25   separately.
26
27          Regarding the network topology, we assume divergent networks, as shown in Figure 3. In other
28   words, a node that holds a material p can only receive this material from a single node and can distribute
29
30   it to one or more locations, as is usual in finished goods supply chains. The same node can be supplied
31
32   of another material q ∈ P from another location, but this last one should be the only supplier of q for
33   that node. The route that each material follows, as well as the lead time distributions between two
34
35   connected nodes, are given. Lead times are assumed to follow an uncorrelated independent normal
36
37   distribution with mean µLTjp and standard deviation σLTjp. They represent the delay that is under the
38   responsibility of node j, including transportation, material handling, and other processing times until
39
40   the material is ready to be shipped (i.e., is fulfilled).

24

26

29
30   the modeler can also ask for a fill rate to be considered as a target service measure.

34
35         The multi-echelon safety inventory optimization problem can be formulated as a nonlinear
36
37   program (NLP) that deals with the safety inventory planning in a given supply chain. The model
38   proposed in Graves and Willems (2000) is used as a basis, and all sets, parameters, and variables of this
39
40   model are presented in the Nomenclature section. First, we assume that external demand is propagated
41
42   upstream to define internal demands through the complete network. If there are stages with more than
43   one successor, we require a decision on how to combine the demand bounds for the downstream stages
44
45   to obtain a relevant demand bound for the upstream stage to position the safety stock (Graves &
46
47   Willems, 2000). There will be a relative reduction in variability as we combine demand streams due to
48   risk pooling. Therefore, the dependent demand for material p at node j is obtained by converting the
49
50   demand for all materials q that require p as an input at all successor nodes k. The conversion is done via
51
52   the bill of materials ϕpq as a pre-processing step. The total demand is then the sum of the independent
53   and dependent demands as shown in Equations (1)-(2). Note that the second term on both equations
54
55   correspond to the dependent demand mean and deviation, that is µDjp and σDjp. For nodes where material
56

13       5. VALIDATION THROUGH SIMULATION
14
15         All results obtained from the developed model are validated using simulation. The aim is to
16
17   evaluate if safety stocks can meet expected customer service levels (CSL and/or fill rate). This is done
18
19   using an open-sourced discrete-time inventory simulation software package written in the Julia
20
     language: InventoryManagement.jl (Perez, 2021). This simulator allows modeling multiproduct supply
21
22   networks of any topology (e.g., serial, divergent, convergent, tree, or general). Each of the features
23
24   included in the extended GSM model can be simulated using this software: hybrid nodes, MOQ, bill of
25
     materials, stochastic demand, and stochastic lead times. The software allows evaluating any static
26
27   (continuous or periodic review) and dynamic inventory control policy. For greater clarity, the validation
28
29   of the illustrative example is presented with two extra scenarios to analyze how some features affect the
30
     system behavior in the simulation.
31
32         Demand and lead times are randomly generated using normal distributions for each period.
33
34   Basestock levels are calculated following Equation (21). Note that the safety stock level SSjp includes
35
36
     both safety stocks to satisfy dependent and independent demands at every location. The two following
37   terms refer to the previously mentioned expected demands at each location during the Net Lead Time.
38
39    𝐵𝑗𝑝 = 𝑆𝑆𝑗𝑝 + 𝜇𝐷𝑗𝑝 (𝑆𝐼𝑗𝑝 − 𝑆𝑗𝑝 + ⌈𝑙𝑡𝑗𝑝 + 𝑧 𝜎𝐿𝑇𝑗𝑝 ⌉ + 𝑟𝑗𝑝 − 1) + 𝜇𝐼𝑗𝑝 (𝑆𝐼𝑗𝑝 − 𝑆𝑗𝑝 + ⌈𝑙𝑡𝑗𝑝 ⌉ + 𝑟𝑗𝑝 )
40
                                                                                                                (21)
41                                                                                 ∀ 𝑗 ∈ (𝐽𝑝 − 𝐽𝑝𝐼𝐷 ), 𝑝 ∈ 𝑃𝑗
42
43          In each period, demand is generated, orders are placed and delivered, and inventory levels are
44
45   updated. During each review period, an order is placed if the inventory position of a material on a
46   location is below the basestock level. The information is known at all levels but the management policy
47
48   is decentralized: each location asks the amount they need to reach the basestock level, no matter how
49
50   much the upstream node has on stock. If the available inventory is not able to meet demand, lost sales
51   are considered (the extraordinary measures that are referred to in the GSM approach are ignored in the
52
53   simulation). In addition, there is no delay in ordering, so all echelons place orders simultaneously. The
54
55   period selected in this case is one day with 7,000 days (1,000 weeks) in each run, so as to simulate the
56   stationary state at each location. Demand and review periods have a weekly basis. The sequence of
57
58   steps in the simulation of each period is the following:
59
60
61
62
           20
63
64
65
         1. External demand is observed and discounted at each node with independent demand.
 1
 2           Unfulfilled demands are marked as lost sales.
 3       2. If the review period has been meet for a material, internal replenishment and lead times ltjp are
 4
 5           observed. Orders start being processed immediately with no delay (processing time includes
 6
 7           material handling, order preparation, shipments and/or manufacture). Internal demand is
 8           discounted at each node. Unfulfilled replenishment orders are marked as lost sales.
 9
10       3. Stocks are updated with the replenishment orders that arrive at each node (placed ltjp periods
11
12           before).

17       6. CONCLUSIONS
18
19         In this paper, we have presented an NLP optimization model based on the guaranteed-service
20
21   approach model that determines the optimal safety stock allocation in multi-echelon divergent
22   networks. The proposed model integrates several features commonly found in industrial practice that
23
24   have a strong impact on inventory levels. Therefore, the proposed tool is able to accurately represent
25
26   real systems, and to set tight safety stock levels in order to achieve target customer service levels with
27   a minimum capital in inventory. Real-world examples from the pharmaceutical industry are presented
28
29   to illustrate the applicability of the proposed formulation. To solve the model efficiently, a QCP
30
31   reformulation is proposed by exploiting the mathematical structure of the constraints. The QCP
32   outperforms the NLP formulation by allowing the use of QCP solvers, which leads to order of
33
34   magnitude reductions in computational time. Optimal solutions can be found with small computational
35
36   expense for medium/large scale problems (less than 5 seconds). To the best of our knowledge, this is
37   the first model that brings together multiple features typical of industrial practice, such as MOQs, hybrid
38
39   nodes, and alternative service level measures to determine safety stock levels. It is also the first model
40
41   to introduce the QCP reformulation to improve the computational efficiency of the optimization. The
42
     simulation of the results demonstrates that the model is valid for achieving target service levels.
43
44         Future work will address an extension of the present formulation for cases of non-normal
45
46   demand, and a pre-processing procedure of input data in order to decide which mathematical
47
     formulation is appropriate to optimally determine safety stock levels. The effects of CV and MOQ on
48
49   CSL estimation can also be analyzed to review other potential safety stocks reductions. This research
50
51   can also be extended by including responsive characteristics to account for supply chain disruptions and
52
     by including storage capacity limitations. Another important extension is on constrained capacity on
53
54   nodes.
