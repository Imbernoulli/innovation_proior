Let me get the question exactly right before I reach for anything. Ramsey's theorem tells me that if I take a graph on enough vertices — call the threshold $f(k,k)$ — then no matter how the edges fall, I am forced to find either a clique of size $k$ or an independent set of size $k$. Order is unavoidable in the large. Fine. But the theorem is a pure existence statement about the threshold; it never tells me how big $f(k,k)$ is. And the size is the whole game.

There are two sides to "how big". One side asks: how *soon* is order forced? That side I understand well, because Erdős and Szekeres handled it a dozen years ago. Fix any vertex $v$ in a red/blue colored $K_m$, and set $m=f(k-1,l)+f(k,l-1)$. If the red-neighborhood of $v$ has fewer than $f(k-1,l)$ vertices and the blue-neighborhood has fewer than $f(k,l-1)$ vertices, then the two neighborhoods together have at most $m-2$ vertices. But they must contain all $m-1$ other vertices. So one side is large enough to recurse: in the red-neighborhood I either find a red $K_{k-1}$, which with $v$ becomes a red $K_k$, or I find a blue $K_l$ already; in the blue-neighborhood I either find a red $K_k$ already, or a blue $K_{l-1}$, which with $v$ becomes a blue $K_l$. This gives the recursion
$$f(k,l)\le f(k-1,l)+f(k,l-1),$$
which with the trivial boundary unrolls into $f(k,l)\le\binom{k+l-2}{k-1}$, so $f(k,k)\le\binom{2k-2}{k-1}<4^{k-1}$. That argument is constructive in spirit: follow a vertex, split by color, induct. It pins order from above; by the time the vertex count reaches that binomial scale, order is inevitable.

The other side is the one I'm stuck on. How *long* can order be *avoided*? To say $f(k,k)>n$ I have to produce — or at least guarantee — a single graph on $n$ vertices that has *no* $K_k$ and *no* independent set of size $k$ at the same time. A two-coloring of $K_n$ with no monochromatic $K_k$ in either color. And here the natural instinct, the same instinct that worked above, betrays me: I want to *build* one. Take some structured graph, maybe an algebraic one, and verify it dodges every $k$-clique and every $k$-independent-set. I sit with small cases. For $k=3$ I can use a 5-cycle to stay under $f(3,3)=6$. But push $k$ up and I have nothing. Every explicit graph I can write down gives out embarrassingly early — far below the $4^{k-1}$ upper scale, far below anything I'd be proud of. There's no candidate family that scales. The construction route has stalled completely, and not because I'm not clever enough this afternoon: nobody has an explicit order-free graph that grows well.

So let me stop banging on the door marked "construct one." I keep failing to exhibit a single good graph. But what do I actually *need*? I need to know one *exists*. Existence and exhibition are not the same thing, and I've been conflating them. If I could somehow argue that good graphs are not rare — that they can't *all* be absent — I'd be done, and I'd never have to name one.

Here's the reframe. There are only finitely many graphs on $n$ labeled vertices — exactly $2^{\binom{n}{2}}$ of them, one for each way of deciding present/absent on the $\binom{n}{2}$ possible edges. That's a finite universe. A graph is "bad" if it contains a $K_k$ or its complement contains one (i.e. it has an independent $k$-set). If I can show the bad graphs are a *minority* of this universe — fewer than all of them — then a good graph survives, purely by cardinality, and I never touch it.

Let me just count. How many of the $2^{\binom{n}{2}}$ labeled graphs contain a clique on one *particular*, fixed set $R$ of $k$ vertices? Those $\binom{k}{2}$ edges inside $R$ are forced to be present; the remaining $\binom{n}{2}-\binom{k}{2}$ edges are free. So the count is $2^{\binom{n}{2}-\binom{k}{2}} = 2^{\binom{n}{2}}/2^{\binom{k}{2}}$. A clean fraction: a $1/2^{\binom{k}{2}}$ slice of the universe has a clique on that specific $R$.

Now I want the graphs that contain a clique on *some* $k$-set. There are $\binom{n}{k}$ choices of $R$, and a graph might be counted under several of them — but I only need an *upper* bound on how many bad graphs there are, so over-counting is harmless; I just sum. Let $C_{n,k}$ be the number of graphs on $n$ vertices containing at least one $K_k$. Then
$$C_{n,k} \le \binom{n}{k}\,\frac{2^{\binom{n}{2}}}{2^{\binom{k}{2}}} < \frac{n^k}{k!}\,\frac{2^{\binom{n}{2}}}{2^{\binom{k}{2}}},$$
using $\binom{n}{k}<n^k/k!$. I want this to be less than *half* the universe, $\tfrac12\,2^{\binom{n}{2}}$, because then the clique-containing graphs are under half and — by the red/blue symmetry I'll use in a moment — the independent-set-containing graphs are also under half, and two things each under half can't cover everything. So I need
$$\frac{n^k}{k!}\,\frac{1}{2^{\binom{k}{2}}} < \frac12,\qquad\text{i.e.}\qquad 2n^k < k!\,2^{\binom{k}{2}} = k!\,2^{k(k-1)/2}.$$

Where does that bite? The right side has $2^{k(k-1)/2}=2^{k^2/2-k/2}$. The dominant growth is $2^{k^2/2}=(2^{k/2})^k$. The left side is $2n^k$. So if I take $n\le 2^{k/2}$, then $n^k\le 2^{k^2/2}$, and what remains is the comparison of $2$ against $k!\,2^{-k/2}$. For $k\ge 3$, $k!\,2^{-k/2}$ comfortably beats $2$ — at $k=3$, $3!\,2^{-3/2}=6/2.83\approx2.12>2$, and the quantity only grows from there. So $2n^k<k!\,2^{k(k-1)/2}$ holds for every integer $n\le 2^{k/2}$ once $k\ge 3$. Let me take $n=\lfloor 2^{k/2}\rfloor$.

Wait — I should double check I haven't fooled myself at the threshold. Take $n=\lfloor 2^{k/2}\rfloor$ and compute $\binom{n}{k}/2^{\binom{k}{2}}$ honestly for a couple of $k$. At $k=4$: $n=4$, $\binom44=1$, $2^{\binom42}=2^6=64$, ratio $1/64$ — way under $1/2$, even under $1$ with the factor-of-two doubling for both colors. At $k=5$: $n=5$, $\binom55=1$, $2^{10}=1024$, ratio $\approx10^{-3}$. Good, the inequality is not delicate; it has room to spare. The crude $n^k/k!$ bound was wasteful and it still worked, which is exactly the kind of slack I want.

So: fewer than half of all labeled $n$-vertex graphs contain a $K_k$. Now the symmetry. The complement map $G\mapsto G'$ (present $\leftrightarrow$ absent on every edge) is a bijection of my finite universe onto itself, and a $K_k$ in $G'$ is precisely an independent $k$-set in $G$. So the number of graphs whose *complement* contains a $K_k$ equals the number whose complement... no, more directly: the number of graphs with an independent $k$-set equals the number with a $k$-clique, by relabeling through the complement bijection — it's the same count $C_{n,k}$. Both are under $\tfrac12\,2^{\binom{n}{2}}$. Their union is therefore strictly under $2^{\binom{n}{2}}$, the whole universe. So some graph lies in neither bad set: it has no $K_k$ and no independent $k$-set. That graph certifies
$$f(k,k)>\lfloor 2^{k/2}\rfloor.$$

I never built it. I don't know which of the $2^{\binom{n}{2}}$ graphs it is. I proved it's there by showing the bad ones can't fill the room. Since $f(k,k)$ is an integer, $f(k,k)>\lfloor 2^{k/2}\rfloor$ is the usual $f(k,k)>2^{k/2}$ lower-bound form. And look at what fell out: a *lower* bound $2^{k/2}=(\sqrt2)^k$ to set against the upper scale $4^{k-1}$. The base of the exponential is now trapped between $\sqrt2$ and $4$. The two sides come from opposite styles — Erdős–Szekeres *forces* order by induction from above; this *avoids* order by a cardinality count from below — and neither pins the base. I'd love a formula for $f(k,l)$. I can't give one. But I've turned the lower side from "no idea" into $(\sqrt2)^k$, and I did it by refusing to construct.

Let me restate the engine to myself, because I think it's bigger than this one bound. I had a finite set of structures and a property I wanted realized. I put the uniform distribution on the structures — every labeled graph equally likely is the same as my counting, since a fraction *is* a probability — and I showed the "bad" event has probability below $1$. Concretely, picking each edge red or blue by an independent fair coin, the probability that a fixed $k$-set $R$ is monochromatic is $2\cdot 2^{-\binom k2}=2^{1-\binom k2}$ (two ways, all-red or all-blue, out of $2^{\binom k2}$), and
$$\Pr[\text{some monochromatic }K_k]\le \sum_{R}\Pr[R\text{ mono}]=\binom nk 2^{1-\binom k2}.$$
When that sum is $<1$, the bad event misses positive probability, so a *good* coloring occurs with positive probability — hence exists. This is just the union bound, the same sub-additivity I used as over-counting, now wearing probabilistic clothes. The probability isn't strictly necessary here — it's literally a count over a finite space — but the *language* is the point: "a property holds with positive probability, therefore some object has it." That sentence is the method. And I notice the threshold $\binom nk 2^{1-\binom k2}<1$ is slightly more generous than my "under a half each" bookkeeping; chasing the largest asymptotic $n$ with $\binom nk 2^{1-\binom k2}<1$ and using $k!^{1/k}\sim k/e$ and $2^{(\binom k2-1)/k}\sim 2^{k/2}/\sqrt2$ gives $n\sim \tfrac{1}{e\sqrt2}(1+o(1))\,k\,2^{k/2}$ — already a factor $k$ better than the basic $2^{k/2}$, just from being careful with the binomial.

Now I'm bothered by something, and the irritation is productive. The union bound throws away information exactly in the interesting regime. It demands $\sum_R\Pr[R\text{ mono}]<1$ — it insists the *expected number* of monochromatic $k$-sets be below one, so that I can hope for zero of them. But suppose I'm a little past that, where the expected number of bad sets is small but not below one — say it's $m$. The union bound gives up and says nothing. Yet a graph with only a handful of monochromatic $k$-sets is *almost* what I want. Why throw it away? Why insist on zero blemishes when I could tolerate a few and then *remove* them?

Let me make that precise. Let $X$ be the number of monochromatic $k$-sets in a random 2-coloring of $K_n$. By linearity of expectation — and I want to stress this needs no independence, it's just that expectation of a sum is the sum of expectations —
$$\mathbb E[X]=\sum_{R}\Pr[R\text{ mono}]=\binom nk 2^{1-\binom k2}=:m.$$
So there *exists* a coloring with $X\le m$ (something is at most its average). Fix that coloring. It has at most $m$ monochromatic $k$-sets. Now alter it: from each monochromatic $k$-set, delete one vertex. I delete at most $m$ vertices — fewer if some vertex sits in several bad sets, which only helps. What remains is a set of $s\ge n-m$ vertices, and on those vertices *every* monochromatic $k$-set has been broken, since each had a vertex removed. So the coloring restricted to these $s$ vertices has no monochromatic $K_k$ at all, and
$$f(k,k)>s\ge n-m=n-\binom nk 2^{1-\binom k2}.$$

This is a different optimization. Before, I pushed $n$ up only until $m<1$; now I push $n$ further and pay the price $m$, trying to maximize $n-m$. Treat $\binom nk\approx n^k/k!$, so $m\approx \tfrac{n^k}{k!}2^{1-\binom k2}$, and maximize $g(n)=n-\tfrac{n^k}{k!}2^{1-\binom k2}$. Set $g'(n)=0$: $1=\tfrac{k\,n^{k-1}}{k!}2^{1-\binom k2}$, i.e. $n^{k-1}=(k-1)!\,2^{\binom k2-1}$. Taking $(k-1)$-th roots, $(k-1)!^{1/(k-1)}\sim (k-1)/e\sim k/e$ and $2^{(\binom k2-1)/(k-1)}=2^{k/2-1/(k-1)}\sim 2^{k/2}$, so the optimal $n\sim \tfrac ke\,2^{k/2}$. At that $n$, the derivative equation makes $m\approx n/k$, a lower-order share of $n$, so $g(n)\sim n$ and I land
$$f(k,k)>\frac1e(1+o(1))\,k\,2^{k/2}.$$
Let me sanity-check the gain. The bare union bound gave $2^{k/2}$; the careful union bound gave $\tfrac{1}{e\sqrt2}k\,2^{k/2}$; tolerating-and-deleting gives $\tfrac1e k\,2^{k/2}$ — a clean factor of $\sqrt2$ over the careful union bound, on top of the factor $k$. The improvement is real and it came from a simple refusal: don't demand a pristine random object, accept a slightly flawed one and surgically fix it.

Now I want to follow that "something is at most/at least its average" lever on its own, away from Ramsey, because I suspect it's a general existence tool and I want to see how little it needs. The leanest possible version: I have a quantity $X$ I want to make large (or small); I compute its average over a random object; some object beats the average. No clique-counting, no union bound — just one expectation.

Take tournaments. An orientation of $K_n$: for each pair, exactly one of $x\to y$, $y\to x$. I want a tournament with many Hamiltonian paths — directed paths visiting every vertex once. Building one to maximize Hamiltonian paths sounds hard. So I won't. Orient each edge by an independent fair coin; that makes all $2^{\binom n2}$ tournaments equally likely. Let $X$ be the number of Hamiltonian paths. For a *fixed* ordering $\sigma$ of the $n$ players, $\sigma$ is a directed Hamiltonian path iff its $n-1$ consecutive arcs all point forward — each forward with probability $1/2$, and these $n-1$ arcs are distinct edges, so the probability is $2^{-(n-1)}$. There are $n!$ orderings, so by linearity again,
$$\mathbb E[X]=\sum_\sigma \Pr[\sigma\text{ is a directed Ham path}]=n!\,2^{-(n-1)}.$$
The indicators for different orderings are wildly dependent — overlapping orderings share arcs — but linearity does not care; the sum of expectations is the expectation of the sum regardless. Some tournament achieves at least the average, so there is a tournament with at least $n!\,2^{-(n-1)}$ Hamiltonian paths. One expectation, one "$\ge$ average," done — and no tournament was ever constructed. This is the existence engine in its barest form.

One more, because it's the prototype of a whole family. Given a graph $G$ with $e$ edges, I want a large bipartite subgraph — split the vertices into two sides so that many edges cross. Maximizing the cut is a hard combinatorial problem; ignore that and just flip a fair coin per vertex to assign it to side $T$ or side $B$. An edge $\{x,y\}$ crosses iff its two endpoints land on opposite sides, which happens with probability $1/2$ (the two coin flips disagree half the time). Let $X$ be the number of crossing edges. By linearity over the $e$ edge-indicators — again no independence among them needed, edges sharing a vertex are correlated and it's irrelevant —
$$\mathbb E[X]=\sum_{\{x,y\}\in E}\Pr[\{x,y\}\text{ crosses}]=\frac e2.$$
So some split has at least $e/2$ crossing edges: every graph has a bipartite subgraph with at least half its edges. The crossing edges of that split form the bipartite subgraph I wanted. And this is exactly the satisfy-half-the-constraints archetype — read each edge as a clause asking its endpoints to differ, and I've satisfied at least half; the same fair-coin-plus-linearity argument gives, for $m$ clauses each falsified with probability at most $1/2$ under a random assignment, an assignment satisfying at least $m/2$ of them. The expectation has a witness; the witness is what I wanted.

So here is the whole through-line, as one causal chain. I wanted to lower-bound the Ramsey number, which means proving an order-free graph *exists*; direct construction stalls and there's no good explicit family. I stopped trying to build one and counted instead: in the finite universe of labeled graphs, the fraction containing a fixed $K_k$ is $2^{-\binom k2}$, summing over the $\binom nk$ choices keeps the clique-containing graphs under half once $2n^k<k!\,2^{k(k-1)/2}$, which holds for $n\le 2^{k/2}$, $k\ge3$; the complement bijection puts the independent-set-containing graphs under half too, so a graph escaping both must exist, giving $f(k,k)>\lfloor 2^{k/2}\rfloor$, hence the usual $f(k,k)>2^{k/2}$ form. Recast as "a random coloring is monochromatic-free with positive probability," this is the union bound, and being careful with the binomial sharpens it to $\tfrac1{e\sqrt2}(1+o(1))k2^{k/2}$. Noticing the union bound wastes the just-past-threshold regime, I let $X$ count the monochromatic sets, used linearity to get $\mathbb E[X]=\binom nk 2^{1-\binom k2}$, took a coloring at most its average, and deleted one vertex per bad set, pushing the bound to $\tfrac1e(1+o(1))k\,2^{k/2}$. And stripping that down to a single expectation with one "beats-the-average" step recovers, with no independence assumed, that some tournament has $\ge n!\,2^{-(n-1)}$ Hamiltonian paths and every $e$-edge graph has a cut of $\ge e/2$ edges. The principle is simple: put a distribution on candidates and show the desired event has positive probability, or compute an average and take a point at least or at most that average; when the random object is only nearly good, delete its blemishes.

I can now leave the clean statements in the form the argument has earned. If
$$\binom nk 2^{1-\binom k2}<1,$$
then a red/blue coloring of $K_n$ with no monochromatic $K_k$ exists, so $R(k,k)>n$. For a fixed $k$-set the monochromatic probability is exactly $2^{1-\binom k2}$, and the union bound over the $\binom nk$ choices gives the displayed condition. Taking $n=\lfloor 2^{k/2}\rfloor$ gives $R(k,k)>\lfloor 2^{k/2}\rfloor$ for every $k\ge3$. In the original labeled-graph count, a fixed $K_k$ is contained in exactly $2^{\binom n2-\binom k2}$ graphs; the number containing some $K_k$ is at most
$$\binom nk2^{\binom n2-\binom k2}<\frac{n^k}{k!}2^{\binom n2-\binom k2},$$
so it is below half of all graphs whenever $2n^k<k!2^{k(k-1)/2}$, and that inequality holds for $n\le2^{k/2}$, $k\ge3$. Complementation gives the same bound for graphs with an independent $k$-set, so the two bad classes cannot cover the whole labeled universe.

I also have the altered Ramsey statement. For every $n$,
$$R(k,k)>n-\binom nk2^{1-\binom k2}.$$
The proof is the expectation-and-deletion argument: choose a coloring with at most $\binom nk2^{1-\binom k2}$ monochromatic $k$-sets, delete one vertex from each such set, and keep the remaining vertices. Optimizing the asymptotic form of the right side gives
$$R(k,k)>\frac1e(1+o(1))k2^{k/2}.$$
The same linearity calculation gives the two basic averaging examples: in a random tournament, each of the $n!$ vertex orderings is a directed Hamiltonian path with probability $2^{-(n-1)}$, so some tournament has at least $n!2^{-(n-1)}$ Hamiltonian paths; in a random two-way vertex split of a graph with $e$ edges, each edge crosses with probability $1/2$, so some split has at least $e/2$ crossing edges.
