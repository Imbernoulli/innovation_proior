I start with the fact that the ordinary checks are almost designed to be fooled. The attacker is not trying to make the classifier bad on normal images; the attacker is trying to make it normal everywhere except on inputs carrying a trigger. So clean validation accuracy tells me very little. Looking at raw pixels is not much better. A patch or a blended pattern may be obvious to the training loss once it is consistently tied to a label, but as a vector displacement inside the space of natural images it can be tiny compared with pose, background, color, and object variation. If I take all images assigned to the target label and look for raw input outliers, the honest variance can easily be the largest thing I see.

So I need to stop thinking of the poison as "far from the target class" in every sense. It may be far only after the network has learned what the trigger means. Within the target training label I have two populations: mostly clean target-class examples and a smaller group that came from somewhere else but now carries the target label because of the trigger. Call them `D` and `W`, with mixture `F=(1-epsilon)D+epsilon W`. Let their means be `mu_D` and `mu_W`, and let `Delta=mu_D-mu_W`. The mixture mean is `mu_F=(1-epsilon)mu_D+epsilon mu_W`, so `mu_D-mu_F=epsilon Delta` and `mu_W-mu_F=-(1-epsilon)Delta`. Those signs matter: the clean mean lies a small distance from the mixture mean, and the poison mean lies the larger opposite distance because the poison is the minority.

Now I compute the covariance about the mixture mean. For clean points, `X-mu_F=(X-mu_D)+epsilon Delta`, so the clean contribution is `Sigma_D+epsilon^2 Delta Delta^T`. For poisoned points, `X-mu_F=(X-mu_W)-(1-epsilon)Delta`, so the poison contribution is `Sigma_W+(1-epsilon)^2 Delta Delta^T`. Mixing the two terms gives `Sigma_F=(1-epsilon)Sigma_D+epsilon Sigma_W+epsilon(1-epsilon)Delta Delta^T`. That last term is the whole opening: a small shifted subpopulation adds a rank-one bump in the direction of the mean gap. If the bump is large relative to the within-population covariance, the top covariance direction should tilt toward `Delta`.

This explains why the input space can fail without killing the idea. In pixels, `Delta` is the trigger's direct displacement plus whatever source-class structure remains, and clean image variance can bury the bump. In a learned representation, the situation can flip. The trigger is a very reliable training cue for the target label, so gradient descent has an incentive to build a feature that responds to it. The representation can make the triggered examples separate much more strongly than the raw pixels do. I do not need to assume the network deliberately builds a clean detector for the trigger; I only need the learned features to enlarge the poison-clean mean gap enough that the covariance bump becomes visible.

The spectral part is straightforward once I am in the right space. Suppose both within-population covariances obey `Sigma_D, Sigma_W <= sigma^2 I`. Let `v` be the top eigenvector of `Sigma_F`. Since the rank-one bump is positive semidefinite, the top eigenvalue is at least `epsilon(1-epsilon)||Delta||^2`. On the other hand, evaluating the top eigenvalue at `v` gives at most `sigma^2+epsilon(1-epsilon)<v,Delta>^2` from the two bounded covariance terms plus the bump. Therefore `<v,Delta>^2 >= ||Delta||^2 - sigma^2/(epsilon(1-epsilon))`. So if the population means are far enough apart, the leading covariance direction is not arbitrary; it has real correlation with the poison-clean direction.

I have to be careful with the threshold argument, because the score I actually want is an absolute or squared projection. A direction correlated with `Delta` is not enough unless the clean and poisoned absolute offsets around the mixture mean have a gap. For a unit direction `u`, write `c=|<u,Delta>|` and orient the line so the clean projected mean around `mu_F` has magnitude `epsilon c`, while the poisoned projected mean has magnitude `(1-epsilon)c` on the other side. If I threshold absolute projection at `t=c/2`, the clean and poison margins are both `(1/2-epsilon)c`. Chebyshev then makes both errors below `epsilon` only when `(1/2-epsilon)c > sigma/sqrt(epsilon)`. That is the honest margin condition for the squared score. It also explains the minority requirement: as `epsilon` approaches `1/2`, the two absolute offsets become nearly equal and the needed separation has to blow up. A constant like `6 sigma^2/epsilon` is a useful low-poison-rate rule of thumb and gives the right qualitative message, but by itself it does not prove every absolute-threshold case all the way up to `epsilon<1/2`. The safe statement is that the mean gap must make `<v,Delta>` exceed `2 sigma / ((1-2epsilon)sqrt(epsilon))`, or equivalently one can impose a stronger `epsilon`-dependent constant on `||Delta||^2`.

Now the algorithm is forced. I should not run one covariance over the whole dataset, because ordinary class means would dominate it. The mixture lives inside a training label: the target-label bag contains clean target examples and relabeled triggered examples. For each training label, I gather the learned representations for examples carrying that label, subtract that label's feature mean, and build a centered matrix `M` with one row per example. The top right singular vector of `M` is the feature-space direction of largest empirical variance, the same as the top eigenvector of `M^T M` up to scaling. I score an example by the squared centered projection onto that direction, `((R(x_i)-Rhat).v)^2`, because that is its contribution to the suspicious variance direction.

The grouping label has to be the training label, not the model's current prediction. The representation direction is fit from the bag the network saw as one class during training. If I regroup by predicted class at scoring time, I can move hard or suspicious examples into the wrong covariance problem and destroy the very mixture I am trying to inspect. The logits can be present in an interface, but they are not the grouping signal.

I also need to over-remove a little. The theory says that if a threshold exists, the largest projected outliers should be mostly poison, but the threshold is not known exactly and finite samples blur the clean and poison score distributions. Leaving a small amount of poison can preserve the backdoor shortcut, while removing a modest number of extra clean points is usually cheaper. Removing the top `1.5` times the suspected poison budget is a practical safety margin, followed by retraining from scratch so the trigger no longer has training support.

There is one implementation wrinkle I should not hide. The clean mathematical score centers before both SVD and scoring, and squares the centered projection. A target-label experiment can instead center for SVD, then score by the absolute projection of the uncentered representation, because it already knows which label to inspect. That is close in spirit but not identical in ranking. In a generic defense object where the target label is not provided, I should run the class-conditional centered SVD for every training label and use the centered squared score; if I am reproducing the target-label experiment exactly, I must say that it uses the target label and the uncentered absolute projection score.
