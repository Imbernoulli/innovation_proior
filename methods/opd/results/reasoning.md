Let me start from what actually hurts. I have a big teacher that reasons well — solves competition math — and a small student I can afford to serve, and I want the student to do what the teacher does. The naive thing is what everyone does: take a pile of the teacher's solutions, or even ground-truth solutions, and fine-tune the student to predict them token by token. Maximum likelihood. And it sort of works and it stalls, and I want to understand precisely *why* it stalls before I reach for anything fancier, because the failure mode is going to tell me what the fix has to be.

So picture the student at inference. It's auto-regressive: it writes token one, then conditions on token one to write token two, and so on. The prefix it conditions on at step `n` is *its own* output `y_<n`. Now picture training: I fed it the teacher's prefixes. At every step during training the prefix `y_<n` was a teacher prefix — a clean, correct, in-distribution chain of reasoning. The student never once practiced continuing from one of *its own* prefixes. Why does that matter? Because the student is smaller; it *will* make an early slip the teacher never makes — a wrong sign, a dropped term in line two of the algebra. The instant it does, it's standing in a state that has zero support in its training data: "given this slightly-wrong partial derivation, what's next?" It has no idea, because it only ever saw teacher-clean prefixes. It improvises, drifts further, and because each token conditions on the last, the drift compounds — by the end of the solution it's far out in a region of sequence space it was never trained on. This is exactly the cascade that behavior cloning suffers in imitation learning, the thing DAgger was built to fix: training on the expert's state distribution, testing on your own. The error isn't in any single token I trained on; it's that I trained on the *wrong distribution of states*. The states I trained on are the teacher's; the states that matter are the student's. That's the first wall, and it's structural: any method that trains on a *fixed* set of sequences — teacher-generated or ground-truth, doesn't matter — is off-policy and inherits this cascade.

There's a second, separate thing wrong, and I want to keep it distinct from the first because they have different fixes. It's about the *objective*, not the *data distribution*. Maximum likelihood on the teacher's tokens is, in the limit of soft targets, minimizing the forward KL, `D_KL(p_T || p_S) = Σ_v p_T(v) log(p_T(v)/p_S(v))`, at each position. Stare at that sum. The weight on each vocabulary token `v` is `p_T(v)` — the *teacher's* probability. Wherever the teacher puts mass, the term `p_T(v) log(p_T(v)/p_S(v))` punishes the student hard for putting *too little* mass there: as `p_S(v) → 0` with `p_T(v) > 0`, the term goes to `+∞`. So forward KL is mass-covering — it forces the student to put probability *everywhere the teacher does*. Now, if the student had the teacher's capacity that would be fine, it could match the whole distribution. But it doesn't. A 0.5B student cannot represent every mode of a 7B math model's next-token distribution. So "cover all the teacher's modes" forces it to spread its limited mass thin — including over the teacher's long-tail, low-probability tokens — and the result at free-run generation is a smeared, hedge-everything distribution that samples incoherent or hallucinated continuations. The mode-covering instinct is *wrong* when the student is the smaller party. I want the opposite instinct: when you can't be everything, *pick one teacher behavior and execute it cleanly*. So both the data and the objective are pushing me in a direction.

Let me think about what the *opposite* objective looks like, because the asymmetry of KL is the whole game here. Forward KL weights by `p_T`. The other direction, reverse KL, weights by `p_S`: `D_KL(p_S || p_T) = Σ_v p_S(v) log(p_S(v)/p_T(v))`. Read what *this* one punishes. The weight is the student's own probability, so the term is large exactly where the student puts mass that the teacher finds unlikely — `p_S(v)` big, `p_T(v)` small makes `log(p_S/p_T)` big and positive. To drive it down the student must *withdraw* mass from any token the teacher doesn't like. It is zero-forcing: it would rather put zero on a region than spread thin to cover it. That's mode-seeking — it concentrates the student onto the teacher's *major* modes and abandons the long tail. Picture fitting a single Gaussian to a two-bump mixture: forward KL parks the Gaussian in the valley between the bumps to "cover" both, which is exactly the incoherent average I'm afraid of; reverse KL snaps onto one bump and commits. For compression under capacity mismatch, committing to one coherent teacher behavior is precisely what I want. So I want reverse KL.

But now I've created a problem for myself. Forward KL was easy to optimize — it's maximum likelihood, the student's own log-prob of given tokens, a clean supervised loss. Reverse KL is `E_{p_S}[log p_S - log p_T]`, an expectation *under the student's own distribution*. The thing I'm differentiating sits inside an expectation over the distribution whose parameters I'm changing. At the sequence level it's worse: I want `D_KL(p_S(·|x) || p_T(·|x)) = E_{y~p_S}[log p_S(y|x) - log p_T(y|x)]`, with `y` a whole solution sampled from the student. That's a sample-based objective over an enormous, model-defined sample space. This is an RL problem. The student is a policy, sampling trajectories `y ~ p_S(·|x)`, and I want to minimize that expectation. Notice this *automatically* solves my first wall: the expectation is over the student's *own* rollouts, so I'm training on exactly the states the student visits at inference. On-policy. The cascade is gone by construction — if the student drifts into a weird state, that weird state now appears in training, with a label. The reverse KL fixes the objective problem *and* its on-policy nature fixes the data-distribution problem. So the target is clear: minimize the sequence-level reverse KL under student rollouts. The only question is how to get a gradient.

The textbook move is the policy gradient theorem. Treat `-(log p_S(y) - log p_T(y))` as the return of trajectory `y`, decompose it per token. Write the per-step "reward" `r_t = log( p_T(y_t|y_<t,x) / p_S(y_t|y_<t,x) )` — high when the teacher likes the student's token and the student wasn't already overconfident in it. The sequence reverse KL is, up to the `x`-expectation, `E_{y~p_S}[ -Σ_t r_t ]`, and the policy gradient comes out as

  ∇L = -E_{y~p_S} Σ_t (R_t - 1) ∇ log p_S(y_t|y_<t,x),   with R_t = Σ_{t'≥t} r_{t'}.

The `R_t` is the reward-to-go: at token `t`, the credit is the *sum of all future per-step rewards* from `t` to the end of the solution. The `-1` is the entropy/normalization piece that falls out of differentiating `log p_S` inside the expectation. So far so standard. And I could just run this — sample student solutions, compute `R_t`, REINFORCE. Let me think about whether it'll actually train, because REINFORCE on long sequences has a reputation.

It's bad, and I can see exactly why before I even run it. `R_t` is a sum over the *future* of a stochastic trajectory — every downstream token I happened to sample feeds into the credit for token `t`. That's a high-variance random quantity; the gradient estimate at each token is multiplied by the noisy sum of everything that came after. Long solutions, more future, more variance. Worse, there are two specific failure modes I can name. One: reward hacking. The "reward" is `log p_T(y_t) - log p_S(y_t)`, and a small student will discover that certain *degenerate* strings — a phrase repeated over and over — get assigned high probability by the teacher (teachers are smooth; repetition is locally probable) while being garbage as reasoning. Nothing in the per-token reward stops it from farming those. Two: a length bias baked right into `R_t`. Each `r_t` for a typical token is roughly `log(p_T/p_S)`, and summed over many tokens `R_t` is *more negative for longer sequences* — so the objective mechanically prefers the student to stop early, to emit short or even empty responses, just to avoid accumulating reward-to-go. So if I take the raw policy gradient I'm signing up for high variance, plus reward hacking, plus a drive toward empty answers. To make it usable I'd have to bolt on stabilizers — a baseline or single-step decomposition for variance, mixing the teacher into the sampling distribution to suppress the degenerate strings (with importance weights to stay unbiased), length normalization to kill the short-answer bias, PPO-style clipping on top. That's a pile of machinery and new hyperparameters, and it drags me away from the simple, stable supervised loop I liked about forward KL. Wall. I have the right objective and the right data, but the obvious estimator for it is a mess.

Let me back up and look harder at *where* the mess comes from, because I suspect not all of `∇L` is bad. Take the per-step decomposition seriously: split the reward-to-go at token `t` into the *current* step `r_t` and *everything after*, `R_{t+1}`. The single-step generation quality is what actually controls error accumulation — a wrong early token is what dooms the whole chain — so let me separate the gradient of the immediate term from the gradient that carries the long future. I'll directly differentiate the *expected immediate reward* at each step instead of leaving it inside the REINFORCE estimator:

  ∇L = -E_{y~p_S}[ Σ_t ∇ E_{y_t~p_S(·|y_<t,x)}[ r_t ] ]  −  E_{y~p_S}[ Σ_t R_{t+1} ∇ log p_S(y_t|y_<t,x) ].

Call the first piece "Single" and the second "Long." Now here's the thing I want to stare at. The inner object in Single is `E_{y_t~p_S(·|y_<t,x)}[ r_t ] = Σ_{y'} p_S(y'|y_<t,x) · log( p_T(y'|y_<t,x) / p_S(y'|y_<t,x) )`. That is a sum over the *whole vocabulary* — and it equals `-Σ_{y'} p_S(y') log(p_S(y')/p_T(y')) = -D_KL(p_S(·|y_<t,x) || p_T(·|y_<t,x))`. The single-step term *is the per-token reverse KL*, evaluated analytically over the vocabulary at the prefix `y_<t`. I don't have to Monte-Carlo it — I have the full student and teacher distributions at that position, so I can compute the expectation *exactly* by summing over `V`, and it's differentiable in `θ` directly. No sampling noise in this piece at all. The variance, the reward hacking via sampled degenerate completions, the reward-to-go length bias — all of that lives in the *Long* term, the one with `R_{t+1}` and the REINFORCE `∇ log p_S` factor and the sampled future. The Single term is clean.

So the question becomes sharp: do I actually need the Long term? What is it buying me? It's the long-horizon credit assignment — it lets a token `t` be rewarded for good *future* tokens it enabled. In a sparse-reward RL problem that's essential; you only learn at the end so you must propagate credit back. But my reward is *not* sparse. I have a dense, informative signal — the full teacher distribution — at *every single token*. I don't need to wait for the future to find out whether token `t` was good; the teacher tells me right now, at token `t`, by how much probability it assigns to what the student did. The whole reason I'd tolerate the variance of long-horizon credit assignment is sparse reward, and I don't have sparse reward. So let me just *drop the Long term*. Set its weight to zero. In RL language: use a discount factor of zero. At each timestep the student optimizes only the immediate next token against the teacher, with no consideration for future tokens.

Is that actually justified, or am I throwing away signal to buy stability? Let me check what the zero-discount surrogate still targets. The sequence-level reverse KL factorizes auto-regressively: `log p_S(y|x) - log p_T(y|x) = Σ_t [log p_S(y_t|y_<t,x) - log p_T(y_t|y_<t,x)]`, so `D_KL(p_S(·|x) || p_T(·|x)) = E_{y~p_S} Σ_t [log p_S(y_t|·) - log p_T(y_t|·)]`. At a fixed prefix, the conditional expectation of that same log-ratio is exactly `D_KL(p_S(·|y_<t,x) || p_T(·|y_<t,x))`. Each conditional KL is non-negative, and the sequence KL is the expectation of these conditional KLs under the student's prefix distribution. The full sequence-KL gradient contains two effects: the direct gradient of the conditional KLs and the gradient of the future prefix distribution. The direct effect is the Single term I can compute exactly; the future-prefix effect is the Long term with `R_{t+1}`. Setting the discount factor to zero is therefore a deliberate surrogate: keep the exact immediate conditional-KL gradient on the states the student actually visits, and drop the sampled state-distribution credit assignment. I'm not changing the local object from reverse KL into something else; I'm removing the long-horizon score-function part whose only reason to exist would be sparse reward. The dense teacher already gives me the next-token correction at every state.

And look what falls away the moment the Long term is gone. The variance source — gone, the Single term is an exact vocabulary sum. Reward hacking via sampled degenerate completions getting farmed by `R_t` — gone, there's no `R_t`. The length bias from accumulating reward-to-go — gone, there's no reward-to-go; each token's loss is a self-contained per-position KL that doesn't sum over the future, so a long correct solution isn't penalized for being long. The whole pile of stabilizers — teacher-mixed sampling, importance weights, length normalization, clipping — existed *to tame the Long term*. With the Long term deleted I need none of them. What I'm left with is startlingly simple: at each on-policy state, compute the per-token reverse KL between student and teacher analytically over the vocabulary, sum over the completion tokens, backprop. It's structurally a *supervised* loss — a differentiable function of the two logit tensors at matching positions — that just happens to be evaluated on student-generated prefixes. I get the on-policy state distribution from the rollout and the dense mode-seeking signal from the reverse KL, with the stability of a supervised step. That's the whole method.

Let me sanity-check the two ways of writing it agree, because I'll see both in practice. One realization computes the analytic per-token reverse KL directly: at each position, `Σ_v p_S(v)[log p_S(v) - log p_T(v)]`, a closed-form differentiable scalar, and I just sum it over tokens and step. The other realization is the score-function flavor with discount zero. For a sampled token, the reverse-KL contribution is `log p_S(y_t) - log p_T(y_t) = -r_t`, so the reward-style advantage people write down is its negative, `r_t = log p_T(y_t) - log p_S(y_t)`. If I want the exact score-function gradient of the reverse KL, I must not drop the normalization term: the weight is `(r_t - 1)`, giving `-E[(r_t - 1) ∇log p_S(y_t|y_<t,x)]`. That `-1` is the derivative of the student's own `log p_S` inside the expectation. With the teacher's full logits in hand I do not need the noisy sampled estimator at all — the analytic sum integrates the next-token expectation exactly, keeps the normalization term automatically through autodiff, and has lower variance.

Now, where does this sit relative to what I already know? It clicks into place as a *corner* of a more general design space, and seeing that convinces me I've found the right point rather than a random third option. The general object is: choose a divergence `D` between teacher and student token distributions, and choose how much of the training data comes from the student — a fraction `λ` between 0 (purely fixed dataset) and 1 (purely student rollouts) — and minimize `(1-λ) E_{data}[D(p_T||p_S)] + λ E_{x, y~p_S}[D(p_T||p_S)]`, not backpropagating through the sampling. Off-policy supervised KD is `λ=0` with forward KL. Student-rollout KD with forward KL is `λ=1` but still mass-covering. And the method I just derived is `λ=1` with `D = D_KL(p_S||p_T)`. For the divergence axis there's even a clean one-parameter bridge in the implementation: the generalized Jensen-Shannon family behaves like forward KL as `β→0` and like reverse KL at the `β=1` endpoint used for this loss. So my method is the `β=1`, `λ=1` corner: maximally student-rollout, maximally mode-seeking. That's not an arbitrary pick — it's the corner the problem selects. Student rollouts because the state cascade demanded them; reverse KL because the capacity mismatch demanded mode-seeking; and the analytic vocabulary sum, rather than a sampled policy-gradient estimator, because the teacher's log-probs are right there, making the dense closed form available, which is what lets me set the discount to zero and shed all the stabilizers.

A couple of design points I should pin down before writing code, since they're easy to get subtly wrong. Temperature: I divide *both* logit tensors by the same temperature before softmax. This is the distillation softmax-temperature — it softens both distributions so the divergence sees the teacher's relative preferences among plausible tokens, not just its argmax. It must hit teacher and student symmetrically, or I'd be comparing distributions at different sharpnesses and the KL would be measuring temperature, not behavior. Training temperature near 1 keeps the student's rollouts diverse enough to visit a real spread of its own states. Masking: the loss is only meaningful on the *completion* tokens — the student didn't generate the prompt, and padding is meaningless — so I mask to the positions where `labels != -100` and never let prompt/pad positions into the reduction. Reduction: "batchmean" should mean *per-token* mean over the kept tokens, sum of the per-token KLs divided by the number of unmasked tokens, with the denominator clamped to at least 1 so an all-masked edge case can't divide by zero. That per-token normalization is itself the right thing — it makes the loss scale-free in sequence length, the same length-bias hygiene I got for free by dropping `R_t`, now made explicit in the reduction.

The one genuinely fiddly bit is the KL direction in the implementation, and getting it backwards would silently train forward KL — the *opposite* of what I want — so let me be careful. The framework's primitive computes, per element, `target · (log target − input)` and sums; that is `Σ p · (log p − log q) = D_KL(p || q)` when I pass `input = log q` and `target = log p`. I want `D_KL(p_S || p_T) = Σ_v p_S (log p_S − log p_T)`, so `p` is the *student* and `q` is the *teacher*: pass the *student* log-probs as `target` and the *teacher* log-probs as `input`. So the call is `kl_div(input = teacher_log_probs, target = student_log_probs, log_target=True)`, summed over the vocabulary. (The off-the-shelf default, `kl_div(student_log_probs, teacher_log_probs)`, computes `D_KL(p_T || p_S)` — forward KL — which is precisely the mass-covering objective I'm trying to get away from. The two differ only by swapping the two arguments, which is exactly why it's worth stating out loud.)

So let me fill the one empty slot in the trainer — the per-token transfer loss — with the discount-zero, on-policy, reverse-KL objective I derived:

```python
import torch
import torch.nn.functional as F


def compute_distill_loss(
    student_logits: torch.Tensor,    # [B, T, V] student logits over the rollout tokens
    teacher_logits: torch.Tensor,    # [B, T, V] frozen-teacher logits over the same tokens
    labels: torch.Tensor = None,     # [B, T]; -100 on prompt/padding, token id on completion
    beta: float = 1.0,               # reverse-KL endpoint; kept for trainer API
    temperature: float = 1.0,
    reduction: str = "batchmean",
    step: int = 0,
    total_steps: int = 0,
    lmbda: float = 1.0,              # student-rollout corner; applied upstream by the trainer
) -> torch.Tensor:
    # Per-token reverse KL on the student's own (on-policy) tokens: KL(p_S || p_T).
    # Mode-seeking under capacity mismatch; dense at every token; discount factor 0
    # (no long-horizon term), so this is a supervised-style differentiable loss.

    # Symmetric distillation temperature: soften BOTH distributions equally so the
    # divergence measures behavior, not a sharpness mismatch.
    student_logits = student_logits / temperature
    teacher_logits = teacher_logits / temperature
    student_log_probs = F.log_softmax(student_logits, dim=-1)
    teacher_log_probs = F.log_softmax(teacher_logits, dim=-1)

    # KL(p_S || p_T) = Sum_v p_S(v) [log p_S(v) - log p_T(v)], summed over vocab -> [B, T].
    # F.kl_div(input=log_q, target=log_p, log_target=True) = Sum_v p_(target) (log p_target - input).
    # With input = teacher_log_probs and target = student_log_probs this is KL(p_S || p_T)
    # (reverse). Swapping the two args would give forward KL(p_T || p_S) -- the wrong direction.
    per_token = F.kl_div(
        teacher_log_probs, student_log_probs, reduction="none", log_target=True
    ).sum(dim=-1)  # [B, T]

    # Only completion tokens count; prompt/padding (labels == -100) are excluded.
    if labels is not None:
        mask = labels != -100
        per_token = per_token[mask]
        denom = mask.sum().clamp_min(1)
    else:
        denom = torch.tensor(
            per_token.numel(), device=per_token.device, dtype=torch.long
        ).clamp_min(1)

    # "batchmean" = per-token mean over kept tokens -> length-scale-free, matching the
    # discount-0 design (no reward-to-go, so no penalty for long correct solutions).
    if reduction == "batchmean":
        return per_token.sum() / denom
    elif reduction == "sum":
        return per_token.sum()
    elif reduction == "mean":
        return per_token.mean()
    return per_token
```

Let me trace the whole causal chain one more time to be sure it holds. I started stuck: maximum-likelihood / forward-KL distillation on a fixed dataset stalls, and it stalls for two distinct reasons — off-policy training leaves the student untrained on the states its own generations reach, so an early slip cascades (the imitation-learning state-distribution mismatch), and forward KL is mass-covering, so a low-capacity student is forced to smear probability over the teacher's full support and generates incoherently. The state-distribution problem says: sample from the student, train on its own rollouts — go on-policy. The capacity problem says: use a mode-seeking objective that commits to one teacher behavior — reverse KL, which weights by `p_S` and is zero-forcing rather than mass-covering. Putting both together makes the target the sequence-level reverse KL under student rollouts, which is an on-policy RL problem; the naive policy-gradient estimator for it is high-variance and suffers reward hacking and a short-response length bias, all of which trace to its long-horizon reward-to-go term. Decomposing the gradient per step exposes that the immediate-step term is exactly the per-token reverse KL, computable analytically over the vocabulary and differentiable with no sampling noise, while every pathology lives in the long-horizon term. Since the teacher gives a dense per-token signal, I do not need long-horizon credit assignment to get useful local corrections, so I set the discount to zero and keep the direct conditional-KL gradient on student-visited states. What remains is a clean, supervised-style loss: divide both logit tensors by a shared temperature, take the analytic per-token reverse KL over the vocabulary, mask to the student's completion tokens, average per token. It sheds all of the policy-gradient stabilizers (variance baselines, teacher-mixed sampling, importance weights, length normalization, clipping) because the term that needed them is gone. And it sits as the `λ=1`, reverse-KL corner of the on-policy-fraction × divergence design space — on-policy because the cascade demanded it, reverse KL because the capacity mismatch demanded mode-seeking, analytic-and-discount-zero because the teacher's dense log-probs made the closed form available.
