I want the perfect-matching question to stop being a search through possible edge sets. First I separate the trivial parity case: if the graph has odd order, no pairing can cover every vertex, so there is nothing to compute there. The real work is even order. A matching is only a partition of the vertices into disjoint pairs, so I would like an algebraic expression whose native terms are already such pairings — then "does a matching exist" becomes "is this expression nonzero," and I never enumerate edge sets by hand.

A determinant is the obvious first thing to reach for, because its terms range over all the ways of pairing rows with columns. So suppose I just build a symbolic adjacency matrix: put a variable in position `(i, j)` when `{i, j}` is an edge, zero otherwise, and take the determinant. The determinant expands over permutations `sigma` as a signed sum of products of entries `a_{i, sigma(i)}`. A product survives only if every `a_{i, sigma(i)}` is a nonzero entry, i.e. every `(i, sigma(i))` is an edge. That is encouraging — but a permutation is not a pairing. Its cycle structure can have cycles of length three, four, and longer, and a length-three cycle is a closed triangle in the graph, not a matching edge. So the surviving terms of a plain adjacency determinant correspond to disjoint-cycle covers of the graph, which is a strictly larger family than perfect matchings. I should check whether that larger family corrupts the test.

Take two disjoint triangles on vertices `{0, 1, 2}` and `{3, 4, 5}`. The whole graph has six vertices but no perfect matching, because each triangle is an odd component and a pairing inside it must leave one vertex uncovered. If I write the symmetric variable adjacency matrix and expand its determinant symbolically, I get

```text
det(symmetric) = 4 * x01 * x02 * x12 * x34 * x35 * x45,
```

which is not the zero polynomial. So the symmetric determinant reports a false positive on a graph with no matching: the two surviving terms are the two 3-cycles, one per triangle, and together they cover all six vertices as a cycle cover even though no pairing does. A plain determinant therefore tests the wrong thing. I need to kill the odd cycles.

This is where skew-symmetry earns its place. If I make the matrix skew-symmetric — entry `x_ij` above the diagonal and `-x_ij` below — then odd-length permutation cycles cancel against their reversals, because reversing an odd cycle flips an odd number of the negated entries and so flips the overall sign while leaving the monomial fixed. The same two-triangles matrix, built skew-symmetric, gives

```text
det(skew) = 0,
```

which is the correct verdict. The contrast is exactly the diagnostic I wanted: same edges, same variables, only the sign convention below the diagonal differs, and that flip is what suppresses the spurious odd-cycle terms. The odd-order parity case falls out of the same mechanism — every odd-order skew-symmetric determinant is identically zero, which is consistent with there being no matching on an odd vertex set, so I do not even need a separate argument for it.

The skew-symmetric structure has more to give than just killing odd cycles in a determinant. For an even skew-symmetric matrix the determinant is the square of a Pfaffian, and the Pfaffian is itself the signed sum over ways to partition the indices into unordered pairs — with no extra numeric factor in front, each pairing contributing one product with sign `+1` or `-1`. That is the object whose terms are literally pairings, which is what I was after from the start. So I should work with the Pfaffian directly and let the determinant be its square at the end.

Now I build the filter. I number the vertices and place `x_ij` in position `(i, j)` with `i < j` only when `{i, j}` is an edge, `-x_ij` in the transposed position, and `0` on the diagonal and on every nonedge. Choosing the opposite orientation for some edges would only flip the signs of those variables, which cannot change whether the resulting polynomial is identically zero, so the orientation convention is free. The zeros are the real content: an entry of zero forces every Pfaffian term that uses that pair to vanish.

Trace a Pfaffian term. It picks a set of pairs covering all vertices. If one chosen pair is a nonedge, its entry is `0` and the whole product dies. If every chosen pair is an edge, those pairs are disjoint and cover all vertices, so they form a perfect matching, and the term is the product of that matching's edge variables (up to sign). So the surviving terms are in bijection with perfect matchings.

The one thing that could still ruin this is cancellation: if two matchings contributed with opposite signs and the same monomial, the Pfaffian could be zero even when matchings exist. Independence of the variables is supposed to prevent that, but I should see it happen rather than trust it. Take the 4-cycle on vertices `1, 2, 3, 4` with edges `12, 23, 34, 14`. It has exactly two perfect matchings, `{12, 34}` and `{23, 14}`. Expanding the `4 x 4` Pfaffian `a12 a34 - a13 a24 + a14 a23` on this matrix (with `a13 = a24 = 0`, since `13` and `24` are nonedges) gives

```text
Pf = x12 * x34 + x14 * x23.
```

Two terms, one per matching, distinct monomials, no cancellation — exactly as the independence argument predicts, and now I have seen it. The point is general: two different perfect matchings differ in at least one edge, so their products are different monomials in distinct variables, and distinct monomials cannot cancel in a polynomial ring over the integers regardless of their signs. So the Pfaffian vanishes identically if and only if the graph has no perfect matching. I can double-check the determinant relation on the same example: squaring,

```text
Pf^2 = x12^2 x34^2 + 2 x12 x14 x23 x34 + x14^2 x23^2,
```

and expanding `det(T)` directly by cofactors on the same four-by-four matrix, with no shortcut through the Pfaffian at all, gives exactly the same polynomial. So the squaring identity checks out on an actual graph, not only in the abstract algebra I was trusting before I saw it act on edges.

Now I want to actually use this. For a graph with hundreds of vertices, `det(T(G))` is a polynomial in hundreds of thousands of edge-indeterminates, and "does a matching exist" has become "is this specific enormous polynomial the zero polynomial." I cannot answer that by writing the polynomial out: the Pfaffian has one term per perfect matching, and the whole point of the noncancellation argument above is that there is no shortcut around those terms for a graph whose matchings I don't already know. Symbolic Gaussian elimination does not rescue me either. Every elimination step divides one symbolic row by a symbolic pivot and subtracts a multiple of it from every other row, and each such division and subtraction turns the remaining entries into a bigger rational expression in the `x_ij` than they were before. After `n` rounds of that I am carrying around expressions at least as large as the determinant I was trying to avoid writing down. The symbolic route is a dead end no matter which linear-algebra method walks it.

What I can compute cheaply is the determinant of a matrix of actual numbers. So instead of asking the symbolic question, I choose one number for every `x_ij`, substitute those numbers into `T(G)`, and run ordinary Gaussian elimination on the result: that is `O(n^3)` arithmetic, nothing like the size of the symbolic expansion. A single numeric matrix, though, only reports the value of `det(T(G))` at one point among all possible substitutions, not whether the polynomial is identically zero. If that one evaluation is nonsingular, I am still completely justified: a polynomial that is nonzero at even one point cannot be the zero polynomial, so a matching must exist, and there is no probability involved in that direction. The only risk sits on the other side. A singular numeric matrix could mean the polynomial really is zero, i.e. no matching, or it could mean I picked an accidental root of a polynomial that is not zero.

I need to bound how unlucky a random choice can be. Every surviving Pfaffian term is a product of exactly `n / 2` matching-edge variables, one per pair, and no term has any other shape, so the Pfaffian is homogeneous of degree `n / 2` in the `x_ij`, and the determinant, being its square, is homogeneous of degree `n`. Freeze every variable except one at whatever values I already drew: what remains is a polynomial in that one variable of degree at most `n`, so it has at most `n` roots, and drawing that variable from a set `S` of candidates makes the chance of landing on one of them at most `n / |S|`. Carrying that same freeze-and-count argument through the rest of the variables one at a time, the same bound governs the whole random point: a nonzero polynomial of degree `n`, evaluated at an independently random point drawn coordinate-wise from a set of size `|S|`, comes out zero with probability at most `n / |S|`. So drawing every `x_ij` from a large set and testing the resulting numeric matrix for full rank gives a one-sided test. Full rank always certifies a matching. A singular result is wrong with a probability I can push as low as I like, by making `|S|` larger or by repeating the trial on fresh independent draws.

Doing this arithmetic over the integers or rationals works but is wasteful: each elimination step can grow the numerators and denominators of the intermediate entries, and after `n` rounds the numbers being divided can run to thousands of bits even though I only wanted a single yes-or-no answer. The fix is to do the whole computation inside a finite field: pick a prime `p`, draw every `x_ij` uniformly from `{1, ..., p - 1}`, and eliminate modulo `p` the entire way, so every entry stays one machine word no matter how many pivot steps I take.

That substitution raises a sharper worry than ordinary bad luck. Reducing an integer polynomial modulo a prime can kill it outright: if `p` happened to divide every one of `det(T(G))`'s integer coefficients, the reduced polynomial would be identically zero modulo `p` even though it is not zero over the integers, and every trial would come back singular no matter how many times I resampled. That would not be bad luck, it would be a permanent blind spot built into the test. I need one coefficient no prime can erase, and I already have it: when the graph does have a perfect matching, that matching's Pfaffian term carries no numeric factor at all, coefficient exactly `+1` or `-1`, and squaring it into the determinant leaves a monomial with coefficient exactly `1`, and no other matching produces that same monomial, since two different matchings differ in at least one edge. No prime divides `1`. So whenever a matching exists, `det(T(G))` reduced modulo any prime whatsoever still has at least this one nonzero coefficient, and is still not the zero polynomial. The only risk left is the `n / |S|` bad-luck bound, which the size of the field controls, not a hidden certainty of failure.

So the decision procedure is: fix a prime large compared to `n`, draw each edge's value independently and uniformly from that field, build the signed skew matrix, and compute its rank by modular Gaussian elimination. Full rank is a proof that a perfect matching exists. A rank deficiency is evidence, not proof, that none does — evidence I can strengthen as far as I like by repeating the draw with fresh independent randomness and reporting existence the moment any trial comes back full rank.
