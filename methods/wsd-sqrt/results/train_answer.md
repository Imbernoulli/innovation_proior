Pretraining a decoder-only transformer with AdamW is one enormous stochastic run, and the only thing I get to shape over its entire length is the learning rate as a function of step. The reigning recipe is a linear warmup to a peak $\eta$ followed by a half-cosine decay down to about a tenth of the peak, with the cosine cycle stretched to cover the whole run. It works, but it carries a constraint that keeps biting me. The cosine has a length parameter $T$ — the step where it first reaches its minimum — and the empirical record pins exactly where $T$ has to be. Looking at the summed learning rate over a run, the loss keeps dropping as that sum grows (Kaplan et al. 2020), so decaying early, $T < S$, spends less total mass up high and leaves performance on the table; looking at the other side, $T > S$ — the cosine not finished decaying by the end — actually *drops* performance (Hoffmann et al. 2022), while $T = S$ is both the lowest loss and the most efficient. Put together, the optimum is welded to $T = S$: the cosine must be precisely as long as the run.

That is a far worse constraint than it first appears. It means the schedule is bolted to a token budget I must commit to up front. If I want to train longer I cannot simply continue — the rate is already sitting at its floor $0.1\eta$ where it makes almost no progress, and re-warming it spikes the loss and has been reported to hurt and even induce forgetting. Worse is the science problem: every scaling-law, data-mixture, or architecture study is really the question "how would this model have done at a *different* number of tokens?", and because the cosine has to match the length I cannot read that off one run — I must retrain from scratch at each target length. For $m$ model sizes crossed with $m$ token counts that is $O(m^2)$ full pretraining runs, which is the cost that makes scaling research a luxury. The constant-rate alternative removes the length commitment but never anneals, so it leaves the late-settling benefit unrealized and lands at a worse loss. What I want is a schedule that matches a tuned cosine's final loss, does *not* need $S$ fixed in advance so the run can stop or continue on demand, lets intermediate checkpoints be reused so probing many token counts costs roughly linear effort, and changes nothing but the schedule layer — same model, same data, same AdamW, same total update budget.

The path opens when I ask *why* cosine works at all. The folklore is a trade-off: a high rate early helps the optimizer explore the landscape, and a slow anneal to a low rate at the end lets a deep net settle into a good minimum. The cosine bundles those two jobs into one smooth curve of fixed length — but there is no law that they must be the *same* curve, and that fusion is precisely what welds $T$ to $S$. So I propose to separate them explicitly. The method is the Warmup-Stable-Decay schedule with a $(1-\sqrt{p})$ cooldown — WSD-sqrt. Split the run into three stages: a linear **warmup** ramping from near zero to the peak $\eta$; a **stable** stage that holds $\eta$ *constant* for the bulk of training; and a short final **decay** stage that anneals the rate down. With warmup end $W$, decay start $T$, and run end $S$, $$\mathrm{WSD}(s) = \begin{cases} (s/W)\,\eta & s < W \\ \eta & W \le s < T \\ \min\_lr + (\eta - \min\_lr)\,(1 - \sqrt{p}) & T \le s < S \end{cases}$$ with decay-progress $p = (s - T)/(S - T)$. The decisive property of this shape is that the stable stage is a single reusable trunk: I run it as long as I like, and whenever I want a finished model at the current step I branch off a decay — I never had to know $S$ when I started. For scaling studies I run *one* long stable trunk per model size and branch a cheap decay from checkpoints at $10N, 20N, \dots$ tokens, which is the difference between $O(m^2)$ and $O(m)$.

This only buys anything if the short decay actually recovers cosine's final loss, and the reason it does is geometric. When I anneal from the constant high rate, the loss drops sharply and fast and reaches or beats the cosine-at-$T{=}S$ value — not a gentle convergence but a sudden cliff tracking the rate coming down. The signature in the weights is peculiar: during the stable stage updates are large yet the loss barely moves, while during decay updates are small yet the loss plummets. Looking closer, in the decay phase the gradient norm shrinks, consecutive-step gradients start *agreeing* (their cosine turns positive), the directional derivative along the trajectory falls roughly in lockstep with the rate, and the curvature along the path climbs by about an order of magnitude — the signature of dropping into the bottom of a sharp optimum. And a checkpoint from before the decay and one from after lie in a single connected basin: linearly interpolating the two weight vectors makes the loss fall smoothly the whole way, with no barrier between them. The picture that explains the "big steps, no progress / small steps, huge progress" mismatch is a long valley with a river along its floor: one nearly-flat direction down the river along which steady progress is possible, flanked by steep walls in the sharp cross-valley directions. A high constant rate races the iterate fast down the river — real progress — but across the valley it overshoots and oscillates between the walls, so the iterate is perpetually parked partway up the steep banks and the measured loss stays *elevated* even though the weights are genuinely moving. Lower the rate and the cross-valley steps fall below the overshoot threshold, the oscillation dies, and the iterate simply drops the short distance into the channel — small steps, but each removes the cross-valley loss the high rate was holding it away from. The stable stage's job is to travel as far down the river as possible (high and long); the decay's job is purely to quench the oscillation and let the iterate fall into the channel — and because you only descend the valley's *width*, not the river's *length*, a short cooldown of roughly 10% of tokens suffices and the loss drop is abrupt.

That mechanism is also what fixes the shape of the decay, the question earlier constant-plus-cooldown ancestors (the trapezoidal "infinite-LR" schedule, and DeepSeek's multi-step shape) never pinned down — they simply took the tail linear, $f(p) = 1 - p$. The cooldown should quench the high-rate oscillation *early* while leaving enough remaining steps at useful low rates, so with a fixed number of decay steps I want to get through the high part of the decay quickly and spend more of the cooldown low. Linear spends equal step-density at every rate level, which is too slow to quench the oscillation. The function that drops fast at the start and then flattens is exactly $1 - \sqrt{p}$: its derivative is $-1/(2\sqrt{p})$, singular at $p = 0$ in the continuous idealization and relaxed to $-1/2$ by $p = 1$, against linear's constant slope of $-1$. Since $\sqrt{p} > p$ on $(0,1)$, the sqrt tail lies *below* linear everywhere — at $p = 1/4$ it is at $0.5\,\eta$ versus linear's $0.75\,\eta$, at $p = 1/2$ about $0.293\,\eta$ versus $0.5\,\eta$ — so it reaches any chosen low multiplier earlier and leaves more of the remaining cooldown below it. That is exactly the "quench earlier, settle lower" profile the river-valley picture demands, and the ablations agree: it consistently beats linear in the tested 10–20% cooldowns, with the advantage growing for longer training. The exponent is empirical, not algebraically forced — in the $1 - p^a$ family, very small exponents like $a = 0.1$ or $0.2$ strand the run at a rate too low for too many steps and underperform, while the remaining tested exponents below $0.5$ differ only marginally and $a = 0.5$ still comes out on top; gentler tails such as $1 - p^2$ or an in-decay cosine leave the rate high too long and fall behind for practical cooldown lengths.

I write the decay with a floor so it interpolates cleanly between the peak and $\min\_lr$ rather than crashing to exactly zero: $$lr(s) = \min\_lr + (\eta - \min\_lr)\,(1 - \sqrt{p}),$$ which is continuous with the plateau at $p = 0$ ($lr = \eta$) and lands at $\min\_lr$ at $p = 1$. The floor stays an independent knob because the maximum and final rates should be set separately: lower final rates help pretraining loss — pushing cosine below its usual $0.1\eta$ floor can match the best sqrt cooldown — but exact-zero annealing can hurt downstream metrics, plausibly by saturating the model too early, so $\min\_lr = 0$ reproduces the canonical cooldown-to-zero while a small positive floor is there when downstream evaluation prefers it. On length, the geometry already says short: 2.5% of the run leaves the rate still falling at the end so I read off a half-quenched loss, around 10% reliably reaches the channel bottom, and beyond that the benefit plateaus, placing the practical band near 10–20%; what really matters is the *absolute* number of cooldown steps, so on a very long run a smaller fraction is plenty because it is still many thousands of settling steps. Warmup I keep linear and do not over-think — final performance is insensitive to its length as long as it is long enough, so a generous warmup is a safe, cost-free choice.

Assembling this against the fixed interface `get_lr(it, warmup_iters, lr_decay_iters, learning_rate, min_lr)` — where `learning_rate` is the peak $\eta$, `min_lr` the floor, and `lr_decay_iters` the horizon by which the schedule has fully decayed — gives three short branches and one square root, dropped straight into the existing loop with the model, data, AdamW, and step budget all untouched:

```python
import math


def get_lr(it, warmup_iters, lr_decay_iters, learning_rate, min_lr):
    """WSD learning-rate schedule with (1-sqrt) cooldown.

    warmup -> constant stable plateau -> short final cooldown shaped as
    (1 - sqrt(progress)): drops steeply at first to quench the cross-valley
    oscillation, then flattens so most cooldown steps settle into the basin.
    """
    # Warmup: linear ramp from ~0 up to the peak learning rate.
    if it < warmup_iters:
        return learning_rate * (it + 1) / (warmup_iters + 1)

    # Decay window: the final fraction of the horizon (~20% of steps).
    decay_start = int(lr_decay_iters * 0.8)

    # Cooldown: 1 - sqrt(progress), front-loaded drop.
    if it >= decay_start:
        decay_len = max(1, lr_decay_iters - decay_start)
        p = (it - decay_start) / decay_len
        p = min(max(p, 0.0), 1.0)                 # clamp to [0, 1]
        coeff = 1.0 - math.sqrt(p)                # 1 at p=0, 0 at p=1
        return min_lr + (learning_rate - min_lr) * coeff

    # Stable stage: hold the peak rate — the reusable trunk, cooldown on demand.
    return learning_rate
```
