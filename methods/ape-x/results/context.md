# Context

## Research question

A broad empirical regularity in deep learning is that combining more computation with more powerful
models and larger datasets yields better results, and there is good reason to hope the same holds for
deep reinforcement learning: the most striking RL successes of the moment — Gorila, A3C, GPU
Advantage Actor-Critic, Distributed PPO, AlphaGo — all leaned heavily on greater compute. Yet most
deep-RL research still optimizes within the budget of a single machine, and the question of how best
to *harness more machines* is comparatively underexplored.

Standard distributed training of neural networks parallelizes the computation of **gradients** so as
to update parameters faster. The narrower question here is whether there is a *different* axis to
scale on: instead of (or in addition to) distributing gradient computation, distribute the
**generation and selection of experience data**. Concretely — if we can run hundreds of environment
instances in parallel and feed all their experience into one place, which experience should the
learner actually train on, and does generating-and-selecting more data, by itself, suffice to push
the state of the art? A solution would have to reach the best-known final performance on the Arcade
Learning Environment in a small fraction of the usual wall-clock time, without per-game
hyperparameter tuning, and ideally generalize to continuous-control problems as well.

## Background

**Distributed stochastic gradient descent.** In supervised learning, training of deep networks is
routinely sped up by parallelizing gradient computation across workers, applying the parameter
updates either synchronously (Krizhevsky 2014) or asynchronously (Dean et al. 2012, DistBelief). A
recurring constraint is that gradients become **stale** quickly: a gradient computed against
parameters that are now several updates old is a worse and worse estimate, so these systems need
low-latency communication and short synchronization periods.

**Distributed importance sampling.** A complementary way to speed up training is variance reduction
by non-uniform sampling: draw training examples with probability proportional to, say, the L2 norm
of their gradient, and reweight the updates by the inverse sampling probability to remove the bias
(Hinton 2007 obtained roughly a 3× speedup on MNIST this way; Alain et al. 2015 extended it to the
distributed setting). A robust variant ranks examples by their last-known loss and makes the
sampling probability a function of the rank rather than the loss value itself (Loshchilov &
Hutter 2015), which is less sensitive to outliers.

**Experience replay and prioritization.** Experience replay (Lin 1992) stores past transitions and
reuses them for many learning updates instead of learning once and discarding; it decorrelates
consecutive updates and lets rare transitions be used more than once, and it is central to deep
value-based agents — Neural Fitted Q-Iteration (Riedmiller 2005) and DQN (Mnih et al. 2015), which
keeps a sliding window of the last ~10⁶ transitions and draws minibatches of 32 uniformly at random.
Because DQN learns off-policy, replay also lets it learn from data generated by *previous* versions
of the policy, which additionally guards against overfitting. Prioritized experience replay
(Schaul et al. 2016) replaces uniform sampling with sampling proportional to a priority — typically
the absolute temporal-difference error |δ|, a free by-product of each update that measures how
"surprising" a transition still is. It draws transition k with probability
`P(k) = p_k^α / Σ_j p_j^α` (so α = 0 recovers uniform sampling), corrects the induced bias with an
importance-sampling weight `w_k = (N·P(k))^{-β}` normalized by its maximum, and inserts each new
transition at the **maximum** priority seen so far, only refining that priority once the transition
is first sampled. Sampling and updating at scale are made O(log N) by a sum-tree (and a parallel
min-tree for the maximum weight). A large ablation across value-based ingredients (Rainbow,
Hessel et al. 2018) found prioritization to be the single most important contributor to performance.
Biased replay is especially helpful in RL because the reward signal can be sparse and the data
distribution depends on the agent's own policy.

**Off-policy value learning building blocks.** Double Q-learning (van Hasselt 2010; van Hasselt,
Guez & Silver 2016) reduces the systematic overestimation of the `max` operator by decoupling the
choice of the bootstrap action from its evaluation: select with the online network, evaluate with a
slowly-updated target network θ⁻ (Mnih et al. 2015). Multi-step (n-step) bootstrap targets
(Sutton 1988) propagate reward information faster by summing n discounted rewards before
bootstrapping. The dueling network architecture (Wang et al. 2016) splits the value head into a
state-value stream V and an advantage stream A and recombines them as `Q = V + (A − mean_a A)`.

**Deterministic policy gradients for continuous control.** For continuous actions, DDPG
(Lillicrap et al. 2015), the deep form of the deterministic policy gradient (Silver et al. 2014),
learns a critic `q(s,a)` by temporal-difference regression and a deterministic actor `μ(s)` that is
pushed up the critic's value via the chain rule `∇_φ q(s, μ_φ(s))`; exploration is injected as
additive noise on the action.

**The first distributed deep-RL architecture.** Gorila (Nair et al. 2015, "Massively Parallel
Methods for Deep RL") established the template that everything here builds on. In its bundled mode it
replicates a complete unit — an actor in its own environment copy, *its own local replay memory*, and
a learner — many times over. Each learner samples (uniformly) from its **local** replay, computes the
DQN gradient, and sends that **gradient** asynchronously to a central **parameter server** that
applies the update; the actor/learner Q-networks are periodically re-synced from the server. To cope
with asynchrony it discards gradients older than a staleness threshold and gradients whose absolute
loss exceeds a running mean-plus-several-standard-deviations. A3C (Mnih et al. 2016) showed the same
acting/learning parallelism inside a single multi-threaded machine with an on-policy n-step
actor-critic; GA3C and PAAC adapted it to GPUs.

## Baselines

**Gorila DQN (Nair et al. 2015).** Distributes deep RL by replicating actor + local-replay + learner
bundles and **distributing gradient computation**: local learners compute gradients and ship them to
a central parameter server. Gap it leaves open: each bundle's replay is *local* and sampled
*uniformly*, so a valuable transition found by one bundle cannot be exploited by another; and because
the thing communicated is gradients, the system inherits distributed-SGD's sensitivity to staleness
and latency. It distributes the wrong quantity (gradients) and shares nothing useful between workers.

**DQN with uniform experience replay (Mnih et al. 2015).** Single learner, sliding-window 10⁶
buffer, minibatches of 32 sampled uniformly, target network, reward clipping, ε-greedy behavior.
The state of the art on Atari for its time, but single-machine and sample-bound; uniform replay
spends most updates on already-mastered transitions.

**Prioritized / Prioritized Dueling DQN (Schaul et al. 2016; Wang et al. 2016).** DQN with sampling
∝ |δ|^α and an annealed IS correction (β: β₀ → 1), and a dueling value head. Substantially more
sample-efficient than uniform DQN, but still single-machine: one process both acts and learns, so
throughput is capped by a single environment-interaction stream, and the "insert at max priority,
refine on first sample" rule assumes a single slow producer of new transitions.

**A3C (Mnih et al. 2016).** Multi-threaded on-policy n-step advantage actor-critic; effective and
fast on a single machine, but on-policy — it cannot reuse past experience and so is sample-bound, and
it parallelizes only within one machine.

**Distributional DQN / C51 (Bellemare et al. 2017) and Rainbow (Hessel et al. 2018).** C51 learns a
distribution over returns instead of a scalar; Rainbow combines double Q-learning, prioritization,
dueling, multi-step returns, distributional values, and noisy nets into the strongest single-machine
value-based agent. Still single-machine and sample-bound.

**DDPG (Lillicrap et al. 2015).** The standard off-policy actor-critic for continuous control, the
natural single-machine yardstick for any continuous-control extension.

## Evaluation settings

The primary benchmark is the Arcade Learning Environment (Bellemare et al. 2013): 57 Atari 2600 games
from raw pixels, with the standard DQN preprocessing — greyscaling, frame stacking, action repeat of
4, and reward clipping to [−1, 1]. Per-game scores are normalized against a random agent and a human
reference and aggregated by the **median** human-normalized score across games, under two protocols:
*no-op starts* (up to 30 no-ops at episode start) and the more demanding *human starts* (episodes
initialized from human play traces, introduced with Gorila). Because the point is scaling, results
are also read against **wall-clock time** and against **environment frames consumed**, alongside the
hardware used (CPU cores, GPUs). The natural continuous-control yardstick is a set of MuJoCo tasks
from the DeepMind Control Suite (Tassa et al. 2018) — a 21-DoF humanoid (stand / walk / run) and a
planar manipulator that must catch a moving ball — learned from low-dimensional features rather than
pixels.
