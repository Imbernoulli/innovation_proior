I want real inference speedups out of quantization, and that forces a specific target: W8A8, both weights and activations in INT8, so the large linear layers actually execute on hardware INT8 GEMM units rather than just sitting in memory more compactly while the arithmetic stays FP16. Weight-only quantization halves storage but leaves the multiply in floating point, so it never touches the tensor cores. The trouble is that activations of large LLMs are brutal to quantize. When I push a few large linear layers to INT8 and look at the accuracy, the weights behave perfectly — their distribution is flat and uniform, so INT8 (even INT4) barely moves perplexity — but the activations fall apart, because a handful of values are roughly $100\times$ larger than the typical magnitude. Under per-tensor quantization with one step $\Delta = \max(|\mathbf X|)/(2^{N-1}-1)$ set by the single largest magnitude $m$ in the matrix, a normal channel whose own max is $m_i \ll m$ uses only about $2^8\cdot m_i/m$ of the available levels; at $m_i/m\approx 1/100$ that is two or three usable levels, and the channel is effectively erased. The outliers spend the whole dynamic range and leave nothing for the values that carry information.

Looking at the activation matrix $\mathbf X\in\mathbb R^{T\times C_i}$, the big values are not scattered: they sit in a small set of input channels (columns), and within those channels they are large for essentially every token. The variance is lopsided — across channels for a single token it is enormous, while within a channel across tokens it is small, so the troublesome channels are the *same* channels regardless of which tokens flow through. The difficulty is organized by channel and is static in that sense. That immediately points to per-channel activation quantization: give each input channel its own $\Delta$, so an outlier channel gets a large step to itself and a normal channel a small one, and nobody borrows anyone else's range. The diagnostic confirms it — simulated per-channel activation quantization closes the gap to FP16, while per-token quantization barely helps, because a token's row still mixes the giant outlier value with all the normal channels under one shared scale. But this is exactly where the hardware wall stands. A hardware INT8 GEMM is a tight march of tensor-core MMA instructions over the contraction dimension $C_i$, and it will not tolerate a different scale per $C_i$ index inserted *inside* that accumulation; dequantization scales can only be applied as an epilogue after the integer sum finishes, which restricts them to the matmul's outer dimensions — the token dimension $T$ of $\mathbf X$ and the output-channel dimension $C_o$ of $\mathbf W$. The kernel can do $\mathbf Y = \mathrm{diag}(\boldsymbol\Delta_{\mathbf X})\,(\bar{\mathbf X}\bar{\mathbf W})\,\mathrm{diag}(\boldsymbol\Delta_{\mathbf W})$ with a per-token activation scale on the left and a per-output-channel weight scale on the right, but a per-input-channel activation scale lives on $C_i$, the contraction axis, precisely where it is forbidden. Existing options are caught in this bind: LLM.int8() keeps the outlier feature dimensions in FP16 via a mixed-precision decomposition, which is lossless but does not map onto a single dense INT8 GEMM and yields no latency speedup; plain per-tensor/per-token W8A8 is hardware-friendly but cannot confine the per-channel outliers, so accuracy degrades on large models; ZeroQuant/nuQmm work around the difficulty with extra granularity and custom kernels rather than removing it. The granularity that fixes accuracy is the one the fast kernel forbids, and the granularity the kernel allows does not address the outliers.

So instead of fighting the asymmetry I lean into it. Weights are easy; activations are hard, and the hardness lives in a few input channels. I propose SmoothQuant: rather than scale the outliers away at runtime, move the difficulty off the activations and onto the weights, where there is room to spare. Channel $j$ of $\mathbf X$ multiplies row $j$ of $\mathbf W$ (with $\mathbf W\in\mathbb R^{C_i\times C_o}$), so if I shrink activation channel $j$ by a factor $s_j$ and grow that weight row by the same $s_j$ the product is unchanged — I am just inserting $\mathrm{diag}(\mathbf s)^{-1}\mathrm{diag}(\mathbf s)$ between the operands:
$$\mathbf Y = \big(\mathbf X\,\mathrm{diag}(\mathbf s)^{-1}\big)\big(\mathrm{diag}(\mathbf s)\,\mathbf W\big) = \hat{\mathbf X}\hat{\mathbf W}.$$
This is exact, no approximation, and although $\mathrm{diag}(\mathbf s)$ acts along $C_i$ it is a *constant* offline rebalancing of the two operands, not a per-channel scale applied inside the GEMM. After rebalancing, $\hat{\mathbf X}$ and $\hat{\mathbf W}$ are quantized with ordinary hardware-friendly scales — per-token or per-tensor on activations, per-tensor or output-channel epilogue scales on weights — and the runtime kernel never sees a contraction-axis scale because the per-channel structure has been baked into the operands. The rebalancing also costs nothing at runtime: since $\mathbf X$ is the output of whatever came before, a LayerNorm or a previous linear, I fold $\mathrm{diag}(\mathbf s)^{-1}$ into that producer's parameters — divide the LayerNorm's affine weight and bias, or the previous linear's output-channel weights and bias, by $\mathbf s$ offline — so $\hat{\mathbf X}$ emerges already smoothed with zero extra kernel launches, while $\hat{\mathbf W} = \mathrm{diag}(\mathbf s)\mathbf W$ is a one-time offline reweighting. When the input is a residual add rather than a clean prior layer, I add an explicit scaling on the residual branch.

The load-bearing question is how to choose $\mathbf s$. The most effective use of the dynamic range — the most usable levels for everyone — is when all channels share the same max magnitude, so dividing channel $j$ by its own max $\max(|\mathbf X_j|)$ would flatten the activations completely. But that extreme is wrong, because dividing an activation channel with a $100\times$ outlier by $100$ multiplies weight row $j$ by $100$, handing the beautifully flat weights a $100\times$ outlier of their own; I would have moved the problem, not solved it. The symmetric choice $s_j = 1/\max(|\mathbf W_j|)$ flattens the weights and dumps everything back onto the activations, equally bad. The difficulty has to be *split* so both operands are tolerable, neither perfectly flat nor catastrophic. I introduce a single knob $\alpha$ that controls how much difficulty migrates from activations to weights and interpolate the two extremes in the exponent:
$$s_j = \frac{\max(|\mathbf X_j|)^{\alpha}}{\max(|\mathbf W_j|)^{1-\alpha}}.$$
At $\alpha=1$ this is $s_j=\max(|\mathbf X_j|)$, the full activation-flattening that overloads the weights; at $\alpha=0$ it is $s_j=1/\max(|\mathbf W_j|)$, the full weight-flattening that overloads the activations; in between, $\alpha$ slides the difficulty smoothly between the two sides. The balanced point falls out of matching the smoothed maxima: the smoothed activation max of channel $j$ is $\max(|\mathbf X_j|)/s_j = \max(|\mathbf X_j|)^{1-\alpha}\max(|\mathbf W_j|)^{1-\alpha}$ and the smoothed weight max is $\max(|\mathbf W_j|)\,s_j = \max(|\mathbf X_j|)^{\alpha}\max(|\mathbf W_j|)^{\alpha}$, and at $\alpha=0.5$ both equal $\big(\max(|\mathbf X_j|)\max(|\mathbf W_j|)\big)^{1/2}$ — the geometric mean of the activation and weight maxima — so channel $j$'s activation and weight end up with the same magnitude and the same quantization difficulty. With a symmetric per-tensor quantizer on both sides, $\alpha=0.5$ is the sweet spot for all the OPT and BLOOM sizes; for a model whose activation outliers are unusually severe, such as GLM-130B with its far larger outlier fraction, I push more difficulty onto the weights with a larger $\alpha$ around $0.75$. The activation max per channel is input-dependent, so I estimate $\max(|\mathbf X_j|)$ over calibration samples — 512 random Pile sentences in the experiments — and pick a suitable $\alpha$ with a quick grid search on a Pile validation subset; the weight max is exact. Once $\alpha$ and both sets of statistics are fixed, $\mathbf s$ is a closed-form per-channel vector with no gradients and no iterative optimization loop. That is the whole method: no backprop and no mixed FP16 outlier-decomposition path, just a per-channel rebalancing chosen by a single $\alpha$, folded into adjacent layers, after which a plain hardware INT8 GEMM runs both operands. I apply the smoothing wherever the input-channel statistics are shared by a preceding normalization and one or more following linears — the attention LayerNorm feeding Q/K/V together, the feed-forward LayerNorm feeding the first MLP projection — then replace those linears with W8A8 linears; the attention batched matmuls go INT8 too, while Softmax, LayerNorm and residual adds stay FP16 since they are cheap and not the bottleneck.

```python
import torch

@torch.no_grad()
def smooth_ln_fcs(ln, fcs, act_scales, alpha=0.5):
    if not isinstance(fcs, list):
        fcs = [fcs]
    device, dtype = fcs[0].weight.device, fcs[0].weight.dtype
    act_scales = act_scales.to(device=device, dtype=dtype)

    # nn.Linear stores weights as [out_features, in_features], so dim=0
    # gives the per-input-channel max across output channels.
    weight_scales = torch.cat(
        [fc.weight.abs().max(dim=0, keepdim=True)[0] for fc in fcs], dim=0
    ).max(dim=0)[0].clamp(min=1e-5)                        # per-input-channel

    s = (act_scales.pow(alpha) / weight_scales.pow(1 - alpha)).clamp(min=1e-5)

    ln.weight.div_(s)                                      # fold diag(s)^-1 into LayerNorm
    if getattr(ln, "bias", None) is not None:
        ln.bias.div_(s)
    for fc in fcs:
        fc.weight.mul_(s.view(1, -1))                      # fold diag(s) into next linears
```
