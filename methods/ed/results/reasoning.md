Let me start from what actually bothers me about the emulators we already have, because they work and I still don't trust them. The setup is fixed and I'm not going to relitigate it: run the superparameterized model once, and for every atmospheric column at every timestep record the large-scale state going in — temperature and specific humidity profiles on the vertical levels, plus a few scalars: solar insolation, the surface sensible and latent heat fluxes, surface pressure — and the sub-grid tendencies coming out: the diabatic heating profile dT/dt, the moistening profile dq/dt, the radiative fluxes at the model top and surface, and precipitation. Stack the state into a vector X, stack the tendencies into Y, and learn Y = f(X). Gentine and then Rasp showed a deep fully-connected net does this well: nine layers of 256 units, LeakyReLU, Adam, mean-squared error, and it's even prognostically stable when you couple it back into the GCM. So the regression is solved. What's not solved is that the thing I get out is a wall of half a million weights with no structure I can point at and say "this is what convection is doing." If I want to ask which large-scale condition drives shallow-cloud heating, I have to run a saliency or attribution method on the trained net, which is expensive and shaky on a regression this high-dimensional. There's no compact summary to look at. That's the itch.

And there's a second thing the flat net does that I keep coming back to. It's trained deterministically against MSE, and the boundary-layer targets — the shallow-cloud heating and moistening around 900 hPa — are partly chaotic: the embedded cloud-resolving model is itself turbulent, and for a given coarse state the true tendency is a *spread* of outcomes, not a single number. What does an MSE-optimal point prediction do when the target conditional distribution has real width? It returns the mean of that spread, sample by sample, because the average is what minimizes squared error against a cloud of possible values. So the emulator's fields come out visibly smoother than the truth, with the variance squashed exactly where the target is most stochastic — the boundary layer, the shallow-cloud level. That's not a tuning failure. That's the generic behaviour of a deterministic regressor on a stochastic target, and people have been muttering for a while that it's an under-representation of the actual variability of convection. So I have two complaints, and they feel like they might want the same kind of fix: I want a low-dimensional, inspectable handle on the problem, and I want to stop regressing to the mean.

Take the inspectability first. The classical move to get a low-dimensional, interpretable summary of a high-dimensional state is PCA: project X onto its leading variance directions and you get a handful of orthogonal components you can plot and reason about. If I did principal-component regression — reduce X to a few PCs, regress Y on those — I'd have an interpretable bottleneck. But PCA is linear, and the convection map is not. The response of deep convection to the large-scale state is sharply nonlinear; a linear projection of X is going to throw away most of the predictable nonlinear signal and underfit Y badly. So linear reduction buys interpretability at a skill I can't accept. I want the *nonlinear* analogue of "squeeze the state through a few numbers."

That's an autoencoder. Funnel the input down through a narrowing stack of nonlinear layers to a small bottleneck, then expand it back up and ask it to reconstruct what went in; train on reconstruction error. The bottleneck — fewer units than the input — forces the network to find a compressed code that retains whatever is needed to rebuild the input, and because the layers are nonlinear, that code is a nonlinear generalization of the principal components. It's nonlinear PCA. And here's the sanity check that tells me the framing is right: if I strip the nonlinearity out — make every activation the identity — the autoencoder collapses back to a linear subspace projection, essentially PCA again (the latent isn't orthogonalized, so it's PCR rather than textbook PCA, but the same family). So a linear-activation autoencoder *is* my linear baseline, and the nonlinear one is the generalization I want. Good. The bottleneck is the inspectable handle I was missing in the flat net: a few numbers I can plot, instead of half a million weights I can't.

But wait — a vanilla autoencoder reconstructs its *input*. It learns a map from a field to itself. My problem is a map *across* domains: from the large-scale state X to the *different* sub-grid tendencies Y. So I can't just autoencode X. I need the bottleneck in the middle of an X-to-Y map: encode X down to a small code z, then decode z up to Y. An encoder-decoder, where the decoder targets a different thing than the encoder ingested. Fine — structurally that's easy, encoder X → z, decoder z → Y, train on MSE between predicted and true Y. Call it ED for now.

Now I have to decide how small z can be without wrecking skill, and this is where I get to test the actual physics question hiding underneath. Reduced-complexity atmospheric models — multi-cloud models, the quasi-equilibrium tropical circulation model — are all built on the premise that the number of degrees of freedom that really govern convection is *small*, much smaller than the raw state vector. If that premise is true for the learned map, then a tiny bottleneck should still predict Y well, and the dimensionality of the bottleneck where skill starts to fall off is itself a measurement of the effective dimensionality of the convective problem. So the bottleneck width isn't just a hyperparameter; it's an instrument. I'll sweep it. The expectation: at a width near the full input dimension the ED should approach the flat reference net (no real compression, no bottleneck cost); shrink it and skill degrades gracefully until some width below which it falls off a cliff. Where the curve has its knee tells me the effective dimension.

Let me think about how to shape the funnel. Input is on the order of sixty-some numbers — two thirty-level profiles plus four scalars, sixty-four. I want to descend to the bottleneck over a few layers, smoothly, so each layer is a modest compression rather than one violent squeeze. A geometric taper is the natural choice: pick a width for the first hidden layer, then halve. If I start wide — say a few hundred units, wider than the input so the first layers can lift X into a richer representation before compressing — and then go full width, full width, half, quarter, eighth, sixteenth, I get something like 463, 463, 232, 116, 58, 29, and then a final linear projection down to the latent. Six hidden layers into the code. The 463 is set by a search over the first/last layer width and the depth; the point of the structure is the smooth halving into the bottleneck, mirrored on the way back out. The decoder is the funnel run backwards: from the code up through 29, 58, 116, 232, 463, 463, and then out. Symmetric, because there's no reason to make the expansion path shaped differently from the compression path, and symmetry keeps the parameter counts balanced.

Activations: ReLU on the hidden layers. It's the cheap, standard choice; the flat net used LeakyReLU and got marginally lower loss, but with this funnel structure plain ReLU is fine on the hidden layers and I'll keep it simple. The output layer is where I have to think. The targets are normalized tendencies and fluxes, and they're real-valued and frequently *negative* — cooling tendencies, drying tendencies, downward fluxes are negative numbers. So whatever sits on the output must be able to emit negatives. ReLU on the output is immediately disqualified: it clips every negative to zero, so the net literally could not represent a cooling tendency. The obvious safe choice is a linear output, and that's what the flat emulators use. But let me reach for something slightly better. The exponential linear unit, ELU — identity for positive inputs, and α(e^x − 1) for negative ones with α = 1 — is identity on the positive side, smooth through the origin, allows negative outputs, and saturates gently toward −1 for very negative inputs rather than running off linearly. It pushes mean activations toward zero and dodges the dying-ReLU problem. On a real-valued output that's both signed and occasionally has long tails, that gentle lower saturation is a mild, sensible inductive bias, and in small trials it gives a touch more skill than the bare linear output. So: ReLU through the hidden layers, ELU on the output. That's the ED, the deterministic encoder-decoder: encode X → small code, decode code → Y, MSE loss.

Now I want to come back to the bottleneck and reconsider what to *put through* it, because I realize I've quietly thrown away part of what made the autoencoder interpretable in the first place. In a plain autoencoder, the code z is forced to summarize the *input field* — it has to retain enough of X to rebuild X. That's why the code is meaningful: it's a compressed description of the state. But in my X → Y encoder-decoder, the code is only ever asked to retain whatever is needed to predict Y. So z will encode the *magnitude of the convective response* — how much heating, how much rain — and very little of the large-scale climate state itself, because the state per se isn't the decoder's target. If I then go to inspect the code hoping to see the *drivers* of convection — the geographic structure, the temperature regimes, the day/night cycle — they won't be cleanly there, because nothing pushed the code to keep them. The interpretability I wanted is about the relationship between large-scale conditions and convection, and an X → Y code mostly knows about the convection half.

So the decoder has to reconstruct *both* Y *and* X. Concatenate onto the target vector a reconstruction of the large-scale input, so the output O is [Y ; X-recon]. Now the code has two jobs — predict the sub-grid tendencies *and* rebuild the large-scale state — and to do the second it is *forced* to encode the climate state in the bottleneck alongside the convective response. That's exactly what makes z interpretable as a joint summary of "what the atmosphere looks like" and "what convection it's doing." The output dimension grows from the 65 of Y to 65 plus the 64 of X, so 129. It costs me a wider output and a little skill spent on rebuilding X, but without that reconstruction pressure the latent can solve the regression by tracking only the magnitude of the convective response, which is not the handle on drivers that I wanted.

That handles inspectability. Now the second complaint — the mean regression. The ED is still a deterministic net under MSE, so on the chaotic boundary-layer targets it'll hedge to the conditional mean and squash variance, same disease as the flat net. The bottleneck didn't cure that; it just made the net smaller and readable. What I actually want is for the model to represent a *distribution* over outcomes for a given state, not a single point. And there's a model whose entire job is to learn a smooth low-dimensional latent that parameterizes a distribution: the variational autoencoder. It already has the encoder-decoder-with-bottleneck shape I've built. So let me make the latent *probabilistic* instead of a single deterministic vector, and see if the variational machinery gives me both a generative, distribution-aware model and a better-organized latent for free.

In the deterministic ED, the encoder ends in a linear projection to the code — one vector of, say, five numbers. To go variational, the encoder instead emits, for each latent coordinate, a *mean* μ and a *variance* — and I draw the actual code from that Gaussian. The recognition distribution is q(z|x) = N(μ(x), σ²(x)), diagonal. The decoder then maps a *sample* z up to O as before. Two things have to be settled: how to backpropagate through a random sample, and what objective replaces plain MSE.

The sampling first, because naively it's a wall. If z is literally drawn from N(μ, σ²) inside the forward pass, then z is a stochastic node and there's no pathwise derivative of z with respect to μ and σ — you can't differentiate "draw a sample." The reparameterization trick gets past exactly this: push the randomness into an input-independent noise variable. Write z = μ + σ·ε with ε ~ N(0,1). Now ε carries all the stochasticity, and z is a deterministic, differentiable function of μ, σ, and the (fixed-for-this-pass) noise ε; gradients flow straight through μ and σ. One subtlety I should get right: I'll have the encoder emit not σ or σ² but the *log-variance* lnσ². Why log? Because the network output is unconstrained — it can be any real number — and σ² must be strictly positive; parameterizing the log lets the linear output layer produce anything and I recover σ² = exp(lnσ²) ≥ 0 with no constraint to enforce, no clipping, no risk of a negative variance. And the reparameterization becomes z = μ + exp(½·lnσ²)·ε, since σ = exp(½·lnσ²). The square root of the variance is half the log inside the exponential — easy to get the factor wrong, so: σ = √(σ²) = √(exp(lnσ²)) = exp(½·lnσ²). Good. So the encoder has two linear heads off the last hidden layer, μ and lnσ², both width five, both linearly activated; then sample.

Now the objective. The variational bound says: maximize the expected log-likelihood of the data under the decoder, minus the KL divergence of the recognition distribution from a prior. Maximizing log-likelihood with a Gaussian observation model is, up to constants, minimizing squared error — so the first term is just my reconstruction MSE between O and the predicted O, over the 129 outputs and the batch. The second term is new: D_KL(q(z|x) ‖ p(z)), with the prior p(z) = N(0,I), the standard isotropic Gaussian. This term regularizes the latent — it pulls each posterior toward the unit Gaussian, which is what keeps the latent space smooth and organized rather than letting the encoder scatter codes anywhere. That regularization is, I suspect, going to be the thing that makes the variational latent *more* interpretable than the deterministic ED's: it presses the latent coordinates into a tidy, comparable, near-standardized space, so the regime structure separates cleanly instead of smearing.

I need the KL in closed form so I can actually compute it. Both distributions are Gaussian — posterior N(μ, σ²) diagonal, prior N(0,I) — and for that case the KL integrates analytically. Let me just do it rather than quote it. The KL is the expectation under q of (log q − log p). Take it one latent coordinate at a time, dimensionality J. The two pieces are E_q[log p(z)] and E_q[log q(z)]. For the prior, log N(z; 0, 1) = −½log(2π) − ½z², and E_q[z²] = μ² + σ² (mean of the square is variance plus mean squared), so per coordinate E_q[log p] = −½log(2π) − ½(μ² + σ²); summed, −J/2·log(2π) − ½Σ(μ_j² + σ_j²). For the posterior's own entropy term, log N(z; μ, σ²) = −½log(2π) − ½log σ² − (z−μ)²/(2σ²), and E_q[(z−μ)²] = σ², so that last fraction averages to ½; per coordinate E_q[log q] = −½log(2π) − ½log σ² − ½, summed −J/2·log(2π) − ½Σ(1 + log σ_j²). Now −D_KL = E_q[log p] − E_q[log q] = [−J/2 log2π − ½Σ(μ²+σ²)] − [−J/2 log2π − ½Σ(1 + logσ²)]. The −J/2·log(2π) terms cancel exactly — nice, the 2π constants drop out — and I'm left with −D_KL = ½ Σ_j (1 + log σ_j² − μ_j² − σ_j²). So the KL *loss* I add (a positive quantity to minimize) is its negative: KL = −½ Σ_j (1 + lnσ_j² − μ_j² − σ_j²), and since σ_j² = exp(lnσ_j²) it's a clean function of exactly the two things the encoder emits. Let me sanity-check the sign and the fixed point: if μ = 0 and σ² = 1 — the posterior equals the prior — then 1 + log1 − 0 − 1 = 1 + 0 − 0 − 1 = 0, KL = 0. Correct, the divergence vanishes when posterior matches prior. And it's nonnegative elsewhere, as a divergence must be. Good. So the total loss is the reconstruction MSE plus the batch mean of this KL term.

But I shouldn't just add them with equal weight, because there's a known trap. If the KL term is at full strength from the very first step, the cheapest way for the optimizer to lower it is to drive μ → 0 and σ² → 1 for every input — collapse the posterior onto the prior — which makes the latent carry *no* information about x at all. That's posterior collapse: the decoder learns to ignore a useless code, the encoding is dead, and I've lost the entire point of having a latent. The reconstruction term wants the latent to be informative; the KL term, taken too seriously too early, wants it to be uninformative, and early in training the KL pressure can win before the reconstruction has anything to protect. The standard cure is to anneal: weight the KL by a coefficient λ that starts at zero and ramps up. Let reconstruction establish a real, informative latent first, *then* gradually turn on the regularizer to tidy it. So I write the loss as reconstruction + λ·KL with λ growing from 0 — I'll hold it at 0 for the first couple of epochs while the encoder learns to actually use the code, then ramp λ linearly up to 1 over the next handful of epochs (a step of 1/5 per epoch from epoch two, reaching full strength by epoch seven). λ stays nonnegative throughout; it's a schedule on the strength of the prior pull, nothing more.

Everything else I inherit from the precedent, deliberately, so the comparison is clean. Adam as the optimizer — same as the flat emulator. MSE for the reconstruction. The input normalization is the established recipe: subtract each variable's per-level mean and divide by the range of the anomalies, which keeps tiny upper-level humidities from being divided by near-zero. The output normalization needs more care than I first thought, and it's worth pinning down why: in a quadratic loss the *magnitude* of each output directly sets how much the loss cares about it, so a field that's numerically large will dominate and a field that's numerically small will be ignored. The flat net's output scaling, lifted directly, turns out to make the encoder-decoder fixate on the radiative fluxes and neglect the heating and moistening profiles — the fluxes were simply bigger numbers. So I rescale: normalize the temperature, humidity, and moistening-tendency profiles by a long-term standard deviation taken at the near-surface level, and — this is a small physically-motivated choice — normalize the *temperature*-tendency profile by the standard deviation at the level near 845 hPa, the top of the planetary boundary layer, because that's where convective temperature variability dominates and it gives the right reference scale for dT/dt. The 2-D radiative, precipitation, and surface-pressure fields are standardized. The whole point is to put every field at a comparable order of magnitude so each contributes meaningfully to the loss instead of one swamping the rest.

And now the family is visible, which is the test that I built the right thing rather than a one-off. Turn the KL off and replace the probabilistic latent with a single linear projection, and the variational encoder-decoder collapses back to the deterministic ED — the μ and lnσ² heads disappear, the sampling disappears, the loss is reconstruction-only. So ED is the no-regularization special case. Take the ED and make all activations linear, and it collapses further to a linear-subspace-projection-plus-regression — principal-component regression with a non-orthogonal latent, my linear baseline. And let the bottleneck width grow toward the input dimension and the whole thing should approach the flat reference net, because a wide enough "bottleneck" is no bottleneck. One construction, and the deterministic encoder-decoder, the linear reduced model, and the flat emulator all fall out as corners of it. That's the coherence I wanted: the variational model is the general object, ED is it with the variational and regularization machinery stripped, and the prior approaches are further degenerations.

A few words on the bottleneck width itself, since it's the instrument. I need the code to be genuinely small, not just a slightly narrower hidden layer, because otherwise I have not tested the reduced-complexity premise at all. But I also need enough coordinates that different physical factors do not have to collapse onto one overloaded scalar. Five is the practical compromise I can defend before looking at any diagnostic plots: small enough to inspect coordinate by coordinate, large enough to let large-scale temperature/humidity structure, radiative forcing, and convective intensity occupy separable directions if the data support that separation. So I fix the latent at five. If a five-coordinate code can hold the information needed by the decoder, then the low-dimensional premise has been put under real pressure; if it cannot, the reconstruction loss will expose that immediately.

Let me write it as the model I'd actually train, filling the empty architecture slot in the harness. The canonical build is in Keras' functional style — encoder, decoder, and either the deterministic join (ED) or the variational join with reparameterization, KL, and annealing (VED). First the deterministic ED, the plain encoder-decoder:

```python
import numpy as np
from tensorflow.keras.layers import Input, Dense
from tensorflow.keras.models import Model
from tensorflow.keras.losses import mse
from tensorflow.keras.callbacks import LearningRateScheduler
import tensorflow as tf

original_dim_input  = 64          # large-scale state X: q(p), T(p) profiles + 4 scalars
original_dim_output = 65 + 64     # O = [Y (sub-grid tendencies) ; X-reconstruction] = 129
intermediate_dim = 463            # first/last hidden width; the funnel halves from here
latent_dim = 5                    # the bottleneck = effective dimensionality of the problem

 # Encoder: 64 -> 463 -> 463 -> 232 -> 116 -> 58 -> 29 -> (linear) 5
inputs = Input(shape=(original_dim_input,), name='encoder_input')
x = Dense(intermediate_dim, activation='relu')(inputs)
x = Dense(intermediate_dim, activation='relu')(x)
x = Dense(int(np.round(intermediate_dim / 2)),  activation='relu')(x)   # 232
x = Dense(int(np.round(intermediate_dim / 4)),  activation='relu')(x)   # 116
x = Dense(int(np.round(intermediate_dim / 8)),  activation='relu')(x)   # 58
x = Dense(int(np.round(intermediate_dim / 16)), activation='relu')(x)   # 29
code = Dense(latent_dim, name='encoder_output')(x)                      # linear projection to 5
encoder = Model(inputs, code, name='encoder')

 # Decoder: 5 -> 29 -> 58 -> 116 -> 232 -> 463 -> 463 -> (ELU) 129  (mirror of the encoder)
dec_in = Input(shape=(latent_dim,), name='decoder_input')
y = Dense(int(np.round(intermediate_dim / 16)), activation='relu')(dec_in)  # 29
y = Dense(int(np.round(intermediate_dim / 8)),  activation='relu')(y)       # 58
y = Dense(int(np.round(intermediate_dim / 4)),  activation='relu')(y)       # 116
y = Dense(int(np.round(intermediate_dim / 2)),  activation='relu')(y)       # 232
y = Dense(intermediate_dim, activation='relu')(y)
y = Dense(intermediate_dim, activation='relu')(y)
outputs = Dense(original_dim_output, activation='elu')(y)   # signed real outputs -> ELU
decoder = Model(dec_in, outputs, name='decoder')
```

And the variational version is the same backbone with the probabilistic latent bolted on — two heads for μ and lnσ², the reparameterized sample, the closed-form KL added as a loss, and the KL annealing:

```python
from tensorflow.keras.layers import Lambda
from tensorflow.keras import backend as K
from tensorflow.keras.callbacks import Callback

def sampling(args):                       # reparameterization: z = mu + exp(0.5*log_var)*eps
    z_mean, z_log_var = args
    eps = K.random_normal(shape=(K.shape(z_mean)[0], K.int_shape(z_mean)[1]))  # eps ~ N(0, I)
    return z_mean + K.exp(0.5 * z_log_var) * eps                               # sigma = exp(.5*logvar)

 # --- encoder backbone identical to the ED above, ending in hidden features `x` ---
z_mean    = Dense(latent_dim, name='z_mean')(x)        # linear head: posterior mean
z_log_var = Dense(latent_dim, name='z_log_var')(x)     # linear head: posterior log-variance
z = Lambda(sampling, name='z')([z_mean, z_log_var])    # differentiable sample
encoder = Model(inputs, [z_mean, z_log_var, z], name='encoder')

 # --- decoder backbone identical to the ED above (5 -> ... -> 129, ELU output) ---
emul = decoder(encoder(inputs)[2])                      # decode the sampled code

 # closed-form Gaussian KL:  KL = -0.5 * sum(1 + log_var - mu^2 - exp(log_var))
kl_loss = -0.5 * K.sum(1 + z_log_var - K.square(z_mean) - K.exp(z_log_var), axis=-1)

weight = K.variable(0.0)                                # KL annealing coefficient lambda, starts 0
VED = Model(inputs, emul)
VED.add_loss(K.mean(kl_loss * weight))                  # total = mse reconstruction + lambda*KL
VED.add_metric(kl_loss, name='kl_loss', aggregation='mean')

klstart, kl_annealtime = 2, 5                           # lambda: 0 until epoch 2, then +1/5 to 1
class AnnealingCallback(Callback):
    def __init__(self, weight):
        self.weight = weight

    def on_epoch_end(self, epoch, logs=None):
        if epoch > klstart:
            K.set_value(self.weight, min(K.get_value(self.weight) + 1.0 / kl_annealtime, 1.0))

encoder.load_weights('./saved_models/VAE_climate_encoding/encoder_JAS.h5')
VED.compile(tf.keras.optimizers.Adam(lr=1e-4), loss=mse, metrics=['mse'])  # mse = reconstruction
VED.fit(train_gen, validation_data=(val_gen, None), epochs=40,
        callbacks=[LearningRateScheduler(schedule), AnnealingCallback(weight)])
```

Let me retrace the chain so I'm sure each step was forced, not decorative. I started with a flat deep emulator that fits the convection map well but is opaque — half a million unstructured weights, no compact handle — and, being deterministic under MSE, regresses to the mean and squashes the variability of stochastic boundary-layer convection. Wanting an interpretable low-dimensional summary, I reached for dimensionality reduction; PCA is interpretable but linear and underfits the nonlinear map, so I went to its nonlinear generalization, an autoencoder bottleneck, and noticed that stripping the nonlinearity recovers PCA exactly, which told me the family was right. Since my problem maps across domains, I put the bottleneck in the middle of an X-to-Y encoder-decoder, tapering the layers in a geometric halving funnel to a five-node code and mirroring on the way out, ReLU through the hidden layers and ELU on the output because the targets are signed reals that a ReLU output would clip. I caught that an X-to-Y code would only encode the convective response and not the large-scale drivers, so I made the decoder reconstruct the input alongside the target — output [Y ; X], dimension 129 — which is what forces the latent to be an interpretable joint map of climate state and convection. That gave me the deterministic ED. To also fix the mean regression, I made the latent probabilistic: the encoder emits μ and lnσ² (log for unconstrained positivity), the reparameterization trick z = μ + exp(½lnσ²)·ε lets gradients flow through the sample, the objective becomes reconstruction MSE plus the KL of the posterior from an N(0,I) prior, which I derived in closed form to −½Σ(1+lnσ²−μ²−σ²) — the 2π constants cancelling, the divergence vanishing exactly when posterior meets prior — and I anneal the KL weight up from zero to dodge posterior collapse, so the code becomes informative first and tidy second. The KL regularization also gives the latent a common prior geometry, which is exactly what I want if I am going to inspect and traverse it. And the whole construction has the deterministic ED as its KL-off special case, the linear reduced model as the all-linear corner, and the flat emulator as the wide-bottleneck limit — one object, with the prior methods sitting inside it.
