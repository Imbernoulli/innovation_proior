Let me start from what actually hurts. I have a stochastic policy `pi_theta(a|s)` — a neural net with millions of parameters — and I want to push up its expected return `eta(theta) = E[sum_t gamma^t r_t]`. The only handle I have is the policy gradient: `g_hat = E_t[ grad_theta log pi_theta(a_t|s_t) A_hat_t ]`, the score function times an advantage estimate, averaged over a batch of trajectories. I take the advantage estimator as given — somebody hands me `A_hat_t`, a variance-reduced signal of how much better action `a_t` was than the policy's average at `s_t`. The thing I control is what I *do* with `(old_log_prob, log_prob, advantage)` once I have a batch. And the thing that hurts is sample cost. Every trajectory is expensive — environment steps, or for a language model an entire generation scored by a reward model. So after I pay for a batch, I want to wring multiple gradient steps out of it, not one. That single desire — reuse the batch — is where all the trouble lives.

Here is why it's trouble. The estimator `E_t[...]` is an empirical average over samples drawn from *one specific policy*: the policy that collected the batch, call it `pi_old`. The expectation `eta` is over trajectories of the *current* policy. The instant I take a gradient step, `theta` moves, `pi_theta` is no longer `pi_old`, and my batch is stale — it was sampled from the wrong distribution. If I just keep ascending `L^PG = E_t[ log pi_theta(a_t|s_t) A_hat_t ]` on the same trajectories epoch after epoch, nothing in that objective knows the policy has drifted. And it's documented what happens: a few epochs on the same batch with the plain log-prob-weighted objective give destructively large policy updates. The batch carries real information, but `L^PG` has no notion of "you've now walked far enough from the data-collecting policy that this information is no longer valid." That's the wall I have to get past: how do I reuse a batch for several SGD steps while keeping the policy inside the region where the batch still tells me the truth.

So let me get honest about *what* the batch tells me as `theta` moves. The samples are from `pi_old`; I want to evaluate the new policy `pi_theta`. That's a textbook importance-sampling situation. The expected advantage of the new policy, measured on states the old policy visited, is `E_{a ~ pi_theta}[A] = E_{a ~ pi_old}[ (pi_theta(a|s)/pi_old(a|s)) A ]` — reweight each sampled action by the ratio of how likely the new policy is to take it versus the old. Define `r_t(theta) = pi_theta(a_t|s_t) / pi_old(a_t|s_t)`. Then the quantity I can estimate from the frozen batch, with no resampling, is `L^CPI(theta) = E_t[ r_t(theta) A_hat_t ]`. At `theta = theta_old` every `r_t = 1` and this is just the average advantage, zero in expectation against the baseline; as `theta` moves, `r_t` tilts the weight toward actions the new policy now prefers, and if those actions had positive advantage the surrogate goes up. This is the right object: it's an honest off-policy estimate of "how good is the new policy" built entirely from old samples. And differentiating it at `r = 1` recovers exactly the policy gradient — `grad r_t * A = (pi_theta/pi_old) grad log pi_theta * A`, which at `theta_old` is `grad log pi_theta * A`. So `L^CPI` is the policy gradient's surrogate, valid for multiple steps in principle, because it's written as a function of `theta` I can keep differentiating without new data.

But I need to be careful about *how far* this surrogate is trustworthy, because that's the whole game. The conservative-policy-iteration analysis nails it. There, you study the mixture update `pi_new = (1-alpha) pi + alpha pi'` and the policy advantage `A_{pi,mu}(pi') = E_{s ~ d_{pi,mu}} E_{a ~ pi'}[A_pi(s,a)]`, and you find the return moves, to first order in the step, as `d eta_mu / d alpha |_{alpha=0} = (1/(1-gamma)) A_{pi,mu}(pi')`. The surrogate is the *linearization* of the true return around the current policy. And the guarantee comes with a penalty: `eta_mu(pi_new) - eta_mu(pi) >= (alpha/(1-gamma)) ( A - 2 alpha gamma eps / (1 - gamma(1-alpha)) )`, where `eps` is the largest expected advantage magnitude. Stare at that penalty: it's `O(alpha^2)`, and it grows with the step. So `L^CPI` overestimates the true improvement, and the overestimate scales with how far you move. Maximizing `L^CPI` with no leash is therefore exactly the destructive-update failure I already saw, now explained: the surrogate keeps promising improvement that the true return won't deliver once the step is large, and an unconstrained optimizer happily chases that phantom by driving `r_t` as far from 1 as it can. The information in the batch is local; the optimizer is global; that mismatch is the disease.

The clean cure already exists, and I should understand precisely why it's right and where it stops being usable. Keep the surrogate `E_t[ r_t A_hat_t ]` but only optimize it inside a region where it stays trustworthy — a trust region measured by how far the new policy has moved from the old one. The natural ruler for "moved" is the KL divergence `E_t[ KL(pi_old(.|s_t), pi_theta(.|s_t)) ]`, because the theory says a surrogate that subtracts a multiple of the max-KL is a genuine lower bound on the return — KL is the currency in which "you've gone too far" is denominated. So: maximize `E_t[ r_t A_hat_t ]` subject to `E_t[ KL(pi_old, pi_theta) ] <= delta`. This is correct and reliable. But look at what it costs to actually solve. The constrained problem is handled by linearizing the objective, taking a quadratic (Fisher) approximation of the KL constraint, and using conjugate gradient to apply the inverse-Fisher direction without forming the Fisher matrix. That's second-order machinery — Fisher-vector products, a CG loop, a line search — per update. Three things break for me. It's complicated to implement and slow. It assumes a clean curvature estimate, which dropout and other injected noise wreck, because the Fisher-vector product is computed through a network whose effective function is randomized every forward pass. And it doesn't play nicely with sharing parameters between the policy and a value head — the curvature object isn't clean anymore. On top of all that, it's a single constrained solve per batch, not the many cheap minibatch-SGD epochs I want. So the *idea* — surrogate plus a leash — is right, but the *implementation* of the leash via a hard KL constraint and second-order solve is the wrong tool for my constraints. Wall.

The obvious thing to try next is to soften the constraint into a penalty I can just plug into plain SGD: maximize `E_t[ r_t A_hat_t - beta KL(pi_old, pi_theta) ]`. Now it's an unconstrained objective, first-order, multi-epoch-friendly. Let me think about whether `beta` can be a fixed number. The penalty form follows from the same lower-bound theory — there *exists* a `beta` for which this is a valid bound — but "exists" is per-problem and per-iterate. If I fix `beta`, then early in training when advantages are large and the landscape is steep, a given `beta` barely restrains the update; later, near convergence, the same `beta` is far too strong and freezes learning. And the right `beta` for one environment is wrong for another. I could adapt it — measure the realized KL each update, halve `beta` when the KL came in well under a target, double it when it overshot. That helps, and it's worth keeping as a fallback. But it's a controller chasing a moving target: it lags, it occasionally lets an update land far past the target KL before it reacts, and it adds a coefficient and a schedule to tune. I wanted *fewer* knobs and *more* robustness, and a fixed `beta` doesn't emulate the reliable improvement of the hard constraint while an adaptive `beta` reintroduces a tuning loop. So the penalty-on-KL route gives me the first-order, multi-epoch property but loses the reliability. I keep circling the same tension: the constraint is reliable but heavy, the penalty is light but unreliable.

Let me step back and ask what the KL leash is actually *for*, because maybe I'm overpaying for it. What goes wrong without a leash is specific: the optimizer drives `r_t` far from 1 to chase surrogate improvement that won't materialize. The damage is mediated entirely through `r_t` getting large (or small). KL is a smooth, global measure of that drift; but the actual mechanism I need to defeat is local and per-sample — *this* token's ratio running away. What if I attack the ratio directly, in the objective itself, rather than constraining a global summary of it? Here's the situation I want to make impossible: a sample with positive advantage where the optimizer keeps pushing `r_t` up past, say, 1.2, harvesting more and more surrogate value `r_t A` from a single stale sample it has no right to trust that much. The fix that suggests itself: stop rewarding the optimizer for pushing `r_t` beyond some band around 1. Clip the ratio. Define a half-width `eps` (say 0.2) and consider `clip(r_t, 1-eps, 1+eps)` — the ratio pinned to `[1-eps, 1+eps]`. If I use the *clipped* ratio in the surrogate, then once `r_t` leaves the band the term flatlines: its gradient with respect to `theta` is zero out there, so there is simply no incentive to move `r_t` any further. That's a per-sample, first-order, dropout-friendly leash — no Fisher, no CG, no global KL, just a clamp on each token's ratio.

But I have to be careful, because a naive clip is exploitable and I can feel the trap. Suppose I just used `clip(r_t, 1-eps, 1+eps) A_t` everywhere. Consider a positive-advantage sample. While `r_t` is inside the band the objective is `r_t A`, fine; once `r_t > 1+eps` it's `(1+eps)A`, capped, also fine — that's exactly the "no reward for going further" I wanted. But now consider that the optimizer might first push `r_t` *down* below `1-eps` (maybe other samples in the minibatch pull it there), where the clipped term is `(1-eps)A`, still positive but smaller; the clip on the low side here is doing nothing useful — it's *removing* a penalty the optimizer should feel for having moved a good action's probability the wrong way. The point of the surrogate is that moving a positive-advantage action's probability *down* should *hurt*; clipping the low side hides that. So a symmetric clip applied to both sides is wrong: I want to clip away the *incentive to over-improve* but keep the full *penalty for getting worse*. The asymmetry depends on the sign of the advantage, and I need to bake that in.

The clean way to bake it in is to take the worse of the clipped and unclipped surrogate — a pessimistic bound. Form both `r_t A_t` and `clip(r_t, 1-eps, 1+eps) A_t`, and take the *minimum*: `L^CLIP = E_t[ min( r_t A_t, clip(r_t, 1-eps, 1+eps) A_t ) ]`. I claimed this gives the asymmetry I wanted, but I derived that asymmetry by hand and I don't trust hand-derived sign arguments — the sign of `A_t` flips which term is the minimum, and it's easy to talk myself into the wrong branch. So let me actually evaluate `min( r A, clip(r,0.8,1.2) A )` at `eps = 0.2` for both signs of `A` and a spread of ratios, and read off whether it does what I want.

Take `A = +2` (a good action). Walking `r` from below 1 to far above:

```
r=0.5 : min(1.0, 1.0)  = +1.0    (clip floors r at 0.8, but 0.8*2=1.6 > 1.0, so min picks unclipped 1.0)
r=0.8 : min(1.6, 1.6)  = +1.6
r=1.0 : min(2.0, 2.0)  = +2.0
r=1.2 : min(2.4, 2.4)  = +2.4
r=1.5 : min(3.0, 2.4)  = +2.4   <- clipped term wins
r=5.0 : min(10.0, 2.4) = +2.4   <- still 2.4
```

Read it: for `r < 1` the objective tracks the *unclipped* `r A`, so a good action whose probability got pushed *down* (`r=0.5` gives `+1.0`, well below the `+2.0` at `r=1`) is fully penalized — exactly the downside I wanted to keep. For `r > 1+eps` the objective flatlines at `+2.4 = (1+eps)A` no matter how large `r` gets — over-improving a good action from one stale sample stops paying. So for `A>0` this is `min(r, 1+eps) A`, full downside, ceilinged upside. Confirmed numerically, not just argued.

Now `A = -2` (a bad action):

```
r=0.05: min(-0.1, -1.6)  = -1.6   (clip floors r at 0.8; -1.6 < -0.1, so min picks clipped -1.6)
r=0.5 : min(-1.0, -1.6)  = -1.6
r=0.8 : min(-1.6, -1.6)  = -1.6
r=1.0 : min(-2.0, -2.0)  = -2.0
r=1.2 : min(-2.4, -2.4)  = -2.4
r=5.0 : min(-10.0, -10.0)= -10.0  <- clip does nothing on the high side!
```

Read it: for `r > 1` the objective tracks the *unclipped* `r A`, so a bad action whose probability got pushed *up* (`r=1.2` gives `-2.4`, below the `-2.0` at `r=1`) is fully penalized — the upside penalty I wanted. For `r < 1-eps` it floors at `-1.6 = (1-eps)A` — over-suppressing a bad action stops paying extra. So for `A<0` this is `max(r, 1-eps) A`: full upside penalty, floored downside. Both signs now check out against numbers: the single `min` ceilings over-improvement and keeps the full penalty for making things worse, asymmetrically, with no sign flag — the advantage's own sign does the routing.

And it's a *lower bound* on the unclipped surrogate `L^CPI` — `min(r A, clip(r) A) <= r A` by construction — which is the right sign: I only ever ignore a ratio change when ignoring it makes the objective look *worse*, never better, so I can never over-estimate improvement, which is the failure mode the conservative-policy-iteration penalty warned about.

But the `A = -2`, `r=5.0` row is a red flag I can't ignore: the objective there is `-10.0`, and the table shows the clip term `max(r,1-eps)A = r A` does *nothing* on the high-ratio side for negative advantages. I'll come back to that.

Notice what `eps` *is* now: it's the half-width of a no-incentive band around `r = 1`. It plays the role the KL `delta` played, but per-sample and built into the gradient rather than enforced by a solver. I'll set `eps = 0.2` as the working default — wide enough that each step makes real progress, tight enough that a single stale sample can't be milked for unbounded surrogate value. (I'd want to sweep it; `0.1`, `0.2`, `0.3` are the natural grid, and `0.2` is the value I'll carry forward.)

There's one property I want to be sure I haven't broken: at the *start* of each batch every `r_t = 1`, and I need the gradient there to still be the policy gradient — if the clip distorted the very first step, I'd be optimizing something other than the return to first order. Since `r=1` sits strictly inside the band `[0.8, 1.2]`, the clip is inactive at that point and `L^CLIP = L^CPI` locally, so the derivative should be unchanged. Let me confirm it numerically rather than trust the "strictly inside the band" hand-wave, by central-differencing `d(objective)/dr` at `r=1`: for `A=+2` the finite difference gives `(obj(1+h) - obj(1-h)) / 2h = +2.0000`, and for `A=-2` it gives `-2.0000`. Both equal `A`, which is exactly the `L^CPI` gradient `d(rA)/dr = A` at `r=1`. So the clip leaves the first-order behavior untouched and only bends the surrogate as the policy drifts away from `r=1`, capping it exactly where it would otherwise lie.

I should be honest that clipping alone does not *bound* the KL. The clip zeroes the gradient of the over-improvement direction, but the policy can still move — other samples push it, and the unclipped branch on the "getting worse" side still has gradient. So the realized KL per batch is *restrained* but not *capped*; it's possible to still drift further than I'd like over several epochs. The practical patch is cheap: monitor the mean KL, and if it grows past a threshold, stop taking gradient steps on this batch early. So I'll want a KL diagnostic computed alongside the loss. The approximate KL I can read straight off the log-prob difference: `pi_old` and `pi_theta` log-probs are both in hand, so `log_prob - old_log_prob` is the per-sample log-ratio, and `E_t[ old_log_prob - log_prob ] = E_t[ -(log_prob - old_log_prob) ]` is a cheap one-sample estimate of `KL(pi_old, pi_theta)` (it's the `k1` estimator — unbiased for the KL in expectation over `pi_old` samples). I'll emit that as `ppo_kl`. And I'll emit the fraction of samples where the clip was active — `clipfrac` — as a second diagnostic of how hard the leash is biting.

That `A = -2`, `r=5.0 -> -10.0` row is the corner the simple two-term `min` doesn't cover, and it bites hard in very off-policy, heavily distributed training. The clipped objective for `A_t < 0` is `max(r_t, 1-eps) A`. The clip protects the *low* side of `r_t` (over-suppressing a bad action), but the table showed the *high* side is wide open: when `r_t` becomes very large — which happens when the old policy assigned a tiny probability to a sampled action and the current policy now assigns a much larger one, easy when the batch is many steps stale — then `max(r_t, 1-eps) A = r_t A`, and with `A < 0` that's a large *negative* surrogate, i.e. a large *positive loss* proportional to `r_t |A|`. At `r=5` the objective was `-10`; nothing in the two-sided clip stops it from being `-100` at `r=50`. A handful of stale, originally-low-probability, negative-advantage samples can then dominate the whole minibatch loss and blow up the gradient. So I add a third, hard floor on the objective for negative-advantage samples: never let the per-sample objective drop below `c * A_t` for some constant `c > 1`. Concretely, for `A_t < 0`, the objective becomes `max( min(r_t A_t, clip(r_t,1-eps,1+eps) A_t), c A_t )`.

Before trusting that, let me re-run the same `A = -2` sweep with the floor `c A_t = -6` (taking `c = 3`) added, and check two things at once: that it caps the blow-up *and* that it doesn't quietly clobber the well-behaved rows:

```
r=0.05: max(-1.6,  -6) = -1.6    floor inactive (over-suppression side untouched)
r=0.5 : max(-1.6,  -6) = -1.6    floor inactive
r=1.0 : max(-2.0,  -6) = -2.0    floor inactive
r=1.2 : max(-2.4,  -6) = -2.4    floor inactive
r=5.0 : max(-10.0, -6) = -6.0    floor ENGAGES (was -10.0, now capped)
r=50.0: max(-100., -6) = -6.0    floor holds at -6.0
```

So the floor caps the per-sample objective at `c A_t = -6` (a loss of `c|A_t| = 6`) and the `r=50` row confirms it stays there however large `r` grows — the runaway is bounded. Just as important, the floor leaves the small-`r` and in-band rows exactly as they were (`-1.6`, `-2.0`, `-2.4` unchanged): it only catches the high-ratio blow-up, not the over-suppression side the original clip already handled. I'll take `c = 3` (any `c > 1` works as a multiplier; with `c=3` the floor `-6` sits well below the `-2.4` that an in-band step produces, so it only engages on genuine blow-ups like the `r=5`, `r=50` rows). For `A_t > 0` I leave this dual floor off — the positive-advantage sweep already flatlined at `+2.4 = (1+eps)A` for every large `r`, so that quadrant is already bounded by the upper clip and needs no floor.

Let me now translate all of this into the per-step tensor operations I'd actually run, and here I have to be deliberate about sign conventions, because the optimizer minimizes a *loss* while everything above is phrased as an *objective* to maximize. The loss is the negative objective: `loss = -objective`, and `min` over objectives becomes `max` over losses, `max` over objectives becomes `min` over losses. I'll carry the negation through carefully.

First the ratio, with a numerical guard. The log-ratio is `negative_approx_kl = log_prob - old_log_prob` (named for the KL diagnostic: its mean negated is the KL estimate). Before exponentiating I clamp it to `[-20, 20]` — `exp` of a large log-ratio overflows, and a single pathological token (e.g. one the current policy now finds essentially certain or essentially impossible) would otherwise produce `inf`/`0` and poison the batch; clamping to `[-20, 20]` caps `r_t` to roughly `[2e-9, 5e8]`, far outside any band that matters, so it never distorts a real update, only defuses the overflow. Then `ratio = exp(negative_approx_kl)`, and `ppo_kl = masked_mean(-negative_approx_kl, response_mask)` is my KL diagnostic over the valid (non-padding) tokens.

Now the two surrogate terms, as losses (negated). The unclipped loss is `pg_losses1 = -advantages * ratio` (negative of `r A`). The clipped loss is `pg_losses2 = -advantages * clamp(ratio, 1-eps_low, 1+eps_high)` (negative of `clip(r) A`). I'll allow the band to be asymmetric — separate `eps_low` and `eps_high`, each defaulting to `eps` if not set — because there's no reason the trust region has to be symmetric and a decoupled lower/upper width is a free generalization. The pessimistic combine that was `min` over the objective becomes `max` over the loss: `clip_pg_losses1 = maximum(pg_losses1, pg_losses2)`. Sign flips like this are where I make mistakes, so rather than sanity-check one case verbally I'll just compute the whole loss recipe — `pg_losses1`, `pg_losses2`, `max`, then the floor `pg_losses3 = -c A` with `min`, then `where(A<0, ...)` — and check it equals `-objective` for every `(A, r)` in the two tables above. Running it: for `A=+2` the recipe gives losses `-1.0, -1.6, -2.0, -2.4, -2.4, -2.4` at `r = 0.5, 0.8, 1.0, 1.2, 1.5, 5.0` — exactly the negatives of the objective column I tabulated. For `A=-2` it gives `1.6, 1.6, 1.6, 2.0, 2.4, 6.0, 6.0` at `r = 0.05, 0.5, 0.8, 1.0, 1.2, 5.0, 50.0` — again the negatives of the (dual-floored) objective, including the `6.0` cap at the blow-up rows. So `loss = -objective` holds across every case, which means the `max`/`min` directions and the `where` branch are all oriented correctly. The clip-fraction diagnostic: the clip was active wherever the clipped loss exceeded the unclipped loss, `pg_clipfrac = masked_mean( (pg_losses2 > pg_losses1).float(), response_mask )`.

Then the dual floor. The objective floor `c A` (for `A<0`) becomes, as a loss, a *ceiling* `-c A = c|A|`. So `pg_losses3 = -advantages * c` (which is `c|A|` when `A<0`, a positive number), and the floored loss is `clip_pg_losses2 = minimum(pg_losses3, clip_pg_losses1)` — `min` over losses, because `max` over the objective is `min` over the loss, capping the loss at `c|A|`. The lower-clip diagnostic counts where this floor engaged on a negative-advantage token: `pg_clipfrac_lower = masked_mean((clip_pg_losses1 > pg_losses3) * (advantages < 0).float(), response_mask)`. And the dual floor only applies for negative advantages, so I select it conditionally: `pg_losses = where(advantages < 0, clip_pg_losses2, clip_pg_losses1)` — for positive advantages keep the plain pessimistic loss (already ceilinged on the upside), for negative advantages use the dual-floored one.

One more hook I'll leave in: if the rollouts were generated by a *third* distribution (an inference engine whose numerics differ from the training forward pass), I may be handed per-token correction weights `rollout_is_weights`; if present I multiply them onto `pg_losses` before aggregation, since they're an outer importance correction orthogonal to the clipping. Then aggregate over the valid tokens. The aggregation is the piece that decides the *granularity* of the whole method: I use `agg_loss(..., loss_agg_mode="token-mean")`, which sums the per-token losses over the mask and divides by the token count — every valid token contributes its own clipped ratio and its own clip decision, equally weighted. This is the per-token reading of the method: one importance ratio per token, one clip per token, averaged across tokens. (Aggregating instead by collapsing each sequence to a single scalar ratio would be a different granularity entirely; here the granularity is the token.)

Let me write the slot exactly as I'd ship it, filling the one empty function in the training loop — the map from `(old_log_prob, log_prob, advantages)` to a scalar loss plus diagnostics:

```python
from typing import Any, Optional
import torch


def compute_policy_loss_vanilla(
    old_log_prob: torch.Tensor,      # (bs, length) log pi_old(a) at collection time
    log_prob: torch.Tensor,          # (bs, length) log pi_theta(a) now
    advantages: torch.Tensor,        # (bs, length) advantage estimate (given)
    response_mask: torch.Tensor,     # (bs, length) 1 for valid tokens, 0 for padding
    loss_agg_mode: str = "token-mean",
    config: Optional[Any] = None,     # holds clip_ratio, optional asymmetric widths and dual floor
    rollout_is_weights: Optional[torch.Tensor] = None,
) -> tuple[torch.Tensor, dict[str, Any]]:
    assert config is not None
    clip_ratio = config.clip_ratio                              # eps; trust-region half-width
    clip_ratio_low = config.clip_ratio_low if config.clip_ratio_low is not None else clip_ratio
    clip_ratio_high = config.clip_ratio_high if config.clip_ratio_high is not None else clip_ratio
    clip_ratio_c = config.get("clip_ratio_c", 3.0)             # c > 1: dual floor for A < 0
    assert clip_ratio_c > 1.0

    # per-token log-ratio; clamp before exp so one runaway token can't overflow the batch
    negative_approx_kl = log_prob - old_log_prob
    negative_approx_kl = torch.clamp(negative_approx_kl, min=-20.0, max=20.0)
    ratio = torch.exp(negative_approx_kl)                       # r_t = pi_theta / pi_old, per token
    ppo_kl = masked_mean(-negative_approx_kl, response_mask)    # k1 KL(pi_old, pi_theta) diagnostic

    # surrogate as a LOSS (= -objective): unclipped and ratio-clipped terms
    pg_losses1 = -advantages * ratio                                              # -(r * A)
    pg_losses2 = -advantages * torch.clamp(ratio, 1 - clip_ratio_low, 1 + clip_ratio_high)  # -(clip(r) * A)
    # pessimistic combine: min over objective  ==  max over loss
    clip_pg_losses1 = torch.maximum(pg_losses1, pg_losses2)
    pg_clipfrac = masked_mean(torch.gt(pg_losses2, pg_losses1).float(), response_mask)

    # dual floor for A < 0: cap the loss at c*|A| so a huge r_t with A<0 can't explode
    pg_losses3 = -advantages * clip_ratio_c                                       # c*|A| when A<0
    clip_pg_losses2 = torch.min(pg_losses3, clip_pg_losses1)                      # ceiling the loss
    pg_clipfrac_lower = masked_mean(
        torch.gt(clip_pg_losses1, pg_losses3) * (advantages < 0).float(), response_mask
    )
    # apply dual floor only where the advantage is negative
    pg_losses = torch.where(advantages < 0, clip_pg_losses2, clip_pg_losses1)

    if rollout_is_weights is not None:                          # outer (rollout-vs-train) IS correction
        pg_losses = pg_losses * rollout_is_weights

    # token-mean aggregation: each valid token contributes its own clipped ratio, equally weighted
    pg_loss = agg_loss(
        loss_mat=pg_losses, loss_mask=response_mask,
        loss_agg_mode=loss_agg_mode, **config.global_batch_info,
    )
    return pg_loss, {
        "actor/pg_clipfrac": pg_clipfrac.detach().item(),
        "actor/ppo_kl": ppo_kl.detach().item(),
```

Let me retrace the causal chain so I'm sure it holds together. I want to reuse an expensive rollout batch for several first-order gradient steps, but the plain policy-gradient objective has no awareness of policy drift, so reusing it gives destructive updates. Importance sampling turns the frozen old-policy batch into an off-policy estimate of the new policy's expected advantage, `L^CPI = E_t[r_t A_t]`, which I can keep differentiating — but the conservative-policy-iteration analysis shows this surrogate is only a *local* model of the return, overestimating improvement with an `O(step^2)` penalty, so an unconstrained optimizer chases phantom gains by driving `r_t` away from 1. The reliable fix — constrain the KL drift — is right in spirit but its hard-constraint, second-order implementation is heavy, dropout-incompatible, parameter-sharing-incompatible, and not multi-epoch-SGD-friendly; and softening it to a fixed KL penalty loses the reliability while an adaptive penalty reintroduces tuning. So I attack the drift where it actually does damage, per-sample through the ratio: clip `r_t` to `[1-eps, 1+eps]` and take the *minimum* of clipped and unclipped surrogate, which gives a pessimistic lower bound that, by the sign of `A_t`, ceilings over-improvement of good actions and keeps the full penalty for making good actions worse (and symmetrically for bad actions) — a first-order, dropout-friendly, per-token leash with `eps` playing the role of the trust-region width. Clipping restrains but does not cap KL, so I emit a cheap `k1` KL and a clip-fraction for early-stopping. Finally, the negative-advantage / large-ratio quadrant slips past the two-sided clip, so a dual floor `c*A` (`c>1`) caps that loss and stops a few stale low-probability samples from exploding the gradient. Negating objective into loss turns the `min` into a `maximum` and the floor into a `minimum`, the advantage sign selects whether the dual floor applies, and token-mean aggregation makes the whole thing per-token: one ratio, one clip, one decision per token, averaged. First-order only, a handful of elementwise ops, reusable across epochs — this is the set of constraints the heavier trust-region route does not meet.
