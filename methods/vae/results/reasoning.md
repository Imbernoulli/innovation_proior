Let me think this through from scratch. I have a very concrete wish: I want a directed generative model that first draws a continuous latent `z ~ p(z)`, then draws an observation `x` from a conditional likelihood `p(x|z)` — and I want `p(x|z)` to be an arbitrarily flexible function, concretely a neural network with nonlinear hidden layers, because only then is it expressive enough to bend itself around the curved manifolds real data lives on. The linear-Gaussian model is of course solvable: Roweis & Ghahramani (1998/99) showed PCA is exactly the maximum-likelihood solution of a special case with `p(z)=N(0,I)`, `p(x|z)=N(Wz, εI)`, `ε→0`. That lineage is beautiful — it says a "linear autoencoder" and a "generative latent-variable model" were the same object all along. But linear is far too weak; it can't carry the structure in real images. The trouble is that the moment I swap `p(x|z)` for a nonlinear network, the whole thing falls apart. Let me first get clear on *why* it falls apart, before I think about how to rescue it — otherwise I can't even name which integral I'm trying to solve.

The marginal likelihood is `p(x) = ∫ p(z) p(x|z) dz`. If `p(x|z)` is a nonlinear network, the integrand is "a Gaussian prior times the likelihood spat out by a neural net," and this integral has no closed form: I can neither evaluate it nor differentiate it. The posterior `p(z|x) = p(x|z)p(z)/p(x)` therefore has no closed form either — its denominator *is* that uncomputable normalizing integral, so the two intractabilities are welded together. That's the core pain: the marginal is intractable, the posterior is intractable, and because they share the same denominator, cracking one cracks the other. On top of that comes a second constraint: `N` is large, I can't run over the full dataset each step; I want to update parameters on a small minibatch, even a single point. Any method that runs an inner optimization loop or a sampling chain *per datapoint* dies at this scale — and just the bare fact that "every new test point needs its posterior re-inferred from scratch" blocks both online learning and test-time inference at once.

So what tools do I have? Let me weigh them one at a time and see exactly where each one hits a wall.

The most classical is EM. The lovely thing about EM is an identity that holds for *any* distribution `q(z)`, and every road below stands on it, so I derive it once here. Start from the definition of KL: `KL(q || p(z|x)) = E_q[log q(z) - log p(z|x)]`. Substitute `log p(z|x) = log p(x,z) - log p(x)`: `= E_q[log q(z) - log p(x,z) + log p(x)]`. Since `log p(x)` doesn't depend on `z`, pull it straight out of the expectation: `= log p(x) - E_q[log p(x,z) - log q(z)]`. Rearrange:
`log p(x) = E_q[log p(x,z) - log q(z)] + KL(q || p(z|x))`.
Let `L := E_q[log p(x,z) - log q(z)]`. Because KL ≥ 0, `L` is a lower bound on `log p(x)`, tight exactly when `q(z) = p(z|x)` and KL = 0.

This identity immediately answers a question I have to settle before anything else: why maximize the bound `L` instead of going straight for `log p(x)`? Because `log p(x)` itself is uncomputable — that `∫dz` is stuck *inside* the log — never mind differentiating it. Whereas `L` replaces the log-of-an-integral with an *expectation* over `q`: an expectation I can estimate by sampling, with the log now *inside* the expectation rather than outside the `∫`. That's a qualitative difference. And the swap isn't a free lunch I'm sneaking — the identity tells me `log p(x) - L = KL(q||p(z|x)) ≥ 0`, so what I'm optimizing is always a *guaranteed underestimate* of the true likelihood; the truth can only be better than the number in my hand, never worse. And this single quantity does two jobs: fix the model, tune `q` to push `L` up, and that's the same as pushing `KL(q||p(z|x))` down — `q` becomes a better and better posterior approximation, which is *inference*; fix `q`, tune the model to push `L` up, and that's raising a guaranteed lower bound on the likelihood — that's *learning*. One objective doing inference and learning together is exactly the "single objective" I'm reaching for.

EM exploits exactly this: the E-step sets `q = p(z|x; θ_old)`, making KL = 0 and the bound tight; the M-step, with that `q` fixed, maximizes `L = E_q[log p(x,z|θ) - log q(z)]` — and since `log q` doesn't contain `θ`, that's the same as maximizing `E_q[log p(x,z|θ)]`, the expected complete-data log-likelihood. Why is *this* step better than going at `log p(x|θ)` directly? Because `log p(x|θ) = log E_{p(z)}[p(x|z,θ)]` has the log stuck outside the expectation — differentiating it in `θ` means pushing through that integral — whereas `E_q[log p(x,z|θ)]` has pulled the log *inside* the expectation, so it's term-by-term in `θ` and easy to optimize. That's the same "log-goes-inside" payoff of the ELBO, cashed out inside EM. In the linear-Gaussian model this road is wide open: the posterior `p(z|x)` is still Gaussian, writable in closed form, and that's exactly how the Roweis & Ghahramani PCA special case gets solved. But the E-step *requires* that I can **write down** `p(z|x)`. For a nonlinear-network likelihood that posterior has no closed form — the E-step can't even take its first step. EM dies at the door. And that wall tells me something important: I have to give up on the exact equality `q = p(z|x)` and settle for "approximate the posterior with a `q` from some tractable family."

So I retreat to using a `q` from a **tractable family** to approximate the posterior, and maximize the bound `L` — that's variational inference. Mean-field assumes `q(z) = ∏_j q_j(z_j)`, updating one factor at a time by coordinate ascent, with the standard result `log q_j*(z_j) = E_{-j}[log p(x,z)] + const`. That update demands a lot. To compute `q_j*` I have to evaluate `E_{-j}[log p(x,z)]` — take the expectation over every coordinate except `j` — and then write the result as a closed-form function of `z_j`. But `log p(x,z) = log p(z) + log p(x|z)`, and that `log p(x|z)` is a nonlinear network. To turn "the expectation under `q_{-j}`" into an elementary function of `z_j`, I essentially need `log p(x,z)` to be some conjugate exponential-family form in `z` — which only holds for conditionally-conjugate models. My nonlinear network blows that expectation up: no closed form. And even if I could compute it, the factorial `∏_j q_j` assumption fundamentally can't represent correlations between coordinates in the posterior. Stochastic VI (Hoffman et al. 2013) turns the **global**-parameter update into a stochastic natural-gradient step over minibatches, solving half the scale problem — but at its core it still needs conjugacy, still needs analytic local updates, and still optimizes each point's local variational parameters to convergence. Wall: mean-field is blocked by the "analytic-expectation + conjugacy" requirement, and my model is precisely built to break conjugacy.

Fine — can I just refuse the analytic expectation and do Monte Carlo on the gradient of the bound directly? View `L = E_{q_φ(z)}[f_φ(z)]` as an expectation under `q_φ`, with `f_φ(z) = log p_θ(x,z) - log q_φ(z)`. The difficulty is sharp: the *measure* `q_φ` of the expectation itself carries `φ`, so I can't just slide `∇_φ` inside — that would drop the measure's dependence on `φ`. I also have to be careful about one detail: this `f_φ` is not parameter-free, because it contains `-log q_φ(z)`. The general score-function differentiation is
`∇_φ E_{q_φ}[f_φ(z)] = ∇_φ ∫ q_φ(z) f_φ(z) dz = ∫ q_φ(z) f_φ(z) ∇_φ log q_φ(z) dz + ∫ q_φ(z) ∇_φ f_φ(z) dz`
`= E_{q_φ}[ f_φ(z) ∇_φ log q_φ(z) + ∇_φ f_φ(z) ]`.
For the ELBO integrand, holding `z` as the integration variable, `∇_φ f_φ(z) = -∇_φ log q_φ(z)`, since `log p_θ(x,z)` has no `φ`. Its expectation cancels exactly:
`E_{q_φ}[∇_φ f_φ(z)] = -E_{q_φ}[∇_φ log q_φ(z)] = -∫ ∇_φ q_φ(z) dz = -∇_φ ∫ q_φ(z) dz = 0`.
So the entropy-term derivative has not been ignored; it cancels by normalization. The resulting naive Monte Carlo gradient for this ELBO is therefore
`(1/L) Σ_l [log p_θ(x,z^(l)) - log q_φ(z^(l))] ∇_φ log q_φ(z^(l))`, `z^(l) ~ q_φ`. Unbiased, black-box — it only asks me to evaluate the joint density and the approximate-posterior density, and it even works for discrete `z`. Very tempting; this is the REINFORCE/log-derivative route.

"High variance" is the word everyone attaches to this estimator, but I don't want to take it on faith — I want to see *where* the variance comes from and whether it's a fixable nuisance or something structural. After the entropy cancellation, the estimator is `f_φ(z) · s(z)`, where `s(z) = ∇_φ log q_φ(z)` is the score. First note the score has zero mean: `E_{q_φ}[s(z)] = ∫ q_φ ∇_φ log q_φ dz = ∫ ∇_φ q_φ dz = ∇_φ ∫ q_φ dz = ∇_φ 1 = 0`. So the estimator is "a zero-mean score weighted by the integrand." That structure already worries me — a zero-mean multiplier means the gradient signal lives entirely in the *correlation* between `f_φ(z)` and `s(z)`, and any part of `f_φ` that doesn't correlate with the score just adds noise scaled by `f_φ`'s magnitude.

Let me actually measure this on a one-dimensional case I can check against an exact answer. Take `q_φ(z) = N(μ, σ²)` with `φ=μ`, `σ=1.3`, `μ=0.7`, and a smooth integrand `f(z) = -(z-0.4)²` (a stand-in for the model's reconstruction term, with no explicit `φ`). The true gradient is exact: `E_q[f] = -((μ²+σ²) - 2(0.4)μ + 0.4²)`, so `d/dμ E_q[f] = -2(μ-0.4) = -0.6`. The score-function single-sample estimator is `f(z)·(z-μ)/σ²`; the pathwise one (which I'm about to justify) is `f'(z)·dz/dμ = -2(z-0.4)`. Drawing 200k samples, both estimators give the right mean — score-function `-0.597`, pathwise `-0.600` — so unbiasedness isn't the issue. But the single-sample variances are `26.6` for score-function versus `6.8` for pathwise, a 4× gap on this benign quadratic.

Where does that gap come from? Push `f` to the worst case the structure predicts: a *constant* integrand `f(z)=c`. Then the true gradient is exactly 0 (a constant's expectation doesn't move with `μ`), and the pathwise estimator returns `f'(z)·1 = 0` exactly, variance 0. But the score-function estimator still emits `c·s(z)`, with variance `c²·Var(s) = c²/σ²`. With `c=5`, that's `25/1.69 ≈ 14.79` — and the simulation gives `14.83`. So the prediction holds: **the score-function variance does not vanish even when the gradient is zero; it's set by the noise of the score, scaled by the magnitude of the reward, regardless of how smooth `f` is.** That's the disease in one line. The ELBO's explicit `-log q_φ` derivative cancels in expectation, so it repairs *unbiasedness*, but it does nothing to this variance, because the variance lives in the `f·s` product, not in the cancelled term. Intuitively I'm probing the gradient *indirectly* — "the randomness of `z` times the sensitivity of `log q` to `φ`" — and throwing away the steadier local slope `∂ log p_θ(x,z)/∂z` of the model. Someone will say add a baseline `b` and use `(f_φ(z)-b)·s(z)`; that does help, since `E[b·s]=b·E[s]=0` keeps the estimator unbiased while shrinking the multiplier toward its mean. But it treats the symptom: even the optimal baseline leaves `Var(s)` sitting in the variance, and the model's smooth derivative through `z` still isn't used. The upshot is you need control variates (Blei et al. 2012 for exponential-family posteriors), Rao-Blackwellization, baselines (Ranganath et al. 2014's black-box variational inference) — a pile of variance-reduction machinery just to make it barely usable. For my default setting — continuous latents plus a neural-network `q` — that's too fragile, too slow, too many knobs. So the score-function gradient is unbiased but the variance is structural, and the simulation pins the cause: `φ` lives in the measure, so the gradient has to flow through the score rather than through `f`. I'll keep that lesion in mind: "`φ` is in the measure."

Has anyone already walked this exact setting — continuous latents, online, with a recognition network? Yes: wake-sleep / the Helmholtz machine (Hinton, Dayan, Frey, Neal 1995). It already has the *shape* I want: a separate recognition network `q(z|x)` (an encoder) approximating the posterior, trained alongside a generative network `p(x|z)`. The wake phase samples `z ~ q(z|x)` and raises `log p(x,z)` to update the generative weights; the sleep phase "dreams" `(x,z) ~ p` and uses `x` to predict `z` to update the recognition weights. The shape is exactly right — and it incidentally solves a big problem I hadn't named: amortization. Classical variational inference gives each point `x^(i)` its own local variational parameters `(μ^(i), σ^(i))`, which must be optimized to convergence before one global update can happen; the parameter count grows with `N`, and a new test point has to be re-optimized from scratch. Wake-sleep's recognition network changes that into: use one **shared-weight** network `φ` to **predict** each point's local parameters, `q_φ(z|x)`. Inference becomes a single forward pass, the parameter count is independent of `N`, and a new point just generalizes. That is the only affordable approach at scale, and I definitely want this amortized encoder. The idea of a predictive encoder also shows up in predictive sparse decomposition (Kavukcuoglu et al. 2008), which gives me some confidence: using a network to *predict* a quantity you'd otherwise optimize per-point is viable.

But wake-sleep optimizes **two different objectives**, and the two together do not correspond to optimizing any single bound on `log p(x)`. Stare at the sleep phase: it "dreams" `(x,z)` from the model `p`, then trains `q` to predict `z` from `x`, which amounts to minimizing `E_{p(x)}[KL(p(z|x)||q(z|x))]` — a **reversed** KL, and computed on `x` the **model has hallucinated**. But what I actually want to shrink is `KL(q(z|x)||p(z|x))`, the opposite direction, and on **real data**. Both are zero when `q = p(z|x)`, but the moment `q` isn't expressive enough to hit the exact equality, the two KLs have different optimal `q` (reverse KL is mode-seeking, forward KL is mass-covering), so optimizing them pulls `q` toward different solutions. So there's no coherent objective function and no guarantee the marginal likelihood is improving; the two networks can drift in inconsistent directions. What I want is **one** objective (the ELBO `L`) that trains both the encoder `φ` and the decoder `θ`. A wall — but this wall hands me the shape of the answer: the recognition network (amortized encoder) is right; the missing piece is "a single ELBO objective I can differentiate with respect to `φ`."

There's also a pragmatic fallback: Monte Carlo EM. Skip the closed-form posterior in the E-step; instead use gradient-based MCMC (say HMC, with `∇_z log p(z|x) = ∇_z log p(z) + ∇_z log p(x|z)`, computable because it doesn't contain the intractable normalizer) to sample from the posterior, then do the M-step. It can give small-scale reference results. But it runs a sampling chain per point — it's not an online algorithm, and it collapses when `N` is large. Dead on scale.

Totaling the books: EM needs a tractable posterior (dead), mean-field needs conjugacy / analytic expectations (dead), the score-function gradient has exploding variance (limping), MCMC-EM runs a chain per point and isn't online (dead on scale), wake-sleep has no single objective (incoherent). All roads jam at the **same place** — taking `∇_φ` of an expectation whose **measure depends on `φ`** — and either it can't be computed (conjugacy gone) or the variance explodes (score-function). Meanwhile the substrate — SGD, backprop, GPUs, Adagrad (Duchi et al. 2010) — is all mature. What's missing is **only** an estimator of the ELBO that is differentiable, low-variance, and minibatch-friendly. The problem has been squeezed down to a single point.

Back to that structural lesion in the score-function approach: "`φ` is in the measure," so the sampling step is non-differentiable and I'm forced to detour through the score `∇_φ log q_φ`, which means the model term's gradient in `z` goes unused. What if… what if I could split "the randomness" from "the parameters"? Rewrite "draw a `z` from `q_φ`" as: first draw a fixed noise `ε ~ p(ε)` from a distribution that is **independent of `φ`**, then push that noise through a **deterministic, differentiable** transform `z = g_φ(ε, x)` to get my sample. Then all the randomness is in `ε`, independent of `φ`, and `φ` appears only in the deterministic map `g_φ` — `φ` has moved from the **measure** into the **integrand**. The score-function setting was "`φ` in the measure, the smooth model slope only used as a scalar weight"; this move flips it into an ordinary pathwise derivative.

The change of variables has to be legitimate, not just a feeling. Given a deterministic map `z = g_φ(ε, x)`, probability conservation (change of variables) gives `q_φ(z|x) ∏_i dz_i = p(ε) ∏_i dε_i`. So for any `f`,
`∫ q_φ(z|x) f(z) dz = ∫ p(ε) f(z) dε = ∫ p(ε) f(g_φ(ε, x)) dε`,
i.e. `E_{q_φ(z|x)}[f(z)] = E_{p(ε)}[f(g_φ(ε, x))]`. This is the **same expectation**, but now the measure `p(ε)` doesn't carry `φ`. The Monte Carlo estimate is `≈ (1/L) Σ_l f(g_φ(ε^(l), x))`, `ε^(l) ~ p(ε)`. And the key point: now `∇_φ` can move **straight inside** the expectation, because the measure is independent of `φ` —
`∇_φ E_{p(ε)}[f(g_φ(ε,x))] = E_{p(ε)}[∇_φ f(g_φ(ε,x))] = E_{p(ε)}[ ∂f/∂z · ∂g_φ/∂φ ]`,
with the chain rule running all the way back through `g_φ`. This is the channel the score-function estimator couldn't use: the local slope of the integrand with respect to `z` is now the backbone of the gradient. And this is exactly the estimator I just measured as "pathwise" in the 1-D simulation — `∂f/∂z·∂g/∂φ`. So I already have the numbers: on the smooth quadratic it cut the single-sample variance from `26.6` to `6.8`, and in the constant-reward case it gave variance `0` against the score-function's `14.8`, because `∂f/∂z` is then identically `0`. The pattern those numbers exposed is the whole point — the pathwise estimator's variance is governed by `f`'s smoothness (the scale of `∂f/∂z`), whereas the score-function's variance is governed by the score's noise, independent of whether `f` is smooth. The structural cause I'd named — "`φ` in the measure" — is removed by this change of variables, and the variance drops with it, which is what the measurement confirms.

Let me pin it down with a minimal example, and along the way make clear why it's especially nice for Gaussians. Univariate Gaussian `z ~ N(μ, σ²)`, parameters `φ=(μ,σ)`. Take `ε ~ N(0,1)` and set `z = μ + σ·ε` — that's the location-scale reparameterization. Then `E_{N(z;μ,σ²)}[f(z)] = E_{N(ε;0,1)}[f(μ+σε)] ≈ (1/L) Σ_l f(μ+σε^(l))`, explicitly differentiable in `μ` (directly) and in `σ` (multiplying `ε`). More generally, which `q_φ(z|x)` can be written this way? The coverage question matters — this had better not be a cute trick for Gaussians only. (1) Those with a tractable inverse CDF — take `ε ~ U(0,I)` and let `g` be the inverse CDF (Exponential, Cauchy, Logistic, Rayleigh, Pareto, Weibull, Gompertz, Gumbel, Erlang, …). (2) Any location-scale family — take the standard member (location 0, scale 1) as `ε` and set `g = location + scale·ε` (Laplace, Elliptical, Student-t, Logistic, Uniform, Triangular, Gaussian, …). (3) Compositions — write the target random variable as several transforms of auxiliary variables (Log-Normal is exp of a normal, Gamma is a sum of exponentials, Dirichlet is weighted Gammas, Beta/Chi²/F, …). When all three fail, the inverse CDF still has high-quality numerical approximations at cost comparable to the PDF (Devroye 1986). Coverage is plenty; this is a general operator.

Now snap this step back into the ELBO. Return to the per-datapoint bound, and first write it in two equivalent forms, because I'll want each separately. Form one, straight from the joint:
`L(x) = E_{q_φ(z|x)}[ log p_θ(x,z) - log q_φ(z|x) ]`.
Form two: write `log p_θ(x,z) = log p_θ(x|z) + log p_θ(z)`, and gather the `log p_θ(z) - log q_φ(z|x)` part into a KL:
`L(x) = - KL( q_φ(z|x) || p_θ(z) ) + E_{q_φ(z|x)}[ log p_θ(x|z) ]`.

Plug the reparameterization into form one and I get a **generic** stochastic estimator, call it A:
`L̃^A(x) = (1/L) Σ_l [ log p_θ(x, z^(l)) - log q_φ(z^(l)|x) ]`, where `z^(l) = g_φ(ε^(l), x)`, `ε^(l) ~ p(ε)`.
It's differentiable in both `θ` and `φ`, because `z^(l)` is a differentiable function of `φ` while `ε^(l)` is fixed noise. The virtue of A is that it's **fully generic**: even `log q_φ(z|x)` only needs to be evaluated, the KL needn't be in closed form, so it works even when prior/posterior aren't Gaussian.

But whenever the KL *does* have a closed form, I can do better. Notice the KL term `KL(q_φ(z|x) || p_θ(z))` in form two can often be integrated analytically, and then there's **no reason** to estimate it by sampling — handing a quantity I can compute exactly to Monte Carlo only injects variance for nothing. Only the reconstruction term `E_q[log p_θ(x|z)]` truly has to be sampled. That gives estimator B:
`L̃^B(x) = - KL( q_φ(z|x) || p_θ(z) ) + (1/L) Σ_l log p_θ(x | z^(l))`, `z^(l) = g_φ(ε^(l), x)`, `ε^(l) ~ p(ε)`.
B usually has lower variance than A, for the reason just given — I've **removed** the part I can compute exactly (the KL regularizer) from the stochastic estimate, and only use sampling on the reconstruction error I can't avoid. It's a Rao-Blackwell-flavored move: keep the analytic part analytic. This form also instantly exposes the connection to the autoencoder: the first term is the KL of `q` from the prior `p(z)`, a regularizer keeping the approximate posterior from straying too far from the prior; the second term is the expected reconstruction log-likelihood. Given `x`, `g_φ` maps it (with noise) to a latent code `z` (encode), which is fed to `log p_θ(x|z)` to score the reconstruction (decode) — a noisy, regularized autoencoder. And that regularizer comes **built into** the variational bound, unlike the denoising/contractive/sparse autoencoders (Vincent et al. 2010, Bengio et al. 2013) whose nuisance regularizer hyperparameters carry no probabilistic meaning and have to be hand-tuned — here the "strength" of the KL term is fixed by the `log p(x)` objective itself, not something I dial in by hand.

What about the whole dataset? `N` is too large for full batches. Use a minibatch: draw `M` points at random, `X^M = {x^(i)}_{i=1}^M`,
`L(X) = Σ_{i=1}^N L̃(x^(i)) ≈ L̃^M(X^M) = (N/M) Σ_{i=1}^M L̃(x^(i))`.
That `N/M` isn't placed casually — it's what makes the estimate unbiased. The full objective is a sum of `N` per-point bounds; if I draw `M` of the `N` **uniformly** and sum, `E[Σ_{i∈minibatch} L̃(x^(i))] = M · (1/N Σ_all L̃) = (M/N)·L(X)`, so multiplying by `N/M` corrects this minibatch sum's expectation back to the full-dataset sum `L(X)`. Then each minibatch gradient is an unbiased estimate of the full-dataset gradient, which is what makes SGD meaningful.

What about `L`, the number of samples per point? My instinct is to draw more to reduce variance. But do the arithmetic: the minibatch has `M` points, each drawing `L` values of `ε`, so the total sample count is `M·L`, the estimator is the average of these `M·L` terms, and the variance scales roughly as `1/(M·L)`. Under a fixed compute budget, rather than drawing `L` values of `z` for the **same** `x` (highly correlated samples, since they share the same `x` and the same `(μ,σ)`), I'm better off spending the budget on **more distinct** `x` — the randomness across different points is more independent, so the average is harder-hitting. Empirically, as long as the minibatch is large enough (`M=100`), `L=1` suffices: one point, one noise sample, with the average over 100 **distinct points** inside the minibatch already squeezing the variance down to a usable level. That turns each step into the plainest possible forward pass: per point draw one `ε`, compute one reconstruction, add the analytic KL, one backward pass to get `∇_{θ,φ}`, feed it to SGD or Adagrad. The skeleton of the algorithm is just: initialize `θ,φ`; loop { draw a minibatch `X^M`; draw noise `ε`; `g ← ∇_{θ,φ} L̃^M(X^M, ε)`; update `θ,φ` with `g` } until convergence.

Now let me make the model concrete, down to a runnable form. For every choice I want to be able to say "why this, and why not the obvious alternative."

What prior should I take? I want the KL term to integrate cleanly, the latent space to carry no artificial preference, and the prior not to compete to be learned itself. A centered isotropic Gaussian `p(z) = N(0, I)` hits all three: it's **parameter-free**, so there's no prior to learn and no risk of the prior and posterior accommodating each other into a degenerate solution; isotropic means the latent space has no preferred direction, is rotation-symmetric, and imposes no structure; and — most practically — when I go to compute `KL(q||p)`, a prior of `N(0,I)` gives the cleanest closed form. Someone will worry "isn't this prior too simple to be expressive?" — no, because the decoder is a nonlinear network, and it can **deform** a simple Gaussian prior into an arbitrarily complex distribution in data space; the complexity should be borne by `p(x|z)`, not by the prior. So the prior is fixed at `N(0,I)`.

What family should the approximate posterior `q_φ(z|x)` be? I want it to reparameterize most cleanly and to pair with the `N(0,I)` prior for a closed-form KL — Gaussian satisfies both: it's a location-scale family (`z=μ+σ⊙ε`), and paired with `N(0,I)` the KL is closed-form. Full covariance or diagonal? Full covariance means learning `J(J+1)/2` parameters plus a Cholesky factorization to sample and to compute the KL, per point — too expensive; diagonal needs only `J` values of `μ` and `J` of `logσ²`, with sampling, KL, and gradients all elementwise, cost linear in `J`. The price is that `q` can't represent correlations between coordinates of the posterior — but that's a **simplifying** choice, not a limitation of the method; if the true posterior is roughly axis-aligned, a diagonal Gaussian is enough, and I can swap in a richer `q` when needed. So `log q_φ(z|x) = log N(z; μ^(i), σ^(i)² I)`, with `μ^(i)`, `σ^(i)` the two output heads of an encoder MLP run on `x^(i)`. Sampling uses location-scale: `z^(i,l) = μ^(i) + σ^(i) ⊙ ε^(l)`, `ε^(l) ~ N(0,I)`, `⊙` elementwise — precisely the concrete form of the earlier `g_φ`.

One small but important choice: does the encoder output `log σ²` or `σ`? I have it output `log σ²`. Because the network can freely emit any real number, and taking `exp` makes it automatically positive, so I don't have to impose a positivity constraint by hand; and it's numerically stable — no risk of taking the log of a possibly-negative number.

Since prior and posterior are both Gaussian, the KL can be computed in closed form. Let me derive that closed form from scratch now, because this is the term going **straight into the code** and there's no room for hand-waving. Let `J` be the dimension of `z`, and `μ_j, σ_j` the `j`-th coordinate. `-KL = ∫ q(z)(log p(z) - log q(z)) dz`; I'll compute the two integrals separately.

First, `∫ q(z) log p(z) dz`, with `q=N(μ,σ²I)`, `p=N(0,I)`. `log N(z;0,I) = -(J/2)log(2π) - ½ Σ_j z_j²`. Taking the expectation under `q`, using the second moment `E_q[z_j²] = μ_j² + σ_j²`:
`∫ q log p = -(J/2)log(2π) - ½ Σ_j (μ_j² + σ_j²)`.
Second, `∫ q(z) log q(z) dz` (this is the negative entropy of `q`). `log N(z;μ,σ²) = -(J/2)log(2π) - ½ Σ_j [ log σ_j² + (z_j-μ_j)²/σ_j² ]`. Taking the expectation under `q`, using the central second moment `E_q[(z_j-μ_j)²] = σ_j²`, so the quadratic term contributes `σ_j²/σ_j² = 1` per coordinate:
`∫ q log q = -(J/2)log(2π) - ½ Σ_j (1 + log σ_j²)`.
Subtract:
`-KL(q||p) = ∫ q log p - ∫ q log q = [-(J/2)log(2π) - ½ Σ_j(μ_j²+σ_j²)] - [-(J/2)log(2π) - ½ Σ_j(1+log σ_j²)]`
`= -½ Σ_j(μ_j²+σ_j²) + ½ Σ_j(1+log σ_j²) = ½ Σ_{j=1}^J ( 1 + log σ_j² - μ_j² - σ_j² )`.
The two `-(J/2)log(2π)` constants cancel exactly — and that's precisely why I want prior and posterior both Gaussian densities over the same `J`-dimensional space: the shared normalizing constant drops out, leaving everything as an elementary function of `μ,σ` that I can differentiate directly. With a less compatible prior or posterior family, the KL may not have this tidy analytic form, and I'd fall back to estimating the KL by sampling (estimator A, higher variance). That's the real algorithmic reason behind the "prior and posterior both Gaussian" choice.

Since this term goes straight into the code, a dropped factor or sign error would silently corrupt every gradient, so I check it twice. First the signs: at `σ_j→1, μ_j→0`, each coordinate gives `1+0-0-1=0`, so KL=0 — exactly the `q=p` case; when `μ_j` grows, `-μ_j²` penalizes it back toward 0; and `log σ_j² - σ_j²` is maximized at `σ_j=1` (derivative `1/σ²−1`, zero at `σ=1`), pulling the variance back toward 1. Consistent with "regularize toward the prior." Second, a numeric cross-check against a direct estimate, in case the algebra and the Monte-Carlo definition disagree. Take `J=3`, `μ=(0.5,-1.0,0.2)`, `σ²=(0.5,2.0,1.0)`. The closed form gives `-KL = ½[(1+log0.5−0.25−0.5)+(1+log2−1−2)+(1+0−0.04−1)] = -0.895`, so `KL = 0.895`. Now estimate `KL(q‖p)=E_q[log q − log p]` by drawing 2M samples `z=μ+σ⊙ε` and averaging `log N(z;μ,σ²I) − log N(z;0,I)` with the *full* densities (every `2π` and normalizer kept, nothing cancelled by hand): the average comes out `0.896`. It matches the closed form to three digits, which tells me the cancellation of the `log2π` constants and the second-moment substitutions `E[z_j²]=μ_j²+σ_j²`, `E[(z_j−μ_j)²]=σ_j²` were all done correctly — the analytic expression is the real KL, not an expression that merely looks like one.

What should the reconstruction term `log p_θ(x|z)` look like? It must match the **data type** of `x`, or the likelihood is simply wrong — this isn't an aesthetic question. The parameters are all computed by a single-hidden-layer MLP from `z`, but the distribution at the end depends on the data. Binary data (e.g. binarized MNIST pixels ∈ {0,1}) uses a multivariate Bernoulli: the decoder outputs the probability each pixel is 1, `y = f_σ(W₂ tanh(W₁ z + b₁) + b₂)` (with `f_σ` the sigmoid, which conveniently squashes the output into (0,1) to serve as probabilities), so
`log p(x|z) = Σ_{d=1}^D [ x_d log y_d + (1-x_d) log(1-y_d) ]`,
which is per-pixel **negative binary cross-entropy** — the reconstruction term comes out as BCE naturally, not because I rigged up a loss, but because that *is* the log of the Bernoulli likelihood. Continuous data (e.g. Frey Faces grayscale ∈ (0,1)) uses a diagonal Gaussian: `h = tanh(W₃ z + b₃)`, `μ = W₄ h + b₄`, `log σ² = W₅ h + b₅`,
`log p(x|z) = log N(x; μ, σ² I)`,
i.e. (up to a constant) `-½ Σ_d [ (x_d-μ_d)²/σ_d² + log σ_d² ]` — the reconstruction term becomes squared error with a learned variance. For (0,1) data like Frey Faces, I also put a sigmoid on the output `μ` to squash the mean into (0,1) to match the data range. The cost of choosing the wrong likelihood is concrete: a Gaussian (squared error) on binary pixels forces the model to estimate variance at 0/1 where it shouldn't, wasting capacity; a Bernoulli on continuous grayscale has no defined likelihood at all.

So the objective for this model at a single point `x^(i)` (estimator B made concrete) is:
`L(x^(i)) ≈ ½ Σ_{j=1}^J ( 1 + log σ_j^(i)² - μ_j^(i)² - σ_j^(i)² ) + (1/L) Σ_{l=1}^L log p_θ(x^(i) | z^(i,l))`,
`z^(i,l) = μ^(i) + σ^(i) ⊙ ε^(l)`, `ε^(l) ~ N(0,I)`.
The first term is the analytic KL regularizer, the second the sampled reconstruction log-likelihood, with the decoder taking the Bernoulli or Gaussian form per data type. In training I maximize `L` (equivalently minimize `-L`), plus a small weight decay corresponding to `p(θ)=N(0,I)` — which makes the whole optimization approximate MAP (the gradient is the likelihood-bound gradient plus a quadratic term pulling `θ` toward 0), cheap and probabilistically meaningful. All of it runs with ordinary stochastic gradient ascent, Adagrad or SGD, either is fine.

The whole chain is differentiable end to end, and only through the low-variance channel: the encoder emits `(μ, logσ²)`; the reparameterization `z=μ+σ⊙ε` injects `ε` as a constant in the computation graph; the decoder scores the reconstruction. One backward pass sends the reconstruction gradient along `∂log p/∂z · ∂z/∂(μ,σ)` back to the encoder — exactly the model-slope channel the score-function estimator couldn't use — and through the decoder network to `θ`, while the analytic KL differentiates directly in `(μ,logσ²)`. One objective, two networks trained together; every wall from the survey is cleared by the same single move rather than a patch per wall.

Let me push one step further and take the framework to its limit, to see if there's a deeper unification — can I do variational inference even on the **global parameters `θ` themselves**, rather than just a point estimate (MAP) of them? This step doesn't affect the main algorithm, but it'll tell me whether the reparameterization is more than a trick for latent variables. Give `θ` a hyperprior `p_α(θ)`, and give its approximate posterior its own variational parameters, `q_λ(θ)`. Keep the amortized local posterior separate as `q_φ(z|x)`. The outer ELBO is
`log p_α(X) = KL(q_λ(θ)||p_α(θ|X)) + L(λ;X)`,
where
`L(λ;X) = E_{q_λ(θ)}[ log p_α(θ) - log q_λ(θ) + Σ_i log p_θ(x^(i)) ]`.
For each datapoint inside that sum, apply the same identity over `z`:
`log p_θ(x^(i)) = KL(q_φ(z|x^(i))||p_θ(z|x^(i))) + L(θ,φ; x^(i))`,
with
`L(θ,φ; x^(i)) = E_{q_φ(z|x^(i))}[ log p_θ(x^(i)|z) + log p(z) - log q_φ(z|x^(i)) ]`
in the usual fixed-prior case. Now **both layers** of randomness — `θ ~ q_λ(θ)` and `z ~ q_φ(z|x)` — can be unpicked by reparameterization: `θ = h_λ(ζ)`, `ζ~p(ζ)`; `z = g_φ(ε,x)`, `ε~p(ε)`. For a minibatch `B`, the sampled full-data bound has the shape
`log p_α(θ) - log q_λ(θ) + (N/M) Σ_{i∈B} [ log p_θ(x^(i)|z^(i)) + log p(z^(i)) - log q_φ(z^(i)|x^(i)) ]`,
with `θ=h_λ(ζ)` and `z^(i)=g_φ(ε^(i),x^(i))`. The `N/M` factor is the same full-dataset rescaling as before; the global term appears once because `θ` is shared by the whole dataset. If the global posterior/prior and the local posterior/prior are Gaussian, the analytic low-variance version contains **two KL divergences**, not four KL terms: `KL(q_λ(θ)||p_α(θ))` for the global parameters, and `KL(q_φ(z|x)||p(z))` for each local latent. Each KL has the same closed-form Gaussian pattern `½Σ(μ²+σ²-1-logσ²)` when written as a penalty, while the reconstruction term still has to be sampled. The reparameterization is reused verbatim across two levels — it's a **general operator** for unpicking any parameterized measure, not a special case for Gaussian latents. The main line still just does MAP on `θ` (enough, and simpler), but knowing this generalizes settles my mind.

Down to real code (PyTorch), with every block mapping to a step in the derivation above: the inference module becomes an encoder MLP with two heads for `μ` and `logσ²`, the latent sampler becomes the location-scale reparameterization, the objective becomes the analytic KL plus the per-pixel BCE, and the observation model becomes a sigmoid Bernoulli network. The implementation writes itself from these pieces one-to-one — there is no line in it that a step above hasn't already pinned. What remains is to run it: the bet, honestly statable in advance, is that one noise sample per point on a minibatch of 100 keeps the gradient variance low enough for the bound to converge to MCMC-EM-quality solutions at a per-step cost orders of magnitude cheaper.
