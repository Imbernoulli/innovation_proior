I start with the part that is hard to ignore: dominance gives me a partial order, but it goes silent exactly where most of the selection work happens. Once two solutions are on the same nondominated front, neither dominates the other, and I still have to decide which one deserves a place in a finite population. NSGA-II answers with crowding distance. SPEA2 answers with nearest-neighbour density. Those are useful local density estimates, but they are not the quality measure I usually trust for the final set. If I judge an approximation by the dominated hypervolume it covers, then it seems wasteful to drive survival with a separate geometric proxy that only correlates with that hypervolume. The natural thing to try is to let the same indicator I use to score the output also decide who lives.

So suppose I take that literally: after I add new material to the population, keep the fixed-size subset with the largest S-metric value. That sentence is clean, but I should price it before I commit. If I make a whole offspring population of size `lambda` and then choose `mu` points out of `mu + lambda`, I am choosing the best `mu`-subset by hypervolume, and that is a subset-selection problem over `C(mu+lambda, mu)` candidates. For even modest `mu` and `lambda` this is astronomical, and there is no cheap exact shortcut for it in general. That tells me the literal greedy-set idea is fine as an objective but unusable as a per-generation operation if I keep the usual generational rhythm.

The thing I can actually change cheaply is the rhythm, not the metric. If I never create a large batch, I never have to solve a large subset problem. So I create one offspring, form a pool of size `mu + 1`, and delete exactly one solution. Then "which subset of size `mu` maximizes `S`" collapses to "which single point, when removed, costs me the least dominated volume." That is a `mu + 1`-way comparison, not a combinatorial search, and it keeps the population size fixed by construction.

For a candidate `s` inside a set `R`, the volume I lose by deleting it is `Delta S(s, R) = S(R) - S(R \ {s})`. I want to make sure this quantity behaves the way I am assuming. Let me put three nondominated minimization points on paper, `R = {(1,4), (2,2), (4,1)}`, with reference `r = (5,5)`. The dominated area is the union of the three boxes reaching up to `r`. Summing the staircase strips: the strip left of `f_1 = 2` has height `5-4=1` and width `5-1=4`... it is easier to just compute it, and when I do the staircase sum I get `S(R) = 11`. Removing the interior point `(2,2)` leaves `{(1,4),(4,1)}` with dominated area `7`, so `Delta S((2,2)) = 11 - 7 = 4`. Removing either extreme, say `(1,4)`, leaves area `10`, so `Delta S = 1`. So the interior point here actually owns more exclusive volume than the extremes do under this reference, and every `Delta S` came out nonnegative as it must — deleting a point can never increase dominated volume. The sign convention is the one I want: large positive `Delta S` means `s` owns a region nobody else covers, near-zero means `s` is redundant, and the point to discard is the one with minimum `Delta S`. I have to keep the subtraction in this order, `with minus without`; if I reversed it to `S(R \ {s}) - S(R)` every contribution would be `-4, -1, -1` and `argmax`/`argmin` would point me at deleting the most valuable point instead of the least.

That small example also flags something about the extremes that I need to handle, not just note. The two extreme points' contributions were `1` each under `r = (5,5)`. Let me push the reference out to `r = (8,8)` and recompute on the same three points: now both extreme contributions become `4`, while the interior point's contribution stays exactly `4`. So the interior contribution is a property of the front geometry alone, but the extreme contributions are whatever I declare the reference point to be — they scale with `r`. That is uncomfortable: it means in two objectives the reference point can decide which endpoint survives, for reasons that have nothing to do with the trade-off structure. I will come back to this.

First, though, I cannot apply the `Delta S` comparison to the whole pool blindly, because dominance has to stay the primary rule. A point on a worse nondominated-sorting level should be eligible for deletion before any better-front point, regardless of hypervolume bookkeeping. So I partition the `mu + 1` pool into fronts `R_1, ..., R_v` and only look at `R_v`, the worst-ranked front. If `R_v` has one point, the decision is forced. If it has several mutually nondominated points, I compare their hypervolume contributions within that front and remove the smallest contributor. This nests the two rules cleanly: rank first, volume contribution second, so the indicator never overrides dominance.

Now back to the extremes, in the case I can fully control: two objectives. A nondominated front sorted by increasing `f_1` is automatically sorted by decreasing `f_2`, so for an interior point `s_i` its unique exclusive rectangle starts at its own coordinates, ends in `f_1` at the next point, and ends in `f_2` at the previous point. The contribution should be `(f_1(s_{i+1}) - f_1(s_i)) * (f_2(s_{i-1}) - f_2(s_i))`. I do not want to trust that I have the neighbours right from a picture, so I check it against the brute-force subtraction on the same three points: sorting by `f_1` the middle point is `(2,2)`, with next `(4,1)` and previous `(1,4)`, giving `(4-2) * (4-2) = 4`. That matches the `Delta S((2,2)) = 4` I computed by actually removing the point and re-running the area calculation. Both factors are nonnegative under this ordering, and if either factor is small the point is crowded against a neighbour — exactly the deletion candidate I want. If I had swapped a neighbour, used `s_{i-1}` in `f_1` or `s_{i+1}` in `f_2`, the rectangle would be the wrong area and could even go negative, so the matching number is a real check that the index bookkeeping is right.

The extremes I now treat deliberately rather than numerically: because their contributions are pure functions of the reference point (the `1` versus `4` I saw above), letting the reference decide an endpoint is arbitrary. The clean move in two objectives is to protect the two extreme points and compute finite contributions only for interior points, so the reference never enters the comparison at all. There is one edge case this leaves: a degenerate front with two or fewer points, where "interior" is empty. There I have nothing to compare, so I fall back to the ordinary finite-reference hypervolume contribution just to return one deletion. To confirm the protection logic does what I intend, I run the worst-front selection on five nondominated points `{(1,5),(2,3),(2.5,2.9),(3,2.5),(5,1)}`: the two extremes come back as `inf` (protected), and among the interior three the smallest exclusive rectangle belongs to `(2.5,2.9)`, the point wedged between `(2,3)` and `(3,2.5)`. That is the index the selector returns, which is the crowded point I would delete by eye.

For three objectives I cannot protect every boundary point. Many points can carry one worst coordinate, so "boundary" would include a large fraction of the front, and protecting all of them would let the population pile up there — a population-size trap. Here I have to commit to a concrete reference point. A static one chosen up front can be wrong as the front moves, so the neutral dynamic choice is to recompute it from the current front as the coordinatewise worst objective vector plus a small offset: `r = nadir + 1.0`. The `+1.0` gives the boundary boxes positive thickness without introducing a scale-dependent span multiplier that would change relative contributions as the front shrinks. That fixes the implementation pattern: whenever I need an exact contribution beyond the simple 2D interior formula, compute `S(R, r) - S(R \ {s}, r)` with `r` just beyond the current nadir. I do want to sanity-check the 3D volume routine itself before trusting it inside a contribution loop, so I test it on a single box `{(1,1,1)}` against `r=(3,3,3)`: it returns `8`, which is `2^3`, the correct box volume. Adding a dominated second point `(2,2,2)` leaves the volume at `8`, since that point's box sits entirely inside the first — also correct. The slab-sweep over `z` is doing the right union.

There is one useful variant for dominated worst fronts. If `R_v` is not the first front, every point in it is already dominated by something better, and spending hypervolume computation there is partly wasted on material that is leaving the population anyway. A cheaper variant deletes, from the worst front, the point dominated by the largest number of pool members; when the whole pool is nondominated, that count is zero for everyone, so the rule has to defer back to the hypervolume contributor. I will keep this as a mode, not as the basic rule, because it changes what "least useful" means.

The algorithm is now small. Initialize a fixed population. Repeat: generate one child, evaluate it, append it, nondominated-sort the `mu + 1` pool, and delete one member from the worst front — the minimum hypervolume contributor in the basic version, or the highest domination count in the dominated-front variant. Because each step deletes exactly one member, the population size stays fixed without any separate truncation pass, and because I always delete the minimum-`Delta S` member of the worst front, the deletion never throws away a point that some other member could not replace in dominated volume.

The important thing is that the code refuses the batch-selection shortcut. `reduce` expects `mu + 1`, not `2 * mu`, because the whole design is a steady-state answer to the cost of hypervolume subset selection. The signs are all minimization signs: lower objectives dominate, the reference point is worse, and a contribution is "with minus without," which is the order I verified gives nonnegative `Delta S`. The 2D interior formula uses the next point in `f_1` and the previous point in `f_2`, the pairing I matched against brute-force subtraction; swapping either neighbour would turn a positive exclusive rectangle into the wrong area. For three objectives the exact contribution falls back to repeated dominated-volume calculations against the dynamic `nadir + 1.0` reference, which is slower but faithful for the two and three objective dimensions this algorithm is meant to handle.
