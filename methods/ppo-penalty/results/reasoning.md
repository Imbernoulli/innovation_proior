Let me start from what actually hurts. I have a stochastic policy `ПҖ_Оё(a|s)` вҖ” a diagonal Gaussian whose mean comes out of an MLP, with a learned log-standard-deviation вҖ” and I want to maximize the discounted return `О·(Оё) = E[ОЈ_t Оі^t r_t]` by climbing a Monte-Carlo estimate of its gradient. The estimator I trust is the score-function form, `вҲҮ_Оё О· = E[ОЈ_t ОЁ_t вҲҮ_Оё log ПҖ_Оё(a_t|s_t)]`, and among the choices for `ОЁ_t` the advantage `A_ПҖ(s,a) = Q_ПҖ(s,a) вҲ’ V_ПҖ(s)` is the one with nearly the lowest variance, because it asks the right question: was this action better or worse than what the policy does on average in this state. So a gradient step nudges up the log-probability of better-than-average actions and down for worse-than-average ones. Good. I'll estimate `A` from a learned value function and call the estimate `ГӮ_t`.

The thing that bites is data efficiency. That gradient is only unbiased for data sampled from the very policy I'm differentiating. So the honest recipe is: roll out `ПҖ_Оё`, form one gradient, take one step, throw the batch away, roll out again. Each batch of expensive environment interaction вҖ” and in MuJoCo a batch is thousands of simulator steps вҖ” buys exactly one gradient step. I'd love to be greedier: collect one batch and do several epochs of minibatch SGD on it, the way I would on any supervised objective. But the instant `Оё` moves off `Оё_old`, the data no longer comes from the current policy, the estimate goes stale, and what I see in practice is that the updates don't just degrade gracefully вҖ” they go destructively large. One batch can shove the policy off a cliff. So the real problem is sharp: how do I reuse a batch for many cheap first-order steps *without* the policy wandering so far that the objective I'm optimizing stops predicting the true return?

Let me get precise about why it goes wrong, because the fix has to attack the actual mechanism. Write the return of a candidate policy `ПҖМғ` relative to my current `ПҖ` exactly: `О·(ПҖМғ) = О·(ПҖ) + ОЈ_s ПҒ_{ПҖМғ}(s) ОЈ_a ПҖМғ(a|s) A_ПҖ(s,a)`, where `ПҒ_{ПҖМғ}(s) = ОЈ_t Оі^t P(s_t = s | ПҖМғ)` is the discounted state-visitation measure under `ПҖМғ`. This is exact and it's beautiful вҖ” the improvement is the advantage of `ПҖ` summed over the states `ПҖМғ` actually visits. But `ПҒ_{ПҖМғ}` depends on `ПҖМғ` in a way I can't sample, because I'd have to run `ПҖМғ` to know where it goes, and `ПҖМғ` is the thing I'm trying to choose. So I do the only tractable thing: freeze the visitation at the old policy's,

  `L_ПҖ(ПҖМғ) = О·(ПҖ) + ОЈ_s ПҒ_ПҖ(s) ОЈ_a ПҖМғ(a|s) A_ПҖ(s,a)`,

which I *can* estimate from old-policy rollouts. With importance sampling I turn the inner sum over actions into an expectation under the data: `ОЈ_a ПҖМғ(a|s) A = ОЈ_a ПҖ_old(a|s) [ПҖМғ(a|s)/ПҖ_old(a|s)] A = E_{aвҲјПҖ_old}[ r(Оё) A ]` with the ratio `r_t(Оё) = ПҖ_Оё(a_t|s_t)/ПҖ_old(a_t|s_t)`. So the surrogate I can actually optimize on a batch is `L^CPI(Оё) = ГҠ_t[ r_t(Оё) ГӮ_t ]`. At `Оё = Оё_old` every `r_t = 1`, and there `L` agrees with `О·` to first order вҖ” its value and gradient match. That's the whole license to optimize it: near `Оё_old`, climbing `L` climbs `О·`. But the license is *local*. I substituted `ПҒ_ПҖ` for `ПҒ_{ПҖМғ}`; that substitution is exact only when `ПҖМғ = ПҖ`, and the error grows as `ПҖМғ` pulls away, because the states `ПҖМғ` really visits drift from the ones `ПҖ` visited. So `L^CPI` is a tangent that's faithful in a neighborhood and lies about `О·` outside it. Maximizing it with no leash walks straight out of the neighborhood and reports a huge "improvement" that isn't real. That's the destructive update, and now I can see it's not a numerical accident вҖ” it's me trusting a local model globally.

So the problem reduces to: maximize `L^CPI` but keep the policy inside the region where `L^CPI` is still a good model of `О·`. I need to quantify "the region." How much can `О·` and `L` disagree as `ПҖМғ` moves? There's a classical handle on exactly this. Kakade and Langford studied conservative policy iteration, where the new policy is a mixture `ПҖ_new = (1вҲ’Оұ)ПҖ_old + Оұ ПҖ'`, and proved a lower bound: `О·(ПҖ_new) вүҘ L_ПҖ(ПҖ_new) вҲ’ (2ОөОі/(1вҲ’Оі)ВІ) ОұВІ` with `Оө = max_s |E_{aвҲјПҖ'}[A_ПҖ(s,a)]|`. So the gap between the true return and the surrogate is controlled by `ОұВІ`, the size of the mixing step, times a constant. That's the shape I want вҖ” surrogate minus a penalty for moving вҖ” but it's stuck on mixture policies, which I don't use; my policy is a Gaussian-MLP I update by gradient. I need this for general stochastic policies.

The mixture coefficient is really standing in for "how often the two policies can force different trajectories." For a general stochastic policy I can measure that directly per state. Let `Оұ = D_TV^max(ПҖ,ПҖМғ) = max_s D_TV(ПҖ(В·|s), ПҖМғ(В·|s))`, where `D_TV(p,q) = ВҪОЈ_i|p_iвҲ’q_i|`. A maximal coupling says two action distributions at TV distance at most `Оұ` can be made to choose the same action with probability at least `1вҲ’Оұ`. Then the probability that a coupled rollout has disagreed at least once by time `t` is at most `1вҲ’(1вҲ’Оұ)^t`. When no disagreement has occurred, the two state histories match; when a disagreement has occurred, the expected advantage contribution can differ by at most the worst-case advantage scale. The TRPO bound makes that last step precise as `|E_{ПҖМғ}[AМ„(s_t)] вҲ’ E_ПҖ[AМ„(s_t)]| вүӨ 4ОөОұ(1вҲ’(1вҲ’Оұ)^t)`, with `Оө = max_{s,a}|A_ПҖ(s,a)|`. Summing the discounted error gives

  `ОЈ_{tвүҘ0} Оі^t В· 4ОөОұ(1вҲ’(1вҲ’Оұ)^t)
   = 4ОөОұ(1/(1вҲ’Оі) вҲ’ 1/(1вҲ’Оі(1вҲ’Оұ)))
   = 4ОөОі ОұВІ / ((1вҲ’Оі)(1вҲ’Оі(1вҲ’Оұ)))
   вүӨ 4ОөОі ОұВІ/(1вҲ’Оі)ВІ`.

So the CPI mixture penalty turns into a general-policy trust penalty:

  `О·(ПҖМғ) вүҘ L_ПҖ(ПҖМғ) вҲ’ (4ОөОі/(1вҲ’Оі)ВІ) (D_TV^max)ВІ`.

Now I want KL, because KL is what I can actually compute and differentiate for a Gaussian and it's the natural currency for "how much did the policy distribution move." Pinsker's inequality hands me the bridge: `D_TV(p,q)ВІ вүӨ D_KL(p,q)`. Substitute and the `(D_TV^max)ВІ` becomes a clean `max_s KL`:

  `О·(ПҖМғ) вүҘ L_ПҖ(ПҖМғ) вҲ’ C В· max_s KL[ПҖ(В·|s), ПҖМғ(В·|s)]`,   with `C = 4ОөОі/(1вҲ’Оі)ВІ`, `Оө = max_{s,a}|A_ПҖ(s,a)|`.

The true return is *at least* the surrogate minus a KL penalty. Both `L` and the right-hand side touch `О·` at `ПҖМғ = ПҖ`: there `r_t = 1` so `L_ПҖ(ПҖ) = О·(ПҖ)`, and `KL = 0` so the penalty vanishes, and they agree with `О·` to first order. So define `M_i(ПҖ) = L_{ПҖ_i}(ПҖ) вҲ’ CВ·max_s KL`. Then `О·(ПҖ_{i+1}) вүҘ M_i(ПҖ_{i+1})` from the bound, and `О·(ПҖ_i) = M_i(ПҖ_i)` from the tightness at the current policy. Subtract the two: `О·(ПҖ_{i+1}) вҲ’ О·(ПҖ_i) вүҘ M_i(ПҖ_{i+1}) вҲ’ M_i(ПҖ_i)`. If I pick `ПҖ_{i+1}` to maximize `M_i`, then `M_i(ПҖ_{i+1}) вүҘ M_i(ПҖ_i)`, so the right side is `вүҘ 0` and `О·` does not decrease. That's the chain I needed вҖ” a minorize-maximize step that monotonically improves `О·`. And `M_i` is just the affine-in-the-probabilities surrogate `L` minus a term that grows with how far the policy moves, which is the shape of a proximal update with KL as the move penalty. So the penalty isn't bolted on; it's the price the theory charges for having frozen the state-visitation.

So the theory hands me a concrete objective: each iteration, `maximize_Оё L_{Оё_old}(Оё) вҲ’ C В· max_s KL[ПҖ_old, ПҖ_Оё]`. First-order, one differentiable scalar вҖ” exactly the cheap thing I was after. The whole proposal lives or dies on whether the prescribed penalty leaves any room to move, so put numbers on `C`. With `Оі = 0.99`, `(1вҲ’Оі)ВІ = 10^{-4}`, and being generous with `Оө = 1` (advantages are standardized, so an order-1 scale is about right), `C = 4В·1В·0.99 / 10^{-4} вүҲ 3.96В·10^4`. Now ask what KL that actually permits. The surrogate `ГҠ[r ГӮ]` is order 1; for the penalty `CВ·KL` not to swamp it I'd need `KL вүІ 1/C вүҲ 2.5В·10^{-5}`. That is a vanishingly small policy step вҖ” about three orders of magnitude below the `~10^{-2}` per-update KL I'd actually want. So respecting the theoretical `C` pins `ПҖ_Оё` to `ПҖ_old` and the policy crawls. The bound is honest, but it's a worst-case minorant: it implicitly assumes the advantage scale `Оө` and the `(1вҲ’Оі)ВІ` horizon blowup conspire everywhere at once, which they essentially never do, so the leash it prescribes is far too short to learn with.

What did TRPO do at this same wall? It abandoned the penalty form and switched to a hard constraint. Instead of subtracting `CВ·KL`, bound it: `maximize_Оё L_{Оё_old}(Оё)` subject to `max_s KL вүӨ Оҙ`. The max over states is itself impractical вҖ” too many constraints вҖ” so use the *mean* KL over visited states, `ГҠ_t[ KL[ПҖ_old(В·|s_t), ПҖ_Оё(В·|s_t)] ] вүӨ Оҙ`. Now `Оҙ` is an interpretable knob: a budget on how far, in policy-distribution space, one update is allowed to move. This is far easier to set than `C` because it directly controls the thing I care about вҖ” the size of the step вҖ” rather than a coefficient whose right value depends on the (unknown, drifting) advantage scale `Оө`. TRPO solves it with a linear approximation to the objective and a quadratic approximation to the constraint, which gives a natural-gradient direction computed via conjugate gradient on Fisher-vector products, plus a backtracking line search to enforce the constraint exactly.

And that's a beautiful algorithm, but for *my* goal it's the wrong tool. I wanted first-order and simple. TRPO is second order: Fisher-vector products, conjugate gradient, a line search every update. It's a constrained solve per batch, not the cheap "run a few epochs of minibatch SGD" I was after. And the constrained/Hessian machinery is awkward exactly where I'd like to be flexible вҖ” sharing parameters between the policy and value networks, or using architectures with noise вҖ” because the Fisher and the line search assume a clean policy-only objective. So TRPO trades the penalty's pathology (bad `C`) for a constraint, but pays in complexity I don't want.

The tension is narrow now. The *penalty* form is what I want structurally вҖ” it's just SGD on a single differentiable scalar, trivially compatible with shared nets and several epochs per batch, no second-order anything. Its only sin is that I can't pick `ОІ` (the role `C` plays). The *constraint* form solved the picking problem вҖ” by targeting a KL budget `Оҙ` instead of guessing a coefficient вҖ” but at the cost of the second-order machinery. So why am I choosing between them? The thing that made the constraint usable was *targeting the KL*, not the constraint mechanism itself. What if I keep the cheap penalty form but borrow the constraint's idea of controlling the KL вҖ” by *adjusting* `ОІ` to hit a target KL, instead of fixing it?

Could a fixed `ОІ` work? Suppose I pick some `ОІ` and run `ГҠ_t[ r_t ГӮ_t вҲ’ ОІВ·KL ]`. The worry is that the KL a fixed `ОІ` produces isn't stable вҖ” pin down how unstable with a clean toy model of one update: a single Gaussian-mean parameter `Ој` (std `Пғ` fixed). The surrogate, to first order, pushes the mean with some advantage-driven drive `g = ГҠ[ГӮ вҲӮ_Ој log ПҖ]`, and the penalty is `ОІВ·KL вүҲ ОІВ·О”ОјВІ/(2ПғВІ)`. Maximizing `gВ·О”Ој вҲ’ ОІВ·О”ОјВІ/(2ПғВІ)` over `О”Ој` gives `О”Ој* = gПғВІ/ОІ`, so the achieved step is `KL* = О”Ој*ВІ/(2ПғВІ) = gВІПғВІ/(2ОІВІ)`. The controlled quantity scales as `gВІ/ОІВІ` вҖ” it depends on the *square* of the advantage drive. Now numbers: hold `ОІ = 1`, `Пғ = 1`, and let `g` fall from `1.0` early in training to `0.1` near convergence (advantages shrink as the policy stops finding easy gains). The achieved KL runs `0.50 вҶ’ 0.045 вҶ’ 0.005` вҖ” a 100Г— collapse in the step size, from one fixed coefficient, within a single run. Equivalently, the `ОІ` that *would* hit a target KL of `0.01` ranges from `7.07` at `g=1` down to `0.71` at `g=0.1`, a 10Г— spread. And that's before adding cross-environment differences in curvature `Пғ`, which move it further. So a `ОІ` tuned to land at a sensible KL early will overshoot or starve later, and one tuned on HalfCheetah won't transfer to Hopper. The right penalty isn't a constant вҖ” it's whatever value happens, *right now, on this batch*, to produce the policy step I want. That settles it: there is no single good `ОІ`, even within one run.

So make `ОІ` chase a target. Pick a target KL `d_targ` вҖ” the policy-space step size I'm willing to take per update, the analogue of TRPO's `Оҙ`. After each update, measure the actual KL `d` the policy moved. If I moved too far, `d > d_targ`, the leash was too loose, so tighten it: increase `ОІ`. If I moved too little, `d < d_targ`, loosen: decrease `ОІ`. The controlled quantity is the KL вҖ” the step size in distribution space вҖ” and `ОІ` is just the dial I turn to keep it on target, batch by batch. This is the same logic TRPO used to justify a constraint over a fixed penalty, now realized inside a pure first-order penalty loop.

How should I adjust `ОІ`? Additively or multiplicatively? The same toy model tells me the response of KL to `ОІ`: `KL* = gВІПғВІ/(2ОІВІ)`, so KL is a *decreasing* function of `ОІ` that scales as `1/ОІВІ`. Doubling `ОІ` doesn't subtract a fixed amount of KL, it divides the step by four. So the response lives on a multiplicative scale, and the natural controller is multiplicative too: nudge `ОІ` up or down by a factor, not by an increment. A factor of two per adjustment is a sensible size вҖ” and I can sanity-check that it's enough range to track the drift I measured above: the `ОІ` needed across a run spanned about 10Г—, and since `logвӮӮ(10) вүҲ 3.3`, three or four doublings cover it, so a few updates of `Г—2`/`Г·2` should catch a drifting equilibrium without thrashing. And I don't want to react to every tiny miss, or `ОІ` will oscillate; I want a deadband around `d_targ` where I leave `ОІ` alone. So: if `d > 1.5В·d_targ`, the step was clearly too big вҖ” `ОІ вҶҗ ОІВ·2`. If `d < d_targ/1.5`, clearly too small вҖ” `ОІ вҶҗ ОІ/2`. Otherwise leave it. The `1.5` band and the factor `2` are heuristic; the algorithm should be fairly insensitive to them, since they only set how fast and how tightly `ОІ` tracks rather than the target it tracks toward. By the same `1/ОІВІ` logic the *initial* `ОІ` should matter little: whatever I start with, a handful of doublings or halvings carries it to the right neighborhood within the first updates. I'll start it at something modest like `0.5` and let the controller find the level. Occasionally a single update may overshoot the KL badly; those should be rare, with `ОІ` snapping back on the next one.

Now I have to actually compute the KL penalty on a minibatch, and this is subtler than it looks. I have samples `a_t вҲј ПҖ_old`, and for each I can evaluate both log-probs, so I can form the log-ratio `logratio = log ПҖ_Оё(a_t) вҲ’ log ПҖ_old(a_t)` and the ratio `r_t = exp(logratio)`. The textbook KL `KL[ПҖ_old, ПҖ_Оё] = E_{aвҲјПҖ_old}[ log(ПҖ_old/ПҖ_Оё) ] = E[вҲ’logratio]`. So the naive per-sample estimator of the KL is just `вҲ’logratio`, average it over the batch. It's unbiased вҖ” its mean is the KL by definition. But is it a good estimator to *use*? KL is always nonnegative, yet `вҲ’logratio` is negative for any sample where the new policy happens to assign higher probability than the old, which should be roughly half the samples near `Оё_old`. Let me put a number on how bad that is. Take two close 1-D Gaussians вҖ” old `N(0,1)`, new `N(0.15, 1.05)`, a realistic small policy move вҖ” draw 2В·10вҒө actions from the old, and form both estimators. The true KL has a closed form here, `0.01251`. The naive `вҲ’logratio` gives mean `0.01296` (close, as expected) but standard deviation `0.151`, and 35% of its per-sample values are negative, with a minimum of `вҲ’1.44`. So a finite minibatch can genuinely report a *negative* "KL," which is nonsense for a quantity I'm scaling by `ОІ` and feeding to the optimizer as a penalty. I want something unbiased, low-variance, and вҖ” ideally вҖ” nonnegative on every sample, so the penalty is honestly a penalty on each minibatch.

Lower the variance with a control variate. I want to add to `вҲ’logratio` some quantity with zero mean (so I don't bias the estimate) that's negatively correlated with `вҲ’logratio` (so it cancels the swings). The one obviously-zero-mean quantity available is `r вҲ’ 1`: under `a вҲј ПҖ_old`, `E[r] = E[ПҖ_Оё/ПҖ_old] = ОЈ ПҖ_old В· (ПҖ_Оё/ПҖ_old) = ОЈ ПҖ_Оё = 1`, so `E[r вҲ’ 1] = 0` exactly, for free. (In the Gaussian experiment the sample mean of `r вҲ’ 1` came out `вҲ’5В·10^{-4}`, i.e. zero to sampling noise вҖ” the control variate really is unbiased.) So for any scalar `c`, `вҲ’logratio + c(r вҲ’ 1)` is still an unbiased estimator of the KL. Now pick `c` not by minimizing variance analytically (that needs `p` and `q` I don't have in closed form) but by a cleaner criterion: make the estimator nonnegative pointwise. Since `log` is concave, `log x вүӨ x вҲ’ 1` for all `x > 0`, i.e. `(x вҲ’ 1) вҲ’ log x вүҘ 0` always, with equality at `x = 1`. So if I take `c = 1`,

  `KL estimate per sample = (r вҲ’ 1) вҲ’ log r вүҘ 0`,

which is exactly the vertical gap between the tangent line `x вҲ’ 1` and the curve `log x`. Back to the same experiment to see whether the theory pays off: `(rвҲ’1) вҲ’ logratio` gives mean `0.01247` (still matching the true `0.01251`, so unbiased), but standard deviation `0.035` вҖ” about 4.3Г— tighter than the naive estimator's `0.151` вҖ” and its minimum over all 2В·10вҒө samples is `+4В·10^{-12}`, i.e. nonnegative to floating point, never once negative. So both claims hold on real numbers: the control variate cuts the variance several-fold and removes the sign-swinging entirely, because near `r = 1` the gap-to-tangent is small and smoothly varying instead of flipping sign. That's the estimator I'll use for the penalty: `kl = mean( (r вҲ’ 1) вҲ’ logratio )`, where `logratio = log r`. And crucially I keep its gradient вҖ” this isn't a diagnostic, it's the term `ОІВ·KL` in the loss that the optimizer differentiates to pull `ПҖ_Оё` back toward `ПҖ_old`. The very same expression, detached, is also the clean nonnegative number I read off to decide whether to raise or lower `ОІ`.

I still need `ГӮ_t`, and I should pin down its bias-variance behavior rather than reach for a default. The one-step option is the TD residual `Оҙ_t = reward_t + Оі V(s_{t+1}) вҲ’ V(s_t)`. If `V` were exactly `V_ПҖ`, then `E[Оҙ_t] = A_ПҖ(s_t,a_t)` exactly, so `Оҙ_t` is a low-variance advantage estimate вҖ” but it's biased whenever `V` is imperfect, which it always is early on. The opposite extreme uses the empirical return: `ГӮ_t = ОЈ_l Оі^l reward_{t+l} вҲ’ V(s_t)`, unbiased given a correct value baseline but high variance because it sums a whole noisy trajectory. Between them, sum `k` residuals: `ГӮ_t^(k) = ОЈ_{l=0}^{kвҲ’1} Оі^l Оҙ_{t+l}`, which telescopes to `вҲ’V(s_t) + ОЈ_{l=0}^{kвҲ’1} Оі^l reward_{t+l} + Оі^k V(s_{t+k})` вҖ” bigger `k` leans more on real rewards (less value-function bias, more variance). I don't want to pick a single `k`; I want to blend them. Take the exponentially-weighted average with weight `О»^{kвҲ’1}` on `ГӮ_t^(k)`, normalized by `(1вҲ’О»)`:

  `ГӮ_t = (1вҲ’О»)( ГӮ_t^(1) + О» ГӮ_t^(2) + О»ВІ ГӮ_t^(3) + ... )`.

Substitute `ГӮ_t^(k) = ОЈ_{l=0}^{kвҲ’1} Оі^l Оҙ_{t+l}` and collect the coefficient of each `Оҙ_{t+l}`. The residual `Оҙ_{t+l}` (carrying its `Оі^l`) appears in every `ГӮ_t^(k)` with `k > l`, so its total weight is `(1вҲ’О») Оі^l (О»^l + О»^{l+1} + ...) = (1вҲ’О») Оі^l О»^l / (1вҲ’О») = (ОіО»)^l`. Everything should collapse to

  `ГӮ_t = ОЈ_{l=0}^{вҲһ} (ОіО»)^l Оҙ_{t+l}`.

Check it on numbers, since that kind of telescoping is easy to get wrong on paper. Take `Оі = 0.99`, `О» = 0.95`, and eight arbitrary residuals `Оҙ_{t..t+7}` drawn at random. Computing the weighted blend `ОЈ_k (1вҲ’О»)О»^{kвҲ’1} ГӮ_t^(k)` directly (giving the tail past the truncation horizon its remaining weight on `ГӮ_t^(8)`, which is what a finite segment does) and comparing to the closed form `ОЈ_l (ОіО»)^l Оҙ_{t+l}`, the two agree to `4В·10^{-16}` вҖ” machine precision. So the collapse is real, not a hopeful rearrangement: one discounted sum of TD residuals reproduces the whole exponentially-weighted family. And `О»` is the bias-variance dial: `О» = 0` gives `ГӮ_t = Оҙ_t` (lowest variance, most value-function bias), `О» = 1` gives the Monte-Carlo `ОЈ Оі^l r_{t+l} вҲ’ V(s_t)` (unbiased, highest variance). In a finite length-`T` segment I truncate the sum, which I compute cheaply backward: `ГӮ_t = Оҙ_t + ОіО»В·ГӮ_{t+1}`, masking across episode boundaries with `(1 вҲ’ done)`. Defaults `Оі = 0.99`, `О» = 0.95` sit near the low-variance end where the learned value function does most of the work but a little real reward leaks in. And the value target for fitting `V` is just `ГӮ_t + V(s_t)`, the GAE return.

Now assemble the full loss on a minibatch. The policy surrogate is `вҲ’ГҠ[ r_t ГӮ_t ]` (negative because I minimize) plus the penalty `+ ОІВ·kl`. Concretely with the standardized advantages already in hand,

  `pg_loss = вҲ’mean( ГӮ_t В· r_t ) + ОІ В· kl`.

I want a value function too, and I'll fit it by plain regression to the GAE returns, `v_loss = ВҪВ·mean( (V_Оё(s_t) вҲ’ R_t)ВІ )` вҖ” squared error, no clipping, the straightforward critic loss. And following the actor-critic tradition I add an entropy bonus to keep the policy from collapsing its exploration prematurely; for a diagonal Gaussian the entropy has a closed form and the bonus just rewards keeping the action distribution wide. Combine with coefficients: `loss = pg_loss вҲ’ c_entВ·entropy + c_vfВ·v_loss` (entropy enters with a minus because more entropy is good and I'm minimizing). Then the outer machinery is the actor-critic loop I already have: `N` parallel actors each collect `T` steps; compute `ГӮ` and returns by GAE on the segment; snapshot `ПҖ_old`; run `K` epochs of minibatch SGD with Adam on `loss`; read the detached KL values produced by those policy evaluations and adjust `ОІ` by the controller for subsequent minibatch calls; repeat. The only reason it's safe to do `K` epochs on one batch вҖ” the thing vanilla policy gradient couldn't do вҖ” is that the `ОІВ·KL` penalty keeps each pass proximal, with `ОІ` self-tuned so the update stays near `d_targ` in policy space.

Two lines in the slot are the only subtle part; the rest is direct transcription of what's already derived. Re-evaluating the current policy on the stored old-policy actions gives `logratio = newlogprob - mb_logprobs` and `ratio = logratio.exp()`; the KL estimator, kept *with* gradient, is `kl = ((ratio - 1) - logratio).mean()` вҖ” this literal expression is the `ОІВ·kl` penalty term itself, not a diagnostic computed on the side. Then

  `pg_loss = -(mb_advantages * ratio).mean() + beta * kl`,

and, reading the same `kl` value detached as `approx_kl`, the controller runs under `no_grad` so the mutation doesn't touch the graph:

```python
if approx_kl > 1.5 * agent._target_kl:
    agent._kl_beta = min(agent._kl_beta * 2.0, 100.0)
elif approx_kl < agent._target_kl / 1.5:
    agent._kl_beta = max(agent._kl_beta / 2.0, 1e-4)
```

with the clamps at `100` and `1e-4` just guardrails against a pathological batch sending `ОІ` to zero or infinity. Everything else the slot needs вҖ” the squared-error `v_loss`, the entropy bonus, the combination `loss = pg_loss - c_entВ·entropy + c_vfВ·v_loss` вҖ” is exactly the formulas already worked out above, wired into the harness's `compute_losses(agent, mb_obs, mb_actions, mb_logprobs, mb_advantages, mb_returns, mb_values, args)` signature with `ОІ` and `d_targ` carried as state on the agent across minibatch calls, initialized once on first use.
