The symptom that started this is concrete and expensive: pretraining a large decoder-only Transformer in bfloat16, the validation loss descends nicely and then, without warning, spikes — sometimes recoverable, sometimes not — and the larger the model, the more often it happens. Underneath the spikes there is a slower companion: the global gradient norm drifts upward over the run, and the spike frequency rises with it. Global-norm gradient clipping is enabled and capped at $1.0$, yet the spikes come anyway, which is itself a clue: clipping is rescaling a step that was already produced from a bad state, so it is treating a symptom downstream of the real cause. The usual response — restart from an earlier checkpoint and skip data to dodge the spike — is a band-aid that burns compute. We want to find what is drifting and stop it at the source. Almost every other part of the forward pass has normalization stapled to it that holds its scale in check; the one place a wide vector of unbounded real numbers gets pushed through an exponential with nothing normalizing it first is the final softmax over the vocabulary. That is the suspect. The existing tools at this layer do not address it: label smoothing softens the one-hot target, which deliberately changes *what* distribution is modeled and only constrains the logit gaps between classes; gradient-norm clipping is reactive and fires after the bad step is computed; tightening the clip to force stability is known to degrade the loss. None of these touches the actual mechanism, so the need is for an output-layer change that keeps the same maximum-likelihood objective while removing the numerical obstacle.

To find that obstacle I write cross-entropy at one position not as it is usually quoted but in a form I can manipulate. With logit vector $l = (l_1, \dots, l_V)$, predicted probability $p_j = \exp(l_j)/Z$ and partition function $Z = \sum_k \exp(l_k)$, the cross-entropy against the true next token $y$ is

$$\mathrm{CE} = -\log p_y = -(l_y - \log Z) = \log Z - l_y,$$

with $\log Z = \log\sum_k \exp(l_k)$ the log-partition. So the loss decomposes exactly into two pieces pulling opposite ways: raise the true-token logit $l_y$, lower the log-partition $\log Z$. Now the crux — cross-entropy depends on the logits only through their differences. Add a constant to every logit, $l \to l + c\mathbf{1}$: the softmax does not move, $\exp(l_j + c)/\sum_k \exp(l_k + c) = p_j$ because the $e^c$ cancels, so $\mathrm{CE}$ is unchanged. Yet $l_y \to l_y + c$ and $\log Z \to \log Z + c$, and inside $\mathrm{CE} = \log Z - l_y$ the two shifts cancel. Cross-entropy is *exactly* invariant to a uniform shift of all logits. This means there is a whole one-parameter family $l + c\mathbf{1}$ producing identical predictions and identical loss, along which $\log Z$ ranges over the entire real line while the loss the optimizer sees stays constant. Cross-entropy pins down the logit *gaps* and says nothing about the overall *level*. The level is a free gauge, and with no restoring force from the loss, the documented behavior is that it drifts *upward*: the activations feeding the final softmax grow over the run. That is exactly the slow gradient-norm growth, and the loss is indifferent to the one quantity — the absolute logit level, equivalently $\log Z$ — that is drifting. Why is a drifting $\log Z$ dangerous here specifically? Because these large numbers go straight into an exponential, in bfloat16, which keeps float32's $8$ exponent bits but only $7$ mantissa bits against $23$. A float holds a fixed number of mantissa bits within each octave $[2^k, 2^{k+1})$, so the unit in the last place scales like $2^k \cdot 2^{-\text{mantissa}}$ — absolute roundoff grows with magnitude, and bfloat16 rounds about $2^{23-7} = 65536$ times more coarsely than float32. Since $d(\exp x) = \exp(x)\,dx$, a small *absolute* error in the argument becomes a *relative* error of the same size in the output, and that error grows as the logits drift up. Concretely, ten logits at $128$ and one at $128.5$ in bfloat16: the $0.5$ gap is at the rounding threshold and can round away, collapsing the softmax weight on the distinguished token from $\approx 0.142$ to $\approx 0.091$, a $36\%$ swing manufactured entirely by roundoff. The free gauge lets the logits drift large, large logits are where the bfloat16 exponential becomes unfaithful, and an unfaithful exponential that tips over is a spike. The slow growth and the sudden discontinuity are one disease at two timescales.

I propose Z-loss: an auxiliary penalty added to next-token cross-entropy that squares the log-partition of the final softmax, supplying the restoring force on the gauge that cross-entropy by construction cannot. I want to pin the gauge to the small, symmetric, numerically-safe target $\log Z = 0$, i.e. $Z = 1$, but as a soft nudge the optimizer trades off rather than a hard projection that would fight it. So I add a penalty $\phi(\log Z)$ minimized at $\log Z = 0$. The shape matters and is forced by three rejections. The signed penalty $\log Z$ has no minimum — it would shove $\log Z \to -\infty$ — so the penalty must be symmetric, treating an excursion in either direction as equally far from the normalized slice. The absolute value $|\log Z|$ is symmetric and minimized at $0$ but has a kink there: its gradient is $\mathrm{sign}(\log Z)$, a constant-strength pull right up to the minimum that keeps yanking even when $\log Z$ is already tiny, and it is nonsmooth exactly where I want the system to settle. The square $(\log Z)^2$ is symmetric, smooth and convex with its minimum at $0$, and — the property I actually want — its gradient $2\log Z$ is *proportional to the violation*: gentle when $\log Z$ is near $0$, stronger the further it has drifted. That is precisely a restoring force, so the penalty is $(\log Z)^2$ and the augmented per-position objective is

$$L = \mathrm{CE} + \lambda\,(\log Z)^2 = (\log Z - l_y) + \lambda\,(\log Z)^2.$$

The gradient confirms it does what I think. The key derivative is $\partial \log Z / \partial l_j = \exp(l_j)/Z = p_j$, the token's own softmax probability. The cross-entropy part gives the familiar $\partial(\log Z - l_y)/\partial l_j = p_j - \mathbf{1}\{j = y\}$, and the penalty part, by the chain rule, gives $\partial[\lambda(\log Z)^2]/\partial l_j = 2\lambda\log Z \cdot p_j$. Stacking them,

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial l_j} = (p_j - \mathbf{1}\{j = y\}) + 2\lambda\,\log Z\,p_j.$$

The new term is a probability-weighted pull: when $\log Z > 0$ (the dangerous drift), it is positive on every coordinate so the descent step subtracts it, pushing down hardest on the logits that contribute most to $Z$ and shrinking $\log Z$ toward $0$; when $\log Z < 0$ it reverses and pushes those same coordinates back up. Its strength $2\lambda\log Z$ is proportional to how far the gauge has drifted — the squared penalty's signature — and because its scalar driver is the otherwise-free $\log Z$, cross-entropy remains the term that primarily sculpts the probability gaps. Crucially, pinning the gauge provably bounds the magnitude that feeds the exponential. Since $Z \ge \exp(\max_k l_k)$ and $Z \le V\exp(\max_k l_k)$,

$$\max_k l_k \le \log Z \le \max_k l_k + \log V,$$

so $\log Z$ and the largest logit differ by at most $\log V$ — for a $50\text{k}$ vocabulary, about $11$ nats. Holding $\log Z$ near $0$ caps the top logits (the ones that dominate the exp and the softmax) from becoming large and positive, putting the exponential back in the regime where bfloat16 stays faithful. The same target carries a bonus: $\log Z = 0 \iff \sum_k \exp(l_k) = 1$, so the raw logits behave like *normalized* log-probabilities — $l_j \approx \log p_j$ directly, not up to an arbitrary additive constant — meaning the gauge-fixing and the "make the logits honest" goals are the same move. This is the right lever where the alternatives are not. Label smoothing changes the target distribution and only touches gaps, the gauge-invariant part, so it cannot see the level at all; Z-loss is its complement, touching the level while leaving the target distribution and the CE term intact, so it sits on top of plain cross-entropy without becoming label smoothing in disguise. Logit clipping clamps values *after* they are already roundoff-corrupted and introduces its own discontinuity, whereas the smooth penalty acts *during* optimization to discourage large logits from ever being produced. Gradient-norm clipping is further downstream still, acting after the bad step. For the coefficient, an order-of-magnitude argument fixes $\lambda = 1\mathrm{e}{-4}$: cross-entropy per token is $O(1)$ nats and a healthy $(\log Z)^2$ is $O(1)$, so $\lambda = 1\mathrm{e}{-4}$ makes the penalty about a ten-thousandth of the CE scale — invisible to the modeling objective — while its gradient $2\lambda\log Z$ still applies a persistent downward pull that accumulates over many steps to hold the gauge. Much larger and it would crush $\log Z$ and compete with cross-entropy for control of the logits, hurting quality; much smaller and the restoring force loses to the drift. Two implementation details are load-bearing: the penalty must be reduced over exactly the same valid positions cross-entropy uses (mask to $\text{target} \neq -1$, then mean), both because ignored positions carry garbage logits and because matching the per-token count is what makes $1\mathrm{e}{-4}$ mean what the scale argument says; and $\log Z$ must be computed with the max-subtraction-stabilized $\mathrm{logsumexp}$, $\log Z = \max_k l_k + \log\sum_k \exp(l_k - \max_k l_k)$, so I do not introduce an overflow while fixing one. It drops straight into the `compute_loss` slot as ordinary cross-entropy plus a one-line auxiliary term — no temperature, no target smoothing, no clipped logits.

```python
import torch
import torch.nn.functional as F


def compute_loss(logits, targets):
    """Cross-entropy with z-loss regularization.

    z-loss penalizes the squared log-partition (log Z)^2 of the final softmax,
    putting a smooth restoring force on the otherwise-free overall logit level
    so the logits stay small and the low-precision softmax stays numerically
    faithful. lambda = 1e-4 makes it a regularizer, not a second objective.
    """
    flat_logits = logits.view(-1, logits.size(-1))      # (B*T, V)
    flat_targets = targets.view(-1)                      # (B*T,)

    # Primary maximum-likelihood objective: CE = log Z - l_y,
    # averaged over valid positions (ignore_index=-1).
    ce_loss = F.cross_entropy(flat_logits, flat_targets, ignore_index=-1)

    # Auxiliary softmax z-loss:
    # add lambda * square(log_z), where log_z is the softmax log-partition.
    log_z = torch.logsumexp(flat_logits, dim=-1)         # log Z per position
    mask = flat_targets != -1                            # same valid positions as CE
    z_loss = (log_z[mask] ** 2).mean()                   # mean over valid positions

    return ce_loss + 1e-4 * z_loss
```
