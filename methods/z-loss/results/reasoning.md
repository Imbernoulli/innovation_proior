Let me start from what actually goes wrong, because the symptom is concrete: I'm pretraining a big decoder-only Transformer in bfloat16, and the run keeps blowing up. The loss sits on its nice downward curve and then, with no warning, jumps — a spike — sometimes recoverable, sometimes not, and the bigger I make the model the more often it happens. Gradient clipping is on, the global norm is capped at 1.0, and the spikes come anyway, which already tells me something: whatever is going wrong has produced a state from which even a norm-limited step is bad, so clipping is treating a symptom downstream of the real problem. And there's a slower companion to the spikes — the gradient norm tends to drift upward over the run, growing the longer I train, and the spike frequency rises with it. So I have two coupled things: a slow growth and sudden discontinuities. I don't want to keep restarting from checkpoints and skipping data batches to dodge spikes; that's a band-aid that costs real compute. I want to know what's drifting, and stop it at the source.

Where would a drift like that even live? The training loop has a lot of moving parts, but most of them — the attention, the MLPs, the layer norms — have normalization stapled to them that keeps their scales in check. The one place in the whole forward pass where a wide vector of unbounded real numbers gets pushed through an exponential with nothing normalizing it first is the final softmax over the vocabulary. That's the suspect. So let me stare hard at exactly that layer and write down what the objective there really is, not the way it's usually quoted but in a form I can manipulate.

For one position, I have a logit vector `l = (l_1, ..., l_V)` over the vocabulary, the predicted distribution is `p_j = exp(l_j)/Z` with the normalizer `Z = sum_k exp(l_k)`, and the cross-entropy against the true next token `y` is the negative log-probability of that token. Write it out:

  `CE = -log p_y = -( l_y - log Z ) = log Z - l_y`,

where `log Z = log sum_k exp(l_k)` is the log of the partition function. So the loss I'm minimizing decomposes, exactly, into two pieces pulling in opposite directions: raise the true-token logit `l_y`, and lower the log-partition `log Z`. That's the whole game cross-entropy plays.

Cross-entropy depends on the logits *only through their differences*. Let me actually check that, because it's the crux. Add a constant `c` to every single logit, `l -> l + c·1`. The softmax doesn't move at all — `exp(l_j + c)/sum_k exp(l_k + c) = e^c exp(l_j) / (e^c sum_k exp(l_k)) = p_j`, the `e^c` cancels. So `CE` is unchanged. But look at what happened to the two pieces individually: `l_y -> l_y + c`, and `Z -> e^c Z` so `log Z -> log Z + c`. Both terms moved by `+c`, and inside `CE = log Z - l_y` the two `c`'s cancel. The cross-entropy is *exactly* invariant to a uniform shift of all the logits.

Sit with that for a second, because it's the actual problem. There is a whole one-parameter family of logit vectors — `l + c·1` for every real `c` — that produce identical predictions and identical cross-entropy. Along that family, the predicted distribution is frozen, but `log Z` ranges over the entire real line: as `c -> +infinity`, `log Z -> +infinity`, and the individual logits grow without bound, all while the loss the optimizer sees is *constant*. Cross-entropy pins down the logit *gaps* — the differences that set the probabilities — and says nothing whatsoever about the overall *level*. The level is a free gauge. The optimizer has no instruction, from the loss, about where to sit along that gauge orbit.

So which way does the gauge actually drift in practice? Not randomly, and not down. There's gradient flow into the logits from a lot of places, weight decay and the optimizer interact with the embedding/unembedding, and there's no restoring force on the level — any push that inflates the logits is free as far as the loss is concerned, so the level wanders, and the documented behavior is that it wanders *up*: the activations feeding the final softmax grow over the run, the model getting more and more extreme in magnitude without the loss objecting. That is exactly the slow growth I'm seeing in the gradient norm. The cross-entropy is indifferent to the one quantity — the absolute logit level, equivalently `log Z` — that I now suspect is drifting.

And why is a drifting `log Z` dangerous *here* specifically, in bfloat16? Because of where these large numbers go: straight into an exponential. bfloat16 keeps float32's eight exponent bits, so it has the same enormous range, but it has only seven mantissa bits against float32's twenty-three. A float stores a fixed number of mantissa bits within each octave `[2^k, 2^{k+1})`, so the gap between representable numbers — the unit in the last place — scales like `2^k · 2^{-mantissa}`, which means the absolute roundoff error *grows with the magnitude of the number*. Two facts fall out: for the same number, bfloat16 rounds about `2^{23-7} = 65536` times more coarsely than float32; and the larger a number gets, the coarser its rounding. Now push a large, coarsely-rounded logit through `exp`. The differential is `d(exp x) = exp(x)·dx`, so a small *absolute* error `dx` in the argument becomes a *relative* error of size `dx` in the output of the exponential. Make the logits large and `dx` (the roundoff) grows with them, so the softmax outputs get corrupted by an amount that grows as the logits drift up. I can make this concrete to convince myself it's not marginal: ten logits at 128 and one at 128.5 in bfloat16 — the 0.5 gap is at the rounding threshold for that magnitude and can round away, and the softmax weight on the distinguished token collapses from about 0.142 to about 0.091, a 36% swing, entirely manufactured by roundoff. That's the mechanism. The free gauge lets the logits drift large; large logits are where bfloat16's exponential becomes unfaithful; an unfaithful exponential corrupts the softmax and, when it tips over, produces a spike. The slow growth and the sudden discontinuity are the same disease seen at two timescales.

So I need a restoring force on the gauge. Cross-entropy won't supply it — by construction it can't, it's gauge-invariant — so I have to add something that *does* care about the level. What do I want that something to prefer? I want the logits not to drift large; equivalently I want `log Z` not to drift large. The cleanest target value to pin the gauge to is `log Z = 0`, i.e. `Z = 1`, i.e. `sum_k exp(l_k) = 1`. If I had freedom to choose the gauge `c` for a given prediction, the `c*` that sets `log Z = 0` is `c* = -log Z(l)`, and it leaves the predictions untouched (it's a gauge move) while killing the magnitude. So the *hard* version of what I want is "project onto the slice `log Z = 0` of every gauge orbit." But I don't want to hard-project — that's a constraint I'd have to enforce, and it would fight the optimizer. I want a *soft* nudge: add a penalty to the loss that is minimized when `log Z = 0` and grows as `log Z` moves away from it, then let the optimizer trade it off against the cross-entropy.

What's the right shape for that penalty as a function of `log Z`? Call `s = log Z`. I want a function `phi(s)` minimized at `s = 0`. The obvious candidates: `|s|`, `s^2`, or — tempting but wrong — just `s`. Penalizing `s` itself (signed) has no minimum; it would shove `log Z -> -infinity`, sending the whole gauge level downward without ever settling. So it must be symmetric in `s`, penalizing large excursions in either direction: the representative with `Z` near 0 is not more meaningful than the representative with `Z` huge; both are far from the normalized-log-probability slice I want. `|s|` is symmetric and minimized at 0, but it has a kink at `s = 0`: its gradient is `sign(s)`, a constant pull of fixed strength right up to the minimum, which keeps yanking even when `log Z` is already tiny, and it's nonsmooth exactly where I want the system to settle. `s^2` is symmetric, minimized at 0, smooth everywhere including at the minimum, convex, and — this is the property I actually want — its gradient is `2s`, *proportional to the violation*: gentle when `log Z` is already near 0, stronger the further it has drifted. That's exactly the behavior of a restoring force; it leaves a well-behaved gauge alone and pulls hard only on a runaway. So the penalty is `(log Z)^2`. I'll square the log-partition.

Let me write the augmented per-position objective and, more importantly, work out its gradient, because the gradient is what tells me whether this actually does what I think:

  `L = CE + lambda·(log Z)^2 = (log Z - l_y) + lambda·(log Z)^2`,

with `lambda` a small coefficient I'll pin down later. I need the gradient with respect to each logit `l_j`. The key derivative is `d(log Z)/d l_j`. Since `log Z = log sum_k exp(l_k)`,

  `d(log Z)/d l_j = exp(l_j) / sum_k exp(l_k) = exp(l_j)/Z = p_j`.

So the derivative of the log-partition with respect to a logit is just that token's softmax probability — a clean fact I'll lean on. Now the cross-entropy part: `d(log Z - l_y)/d l_j = p_j - 1{j = y}`, the familiar "predicted minus one-hot." And the penalty part, by the chain rule:

  `d/d l_j [ lambda·(log Z)^2 ] = lambda · 2 log Z · d(log Z)/d l_j = 2 lambda·log Z · p_j`.

Stack them:

  `dL/d l_j = (p_j - 1{j=y}) + 2 lambda·log Z · p_j`.

Read what the new term does. It's `2 lambda log Z · p_j` — a probability-weighted pull on every logit. When `log Z > 0` (the logits have drifted up, the dangerous case), this term is positive on every coordinate, weighted by `p_j`, so the gradient descent step *subtracts* it: it pushes down hardest on the logits that currently contribute most to `Z`, shrinking `log Z` back toward 0. When `log Z < 0` it reverses sign and pushes those same high-probability coordinates back up. It is a restoring force on the gauge, and its strength `2 lambda log Z` is proportional to how far the gauge has drifted — the squared penalty's signature. It is not a perfectly uniform shift, so it can still alter gaps at finite `lambda`; the point is that its scalar driver is the otherwise-free `log Z`, while cross-entropy remains the term that primarily sculpts the probability gaps. With `lambda` tiny, the new term fixes the level without replacing maximum likelihood as the main objective.

Now does pinning the gauge actually bound the thing I care about — the logit magnitudes that feed the exponential? I want a guarantee, not a hope. The log-sum-exp is sandwiched: on one side, `Z = sum_k exp(l_k) >= exp(max_k l_k)`, so `log Z >= max_k l_k`. On the other, `Z <= V · exp(max_k l_k)`, so `log Z <= max_k l_k + log V`. Putting them together,

  `max_k l_k <= log Z <= max_k l_k + log V`.

So `log Z` and the largest logit differ by at most `log V`: equivalently, `log Z - log V <= max_k l_k <= log Z`. If the penalty holds `log Z` near 0, then the largest logit cannot become large and positive; for a 50k vocabulary it lives no more than about 11 nats below `log Z` and never above it. This does not pin every logit to a point — low-probability logits can be very negative — but it caps the top logits, the ones that dominate the exponential and the softmax probabilities. That is the difference between "the exponent sees numbers near the normal log-probability scale" and "the exponent is free to see 128." That's precisely the regime where bfloat16's exponential stays faithful, where `dx` roundoff is small because the relevant numbers themselves are small. The penalty doesn't just *feel* like it should help numerically; it provably caps the largest number going into the exp.

That same target has another consequence. I chose to pin `log Z` to 0 because 0 is the small, symmetric, numerically-safe target, and `log Z = 0` means `sum_k exp(l_k) = 1`. Combine that with what cross-entropy is already doing — driving the softmax to match the targets — and the raw logits themselves start to behave like *normalized* log-probabilities: `exp(l_j)` summing to one means `l_j` is approximately `log p_j` directly, not up to an arbitrary additive constant. So the same penalty that keeps the numbers small also encourages the logits to *be* honest log-probabilities rather than log-probabilities-plus-junk. The gauge-fixing and the "make the logits meaningful" goal are the same move. I didn't have to choose between them.

Let me make sure I'm not reinventing something already on the table, and that I've picked the right lever. The standard over-confidence fix at this layer is label smoothing — soften the one-hot target into `(1-eps)` on the true class plus `eps/V` spread over the rest, so the loss becomes a mixture of the true cross-entropy and a uniform-target cross-entropy. Does that solve my problem? No, and seeing why sharpens what my penalty is for. Label smoothing changes the *target distribution* — it deliberately fits the model to a softened distribution instead of the data, trading a bit of true log-likelihood for calibration. That is the wrong contract for this problem: I want to keep modeling the same next-token distribution, not lower a number by changing what is being modeled. And mechanically, label smoothing acts on the logit *gap* between the true class and the rest — it caps how far apart the classes get pushed — which is a statement about *differences*, the gauge-invariant part. It does nothing to the free *level*. It's pulling on the wrong handle: the gauge is exactly the direction label smoothing can't see. My penalty is the complement — it touches the level through `log Z`, leaves the target distribution alone, and so it can sit on top of plain cross-entropy without becoming label smoothing in disguise. Different lever, and the right one for a numerical drift.

What about clipping the logits directly — just cap them at some value before the softmax? That's the brute-force version and it's worse, for a reason that's specific to the roundoff story. The whole point is that large numbers are *already* corrupted by roundoff by the time they exist; clipping happens *after* that corruption, so it clamps an already-wrong value, and a hard clip is itself a discontinuity — in effect another roundoff, a sharp kink the optimizer has to route gradients around. The penalty, by contrast, is smooth and acts *during* optimization to discourage the model from ever producing the large logits in the first place, so the numbers are small and faithful before they hit the exp, not clamped after they've already gone bad. Prevention at the source beats a hard cap downstream. And the reactive option I already have, gradient-norm clipping, is even further downstream — it acts after the bad step has been computed. The penalty is the only one of these that addresses the cause.

Now `lambda`. It has two jobs to balance and that fixes its scale by an order-of-magnitude argument rather than a guess. It must be small enough that the penalty is a gentle regularizer and cross-entropy stays essentially the maximum-likelihood loss, with the penalty only nudging the gauge, or I'd be back to changing what's modeled. And it must be large enough that the restoring force actually keeps `log Z` near 0 against whatever is inflating it. Scale check: cross-entropy per token is order one-to-ten nats early and settles to a few nats; a *healthy* `log Z` (when the gauge is well-behaved) is order one, so `(log Z)^2` is order one too. If I take `lambda = 1e-4`, the penalty contributes on the order of `1e-4` against a cross-entropy of order one — about a ten-thousandth of the CE scale — yet with gradient `2 lambda log Z = 2e-4 · log Z` it still applies a steady downward pull on the level that accumulates over many steps to hold `log Z` down. That's the sweet spot: invisible to the modeling objective, persistent on the gauge. Much larger and the penalty would start to dominate, crushing `log Z` so hard it pins it essentially to zero and begins competing with cross-entropy for control of the logits, which would hurt quality. Much smaller and the restoring force is too weak to win against the drift. `1e-4` is the canonical large-vocabulary coefficient that keeps the penalty a regularizer and not a second objective; I'll use it.

A couple of implementation details I have to get right or the whole thing is subtly wrong. First, reduction: cross-entropy here ignores padding / packed-boundary positions, the ones whose target is the sentinel `-1`, and averages over the valid positions. My penalty has to be reduced the *same* way — averaged over exactly the valid positions — for two reasons. The logits at ignored positions are garbage I never train, so penalizing their `log Z` is meaningless and would inject noise. And if I averaged the penalty over a different count than cross-entropy uses, the effective `lambda` would no longer be the clean ratio I just reasoned about; the two terms have to be on the same per-token footing for `1e-4` to mean what I think it means. So: mask to `target != -1`, take the mean of `(log Z)^2` over those positions.

Second, numerics of computing `log Z` itself — I'd be embarrassed to introduce an instability while fixing one. `log Z = log sum_k exp(l_k)` computed naively overflows the moment any logit is largish, which is the whole situation I'm in. The standard stabilized log-sum-exp subtracts the max first: `log Z = max_k l_k + log sum_k exp(l_k - max_k l_k)`, which is exact and never exponentiates a positive argument. I'll use the library `logsumexp` primitive, which does exactly this. Note the satisfying loop: the same max-subtraction trick that makes `logsumexp` safe is the operation that, in the corrupted-128.5 example earlier, *was* the source of roundoff trouble when the logits were huge — which is one more reason to keep the logits small in the first place, and exactly what my penalty does.

Third, the code should keep the loss exactly in the simple softmax-loss shape I just derived. The primitive computation is: compute `log_z = logsumexp(logits)`, form `log_softmax = logits - log_z`, take the negative true-token log-softmax as cross-entropy, and then, if the auxiliary coefficient is nonzero, add `z_loss * log_z^2` at that same per-position shape. In the hard-target `compute_loss(logits, targets)` slot I can delegate the first part to the library cross-entropy, but the auxiliary term must still be literally the same per-position `log_z` squared. That keeps the implementation faithful to the loss: no temperature, no target smoothing, no clipped logits, just the ordinary cross-entropy plus the small log-partition penalty.

Let me put it together against the actual interface I have to fill — a function that takes the logit tensor of shape `(B, T, V)` and the targets of shape `(B, T)`, with `-1` ignored, and returns the scalar to backpropagate. Flatten the batch and time into one axis of positions, get cross-entropy the usual way (it handles the ignore index and the averaging), compute `log Z` per position with the stabilized primitive, mask to the valid positions, square, average, and add with the small coefficient:

```python
import torch
import torch.nn.functional as F


def compute_loss(logits, targets):
    # logits: (B, T, V); targets: (B, T) with -1 marking ignored positions.
    flat_logits = logits.view(-1, logits.size(-1))     # (B*T, V)
    flat_targets = targets.view(-1)                     # (B*T,)

    # Primary objective: plain next-token cross-entropy, CE = log Z - l_y,
    # averaged over valid positions (ignore_index=-1).
    ce_loss = F.cross_entropy(flat_logits, flat_targets, ignore_index=-1)

    # Auxiliary softmax z-loss:
    # add lambda * square(log_z), where log_z = logsumexp(logits) is the
    # max-subtraction-stabilized softmax log-partition.
    log_z = torch.logsumexp(flat_logits, dim=-1)        # (B*T,)
    mask = flat_targets != -1                            # same valid positions as CE
    z_loss = (log_z[mask] ** 2).mean()                  # mean over valid positions

    # lambda = 1e-4, the canonical large-vocabulary coefficient.
    # Its logit gradient contribution is 2*lambda*log_z*p_j.
    return ce_loss + 1e-4 * z_loss
```

So the causal chain, end to end. The run spikes and the gradient norm slowly grows, and clipping doesn't stop it, which says the trouble is upstream of the step. The only unnormalized exponential in the forward pass is the final softmax, so I wrote cross-entropy as `log Z - l_y` and discovered that cross-entropy is exactly invariant to a uniform shift of the logits — the overall level, equivalently `log Z`, is a free gauge that the loss never constrains, so it drifts, and in practice drifts up. Large logits are precisely where bfloat16's coarse mantissa makes the exponential unfaithful, because absolute roundoff grows with magnitude and `exp` turns absolute argument error into relative output error; that's the slow growth tipping into spikes. To put a restoring force on the gauge I added a penalty minimized at `log Z = 0`, ruled out the signed and the kinked options, and landed on `(log Z)^2` because its gradient `2 log Z` is a smooth, proportional restoring force; the gradient of the augmented loss, `(p_j - 1{j=y}) + 2 lambda log Z · p_j`, shows the new term pushes down most on the logits contributing most to an oversized partition while leaving cross-entropy as the main force on the probability gaps. The sandwich `max_k l_k <= log Z <= max_k l_k + log V` proves that holding `log Z` small caps the largest logit, putting the exponential back in the faithful regime; and `log Z = 0` doubles as making the logits honest normalized log-probabilities. It's the right lever where label smoothing (which distorts the target distribution and only touches gaps) and logit clipping (which clamps already-corrupted values after the fact) are not. `lambda = 1e-4` is the order of magnitude that keeps the penalty a regularizer rather than a second objective while still holding the gauge. Reduce the penalty over exactly the valid positions cross-entropy uses, compute `log Z` with the stabilized log-sum-exp, keep the implementation a transparent composition so the backward pass is trustworthy — and it drops straight into the `compute_loss` slot as cross-entropy plus a one-line auxiliary term.
