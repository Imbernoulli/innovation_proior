Let me start from the thing that nags me, which is not a bacterium at all — it's a drug that works too well and not at all. The cause of peptic ulcer is supposed to be settled: acid. "No acid, no ulcer," Schwarz said in 1910, and seventy years on it is doctrine. The ulcer is the stomach digesting itself, acid and pepsin eating a crater where the mucosa fails to hold, and the failure is blamed on stress, an "ulcer personality," smoking, an inherited tendency. And then cimetidine arrives, blocks the H2 receptor on the parietal cell, shuts down acid, and the ulcers heal — almost all of them. Tagamet earns a billion dollars a year. Everyone reads this as confirmation: suppress acid, heal the ulcer, therefore acid is the cause. But stare at the actual clinical course. You stop the drug and the ulcer comes back. Not sometimes — characteristically, for years, healing and relapsing. So cimetidine is a treatment, not a cure. It heals the lesion and does nothing to the disease. That is a strange thing for a *cause* to do. If acid were the cause, removing it should end the matter; instead, removing it heals the wound and leaves the patient permanently susceptible to the next one. The honest description is that acid is the proximate irritant sitting on top of some persistent process underneath, and that process is what actually has the disease. Nobody is looking for it, because the cause is "already known." But a relapsing, lifelong, familial disease that simmers for decades and erupts unpredictably — that does not behave like a chemistry problem. It behaves like a chronic infection.

I cannot say that out loud, because everyone knows the stomach is sterile. The acid is supposed to kill anything that comes in. And yet Robin keeps showing me biopsies. He is a pathologist; he silver-stains the antral mucosa and there, lying on the epithelial surface, are curved bacilli — little spiral rods — and they sit exactly where there is active chronic gastritis. Always with the inflammation. Now, the textbook gives me two ways to dismiss this. Either they are contaminants — something that got onto the slide, or onto the dead tissue after the fact — or they are harmless commensals, normal flora that happen to live in stomachs that are inflamed for some other reason. That second reading is the seductive one, because it costs nothing: the bacteria are passengers, the inflammation has a real cause elsewhere, and the dogma survives intact. Palmer at Walter Reed looked for gastric bacteria in over a thousand patients back in 1954, found none, and declared them post-mortem contaminants; the question has been dead for twenty years on the strength of that. So the safe move is to call Robin's bacilli passengers and move on.

But a passenger does not pick its seat. A contaminant should be scattered at random, indifferent to whether the mucosa beneath it is healthy or inflamed. Robin's bacteria are not indifferent — they are *with the gastritis*, every time. That is the behaviour of something involved, not something blown in. And there is the history nobody connected. Once I start reading I find these organisms have been seen over and over and waved away: Bizzozero in dog stomachs in 1892, Salomon propagating them in mice in 1896, Krienitz in human specimens in 1906, Freedberg at Harvard finding spirochaetes in 40% of stomachs cut out for ulcer or cancer in 1940, Steer seeing spiral bacteria in 80% of gastric-ulcer specimens in 1975, and Ito photographing the thing beautifully under the electron microscope in 1967 — from his *own* stomach — and calling it a commensal because the same shape lives harmlessly in cats. The pattern in that literature is not "there are no bacteria." It is "there are bacteria, and everyone has decided in advance they don't matter." And Palmer's negative study, the one that closed the case — he used blind suction biopsy and ordinary stains. If the organism is patchy and faint on routine staining, a blind grab with the wrong stain is exactly how you would fail to find something that is really there. The unanimous "stomach is sterile" turns out to rest on a method that would miss this bug. So I will not assume it is a passenger. I will treat both readings as live and let the data decide between commensal and pathogen.

To decide, I have to do better than anecdote, because association is the whole question and association is easy to fool yourself about. Robin's first cases — a chart review of his 27 best — show nothing clean, because the histology around an ulcer is always disturbed and inflamed, so of course you find gastritis next to an ulcer; that proves nothing and it is precisely the confound the commensal theory feeds on. The fix is to sample the antral mucosa *away* from any ulcer or visible lesion, so that whatever gastritis I score is general, not a reaction to the nearby crater. And the scoring has to be blind: the pathologist reads the histology without knowing the endoscopic diagnosis, and I read the endoscopies without seeing the lab results, so neither of us can unconsciously make the association we are hoping for. So: a prospective study, a hundred consecutive patients coming for gastroscopy — a hundred only because in the days before spreadsheets a hundred makes the percentages fall out by inspection — biopsy each one in intact mucosa, culture it, score everything blind, and at the end cross-tabulate the bacteria against every diagnosis.

The culture nearly defeats me. For six months I can see masses of the bacteria on the Gram-stained smear minutes after the biopsy, and grow nothing. It is maddening. The reason, when it finally surfaces, is almost funny: the lab has been treating my gastric biopsies like throat swabs and discarding the plates at 48 hours when nothing grows, because throat swabs are overgrown with mouth flora by then. But my gastric specimens are clean — the acid is sucked out through the endoscope, the forceps open only in the stomach — so they grow nothing or they grow this one slow organism. Over Easter 1982 the plates sit untouched for five days because the lab is swamped with a resistant-Staph scare, and on the Tuesday there are colonies. The thing just needs more time than a throat swab. So now I can grow it, pure: a gram-negative rod, microaerophilic, with sheathed flagella at one end — which makes it more a cousin of the cholera vibrios and the spirilla than a true Campylobacter, but Campylobacter-like enough that the microaerophilic methods are right. When I line culture up with the hundred-patient series, bacilli are cultured from 11 of the biopsies where we can demonstrate them, so this is no longer only a histological shape on a slide.

Then the cross-tabs come back, and the numbers are not subtle. Fifty-eight of the hundred carry the bacteria. Among patients with no ulcer, about half are positive — which is exactly what a commensal would do, sit in lots of people. But every single duodenal-ulcer patient is positive: thirteen out of thirteen. I make myself be careful here, because thirteen is a small number and I want this too much. How surprising is thirteen for thirteen? If the bacteria had nothing to do with it and simply infected, say, half of everyone, then a duodenal-ulcer patient being positive is a coin flip, and thirteen positives in a row is like getting thirteen heads — about one chance in eight thousand. The reported P = 0.00044. That is not a coincidence I can shrug off. Gastric ulcers: eighteen of twenty-two, seventy-seven percent — high but not absolute. And the four gastric-ulcer patients who are *negative* are the ones who, on the questionnaire, were taking anti-inflammatory drugs. That fits a picture forming in my head: the duodenum is hard for swallowed drugs to reach in any concentration, so an ulcer down there is a "purer" ulcer, almost always the bacterial kind, whereas up in the stomach an ulcer can also be carved by something you swallow, like an NSAID, and then there is no gastritis and no bug. Which is exactly why duodenal ulcer binds *more* tightly to the bacteria than gastric ulcer does — and it rings a bell, because Magnus found this same lopsided pattern in 1952, gastritis in 80% of gastric ulcers but 100% of duodenal ones, and couldn't explain it and was ignored. My bacterial percentages land almost on top of his gastritis percentages. He was seeing my organism without knowing it.

So the association is real and strong. And it is still not causation, and I know precisely how the objection will run, because I hear it constantly: a harmless commensal infecting people who, for the usual reasons of acid and stress, happen to have ulcers. Correlation. The bacteria like inflamed stomachs and ulcer patients have inflamed stomachs, end of story. I cannot beat this with more correlation. I could biopsy ten thousand patients and the committed skeptic would still say passenger. The disease understanding here is not really science anymore; it is a kind of religion, and no tonnage of association will move what people already know in their hearts. To break it I need to show that the bacterium *does* something — that it produces the disease, rather than merely accompanying it.

There is an old, exact standard for promoting a microbe from associate to cause, and it is Koch's, four postulates. The organism must be present in every case of the disease. It must be isolated from a host with the disease and grown in pure culture. When that pure culture is given to a healthy, susceptible host, it must reproduce the specific disease. And the organism must be recoverable from that experimentally infected host. I have to be precise about the disease this can prove directly. Koch can prove that the bacterium causes gastritis, the inflammatory tissue process sitting under the ulcer; the ulcer itself will still need the clinical test that removing the organism stops relapse. Postulate one gives me a boundary rather than a shortcut: active chronic gastritis tracks the organism and every duodenal-ulcer patient in the hundred-patient series has it, but healthy carriers and NSAID gastric ulcers mean I cannot pretend every ulcer is one undifferentiated bacterial syndrome. Postulate two — pure culture — I have, since Easter. Postulate four depends on postulate three. So everything hinges on postulate three: take the pure culture, give it to a healthy susceptible host, and produce gastritis where there was none. That is the experiment that converts a passenger into a perpetrator. That is the whole game.

And it is a wall. Postulate three needs a healthy *susceptible* host, and that means an animal model, and I do not have one. I try piglets — instil the cultured organism, wait, biopsy. The bug does not take. Their stomachs grow their own spiral Campylobacters but my organism does not colonise, and the biopsies stay stubbornly normal, and eventually the piglets grow too big to handle and I terminate it, unpublished, like most failures. This is genuinely bad. Without an animal in which I can reproduce the disease, the most important postulate is out of reach, and I am stuck at "strong association" forever, which is to say stuck at "commensal" in the minds of everyone who matters. Things are going badly in other ways too — a young man bleeds from his whole stomach, no aspirin, no alcohol, strongly positive on my prototype antibody test, and I cannot get anyone to try a simple course of amoxicillin before they take his stomach out. I am watching a disease I think I understand kill and maim while the field calls my evidence "subtle."

So let me reconsider the host. Postulate three does not actually demand a pig. It demands a healthy susceptible host of a species the organism infects. The species this organism infects is *us*. The pig was only ever a stand-in for the host I actually care about, and the stand-in won't take the bug. Why am I trying to model the human stomach when I have one? The problem with experimenting on a human is consent — who can consent to being deliberately infected with an organism whose behaviour is not yet proven? And the answer falls out the moment I phrase it that way: the only person on earth who can give genuinely informed consent to this particular risk is the person who knows everything that is known about the organism, and that is me. So I am the susceptible host. I have to be my own animal model.

Before I can do it I need two things to be true, and by the middle of 1984 they are. First, it has to be reasonably safe — I cannot do this if there's a serious chance of killing myself — and my crude antibody survey shows that 43% of perfectly healthy blood donors in Fremantle carry this organism. Forty-three percent of well people. So carrying it is plainly survivable; it is not some uniformly lethal agent. Second, I need an exit: if I give myself the disease I had better be able to take it away again, and by now bismuth plus metronidazole has cured my first handful of patients outright, a hundred percent of the first four. I have a cure in my pocket. The pieces are in place. I will not ask an ethics committee, because the honest situation is that they would refuse — and they would be right to refuse to authorize *me to infect someone else* — and I would very likely do it anyway and then be unable to publish it, and possibly lose my job. So I establish a clean baseline first: I have Ian endoscope me and confirm my own stomach is histologically normal with no bacteria. That is the "healthy host" half of postulate three, documented. Then there is nothing left to do but take the bug.

On the morning I do it I skip breakfast and take 400 mg of cimetidine first. The logic there is small but real: a healthy stomach defends itself with acid, and if acid is what normally keeps casual organisms from colonising, then knocking the acid down for a few hours should give the inoculum its best chance to establish — I am trying to *cause* an infection, so I want to lower the barrier, not raise it. Two hours later Neil scrapes a heavy four-day culture plate into alkaline peptone broth and hands me a beaker a quarter full of cloudy brown liquid, and I drink it down in one gulp and fast for the rest of the day. A few stomach gurgles — the bug, or just hunger, I can't tell.

For three days, nothing, and I start to worry it has failed, that the bug won't take in me any more than in the piglets, that the whole hypothesis is wrong or at least far more complicated than I think. Day three I feel over-full after a small dinner. Then on about the fifth to eighth day I wake at dawn, sick to my stomach, and vomit — and here is the detail I almost miss in my sleepiness, the detail that turns out to matter most: what comes up is clear, slimy, *acid-free*. No acid. I have given myself an illness whose vomit has no acid in it, which means the infection has driven my stomach achlorhydric. My breath is putrid; my wife notices, my colleagues notice and are too polite to say. I feel tired, clammy at night, low. On day ten Ian endoscopes me again, and there on the Gram stain of the very first biopsy are the spiral bacteria, and the next day Ross shows me the histology — the mucosa teeming with the curved bacteria and pus cells. Histologically proven gastritis. I took a pure culture of an organism, gave it to a healthy host whose stomach was documented normal, produced gastritis, and found the organism again in the infected stomach. That satisfies postulate three, and the organism is there again in the experimentally infected host for postulate four. The bacterium is not a passenger. It is the cause of the gastritis, demonstrated the way Koch demands.

Then it gets stranger and, oddly, more illuminating: by day fourteen, before any real antibiotic course could have worked, the bacteria are gone — eight biopsies bacteria-free, only healing gastritis left, antibodies negative. (I do take tinidazole, but only after that day-fourteen endoscopy, and a single agent like that wouldn't reliably clear it anyway.) The gastritis has largely resolved by the fourteenth day. Why my acute self-infection cleared on its own when my patients carry theirs for life is a real mystery. But it is also the key to the natural history, the thing I could never get my patients to tell me. I always asked them when they first got sick and they never could remember an acute illness, which made no sense for an infection. Now I have *been* the acute illness, and I know what it looks like: a brief vomiting illness with achlorhydria and foul breath — and now I recognize it, because Osler described exactly this in children in his 1910 textbook, and there are published "epidemics of achlorhydric gastritis" in volunteers where nobody could find the pathogen. So the resolution is this: people catch this organism as small children, two or three years old, as a transient vomiting illness nobody connects to anything; in most it then settles into a lifelong, silent chronic gastritis; and in the susceptible, decades later, that smouldering inflammation erupts as peptic ulcer. That single arc explains everything the acid theory had to wave away. Why ulcer patients recall no acute onset — because it happened in toddlerhood. Why it runs in families and looks hereditary — because mothers and siblings transmit it by the faecal–oral route. Why it tracks poverty — same reason, crowding and hygiene. Why it is chronic and relentlessly relapsing — because none of the acid drugs touch the organism, so heal the crater with cimetidine and the infection is still there, waiting to make the next one. The "ulcer personality" and the stress inverts the causation: chronic infection makes you feel chronically unwell, and that low-grade sickness is what got labelled a personality. The acid story isn't wrong about acid being present; it is wrong about what is primary. The infection is primary; the acid abnormality is downstream of it.

That still leaves the obvious objection — how can any bacterium live in the stomach at all, the objection that made everyone call these things contaminants for a century. From the histology I can already see the answer to half of it: the organisms sit *beneath* the mucus layer, against the epithelium, where the pH is near neutral, not floating in the acid lumen. They shelter under the mucus blanket. And there is a second trick — the Dutch report in 1984 that the organism makes enormous amounts of urease. Urease splits urea into ammonia, ammonia is alkaline, so the bug surrounds itself with a buffer of its own making and neutralizes the acid in its immediate vicinity. That hands me a diagnostic test for free: feed labelled urea, the bug's urease cleaves it, the labelled carbon comes out in the breath as CO2 — a breath test for the infection. So the supposedly forbidden colonist is not forbidden; it is exquisitely adapted to the one organ everyone assumed was off-limits, which is exactly why it had that organ to itself and why it could cause a disease nobody thought to blame on a microbe.

Now I have causation for gastritis established by Koch's postulates and a mechanism that explains the natural history. A clinician's causal test is blunt: remove the suspected cause and see if the disease stops. If this organism is what sustains ulcer disease, then permanently eradicating it should permanently cure the ulcer — not heal-and-relapse like acid suppression, but *cure*. And there is a beautiful piece of evidence already lying in the literature waiting to be read correctly. Bismuth has healed ulcers for two hundred years, and unlike cimetidine it does not lower acid, so the acid theory could never explain why it works at all. But heavy metals are antibacterial — bismuth, arsenic, mercury were all used against the syphilis spirochaete. So I dip filter-paper discs in De-Nol, lay them on a lawn of the organism, and after four days there is a clean zone of inhibition around every disc: bismuth kills my organism. That is the most exciting day of my life, because now Martin and Hollanders' odd 1981 result snaps into focus — duodenal ulcers relapsing in 90% of cimetidine patients but only 60% of bismuth patients, the curves gone flat. The flattening means that 30% of the bismuth patients were not merely delayed, they were *cured*, and bismuth could be acting as a crude antibiotic, eradicating the organism in some of them by accident. The cure was hiding in plain sight, mislabelled as a quirk of an acid drug that doesn't even lower acid.

So the clinching experiment designs itself, and it has to separate "killed the bug" from "lowered the acid," cleanly and blind. I cannot ethically give ulcer patients a placebo with no healing agent — these are potentially fatal ulcers — so every arm must contain a proven ulcer-healer; and I can exploit bismuth's double identity to do it. Four arms: cimetidine alone, cimetidine plus an antibiotic, bismuth alone, bismuth plus an antibiotic — each step adding antibacterial force against the organism while every arm still heals the ulcer by an accepted route. Blind it tightly (bismuth blackens the stool, so a bismuth-plus-placebo arm is needed to keep patients from guessing), pick the worst, smoking, relapse-prone duodenal-ulcer patients so the answer comes fast, and then watch what relapse tracks. It does not track acid suppression. It tracks whether the organism is still there. Heal the ulcer and clear the bacterium and it stays healed off all treatment; heal the ulcer and leave the bacterium and it comes back. Smoking, the most feared risk factor, stops mattering once the organism is gone. A two-week course of antibacterials does what lifelong acid suppression and gastric surgery could not: it ends the disease.

Curved bacteria lie in inflamed gastric mucosa, dismissed for a century as contaminants because the stomach is "known" to be sterile and the lesion "known" to be made by acid — but a blinded prospective study shows them present in every duodenal ulcer (13/13, P = 0.00044) and almost every gastritis, far past chance; they are grown in pure culture; the animal model fails, so the susceptible human host is infected directly, deliberately, with that pure culture, and gastritis duly appears in a stomach documented healthy while the organism is found again in the infected mucosa, satisfying Koch's postulates for the acute gastritis syndrome in the only host that will harbour the organism; eradicating the organism cures the ulcer while merely suppressing acid does not, the interventional proof; and the whole natural history — childhood acquisition, lifelong silent chronic gastritis, adult ulceration, familial and socioeconomic spread, relentless relapse, even the "ulcer personality" — follows from a single chronic bacterial infection that survives the acid by sheltering under the mucus and buffering itself with urease-made ammonia. The cause of peptic ulcer is not stress and it is not, primarily, acid. It is *Helicobacter pylori*, a curable infection.
