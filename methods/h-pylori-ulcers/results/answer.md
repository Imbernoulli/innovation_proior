# Helicobacter pylori and the cause of peptic ulcers

## The problem

Peptic ulcer disease — recurrent, sometimes fatal ulceration of the stomach and duodenum — was attributed to excess gastric acid driven by stress, personality, smoking, and heredity ("no acid, no ulcer," Schwarz 1910). Acid-suppressing H2-receptor antagonists healed ulcers but did not cure the disease: ulcers relapsed the moment treatment stopped, and the underlying course was chronic, relapsing, familial, and lifelong — behaving far more like a persistent infection than a chemical injury. The stomach was believed sterile (acid kills bacteria), so a bacterial cause was not sought.

## The key idea

A spiral, gram-negative, microaerophilic bacterium — *Helicobacter pylori* — chronically infects the gastric mucosa and is the primary cause of chronic active gastritis and most peptic ulcer disease. Acid remains the proximate injuring agent, but the persistent disease process is bacterial. Because the organism is unaffected by acid-suppressing drugs, the disease relapses under acid therapy; eradicating the organism prevents relapse and cures the bacterial ulcer syndrome.

## The causal mechanism

- **Acquisition:** infection is acquired in early childhood (~age 2–3) as a transient acute illness — vomiting with achlorhydria (acid-free vomit) and halitosis — that is not connected to anything at the time. This explains why ulcer patients recall no acute onset.
- **Survival in acid:** the organism shelters beneath the gastric mucus layer against the epithelium, where the pH is near neutral; it also secretes abundant **urease**, splitting urea into ammonia, which buffers its immediate microenvironment. This is why a century of observers saw it yet "knew" no bacterium could live there.
- **Chronicity:** in most hosts the acute illness settles into lifelong, asymptomatic chronic active gastritis.
- **Ulceration:** in susceptible hosts the chronic gastritis creates the setting in which acid and pepsin produce an ulcer decades later; infection is the persistent cause and acid is the local injury.
- **Epidemiology:** spread person-to-person (faecal–oral, within families), so the disease looks hereditary and tracks crowding/low socioeconomic status; relapses relentlessly under acid drugs because they never touch the organism. The "ulcer personality"/stress association inverts cause and effect — chronic infection produces a chronically diminished state of health.

## The argument (Koch's postulates + intervention)

1. **Association.** A prospective, blind study of 100 consecutive gastroscopy patients (biopsies sampled in intact antral mucosa away from any lesion, scored blind to clinical findings): the organism is present in 58/100 overall, in almost all active chronic gastritis, in 18/22 (77%) gastric ulcers, and in **13/13 (100%) duodenal ulcers** (reported P = 0.00044 — far beyond chance). The bacteria-negative gastric ulcers are the NSAID cases. The pattern reproduces Magnus's unexplained 1952 finding (gastritis in 80% of gastric vs 100% of duodenal ulcers).

2. **Koch's postulates.**
   - *Postulate 1 — present in every case of the defined disease:* the organism repeatedly tracks active chronic gastritis and is present in 13/13 duodenal-ulcer patients in the 100-patient series; NSAID-associated gastric ulcers mark the non-bacterial exception and keep the postulate claim focused on gastritis.
   - *Postulate 2 — isolated from a diseased host and grown in pure culture:* satisfied (bacilli were cultured from 11 biopsies in the 100-patient series; the organism grew on Campylobacter-like microaerophilic media after longer incubation than routine cultures).
   - *Postulate 3 — reproduces the specific disease in a healthy susceptible host:* the animal model (piglets) failed — the organism would not colonise. Because the only host the organism reliably infects is human, the investigator infected himself: with a histologically normal, bacteria-free stomach documented beforehand, he took **400 mg cimetidine** to lower the acid barrier, drank a pure culture, developed an acute illness (achlorhydric vomiting, halitosis days ~5–8), and on **day 10** had histologically proven gastritis, with spiral bacteria on Gram stain and mucosa teeming with the organism and pus cells.
   - *Postulate 4 — recoverable from the experimentally infected host:* the day-10 biopsy found the organism again in the infected stomach. The gastritis had largely resolved by **day 14**, when eight biopsies were bacteria-free before tinidazole was taken.

3. **Intervention (the clinching proof).** Remove the suspected cause and the disease should stop. Bismuth — a 200-year-old ulcer remedy that does *not* lower acid — kills the organism in vitro (clear zone of inhibition), explaining Martin & Hollanders (1981): the flattened bismuth relapse curve (~60% vs ~90% for cimetidine at 2 years) suggests ~30% were *cured* because bismuth eradicated the organism in some patients. A four-arm double-blind trial (cimetidine ± antibiotic, bismuth ± antibiotic; every arm a proven healer, tightly blinded) shows that ulcer healing and freedom from relapse track **eradication of the organism**, not acid suppression; smoking ceases to matter once the organism is gone. A two-week antibacterial course cures a disease that lifelong acid suppression and partial gastrectomy could not.

## Consequence

The cause of most peptic ulcer disease is a curable chronic bacterial infection with *Helicobacter pylori*. Eradication therapy replaces lifelong acid suppression and ulcer surgery.
