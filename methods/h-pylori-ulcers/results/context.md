## Research question

Why do people get peptic ulcers — the recurrent, sometimes fatal craters in the lining of the stomach and, more commonly, the duodenum just past the pyloric sphincter? Roughly one person in ten suffers one in their lifetime; ulcers bleed (vomiting of blood), can perforate the wall into the peritoneum, and have no reliable medical cure. The disease has a characteristic natural history: it begins in adulthood, peaks after forty, runs in families, is worse in men and smokers, and once begun lasts for years with an unpredictable rhythm of healing and relapse. The question is what accounts for the cause of the disease and for this lifelong, relapsing, familial course.

## Background

The accepted cause is acid. Karl Schwarz's 1910 dictum, "no acid, no ulcer," had hardened over seventy years into doctrine: the ulcer is a chemical burn, the stomach digesting itself where its own hydrochloric acid and pepsin overwhelm the mucosal defence. Why some people make too much acid, or defend too little, was attributed to constitution — an "ulcer personality," stress, anxiety, smoking, diet, an inherited tendency. Stress-and-acid models in rats and monkeys reinforced the picture.

The therapeutic triumph of the late 1970s reinforced it further. The H2-receptor antagonists — cimetidine (Tagamet, 1977) and then ranitidine (Zantac) — block histamine-driven acid secretion, and on these drugs nearly every ulcer heals. By 1983 Tagamet earned about a billion dollars a year and Zantac was headed past three. When the drug is stopped, the ulcer comes back: cimetidine is a treatment that heals the lesion rather than a cure. For most patients lifelong suppression, or eventually surgical removal of the lower third of the stomach (with roughly one in ten left a "gastric cripple"), were the options.

A second, near-universal belief held that the stomach is essentially sterile: its acid was thought to sterilise whatever entered, so no ordinary bacterium could colonise so hostile an organ. Yet scattered observers across a century had reported spiral organisms in the stomach — Bizzozero described them in the gastric glands of dogs in 1892, Salomon propagated them in mice in 1896, Krienitz reported them in human specimens in 1906, Doenges in the late 1930s, Freedberg and Barron at Harvard in 1940 found "spirochaetes" in about 40% of stomachs resected for ulcer or cancer, and Steer in 1975 saw spiral bacteria in some 80% of gastric-ulcer specimens. Susumu Ito published a clean electron micrograph of the organism in the 1967 *Handbook of Physiology* — taken from his own stomach by blind suction biopsy — and took it, as with the same-looking bacteria in cats, to be a normal commensal. In 1954 Eddie Palmer at Walter Reed examined more than a thousand stomachs by blind suction biopsy, found no bacteria, and concluded they existed only as post-mortem contaminants; this large negative study stood as the reference point for two decades. Blind suction biopsy samples the organism patchily, and routine stains do not show it well; silver (Warthin–Starry) staining of sampled mucosa reveals the curved bacilli lying on the epithelium.

Other observations were on record. Magnus, studying accident victims in 1952, found gastritis in 80% of stomachs with gastric ulcer but 100% of those with duodenal ulcer. Bismuth salts had healed ulcers for two centuries without lowering acid, and heavy metals were known to be antibacterial — bismuth, arsenic, and mercury had all been used against the spirochaete of syphilis. A controlled trial by Martin and Hollanders in 1981 found that after healing, duodenal ulcers relapsed in about 90% of cimetidine-treated patients but only about 60% of bismuth-treated patients by two years, with the curves flattening.

## Baselines

**The acid/stress theory (Schwarz 1910 onward).** Core claim: ulcer = excess acid/pepsin overwhelming mucosal defence, driven by stress, personality, smoking, heredity. Its evidence: ulcers occur in acid-bearing mucosa; acid suppression heals them; rat/monkey stress models produce ulcers.

**H2-receptor antagonist therapy (cimetidine, ranitidine).** Core idea: block the H2 histamine receptor on the parietal cell, cut acid secretion, let the ulcer heal. The mechanism is pharmacological acid suppression. Heal-rate is near-total on drug, with near-total relapse off drug.

**Surgery (partial gastrectomy / vagotomy).** Removing acid-secreting stomach or its nerve supply. Definitive for the lesion, with a substantial fraction of patients left chronically unwell afterward.

**The "harmless commensal" reading of gastric bacteria (Palmer 1954; Ito 1967).** When bacteria were seen at all, they were taken to be contaminants or normal flora — colonisers of stomachs damaged for other reasons rather than agents of damage.

## Evaluation settings

The natural yardsticks already exist. Endoscopy (gastroscopy) with mucosal biopsy lets one see the ulcer and sample the antral mucosa; histology with silver stains reveals both gastritis and any organism; microbiological culture under microaerophilic, Campylobacter-like conditions could in principle grow a fastidious gastric organism. The clinical endpoints are established from the H2RA trials: ulcer healing at endoscopy after a treatment course, and freedom from relapse over months to years off all treatment. A consecutive endoscopy population, biopsied prospectively and scored blind to clinical findings, is the setting in which an association between an organism and ulcer disease could be measured; statistical association (a p-value against chance) is the first hurdle. The standard for promoting a microbe from associate to cause is Koch's four postulates — the organism present in every case, isolated from a diseased host and grown in pure culture, reproducing the disease when given to a healthy susceptible host, and recoverable from that experimentally infected host. The interventional test — eradicate the suspected cause and see whether the disease stops — is available through culture-guided antibacterial therapy and follow-up endoscopy.

## Code framework

This is not a computational discovery; there is no program to run and no algorithm to converge. What exists instead is the standing arsenal of medicine and microbiology — the endoscopy, histology, culture, statistical, and trial methods already named above — and an open question about cause. The available scaffold is a single empty slot:

- **The open slot.** Using those established tools, in whatever order proves necessary, account for the cause of the disease and its full natural history.

The final artifact is a closed causal argument, not a piece of code.
