## Research question

Why do people get peptic ulcers — the recurrent, sometimes fatal craters in the lining of the stomach and, more commonly, the duodenum just past the pyloric sphincter? Roughly one person in ten suffers one in their lifetime; ulcers bleed (vomiting of blood), can perforate the wall into the peritoneum, and have no reliable medical cure. The deeper puzzle is the *natural history*: ulcer disease begins in adulthood, peaks after forty, runs in families, is worse in men and smokers, and once begun lasts for years with an unpredictable rhythm of healing and relapse. Any genuine account of the cause must explain not just the lesion but this relapsing, lifelong, familial pattern — and must explain why the one effective drug class heals the ulcer yet never ends the disease.

## Background

The accepted cause is acid. Karl Schwarz's 1910 dictum, "no acid, no ulcer," had hardened over seventy years into doctrine: the ulcer is a chemical burn, the stomach digesting itself where its own hydrochloric acid and pepsin overwhelm the mucosal defence. Why some people make too much acid, or defend too little, was attributed to constitution — an "ulcer personality," stress, anxiety, smoking, diet, an inherited tendency. Stress-and-acid models in rats and monkeys reinforced the picture.

The therapeutic triumph of the late 1970s seemed to seal it. The H2-receptor antagonists — cimetidine (Tagamet, 1977) and then ranitidine (Zantac) — block histamine-driven acid secretion, and on these drugs nearly every ulcer heals. By 1983 Tagamet earned about a billion dollars a year and Zantac was headed past three. If suppressing acid heals the ulcer, surely acid is the cause. Yet a fact sat awkwardly under the success: the moment the drug is stopped, the ulcer comes back. Cimetidine is a treatment, not a cure; for most patients lifelong suppression, or eventually surgical removal of the lower third of the stomach (with roughly one in ten left a "gastric cripple"), were the only options. A disease that relapses the instant acid suppression ends fits awkwardly with a theory in which acid is the whole cause — yet the discrepancy was left unexamined, because the cause was "already known."

Against this stood a second, near-universal belief: the stomach is essentially sterile. Its acid was thought to sterilise whatever entered; no ordinary bacterium could colonise so hostile an organ. This belief had been earned, in a sense. Scattered observers across a century had in fact *seen* spiral organisms in the stomach — Bizzozero described them in the gastric glands of dogs in 1892, Salomon propagated them in mice in 1896, Krienitz reported them in human specimens in 1906, Doenges in the late 1930s, Freedberg and Barron at Harvard in 1940 found "spirochaetes" in about 40% of stomachs resected for ulcer or cancer, and Steer in 1975 saw spiral bacteria in some 80% of gastric-ulcer specimens. Susumu Ito even published a clean electron micrograph of the organism in the 1967 *Handbook of Physiology* — taken from his own stomach by blind suction biopsy — and assumed, as with the same-looking bacteria in cats, that it was a normal commensal. But in 1954 Eddie Palmer at Walter Reed examined more than a thousand stomachs by blind suction biopsy, found no bacteria, and concluded they existed only as post-mortem contaminants. His large negative study buried the question for two decades. Two methodological facts, invisible at the time, explain the contradiction: blind suction biopsy frequently misses the patchy organism, and routine stains do not show it well — it is silver (Warthin–Starry) staining of properly sampled mucosa that reveals the curved bacilli lying on the epithelium.

There were other loose threads nobody had pulled. Magnus, studying accident victims in 1952, found gastritis in 80% of stomachs with gastric ulcer but 100% of those with duodenal ulcer, could not explain why inflammation should bind more tightly to the duodenal lesion, and was ignored. Bismuth salts had healed ulcers for two centuries (without lowering acid), and heavy metals were known to be antibacterial — bismuth, arsenic, and mercury had all been used against the spirochaete of syphilis. A controlled trial by Martin and Hollanders in 1981 found that after healing, duodenal ulcers relapsed in about 90% of cimetidine-treated patients but only about 60% of bismuth-treated patients by two years, and the curves had flattened — a difference Martin and Hollanders noted but could not explain.

## Baselines

**The acid/stress theory (Schwarz 1910 onward).** Core claim: ulcer = excess acid/pepsin overwhelming mucosal defence, driven by stress, personality, smoking, heredity. Its evidence: ulcers occur in acid-bearing mucosa; acid suppression heals them; rat/monkey stress models produce ulcers. Its unexplained gap: it cannot say why the disease is chronic and relapsing rather than cured by healing the lesion, why it clusters in families in a pattern that fits poorly with a simple polygenic trait, or why bismuth — which does not lower acid — both heals ulcers and lowers their relapse.

**H2-receptor antagonist therapy (cimetidine, ranitidine).** Core idea: block the H2 histamine receptor on the parietal cell, cut acid secretion, let the ulcer heal. The actual mechanism is pharmacological acid suppression, nothing more. Gap: it treats the symptom. Heal-rate near-total on drug; near-total relapse off drug. It is the strongest evidence *for* the acid theory, yet the near-total relapse it leaves behind is the gap the theory cannot close.

**Surgery (partial gastrectomy / vagotomy).** Removing acid-secreting stomach or its nerve supply. Definitive for the lesion but mutilating, with a substantial fraction of patients left chronically unwell. A counsel of despair that itself testifies the medical theory offered no cure.

**The "harmless commensal" reading of gastric bacteria (Palmer 1954; Ito 1967).** When bacteria were seen at all, they were dismissed as contaminants or normal flora — colonisers of stomachs damaged for other reasons, not agents of damage. Gap: this reading was an untested assumption — the organisms were declared inert without any demonstration either way.

## Evaluation settings

The natural yardsticks already exist. Endoscopy (gastroscopy) with mucosal biopsy lets one see the ulcer and sample the antral mucosa; histology with appropriate (silver) stains reveals both gastritis and any organism; microbiological culture under microaerophilic, Campylobacter-like conditions could in principle grow a fastidious gastric organism. The clinical endpoints are well established from the H2RA trials: ulcer healing at endoscopy after a treatment course, and — the harder, more meaningful endpoint — freedom from relapse over months to years off all treatment. A consecutive endoscopy population, biopsied prospectively and scored blind to clinical findings, is the setting in which an association between any organism and ulcer disease could be measured; statistical association (a p-value against chance) is the first hurdle. The decisive standard for promoting a microbe from associate to cause is older still: Koch's four postulates — the organism present in every case, isolated from a diseased host and grown in pure culture, reproducing the disease when given to a healthy susceptible host, and recoverable from that experimentally infected host. And the interventional test — eradicate the suspected cause and see whether the disease stops — is available through culture-guided antibacterial therapy and follow-up endoscopy.

## Code framework

This is not a computational discovery; there is no program to run and no algorithm to converge. What exists instead is the standing arsenal of medicine and microbiology — the endoscopy, histology, culture, statistical, and trial methods already named above — and an open question about cause. The available scaffold is a single empty slot:

- **The open slot.** Using those established tools, in whatever order proves necessary, account for the cause of the disease and its full natural history. The slot to fill: what, if anything, accounts for the relapsing, lifelong, familial pattern that the acid theory cannot.

The final artifact is a closed causal argument, not a piece of code.
