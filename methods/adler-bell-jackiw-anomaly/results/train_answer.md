The starting puzzle is a flat contradiction between a symmetry argument and an experiment, sitting precisely where the symmetry argument was supposed to be most trustworthy. In a Dirac theory the axial-vector current $j^\mu_5 = \bar\psi\gamma^\mu\gamma_5\psi$ has, by the classical equations of motion, the divergence $\partial_\mu j^\mu_5 = 2im_0\,\bar\psi\gamma_5\psi$, which vanishes as $m_0\to 0$ where the Lagrangian is invariant under chiral rotations $\psi\to e^{i\alpha\gamma_5}\psi$. The whole machinery of current algebra and PCAC is built on this relation: matrix elements of $\partial\cdot A$ are obtained by formal manipulation of time-ordered products, equations of motion, Ward identities, and shifts of loop-integration variables. Applied to $\pi^0\to\gamma\gamma$, Sutherland and Veltman (1967) combined PCAC with photon gauge invariance and concluded that the amplitude $T(0)$ must vanish in the soft-pion limit. Yet $\pi^0\to 2\gamma$ is the dominant decay channel, and Steinberger's (1949) explicit nucleon-triangle loop gives a finite nonzero answer $T(0)\propto g/m$ — and, embarrassingly, a *different* finite answer depending on whether the pion couples pseudoscalarly or pseudovectorially, two forms the equations of motion declare equivalent. Every prior treatment lands on one horn or the other: Steinberger and the explicit $\sigma$-model loop get a number but cannot reconcile it with the symmetry theorem; Sutherland–Veltman and formal current algebra get a clean theorem that contradicts the number and experiment. None of them identifies *which* formal step is illegitimate or what the exact correction is.

I propose that the culprit is a single, universally overlooked step — the shift of an integration variable inside a fermion loop — and that handling it honestly produces a fixed, calculable correction to the axial divergence. This is the axial, or Adler–Bell–Jackiw, anomaly. The derivation runs as follows. The formal axial Ward identity, $(p-p')^\mu\Gamma^5_\mu = 2m_0\Gamma^5 + S_F^{-1}(p)\gamma_5 + \gamma_5 S_F^{-1}(p')$, can be checked graph by graph. I split the radiative corrections by where the axial vertex sits. Type (a), with $\gamma_\mu\gamma_5$ on the open external line, telescopes exactly using the pure-algebra identity $(p-p')\!\!\!/\,\gamma_5 = (p\!\!\!/+k\!\!\!/-m_0)\gamma_5 + \gamma_5(p'\!\!\!/+k\!\!\!/-m_0) + 2m_0\gamma_5$ — no integral is touched, and (WI) holds identically. Type (b), with the vertex on a closed loop emitting $2n$ photons, requires at one point the change of variable $r\to r+p'-p$ to make two pieces cancel. That shift is the crack. Power counting a $2n$-photon axial loop gives superficial degree $3-2n$: for $n\ge 2$ it is convergent and the shift is legal, so no anomaly arises. For $n=1$ — the AVV triangle, one axial and two photon vertices, the smallest such loop because Furry's theorem kills the pure-vector analog — the count gives degree $1$. It is only *linearly* divergent rather than quadratically, because the would-be leading numerator term carries $\mathrm{tr}\{\gamma_5\gamma^\alpha\gamma^\beta\}=0$. And linear divergence is exactly the borderline case where, as the old Jauch–Rohrlich result shows, the shift $r\to r+a$ inside $\int d^4r\, f(r)$ leaves a finite surface term proportional to $a$, not zero.

What makes the effect inescapable is that this surface term cannot respect both photon gauge invariance and the axial Ward identity at once. Using Rosenberg's (1963) gauge-invariant AVV expression $R_{\sigma\rho\mu}(k_1,k_2)$ as a sum of six $\varepsilon$-tensor structures with finite Feynman-parameter coefficients, contracting with the axial momentum gives $-(k_1+k_2)^\sigma R_{\sigma\rho\mu} = 2m_0 R_{\rho\mu} + 8\pi^2\,k_1^\xi k_2^\tau \varepsilon_{\xi\tau\rho\mu}$, where the pseudoscalar triangle $R_{\rho\mu} = 8\pi^2 m_0 I_{00}(k_1,k_2)\,k_1^\xi k_2^\tau\varepsilon_{\xi\tau\rho\mu}$. The extra $8\pi^2$ — a constant, with no Feynman-parameter integral and no factor of $m_0$ to make it vanish in the chiral limit — is the surface term that the formal identity misses. Because the triangle is linearly divergent, its bare value depends on the loop-momentum routing, an ambiguity captured by the *unique* admissible structure (mass dimension, three-index pseudotensor, Bose-symmetric in the two photons, free of kinematic singularities): $R_{\sigma\rho\mu}[\zeta] = R_{\sigma\rho\mu} + \zeta\,\varepsilon_{\tau\sigma\rho\mu}(k_1-k_2)^\tau$. The two divergences then read $k_1^\mu R[\zeta] = -\zeta\,k_1^\sigma k_2^\tau\varepsilon_{\tau\sigma\rho\mu}$ for the vector index and $-(k_1+k_2)^\sigma R[\zeta] = 2m_0 R_{\rho\mu} + (8\pi^2-2\zeta)\,k_1^\xi k_2^\tau\varepsilon_{\xi\tau\rho\mu}$ for the axial index. The choice $\zeta=4\pi^2$ makes the axial Ward identity normal but destroys photon gauge invariance; $\zeta=0$ keeps gauge invariance and carries the full $8\pi^2$ anomaly. The single surface term must land somewhere — the only question is which symmetry I sacrifice.

I keep gauge invariance, and I do not merely assert it: two independent physical requirements force $\zeta=0$. First, two real on-shell photons can never form a state of total angular momentum 1, so $l^\sigma\varepsilon_1^\rho\varepsilon_2^\mu R_{\sigma\rho\mu}[\zeta]=0$ whenever $l\cdot(k_1+k_2)=0$ and the photons are transverse — the gauge-invariant part obeys this automatically, the $\zeta$-term does not, so $\zeta=0$. Second, an infinitely heavy loop fermion must decouple, $\lim_{m_0\to\infty}R_{\sigma\rho\mu}[\zeta]=0$ — Rosenberg's part vanishes, the mass-independent $\zeta$-term survives, so again $\zeta=0$. The anomaly is therefore not a choice. The same $8\pi^2$ also saturates the Weinberg high-energy power counting one power above naive, cross-validating the value, and a loop-wise count shows all larger loops (AVVVV, $\ldots$) are convergent enough that their shifts are legal, so the coefficient is fixed at one loop and is not renormalized. Translating to the operator equation — restoring the two photon couplings $e_0$ and the loop measure — yields the corrected divergence

$$\partial_\mu j^\mu_5(x) = 2im_0\,j_5(x) + \frac{e_0^2}{16\pi^2}\,\varepsilon^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}F_{\mu\nu}(x)F_{\rho\sigma}(x),$$

equivalently, with $\alpha_0 = e_0^2/4\pi$ and $\tilde F^{\mu\nu}=\tfrac12\varepsilon^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}F_{\rho\sigma}$, $\partial_\mu j^\mu_5 = 2im_0 j_5 + (\alpha_0/4\pi)\varepsilon^{\xi\sigma\tau\rho}F_{\xi\sigma}F_{\tau\rho} = 2im_0 j_5 + (e_0^2/8\pi^2)F_{\mu\nu}\tilde F^{\mu\nu}$. In massless electrodynamics the right-hand side does not vanish: the axial current is not conserved despite the chiral invariance of the Lagrangian. The symmetry is broken by the very act of regularizing the short-distance behavior of the loop.

This lands directly on the $\pi^0\to\gamma\gamma$ puzzle. The anomaly modifies the PCAC equation for the neutral axial current to $\partial_\mu F^5_{3\mu} = (f_\pi M_\pi^2/\sqrt 2)\,\phi_\pi + S\,(\alpha_0/4\pi)\,\varepsilon^{\xi\sigma\tau\rho}F_{\xi\sigma}F_{\tau\rho}$, with $S=\sum_i(\text{axial coupling})_i\,Q_i^2$ counting the charges of the loop fermions. The Sutherland–Veltman vanishing applies only to the first term at the soft point; the anomaly term does not vanish there, so the entire amplitude comes from the anomaly. In the Gell-Mann–Lévy $\sigma$-model, where PCAC is canonical with $F=f^{-1}=2m/g$, the puzzle becomes fully explicit: PCAC is verified *with no shift of variables*, $k_\sigma F^{\sigma\mu\nu} = -\big(2mg^{-1}\mu^2/(k^2-\mu^2)\big)T^{\mu\nu}$, yet the finite amplitude is $T(0)=4\pi^2 g/m\neq 0$, while gauge invariance fails precisely by the surface term, $F_4-\tfrac12 k^2 F_3 = -4\pi^2$ and $p_\mu F^{\sigma\mu\nu} = -4\pi^2\,\varepsilon^{\sigma\alpha\beta\nu}p_\beta q_\alpha$ — one surface term, showing up in whichever identity is checked by a shift. Reorganizing into physical quantities via Goldberger–Treiman and the pion wave-function renormalization gives the low-energy theorem $F = -(\alpha/\pi)\cdot 2S\cdot\big(g_r(0)/(m_N g_A)\big)$ with $\Gamma(\pi^0\to 2\gamma)=\tau^{-1}=(m_\pi^3/64\pi)F^2$, which yields $\tau^{-1}\approx 9.7\ \mathrm{eV}$ against the measured $7.37\pm 1.5\ \mathrm{eV}$ — the entire observed neutral-pion lifetime is the anomaly, and $S=\tfrac12$ fits, turning the lifetime into a probe of constituent charge structure (the analogous U-spin construction gives $\eta\to 2\gamma$). One might try to engineer a regulator that enforces both gauge invariance and PCAC — mass-dependent Pauli–Villars–Gupta couplings $g_i/m_i=g/m$ giving $T_{\rm reg}(0)=4\pi^2(g/m-g_1/m_1)=0$ — but the regulator couplings then grow as $g_i=(g/m)m_i$, so the regulator fermions fail to decouple and inject unrenormalizable infinities $\propto (g_i/m_i)^{2n}m_i^{2n}$ into the strong interactions. The anomaly cannot be removed without sacrificing renormalizability (or unitarity); it is a genuine physical effect, not a regularization artifact.

```python
import sympy as sp
pi = sp.pi

# AVV triangle divergences as functions of the routing/subtraction freedom ζ:
#   R_{σρμ}[ζ] = R_{σρμ} + ζ ε_{τσρμ}(k1-k2)^τ
zeta = sp.symbols('zeta', real=True)
vector_div_coeff   = zeta              # ∝ k1^μ R[ζ]; must vanish for gauge invariance
axial_anomaly_coeff = 8*pi**2 - 2*zeta # extra term in (k1+k2)·R beyond 2 m0 R_pseudoscalar

zeta_star = sp.solve(sp.Eq(vector_div_coeff, 0), zeta)[0]      # gauge invariance -> 0
anomaly   = axial_anomaly_coeff.subs(zeta, zeta_star)          # -> 8*pi^2

e0 = sp.symbols('e_0', positive=True)
anomaly_operator_coeff = e0**2/(16*pi**2)   # coefficient of ε F F in ∂_μ j^μ_5

# π0 -> 2γ low-energy theorem from the anomaly-modified PCAC
alpha, S, g_r, m_N, g_A, mu = sp.symbols('alpha S g_r m_N g_A mu', positive=True)
F_pi = -(alpha/pi) * 2*S * (g_r/(m_N*g_A))
rate = mu**3/(64*pi) * F_pi**2              # Γ(π0->2γ) = τ^{-1}

print("zeta fixed by gauge invariance:", zeta_star)            # 0
print("axial anomaly coefficient:", anomaly)                   # 8*pi**2
print("operator coefficient of ε F F:", anomaly_operator_coeff)# e_0**2/(16*pi**2)
print("F(π0->2γ) =", F_pi)
print("rate =", sp.simplify(rate))
```
