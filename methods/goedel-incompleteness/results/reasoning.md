The core of Godel's incompleteness theorem is not that self-reference exists. Informal self-reference was already familiar from semantic paradoxes. The decisive move is that statements about formulas and proofs can be translated into statements about natural numbers. Once syntax is arithmetized, arithmetic can encode a fragment of the metatheory that was supposed to stand outside it.

Start with a formal theory T. From the outside, a proof in T is just a finite sequence of formulas satisfying mechanical checks: each line is an axiom or follows from earlier lines by an inference rule. If the axioms are effectively recognizable, checking whether a finite string is a valid proof is itself an effective finite procedure. Godel numbering turns that finite procedure into arithmetic. A symbol receives a number; a string of symbols receives a number, often through prime-exponent coding or another effective sequence coding; formulas and proofs become special kinds of numbers.

The point of this coding is not bookkeeping for its own sake. It ensures that syntactic predicates such as "n is the code of a formula" and "p is the code of a valid T-proof of the formula with code n" are primitive recursive, or at least effectively arithmetizable in the required way. Because theories capable of elementary arithmetic can represent primitive recursive relations, there is an arithmetical formula Proof_T(p, n) that mirrors the metamathematical proof relation. Then Prov_T(n) can be written as "there exists p such that Proof_T(p, n)." This is the step that internalizes metamathematics: the system can now express, in numerical terms, that some numbered formula has a proof in the system.

This should be kept separate from a truth predicate. Godel does not need a formula True(n) saying that the formula with code n is true. Tarski-style truth would lead to a different obstruction. Godel uses only proof, and proof is syntactic: a finite object can be checked line by line. That is why the construction is robust rather than merely paradoxical. It is grounded in the mechanical nature of formal derivation.

Once provability has an arithmetical proxy, the fixed-point step supplies the self-reference. The diagonal or fixed-point lemma says that for any formula A(x) with one free variable, there is a sentence S such that T proves S iff A applied to the code of S, more precisely T proves S <-> A(code(S)) under the usual representability conditions. The sentence does not point to itself by informal quotation. It points to its own Godel number through a precisely constructed substitution operation, itself arithmetized.

Choose A(x) to be "not Prov_T(x)." The fixed point is a sentence G_T satisfying: G_T says, in arithmetic, that the sentence with code code(G_T) is not provable in T. Since code(G_T) is the code of G_T itself, the intended reading is "I am not provable in T." But the exact content is numerical: no number p codes a valid T-proof of the formula with this particular code.

Now suppose T proves G_T. Then, from the proof predicate's representability, T has a proof of the sentence whose own content says that no such proof exists. Under consistency or the appropriate strengthened consistency condition, this is impossible. Therefore T does not prove G_T. If T is sound enough about the relevant proof facts, G_T is true in the intended natural numbers, because it indeed has no T-proof.

For the other side, the original theorem used omega-consistency to rule out T proving not G_T. Modern presentations often separate the assumptions more carefully: consistency can be enough for non-provability of the Godel sentence, while stronger soundness or 1-consistency assumptions support the full undecidability of the original form. The stable conceptual lesson is that any effectively axiomatized, sufficiently arithmetical, consistent system leaves some arithmetical sentence undecided.

The second incompleteness theorem sharpens the blow to Hilbert's program. The system can also formulate Con(T), usually as "there is no number p that codes a T-proof of contradiction." In the metatheory, one shows that T can prove enough of the implication Con(T) -> G_T. If T also proved Con(T), then T would prove G_T. But the first incompleteness argument says a consistent T cannot prove G_T. Therefore a consistent T cannot prove its own consistency, at least when Con(T) is the standard arithmetized consistency statement and T satisfies the usual derivability conditions.

This is why the theorem is not a mere restatement of the liar paradox. The liar says "this sentence is false" and entangles self-reference with semantic truth. Godel constructs a specific arithmetical sentence about the nonexistence of a coded proof. The sentence is meaningful because proofs are finite syntactic objects; it is self-referential because substitution and coding let arithmetic name its own formulas; and it is undecidable because provability and consistency interact in a precise way. The breakthrough is the conversion of metamathematics into arithmetic, not the discovery that circular sentences can be mischievous.

Seen this way, arithmetization is the hinge. Without it, there is an external philosophical worry about formal systems and self-reference. With it, there is a theorem about natural numbers: a certain existential statement about proof codes cannot be settled by the theory whose proofs are being coded. The system is not contradicted; it is shown to have an internal blind spot created by the very fact that its proof theory can be represented inside arithmetic.
