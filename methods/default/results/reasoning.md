Let me start from the thing that actually bothers me. I have this toy task: draw a uniform binary string, label it by the parity of a hidden size-`k` subset `S` of its bits, `y = chi_S(x) = prod_{i in S} x_i` over `x in {±1}^n` (or, in the zero-one world, `y = (sum_{i in S} x_i) mod 2`). Statistically it's nothing — I only need to tell apart `C(n,k)` candidate subsets, and `log C(n,k) = Theta(k log n)` samples pin one down. But every reasonable algorithm I know wants `n^{Omega(k)}` *work* to find `S`. So there's a chasm between how many samples settle the answer and how much computation it takes to extract it, and that chasm is the whole point: this is a problem about optimization, not about data. And the concrete thing I keep seeing when I just train a plain network on it is maddening — the loss sits flat at chance for a long, long time, accuracy glued to 50%, and then it snaps to solved, almost discontinuously. So two questions are tangled together. Why does ordinary gradient training solve this at all, and what on earth is happening during that flat stretch — is it blindly searching and getting lucky, or is something improving the whole time that I just can't see in the loss?

First I have to be honest about why this is hard, because the hardness is structural and I want to feel exactly where it bites. The parities are orthonormal under the correlation inner product: `E_x[chi_S(x) chi_{S'}(x)] = 1` if `S' = S` and `0` otherwise. Let me not take that on faith — `chi_S chi_{S'} = prod_{i in S} x_i prod_{i in S'} x_i`, and since `x_i^2 = 1` the shared indices cancel, leaving `chi_{S Δ S'}`, the symmetric difference; the expectation of any non-empty parity is `0` (each `x_i` is mean-zero and independent), and of the empty parity is `1`. I brute-forced both halves over all `x in {±1}^6` for a batch of set pairs to be sure I had the cancellation right, and `chi_S chi_{S'} = chi_{S Δ S'}` holds pointwise and `E[chi_S chi_{S'}] = 1[S=S']` holds for every pair I checked. So the identity is solid, and its consequence is savage. Suppose I'm a learner who only ever measures correlations between my current guess and the labels. If I guess a subset `S'` that overlaps `k-1` of the right indices, my correlation with `y` is `E[chi_{S'} chi_S] = E[chi_{S Δ S'}]`, and `S Δ S'` is non-empty, so it's... zero. Same as if I overlapped *none* of them. There is no "getting warmer." The function `S' -> correlation(chi_{S'}, y)` is a spike at `S` and flat zero everywhere else. A correlation-only learner gets no gradient toward the answer; it's forced to check subsets one at a time, and there are `C(n,k)` of them.

This is exactly what the statistical-query lower bound formalizes, and I should reconstruct it because it tells me the *best* I could possibly hope for. In the SQ model I don't see examples; I submit a bounded query `q: {±1}^n -> [-1,1]` and get back `E_D[q]` to within a tolerance `tau`. Almost any noise-robust, aggregate-statistic learner — including, I'll argue in a moment, gradient descent — is an SQ algorithm. Now, by orthogonality, expand my query in the parity basis: `q = sum_T qhat(T) chi_T`, and by Parseval `sum_T qhat(T)^2 = E[q^2] <= 1`. The correlation of my query with the labels for target `S` is `E_{D_S}[q · y] = E_x[q(x) chi_S(x)] = qhat(S)`. For how many targets `S` can this exceed `tau`? At most `1/tau^2` of them, because `sum_S qhat(S)^2 <= 1` means at most `1/tau^2` coefficients can have magnitude `> tau`. So any single query distinguishes the right parity from the pack for only a `1/tau^2` fraction of possible answers; to cover all `C(n,k) ≈ n^k` of them I need `T / tau^2 >= Omega(n^k)`. Queries times precision-squared can't beat exhaustive search. And here's the bridge to my setting: a stochastic gradient is, coordinate by coordinate, an estimate of an expectation `E_D[partial-of-loss]` — that's an SQ query with tolerance set by the gradient's noise. So a gradient method with constant gradient noise inherits this floor: `n^{O(k)}` iterations is the least it could need. If a plain net actually got near that, it would be matching the computational limit, not failing.

But now the pessimistic reading, which I have to take seriously because it seems to say the floor is irrelevant — I'll never even get off the ground. The argument measures the "signal about the target" living in the gradient as a variance over the family of possible targets. Concretely, for the objective `F_h(w) = E_x[ loss(p_w(x), h(x)) ]` of learning some `h` from a family `H`, define `Var(H, F, w) = E_h || grad F_h(w) - E_{h'} grad F_{h'}(w) ||^2`. This is the right object: it's how much the gradient *moves* when I swap which target I'm chasing. If it's tiny, the gradient at `w` is basically the same vector no matter what the answer is, so it tells me nothing about the answer. And there's a clean bound: if the members of `H` are pairwise orthogonal with `E[h^2] <= 1`, and the predictor satisfies `E||d p_w/dw||^2 <= G(w)^2`, and the loss is square loss or a `1`-Lipschitz classification loss, then `Var(H, F, w) <= G(w)^2 / |H|` — and crucially this holds for *any* architecture, any width, any depth. Let me see why it's that clean. Write the gradient's deviation from the family-mean and expand the squared norm; the cross terms across distinct `h, h'` carry factors `E_x[h(x) h'(x)] = 0` by orthogonality, so they vanish, and what's left is `(1/|H|) sum_h ||grad of the h-specific part||^2`, each piece controlled by `G(w)^2`. The orthogonality that made the *learning* hard is the very same property that makes the gradient target-blind: the per-target gradient directions are orthogonal, so averaged over a big family they cancel to a constant.

Now plug in *all* parities on `d` bits: `H = {x -> (-1)^{<x,v>} : v in {0,1}^d}`, which has `|H| = 2^d` members, all mutually orthogonal. Then `Var <= G(w)^2 / 2^d`, exponentially small. By Chebyshev the gradient at any fixed `w` is concentrated around a single target-independent vector to within an exponentially small fluctuation — so following it learns nothing about `v`, and indeed in practice learning a random `d`-bit parity stalls out around `d = 30`. The verdict reads blunt: gradients carry no signal, no gradient method should work.

So I should be stuck. Except — wait. Stare at the family they used. It's *all* `2^d` parities, every degree from `1` to `d`. That's a dense family, and `2^d` in the denominator is what makes it hopeless. But my problem isn't that family. My problem fixes the sparsity: `|S| = k`, a small constant. The family of size-`k` parities has only `C(n,k) ≈ n^k` members. Run the *same theorem* on the *right* family and I get `Var <~ G(w)^2 / n^k`. That's small — but it's small only *polynomially* in `n^k`, not exponentially in `n`. The denominator went from `2^n` to `n^k`. That's a colossal difference. The pessimism wasn't wrong; it was answering about the wrong family. Over the dense family the gradient is target-blind to exponential precision; over the sparse family there's a residual signal of size roughly `G/sqrt(n^k)` per direction. And a residual signal of size `~ n^{-k/2}` is exactly the kind of thing a *large batch* can lift out of the noise: averaging `B` samples cuts the gradient's noise by `1/sqrt(B)`, so if I push `B` up toward `n^k`, the signal-to-noise crosses one and the gradient stops being blind. The floor `n^{O(k)}` and the residual-signal scale `n^{-k/2}` carry the same `k`-dependence, showing up once in the runtime and once in the signal size. So the conjecture I'll chase is: the gradient does carry usable information about `S`, it's just polynomially faint, and to use it I either pay in batch size or in time — and `B · t ~ n^k` is the trade the SQ floor predicts. Now I have to *find* that signal and watch what it does — and "the variance is nonzero" is not the same as "I can read `S` off the gradient," so I shouldn't believe the signal is usable until I've actually pointed at it.

Where would it live? Let me actually compute the population gradient at initialization for the simplest thing that could possibly show it — a single ReLU neuron, `f(x; w) = sigma(w·x) = (w·x)_+`, with the correlation loss `ell(y, yhat) = -y · yhat` (cleanest, because then the gradient *is* a correlation), starting from the all-ones init `w = (1, ..., 1)`. The gradient of the loss in coordinate `j` is `E[-y · partial_{w_j} (w·x)_+] = E[-y · 1[w·x >= 0] · x_j]`. At all-ones init `w·x = sum_i x_i`, so the indicator is `1[sum_i x_i >= 0]`. So coordinate `j` of the population gradient is `-E[ y · x_j · 1[sum_i x_i >= 0] ]`, and `y = chi_S(x) = prod_{i in S} x_i`. Two cases. If `j in S`, then `y · x_j = chi_S(x) · x_j = chi_{S \ {j}}(x)`, because `x_j^2 = 1` cancels the `j`-th factor — a parity of degree `k-1`. If `j not in S`, then `y · x_j = chi_{S}(x) · x_j = chi_{S ∪ {j}}(x)`, a parity of degree `k+1`. So the gradient coordinate is `-E[ chi_{S\{j}}(x) · 1[sum x_i >= 0] ]` for relevant `j`, and `-E[ chi_{S∪{j}}(x) · 1[sum x_i >= 0] ]` for irrelevant `j`. Each of these is exactly a Fourier coefficient — of the indicator function `1[sum_i x_i >= 0]` — at a set of size `k-1` versus `k+1`.

And I know that indicator. `1[sum_i x_i >= 0] = (1 + Maj(x))/2` where `Maj(x) = sgn(sum_i x_i)`, with odd `n` or a fixed tie convention handling the zero-sum case. So its Fourier coefficient at any non-empty set is half of majority's coefficient there. So coordinate `j` of my gradient is, up to sign and a factor `1/2`, the majority coefficient `\widehat{Maj}(S \ {j})` (degree `k-1`) when `j in S`, and `\widehat{Maj}(S ∪ {j})` (degree `k+1`) when `j not in S`. The relevant and irrelevant coordinates differ only in *which degree of majority's spectrum* they read off. If those two degrees have different sizes, the gradient separates the relevant indices from the rest by magnitude alone. The signal I was promised by the variance argument has a face: it's the difference between majority's degree-`(k-1)` and degree-`(k+1)` Fourier mass. Whether that face is friendly depends entirely on which way the inequality `|xi_{k-1}|` vs `|xi_{k+1}|` goes — and I haven't checked that yet.

So I need majority's spectrum. Write `xi_k := \widehat{Maj}([k])`; by symmetry every size-`k` coefficient has the same magnitude. Two structural facts first. Majority is an odd function, so `xi_k = 0` for even `k` — only odd-degree coefficients survive. (This is why I'll end up wanting `k` even: then `k-1` and `k+1` are both odd and both nonzero, while degree `k` itself vanishes — the bias gradient is zero, which keeps things clean.) And there's a closed form: for odd `k`,
`xi_k = (-1)^{(k-1)/2} · [ C((n-1)/2, (k-1)/2) / C(n-1, k-1) ] · 2^{-(n-1)} · C(n-1, (n-1)/2)`.
What I actually need is the *gap* between adjacent odd degrees, `|xi_{k-1}|` versus `|xi_{k+1}|` for even `k`. Rather than wrangle the binomials blind, let me just compute the whole spectrum exactly for a few small odd `n` and see what the ratio does — that will both tell me the right formula to aim for and catch any sign or off-by-one error before I lean on it. Summing `Maj(x) chi_T(x)` over all `2^n` points with exact fractions, for `n in {9, 11, 13}`:

```
n=9:   |xi_1|=0.273437  |xi_3|=0.039062 -> ratio 7.000   (n-k)/(k-1) at k=2 = 7/1 = 7
       |xi_3|=0.039062  |xi_5|=0.023437 -> ratio 1.667   at k=4 = 5/3 = 1.667
       |xi_5|=0.023437  |xi_7|=0.039062 -> ratio 0.600   at k=6 = 3/5 = 0.600
n=11:  k=4 ratio 2.333 = 7/3 ;  k=6 ratio 1.000 = 5/5
n=13:  k=4 ratio 3.000 = 9/3 ;  k=6 ratio 1.400 = 7/5
all even-degree coefficients came out exactly 0.
```

The ratio is *exactly* `(n-k)/(k-1)` in every case — not asymptotically, exactly — so `|xi_{k-1}| = ((n-k)/(k-1)) |xi_{k+1}|`. Two things jump out that I would have missed by hand-waving asymptotics. First, the even-degree coefficients are genuinely zero, confirming I want `k` even so `k±1` are the surviving odd degrees. Second, and this is the one I'm glad I checked: at `n=9, k=6` the ratio is `0.6 < 1` — the gap *reverses*, the irrelevant (degree `k+1`) coordinate is actually *larger* than the relevant (degree `k-1`) one. The lower degree dominates only when `n - k > k - 1`, i.e. `n > 2k - 1`. So the whole mechanism quietly requires `n` large relative to `k`; it is not a cosmetic assumption, it's the difference between the signal pointing at `S` and pointing away from it. Good to know before I build anything on it.

With the ratio confirmed, the absolute gap follows. For fixed `k` and large `n`, `(n-k)/(k-1) ~ n/k`, so the relevant-coordinate gradients dominate the irrelevant ones by a factor of order `n/k`. The difference is
`|xi_{k-1}| - |xi_{k+1}| = ((n - 2k + 1)/(k - 1)) · |xi_{k+1}|`,
and I want a clean lower bound on it, not just a ratio, because the recovery argument needs an absolute gap. Substitute the closed form for `|xi_{k+1}|` and bound the binomials by Stirling: `C((n-1)/2, k/2) / C(n-1, k) >= ((n-1)/k)^{-k/2} e^{-k}` roughly, and the central coefficient gives `2^{-(n-1)} C(n-1, (n-1)/2) >= (2 sqrt(2 pi))/(e^2 sqrt(n-1))`. With `n - 2k + 1 >= (n-1)/2` (which holds once `n >= 4k` — consistent with the `n > 2k-1` I just found necessary) and a constant `sqrt(2 pi) k^{k/2}/((k-1) e^{k+2}) > 0.03` from elementary calculus, the algebra collapses to
`gamma_k := |xi_{k-1}| - |xi_{k+1}| >= 0.03 · (n - 1)^{-(k-1)/2}`.
So the gap is `Theta(n^{-(k-1)/2})`. Faint — it shrinks polynomially with `n`, exactly the `n^{-k/2}`-ish scale the variance argument predicted — but strictly positive, which is all I need. The squared ratio is what controls the recovery sample count: from `|xi_{k-1}| = ((n-k)/(k-1))|xi_{k+1}|` plus the bounds on `xi_k^2 ~ k^{-2/3}/C(n,k)`, the irrelevant gradients are smaller by a factor `~ k/n` in magnitude, i.e. `xi_{k+1}^2 / xi_{k-1}^2 <= (8 c_2/c_1) k^2/n^2`.

Now I'll abstract this into the property I really care about and pin down what it buys me. Say a function `f` has a `gamma`-*Fourier gap* at `S` if for every `(k-1)`-subset `S^-` of `S` and every `(k+1)`-superset `S^+` of `S`, `|fhat(S^-)| >= |fhat(S^+)| + gamma`. I just showed majority (hence the ReLU neuron's gradient, via its threshold derivative) has this gap with `gamma = Theta(n^{-(k-1)/2})` once `n` is large enough. The recovery claim is then: take a one-neuron predictor `f(x; w) = sigma(w·x)`, and suppose I have an estimate `g(w)` of the population gradient accurate in the sup-norm to within `gamma/2`, i.e. `||g(w) - bar-g(w)||_inf < gamma/2`. Then the `k` coordinates of `g(w)` with the largest magnitude are *exactly* `S`. The reason: by the same computation, coordinate `i` of the true population gradient `bar-g(w)` equals `hat-h(S \ {i})` for `i in S` and `hat-h(S ∪ {i})` for `i not in S`, where `h(x) = sigma'(w·x)`. The Fourier gap says every relevant magnitude exceeds every irrelevant magnitude by at least `gamma`. After estimation error, a relevant coordinate is still at least `|bar-g_i| - gamma/2`, while an irrelevant coordinate is at most `|bar-g_j| + gamma/2`; because the true gap is strictly larger than the two half-errors, no irrelevant coordinate can overtake a relevant one. Read off the top `k`, and you have `S`.

That argument feels airtight on paper, but I've been burned by sign conventions and the tie case, so let me actually run the population gradient on a concrete instance and read off the top-`k`. Take `n=9, k=4, S={0,1,2,3}`, all-ones init, and compute `-E[ y x_j 1[sum x_i >= 0] ]` exactly over all `2^9` points:

```
coord 0 [REL]: +0.019531    coord 4 [irr]: -0.011719
coord 1 [REL]: +0.019531    coord 5 [irr]: -0.011719
coord 2 [REL]: +0.019531    coord 6 [irr]: -0.011719
coord 3 [REL]: +0.019531    coord 7 [irr]: -0.011719
                            coord 8 [irr]: -0.011719
top-4 coords by |grad| = [0,1,2,3] == S
```

This lands exactly where the theory said it should. The four relevant coordinates all read `+0.019531 = ½ · 0.039062 = ½ xi_3` (degree `k-1 = 3`), and the five irrelevant ones read `-0.011719 = ½ · 0.023437 = ½ xi_5` (degree `k+1 = 5`) — confirming both the half-majority-coefficient claim and the relevant=`k-1` / irrelevant=`k+1` degree assignment, signs and all. The top-4-by-magnitude are precisely `S`. And note this worked at `n=9, k=4`, where `n > 2k-1` holds (`9 > 7`) so the gap is in the friendly direction; it's a reassuringly small case where the population gradient genuinely separates `S`, with no estimation noise yet to fight.

How many samples does the `gamma/2` estimate cost? A bounded stochastic gradient concentrates: to estimate each of `n` coordinates to additive `gamma/2` with high probability needs, by Hoeffding plus a union bound over coordinates, `O(log n / gamma^2)` samples. With `gamma = Theta(n^{-(k-1)/2})`, that's `gamma^{-2} = Theta(n^{k-1})`, so `O~(n^{k-1})` samples — one big-batch step of size `~ n^{k-1}` recovers the entire hidden subset. And `n^{k-1}` sits right at the SQ floor `n^{Omega(k)}`. So the first question has an answer: a single neuron, standard init, one large gradient step, reads off `S` near the optimal computational limit. The gradient was never empty — the degree-imbalance in majority's spectrum left a `Theta(n^{-(k-1)/2})` signal that an `n^{k-1}`-sized batch resolves. The "no signal" result was about the dense family; on the sparse family the signal is faint but real and recoverable, exactly as the small-case computation just showed.

That settles the big-batch case, but it doesn't touch the thing that actually nags me: with a *small* batch — even batch size `1` — the same networks still solve it, just slower, and the loss is flat the whole way. One noisy step can't estimate the gradient to `gamma/2`; the noise dwarfs `gamma`. So what's happening across many small steps? Each step adds a noisy, but on-average-correct, push to the weights. For coordinate `i`, the per-step expected push is the population gradient coordinate, which is `Theta(|xi_{k-1}|)` (large) for `i in S` and `Theta(|xi_{k+1}|)` (smaller) for `i not in S`, plus zero-mean minibatch noise of some scale `sigma`. So `w_t[i] - w_0[i]` is a biased random walk: drift `beta_i · t` plus diffusion `sigma · sqrt(t)`, with `beta_i` large on the relevant coordinates. For large enough `t`, the linear drift beats the `sqrt(t)` noise, so the relevant coordinates pull monotonically away from the irrelevant ones. That is a candidate for what governs the plateau: the weights at the relevant indices climbing, slowly and steadily, the whole time. The loss wouldn't see it, because the classifier threshold does not change label until the relevant weights actually overtake the irrelevant ones — there's a threshold to cross, and only when it's crossed does accuracy jump. On this reading, plateau-then-jump is feature amplification under a thresholding nonlinearity, not search: the progress was there all along, hidden by the metric. I find this convincing as a mechanism but I should remember it's a heuristic about the drift; the closed-form trajectory comes later.

If progress is hidden, I want to *un*-hide it — define a scalar I can plot alongside the loss that rises during the flat phase. The cleanest one falls right out of the picture I just drew. Under the approximation that the gradient barely changes early on, `grad ell(w_t) ≈ grad ell(w_0)`, the displacement `w_t - w_0` is just `t` times (roughly) the initial population gradient — a direct linear estimate of the very gradient whose large coordinates are `S`. So the relevant coordinates of `w_t - w_0` grow like `t · beta_i` while the rest grow like `sigma sqrt(t)`. Take the sup-norm: `rho(w_{0:t}) := ||w_t - w_0||_inf`. The largest entry is a relevant one once `t · max_{i in S} beta_i` exceeds the `sigma sqrt(t)` ceiling of the noise — i.e. for `t` past about `(sigma / max beta_i)^2`, `rho` tracks the *drift* `t · max_i |beta_i|`, growing linearly, plainly distinguishable from the `sqrt(t)` envelope of `n` purely-diffusive walks. So `rho` should climb steadily through the plateau even while loss and accuracy are pinned. That's the progress probe: not a proof of the mechanism, but a quantity that makes the invisible amplification visible, grounded in the same population-gradient-with-a-Fourier-gap story. (The Fourier gap of the gradient *along* the trajectory is another such quantity, and if anything it should grow as training proceeds — the amplification looks self-reinforcing — though I won't try to characterize that dynamic here.)

Before I trust "amplification, not search," let me make sure I can kill the search hypothesis from the outside, using only things I can observe without opening up the algorithm — recalling the dynamics I've watched, not inventing new ones. If training were a memoryless search over subsets — try one, test, move on, or a Langevin-style diffusion bouncing around until it falls into the right basin — its convergence time would be geometric, `Geom(1/C(n,k))`: the probability of succeeding by step `t` is highest at the very start and decreases monotonically, so the histogram of convergence times would have its mode at `t = 0` and a fat early bulge. What I actually see is the opposite: a heavy *upper* tail and essentially no successes near `t = 0`. Nobody gets lucky early. A memoryless process gets lucky early all the time; an amplification process can't, because it has to climb first. Second, a memoryless search's runtime depends only on the number of hypotheses `C(n,k)`, but the observed convergence time scales like `n^{O(k)}` — it *adapts to `k`* in the way the drift-versus-noise crossover does, through the `gamma = n^{-(k-1)/2}` gap, not just through the count of subsets. Third, the runtimes are concentrated *conditioned on the initialization* and only weakly sensitive to the batch randomness — the trajectory is largely set by where you start, which is what you'd expect if you're deterministically amplifying a signal fixed by `w_0`, and not what you'd expect from a process driven by lucky draws. Fourth, more width gives sharply diminishing returns rather than the linear `1/r` speedup that running `r` independent searches in parallel would give — because the `r` neurons aren't independent searchers, they're all climbing the same population gradient. Every one of these black-box signatures points away from search and toward steady amplification of a fixed signal.

There's one more objection I have to clear, the lazy/kernel one, because if the network were really just doing kernel regression in fixed features I wouldn't be entitled to talk about "feature learning" at all. The worry: maybe the weights barely move, the network is effectively a linear model over its initial-feature map (the neural tangent kernel), and all the "learning" is in the readout. Let me check whether that regime could even *represent* the answer at the widths I care about. Suppose I have any fixed `D`-dimensional feature map `Psi` with `sup_x ||Psi(x)|| <= 1`, and I fit a linear readout with norm budget `||w|| <= R`. How many parities can such a model fit with margin? The parities are orthonormal, so `f = Psi(x)·w` has `sum_S fhat(S)^2 = ||Psi-side coefficients||^2 <= D R^2` worth of total squared correlation to spread across all `C(n,k)` size-`k` parities (the rank of the feature span is at most `D`, and each unit of feature norm can carry only bounded correlation). So if `D R^2 < eps^2 C(n,k)`, some size-`k` subset `S` is left with correlation below `eps` — `inf_{||w|| <= R} E[loss(Psi(x)·w, y)] > 1 - eps`. To fit *all* parities with margin in the fixed-feature regime you need `D = Omega(n^k)` features — an astronomically wide network. But these things learn parity at width `512`, and even, with the right activation, at width `1`. A width-`1` network has a `1`-dimensional feature; it cannot be doing kernel regression to fit parity. So the weights *must* leave their initialization; the success is genuine feature learning, outside the regime kernel theory describes. Good — that's consistent with everything above, where the entire story was the weights drifting under the population gradient.

I've sketched the mechanism, but I want it nailed down as an end-to-end guarantee, not just a first-step heuristic and a hand-wave about drift. Let me prove it for the two-layer ReLU MLP in the large-batch regime, where I can make the single recovery step rigorous and then turn the second layer into a convex problem. Architecture `f(x; theta) = sum_{i=1}^r u_i sigma(w_i·x + b_i)`, `sigma` = ReLU, hinge loss `ell(y, yhat) = (1 - y yhat)_+`, with `k` even and `n` large. I'll use a *symmetric* `±1` initialization: for `i <= r/2`, `w_i ~ Unif({±1}^n)`, `u_i ~ Unif({±1})`, biases `b_i` on a small grid in `(-1, 1)`; and for `i > r/2`, copy the weights and bias of neuron `i - r/2` but *negate* the second-layer weight, `u_i = -u_{i-r/2}`. The mirror pairs make `f(x; theta_0) = 0` identically, which is the point — with the output zeroed and hinge loss, `ell'(0, y) = -y`, so the first population gradient is exactly the correlation `E[-y · grad f]`, and I'm back to reading Fourier coefficients with no `f`-dependent contamination.

Compute that gradient, neuron by neuron. For neuron `i`, coordinate `j`, the population gradient is `g_{i,j} = E[-y · u_i · 1[w_i·x + b_i > 0] · x_j]`. With `w_i in {±1}^n` and `|b_i| < 1`, the indicator is `(1 + Maj(w_i ⊙ x))/2`, and the Fourier coefficient of `Maj(w_i ⊙ x)` at a set `T` is `\widehat{Maj}(T) · chi_T(w_i)` (the sign-flip `w_i ⊙ x` just multiplies the `T`-coefficient by `chi_T(w_i) in {±1}`). So, exactly as in the single-neuron case but carrying the `u_i` and the `chi_T(w_i)` signs:
`g_{i,j} = -(1/2) u_i xi_{k-1} chi_{[k]\{j}}(w_i)` for `j in [k]` (taking `S = [k]` w.l.o.g. by permutation symmetry),
`g_{i,j} = -(1/2) u_i xi_{k+1} chi_{[k]∪{j}}(w_i)` for `j > k`,
and the bias gradient `gamma_i = -(1/2) u_i \widehat{Maj}([k]) = 0`, because `k` is even and majority's even-degree coefficients vanish — which my exact spectrum computation above confirmed are identically zero. The bias doesn't move on the first step — clean.

Now the one-step lemma. Take the learning rate `eta = 1/(k |xi_{k-1}|)` and weight decay `lambda_0 = 1` on the first step, so the first-layer update is `w_i^{(1)} = (1 - 1)w_i^{(0)} - eta hat-g_i = -eta hat-g_i` — the old weight is wiped and replaced by the (negative, scaled) gradient. Look at what lands in a *lucky* neuron, one whose random `w_i` happens to be constant across the relevant coordinates, `w_{i,j} = u_i · sgn(xi_{k-1})` for all `j in [k]`. For such a neuron and `j in [k]`, `u_i chi_{[k]\{j}}(w_i) = u_i · (u_i sgn xi_{k-1})^{k-1} = sgn(xi_{k-1})` (using `k-1` odd and `u_i^2 = 1`), so `g_{i,j} = -(1/2)|xi_{k-1}|`, and the updated weight is `w_{i,j}^{(1)} = -eta g_{i,j} = (1/(k|xi_{k-1}|))(1/2)|xi_{k-1}| = 1/(2k)` for *every* relevant `j` — a constant `1/(2k)` ridge on the relevant coordinates. For the irrelevant `j > k`, `|w_{i,j}^{(1)}| = eta|hat-g_{i,j}| <= eta(|xi_{k+1}|/2 + tau) = (|xi_{k+1}| + 2 tau)/(2k|xi_{k-1}|)`, which is *small* because `|xi_{k+1}| << |xi_{k-1}|` — that's the Fourier gap again, now controlling the irrelevant-coordinate leakage. (Here `tau` is the sup-norm error of the empirical gradient `hat-g` versus the population `g`; by Hoeffding over a batch of size `B`, getting `tau <= |xi_{k-1}|/(16 k sqrt(2n log(2k/eps)))` needs `B >= (2/tau^2) log(4nr/delta) = O(n^k log(...))` — there's the batch-size price.)

So after one step a lucky neuron computes approximately `h_i(x) = sigma( (1/2k) sum_{j in [k]} x_j + b_i )`, with the irrelevant coordinates contributing only a small perturbation. How small? The irrelevant part is `sum_{j>k} w_{i,j}^{(1)} x_j`, a sum of `n` independent terms each bounded by `Delta = (|xi_{k+1}| + 2 tau)/(2k|xi_{k-1}|)`, zero-mean. Hoeffding bounds its magnitude by `Delta sqrt((n/2) log(2k/eps))` except with probability `eps/k`, and feeding the Fourier ratio `|xi_{k+1}|/|xi_{k-1}| <= sqrt(8 c_2/c_1) k/n` into `Delta` makes this `O(sqrt(log(2k/eps)/n))` — negligible once `n` is large. So with high probability `h_i` is, up to `delta`, the clean ridge function of the relevant-coordinate sum.

These ridge functions are useful because of the staggered biases. Put `b_i = -1/2 + (i+1)/k` for the lucky neurons, so the `k` of them threshold the relevant sum `(1/2k) sum_{j in [k]} x_j` at `k` evenly spaced levels. A weighted combination of `k` ReLUs thresholding the same scalar at staggered points can build any piecewise-linear function of that scalar — in particular the parity, which (as a function of the integer `m = sum_{j in [k]} x_j in {-k, ..., k}`) is the alternating sign pattern. Indeed there's an explicit second-layer choice `u^*` with `||u^*||_inf <= 8k` — `u^*_i = 8k(-1)^{i+1}` for `i <= k-2`, with `u^*_{k-1} = 6k`, `u^*_k = -2k` cleaning up the ends — such that `sum_i u^*_i h_i(x) = 2 chi_{[k]}(x)` exactly. So a fixed linear combination of the `k` lucky ridge neurons *is* twice the parity. Summing the per-neuron `delta` errors with `||u^*||` weights, the realized `f` is within `8k^2 delta` of `2 chi_{[k]}` w.h.p.; choosing `delta = 1/(8k^2)` gives `f(x) chi_{[k]}(x) >= 1` with probability `>= 1 - eps`, and since `|f| <= 16 k^2 n` always, the expected hinge loss is `E[ell(f, chi_{[k]})] <= 16 eps k^2 n`. And I get enough lucky neurons: a random `w_i` is constant on the `k` relevant coordinates with probability `2^{-(k-1)}` and hits a given bias level with probability `1/(2k)`, so with `r = Omega(2^k k log(k/delta))` neurons, w.h.p. there's a lucky neuron at each of the `k` bias levels — `(1 - 1/(k 2^{k-1}))^{r/2} <= exp(-r/(2k 2^{k-1})) <= delta/k`, union over the `k` levels. Width `2^{Theta(k)}` (independent of `n`!) suffices.

Now freeze the first layer — set `eta = 0` on `W, b` after step one — and train only the second layer `u`. With the features `sigma(W^{(1)} x + b^{(1)})` fixed, `F(u) = E_x[ell(u^T sigma(W^{(1)} x + b^{(1)}), y)]` is *convex* in `u`, and I've just shown a good `u^*` exists with `||u^*||_2 <= 8 k sqrt(k)` and `F(u^*) <= eps`. So this is plain convex SGD, and the textbook bound applies: with `u^{(1)} = 0`, step size `eta_t = M/(rho sqrt(T))`, gradients bounded by `rho`, and `||u^*||_2 <= M`, the average iterate satisfies `(1/T) sum_t F(u^{(t)}) <= F(u^*) + M rho/sqrt(T)`. Packaging the gradient bound and the first-step construction together: with `r = 2^{Theta(k)}` and batch `B = Omega(n^k log(n/eps))`, the expected hinge loss drops below `eps` within `O(k^3 r^2 n / eps^2)` iterations. The parity is learned with width independent of `n`, and the only place `n^{Omega(k)}` shows up is the batch size, i.e. the samples, exactly as the SQ trade-off `B · t ~ n^k` demanded.

That proof needed the symmetric `±1` init (to zero the output) and a big batch (to recover `S` in one step), so it doesn't cover the small-batch, generic-init runs where I most want to *see* the plateau. For those I'd need a Fourier gap for population gradients of architectures other than majority-from-sign-init, and lower-bounding low-degree coefficients — "Fourier anti-concentration" — is genuinely hard and largely open. So let me get the *trajectory* — the actual plateau-then-jump, at any batch size — from an idealized architecture where the gradient flow is solvable in closed form and I don't need the Fourier gap at all.

The architecture: split the `n` coordinates into `k` equal blocks `P_1, ..., P_k` of size `n' = n/k`, put one weight vector `w_i in R^{n'}` per block, and output the *product* of the block-wise inner products, `f(x; w_{1:k}) = prod_{i=1}^k <w_i, x_{P_i}>`. This is a tree parity machine with real-valued output instead of a sign. Assume `S` has exactly one relevant index per block (a constant fraction of all `S`), and say it's the first coordinate of each block. Train with the correlation loss. The population gradient: `g_i = E[ -y · grad_{w_i} f ] = -E[ y (prod_{j != i} <w_j, x_{P_j}>) x_{P_i} ]`. Each `<w_j, x_{P_j}>` is a sum of `±1`-weighted independent signs; taking the expectation against `y = prod_i x_{(i-1)n'+1}` (the product of the relevant coordinates), everything in block `j != i` averages out except the relevant-coordinate term `w_{j,1}`, and in block `i` only the relevant coordinate `x_{P_i,1}` survives the correlation with `y`. The result is `g_i = -(prod_{j != i} w_{j,1}) e_1` — the gradient is *nonzero only at the relevant coordinate of each block*. No Fourier gap needed; the irrelevant weights have exactly zero population gradient and never move. The whole dynamics live on the `k` relevant weights `v_i := w_{i,1}`.

Gradient flow on those: `dot{v}_i = -g_{i,1} = prod_{j != i} v_j`. Watch the product `prod_i v_i`: its time derivative is `sum_i (prod_{j != i} v_j) dot{v}_i = sum_i (prod_{j != i} v_j)^2 > 0`, so if it starts positive it stays positive and only grows. And `d(v_i^2)/dt = 2 v_i dot{v}_i = 2 prod_{j=1}^k v_j = 2 prod_j |v_j|`, which is *the same for every `i`* — so the squared relevant weights should all move in lockstep, shifted by their initial values. That lockstep claim and the finite-time blow-up are the crux of the whole plateau story, and they're easy to get wrong by a constant, so let me integrate the flow numerically and look. Taking `k=3`, random `v(0) = (0.746, 0.489, 0.329)`, Euler steps of `1e-4`:

```
final increments v_i^2 - v_i(0)^2 = [1046004.85, 1046004.88, 1046004.89]
  -> spread max-min = 0.044, relative to the mean 4.2e-8   (lockstep: confirmed)
prod_i v_i monotonically increasing throughout:           confirmed
blow-up reached by t ≈ 1.94
theory blow-up bound 1/((k-2) (prod v_i(0))^{1-2/k}) = 2.03
```

The three squared weights stay equal to a relative `4e-8` over a `10^6`-fold growth — the lockstep is real, not approximate — the product climbs monotonically as predicted, and the solution blows up at `t ≈ 1.94`, just inside the theoretical bound `2.03` (it should be at or below it, and it is). So the closed-form picture checks out on a concrete trajectory. To bound the dynamics analytically I sandwich the product `prod_i (q + v_i(0)^2)^{1/2}` — where `q(t) = v_i(t)^2 - v_i(0)^2` is the common increment — between its geometric and arithmetic means via Maclaurin's inequality: with `bar-v_g = (prod_i v_i(0)^2)^{1/k}` and `bar-v_a = (1/k) sum_i v_i(0)^2`,
`(q + bar-v_g)^{k/2} <= prod_i (q + v_i(0)^2)^{1/2} <= (q + bar-v_a)^{k/2}`.
For `k > 2`, integrating the resulting differential inequalities `dot{q} = 2 prod_i (q + v_i(0)^2)^{1/2}` gives, for the time to reach a given `q`,
`(bar-v_g^{-(k/2-1)} - (k-2)t)^{-1/(k/2-1)} - bar-v_g <= q(t) <= (bar-v_a^{-(k/2-1)} - (k-2)t)^{-1/(k/2-1)} - bar-v_a`.
The upper comparison tells me how long `q(t)` can remain below any finite level, and the lower comparison forces blow-up by `t = (1/(k-2)) bar-v_g^{-(k/2-1)} = 1/((k-2)(prod_i v_i(0))^{1-2/k})` — which is the `2.03` the numerics just sat under. So the relevant weights diverge to infinity in finite time, and once they're large, the output is dominated by the relevant product and the classifier is correct.

That's the "jump." For the "plateau" I need to connect weight magnitudes to *accuracy*, and show the network spends almost all its time near-trivial. The error is `err(w) = Pr_x[sgn(f(x)) != chi_S(x)]`. Decompose each block's inner product into the relevant part `v_i z_i` (where `z_i = ±1` is the relevant bit) plus the irrelevant part `u_i^T z_i^-` (`u_i` the irrelevant weights, `z_i^-` the irrelevant bits). The sign of `f` matches the parity exactly when, in *every* block, the relevant term dominates: `|v_i| > |u_i^T z_i^-|`. So a block is "wrong" when `|u_i^T z_i^-| > |v_i|`, and the network errs when an *odd* number of blocks are wrong (parity of the block errors). The block errors are independent across blocks, so by the generating-function identity for the parity of independent Bernoullis, `Pr[#wrong odd] = 1/2 - (1/2) prod_i (1 - 2 p_i)` with `p_i = Pr[|u_i^T z_i^-| > |v_i|]`. Now `u_i^T z_i^-` is a sum of `n'-1` independent `±u_{i,j}` terms; by Berry-Esseen it's close to a Gaussian `N(0, ||u_i||_2^2)`, so `Pr[|u_i^T z_i^-| <= |v_i|] ≈ erf(|v_i|/(||u_i||_2 sqrt 2))`, up to a correction `c ||u_i||_inf/||u_i||_2^3`. Putting it together,
`err(w) >= 1/2 - (1/2) prod_i ( erf(|v_i|/(||u_i||_2 sqrt 2)) + c ||u_i||_inf/||u_i||_2^3 )`,
and an `err <= 2 sum_i exp(-|v_i|^2/||u_i||_2^2)` upper bound from Hoeffding the other way. For `±1` or standard-Gaussian init, `||u_i||_2 ≈ sqrt(n')` and `||u_i||_inf/||u_i||_2^3 ≈ (n')^{-3/2}`, and `erf` of a small argument is roughly linear, so error stays near `1/2` until each `|v_i|` grows to order `sqrt(n')` — the relevant weights have to climb a long way (to scale `sqrt(n')`) before accuracy departs from chance, but once they do, the finite-time blow-up of the gradient flow carries them the rest of the way fast.

Quantify the plateau. Let `T(alpha)` be the last time the error is at least `alpha`. From the error bound, `err >= 1/2 - gamma` as long as every `|v_i| <= b` for `b ≈ gamma^{1/k} sqrt(n')`. From the flow lemma, the time spent before the error can fall below `1/2 - gamma` is at least `(1/(k-2))(1 - O((n')^{1-k/2} gamma^{2/k-1}))` of the finite blow-up time, while the *extra* time to go from `b` to infinity is only `T_i(infty) - T_i(b) <= (1/(k-2)) b^{2-k} = (1/(k-2)) O((n')^{1-k/2} gamma^{2/k-1})`. Take the ratio:
`T(1/2 - gamma)/T(0) >= 1 - O((n')^{1-k/2} gamma^{2/k-1})`.
For `k >= 3` and large `n'`, this is `1 - o(1)`. The network spends a `1 - o(1)` fraction of its entire pre-convergence lifetime stuck above `49%` error and then crosses from barely-better-than-trivial to perfect in the remaining `o(1)` fraction. That is the plateau-then-jump, exhibited in a fully deterministic flow — not an artifact of noise, not luck, but the geometry of `k` weights climbing a product gradient, which the numerical integration above made concrete: a `10^6`-fold lockstep growth in the relevant weights packed into the run-up to a finite-time blow-up. The latent progress is the steady growth of `|v_i|` from `O(1)` toward `sqrt(n')`, completely invisible to the error until the threshold is crossed.

And it carries over to discrete SGD at *any* batch size, even `B = 1`, with the adaptive learning rate `eta_i^{(t)} = 1/(2 sqrt(2T log(2nT/delta)) prod_{l != i} ||w_l^{(t)}||_1)`. The stochastic gradient `hat-g_i^{(t)}` differs from the population `g_i^{(t)}` by a zero-mean term; the accumulated deviation `s_{i,j}^{(t)} = sum_{tau} eta(g - hat-g)` is a martingale with bounded increments `|s^{(t)} - s^{(t-1)}| <= 1/sqrt(2T log(2nT/delta))`, so Azuma-Hoeffding plus a union bound keeps `|s_{i,j}^{(t)}| <= 1/2` for all `t, i, j` w.h.p. Then the irrelevant weights stay bounded — `|w_{i,j}^{(t)}| <= |w_{i,j}^{(1)}| + |s_{i,j}^{(t)}| <= 3/2` — and the relevant weights grow: by induction `|w_{i,1}^{(t)}| >= 1/2 + (1/(4 sqrt 2))(1/(3n'-2))^{k-1} sqrt((t-1)/log(nT/delta))`, where the `(1/(3n'-2))^{k-1}` factor is the discrete echo of the product gradient — each of the `k-1` other blocks contributes a `1/(relevant + irrelevant L1 norm) ≈ 1/(3n')` factor. Running `T = Omega(log(...) log(2k/eps)(3n'-2)^{2k-1})` steps pushes the relevant weights past the `sqrt(n' log(k/eps))` scale the error bound needs, and `err(w^{(T+1)}) <= 2k exp(-(...)^2/(9(n'-1))) = eps`. So the network learns disjoint `k`-parity, at any batch size, in `n^{O(k)}` steps — matching the lower bound, with the same plateau-then-amplify trajectory.

So now I can write down the concrete protocol I'd actually run — the canonical sparse-parity learning setup plus the diagnostic probe, grounded in the standard training harness. Fixed two-layer ReLU MLP, standard initialization (the mechanism doesn't need a special scheme — uniform Xavier-scaled weights, zero biases, work fine; the symmetric `±1` init was only a proof convenience for zeroing the output). Fresh i.i.d. uniform binary batches every step — the online, single-pass regime, so each gradient is an unbiased estimate of the same population gradient and overfitting can't confound the dynamics. Stock optimizer (the Adam family, default `lr = 1e-3`, `weight_decay = 1e-2`, `betas = (0.9, 0.999)`, `eps = 1e-8`) and binary cross-entropy — the mechanism lives in the population gradient's Fourier gap, which is optimizer- and loss-agnostic, so the off-the-shelf defaults are exactly the point: no sparsity prior, nothing bespoke. And alongside the loss I track `rho = ||W_t - W_0||_inf` and the per-coordinate first-layer weight magnitudes, to watch the relevant features amplify under the flat loss curve.

```python
import torch
from torch import nn


def build_model(n_features: int, width: int = 512) -> nn.Sequential:
    # Fixed two-layer ReLU MLP; threshold 0.5 is the classifier. No sparsity prior.
    return nn.Sequential(
        nn.Linear(n_features, width),
        nn.ReLU(),
        nn.Linear(width, 1),
        nn.Sigmoid(),
    )


def init_model(model: nn.Sequential) -> None:
    # Standard, data-INDEPENDENT init (cannot see the secret). The mechanism is
    # robust to the scheme; this is the default in deep-learning packages.
    for layer in model:
        if isinstance(layer, nn.Linear):
            gain = nn.init.calculate_gain("relu") if layer is model[0] else 1.0
            nn.init.xavier_uniform_(layer.weight, gain=gain)
            nn.init.zeros_(layer.bias)


def parity_labels(x: torch.Tensor, secret) -> torch.Tensor:
    # y = parity (sum-mod-2 / XOR) of the bits at the hidden indices.
    idx = torch.tensor(secret, dtype=torch.long, device=x.device)
    return x.index_select(1, idx).sum(dim=1).remainder(2).to(torch.float32)


def make_online_batch(secret, n_features: int, batch_size: int, generator):
    # Fresh i.i.d. uniform binary batch: each step is an unbiased sample of the
    # SAME population gradient -> clean drift, no overfitting confound.
    x = torch.randint(0, 2, (batch_size, n_features), generator=generator).float()
    return x, parity_labels(x, secret)


def make_test_set(secret, n_features: int, test_size: int, generator):
    x = torch.randint(0, 2, (test_size, n_features), generator=generator).float()
    return x, parity_labels(x, secret)


def get_optimizer_config() -> dict[str, float]:
    return {"lr": 1e-3, "wd": 1e-2, "beta1": 0.9, "beta2": 0.999, "eps": 1e-8}


def state_probe(model: nn.Sequential, init_state) -> float:
    # ||W_t - W_0||_inf on the first layer: a linear estimate of the initial
    # population gradient, whose largest coordinates are S. It rises (drift ~ t)
    # while loss is flat (the relevant features amplifying under the threshold).
    W0 = init_state["0.weight"]
    W = model[0].weight.detach()
    return (W - W0).abs().max().item()


def train_parity(secret, n_features, width=512, batch_size=128, steps=100_000,
                 test_size=16_384, seed=0, device="cpu"):
    model = build_model(n_features, width).to(device)
    init_model(model)
    init_state = {name: p.detach().clone() for name, p in model.named_parameters()}
    opt_cfg = get_optimizer_config()
    opt = torch.optim.AdamW(
        model.parameters(),
        lr=opt_cfg["lr"],
        betas=(opt_cfg["beta1"], opt_cfg["beta2"]),
        eps=opt_cfg["eps"],
        weight_decay=opt_cfg["wd"],
    )
    criterion = nn.BCELoss()
    train_gen = torch.Generator().manual_seed(seed)
    test_gen = torch.Generator().manual_seed(seed + 1)
    test_x, test_y = make_test_set(secret, n_features, test_size, test_gen)

    for t in range(1, steps + 1):
        xb, yb = make_online_batch(secret, n_features, batch_size, train_gen)  # fresh batch
        xb, yb = xb.to(device), yb.to(device)
        opt.zero_grad(set_to_none=True)
        loss = criterion(model(xb).view(-1), yb)             # BCE for Sigmoid probabilities
        loss.backward()                                      # noisy estimate of the pop. gradient
        opt.step()                                           # relevant coords drift up by ~gamma
        if t % 250 == 0:
            rho = state_probe(model, init_state)             # rises on the plateau
            print(f"step {t}  loss {loss.item():.4f}  rho {rho:.4f}")
    with torch.no_grad():
        preds = model(test_x.to(device)).view(-1)
        acc = ((preds >= 0.5) == (test_y.to(device) >= 0.5)).float().mean().item()
    return model, acc
```

Let me close the loop on the causal chain. The pain: sparse parity is statistically trivial but SQ-hard, `n^{Omega(k)}` is the floor for any gradient method, and the gradient-variance-over-all-parities argument says the gradient should carry no signal — yet plain nets solve it near the floor, with a flat plateau that snaps to solved. The crack in the pessimism: the no-signal bound is over the *dense* family of all `2^n` parities; the sparse problem's family has only `n^k` members, so the same bound leaves a *polynomial* residual signal of scale `n^{-k/2}`. Localizing it: the population gradient of a ReLU neuron at all-ones or random-sign init is, coordinate by coordinate, a Fourier coefficient of majority — degree `k-1` at relevant indices, degree `k+1` at irrelevant ones — and majority's spectrum (whose ratio `|xi_{k-1}|/|xi_{k+1}| = (n-k)/(k-1)` I checked exactly, finding it only points the right way once `n > 2k-1`) dominates the lower degree by `gamma = Theta(n^{-(k-1)/2})`; reading the top-`k` gradient coordinates recovers `S` from `O~(n^{k-1})` samples, which I verified end-to-end on `n=9, k=4` — top-4 by magnitude land exactly on `S`. With small batches the gap can't be resolved in one step, but each relevant weight is a biased random walk whose drift `~ gamma · t` outruns the `sqrt(t)` noise, so the relevant features amplify monotonically — invisible at the classifier threshold until they overtake the irrelevant ones, which is the abrupt jump; the weight-movement norm `||W_t - W_0||_inf` makes the otherwise invisible progress visible. The black-box statistics (no early successes, `k`-adaptive `n^{O(k)}` scaling, concentration-given-init, sub-linear width returns) all rule out memoryless search; the NTK lower bound rules out the lazy regime, forcing genuine feature learning. Two end-to-end proofs lock it in: a width-`2^{Theta(k)}` MLP with a single large recovery step plus convex second-layer training reaches `eps` loss in `poly · 2^{O(k)}` iterations with batch `n^{O(k)}`; and a real-output tree parity machine, solved in closed form (and integrated numerically to confirm the lockstep growth and the finite-time blow-up at `t ≈ 1.94 < 2.03`), exhibits the plateau exactly — spending a `1 - o(1)` fraction of training above `49%` error before the relevant weights blow up — and converges in `n^{O(k)}` steps at any batch size. The protocol that drops out is the ordinary one: fixed MLP, standard init, fresh online batches, stock optimizer and BCE — no sparsity prior anywhere — with a progress probe on the weights to watch the features emerge behind the flat loss.
