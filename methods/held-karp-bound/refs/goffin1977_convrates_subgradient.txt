           Rates of convergence of subgradient optimization are studied. If the step size is chosen to
         be a geometric progression with ratio p the convergence, if it occurs, is geometric with rate p.
         For convergence to occur, it is necessary that the initial step size be large enough, and that the
         ratio p be greater than a sustainable rate z(ix), which depends upon a condition number Ix,
         defined for both differentiable and nondifferentiable functions. The sustainable rate z(Ix) is
         closely related to the rate of convergence of the steepest ascent method for differentiable
         functions: in fact it is identical if the function is not too well conditioned.

            Key words : Nondifferentiable optimization, Rates of convergence, Nonsmooth optimization.

7. Conclusions

  The c o n v e r g e n c e properties of subgradient optimization have some similarities
with the properties of the steepest ascent method for differentiable functions;
namely that geometric c o n v e r g e n c e occurs at a rate which depends on the
conditioning of the function to optimize. In fact the sustainable rate of con-
vergence in subgradient optimization reduces to the rate of c o n v e r g e n c e of the
steepest ascent method in the case of a differentiable function provided that
346                    J.L. Goffin[ Convergence rates of subgradient methods

/z-----lx/2; if ~ >½X/2 then the sustainable rate of convergence is slower, with a
best possible value of i if the function satisfies tz = 1.
   The big difference is, of course, that in subgradient optimization, the rate of
convergence z(/x) could be obtained only if it were known (which is clearly not
the case).
   The main expectation from the more sophisticated methods like conjugate
subgradient optimization is that they would guarantee a rate of convergence
which would not require any a priori knowledge of a condition number nor an
estimate of d(x°).
