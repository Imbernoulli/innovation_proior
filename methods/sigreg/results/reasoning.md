Let me start from what actually breaks. I have an encoder `f_theta` and I train it by joint embedding: take two related views of the same input, embed both, and push the encoder so one view's embedding predicts the other's. The trouble is that this predictability objective, on its own, has a trivial global minimum. If `f_theta` maps every input to the same constant vector, then any view predicts any other view perfectly, the loss is zero, and the representation carries nothing. That is complete collapse, and the softer version — all embeddings squashed into a low-dimensional subspace, dimensional collapse — is just as useless and is documented to track the covariance spectrum of the embeddings going rank-deficient. So I need a second term whose only job is to keep the embeddings from degenerating. And the way the field builds that second term today is a mess: feature whitening layers, batches of negative samples, momentum teacher networks feeding students with a stop-gradient on the teacher branch, centering and sharpening of outputs, asymmetric augmentations. Each of these is a patch on a symptom. They share a family of defects I keep running into. They are under-specified — I can drive whitening's identity-covariance criterion, or the variance-covariance terms, all the way to their minimum and still have embeddings sitting in a degenerate configuration, because those criteria only pin down a couple of summary statistics and leave the rest of the distribution free. Several are quadratic in the batch size or the embedding dimension — anything that compares all pairs of samples, or forms and inverts a covariance matrix, costs me `O(N^2)` or `O(K^3)`. They are brittle: the EMA decay schedule is coupled to the learning rate, the temperature has to be retuned, projector widths matter, and a recipe tuned in one setting collapses in another. And there is no theory telling me what the embeddings are supposed to look like — the theory that exists, the mutual-information bounds, was written after the fact to explain methods that already existed, not to tell me what to build. I want to stop patching symptoms and instead answer two questions in order: what distribution *should* the embeddings have, and then how do I enforce that distribution cheaply and stably.

So first: which distribution? The honest constraint is that I do not know the downstream task at pretraining time — the whole point of a foundation model is that it will be probed for tasks I have not seen. That means I should pick the embedding geometry that is best in the *worst case* over unknown tasks, or equivalently, that makes the fewest commitments about which directions in embedding space will matter later. Let me make that precise with the simplest downstream probe, a linear one. Freeze the encoder, collect the `N` embeddings into a matrix `Z`, and fit ridge regression to some unknown labels `y`: `beta_hat = argmin ||y - Z beta||^2 + lambda ||beta||^2`, closed form `beta_hat = (Z^T Z + lambda I)^{-1} Z^T y`. I do not know `y`, but I can still write down how the *estimator* behaves as a function of the geometry of `Z`. Say the labels are generated by some true `beta_true` with mean-zero noise, `y = Z beta_true + eps`. The bias of ridge is `E[beta_hat] - beta_true = (Z^T Z + lambda I)^{-1} Z^T Z beta_true - beta_true`, and a line of algebra collapses that to `-lambda (Z^T Z + lambda I)^{-1} beta_true`. Diagonalize the Gram matrix `Z^T Z = Q Lambda Q^T`. Now compare two embeddings that capture the same features and carry the same total energy — same column span, same `sum_k lambda_k` — but with different *shapes*: one isotropic, all eigenvalues equal to the mean `lambda_bar`, the other anisotropic with at least two distinct eigenvalues. Take the adversarial label direction, `beta_true` aligned with the eigenvector of the *smallest* eigenvalue `lambda_p`. Then the bias norm is `lambda / (lambda_p + lambda) * ||beta_true||` for the anisotropic case versus `lambda / (lambda_bar + lambda) * ||beta_true||` for the isotropic one, and since `lambda_p < lambda_bar` strictly whenever the spectrum is not flat, the anisotropic bias is strictly larger. There always exists a downstream task — pointed along the weak direction — for which anisotropy hurts. And the variance tells the same story even more cleanly: with `lambda = 0` the estimator is unbiased with `Var(beta_hat | Z) = sigma^2 (Z^T Z)^{-1}`, so the total variance is `sigma^2 tr((Z^T Z)^{-1}) = sigma^2 sum_j 1/lambda_j`. The function `1/x` is strictly convex, so by Jensen, at fixed mean eigenvalue, `sum_j 1/lambda_j` is minimized exactly when all the `lambda_j` are equal — the isotropic spectrum minimizes the worst-case-task variance, strictly, unless the embedding is already isotropic. So the linear-probe analysis hands me half the answer with no ambiguity: the embeddings should be isotropic.

But isotropic only fixes the covariance shape — it says nothing about the rest of the distribution, and a worst-case-over-tasks argument should care about more than two moments. Let me push to a nonlinear probe and see whether something more specific than "isotropic" falls out. Take a radius-based `k`-NN probe — predict at a query by averaging the labels of training embeddings within radius `r0` — or its smooth cousin, a kernel (Nadaraya–Watson) estimator. For these, the standard small-bandwidth expansion gives the pointwise bias at `x` as `r0^2/(d+2) * [grad eta(x) . grad log p(x) + (1/2) Delta eta(x)] + o(r0^2)`, where `eta` is the unknown target function and `p` is the embedding density. I want the integrated squared bias over query points drawn from `p`, under a neutral prior on the unknown task: the target's gradient is mean-zero and isotropic, `E[grad eta] = 0` and `E[grad eta grad eta^T] = tau_g^2 I`. Square the bias and take expectations. Three terms appear. The curvature term, `E[(Delta eta)^2]`, is independent of `p` — it does not care about the embedding distribution. The cross term vanishes under the isotropic-gradient prior (or is a `p`-independent constant otherwise). The interesting one is the score-gradient term: `E[(grad eta . grad log p)^2]`. Write `v = grad log p`; then `E_eta[(grad eta^T v)^2] = v^T E[grad eta grad eta^T] v = tau_g^2 ||v||^2`, and integrating over `x ~ p` gives `tau_g^2 integral ||grad log p(x)||^2 p(x) dx`. That integral is exactly the Fisher-information functional `J(p)`. So the part of the downstream bias that I can actually control through the embedding distribution is `tau_g^2 J(p)` — to minimize worst-case bias, I should minimize the Fisher information of the embedding density, subject to a scalar covariance constraint (the total energy is fixed; the specific value does not change the optimal shape).

Now, which density minimizes Fisher information at fixed covariance? This is where "isotropic" sharpens into "isotropic *Gaussian*," and I want to see *why* the Gaussian, specifically, rather than wave at maximum entropy. Fix the covariance `Sigma` and consider the location family `p_theta(x) = p(x - theta)`. Its Fisher information matrix about the location is `I(theta) = E[grad log p (grad log p)^T]`, and `J(p) = tr I`. The identity estimator `T(X) = X` is unbiased for the location `theta` under `p_theta`, and its covariance is `Sigma`. The matrix Cramér-Rao bound says the covariance of any unbiased estimator dominates the inverse Fisher information, `Cov(T) >= I(theta)^{-1}`. For this estimator, `Sigma >= I^{-1}`; inverting the positive-definite order reverses it, so `I >= Sigma^{-1}`. Take traces: `J(p) = tr I >= tr(Sigma^{-1})`. Equality in the Cramér-Rao bound holds if and only if the score `grad log p_theta(X)` is an affine function of `X - theta`, which integrates to a quadratic log-density, so `p` is Gaussian with precision matrix given by the negative affine slope. So among all densities with covariance `Sigma`, the Gaussian `N(0, Sigma)` is the unique minimizer of Fisher information, attaining `tr(Sigma^{-1})`. That is the reason it has to be Gaussian and not merely isotropic: Gaussian is the Fisher-information-minimizer at fixed covariance, and Fisher information is precisely the functional the nonlinear-probe bias depends on. Then I am left to minimize `tr(Sigma^{-1}) = sum_i 1/lambda_i` over the covariance shape under a scalar constraint, and every reasonable scalar constraint gives the same answer. Under a trace constraint `sum_i lambda_i = t`, Cauchy-Schwarz gives `(sum 1/lambda_i)(sum lambda_i) >= d^2`, equality iff all `lambda_i` equal, so `Sigma = (t/d) I`. Under a determinant constraint, AM-GM on `mu_i = 1/lambda_i` gives `(1/d) sum_i mu_i >= (prod_i mu_i)^{1/d}`, with equality iff all `lambda_i` equal. Under a Frobenius constraint `sum lambda_i^2 = c^2`, the Lagrange conditions `-lambda_i^{-2} + 2 nu lambda_i = 0` give `lambda_i^3` constant, again all equal. Under a spectral-radius cap `lambda_i <= r`, the decreasing function `1/x` is minimized by pushing every eigenvalue to `r`. In every case the optimum is `Sigma = s I` — isotropic. So the two axioms drop out: the embeddings should solve the prediction task, and they should be distributed as an *isotropic Gaussian*, `N(0, I)` after the harmless choice of scale. Now I have a concrete target distribution `Q = N(0, I)` and a clean problem: build a regularizer that pushes the batch of embeddings toward `Q`.

The naive instinct is to write a divergence — KL, or maximum mean discrepancy — between the empirical embedding distribution `P_theta` and `Q` and minimize it. But estimating any of these in `K`-dimensional space is exactly the high-dimensional density-estimation problem I am trying to avoid, and the differentiable multivariate options I know are expensive. Let me reframe instead of reaching for a divergence. What I really want to ask is: *is there evidence that `P_theta` differs from `Q`?* That is a hypothesis test. Set up the null `H0: P_theta = Q` against `H1: P_theta != Q`, pick a scalar test statistic `T` that measures evidence against the null, and *minimize `T`* as my regularizer — driving the encoder toward configurations where there is no detectable departure from Gaussianity. The statistic just has to be differentiable in the embeddings and cheap. The trouble is that the off-the-shelf multivariate normality tests are not cheap. The ones built on the characteristic function — Baringhaus–Henze, Henze–Zirkler — reduce to a double sum over all pairs of samples, `(1/n) sum_{j,k} exp(-beta^2 ||Y_j - Y_k||^2 / 2)` and similar. That is `O(N^2)` in the batch, it couples every sample to every other so it does not shard cleanly across data-parallel devices, and in high dimension it needs a huge sample count to have any power. Wall. I cannot pay quadratic cost or break the embarrassingly-parallel structure of SGD.

How do I turn a `K`-dimensional distributional test into something cheap? Slice it. Cramér and Wold proved that two random vectors are equal in distribution if and only if all of their one-dimensional linear projections are equal in distribution. So instead of testing the `K`-dimensional law directly, I can test the *univariate* projections `a^T f_theta(x)` against the univariate projection of the target, for directions `a`. And I can restrict `a` to the unit sphere without losing anything — let me check that, because it is what makes the projections clean. Suppose every unit-direction projection matches: `<u, X> =d <u, Y>` for all `u` on the sphere. Take any `t`; write `t = s u` with `s = ||t||` and `u = t/||t||` a unit vector. Then the characteristic function `phi_X(t) = E[exp(i <t, X>)] = E[exp(i s <u, X>)] = E[exp(i s <u, Y>)] = phi_Y(t)`, because along the *fixed* direction `u` the two scalar random variables `<u,X>` and `<u,Y>` are equal in distribution, hence share all their characteristic-function values, including at `s`. So `phi_X = phi_Y` everywhere, and by the uniqueness theorem for characteristic functions `X =d Y`. Matching all *unit*-direction projections is therefore exactly as strong as matching the full multivariate distribution; the radial part is redundant because the characteristic function along a ray is fixed by its direction. So a `K`-dimensional Gaussianity test reduces to a family of one-dimensional Gaussianity tests, one per direction, each operating on the scalar samples `a^T f_theta(x_n)`. And the target projection is trivial — for `Q = N(0, I)`, every unit projection `a^T Z` is exactly `N(0, 1)`, so each univariate test is just "are these projected scalars standard normal?"

I cannot use infinitely many directions, so I sample a finite set `A` of unit directions and aggregate the per-direction statistics. How to aggregate? The clean theoretical object is the *maximum* over directions: `T_A = max_{a in A} T(a)`. That is what gives a proper test — if I want to *certify* Gaussianity I should reject as soon as *any* direction shows a departure, and the consistency argument is exactly that. If `P != Q`, there is a separating direction `a*` and, by continuity, a whole neighborhood of directions around it where the one-dimensional statistic exceeds the null threshold with probability tending to one; as I sample more directions and the sphere gets densely covered, my set `A` eventually lands a direction inside that neighborhood, so the max rejects with probability one (power), while under the null no direction separates and calibration keeps the rejection rate below `alpha` (level). So `max` is the right object for a *test*. But I am not running a test once; I am minimizing this as a *loss* by gradient descent. And `max` has a fatal flaw as a loss: its gradient flows only through the single arg-max direction. Every other direction in my batch of, say, two hundred and fifty-six gets zero gradient. That is an extremely sparse, jumpy training signal — the encoder only ever hears about the current worst direction, the arg-max flickers from step to step, and the other directions' information is thrown away. So for the loss I replace the max by the *average*: `(1/|A|) sum_{a in A} T(a)`. The average is no longer a calibrated test statistic, but as a training objective it routes gradient through *all* sampled directions at once, which is what I want for stable optimization. Slicing plus averaging — that is the skeleton of the regularizer. The remaining question, the one that decides whether the whole thing works, is *which* univariate goodness-of-fit statistic `T` to put on each slice.

Let me actually walk the three families of univariate tests and find where each breaks, because the choice is not cosmetic — it determines whether training is stable and whether collapse is genuinely ruled out. Start with moment-based tests, the most obvious. Jarque–Bera compares skewness and kurtosis to the Gaussian values; since I want *standard* normal I should also pin the first two moments, giving an extended statistic that matches the first four moments. Moment matching is intuitive and cheap. But there is a conundrum. A finite number of moments does not determine a distribution. Concretely: pick `K+2` distinct support points and look at the linear map sending a probability vector to its first `K` moments; that map is from a `(K+2)`-dimensional space to a `(K+1)`-dimensional space, so it has a nontrivial kernel, and any nonzero kernel vector (which must sum to zero, hence has both signs) lets me perturb a strictly-positive probability vector into two *distinct* distributions with *identical* first `K` moments. So minimizing a weighted sum of squared moment discrepancies up to order `K` does *not* imply `P = Q` — there are non-Gaussian distributions that match any finite set of moments, and those are exactly the shortcut, collapsed-but-moment-correct solutions I am trying to forbid. The textbook fix is to use as many moments as possible, `K -> infinity`. But now stability collapses. The gradient of the `k`-th moment with respect to the embedding goes like `||E[k (a^T f_theta(x))^{k-1} a^T J_f(x)]||`, which scales as `O(k)` and, worse, the Monte-Carlo variance of that gradient grows like `O(k^2 m_{2(k-1)})` — it depends on the `2(k-1)`-th moment, which for any heavy-ish projected sample is enormous. Concretely, the gradient of a moment objective is a polynomial in each sample `X_i` of degree `k-1`, so `|partial / partial X_i|` grows like `|X_i|^{k-1}` and is unbounded for `k >= 2`. So with moments I face a genuine trap: I need many moments for identifiability (to rule out shortcuts), but each extra moment makes the gradients more explosive, and I cannot have both. Wall — moments are out.

Next family: CDF-based tests — Cramér–von Mises (`w = 1`), Anderson–Darling (`w = [F(1-F)]^{-1}`), Watson (`U^2` correction), Kolmogorov–Smirnov, Shapiro–Wilk. These compare the empirical distribution function to the target. They are identifiable — they look at the whole CDF, not a few moments — so they fix the shortcut problem. But they have their own structural defect: the empirical CDF is built by *sorting* the samples and counting, an `O(N log N)` operation, and sorting breaks me twice. First, it is not differentiable: nudging a sample changes the jump locations of the step-function empirical CDF discontinuously, so I would have to swap in some sorting relaxation, which drags in extra hyperparameters — exactly the kind of knob I am trying to eliminate. Second, even the optimized sort destroys the embarrassingly-parallel structure of data-parallel SGD: order statistics need a global synchronization across devices, where a moment or an average needs only a cheap all-reduce. Kolmogorov–Smirnov is worse still because it uses the sup norm, an `L-infinity` distance, whose gradient is sparse — only the single worst point gets signal, the same sparsity disease I just removed by averaging over directions. Shapiro–Wilk needs the inverse covariance matrix of the Gaussian order statistics and I find it unstable in practice. So CDF tests are identifiable but non-differentiable and non-parallelizable. Wall.

That points me at the third family, the characteristic function, and now I can see why it threads the needle. The empirical characteristic function is `phi_hat_X(t) = (1/n) sum_j exp(i t X_j)`. Look at what kind of object that is: a simple *average* of bounded complex exponentials. Averages are differentiable in the samples, with no sorting and no order statistics, and an average reduces across data-parallel devices with a single all-reduce. Already it sidesteps both defects of the CDF tests. And it is identifiable in the way moments are not: the characteristic function determines the distribution uniquely (it *is* the full Fourier content, not a truncation), so matching it rules out the moment-matching shortcuts. The Epps–Pulley statistic compares the empirical characteristic function to the target's in a weighted `L2` norm, `EP = N integral |phi_hat_X(t) - phi(t)|^2 w(t) dt`, where for the standard-normal target `phi(t) = exp(-t^2/2)` and the weight is a Gaussian window `w(t) = exp(-t^2/sigma^2)`, typically `sigma = 1`, which down-weights large `|t|` so the integral converges and concentrates on the informative low-frequency band. So far it has the two properties the others lacked. The real reason to pick it, though, is the gradient behavior, and I should prove it rather than hope.

I want to show the Epps–Pulley weighted-`L2` core has bounded gradient and bounded curvature with respect to each sample, *regardless of the input distribution*. Write the Gaussian weight as `w_s(t) = exp(-s^2 t^2)` — the parameterization `exp(-t^2/sigma^2)` is this with `s^2 = 1/sigma^2` — and take the normalized distance `D = integral w_s(t) |phi_hat_N(t) - phi_G(t)|^2 dt`. Differentiate with respect to one sample `X_i`. The only `X_i`-dependence is through `phi_hat_N(t) = (1/N) sum_j exp(i t X_j)`, whose derivative is `partial phi_hat_N / partial X_i = (1/N) i t exp(i t X_i)`. So `partial D / partial X_i = integral w_s(t) 2 Re[(phi_hat_N(t) - phi_G(t)) conj(partial phi_hat_N / partial X_i)] dt`. Now bound it using only that every characteristic function has modulus at most one: `|phi_hat_N| <= 1`, `|phi_G| <= 1`, and `|exp(i t X_i)| = 1`. Then `|partial D / partial X_i| <= (2/N) integral w_s(t) |t| (|phi_hat_N| + |phi_G|) dt <= (4/N) integral w_s(t) |t| dt`. So the whole bound rests on the value of `integral exp(-s^2 t^2) |t| dt`. Let me actually evaluate it rather than quote it. By symmetry it is `2 integral_0^infty exp(-s^2 t^2) t dt`; substituting `u = s^2 t^2`, `du = 2 s^2 t dt`, the inner integral is `(1/(2 s^2)) integral_0^infty exp(-u) du = 1/(2 s^2)`, so the whole thing is `1/s^2`. Numerically, with `s = 1` (i.e. `sigma = 1`), I integrate `exp(-t^2)|t|` over a wide grid and get `0.99999999999`, matching `1/s^2 = 1`. So `|partial D / partial X_i| <= 4/(N s^2) = 4 sigma^2 / N` — substituting back `1/s^2 = sigma^2`, the per-sample gradient is bounded by `4 sigma^2 / N` no matter what the embeddings look like. Differentiate once more for the curvature: each extra `X_i`-derivative pulls down another factor of `t`, so `|partial^2 D / partial X_i^2| <= (C/N) integral w_s(t) t^2 dt = (C/N) * (sqrt(pi)/(2 s^3))`, using `integral exp(-s^2 t^2) t^2 dt = sqrt(pi)/(2 s^3)` (the standard Gaussian second-moment integral; at `s = 1` I get `0.88622692545`, against `sqrt(pi)/2 = 0.88622692545` — they agree to eleven digits); with `s = 1/sigma` that is `C sqrt(pi) sigma^3 / (2 N)`. Bounded gradient *and* bounded curvature, with the scale set only by `N` and `sigma`. The classical test statistic and the implementation multiply this distance by the global batch size for calibration and scale, so the outer loss coefficient absorbs that fixed rescaling during training; the stability statement is about the distributional distance being optimized.

The bound is a worst case, so let me see how it behaves on real samples — and at the same time check the more important property, that the statistic actually *separates* Gaussian from non-Gaussian. I implement the one-dimensional EP statistic exactly as the code will (seventeen knots, `t_max = 3`, the cosine and sine batch means) and evaluate `D` on a batch of `N = 32` standard-normal draws with autograd; the largest per-sample `|partial D / partial X_i|` I see across fifty random batches is about `0.015`, comfortably under the bound `4/32 = 0.125` — the bound is loose on tame inputs, as a *for-any-distribution* bound should be, and it holds. Then the separation, on batches of two thousand projected scalars: on `N(0,1)` samples the statistic reads about `0.13`; on a near-constant collapsed slice it reads about `820`; on a symmetric `+/-1` two-point distribution — which has exactly mean `0` and variance `1`, so it would pass any first-and-second-moment test — it reads about `210`; on a variance-one uniform, about `33`. So the statistic is tiny precisely on the target and large on every degenerate or non-Gaussian alternative I throw at it, *including* the moment-matching shortcut that a moments-based test would wave through. That is the identifiability I argued for abstractly, now showing up as a number: the two-point distribution that a moment test accepts gets a statistic of `210`, not zero. This is the property moments structurally cannot have on the gradient side either — their gradient is a polynomial in the sample and runs off to infinity — and the bounded gradient is exactly what I need for stable large-scale training: by the chain rule the normalized slice loss obeys `||grad_theta D|| <= (4 sigma^2 / N) sum_i ||a^T grad_theta f_theta(x_i)||`, which never explodes regardless of how degenerate or heavy-tailed the current embeddings are. So the characteristic-function (Epps–Pulley) test is the one that satisfies all four requirements at once — differentiable, parallelizable, identifiable, and bounded in gradient and curvature — where moments fail identifiability-vs-stability and CDF tests fail differentiability and parallelism. So `T` is Epps–Pulley, and the regularizer is: sample unit directions, project the embeddings onto each, compare the empirical characteristic function of each projection to `exp(-t^2/2)` in the Gaussian-weighted `L2` norm, and average over directions.

Before I trust this in high dimension I have a real worry: I am only ever sampling a handful of directions per step — a few hundred, or even sixteen — out of a `K`-dimensional sphere. In a thousand-dimensional embedding space, can so few one-dimensional slices possibly constrain the whole distribution? Naively the sphere needs exponentially many directions to cover, and that would be the curse of dimensionality reasserting itself. Two things rescue it. The first is smoothness. Fix a frequency `t` and look at the function on the sphere `a -> phi_a(t)`, the projected characteristic function as a function of direction. If the embedding density lives in a Sobolev space `H^alpha`, this function inherits smoothness `alpha` on the sphere, and approximating a smooth function on the sphere from its values at `M` sampled points is governed by spherical-harmonic interpolation theory: the squared `L2` interpolation error of an `H^alpha` function from `M` quasi-uniform points decays like `M^{-2 alpha/(K-1)}`. So if I satisfy the test on `M = |A|` directions, the *expected* discrepancy over *all* directions is bounded by `C(K, alpha) |A|^{-2 alpha/(K-1)}` times the squared Sobolev norm of the discrepancy integrated over frequencies. The exponent `-2 alpha/(K-1)` is the crux: when `alpha` is large the rate is fast and `|A| = O(K)` directions should suffice for an epsilon-approximation, beating the naive exponential blowup. Whether `alpha` is large enough here is an empirical bet I cannot settle on paper: I expect deep-network embeddings to be fairly smooth — from the architecture and from the implicit and explicit regularization of training — and the target itself, the Gaussian, is infinitely smooth, so the discrepancy function on the sphere should be smooth with fast-decaying high-frequency spherical-harmonic content; but the actual `alpha` is a property of the trained encoder and I would want to verify that a modest `|A|` really does control the all-directions discrepancy by sweeping `|A|` at training time. The argument tells me the *form* of the trade-off, not that I get it for free. The second rescue is sturdier and does not depend on `alpha`: I do not have to reuse the *same* directions. If I resample `A` fresh at every gradient step, then even though only `|A|` directions act at any one step, the *cumulative* number of directions seen grows linearly with training time, and the sphere gets covered over the course of optimization. So I will draw a new random `A` every step. That also tells me the directions must be drawn the *same* way on every data-parallel device, or different GPUs would project along different axes and the averaged statistic would be incoherent; I will seed the random generator from a global step counter that I synchronize across devices.

One more thing to check before code: the statistic is computed on a minibatch, so it is biased, and I want to know the size of that bias. For a fixed `t`, expand `E[|phi_hat_n(t) - psi(t)|^2]`. The empirical characteristic function is unbiased, `E[phi_hat_n] = phi_theta`, so the cross terms give `-2 Re(conj(psi) phi_theta)`, and the only subtlety is `E[|phi_hat_n|^2]`. Write it as a double sum `(1/n^2) sum_{j,l} E[Z_j conj(Z_l)]` with `Z_j = exp(i t X_j)`. The diagonal terms `j = l` give `E[|Z_j|^2] = 1` (modulus one), and the off-diagonal i.i.d. terms give `E[Z_j] conj(E[Z_l]) = |phi_theta|^2`. So `E[|phi_hat_n|^2] = (1/n^2)(n + n(n-1)|phi_theta|^2) = |phi_theta|^2 + (1 - |phi_theta|^2)/n`. Putting the pieces together, `E[|phi_hat_n - psi|^2] = |phi_theta - psi|^2 + (1 - |phi_theta|^2)/n`. So the expected statistic is the true population discrepancy plus an explicit additive term `(1/n) integral w_s(t)(1 - |phi_P(t)|^2) dt`, and by dominated convergence the gradient picks up the same `O(1/n)` bias. Let me confirm the formula on a case where I can isolate the bias term: take `P = Q = N(0,1)`, so the first term `|phi_theta - psi|^2` is zero and the whole expectation should be just `(1 - |phi_theta(t)|^2)/n = (1 - exp(-t^2))/n` at a single frequency `t`. At `t = 0.8`, `n = 16`, the formula predicts `(1 - exp(-0.64))/16 = 0.029544`. Averaging `|phi_hat_n(0.8) - psi(0.8)|^2` over two hundred thousand independent batches of sixteen normals gives `0.029540` — agreement to four figures, so the bias term is exactly the `(1-|phi|^2)/n` I derived and not something I mis-handled in the double sum. It vanishes as `O(1/N)`: even at the worst case I care about, batch size sixteen, this leftover is at the third decimal place, dominated by the population discrepancy whenever the embeddings are actually non-Gaussian. If I ever wanted it gone exactly I could debias `|phi|^2` with a U-statistic or split the sample, but at this magnitude it is not worth the trouble.

Now the integral over `t` has to become a finite computation. I do not have a closed form for `integral |phi_hat - phi|^2 w dt` over a general empirical characteristic function, so I approximate it by quadrature on a grid of frequency knots, with the trapezoidal rule. How fine a grid? The integrand is smooth and concentrated by the Gaussian window, so it turns out very few knots suffice — seventeen is plenty, and I can sanity-check that a coarser or finer grid barely moves the result. And the range of `t`: the window kills large `|t|`, so I integrate over a bounded interval, something like `[-3, 3]` or `[-5, 5]`; too narrow, like `[-1, 1]`, throws away discriminating frequency content and hurts, but `[-3, 3]` and `[-5, 5]` are both fine. There is a free efficiency win here: the target characteristic function `exp(-t^2/2)` is real and even, and the squared modulus of the discrepancy is even in `t` for a real-valued projection, so I only need to compute the positive half of the grid and double its weight — `t` from `0` to `t_max`, half-weighting the endpoints `t = 0` and `t = t_max`. That doubles my effective resolution for free. And I do not need complex arithmetic at all: `|phi_hat(t) - exp(-t^2/2)|^2 = (Re phi_hat - exp(-t^2/2))^2 + (Im phi_hat)^2`, and `Re phi_hat = mean of cos(t X)`, `Im phi_hat = mean of sin(t X)` over the batch, each a plain average I can all-reduce across devices.

Let me assemble it. The regularizer holds a fixed grid of positive frequency knots `t`, the Gaussian window values `exp(-t^2/2)` (which serve double duty as both the target characteristic function `phi` and the weight `w`), and trapezoidal weights with the symmetry-doubling and half-weighted endpoints folded in. On each forward pass I draw a fresh matrix of random Gaussian directions seeded by the synchronized step counter and normalize its columns to unit norm; I project the batch onto those directions; I form the empirical characteristic function of each projection from the cosine and sine means across the batch (all-reduced across devices); I take the Gaussian-weighted squared discrepancy from the target, integrate over `t` by the precomputed weights, scale by the batch size, and average over directions. That is the anti-collapse penalty. The full regularizer adds it, with a trade-off coefficient, to the invariance term — the mean-squared distance between the two views' embeddings, which is the predictability half of the objective. One coefficient `lambda` (or equivalently a weight on the Gaussianity term) balances prediction against Gaussianity, and that is the only hyperparameter.

```python
import torch
import torch.nn as nn
import torch.nn.functional as F


def all_reduce_mean(x):
    """Average across data-parallel processes (identity on a single device).
    The empirical characteristic function is a per-batch average, so summing the
    per-device means and dividing by world size gives the global ECF."""
    import torch.distributed as dist
    if dist.is_available() and dist.is_initialized():
        dist.all_reduce(x, op=dist.ReduceOp.SUM)
        x = x / dist.get_world_size()
    return x


class CustomRegularizer(nn.Module):
    """SIGReg: sketch a 1-D Epps-Pulley Gaussianity test along random directions.

    Slicing is legitimate by the hyperspherical Cramer-Wold theorem: matching every
    unit-direction projection to N(0,1) is equivalent to the full embeddings being
    isotropic Gaussian. Epps-Pulley (characteristic-function) is chosen for bounded
    gradient/curvature and O(N) DDP-friendly cost; we average over directions (not
    max) for dense gradients, and resample directions every step for sphere coverage.
    """

    def __init__(self, num_slices=256, lmbd=10.0, n_knots=17, t_max=3.0, sigma=1.0):
        super().__init__()
        self.num_slices = num_slices
        self.lmbd = lmbd
        self.sigma = sigma
        self.register_buffer("step", torch.zeros((), dtype=torch.long))
        # positive half of the frequency grid; symmetry of |phi_hat - phi|^2 lets us
        # integrate [0, t_max] with doubled weights instead of [-t_max, t_max]
        t = torch.linspace(0.0, t_max, n_knots)
        dt = t_max / (n_knots - 1)
        weights = torch.full((n_knots,), 2 * dt)
        weights[[0, -1]] = dt                               # trapezoid: half-weight endpoints
        window = torch.exp(-(t ** 2) / (2 * sigma ** 2))    # CF of N(0,1) AND Gaussian weight
        self.register_buffer("t", t)
        self.register_buffer("phi", window)                 # target CF exp(-t^2/2)
        self.register_buffer("weights", weights * window)   # quadrature weight * window

    def _epps_pulley(self, proj):
        # proj: [B, M] batch projected onto M directions
        B = proj.size(0)
        x_t = proj.unsqueeze(-1) * self.t                   # [B, M, n_knots]
        cos_mean = x_t.cos().mean(0)                        # Re phi_hat(t), [M, n_knots]
        sin_mean = x_t.sin().mean(0)                        # Im phi_hat(t), [M, n_knots]
        cos_mean = all_reduce_mean(cos_mean)                # global ECF across devices
        sin_mean = all_reduce_mean(sin_mean)
        err = (cos_mean - self.phi).square() + sin_mean.square()   # |phi_hat - phi|^2
        N = B * (torch.distributed.get_world_size()
                 if torch.distributed.is_initialized() else 1)
        return (err @ self.weights) * N                    # trapezoid integral * N, [M]

    def _sigreg(self, z):
        # fresh unit directions, seeded by a synchronized step so all GPUs agree
        with torch.no_grad():
            step = self.step.clone()
            if torch.distributed.is_available() and torch.distributed.is_initialized():
                torch.distributed.all_reduce(step, op=torch.distributed.ReduceOp.MAX)
            g = torch.Generator(device=z.device)
            g.manual_seed(int(step.item()))
            A = torch.randn(z.size(1), self.num_slices, device=z.device, generator=g)
            A = A / A.norm(p=2, dim=0)                      # unit-norm directions
        return self._epps_pulley(z @ A).mean()             # average over directions

    def forward(self, z1, z2):
        sigreg = 0.5 * (self._sigreg(z1) + self._sigreg(z2))   # Gaussianity on each view
        invariance_loss = F.mse_loss(z1, z2)                   # predictability term
        self.step += 1                                          # advance the seed
        total_loss = invariance_loss + self.lmbd * sigreg
        return {"loss": total_loss, "sigreg": sigreg, "invariance_loss": invariance_loss}
```

The chain that got me here: the bare predictability objective collapses, so I need an anti-collapse term, and instead of patching symptoms I first asked what distribution the embeddings should have. Linear-probe bias and variance forced isotropy; the nonlinear-probe bias depends on the Fisher information of the embedding density, which the Cramér–Rao bound shows is minimized at fixed covariance uniquely by the Gaussian, and minimizing `tr(Sigma^{-1})` under a scalar constraint forces that Gaussian to be isotropic — so the target is `N(0, I)`. Enforcing a `K`-dimensional distribution directly is `O(N^2)` and unparallelizable, so I reframed it as a hypothesis test and sketched it with the hyperspherical Cramér–Wold theorem: matching every unit-direction projection to `N(0, 1)` is equivalent to the full law being isotropic Gaussian, turning one hard multivariate test into many cheap univariate ones, averaged (not maxed) over directions for dense gradients. Among univariate tests, moments are non-identifiable for finite order and explode in gradient for high order; CDF tests are identifiable but need sorting, which is non-differentiable and breaks data-parallel training; the characteristic-function (Epps–Pulley) test is a differentiable, parallelizable average that is identifiable, and it provably has gradient bounded by `4 sigma^2/N` and bounded curvature regardless of the input distribution — the property that makes it stable at scale. A few resampled directions should suffice to the extent the discrepancy function on the sphere is smooth, an `|A|^{-2 alpha/(K-1)}` rate I would want to confirm empirically by sweeping `|A|`, and in any case resampling accumulates coverage over training; the minibatch gradient bias is an explicit, vanishing `O(1/N)` that I checked matches Monte-Carlo at batch size sixteen; and the frequency integral collapses to a seventeen-knot trapezoidal quadrature on the positive half-grid using cosine and sine means. Added to the invariance term with a single coefficient, this is the whole regularizer.
