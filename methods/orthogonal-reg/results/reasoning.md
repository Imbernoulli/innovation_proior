Let me start from what actually goes wrong when I train a deep convolutional net. A signal enters at the bottom and gets multiplied by one layer's weights, then a nonlinearity, then the next layer's weights, and so on up to the logits. A gradient does the same thing in reverse: the error at the top gets multiplied by the transposed weight matrices on its way back down to the early layers. So both the forward activation and the backward gradient are, at heart, a vector being hit by a long product of matrices. And a product of matrices does to a vector roughly what a product of numbers does to a scalar — if the typical multiplicative factor is below one, the vector shrinks geometrically with depth and I get vanishing gradients in the early layers; if it's above one, the vector grows geometrically and I get the explosion. This is the thing that makes deep and recurrent nets hard to train, and it's worth being precise about what "the typical factor" even means, because that's where the lever is.

Pascanu, Mikolov and Bengio nailed the mechanism on recurrent nets, which is the cleanest case because an RNN is just a feedforward net with the same matrix tied across every layer when you unroll it in time. Write the gradient of a loss at step t with respect to a much earlier state at step k. By the chain rule it factors as a product of per-step Jacobians, and each Jacobian is the recurrent weight matrix transposed times a diagonal matrix of activation-function derivatives:

  d x_t / d x_k = prod_{t >= i > k} W_rec^T diag(sigma'(x_{i-1})).

Now take the 2-norm. The norm of a product is at most the product of the norms, so

  || d x_t / d x_k || <= prod || W_rec^T || · || diag(sigma') ||.

The diagonal factor is bounded: || diag(sigma') || <= gamma where gamma = sup |sigma'|, which is 1 for tanh and 1/4 for the logistic sigmoid. And || W_rec^T || is the spectral norm of W_rec. So if ||W_rec||_2 is below 1/gamma, every factor in this uniform bound is below one, the product decays geometrically in t - k, and long-range gradients vanish — that's a sufficient contraction condition. I have to be careful not to reverse a one-sided bound into a proof of explosion. In the linear and bounded-nonlinearity analysis, a recurrent spectral radius above the corresponding threshold is necessary for exploding gradients, but not sufficient by itself; the dynamics also have to align so that products actually grow along some direction. The thing controlling the whole catastrophe is still spectral: singular values, operator norms, and recurrent eigenvalues decide whether long products contract, stay balanced, or have room to blow up. Their fix for the explosion is to clip the gradient when its norm gets too big, and for the vanishing side they float a soft penalty that tries to keep the backpropagated signal's norm from collapsing. But clipping is a band-aid applied to the gradient after the matrix has already done the damage, and it does nothing on the vanishing side, and neither piece directly touches the weights themselves. Still, the diagnosis is gold: I want the relevant singular values of my weight matrices to stay near one.

So what makes a square matrix's singular values all exactly one? A square W with that property is exactly norm-preserving — || W x || = || x || for every x, because the singular values are precisely the factors by which W stretches the orthonormal singular directions, and if they're all one, no direction is stretched or shrunk. For a square matrix that's exactly the orthogonal matrices, and there are two equivalent ways to write the condition: W^T W = I, which says the columns are orthonormal, or W W^T = I, which says the rows are orthonormal. For a rectangular matrix I can only ask for semi-orthogonality on the feasible side: all nonzero singular values can be one, but the longer side leaves null directions. Hold that thought, because for a conv layer the two Gram conditions will not be interchangeable.

Can I just start training there? Saxe, McClelland and Ganguli worked out exactly why this is the right configuration and exactly how to enter it. The naive idea, from Glorot and Bengio, is to scale random Gaussian weights so that norms are preserved on average — draw each entry of an N-by-N matrix i.i.d. zero-mean Gaussian with standard deviation 1/sqrt(N), and then the expected value of v^T W^T W v equals v^T v, so on average the matrix preserves norm. I almost want to stop there, it sounds like it solves it. But "on average" is doing a lot of quiet work and it's the wrong thing. The catch is that norm preservation in expectation is not a flat spectrum. The squared singular values of a scaled Gaussian matrix follow the Marchenko-Pastur law, which has a real spread that does not go away even as N goes to infinity — some singular values are well above one, some well below. And it compounds with depth: a product of many such Gaussian matrices develops a wildly kurtotic singular spectrum, where most of the singular values collapse toward zero and a thin tail blows up. Such a product still preserves the norm of a typical random vector — but it does it anisotropically, by amplifying a handful of directions enormously and crushing all the rest to nothing. Picture what that does to a backpropagated error: any component of the error lying in the crushed subspace is annihilated, so the early layers get essentially no gradient in those directions. Norm-preserving on average, useless for learning. That's the trap.

An orthogonal matrix has none of that spread — every singular value is exactly one, no distribution, no tail. And the beautiful part is that it survives composition: a product of orthogonal matrices is itself orthogonal, so a deep stack of orthogonal layers has an end-to-end map with all singular values still exactly one, no matter how deep. Saxe et al. call that dynamical isometry, and they show it empirically — initialize every layer to a random orthogonal matrix (take the Q from a QR decomposition of a random Gaussian) and learning time stops growing with depth, matching greedy pretraining, where scaled Gaussian init keeps slowing down as you add layers. And it carries into nonlinear nets sitting just past the "edge of chaos," where the gain is tuned so the linear amplification just balances the saturating nonlinearity and the Jacobian still acts as a near-isometry. So orthogonality is not a heuristic; it's the thing the propagation analysis is pointing at.

So I'll initialize orthogonal and I'm done — wait, no. Orthogonal initialization is a condition on the weights at step zero, and nothing holds it there. The instant the data gradient takes its first step, it pulls W off the orthogonal manifold in whatever direction reduces the task loss, with zero regard for the singular value spectrum. By mid-training the matrices have drifted, the spectrum has spread back out, and the flat-spectrum property I paid for at step zero is gone exactly during the long stretch where most of the weight movement happens. A good initial condition does not pin the weights. I need a force that keeps acting throughout training, not a one-time placement.

What kind of force? The obvious heavy-handed answer is to make orthogonality a hard constraint — confine each weight matrix to the feasible Stiefel side, {W : W^T W = I} or {W : W W^T = I}, and do constrained optimization on it, either with Riemannian gradients that move along the manifold, or by re-orthogonalizing after every ordinary step with a fresh QR or SVD that projects W back onto the manifold. That keeps the selected spectrum exactly flat at every iteration, which is more than I even need. But the cost is brutal: a manifold step or an explicit matrix factorization of every weight matrix on every single iteration, on a GPU, and the factorization cost grows badly with matrix size. I'm doing this every step of a long training run over dozens of layers. That's a non-starter for the inner loop. And there's a second problem that's specific to my setting and that I have to confront head-on: convolutional weights are not square.

Let me look at what a conv weight even is as a matrix. A conv layer's weight tensor has shape [out_channels, in_channels, k, k]. To talk about its spectrum I have to flatten it to a 2D matrix, and the natural flattening keeps the output channels as rows and collapses everything else into columns: W in R^{out_channels × (in_channels · k · k)}. Call those dimensions m × n. This is almost never square — often the fan-in in·k·k is larger than out_channels, so the matrix is short and wide. Now go back to my two equivalent orthogonality conditions and watch them stop being equivalent. W^T W = I asks the n columns to be orthonormal vectors in R^m; but you can't have n orthonormal vectors living in an m-dimensional space when n > m — there's only room for m of them. So W^T W = I is feasible only when m >= n, the undercomplete-columns case. The other condition, W W^T = I, asks the m rows — which are exactly the m filters, one per output channel — to be orthonormal vectors in R^n; that's feasible when n >= m, which is the usual fat-conv-weight case. So for a wide conv weight, the achievable, sensible thing is to make the output-channel filters mutually orthonormal: W W^T = I. That also reads beautifully as a goal in its own right — it says no two filters point in the same direction, the filters are decorrelated, none of them is a wasted redundant copy of another, and each has unit norm so none has collapsed. The hard square constraint glossed over this; the rectangular reality forces me to pick the feasible Gram and gives me a clean interpretation for free.

A hard constraint is too expensive and clumsy for rectangular weights. But I don't actually need W to be exactly orthogonal at every step — I just need it to stay close to orthogonal so the spectrum stays roughly flat. "Close to" is the language of a penalty, not a constraint. So instead of confining W to the manifold, I'll add a term to the loss that grows as W gets farther from orthogonal, hand it to the optimizer I'm already running, and let the data loss and this penalty negotiate. No factorization in the inner loop, fully differentiable, and it keeps acting at every step instead of just at step zero — exactly the standing force I was missing.

I need to turn "how far is W from orthogonal" into a differentiable scalar. The orthogonality conditions are W W^T = I (or W^T W = I), so the deviation is just the residual of the Gram matrix from the identity:

  R = W W^T - I.

This is the right thing to measure for several reasons I should make explicit rather than just assert. R is zero exactly when the selected row Gram is orthonormal, so a penalty on R has its minimum exactly where I want it when that side is feasible. R measures the deviation in the natural inner-product geometry — the (i, j) entry of W W^T is the dot product of filter i and filter j, so the off-diagonal entries of R are exactly the pairwise filter correlations I want driven to zero, and the diagonal entries are || filter_i ||^2 - 1, exactly the magnitude-collapse I want prevented. So one matrix R simultaneously encodes "make the filters mutually orthogonal" (off-diagonals) and "keep each filter at unit norm" (diagonal). Compare the obvious alternatives and they all miss. Penalizing || W || pulls every singular value down toward zero — that's plain L2 weight decay, and it's the opposite of holding the nonzero spectrum at one; it controls scale, not shape. Penalizing something with a determinant is non-convex and degenerate for rectangular W. The Gram-from-identity residual is the quantity that is zero iff the selected filters are orthonormal, which is the feasible version of the flat-spectrum property the propagation analysis told me to want. So I'll penalize the size of R.

Now, the size of R measured how? The leanest version is the entrywise absolute residual:

  L_ortho = sum_{i,j} |R_{ij}| = sum_{i,j} |(W W^T - I)_{ij}|.

That says exactly what I mean: every dot-product error and every unit-norm error contributes by its magnitude. It is not differentiable at zero, but it has a perfectly usable subgradient, and an optimizer can backpropagate through the matrix multiplications without any factorization. If I want a smooth variant with a clean closed-form gradient, I square the Frobenius norm instead:

  L_smooth = || W W^T - I ||_F^2 = sum_{i,j} R_{ij}^2 = tr(R^T R).

Squaring is deliberate in the smooth variant. The Frobenius norm itself has a square root and is non-differentiable at zero; squaring removes the root, making the penalty a smooth polynomial in W and quadratic in the residual, which behaves nicely under gradient descent and grows fast when a residual entry gets large, pushing hardest exactly on the worst-off filters.

Let me actually differentiate it, because I'm going to hand the gradient to the optimizer and I want to be sure it does what I think and doesn't cost a factorization. R is symmetric here (R = W W^T - I and (W W^T)^T = W W^T), so L = tr(R^T R) = tr(R^2). Differentiate L with respect to W. Take the differential: dL = 2 tr(R · dR), and dR = d(W W^T) = (dW) W^T + W (dW)^T. So

  dL = 2 tr(R (dW) W^T) + 2 tr(R W (dW)^T).

I could push this through with the trace identities — cyclic permutation plus R symmetric — but I keep second-guessing whether I've matched each dW with the right neighbour and dropped a transpose, so let me just do it entrywise where there's nowhere to hide. L = sum_{i,j} (sum_a W_{ia} W_{ja} - delta_{ij})^2. Then

  dL/dW_{pq} = sum_{i,j} 2 R_{ij} · d/dW_{pq}( sum_a W_{ia} W_{ja} ).

The inner derivative: d/dW_{pq} (sum_a W_{ia} W_{ja}) = delta_{ip} W_{jq} + delta_{jp} W_{iq}. Plug in:

  dL/dW_{pq} = 2 sum_{i,j} R_{ij} (delta_{ip} W_{jq} + delta_{jp} W_{iq})
             = 2 sum_j R_{pj} W_{jq} + 2 sum_i R_{ip} W_{iq}.

The first sum is 2 (R W)_{pq}; the second, using R symmetric (R_{ip} = R_{pi}), is 2 (R W)_{pq} again. So

  dL/dW = 4 R W = 4 (W W^T - I) W.

Clean. No SVD, no eigendecomposition — the squared penalty's gradient is two matrix multiplies, R = W W^T - I and then R W, both cheap relative to the conv itself, and autodiff will compute exactly this for me when I just write the forward penalty. (As a sanity check the W^T W form gives the mirror result: d/dW || W^T W - I ||_F^2 = 4 W (W^T W - I), the same shape with the residual on the other side.) For the absolute-value form, I replace R in the same chain rule with a subgradient S = sign(R), giving 2 S W when S is symmetric, with the usual freedom to choose any subgradient at zero. So either soft penalty costs essentially nothing per step, which was the entire reason to prefer it over the hard constraint. The wall that killed Stiefel optimization — a factorization every step — is gone; I traded an exact projection for a cheap gradient nudge toward the manifold, which is all I need to keep the spectrum from drifting.

There's a subtlety I should pin down about what this penalty can and cannot achieve, because it tells me which Gram orientation to use and keeps me honest about the rectangular case. If I'd penalized W^T W - I on a fat W (n > m), the target W^T W = I is simply unreachable — W^T W is an n×n matrix of rank at most m < n, so it can never equal the full-rank identity, and I'd be minimizing toward a floor I can't hit, a biased objective. With the W W^T - I orientation on a fat W, the target W W^T = I is an m×m identity and W has rank up to m, so it's reachable: the m filters genuinely can be made orthonormal. So the right reading is — penalize the Gram that lives on the smaller dimension, the one whose identity is attainable. For the standard wide conv weight that's W W^T over the output channels, which is also the orientation with the nice "decorrelate the filters, keep them unit-norm" meaning. If a layer were the other shape (more output channels than fan-in), the feasible one flips to W^T W. Either way the recipe is the same: residual of the attainable Gram from identity, using either the absolute residual or the squared Frobenius residual.

How hard should I push? This is a soft encouragement, not a law, so it gets a coefficient lambda in front and added to the data loss:

  L_total = L_CE + lambda · sum_W sum_{i,j} |(W W^T - I)_{ij}|,

or, for the smooth variant,

  L_total = L_CE + lambda · sum_W || W W^T - I ||_F^2,

where the sum runs over every conv filter bank W in the network — each layer's weight matrix penalized independently, since each layer's own propagation matters. lambda has to be small. The data loss is the thing I actually care about; the orthogonality term is there to gently keep the weights near a good manifold while the data loss does the real work. If lambda is too large I'd be optimizing for orthogonal filters at the expense of fitting the data, and a perfectly orthogonal but task-blind filter bank is useless. So lambda is a small number that nudges — something in the range of a fraction of a percent of the loss scale, swept over a few orders of magnitude (1e-3, 1e-1 are reasonable bracketing values to try; very small like 1e-4 or 1e-6 if I want the lightest touch). It coexists with the optimizer's built-in L2 weight decay rather than replacing it: weight decay controls overall magnitude, this controls the shape of the spectrum, and they're pulling on different things — weight decay shrinks all singular values, this flattens the feasible nonzero singular values toward one. The diagonal of R already contains the magnitude term || filter ||^2 - 1, so this penalty even has a mild scale-anchoring effect of its own, but it's complementary to, not a substitute for, weight decay.

Let me think about why this should actually help generalization and not just stabilize gradients, because that's the payoff I'd want to see and I should be able to argue it from what the penalty does rather than from any measurement. Two mechanisms. First, the off-diagonal part: driving filter-filter correlations toward zero means the filters span distinct directions instead of clustering, so the layer isn't wasting capacity on near-duplicate filters — more of the model's nominal parameters do independent work. Second, the diagonal part: pushing || filter ||^2 toward one keeps filter magnitudes from collapsing, so the penalty keeps the weights near a desirable non-zero manifold rather than letting them shrink toward an uninformative origin, which means the available capacity stays usable instead of dying. Both of these are about using the model's capacity fully and keeping the representation well-conditioned, which is a generalization story, not just a gradient-flow story. And it costs almost nothing and changes nothing else — no architecture change, no change to the base loss or optimizer or schedule, just an extra differentiable or subdifferentiable scalar added to the loss every step.

So I'm holding the two ways of measuring R side by side, and they differ only in emphasis. The absolute-value sum grows linearly and pushes uniformly, with a sparse flavor and only a subgradient at zero; it is the bare-minimum statement of the idea, just "sum up the magnitude of how far W W^T is from I." The squared Frobenius norm grows quadratically, so it leans hardest on the largest residual entries and is smooth through zero, with the clean 4 (W W^T - I) W gradient. Either one is the same move: penalize the deviation of the filter Gram matrix from the identity, softly, every step.

Now I can fill the training-loop slot without changing any other machinery. I iterate the convolutional filter banks, reshape each weight tensor to [out_channels, fan_in] so rows are filters, build W W^T, subtract an identity with the same device and dtype, and accumulate the residual. I skip biases, BatchNorm scales, and other one-dimensional parameters because the orthogonality claim is about linear filter banks. Autodiff supplies the sign subgradient for the abs form or the exact 4 (W W^T - I) W gradient for the smooth squared form. The whole thing is the residual computation plus a small coefficient, added to cross-entropy before backpropagation:

```python
import torch


def compute_regularization(model, inputs, outputs, targets, config):
    """Soft orthogonal regularization on convolutional filter banks.

    For each conv weight reshaped to W = [out_channels, in*k*k], penalize the
    deviation of its filter Gram matrix from the identity, summed over layers:
        lambda * sum_W || W W^T - I ||_F^2.
    Pushes the out_channels filters toward mutually orthonormal -> flat (feasible)
    singular spectrum, norm-preserving propagation, decorrelated unit-norm filters.
    No SVD; squared form's gradient is 4*lambda*(W W^T - I) W per layer.
    """
    lam = 1e-4                                     # soft coefficient; complements optimizer weight decay
    reg = torch.zeros((), device=outputs.device)
    for name, p in model.named_parameters():
        if 'weight' in name and p.dim() == 4:      # conv filter banks only (skip bias / BN / 1D params)
            W = p.view(p.size(0), -1)              # [out_channels, in*k*k]: rows = filters
            gram = W @ W.t()                        # W W^T in R^{out x out}: (i,j) = <filter_i, filter_j>
            I = torch.eye(W.size(0), device=W.device, dtype=W.dtype)
            R = gram - I                            # off-diag = filter correlations, diag = ||f||^2 - 1
            reg = reg + (R * R).sum()               # squared Frobenius norm ||W W^T - I||_F^2
    return lam * reg
```

The absolute-value form, the leanest statement of the same idea, just swaps the residual measure:

```python
            reg = reg + R.abs().sum()               # sum |W W^T - I|, the L1 residual size
```

The causal chain, start to finish. Deep and recurrent nets fail because forward activations and backward gradients are repeatedly multiplied by weight matrices, so spectral radii, operator norms, and singular values decide whether signals vanish or have room to explode; Pascanu et al.'s product-of-Jacobians analysis makes that explicit without turning a necessary explosion condition into a sufficient one. A square matrix with all singular values equal to one is exactly norm-preserving, and a rectangular conv filter bank can at least make the feasible side semi-orthogonal, with all nonzero singular values at one. Saxe et al. showed orthogonal weights give dynamical isometry that survives depth, where scaled-Gaussian weights only preserve norm on average and develop a kurtotic, anisotropic spectrum that crushes gradients. Orthogonal initialization buys this at step zero but the data gradient drags the weights off the manifold, so it erodes through training; a hard Stiefel constraint would hold it exactly but costs a factorization every step and sits awkwardly on rectangular conv weights. So instead I measure deviation from orthogonality as the residual of the attainable filter Gram matrix from the identity, R = W W^T - I — zero iff the selected filters are orthonormal, with off-diagonals encoding filter correlations and the diagonal encoding magnitude collapse — and add either its abs-sum or its squared Frobenius norm to the loss with a small coefficient. The rectangular conv shape forces the W W^T orientation over the output channels when fan_in exceeds out_channels, which doubles as "decorrelate the filters, keep them unit-norm." The squared variant's gradient is 4 (W W^T - I) W, and the abs variant uses the same two-matmul chain with sign(R), so this is a cheap standing force the existing optimizer applies every step — keeping the feasible spectrum near flat throughout training, not just at initialization, while leaving the architecture, base loss, optimizer, and schedule untouched.
