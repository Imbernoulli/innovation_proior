I start with the old adjustment picture and immediately run into its limits. If I only say that I should control for confounders, I have no general way to say which variables count, whether a descendant is useful, or whether conditioning on a collider quietly creates the very association I am trying to remove. I need a language that separates two acts that ordinary conditional probability writes too similarly: seeing that a variable has a value and forcing that variable to have the value.

I therefore treat the causal diagram as a compact description of mechanisms. Each node has a local equation, and the arrows into the node name the other variables whose values its mechanism uses. If I intervene on `X`, I do not merely condition on `X=x`; I replace the equation for `X` with the constant `x`. In the graph, that means I remove the incoming arrows into `X` while leaving the other mechanisms in place. This turns the intervention from a vague instruction into a concrete graph transformation.

Once I have that transformed graph, I can bring back the earlier d-separation machinery. The important step is that I do not ask independence questions in the original graph by habit. I ask them in the graph that corresponds to the current intervention expression. If an expression contains `do(x)`, then the arrows into `X` have already been removed for the purpose of deciding which paths remain active. The probability expression and the graph surgery move together.

At first I try to make this a single adjustment criterion, but the front-door pattern shows that a single criterion is too small. There may be unobserved confounding between treatment and outcome, so no ordinary set of pre-treatment covariates repairs the treatment-outcome comparison. Yet a mediator can still carry the causal signal if the treatment-to-mediator part is clean and the mediator-to-outcome part can be repaired by conditioning on treatment. The method has to support a sequence of transformations, not one all-purpose covariate set.

So I build an algebra over mixed expressions that contain both observations and actions. One kind of move deletes an observation when, after the existing actions are represented by graph surgery, the outcome is d-separated from that observed variable given the acted-on variables and the variables already conditioned on. Another kind of move exchanges an action for an observation when the same separation holds after I also delete outgoing arrows from the acted-on variable. A third kind of move deletes an action only under the more careful test: after deleting arrows into the already acted-on variables, I delete arrows into just those newly acted-on variables that are not ancestors of the conditioning variables in that graph, and then check the same separation. These are not verbal adjustment tips; they are rewrite permissions tied to specific mutilated graphs.

This changes the task from "find the right controls" to "reduce the intervention expression." For a back-door situation, the reduction is short: expand over the admissible covariates, exchange the intervention on treatment for observation within each stratum, and obtain the familiar weighted adjustment formula. For the front-door situation, I first replace the interventional distribution of the mediator by its observed conditional distribution given treatment, then express the effect of setting the mediator by averaging outcome regressions over the natural treatment distribution. The hidden treatment-outcome confounding remains in the graph, but it no longer blocks the entire derivation because the algebra decomposes the target into pieces where different transformed graphs license different moves.

I also see why failure matters. If the graph has a structure such as a direct causal link wrapped by latent confounding in the wrong place, every attempted reduction preserves an intervention marker that cannot be eliminated. That is not a limitation of a chosen regression; it means two structural models can agree on all observed probabilities while disagreeing about the intervention effect. The graph-transform algebra therefore acts both as a derivation system and as a warning system.

The final method is a calculus because each step is local, syntactic, and justified by a graphical separation test. It is causal because the syntax is anchored in structural interventions: actions delete mechanisms, observations restrict distributions, and the graph tells me which distinction matters at each point. The distinctive achievement is that informal adjustment reasoning becomes a disciplined sequence of graph-indexed transformations that either removes every intervention symbol or shows why the observational distribution is insufficient.
