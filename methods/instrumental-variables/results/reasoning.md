I start with the contaminated comparison. I want the effect of changing `D` on `Y`, but the data hand me a `D` that reflects choices, assignment rules, market equilibrium, severity, ability, demand, and other forces that also affect `Y`. In potential-outcome language, the treated and untreated groups can have different untreated outcomes before any treatment effect appears. In regression language, if `Y = alpha + tau D + U`, then the problem is `Cov(D, U) != 0`. A regression of `Y` on `D` uses the very movement in `D` that travels with `U`, so it cannot by itself separate causal response from selection.

I then look for a different source of movement, not because I want another control but because I need a way to split the regressor into useful and contaminated components. The Wright supply-demand example makes the geometry clear. Equilibrium price is not a clean treatment because supply and demand shocks jointly determine price and quantity. If I watch price and quantity move together, I mix demand and supply curves. But if weather, crop conditions, or another supply shifter moves supply while staying outside the demand equation, that movement changes equilibrium price and quantity along demand. The shifter does not solve the problem by being interesting in itself; it solves it by moving the suspect regressor from outside the target equation.

So I introduce an instrument `Z`. I need `Z` to move `D`, and I need `Z` not to move the outcome through any path except the movement in `D`. When I take covariance with `Z` in `Y = alpha + tau D + U`, I get `Cov(Z, Y) = tau Cov(Z, D) + Cov(Z, U)`. If the design can drive the last term to zero while keeping `Cov(Z, D)` away from zero, then the slope falls out as `tau = Cov(Z, Y) / Cov(Z, D)`. I want to understand what this ratio is made of before I trust it. The numerator is the reduced-form movement in the outcome when the external source varies. The denominator is the first-stage movement in the contaminated regressor when that same source varies. Dividing asks how much the outcome moves per unit of the regressor movement that comes from the external source.

With a binary instrument, the same object reads as a difference ratio: `{E[Y | Z = 1] - E[Y | Z = 0]} / {E[D | Z = 1] - E[D | Z = 0]}`. The Angrist-Krueger compulsory-schooling example fits this form. Quarter of birth changes school starting age and compulsory-attendance exposure, which changes schooling for some people near the dropout margin. The outcome contrast by quarter of birth is not the schooling effect yet, because quarter of birth mostly changes earnings through the small schooling difference it induces. The treatment contrast rescales the outcome contrast by the amount of schooling that actually changes.

I now have to be careful about what effect this ratio names, and I do not want to take the constant-effect case on faith. Suppose effects differ across units, and split the population by how `D` responds to `Z`. Always-takers receive treatment regardless of `Z`; never-takers do not receive treatment regardless of `Z`; compliers take treatment only when `Z = 1`; defiers do the opposite. Let me put numbers on a population with no defiers and check what the binary ratio actually returns. Take fractions 50% never-takers, 30% compliers, 20% always-takers. Give the types untreated outcomes `Y0 = 1, 2, 5` and individual treatment effects `b = 10, 3, 7` respectively, so that always-takers are positively selected (`Y0 = 5`) and the never-takers happen to have the largest effect. Now compute both moments. Under `Z = 0`, only always-takers are treated, so `E[D | Z = 0] = 0.2` and `E[Y | Z = 0] = 0.5(1) + 0.3(2) + 0.2(5 + 7) = 0.5 + 0.6 + 2.4 = 3.5`. Under `Z = 1`, compliers switch on, so `E[D | Z = 1] = 0.5` and `E[Y | Z = 1] = 0.5(1) + 0.3(2 + 3) + 0.2(5 + 7) = 0.5 + 1.5 + 2.4 = 4.4`. The first-stage denominator is `0.5 - 0.2 = 0.3`, exactly the complier mass. The reduced-form numerator is `4.4 - 3.5 = 0.9`. The ratio is `0.9 / 0.3 = 3.0`. That is the compliers' effect, `b = 3`, and nothing else: the never-takers' large effect of 10 never enters because they are untreated under both states, and the always-takers' selection and their effect of 7 cancel out of the difference because their `D` does not move. So the ratio is not the average effect for everyone. It is the average effect over exactly the units whose treatment status the instrument changes.

That cancellation depended on there being no defiers, so I should test what defiers do rather than assume monotonicity away. Take the starkest case: 50% compliers (`D = Z`) and 50% defiers (`D = 1 - Z`), with effects `b = 3` and `b = 9`. Now `E[D | Z = 1] = 0.5(1) + 0.5(0) = 0.5` and `E[D | Z = 0] = 0.5(0) + 0.5(1) = 0.5`, so the first-stage denominator is `0.5 - 0.5 = 0`. The compliers' upward movement and the defiers' downward movement cancel, and the ratio is undefined. Even keeping the denominator nonzero by unbalancing the masses, the numerator carries the structure `f_c b_c - f_d b_d`: a difference of effects with opposing signs, so it can be negative even when every individual effect is positive (here it evaluates to `0.5(3) - 0.5(9) = -3`). This is the concrete reason monotonicity is not cosmetic. If I rule out defiers, so the instrument never pushes one unit toward treatment while pushing another away, the first stage counts the compliers and the reduced form sums their effects with positive weight, and the ratio is the local average treatment effect for compliers. Without that restriction the denominator can collapse and the weights can go negative.

This local estimand is what the design actually delivers; calling it the average effect for everyone would require assumptions I have not made. A draft lottery identifies the effect for people whose service status changes because of the lottery. A school-entry or compulsory-attendance rule identifies the effect for people whose schooling changes because of the rule. A randomized encouragement identifies the effect for people whose take-up changes because of the encouragement. I can rename it the population average only if I add constant effects or some other restriction that ties the compliers to the rest.

I then add controls by preserving the same source-of-movement logic. If `W` contains predetermined covariates, I remove the linear part explained by `W` from `Y`, `D`, and `Z`, then use the residualized instrument to isolate residualized treatment movement. The scalar estimator becomes `tau_hat = (z_r' y_r) / (z_r' d_r)`. This is the controlled version of the same reduced-form-over-first-stage argument; it does not turn the whole treatment into clean variation.

For multiple instruments or a full linear regression, I want to express the same operation as projection, but I should confirm the projection form does not silently change the estimand in the case I already understand. Let `X = [W, D]` contain the included exogenous controls and the endogenous treatment, and let `Z = [W, Z_ex]` contain the controls plus excluded instruments. The projection `P_Z = Z (Z'Z)^(-1) Z'` keeps the part of the regressors that lives in the exogenous instrument space, and the candidate coefficient is `beta_hat = (X' P_Z X)^(-1) X' P_Z Y`. To check it agrees with the ratio, I simulate `n = 2000` units with a single binary instrument `Z`, an endogenous `D = 0.4 Z + 0.5 U + noise` that loads on the disturbance `U`, and an outcome `Y = 1 + 2 D + U` with true slope `2`. OLS of `Y` on `D` returns a slope near `3.33`, pulled above the truth by the `U` channel, which is the bias I expected. The demeaned Wald ratio `(z_r' y_r)/(z_r' d_r)` returns `1.851`. The projection coefficient `beta_hat` returns `1.851` in its treatment slot, matching the Wald ratio to numerical precision. So in the just-identified single-instrument case the projection is the Wald estimator written in matrix form; it is the computational vehicle, not a different object. With more instruments than endogenous regressors it generalizes the same idea by using the best linear combination of the instruments, but it is still only the externally induced movement in `D` that carries the comparison.

I still need to audit two failure modes that the algebra cannot settle on its own. First, exclusion is a design claim, not something the formula proves. If the instrument changes the outcome directly, or changes another omitted cause of the outcome, then `Cov(Z, U)` is not zero, and the term I assumed away reappears inside the ratio as a direct path I would misread as treatment effect. Second, relevance must be strong in practice. The denominator is the first stage; when it is small, the small-denominator estimate is unstable and finite-sample IV can be pulled toward the very OLS association it was meant to escape, with conventional inference understating the uncertainty. So I treat the first-stage relationship, the reduced form, the exclusion argument, and the complier population as part of the method itself, not as reporting afterthoughts.

The method that survives all of this is concrete. I do not estimate the effect by comparing all units with high and low `D`. I find an external source `Z` that changes `D`, defend why `Z` is as-if random and excluded from the outcome equation, check that it changes `D` strongly enough, and use the outcome movement associated with `Z` divided by the treatment movement associated with `Z`. With heterogeneous effects I report the effect for the units whose treatment status the instrument changes, having seen those are exactly the units the ratio weights; with controls and multiple instruments I implement the same idea through the two-stage projection, having checked it reproduces the ratio in the case I can compute by hand.
