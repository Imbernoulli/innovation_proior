I start with the contaminated comparison. I want the effect of changing `D` on `Y`, but the data hand me a `D` that reflects choices, assignment rules, market equilibrium, severity, ability, demand, and other forces that also affect `Y`. In potential-outcome language, the treated and untreated groups can have different untreated outcomes before any treatment effect appears. In regression language, if `Y = alpha + tau D + U`, then the problem is `Cov(D, U) != 0`. A regression of `Y` on `D` uses the very movement in `D` that travels with `U`, so it cannot by itself separate causal response from selection.

I then look for a different source of movement, not because I want another control but because I need a way to split the regressor into useful and contaminated components. The Wright supply-demand example makes the geometry clear. Equilibrium price is not a clean treatment because supply and demand shocks jointly determine price and quantity. If I watch price and quantity move together, I mix demand and supply curves. But if weather, crop conditions, or another supply shifter moves supply while staying outside the demand equation, that movement changes equilibrium price and quantity along demand. The shifter does not solve the problem by being interesting in itself; it solves it by moving the suspect regressor from outside the target equation.

So I introduce an instrument `Z`. I need `Z` to move `D`, and I need `Z` not to move the outcome through any path except the movement in `D`. When I take covariance with `Z` in `Y = alpha + tau D + U`, I get `Cov(Z, Y) = tau Cov(Z, D) + Cov(Z, U)`. The design is trying to make the last term zero while keeping `Cov(Z, D)` away from zero. Then the slope is visible as `tau = Cov(Z, Y) / Cov(Z, D)`. This ratio is not a mechanical trick. The numerator is the reduced-form movement in the outcome when the external source varies. The denominator is the first-stage movement in the contaminated regressor when that same source varies. Dividing asks how much the outcome moves per unit of the regressor movement that comes from the external source.

With a binary instrument, I read the same object as a difference ratio: `{E[Y | Z = 1] - E[Y | Z = 0]} / {E[D | Z = 1] - E[D | Z = 0]}`. The Angrist-Krueger compulsory-schooling example fits this form. Quarter of birth changes school starting age and compulsory-attendance exposure, which changes schooling for some people near the dropout margin. The outcome contrast by quarter of birth is not the schooling effect yet, because quarter of birth mostly changes earnings through the small schooling difference it induces. The treatment contrast rescales the outcome contrast by the amount of schooling that actually changes.

I now have to be careful about what effect this ratio names. If every unit has the same treatment effect, the ratio can be the common causal slope. But Angrist and Imbens show that constant effects are not the interesting general case. When effects differ, the instrument can leave some units unchanged and move others. Always-takers receive treatment regardless of `Z`; never-takers do not receive treatment regardless of `Z`; compliers move with `Z`; defiers move against it. If I allow both compliers and defiers, the first-stage denominator can hide offsetting movements and the reduced form can combine effects with negative weights. If I impose monotonicity, so the instrument does not push some units one way and other units the opposite way, the first stage counts the people whose treatment changes and the reduced form counts their average causal effect times that count. The ratio identifies the local average treatment effect for those moved by the instrument.

This local interpretation is not a fallback after the algebra fails; it is the honest estimand created by the design. A draft lottery identifies the effect for people whose service status changes because of the lottery. A school-entry or compulsory-attendance rule identifies the effect for people whose schooling changes because of the rule. A randomized encouragement identifies the effect for people whose take-up changes because of the encouragement. I cannot silently rename that effect as the average effect for everyone unless I add stronger assumptions.

I then add controls by preserving the same source-of-movement logic. If `W` contains predetermined covariates, I remove the linear part explained by `W` from `Y`, `D`, and `Z`, then use the residualized instrument to isolate residualized treatment movement. The scalar estimator becomes `tau_hat = (z_r' y_r) / (z_r' d_r)`. This is the controlled version of the same reduced-form-over-first-stage argument; it does not turn the whole treatment into clean variation.

For multiple instruments or a full linear regression, I express the same operation as projection. Let `X = [W, D]` contain the included exogenous controls and the endogenous treatment, and let `Z = [W, Z_ex]` contain the controls plus excluded instruments. The projection `P_Z = Z (Z'Z)^(-1) Z'` keeps the part of the regressors that lives in the exogenous instrument space, and the coefficient is `beta_hat = (X' P_Z X)^(-1) X' P_Z Y`. This is why two-stage least squares is the computational form, but it is not the conceptual discovery. The discovery is that only externally induced movement in the contaminated regressor is allowed to carry the causal comparison.

I still need to audit two failure modes. First, exclusion is a design claim, not something the formula proves. If the instrument changes the outcome directly, or changes another omitted cause of the outcome, then `Cov(Z, U)` is not zero and the ratio imports that direct path. Second, relevance must be strong in practice. Staiger and Stock show why a weak first stage is not a small technical inconvenience: when the denominator is small, finite-sample IV estimates can be biased toward the confounded OLS association and conventional inference can be misleading. I therefore treat the first-stage relationship, the reduced form, the exclusion argument, and the complier population as part of the method itself.

The reconstructed method is now precise. I do not estimate the effect by comparing all units with high and low `D`. I find an external source `Z` that changes `D`, defend why `Z` is as-if random and excluded from the outcome equation, check that it changes `D` strongly enough, and use the outcome movement associated with `Z` divided by the treatment movement associated with `Z`. With heterogeneous effects, I report the effect for the units whose treatment status the instrument changes; with controls and multiple instruments, I implement the same idea through the two-stage projection.
