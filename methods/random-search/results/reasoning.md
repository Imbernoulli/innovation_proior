Let me start from what actually hurts. I have a learning algorithm with a fistful of knobs — the learning rate, its annealing schedule, the number of hidden units, the weight-initialization scheme and its scale, the nonlinearity, the regularization strength, the preprocessing choice — and the model only becomes a concrete predictor once I fix all of them. Call the knob vector lambda. What I want is the lambda that minimizes generalization error, E_x[L(x; A_lambda(X_train))], but I cannot see the expectation over the true data distribution, so I do the only honest thing and replace it by the mean loss on a held-out validation set. That gives me a response function Psi(lambda) = mean over X_valid of the loss, and the whole job collapses to: minimize Psi(lambda) over the configuration space Lambda. The trouble is that Psi is about the worst optimization target I can imagine. Evaluating it once means training an entire model and scoring it — hours of compute per point. I get no gradient of Psi with respect to lambda; there is no analytic form; I do not know its shape, where its optimum sits, or even which coordinates of lambda it cares about. And lambda is a mongrel: some knobs are continuous and span orders of magnitude (learning rate from 0.001 to 10), some are integers (hidden units, layers), some are categorical (sigmoid vs tanh, which preprocessing). So the real question is narrow and concrete: out of a budget of a few dozen to a few hundred evaluations, which set of trial points {lambda^(1), ..., lambda^(S)} should I evaluate Psi at?

What does everyone actually do today? Grid search, usually salted with manual tinkering. Pick a handful of values L^(k) for each of the K knobs and evaluate every combination — the Cartesian product, S = prod_k |L^(k)| trials — then return the best. I understand exactly why this is the default and not some clever global optimizer. It is trivial to write; it needs no infrastructure beyond a list of jobs and a cluster to run them on; every trial is known up front so parallelism is free; and in one or two dimensions it is perfectly reliable, a clean sweep of the box. But stare at that product. S = prod_k |L^(k)| is exponential in K. If I want even four values on each of, say, eight knobs, that is 4^8 = 65536 trainings; for a deep belief network with thirty-two hyper-parameters, even two values per knob is 2^32, over a billion trainings of hours each. Bellman's curse, in its most literal form: the number of joint configurations explodes with the number of knobs. So a grid fine enough to actually resolve the knobs that matter is simply unaffordable past a handful of dimensions. That is the wall, and it is not a tooling problem, it is arithmetic.

The other thing people do is manual search — a human sitting there, fixing the architecture, hand-tuning the learning rate, looking at the validation curve, adjusting, coordinate-descent by intuition. And I have to be fair to it, because it is adaptive: every trial the human runs informs the next one, so a skilled operator can wander to a good configuration in surprisingly few trials even in a thirty-two-dimensional space — the DBN tuning that this is being compared against averaged only about forty-one trials per data set. That is genuinely good. But it is irreproducible — nobody can rerun "what the expert was thinking" — it does not scale, it depends entirely on operator skill, and as a *baseline* it is useless precisely because the human is in the loop and there is nothing to analyze. I want something with the simplicity and reproducibility of grid but without grid's exponential blowup.

So let me think harder about *why* grid wastes so much. The exponential cost is real, but maybe there is something deeper than "too many points." Let me actually look at what a grid does to a single knob. Suppose I have a budget of N trials and I lay them on a grid with g levels per dimension over K dimensions, so N = g^K. Now ask: how many *distinct* values of the first knob does my experiment actually try? On the grid, every level of knob 1 is paired with every combination of the other knobs, so the first coordinate only takes g distinct values — and g = N^{1/K}. With N = 64 trials in K = 3 dimensions, that is g = 4 distinct values per knob. In K = 6 dimensions with N = 64 it is g = 2. Six dimensions, sixty-four expensive trainings, and I have probed each individual knob at only *two* settings. Project all 64 grid points down onto any single axis and they collapse onto just two stacks of 32 coincident points. That is the hidden waste: the grid is spending its budget on the *combinations*, and the combinations are mostly redundant from the point of view of any one coordinate.

Why does that redundancy matter so much? Here is the fact about these response functions that turns it from "wasteful" into "serious." When I fit a Gaussian process to samples of Psi — squared-exponential kernel, exp(-(a-b)^2/l), one length-scale l per knob, chosen by maximizing the marginal likelihood, with relevance read off as 1/l — and look at the relevances across the seven neural-net knobs and across several related data sets, I see two things, reproducibly. First, on any one data set, only a small handful of knobs actually move Psi; the effective dimension is something like one to four inside a nominally seven-dimensional problem. The learning rate is consistently important, and the remaining knobs often matter much less. Second — and this is the sharp part — *which* of those remaining knobs matter changes from data set to data set. On one task the annealing rate is the second important knob; on another it is the L2 penalty; on another the number of hidden units. There is even a clean correlation: the data sets whose effective dimension is one or two are exactly the ones a search solves in two or three trials, and the ones with effective dimension three or four are the ones that need many more. So Psi lives on a low-dimensional subspace of Lambda — but I am not allowed to know which subspace, and the subspace moves when the data moves.

Now put the two observations together and feel the bind. If I *knew* the important subspace, I could just build a fine grid over those two or three knobs and ignore the rest — easy. But I do not know it, and it differs per data set, so I am forced to put grid resolution on *all* K dimensions to be safe. And here is where the projection waste bites: those g levels I lay down on an *unimportant* knob are pure waste, because Psi is flat along it — every one of the g^{K-1} grid points that share a given value of an important knob is re-measuring essentially the same value of Psi, differing only in knobs that do nothing. The number of wasted trials is exponential in the number of dimensions that happen to be irrelevant for this data set. A grid with four levels on each of the few axes that *might* matter already costs 4^k, and still gives me no guarantee that a new data set's important axes are among the ones I gridded finely. The grid's structure is fighting the structure of the problem.

So what do I want instead? I want a trial set such that, no matter which low-dimensional subspace turns out to be the important one, my trials cover *that* subspace well. The grid fails because its points are aligned: they share coordinates, so they collapse under projection. Let me ask what happens if I just refuse to align them — draw each trial's knobs independently. Take N points, and for each point draw every knob independently from its range, uniformly (uniform in the natural range for a plain knob, uniform in log space for a knob that spans orders of magnitude, since for those the response is roughly flat per decade and I want even coverage of the *effect*, not of the raw number). Now project these N points onto any single axis. Because each point drew that coordinate independently and from a continuous distribution, the projected values are N draws from the uniform on that axis — almost surely all distinct, no collapse. So with N independent points I probe *every* knob at N distinct settings, whereas the grid probed each at N^{1/K}. The independent draws have, on every single axis simultaneously, the resolution of the full budget instead of the budget's K-th root.

Let me make sure I am not fooling myself, because "more distinct values per axis" sounds almost too cheap. The grid's even coverage of the *full* K-dimensional box is genuinely better than the independent draws — random points are a little clumpy in the full space, that is the price. But for finding a target that lives in a low-dimensional subspace, the full-space evenness is exactly the thing I do not need, and the per-axis resolution is exactly the thing I do. Picture the cleanest case: Psi depends only on knob 1 and is flat in knob 2, and I have nine trials. The grid is a 3-by-3 lattice, so it tests knob 1 at only three places — nine expensive trainings buy me three samples of the one function that matters, the other six being duplicates in the dimension I care about. Nine independent points test knob 1 at nine distinct places. Same budget, three times the resolution on the only axis that counts. And — this is the part I want to nail — the independent draws get this *for free, without knowing* that knob 1 was the important one. If it had been knob 2 instead, the very same nine points still test knob 2 at nine distinct places. The grid would have had to be rebuilt to put its resolution on knob 2; the independent set does not care. So independent uniform sampling has the same efficiency on the relevant subspace as if I had been told the subspace in advance and searched only it. That is the property I was after, and it falls straight out of *not aligning the points*.

I want this to be quantitative, not just a picture, so let me build the cleanest model of "find the good region" I can and compute. Idealize the good region as a target occupying a fraction v/V of the volume of the unit hypercube — say 1% — placed somewhere I do not know. I throw T independent uniform trials. Each trial independently lands in the target with probability v/V and misses with probability (1 - v/V). The trials are independent, so the probability that *all* T miss is (1 - v/V)^T, and therefore the probability that at least one hits the target is

  P(find) = 1 - (1 - v/V)^T.

With v/V = 0.01 that is 1 - 0.99^T. Let me get a feel for the numbers: 1 - 0.99^60 = 0.4528, so sixty trials is still below a coin flip; 1 - 0.99^69 is about 0.500, so about T = 69 trials gives me a coin-flip chance; for 95% confidence I need 1 - 0.99^T >= 0.95, i.e. 0.99^T <= 0.05, i.e. T >= ln(0.05)/ln(0.99) = 298.1, so the first integer budget that reaches 95% is 299 trials. A few hundred — squarely in the budget I actually have. But look at what is *not* in that formula: the ambient dimension K does not appear anywhere. The only thing that governs how many independent trials I need is the *relative volume* of the target, v/V, not how many irrelevant axes the target is buried in. That is the precise mathematical meaning of "thrives on low effective dimensionality": if the good region is, say, a slab that is wide in the irrelevant directions and only narrow in the one or two that matter, its relative volume v/V can be a perfectly findable 1% even though the space has thirty-two dimensions, and random search needs the same few hundred trials it would need in three dimensions. Grid, by contrast, would have to resolve all thirty-two axes to be sure of catching the narrow ones, and that is 2^32 before it even gets fine.

And there is a second, sharper way grid loses on exactly the low-effective-dimension targets, which I should work out because it is the realistic case. Suppose the good region is not a cube but an axis-aligned elongated rectangle — wide along the irrelevant knobs, thin along the important ones (which is what low effective dimension *looks like* geometrically). A long thin rectangle aligned with the grid axes, if it intersects the lattice at all, tends to intersect *several* collinear grid points at once — a whole row of them threads through it. Those collinear hits are not independent shots; they are the same coordinate value in the thin direction, sampled redundantly, so the rectangle either catches a wasteful cluster of points or, just as easily, slips entirely between two adjacent grid lines and catches none. The grid's effective sample size against such a target collapses. Independent points are never collinear, so each is a genuinely independent shot at the rectangle, and the 1 - (1 - v/V)^T law applies cleanly. (The one escape for the grid is if the target were *not* axis-aligned — rotated off the lattice — in which case its rows would not line up with grid columns and grid would do about as well as random. But hyper-parameter targets are axis-aligned almost by construction, because the important knobs *are* the coordinate axes.)

Now let me be skeptical of my own answer for a moment, because there are obviously cleverer point sets than naive independent draws, and I should check whether I am leaving something on the table. The quasi-Monte-Carlo people have spent decades building low-discrepancy sequences — Sobol, Halton, Niederreiter — and Latin hypercube designs, precisely to place points that look more uniform than uniform: they minimize discrepancy, meaning no clumps and no holes, and the good constructions keep their subspace projections low-discrepancy too. That last property is exactly the "good per-axis coverage" I was praising. In QMC integration these beat independent draws — they drive down the variance of a finite-sample integral faster. So should I use Sobol instead of plain random? Let me reason about the regime I am actually in. First, those QMC advantages are *asymptotic* — they show up as the sample size grows large — whereas I have tens to a few hundred trials, and when the number of dimensions is large relative to the budget a low-discrepancy set is not expected to behave much differently from independent draws (there are not enough points for the structure to assert itself). A simulation of finding a 1%-volume target in three and five dimensions bears this out: on the elongated low-effective-dimension targets no grid is competitive; Latin hypercube comes out about the same as independent random; Sobol is best, but only by a few percentage points, and that edge concentrates in the hundred-to-three-hundred-trial range. So Sobol is a refinement worth knowing about, not the headline.

And there is a property of plain independent draws that the deterministic sets quietly give up and that I value enormously in practice, so let me weigh it explicitly. Because the trials are i.i.d., I can stop the experiment whenever I like and what I have run so far is still a valid experiment; I can add more trials later if a few more machines free up, without recomputing or rebalancing anything; and if a job dies on a flaky cluster — which, with hundreds of multi-hour trainings, it will — I can just drop it or restart it, because no single trial's presence is load-bearing for the set. None of that is true for a grid (omit one point and the lattice has a hole) or for a low-discrepancy sequence (the points are constructed jointly, so dropping or adding one disturbs the discrepancy guarantee). The i.i.d. structure is also what lets me *analyze* the search honestly: S i.i.d. trials can be reinterpreted as N independent experiments of s trials each, for any s dividing S with sN <= S, just by partitioning them — which means from one run of S trials I can read off the whole distribution of "best of s" performance as a function of experiment size s, its median and quartiles and extremes. That efficiency-curve analysis is *only* available because the trials are independent and identically distributed; a grid or a Sobol set cannot be sliced that way. So the independence is not just convenient, it is what makes the method measurable and robust, and it is worth a couple of percentage points of Sobol's edge.

One more thing I have to get right if I am going to report the *result* of such a search honestly, because it is a real statistical trap. After running S trials, the naive thing is to pick the trial with the best validation score and report its test score. But test error is not monotone in validation error, and the validation set is finite, so when several trials have nearly tied validation scores, which one I crown as "best" is itself noisy — a slightly different validation draw would anoint a different trial, with a different test score, and reporting the single luckiest one biases me optimistic. So I should treat the test score z of "the best model" as a random variable and account for the uncertainty in *which* trial is best. Model it as a Gaussian mixture over the S trials: component s has mean mu_s = Psi_test(lambda^(s)) and variance sigma_s^2 = V_test(lambda^(s)), and weight w_s = the probability that trial s is in fact the winner, w_s = P(Z^(s) < Z^(s') for all s' != s) where each Z^(i) ~ N(Psi_valid(lambda^(i)), V_valid(lambda^(i))) — for a 0/1 loss the variance is just the Bernoulli V = Psi(1 - Psi)/(|X| - 1). Then the mean and variance of the best model's generalization are

  mu_z = sum_s w_s mu_s,
  sigma_z^2 = sum_s w_s (mu_s^2 + sigma_s^2) - mu_z^2.

The first is the standard weighted-mean identity; the second is the law-of-total-variance form for a mixture — the average of the component variances plus the spread of the component means, sum_s w_s(sigma_s^2 + mu_s^2) minus mu_z^2 — which I can verify by expanding: E[z^2] over the mixture is sum_s w_s E[Z_s^2] = sum_s w_s(sigma_s^2 + mu_s^2), and sigma_z^2 = E[z^2] - mu_z^2 gives exactly that. The weights w_s I cannot write in closed form for many ties, but they are trivial to estimate by simulation: repeatedly draw hypothetical validation scores Z^(s) from those normals, count how often each trial wins, normalize. A few tens of draws suffice because the winners are close and the weights need not be precise. This is what makes the efficiency curve's gentle downward droop at large experiment sizes sensible rather than alarming: as the experiment grows, more trials have a claim on "best," so the win-weighting averages the lucky-high test scores together with the unlucky-low ones and the best-of-s estimate settles toward an honest value instead of tracking the single most fortunate trial.

So let me commit. The strategy is: draw each trial's configuration by sampling every knob independently from its declared range — uniform for a plain knob, uniform-in-log for a knob that spans orders of magnitude — over the same domain a grid would have spanned, and evaluate Psi there; repeat for the budget; choose the configuration with the best validation value. The Gaussian-mixture calculation is for reporting the chosen model's generalization under finite-validation uncertainty, not for changing which point gets proposed next. It uses none of the history to decide where to look next — it is deliberately non-adaptive, which is the one honest limitation I should name: it ignores results already in hand. An adaptive, sequential, model-based search that learns which dimensions matter as it goes *should* beat this, and the right way to think about this method is as the natural baseline against which to measure those adaptive methods, not as the last word. But as a baseline it is exactly right: it has all of grid's practical virtues — conceptual simplicity, trivial parallelism, reproducibility — and trades a small efficiency loss in genuinely low dimensions for a large efficiency gain in high dimensions with low effective dimension, which is the regime hyper-parameter optimization actually lives in.

Now to drop it into the search harness, which calls a strategy repeatedly, once per trial, handing it the search space, the history of past trials, and the remaining budget, and expects back a configuration to evaluate plus a fidelity in (0, 1]. The proposal rule is the whole content, and after all of the above it is almost shockingly small: ignore the history, ask the space for one independent uniform draw, evaluate at full fidelity. The space already knows how to draw each knob from its own range and type — categorical by a uniform choice over the options, a plain continuous knob by a uniform over [low, high], a log-scaled continuous knob by exponentiating a uniform over [log low, log high], an integer knob by a uniform integer or a rounded log-uniform — which is exactly the per-knob independent uniform/log-uniform draw I argued for, so I lean on it rather than re-deriving the sampling inside the strategy. The seed gives reproducibility; that is the one piece of state I keep.

```python
import numpy as np
from typing import Any, Dict, List, Tuple


class RandomSearch:
    """Random search: each trial is an independent uniform draw from the
    configuration space. Non-adaptive -- the past trials are never consulted,
    because the whole point is that i.i.d. draws probe every knob at the full
    budget's resolution no matter which subspace turns out to matter."""

    def __init__(self, seed: int = 42):
        self.seed = seed
        # one RNG, so the experiment is reproducible from the seed
        self.rng = np.random.RandomState(seed)

    def suggest(
        self,
        space: "SearchSpace",
        history: List["Trial"],     # deliberately unused: the method is non-adaptive
        budget_left: int,           # deliberately unused: every trial is i.i.d.
    ) -> Tuple[Dict[str, Any], float]:
        # one independent draw: each knob sampled from its own range/type,
        # uniform (or uniform-in-log for scale knobs). Projecting many such
        # draws onto any single axis gives that many distinct values -- the
        # per-axis resolution grid throws away.
        config = space.sample_uniform(self.rng)
        # full fidelity: a single-fidelity baseline, no cheap/noisy partial evals.
        return config, 1.0
```

Let me trace the whole chain back so I am sure each link is load-bearing. I needed a way to pick trial points for a black-box, gradient-free, expensive-to-evaluate response function Psi over a configuration space mixing continuous, integer, and categorical knobs. Grid search was the default but its trial count is exponential in the number of knobs, and worse, its aligned points collapse under projection so it probes each knob at only the K-th root of the budget. Manual search was adaptive and cheap in trials but irreproducible and useless as a baseline. The decisive fact about Psi, read off a Gaussian-process relevance analysis, is that it has low effective dimension — only a few knobs matter — but which knobs matter is unknown and changes per data set, so I cannot just grid the important ones. Refusing to align the points — drawing each trial's knobs independently and uniformly — makes the projection onto every axis land on the full budget's worth of distinct values at once, giving the relevant subspace the same coverage as if I had known it in advance, for free. The clean model 1 - (1 - v/V)^T showed the number of independent trials needed depends only on the target's relative volume, not the ambient dimension, so a few hundred draws find a 1%-volume target in any number of dimensions; and against the axis-aligned elongated targets that low effective dimension produces, grid's collinear hits collapse its effective sample size while independent draws stay clean. Low-discrepancy sequences like Sobol can shave a few percent in low dimensions but lose the i.i.d. structure that makes the search anytime-stoppable, extendable, fault-tolerant, and analyzable via the efficiency curve, and in the high-dimension-low-budget regime they are no better. Reporting the result honestly needs the win-weighted Gaussian-mixture estimate of the best model's generalization to avoid crowning the luckiest tied trial. And the whole thing reduces, in code, to one independent uniform draw per call with the history ignored — a non-adaptive baseline that keeps every practical virtue of grid while escaping its exponential waste, and the right yardstick against which adaptive sequential search should be judged.
