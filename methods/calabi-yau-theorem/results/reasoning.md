I start from the original geometric demand: find a Kahler metric whose Ricci form is a prescribed representative of the first Chern class. Stated directly, that is a tensorial existence problem. It asks for a whole metric, constrained by curvature, inside a fixed cohomology class. If I try to solve for the metric components directly, the problem looks overdetermined and coordinate-dependent.

The special structure of Kahler geometry changes the problem. Inside a fixed Kahler class, changing the metric can be expressed by one scalar potential: `omega_phi = omega + i partial barpartial phi`. This is already a compression of the unknown. Instead of solving for all metric coefficients independently, I solve for one function `phi`, while positivity of `omega_phi` keeps the result inside the Kahler cone.

The Ricci form has a matching scalar-determinant structure. Since `Ric(omega) = -i partial barpartial log det(g)`, prescribing the Ricci form amounts to prescribing the change in the logarithm of the volume determinant. The `partial barpartial` lemma identifies the cohomological compatibility condition with a global scalar right-hand side. After exponentiating, the geometric equation becomes `(omega + i partial barpartial phi)^n = e^F omega^n`.

Before I trust this as the equation to attack, I should check that it is even self-consistent, because a determinant equation carries a hidden global constraint. Integrate both sides over the compact manifold `M`. Expand the left side by the binomial theorem with `a = i partial barpartial phi`: `(omega + a)^n = sum_{k=0}^n C(n,k) omega^{n-k} a^k`. The `k = 0` term is `omega^n`. Every term with `k >= 1` contains a factor `i partial barpartial phi`, which is exact, wedged against the closed form `omega^{n-k}`; the whole term is then exact, and on a closed manifold its integral vanishes by Stokes. So `integral_M omega_phi^n = integral_M omega^n` for any admissible `phi`. Setting that equal to `integral_M e^F omega^n` forces `integral_M e^F omega^n = integral_M omega^n`. The right-hand side data `F` is not free: it must satisfy this one scalar normalization. That is reassuring rather than alarming — it is exactly the cohomological volume compatibility (the prescribed Ricci form must represent the right class) showing up as a solvability condition on the analysis. I will assume `F` is normalized this way from now on; otherwise no `phi` can exist.

Now I want to gauge how hard the equation really is, and the honest way to do that is to look at the lowest dimension. Take `n = 1`, a Riemann surface, `omega = i g , dz wedge dbar z`. The top power is just the first power, so the equation reads `omega + i partial barpartial phi = e^F omega`, i.e. `phi_{z bar z} = (e^F - 1) g`. That is a linear Poisson equation for `phi`. In one complex dimension there is no genuine nonlinearity, and existence follows from standard linear elliptic theory plus the normalization I just derived. So the difficulty cannot live at `n = 1`; it must be a feature of higher dimension. Checking `n = 2` with a diagonal complex Hessian `H = diag(h_1, h_2)` makes this concrete: `det(g + H) = g_11 g_22 + g_11 h_2 + g_22 h_1 + h_1 h_2`. The cross term `h_1 h_2` is a product of two second derivatives of `phi`. So for `n >= 2` the equation is genuinely nonlinear in the highest-order derivatives. This is a complex Monge-Ampere equation, not a perturbed Laplace equation, and that is the whole source of trouble.

Given a fully nonlinear equation, the natural existence strategy is the continuity method: connect an easy, solvable equation to the target equation by a parameter `t`, and study the set of `t` for which a smooth solution exists. If that set is nonempty, open, and closed in `[0,1]`, connectedness gives a solution at `t = 1`. Nonemptiness I can arrange by choosing the `t = 0` equation to be `omega_phi^n = omega^n`, solved by `phi = 0`. So the work is openness and closedness.

Openness I can test directly by linearizing. Write the equation locally as `log det(g + phi'') = F + log det(g)`, perturb `phi -> phi + s psi`, and differentiate at `s = 0`. The derivative of `log det` is the trace against the inverse: `(g + phi'')^{-1 , i bar j} psi_{i bar j} = g_phi^{i bar j} psi_{i bar j}`. That is precisely the Laplacian of `psi` with respect to the metric `omega_phi`. So the linearized operator is `L psi = Delta_{omega_phi} psi`. This is second-order elliptic exactly when `g_phi` is positive definite, i.e. when `omega_phi > 0`; on a compact manifold its kernel is the constants, so after fixing a normalization like `integral_M phi , omega^n = 0` it is invertible between the right Holder spaces. Openness therefore comes essentially for free — but only while `omega_phi` stays strictly positive. The linearization just told me where the danger is: ellipticity is not automatic, it is contingent on the deformed metric not degenerating.

That reframes the entire problem. Openness and nonemptiness are cheap; the linearization shows they hold as long as `g_phi > 0` uniformly. So closedness is the only real obstruction, and what it demands is sharp: along a sequence of parameters `t_k -> t_*` with solutions `phi_{t_k}`, I need to prevent the metrics `omega_{phi_{t_k}}` from either blowing up or collapsing, so that the limit equation still has a smooth solution and `g_phi` stays bounded above and below. Because `L = Delta_{omega_phi}` depends on the unknown through its own coefficients, losing control of `g_phi` would simultaneously destroy ellipticity and the regularity it buys. The whole proof hinges on a priori bounds that do not depend on `t`.

So the decisive question is concrete: can every solution on the path be controlled using constants depending only on the background geometry and the prescribed data `F`, not on the deformation parameter? What is needed, in order, is a `C^0` bound on `phi` (size of the potential), then a second-order bound — control of the trace `tr_omega omega_phi`, equivalently the Laplacian of `phi` against the background metric — which is exactly the quantity that keeps `g_phi` bounded away from `0` and `infinity` and thus keeps `L` uniformly elliptic. Once uniform ellipticity is in hand, the equation is a uniformly elliptic Monge-Ampere equation with bounded coefficients, and complex elliptic regularity bootstraps `C^2` control into bounds on all higher derivatives. That higher-derivative control is the compactness that lets a subsequence of `phi_{t_k}` converge in a smooth topology to a solution of the limit equation. That closes the path.

I should be honest about which of these steps I have actually verified here and which I am taking on faith. The normalization on `F`, the `n = 1` reduction, the `n >= 2` nonlinearity, and the identification of the linearization with `Delta_{omega_phi}` I worked out above and trust. The a priori estimates themselves — especially the `C^0` and second-order bounds that hold uniformly in `t` — are the genuinely hard analytic input, and I have not derived them on this page; I would want to check them against an explicit construction (for instance, a flat complex torus, where solutions can be written down) before claiming the circuit is closed in detail. What I can say with confidence is the architecture: the linearization computation shows that everything reduces to keeping `g_phi` uniformly positive, so the entire weight of the proof rests on those uniform estimates, and nowhere else.

That is the shape of the method. The Ricci prescription becomes a determinant equation; the Kahler condition becomes positivity of a potential perturbation; the cohomology condition becomes the volume normalization on `F` that I verified by Stokes. These are not cosmetic translations — they align the global geometry with an analytic bootstrap, turning "there should be a canonical metric" into "no blow-up along a nonlinear elliptic deformation path." The existence of a canonical metric stops being a direct construction and becomes a compactness theorem for a family of deformed solutions, and it succeeds precisely when the estimates form a closed circuit: potential control gives metric control, metric control gives ellipticity, ellipticity gives higher regularity, and higher regularity gives the limit.

In the Calabi-Yau case `c_1 = 0`, the target Ricci form is zero, so `F` is constant and the theorem yields a Ricci-flat Kahler metric in each Kahler class. The topological condition only says there is no cohomological obstruction to Ricci-flatness — by the volume check above, `c_1 = 0` is what makes the constant-`F` equation solvable in principle. The Monge-Ampere equation together with the uniform estimate framework is what turns that absence of obstruction into an actual smooth metric.
