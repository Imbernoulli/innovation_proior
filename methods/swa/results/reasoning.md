I start from the geometry, because whatever algorithm I end up with only makes sense after I am clear about what is wrong with the final iterate. That iterate is the point favored by the regularized training surface once the learning rate has decayed. But the surface I am graded on at test time is shifted relative to the surface I optimized. If I stand at a narrow edge of the training valley, even a small shift between train and test can push me off the edge and make the test error worse. So the object I want to return is probably not the deepest training-loss point; it is something more like a central point in a broad region, a point that stays good when the surface shifts a little.

That nudges me toward width, but width by itself is still too vague to act on. Keskar and Chaudhari give me the prior that flatter regions can generalize better. Garipov gives me a more surprising permission: neural-network optima are not isolated needles in a hopeless nonconvex landscape. There can be simple low-loss paths between modes, and nearby points along such a path can still make diverse predictions. Put together, it is at least plausible that a training trajectory can wander around inside one useful connected region rather than just dropping into a single endpoint. If that is true, then the question is how to summarize the wandering into one central point.

The obvious first idea is to keep training normally and average the late checkpoints. Let me think about whether that can possibly work. If I keep decaying the learning rate, SGD stops exploring; all the late checkpoints pile up near one endpoint, separated by tiny steps. Averaging those is just a smoother version of the last iterate, and "more central" is meaningless when the points are essentially on top of each other. So plain averaging under a decaying schedule cannot be the mechanism. Whatever I do has to keep the late checkpoints genuinely spread out. That is the real lever, and it tells me the learning-rate schedule is not a detail — it is the method.

Why would spread-out checkpoints help an *average* in particular? Here Mandt's constant-LR picture is the thing to lean on: near an optimum, and under simplifying assumptions, constant-step SGD has a stationary, roughly Gaussian distribution whose covariance is set by learning rate, batch size, curvature, and gradient noise. The claim I keep hearing is that in high dimension the individual samples sit out near a shell while the mean sits inward, but I should not just take that on faith — it is the load-bearing geometric claim. Let me actually check it. Draw `n` samples from an isotropic Gaussian `N(0, I_d)`, treat each as a checkpoint, and compare a single sample's distance from the center to the centroid's distance from the center.

For `d = 1000`, drawing one checkpoint a time over many trials, the typical sample sits at distance about `31.6` from the center, and `sqrt(d) = 31.62` — so the samples really do live near a shell of radius `sqrt(d)`, with a standard deviation of about `0.71` that does not grow with `d`. The shell is thin and the samples are genuinely peripheral. Now the centroids: with `n = 3` peripheral checkpoints the centroid sits at distance `~18.2`, with `n = 5` at `~14.1`, with `n = 10` at `~10.0`, with `n = 20` at `~7.1`. The ratios are `0.578, 0.447, 0.316, 0.224`, which match `1/sqrt(n) = 0.577, 0.447, 0.316, 0.224` essentially exactly. So averaging is not just noise reduction — the centroid of peripheral samples sits a factor `1/sqrt(n)` closer to the center than any single sample. That is the inward move I was hoping for, and it only exists because the samples were spread around the shell in the first place. With a decaying schedule the "shell" has zero radius and there is nothing to move inward from. This confirms the two halves have to go together: keep the tail energetic so the checkpoints scatter, then average to reach the center the trajectory only visits in expectation.

So I should deliberately keep the tail of training energetic. A constant high learning rate gives maximal wandering and the simplest schedule. A cycle gives a compromise: jump back up to a high learning rate to move somewhere new, then drop to a low learning rate so the checkpoint I collect at the bottom of the cycle is individually decent rather than mid-flight. I do not want a smooth triangular cycle for its own sake; the abrupt jump from low back to high is the part that does the work, because it forces the next sample to be somewhere genuinely different. So: collect once per epoch under the constant schedule, or at the minimum of each cycle under the cyclic schedule, and let the high phase do the scattering in between.

At this point I could stop and just keep the collected checkpoints as an ensemble — average their *predictions* at test time. Garipov's fast geometric ensembling already does exactly this. But that pays the test-time cost of running several networks, which is the thing I was trying to avoid; I wanted to return one model. So the real question is whether I can average the *weights* instead of the predictions and keep most of the benefit. In general averaging weights is dangerous — averaging two unrelated networks can produce nonsense — but here the checkpoints are not unrelated; they are nearby points from one trajectory inside one connected low-loss region. Nearby is the operative word, and I can make it quantitative.

Let `f(w)` be one scalar output of the network on a fixed input. With checkpoints `w_i`, define `w_avg = (1/n) sum_i w_i` and `Delta_i = w_i - w_avg`, so that `sum_i Delta_i = 0` by construction. Linearize each prediction around `w_avg`: `f(w_i) = f(w_avg) + <grad f(w_avg), Delta_i> + O(||Delta_i||^2)`. Averaging over `i`, the first-order term is `(1/n) sum_i <grad f(w_avg), Delta_i> = <grad f(w_avg), (1/n) sum_i Delta_i> = 0`. So the prediction ensemble and the single averaged-weight model should differ only at second order in the spread. I want to make sure I am not fooling myself with the algebra, so let me build a tiny smooth network — a two-layer tanh net with a scalar output, weight vector in `R^13`, fixed input — and a cloud of `n = 7` nearby checkpoints, and directly compare `(1/n) sum_i f(w_i)` against `f(w_avg)`.

At spread `0.30` the gap `ensemble - f(w_avg)` is about `7.1e-2`; at `0.10` it is `1.55e-2`; at `0.03` it is `1.27e-3`; at `0.01` it is `1.73e-4`. The measured first-order term `(1/n) sum_i <grad, Delta_i>` is `~1e-17` at every spread — it really does vanish, as the algebra said. To pin down the scaling I average the gap over many random clouds at each spread: `gap/spread^2` comes out `1.71, 1.75, 1.78, 1.78` as spread goes `0.20, 0.10, 0.05, 0.025`. It flattens to a constant, so the penalty for replacing the prediction ensemble by one averaged-weight model is genuinely `O(spread^2)`. Meanwhile two checkpoints can disagree at *first* order: `f(w_i) - f(w_j)` contains `<grad f(w_avg), Delta_i - Delta_j>`, which does not cancel. That is the asymmetry I needed — the diversity that makes ensembling useful is first order in the spread, while the cost of collapsing to one averaged network is only second order. As long as I keep the cloud tight enough that `spread^2` is small, weight averaging keeps most of the ensemble benefit for the price of a single model.

This also clarifies why this is not Polyak–Ruppert averaging done out of habit. Classical iterate averaging is about stochastic-approximation efficiency under classical assumptions, typically with a decreasing step size and an asymptotic-variance story. Here the whole point is the *non*-decreasing learning rate: I first manufacture a cloud of nearby, high-performing, diverse weights, then take its centroid. The two checks above are what make the centroid meaningful — the concentration result says the centroid moves inward, and the linearization result says the centroid behaves like the ensemble. If the cloud were allowed to collapse, both effects would vanish and the method would degrade into ordinary smoothing of the last iterate.

The recipe now falls out. Start from a conventionally pretrained weight vector `w_hat`, either after the full budget or after a reduced budget such as `0.75B` to leave room for the tail. From there continue training with either the constant `alpha(i) = alpha_1` or the high-to-low cycle `alpha(i) = (1 - t(i)) alpha_1 + t(i) alpha_2`, where `t(i) = (mod(i - 1, c) + 1) / c`, jumping back to `alpha_1` at the start of the next cycle. To avoid storing every checkpoint, keep an equal running average and fold each new sample in: `w_avg <- (w_avg * n_models + w) / (n_models + 1)`, which is just `w_avg + (w - w_avg)/(n_models + 1)`.

One practical issue is left, and it comes specifically from the fact that I averaged *weights* rather than evaluated a live model. If the network has batch normalization, the averaged weights never actually produced any forward activations, so they have no running means and variances of their own — the buffers sitting on the live model belong to the last live iterate, not to `w_avg`. The fix is not another optimization step; it is one forward pass over the training data in training mode with the averaged weights, recomputing the batch-norm statistics so they match the weights I am about to ship.

Putting the geometry back together: the conventional endpoint can have slightly lower training loss yet worse test error because it sits near the boundary of the broad good region, on the thin shell. The averaged model stays in the same basin but moves inward by the `1/sqrt(n)` factor I measured, which is exactly the part of the region that is robust to moving along random directions and to the train-to-test shift. The cost is one extra weight copy, one occasional weighted sum, and one batch-norm pass, and it returns a single network rather than an ensemble.
