I start from the geometry, because the algorithm only makes sense after the geometry changes. The final SGD iterate is not automatically the right object to ship. It is the point favored by the regularized training surface after the learning rate decays, but the test-error surface is shifted relative to that surface. If I stand at a narrow edge of the training valley, even a small shift between train and test can make the test error worse. The point I want is not necessarily the deepest training-loss point; it is a central point in a broad region that stays good when the surface shifts.

That points me toward width, but width alone is still vague. Keskar and Chaudhari give me the prior that flatter regions can generalize better, and Garipov gives me the more surprising geometric permission: neural-network optima are not just isolated needles in a hopeless nonconvex landscape. There can be simple low-loss paths between modes, and nearby points along such paths can still have diverse predictions. So it is plausible that a training trajectory can move around inside a useful connected region instead of merely falling into one endpoint.

Now I need a way to get a central point in that region. If I keep decaying the learning rate, SGD stops exploring and all late checkpoints pile up near one endpoint. Averaging those checkpoints cannot do much; it is just a smoother version of the last iterate. This is where the constant-learning-rate sampling picture matters. Mandt's analysis says that, near an optimum and under simplifying assumptions, constant-step SGD has a stationary Gaussian-like distribution whose covariance is controlled by the learning rate, batch size, curvature, and gradient noise. In high dimension, samples from a Gaussian do not spend their mass at the center; after whitening they live near a sphere of radius about `sqrt(d)`. So an individual SGD checkpoint with a non-vanishing learning rate is naturally a peripheral sample.

That gives me the first real insight: if the checkpoints are peripheral samples from a good region, their arithmetic mean is not just noise reduction. The mean moves inward. It is a way to get to the central part that SGD visits only in expectation, not as a typical single iterate. The average is useful only because the learning-rate schedule keeps the samples spread out. Without exploration, averaging is habit. With exploration, averaging becomes geometry.

So I should deliberately keep the tail of training energetic. A constant high learning rate gives maximal wandering. A cycle gives a compromise: jump back to a high learning rate to move elsewhere, then decrease to a low learning rate so the checkpoint collected at the bottom of the cycle is individually decent. I should not use a smooth triangular cycle just because cyclic schedules usually do; for this purpose the abrupt jump from low back to high is valuable because it forces exploration. The collected points should come once per epoch under the constant schedule or at the minimum of each cycle under the cyclic schedule.

At this point I could just use those checkpoints as an ensemble. Fast geometric ensembling already does that: collect nearby diverse checkpoints and average their predictions. But that keeps the test-time cost of multiple networks. I want one model, so I need to justify averaging weights rather than predictions. This sounds dangerous in general, because averaging weights from unrelated networks can destroy the function. Here the checkpoints are not unrelated. They are close points from the same trajectory and the same connected low-loss region.

Let `f(w)` be one scalar prediction of the network. Suppose the collected checkpoints are `w_i`, and define `w_avg = (1/n) sum_i w_i`. Let `Delta_i = w_i - w_avg`. By construction, `sum_i Delta_i = 0`. If I linearize each prediction around `w_avg`, I get `f(w_i) = f(w_avg) + <grad f(w_avg), Delta_i> + O(||Delta_i||^2)`. When I average these predictions, the first-order terms vanish because the offsets sum to zero. The difference between the prediction ensemble and the prediction of the averaged-weight model is only second order in the checkpoint spread. Meanwhile two different checkpoints can disagree at first order, because `f(w_i) - f(w_j)` contains `<grad f(w_avg), Delta_i - Delta_j>`. That is exactly the relationship I need: the diversity that helps ensembling is first order, while the penalty for replacing the prediction ensemble by one averaged-weight network is second order.

This also tells me why the method is not Polyak-Ruppert averaging by habit. Classical averaging is about stochastic-approximation efficiency under classical assumptions, usually with a decreasing step size or an asymptotic convergence story. Here the learning-rate choice is the heart of the method. I first manufacture a useful cloud of nearby, high-performing, diverse weights, then take its centroid. If the cloud collapses, the method collapses back into ordinary smoothing.

The final recipe follows directly. I start from a conventionally pretrained weight vector `w_hat`, either after the full budget or after a reduced budget such as `0.75B`. From there I continue training with either `alpha(i) = alpha_1` or the high-to-low cycle `alpha(i) = (1 - t(i)) alpha_1 + t(i) alpha_2`, where `t(i) = (mod(i - 1, c) + 1) / c`, followed by a jump back to `alpha_1` at the next cycle. I maintain an equal running average so I do not store all checkpoints: `w_avg <- (w_avg * n_models + w)/(n_models + 1)`. Algebraically this is the same as adding `(w - w_avg)/(n_models + 1)`.

One practical issue remains. If the network has batch normalization, the averaged weights never produced the training activations that would populate their running means and variances. The buffers attached to the live model are not the right buffers for the averaged model. The fix is not another optimization step; it is one forward pass over the training data in training mode with the averaged weights, recomputing the batch-norm statistics.

The expected geometry is now precise. The conventional endpoint can have slightly lower training loss and still worse test error because it sits near a boundary of the broad good region. The averaged model stays in the same basin, moves toward the center, and is harder to damage by moving along random directions or by the train-to-test shift. The method is one extra weight copy, one occasional weighted sum, and one batch-norm statistics pass, but it returns a single network rather than an ensemble.
