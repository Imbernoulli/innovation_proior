Find all $f:\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}$ with $f(x+y)=f(x)+f(y)$ for all real $x,y$. The reflex is immediate: $f(x)=cx$ works, plug it in, $c(x+y)=cx+cy$, done. So I expect the whole exercise to be proving these are the only ones. Let me just do that and move on.

Start by squeezing the equation. Put $x=y=0$: $f(0)=f(0)+f(0)$, so $f(0)=0$. Put $y=-x$: $f(0)=f(x)+f(-x)$, so $f(-x)=-f(x)$ — the function is odd, good, that's consistent with $cx$. Now stack copies of the same $x$: $f(2x)=f(x)+f(x)=2f(x)$, and inductively $f(nx)=nf(x)$ for every positive integer $n$, then by oddness for every integer $n$. So on integer multiples I'm forced into linearity. Push to fractions: from $f(n\cdot \tfrac{x}{n})=n f(\tfrac{x}{n})$ and the left side being $f(x)$, I get $f(\tfrac{x}{n})=\tfrac1n f(x)$, and combining, $f(\tfrac{m}{n}x)=\tfrac{m}{n}f(x)$ for all rationals $\tfrac{m}{n}$. Set $x=1$ and write $c=f(1)$. Then for every rational $q$,
$$f(q)=cq.$$
Beautiful — the equation has pinned $f$ down completely on $\mathbb{Q}$, with a single free constant $c=f(1)$. This is exactly the shape of "prove $f(x)=cx$ is the only solution." I just need to get from $\mathbb{Q}$ to all of $\mathbb{R}$.

So let me get $f(\sqrt2)$. I want to express $\sqrt2$ using rationals and the additive law and read off its value. Add and subtract rationals... $f(\sqrt2 + q)=f(\sqrt2)+cq$, fine, but that relates $f$ at $\sqrt2+q$ to $f$ at $\sqrt2$; it never lets me compute $f(\sqrt2)$ from rational data alone. I can scale by integers: $f(n\sqrt2)=n f(\sqrt2)$. By rationals: $f(q\sqrt2)=q f(\sqrt2)$. But all of these still carry the unknown $f(\sqrt2)$ as a factor. There is no equation in my hands that connects $f(\sqrt2)$ to $c$. The additive law lets me move within the "$\sqrt2$-line" $\{q\sqrt2\}$ and within the rational line $\{q\}$, but it gives me no bridge between the two. $\sqrt2$ and $1$ don't talk to each other.

That should worry me, and let me take the worry seriously instead of assuming a cleverer substitution exists. Suppose I just *declare* a value, say $f(\sqrt2)=2015$ — some number with no relation to $c$ at all — and see whether anything breaks. On the set of numbers $a+b\sqrt2$ with $a,b$ rational, define $f(a+b\sqrt2)=ac+2015b$. Is this consistent with additivity? Take $a+b\sqrt2$ and $a'+b'\sqrt2$; their sum is $(a+a')+(b+b')\sqrt2$, and my rule gives $(a+a')c+2015(b+b')=(ac+2015b)+(a'c+2015b')$, which is exactly $f$ of the first plus $f$ of the second. It works. And crucially the representation $a+b\sqrt2$ is *unique* — if $a+b\sqrt2=a'+b'\sqrt2$ with rationals then $(b-b')\sqrt2=a'-a$ is rational, forcing $b=b'$ (else $\sqrt2$ rational) and then $a=a'$ — so the rule is well-defined. So here is a perfectly good additive function on $\mathbb{Q}[\sqrt2]$ that is **not** of the form $cx$: it sends $\sqrt2$ to $2015$ instead of to $c\sqrt2$. The "obvious answer is the only answer" instinct is just wrong, and the wall I hit at $f(\sqrt2)$ wasn't my lack of cleverness — it was a genuine degree of freedom.

Why is the freedom there? Because the equation only mentions $+$. The reals carry two operations, $+$ and $\times$, and "$f(x)=cx$" is really the statement that $f$ respects multiplication by the fixed scalar $c$ — but the functional equation never said a word about multiplication. It constrains $f$ only as a map of the *additive* structure. And the additive structure of $\mathbb{R}$, with rational scaling thrown in for free (since I derived $f(qx)=qf(x)$), is precisely the structure of $\mathbb{R}$ as a vector space over $\mathbb{Q}$. That reframing is the whole game: an additive $f$ with $f(qx)=qf(x)$ is exactly a $\mathbb{Q}$-linear map $\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}$. And a linear map is free to do anything it likes on a basis.

So the question becomes: does $\mathbb{R}$, as a vector space over $\mathbb{Q}$, have a basis? If it does — call it $\{e_\alpha\}$ — then every real $x$ is a unique finite rational combination $x=q_1 e_{\alpha_1}+\cdots+q_k e_{\alpha_k}$, and I can hand each basis vector $e_\alpha$ an *arbitrary* value $f(e_\alpha)$ and extend by $f(\sum q_i e_{\alpha_i})=\sum q_i f(e_{\alpha_i})$. That's automatically additive. Choosing the values to not be a single common multiple of the $e_\alpha$ gives a solution that is not $cx$. My $\mathbb{Q}[\sqrt2]$ example was the baby case: $\{1,\sqrt2\}$ is a $\mathbb{Q}$-basis of $\mathbb{Q}[\sqrt2]$, and I assigned $1\mapsto c$, $\sqrt2\mapsto2015$ independently.

So I need a $\mathbb{Q}$-basis of $\mathbb{R}$. Let me try to build one the way I'd build a basis of any vector space: greedily. Start with $\{1\}$. It's independent (one nonzero vector). It's not spanning — $\sqrt2\notin\mathbb{Q}\cdot1$. Throw in $\sqrt2$: $\{1,\sqrt2\}$, still independent. Not spanning — $\sqrt3$ isn't a rational combination (if $a+b\sqrt2=\sqrt3$ with rationals, square it: $a^2+2b^2+2ab\sqrt2=3$, forcing $ab=0$ and then either $\sqrt3$ or $\sqrt{3/2}$ rational, both false). Throw in $\sqrt3$. Then $\pi$. Then $e$. Each time, "independent over $\mathbb{Q}$" means no nontrivial rational combination of the chosen numbers vanishes, and I can always find a fresh number outside the rational span of what I have, because the rational span of finitely many reals is countable and $\mathbb{R}$ is not. So I can always take one more step.

But here is the wall, and it's a real one. Ordinary induction gives me $S_1\subset S_2\subset S_3\subset\cdots$, one independent set for each natural number, and *none of them spans*, because each is countable — a countable independent set can't span $\mathbb{R}$, whose dimension over $\mathbb{Q}$ is uncountable (if a countable basis existed, $\mathbb{R}$ would be a countable union of finite-dimensional rational spans, each countable, making $\mathbb{R}$ countable — false). Induction reaches every finite stage and stops there; it never reaches "infinity." I cannot conclude anything about $\bigcup_n S_n$ from "each $S_n$ works," any more than I can conclude $\bigcap_n(-\tfrac1n,\tfrac1n)$ is an open interval from each finite intersection being one — that intersection is the single point $\{0\}$. Finite induction is structurally too short for this.

So I need to climb past the natural numbers. The right ruler is the ordinals: after $0,1,2,\dots$ comes a number $\omega$, then $\omega+1,\omega+2,\dots$, then $2\omega$, and on up, each ordinal naming a stage of a transfinite process. Recursion along the ordinals splits into three kinds of step. The zero stage: $S_0=\varnothing$. A successor stage $\alpha+1$: given the independent set $S_\alpha$, if it isn't spanning, pick any vector independent of it and adjoin it to get $S_{\alpha+1}$ — exactly my greedy step. The new case, the one finite induction was missing, is a *limit* stage $\lambda$ (like $\omega$): there's no immediately preceding stage to step from, so instead I take the union $S_\lambda=\bigcup_{\gamma<\lambda}S_\gamma$ of everything built so far. I need this union to still be independent — and it is, because any linear dependence among its elements involves only *finitely many* of them, all of which already live in some single $S_\gamma$ with $\gamma<\lambda$ (the stages are nested and increasing), where they were independent by construction. So the union is independent, and the recursion resumes.

Does it stop? It has to, and this is where I need to be honest about what I'm assuming. Each successor stage consumes a brand-new real number. The ordinals, marched out far enough, pass any prescribed set size, so if the process never halted past the size of $\mathbb{R}$ I would be trying to keep adding new reals after all reals had already been accounted for; equivalently I would be claiming an independent subset of $\mathbb{R}$ larger than $\mathbb{R}$ itself. (That I can well-order the construction and keep *choosing* a fresh independent vector through transfinitely many successor stages is exactly where the Axiom of Choice enters.) So at some ordinal the set $S$ cannot be extended: there's no vector independent of it, i.e. $S$ spans. An independent spanning set is a basis. I have a $\mathbb{Q}$-basis of $\mathbb{R}$.

Let me re-package this so I'm sure the choice content is doing exactly what I think. Consider the collection of all $\mathbb{Q}$-independent subsets of $\mathbb{R}$, partially ordered by inclusion. Take any chain $T_0\subset T_1\subset\cdots$ in it (a totally ordered subfamily); its union is again independent, by the same finite-support argument, and is an upper bound for the chain lying in the collection. So every chain has an upper bound. Zorn's Lemma — every nonempty partially ordered set in which each chain has an upper bound contains a maximal element — hands me a maximal independent set $H$. Maximal means nothing can be adjoined while preserving independence, i.e. every real is rationally dependent on $H$, i.e. $H$ spans. So $H$ is a basis. This is the same climb as before, with the transfinite bookkeeping packed into the one hypothesis "every chain has an upper bound." Zorn's Lemma is one of the standard forms of Choice, so the basis has not appeared from elementary construction; it is a Choice-supplied object.

$H=\{e_\alpha\}$ is called a Hamel basis. Every real $x$ has a *unique* expression $x=\sum_i q_i e_{\alpha_i}$ as a finite rational combination. And $H$ must be uncountable, by the dimension-counting argument above. Now the monster. Pick any basis vector, say one of the $e_\alpha$'s — call it $u$ — and define $f$ by $f(u)=1$, $f(e_\beta)=0$ for every other basis vector $e_\beta\ne u$, extended $\mathbb{Q}$-linearly: $f(\sum q_i e_{\alpha_i})=\sum q_i f(e_{\alpha_i})$, which here just reads off the coefficient of $u$. This $f$ is additive — adding two reals adds their coordinate vectors, and reading off a single coordinate is additive. It is emphatically not $cx$: $f(u)=1$ but $f$ of every other basis vector is $0$, so $f(u)/u\ne f(e_\beta)/e_\beta=0$. A monster exists, and there are a great many of them — I had a free real value to assign at each of uncountably many basis vectors.

Now I want to know how badly these monsters behave, because the original problem said "find all functions" and I'd like to understand what I've found. Take any additive $f$ that is *not* of the form $cx$, and look at its graph $G=\{(x,f(x)):x\in\mathbb{R}\}$ in the plane. "Not of the form $cx$" means the ratio $f(x)/x$ is not constant: there are two points $x_1,x_2$ (both nonzero) with $f(x_1)/x_1\ne f(x_2)/x_2$. Equivalently the cross term $x_1 f(x_2)-x_2 f(x_1)\ne0$, which is exactly the determinant of the two vectors $v_1=(x_1,f(x_1))$ and $v_2=(x_2,f(x_2))$. Nonzero determinant means $v_1,v_2$ are linearly independent over $\mathbb{R}$ — they form a basis of the plane. And both of them, and all their *rational* combinations, sit on the graph: by $\mathbb{Q}$-linearity,
$$q_1 v_1+q_2 v_2=\big(q_1 x_1+q_2 x_2,\; q_1 f(x_1)+q_2 f(x_2)\big)=\big(q_1 x_1+q_2 x_2,\; f(q_1 x_1+q_2 x_2)\big)\in G$$
for all rationals $q_1,q_2$. So $G$ contains every rational combination of a basis of $\mathbb{R}^2$. Given any target point $(X,Y)$, write it in this basis as $\alpha v_1+\beta v_2$ with real $\alpha,\beta$, then approximate $\alpha,\beta$ by rationals $q_1,q_2$; since the map $(q_1,q_2)\mapsto q_1 v_1+q_2 v_2$ is continuous and $\mathbb{Q}^2$ is dense in $\mathbb{R}^2$, the image points get arbitrarily close to $(X,Y)$. The graph is *dense in the plane*. If I tried to plot a monster, every pixel of the page would be black.

There's a cleaner one-parameter way to see the same thing that I like better. Subtract off the obvious part: replace $f$ by $g(x)=f(x)-f(1)\,x$, still additive, and now $g(1)=0$. Since $g$ is not identically the zero map (otherwise $f=cx$ with $c=f(1)$), there's some $\alpha$ with $g(\alpha)=z\ne0$. For rationals $q,r$, additivity and $g(1)=0$ give $g(q\alpha+r)=g(q\alpha)+g(r)=q g(\alpha)+r g(1)=qz$. So along the rational two-parameter family $q\alpha+r$, the value of $g$ is $qz$ and the input is $q\alpha+r$ — and I can steer these almost independently: choose $q$ so that $qz$ is as close as I like to any desired height $Y$, then choose $r$ so that $q\alpha+r$ is as close as I like to any desired $X$ (the rationals $r$ slide the input freely without changing the height). Dense graph again.

Every wild solution has a graph that is dense in the plane — so near *every* point, in *every* direction, it takes values that swing arbitrarily high and arbitrarily low. It is discontinuous at every single point; it is unbounded above and below on every interval, however short; it is wildly non-monotone. So *any* hypothesis that forbids this dense, everywhere-exploding graph must force the solution back to a clean $cx$. Let me see exactly how little I need.

Suppose first that $f$ is merely continuous at one single point $x_0$ — that's all, continuity at one place. I claim it's then continuous everywhere. Take any point $a$ and any sequence $x_n\to a$. Then $x_n-a+x_0\to x_0$, and additivity lets me decompose
$$f(x_n)=f\big((x_n-a+x_0)+(a-x_0)\big)=f(x_n-a+x_0)+f(a-x_0).$$
The first term tends to $f(x_0)$ by continuity at $x_0$; the second is a constant. So $f(x_n)\to f(x_0)+f(a-x_0)=f(x_0)+f(a)-f(x_0)=f(a)$, i.e. $f$ is continuous at $a$. Continuity propagates from one point to all points — that's special to additive functions, where translation by $a-x_0$ moves any point to any other while only shifting $f$ by a constant. Now continuity everywhere finishes it: for any real $x$, take rationals $q_n\to x$; then $f(x)=\lim f(q_n)=\lim c q_n=cx$, using $f=cx$ on the dense rationals. So a monster cannot be continuous even at a single point — which I already knew from the dense graph, but now I've nailed the mechanism: continuity at $x_0$ confines the graph to a thin horizontal sliver near $x_0$, and a dense graph has no slivers.

What if instead $f$ is just *bounded* on some interval — not continuous, merely $|f|\le M$ on some $[a,b]$ with $a<b$? The dense graph already says this is impossible for a monster (a dense graph is unbounded over every interval), so this should force linearity too, and I'd like the direct argument because it shows boundedness is genuinely as strong as continuity here. Boundedness on $[a,b]$ transfers to an interval around $0$: for $t\in[0,b-a]$ write $t=x-a$ with $x\in[a,b]$, so $f(t)=f(x)-f(a)$, bounded by $M+|f(a)|=:M'$; and by oddness $f$ is bounded by $M'$ on $[-(b-a),0]$ too. So $|f|\le M'$ on $[-\delta,\delta]$ with $\delta=b-a>0$. Now I get continuity at $0$ out of nothing but this bound and the integer-scaling law. For any $t$ with $|t|<\delta/N$, the point $Nt$ lies in $[-\delta,\delta]$, so
$$|f(t)|=\tfrac1N\,|f(Nt)|\le \tfrac{M'}{N}.$$
Letting $N\to\infty$, the right side goes to $0$ while the allowed window $\delta/N$ also shrinks: for any $\varepsilon$, pick $N>M'/\varepsilon$, and then $|f(t)|<\varepsilon$ whenever $|t|<\delta/N$. Since $f(0)=0$, that is exactly continuity at $0$ — and I just showed continuity at a point forces $f=cx$. So a bound on any interval, with no continuity assumed at all, already tames the monster, by squeezing the value at small $t$ using $f(Nt)=Nf(t)$ to import the far-off bound back to the origin.

Monotonicity is even quicker and doesn't go through continuity. If $f$ is nonincreasing, then $-f$ is additive and nondecreasing, so it is enough to handle the nondecreasing case. Suppose $f$ is nondecreasing. Then $c=f(1)\ge f(0)=0$. For any real $x$, trap it between rationals: pick $p_n\uparrow x$ and $q_n\downarrow x$ with $p_n\le x\le q_n$ and both $\to x$. Monotonicity gives $f(p_n)\le f(x)\le f(q_n)$, i.e. $c p_n\le f(x)\le c q_n$ since $f=cx$ on rationals. As $n\to\infty$ both bounds converge to $cx$, so by squeezing $f(x)=cx$. If the original $f$ was nonincreasing, the same argument applied to $-f$ gives $-f(x)=(-c)x$, hence again $f(x)=cx$. A monster can't be monotone because it can't be trapped — its graph straddles every horizontal line over every interval.

That leaves the subtlest hypothesis, and the one that reveals what's really going on: measurability. Suppose $f$ is merely Lebesgue measurable — no continuity, no monotonicity, no boundedness assumed anywhere. I'll show even this forces $f=cx$. The target, as before, is continuity at $0$: it suffices to show that for every $\varepsilon>0$ there is a $\delta>0$ with $|f(h)|<\varepsilon$ for all $|h|<\delta$, because that gives continuity at $0$, hence (by the translation argument) everywhere, hence $f=cx$. A measurable $f$ may be horrible, but Lusin's theorem says it is "nearly continuous": on the unit interval I can find a compact set $F\subset[0,1]$ with measure $m=\mu(F)>\tfrac34$ on which $f$ is continuous. On the compact set $F$ continuity is uniform, so for my given $\varepsilon$ there is an $\eta>0$ with $|f(x)-f(y)|<\varepsilon$ whenever $x,y\in F$ and $|x-y|<\eta$. Set $\delta=\min(\eta,\,2m-1)>0$. Now take any $h\in(0,\delta)$ and look at $F$ and its translate $F-h=\{x-h:x\in F\}$. Both lie in $[-h,1]$, a set of measure $1+h$. If $F$ and $F-h$ were disjoint, then
$$\mu\big(F\cup(F-h)\big)=\mu(F)+\mu(F-h)=2m,$$
but this union sits inside $[-h,1]$ of measure $1+h<1+\delta\le 2m$ — a measure too big to fit. So they cannot be disjoint: there is a point $x_0\in F\cap(F-h)$. Then $x_0\in F$ and $x_0+h\in F$ (because $x_0\in F-h$ means $x_0+h\in F$), and $|x_0-(x_0+h)|=h<\eta$, so by uniform continuity $|f(x_0+h)-f(x_0)|<\varepsilon$. But additivity collapses that difference: $f(x_0+h)-f(x_0)=f(h)$. So $|f(h)|<\varepsilon$ for all $h\in(0,\delta)$; by oddness the same holds for negative $h$. That is exactly the continuity at $0$ I wanted. Hence a measurable additive function is $cx$.

Two large copies of $F$, shifted by any sufficiently small $h$, are forced to overlap because they're each too fat to avoid each other inside a slightly larger interval — and the overlap point manufactures a pair $x_0,\,x_0+h$ on which uniform continuity controls $f(h)$. The monster has nowhere to hide once $f$ is measurable, because measurability is precisely enough regularity to summon a large set where $f$ behaves, and additivity then spreads that good behavior to the origin. So every wild solution is *non-measurable*. The construction really did lean on the Choice-given Hamel basis: I never wrote down a concrete basis for $\mathbb{R}$ over $\mathbb{Q}$; I used an existence theorem to get one. In any setting where every set of reals is Lebesgue measurable, the Lusin argument would make every additive function linear, and these monsters would vanish.

So here is the complete picture I've arrived at. The additive equation, on its own, sees $\mathbb{R}$ only as a vector space over $\mathbb{Q}$; the solutions are exactly the $\mathbb{Q}$-linear maps $\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}$, and a $\mathbb{Q}$-linear map is determined by arbitrary values on a Hamel basis. The tame ones — values that happen to be a common multiple of the basis vectors — are the $f(x)=cx$. All the others are wild: their graphs are dense in the plane, so they are discontinuous everywhere, unbounded on every interval, non-monotone, and non-measurable. The equation alone cannot tell tame from wild because it knows nothing of the order, topology, or measure of $\mathbb{R}$ — those are the multiplicative/metric structures it ignores. The moment any one of those structures is re-imported, even in the mildest dose — continuity at a single point, monotonicity, boundedness on one short interval, or mere measurability — the dense graph becomes impossible and the solution snaps back to the unique line $f(x)=cx$, $c=f(1)$.

Without any regularity, the solution set is precisely the set of $\mathbb{Q}$-linear maps $\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}$. Concretely: fix a Hamel basis $\{e_\alpha\}$ of $\mathbb{R}$ over $\mathbb{Q}$ (it exists by the Axiom of Choice / Zorn's Lemma, and is uncountable). For any prescription of real values $\{v_\alpha=f(e_\alpha)\}$, the map
$$f\Big(\sum_i q_i e_{\alpha_i}\Big)=\sum_i q_i v_{\alpha_i}\qquad(q_i\in\mathbb{Q})$$
is a solution, and every solution arises this way. Among these, the ones with $v_\alpha=c\,e_\alpha$ for a single constant $c$ are the linear $f(x)=cx$; every other choice gives a function whose graph $\{(x,f(x))\}$ is dense in $\mathbb{R}^2$, hence is everywhere discontinuous, unbounded on every interval, non-monotone, and non-Lebesgue-measurable. There are uncountably many of these wild solutions.

With *any one* of the regularity hypotheses — $f$ continuous at even a single point, or monotone, or bounded on some interval, or Lebesgue measurable — the only solutions are
$$f(x)=cx,\qquad c=f(1).$$
The chain of it: the equation forces $f=cx$ on $\mathbb{Q}$ but says nothing across $\mathbb{Q}$-independent directions; a Hamel basis (Choice) lets each direction be set freely, breeding uncountably many additive solutions; all the non-linear ones have dense graphs and so are pathological; and any whiff of order/topology/measure on $\mathbb{R}$ contradicts a dense graph and collapses $f$ back to the single line through the origin.
