I start with the product form, because that is the only part of the exact posterior that remains cheap. I can write the target as `p(x) proportional to prod_i f_i(x)`, and each `f_i` has a local meaning, but the product itself quickly becomes impossible to normalize or marginalize. If an observation factor is a mixture, the exact belief can become a mixture with exponentially many components. If the variables are hybrid or continuous, exact messages can become arbitrary functions. The graph can look innocent while the belief state is already too large to carry.

My first temptation is to choose a tractable family `Q` and fit one global `q(x)` to the whole product. That gives a clean variational problem, but it hides the factorization at the moment when I need it most. A global `KL(q || p)` objective can be optimized, yet it often prefers to avoid parts of the posterior mass that the approximation cannot represent well. A Laplace approximation is even more local: it trusts one mode and its curvature. These methods produce a single tractable object, but they do not tell me how an individual troublesome factor should be repaired in its current context.

Assumed-density filtering gets closer to the mechanism I need. I process one factor, multiply it into the current approximation, and project the result back to the tractable family. In an exponential family, minimizing `KL(exact one-step posterior || q)` preserves the chosen sufficient-statistic expectations, so a Gaussian projection preserves mean and covariance. This is attractive because one exact factor times a simple current belief can be much easier than the full product. But the method is one pass. When I approximate the first factor, I do not yet know what the later factors will make important. If I throw away information too early, I have no way to ask that first decision to adapt. The answer depends on the order in which the data arrive, which is the wrong behavior for a batch posterior.

I need to keep the local nature of filtering but remove its irreversibility. The small reinterpretation that changes the problem is this: after a filtering step, the new approximate posterior divided by the old approximate posterior is itself an approximate factor. So I do not need to think of filtering as merely updating one global belief. I can keep any exact prior or base term untouched, build a tractable replacement `t_i(x)` for each remaining exact factor `f_i(x)`, and combine them as `q(x) proportional to p0(x) prod_i t_i(x)`. Once I have stored the approximation factor by factor, I can revisit any old local decision.

Now I ask what it means to revisit factor `i`. I cannot simply multiply `f_i` into the current `q`, because the current `q` already contains the old approximation to `f_i`; that would count the same evidence twice. So the first move has to be deletion. I remove the old site and form the cavity `q_{-i}(x) proportional to q(x) / t_i(x)`. This cavity is the current belief supplied by all the other factors. It is the context in which factor `i` must now justify its approximation.

With the old local contribution removed, I put the true factor back in exactly. I form `p_hat_i(x) proportional to f_i(x) q_{-i}(x)`. This tilted distribution is not the full posterior, and it is usually not in the tractable family, but it is locally exact for the factor I am repairing. That is the crucial reduction: instead of computing moments under the whole product of all exact factors, I compute moments under one exact factor multiplied by a tractable context. In common Gaussian latent models, this can collapse to a one-dimensional or low-dimensional moment calculation.

The tilted distribution still needs to be compressed. I project it back into the chosen family by minimizing `KL(p_hat_i || q_new)`. For an exponential family, the stationarity condition is moment matching: `E_{p_hat_i}[phi(x)] = E_{q_new}[phi(x)]`. This projection direction matters because it punishes a proposed `q_new` for missing mass of the tilted distribution. In a Gaussian family, I match the tilted mean and covariance. In a disconnected family, I match local marginals. I am not optimizing a single global lower bound here; I am repeatedly asking local exact experiments to agree on the expectations that I decide to preserve.

After the projection, I need to turn the projected global belief back into a local replacement for factor `i`. Since the cavity already accounts for every other site, the repaired site is the ratio `t_i_new(x) proportional to q_new(x) / q_{-i}(x)`. In natural parameters this is just subtraction: projected parameters minus cavity parameters. If the replacement is too aggressive, I can damp this change in natural-parameter space. Then I recombine the sites and move to another factor.

The fixed point now has a precise meaning. For every `i`, if I delete site `i`, insert the exact `f_i`, and project the resulting tilted distribution, I recover the same retained moments as the current global `q`. The global approximation is therefore not a one-shot fit to an intractable posterior. It is the solution of many local consistency tests: each approximate factor is repeatedly removed, checked against the true factor in the current context, projected by moments, and written back.

This also explains why loopy belief propagation appears as a special case. If my approximation is fully disconnected and my site factors are messages into individual variables, the same deletion and local projection logic becomes marginal matching in a loopy message-passing scheme. I keep the iterative consistency idea but let the messages be compressed in richer exponential-family forms, such as correlated Gaussians, so I am not limited to exact discrete belief states.

I also have to respect the failure modes. The fixed points can be multiple, the fast iterations can diverge, and a multimodal posterior can make a Gaussian approximation look confidently wrong. In a Gaussian implementation, the site factors need not be proper densities, so negative or otherwise unusable site-variance updates can appear; practical code may skip a bad cavity, restrict a negative site variance, or damp the natural-parameter change. These are not incidental engineering details; they reveal that the method is a local consistency repair procedure, not a guaranteed convex descent to a unique global optimum. Its distinctive insight is still sharp: replace the impossible global posterior calculation with repeated local deletion, exact tilted update, and moment projection until the local moment agreements stop changing.
