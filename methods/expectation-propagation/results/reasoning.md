I start with the product form, because that is the only part of the exact posterior that remains cheap. I can write the target as `p(x) proportional to prod_i f_i(x)`, and each `f_i` has a local meaning, but the product itself quickly becomes impossible to normalize or marginalize. If an observation factor is a mixture, the exact belief can become a mixture with exponentially many components. If the variables are hybrid or continuous, exact messages can become arbitrary functions. The graph can look innocent while the belief state is already too large to carry.

My first temptation is to choose a tractable family `Q` and fit one global `q(x)` to the whole product. That gives a clean variational problem, but it hides the factorization at the moment when I need it most. A global `KL(q || p)` objective can be optimized, yet it often prefers to avoid parts of the posterior mass that the approximation cannot represent well. A Laplace approximation is even more local: it trusts one mode and its curvature. These methods produce a single tractable object, but they do not tell me how an individual troublesome factor should be repaired in its current context. I want a procedure whose unit of work is one factor, not the whole product.

Assumed-density filtering gets closer to that. I process one factor, multiply it into the current approximation, and project the result back to the tractable family. In an exponential family, minimizing `KL(exact one-step posterior || q)` preserves the chosen sufficient-statistic expectations, so a Gaussian projection preserves mean and covariance. This is attractive because one exact factor times a simple current belief can be much easier than the full product.

Before I trust it, I want to see one update in the simplest possible case, where the family contains the factor and projection should lose nothing. Take a 1D Gaussian cavity `N(x; m_c=0.7, v_c=2.0)` and a Gaussian factor `N(x; y=1.5, v_obs=0.5)`. In natural parameters the cavity is `(r_c, p_c) = (0.35, 0.5)` and the factor is `(r_f, p_f) = (3.0, 2.0)`. Their product is exactly Gaussian, so the projected posterior has natural parameters `(r_t, p_t) = (3.35, 2.5)`, i.e. mean `1.34`, variance `0.4`. That is the mechanics working as intended. But I notice the projected belief is a global object; what I actually want to store, if I am going to revisit factors later, is the factor's own contribution. If I subtract the cavity back out, `(r_t - r_c, p_t - p_c) = (3.0, 2.0)`, which is exactly the true factor `(r_f, p_f)`. So the natural-parameter subtraction recovers the factor itself in the conjugate limit. Good: the bookkeeping is consistent.

That subtraction is not a cosmetic step. It warns me about a trap. Suppose I had already stored a contribution for this factor and I tried to "refresh" it by multiplying the true `f_i` straight into the current global `q`. If the stored contribution were already the correct `(3.0, 2.0)`, the refreshed precision would be `p_c + p_f + p_f = 0.5 + 2.0 + 2.0 = 4.5`, whereas the correct precision is `2.5`. The factor's evidence gets counted twice. So any scheme that revisits a factor must first delete the old contribution before reinserting the true one. The cavity is not an optimization; it is what keeps me from double-counting.

The deeper problem with plain filtering is that it is one pass. When I approximate the first factor, I do not yet know what the later factors will make important, and if I discard information too early I have no way to ask that first decision to adapt. I want to know how badly this actually bites, so I build a small batch model where exact inference is reachable by quadrature and one factor is genuinely nonconjugate. Take three factors on a scalar `x`: `f1 = N(x; 0, 1)`, `f3 = N(x; 1, 1)`, and a heavy-tailed `f2(x) = 1/(1 + (x-4)^2)`, an unnormalized Cauchy pulling toward 4. By dense quadrature the true posterior has mean `0.79259` and variance `0.54085`.

Now I run ADF as a single sweep, projecting to a Gaussian after each factor, and I do it in two orders. Folding in the order `(f1, f2, f3)` gives posterior mean `0.79792`; folding in the order `(f3, f2, f1)` gives `0.81086`. The two answers differ by about `0.013`, even though they describe the same batch posterior. That is the irreversibility made concrete: the moment at which the nonconjugate `f2` meets the running Gaussian depends on how much Gaussian mass has already accumulated, and reordering the data changes the result. For a batch posterior that order-sensitivity is the wrong behavior, and now I have measured it rather than merely worried about it.

So I want the local nature of filtering without its irreversibility. The reinterpretation that buys this is the subtraction I already saw: after a filtering step, the new approximate posterior divided by the old one is itself an approximate factor. So I do not have to treat filtering as updating one opaque global belief. I can keep any exact prior or base term untouched, build a tractable replacement `t_i(x)` for each remaining exact factor `f_i(x)`, and combine them as `q(x) proportional to p0(x) prod_i t_i(x)`. Once the approximation is stored factor by factor, I can revisit any old local decision.

What does it mean to revisit factor `i`? I already know I cannot multiply `f_i` into the current `q`, because `q` still contains the old `t_i`; that is exactly the `4.5`-versus-`2.5` double count. So the first move is deletion. I remove the old site and form the cavity `q_{-i}(x) proportional to q(x) / t_i(x)`. This cavity is the belief supplied by all the other factors, and it is the context in which factor `i` must now justify its approximation.

With the old local contribution removed, I put the true factor back in exactly: `p_hat_i(x) proportional to f_i(x) q_{-i}(x)`. This tilted distribution is not the full posterior, and it is usually not in the tractable family, but it is locally exact for the factor I am repairing. That is the crucial reduction: instead of computing moments under the whole product of all exact factors, I compute moments under one exact factor multiplied by a tractable context. In the Gaussian latent case `q_{-i}` is a single Gaussian, so for a scalar likelihood the tilted moments are a one-dimensional integral.

The tilted distribution still needs to be compressed. I project it back into the family by minimizing `KL(p_hat_i || q_new)`. For an exponential family the stationarity condition is moment matching: `E_{p_hat_i}[phi(x)] = E_{q_new}[phi(x)]`. This projection direction matters because it punishes a proposed `q_new` for missing mass of the tilted distribution. In a Gaussian family I match the tilted mean and covariance; in a disconnected family I match local marginals. I am not optimizing a single global lower bound; I am repeatedly asking local exact experiments to agree on the expectations I decide to preserve. Then I turn the projected global belief back into a local site by the same subtraction as before: `t_i_new(x) proportional to q_new(x) / q_{-i}(x)`, which in natural parameters is `eta(q_new) - eta(q_{-i})`. If the change is too aggressive I damp it in natural-parameter space, `eta_i <- (1-rho) eta_i + rho eta_i_new`, then recombine and move on.

I should check that this revisiting actually cures the order problem I measured. On the same three-factor model I keep a site for each factor and sweep until the sites stop changing. It converges in two sweeps to mean `0.79259` and variance `0.54085` — matching the quadrature posterior to the precision of my grid, where ADF was off by `0.005` in one order and disagreed with itself by `0.013` across orders. There is no remaining order dependence: the fixed point is defined by mutual consistency of the sites, not by a sweep order. I will be honest about why the match here is essentially exact: with only one nonconjugate factor, the Gaussian that matches that factor's tilted moments happens to carry the true posterior's mean and variance, so projection costs nothing in this particular model. I would not expect EP to be exact with several nonconjugate factors or a multimodal posterior; what I have verified is the structural claim, that revisiting removes the irreversibility, not that the Gaussian family is always adequate.

The fixed point now has a meaning I can state from the construction. For every `i`, deleting site `i`, inserting the exact `f_i`, and projecting the tilted distribution returns the same retained moments as the current global `q`; otherwise that site would still be moving. So the global approximation is not a one-shot fit to an intractable posterior. It is the simultaneous solution of many local consistency tests, each one a delete / insert-true-factor / project / write-back cycle.

This makes me suspect loopy belief propagation is the same procedure in a disconnected family, so I check the smallest case where I can compare against an exact answer. Take two binary variables with unaries `phi1 = (0.6, 0.4)`, `phi2 = (0.3, 0.7)` and an attractive pairwise factor `psi = [[2,1],[1,2]]`. The exact marginals from the full joint are `x1 = (0.53425, 0.46575)` and `x2 = (0.32877, 0.67123)`. Running sum-product on the single edge — messages `m_{2->1} = psi phi2`, `m_{1->2} = psi^T phi1`, beliefs `phi * message` normalized — gives `x1 = (0.53425, 0.46575)` and `x2 = (0.32877, 0.67123)`, identical to exact. With a fully disconnected Bernoulli family and per-variable sites, EP's cavity-and-marginal-match step is exactly this message passing, so the two coincide here; on a tree both are exact, and on loops both become the same approximation. The advantage I keep by viewing it as EP is that the sites need not be exact discrete tables: they can be correlated Gaussians or other exponential-family forms, so the same iterative consistency carries to models BP cannot directly handle.

I also have to respect the failure modes, and the experiments above already hint at them. The fixed points can be multiple, the fast iterations can diverge, and a multimodal posterior can make a Gaussian approximation look confidently wrong — the order-independence I verified is a property of fixed points, not a promise that the sweeps reach one. In a Gaussian implementation the site factors need not be proper densities, so negative or otherwise unusable site-variance updates can appear; that is exactly the regime my conjugate check stayed safely inside, and outside it practical code may skip a bad cavity, restrict a negative site variance, or damp the natural-parameter change. These are not incidental engineering details; they are why the method is best understood as a local consistency repair procedure rather than a guaranteed convex descent to a unique global optimum. What survives all of this is the move I verified piece by piece: replace the impossible global posterior calculation with repeated local deletion, exact tilted update, and moment projection, iterated until the local moment agreements stop changing.
