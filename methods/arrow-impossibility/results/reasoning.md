I want a rule that turns many rankings into one ranking. Not a utility sum, not a compromise over intensities, just rankings in and a ranking out. With two alternatives this feels easy: look at how people compare the two and choose the side that wins, or use any other monotone pairwise rule. The trouble begins when the output has to rank three alternatives at once. I can decide `x` versus `y`, `y` versus `z`, and `x` versus `z` separately, but the three answers have to fit together as a transitive social ordering.

The first thought is pairwise majority, because it treats each pair cleanly and democratically. But I should check whether the pairwise answers actually cohere. Take three voters with the rankings `x > y > z`, `y > z > x`, `z > x > y`. On `x` versus `y`: voters one and three rank `x` above `y`, voter two ranks `y` above `x`, so majority gives `x > y`. On `y` versus `z`: voters one and two rank `y` above `z`, so `y > z`. On `x` versus `z`: voters two and three rank `z` above `x`, so `z > x`. Putting them together: `x > y`, `y > z`, `z > x`. That is a strict cycle, not an ordering at all. So the failure is not some incidental defect of majority rule. The deeper problem is that pairwise decisions can each be locally reasonable and globally inconsistent.

Borda count avoids that by letting the position of every alternative affect every comparison. If `z` moves around in the voters' rankings, the point totals for `x` and `y` can change. That is exactly what independence of irrelevant alternatives refuses to allow. IIA wants the social comparison `x` versus `y` to depend only on the individual comparisons `x` versus `y`. It is a clean fairness axiom, but it removes the extra context Borda uses to keep the ranking coherent. So neither baseline survives both constraints at once: majority keeps IIA but loses transitivity, Borda keeps transitivity but loses IIA. The question is whether anything other than a trivial rule can hold both.

Let me set up the object carefully. I have a rule `F`. For every profile of strict individual rankings, `F` returns a strict social ranking. If every voter has `x > y`, then society has `x > y` (Pareto). If two profiles induce the same individual comparisons on `x` and `y`, then the social comparison of `x` and `y` is the same in the two profiles (IIA). I want to see whether any nondictatorial rule can survive those three requirements together with transitivity of the output.

Before reasoning in the general case, I want to know what I am even hunting for. Three alternatives, a handful of voters — that is small enough to just enumerate. IIA means the rule reduces to one pairwise aggregator per pair: a function from the pattern of who-prefers-`a`-over-`b` to the social verdict on that pair. Pareto pins the two unanimous patterns. With two voters the only non-unanimous patterns on a pair are "voter 0 prefers `a`, voter 1 prefers `b`" and the reverse, so each pair has exactly two free bits, six free bits total across the three pairs, and I can sweep all 64 combinations, then for each candidate rule check on all 36 profiles whether the three pairwise verdicts ever form a 3-cycle. I run that sweep. Out of all the rules consistent with Pareto and IIA, exactly two also stay transitive on every profile. And when I ask which voter each of those two rules copies on every pair and profile, the answer is: one of them copies voter 0 always, the other copies voter 1 always. So with three alternatives and two voters, the only rules satisfying all four conditions are the two dictatorships. I push the same enumeration to one and three voters: I get exactly one rule for one voter, two for two voters, three for three voters, and in every case each surviving rule is a dictatorship by some single voter. That is a strong signal. It does not prove the general theorem, but it tells me the conclusion to aim for is dictatorship, and that nondictatorial survivors are not merely rare but entirely absent in these cases. Now I need an argument that does not rely on enumeration, because the real claim is for arbitrarily many voters.

I should stop thinking in terms of formulas for scores and start thinking in terms of power over a pair. Say a coalition `S` is decisive for `x` over `y` if, whenever every voter in `S` ranks `x > y` and every voter outside `S` ranks `y > x`, society ranks `x > y`. Pareto says the full voter set is decisive for every ordered pair. The empty set cannot be decisive, because if everyone ranks `y > x`, Pareto gives society `y > x`.

The useful question is whether decisiveness for one pair spreads to other pairs. Suppose `S` is decisive for `x` over `y`. Can I force `S` to be decisive for `x` over `z` too? Build a profile where voters in `S` rank `x > y > z`, and voters outside `S` rank `y > z > x`. Let me read off the three pairwise patterns. On the pair `x,y`: voters in `S` have `x > y`, voters outside have `y > x` — that is exactly the coalition pattern in which `S` is decisive, so society has `x > y`. On the pair `y,z`: voters in `S` have `y > z` (from `x > y > z`) and voters outside also have `y > z` (from `y > z > x`), so everyone agrees and Pareto gives `y > z`. Transitivity of the social ordering then forces `x > z`. Now look at how the individuals split on `x,z` in this same profile: voters in `S` have `x > z` (from `x > y > z`), voters outside have `z > x` (from `y > z > x`). That is precisely the decisiveness pattern for `x` over `z`.

Let me sanity-check that the construction is internally consistent before leaning on it, by instantiating it with `S` a single voter among three. Voter 0 in `S` gets `x > y > z`; voters 1 and 2 outside get `y > z > x`. Pair `x,y`: voter 0 has `x>y`, voters 1,2 have `y>x` — decisiveness pattern, yes. Pair `y,z`: all three have `y>z` — unanimous, yes. Pair `x,z`: voter 0 has `x>z`, voters 1,2 have `z>x` — decisiveness pattern for `x` over `z`, yes. The patterns line up exactly as the argument needs; nothing about it depended on `S` being a singleton, only on `S` being the decisive set. So decisiveness is not trapped on the original pair: once a coalition can force `x > y`, Pareto plus transitivity plus IIA lets it force `x` over a third alternative, and by IIA this holds in *every* profile with that `x,z` split, not just the constructed one. Relabeling the alternatives runs the same construction for any pair, so decisiveness for one ordered pair gives decisiveness for every ordered pair. The third alternative is what carries the power across; with only two alternatives there is no carrier and the argument has nothing to work with, which fits the fact that two-alternative majority rule is perfectly fine.

Now I need a decisive coalition that is as small as possible. The full voter set is decisive. Among all decisive coalitions, choose a minimal one, call it `S`. If `S` has exactly one voter, then that voter is decisive for every pair, hence a dictator. The only possible escape is that a minimal decisive coalition has at least two voters, so I want to show that case is impossible.

Assume `S` has at least two voters. Split it into a nonempty proper subset `A` and the rest `B = S \ A`. Pick three alternatives `x,y,z`. I want a profile that makes `A`, `B`, and everyone outside `S` pull against each other so that transitivity forces one of the smaller pieces `A` or `B` to be decisive — contradicting minimality of `S`.

Let voters in `A` rank `x > y > z`, voters in `B` rank `y > z > x`, voters outside `S` rank `z > x > y`. Let me actually read off what is forced. On `y` versus `z`: voters in `A` have `y > z`, voters in `B` have `y > z`, so all of `S` has `y > z`; voters outside have `z > y`. That is the decisiveness pattern for `S`, so society ranks `y > z`. Good, that part is clean.

Now I need society's verdict on the other two pairs. On `x` versus `y`: voters in `A` have `x > y`, voters in `B` have `y > x`, and outside voters (`z > x > y`) have `x > y`. So the set supporting `x > y` is `A` together with the outsiders. On `x` versus `z`: voters in `A` have `x > z`, voters in `B` have `z > x`, outsiders have `z > x`. I was hoping transitivity would pin one of these and hand me a smaller decisive set. Suppose neither `x > y` nor `z > x` is forced. Strict completeness then gives `y > x` and `x > z`. Together with the forced `y > z`, the social triple is `y > x`, `x > z`, `y > z`: that reads `y > x > z` with `y > z`, which is perfectly transitive. So transitivity does *not* force a contradiction here — the profile is consistent with `S` itself being the minimal decisive set. The split-into-`A`-and-`B` route leaks: it does not isolate a smaller coalition. I need a sharper instrument that does not let the contradiction escape into a consistent ordering.

The pivotal-voter version is the instrument that closes the leak, because it tracks a single voter across a boundary rather than a coalition across a profile. Instead of choosing a minimal decisive coalition abstractly, line the voters up and walk from a profile where everyone ranks `x > y` to one where everyone ranks `y > x`, flipping one voter at a time. Pareto says the social comparison starts as `x > y` and ends as `y > x`. As voters flip one by one, there is a first voter whose flip changes the social comparison from `x > y` to `y > x`. Call that voter `i`. The voters flipped before `i` are already on the `y > x` side; voter `i` is pivotal; voters not yet flipped remain on the `x > y` side.

That such a first flip exists is just the discrete intermediate-value fact — a function on a finite chain that starts True and ends False must change somewhere. I can see it concretely: take three voters all ranking `x` above `y`, and flip them one at a time to rank `y` above `x`; for any rule the social verdict on `x,y` is `x > y` at the start and `y > x` at the end, so it flips at some specific step. There is genuinely a pivotal step. The content of the theorem is not that a pivot exists — that is trivial — but that this single pivotal voter must control *every* pair, not just the one I walked. That is what I have to prove using only IIA, Pareto, and transitivity.

Bring in a third alternative `z`. I want to show voter `i` controls `x` versus `z`. Hold the `x,y` pattern fixed at the pivotal boundary and place `z` freely around it — the unrestricted domain lets me do this. Take the pre-switch profile, where voter `i` still ranks `x > y` and society ranks `x > y`, and arrange `z` so that everyone ranks `y > z`. Concretely: voters before `i` rank `y > z > x` (still `y > x`, as the pre-switch boundary requires for them), voter `i` ranks `x > y > z`, voters after `i` rank `x > y > z`. Check the `x,y` pattern: voters before `i` have `y > x`, voter `i` and the later voters have `x > y` — that is exactly the pre-switch pattern, so society has `x > y`. Everyone has `y > z`, so Pareto gives `y > z`. Transitivity then gives society `x > z`. In this profile voter `i` ranks `x > z`; by IIA the social verdict on `x,z` is fixed by the `x,z` split here, regardless of the rest.

Now the after-switch side, to remove any dependence on how the others split. Take the post-switch profile, where flipping voter `i` makes society rank `y > x`, and arrange `z` so that everyone ranks `z > y`. Voters before `i` rank `z > y > x`, voter `i` ranks `z > y > x` after the flip — wait, I need voter `i`'s `x,z` comparison to be the thing under test, so let voter `i` rank `y > x` for the pivotal flip but keep `x > z` for voter `i`; put `z` below `x` for `i` by using `i`: `y > x > z`? Then `i` has `z` at the bottom and `z > y` fails for `i`. Let me redo this so the unanimous link is `z > y`, not touching `i`'s `x,z` view. The link I need from Pareto is on the pair `y,z`, and the pivotal control is on `x,y`; voter `i`'s view of `x,z` is the unknown I am solving for, so I must not pin it by the same unanimity. So on the after-switch side I make everyone rank `z > y`: voters before `i` rank `z > x > y`? that gives them `x > y`, breaking the post-switch boundary which needs them at `y > x`. The boundary on `x,y` for the pre-`i` voters is `y > x`. To have both `y > x` and `z > y` for those voters I need `z > y > x`; that also gives `z > x`. Voter `i` after the flip needs `y > x` and I want to read off `i`'s `x,z`; put `i` at `y > x > z`, giving `i` the comparison `x > z`. Later voters: `x > y` boundary and `z > y` link means `x > y` and `z > y`; either `x > z > y` or `z > x > y`. Take them `z > x > y`. Now the `x,y` pattern: before-`i` voters `y > x`, voter `i` `y > x`, later voters `x > y` — that is the post-switch pattern (voter `i` now on the `y` side), so society ranks `y > x`. Everyone ranks `z > y`, so Pareto gives `z > y`. Transitivity from `y > x` and `z > y` gives `z > x`. And in this profile voter `i` ranks `x > z` (from `y > x > z`) — yet society ranks `z > x`. Hold on: that says when `i` ranks `x > z`, society can rank `z > x`, which would mean `i` does *not* control the pair. Let me recheck `i`'s ranking. I set `i` to `y > x > z`, so `i` has `x > z`. Society got `z > x`. So this particular after-switch arrangement makes `i`'s `x,z` view and the social `x,z` view disagree.

That disagreement is the signal that I mislabeled the construction, not a refutation — the pre- and after-switch profiles must agree on voter `i`'s `x,z` comparison for the IIA splice to be about a single fixed pattern. In the pre-switch profile `i` had `x > z`; for the after-switch profile I must also keep `i` at `x > z` and read what society does. With `i` at `x > z` throughout, the pre-switch side forced society `x > z`. For the after-switch side I should instead drive the unanimous link the other way so transitivity pushes society toward `x > z` from the `y > x` pivot: I want society `y > x` (pivotal) and a Pareto link that combines with it. If everyone ranks `x > z`... no, that pins `i` trivially by Pareto. The honest reading is simpler: the pre-switch profile already did the work. It produced a profile in which voter `i` ranks `x > z` and, forced by the pivotal `x > y` plus unanimous `y > z` plus transitivity, society ranks `x > z`. By IIA, *every* profile in which the `x,z` split matches that one yields social `x > z`. The role of the after-switch side is not to re-derive `x > z`; it is to confirm that when voter `i` instead ranks `z > x`, society ranks `z > x`, so that `i`'s `x,z` view fully determines the outcome rather than being one of two cases that happen to agree. Let me build that. Take voter `i` ranking `z > x` and everyone unanimous on, say, the link that pairs with the post-switch pivot. Voters before `i` at `y > x` boundary, voter `i` at `y > x` (post-switch) and now `z > x`, later voters at `x > y` boundary. Put the unanimous link as `z > y`: before-`i` voters `z > y > x`, voter `i` `z > y > x` (post-switch `y > x`, and `z > x`), later voters `z > x > y` (boundary `x > y`, link `z > y`). The `x,y` pattern: before-`i` `y > x`, `i` `y > x`, later `x > y` — post-switch pattern, society `y > x`. Unanimous `z > y`, Pareto `z > y`. Transitivity from `z > y` and `y > x` gives `z > x`. Voter `i` ranks `z > x`, society ranks `z > x` — agreement. So the two cases are: `i` ranks `x > z` ⟹ society `x > z`, and `i` ranks `z > x` ⟹ society `z > x`. By IIA those two patterns exhaust the `x,z` splits as far as voter `i`'s entry is concerned, and the others' entries do not matter, so voter `i` is decisive for the pair `x,z`.

That is the step I was missing, and it cost me a wrong arrangement to find it: the pivotal voter for `x,y` is decisive for `x,z`, with the third alternative again acting as the carrier and IIA freezing the conclusion across all profiles with the same split. Relabeling alternatives runs the identical construction for every pair, so voter `i` is decisive for every ordered pair. Since social rankings are strict and complete, whenever `i` ranks `a > b`, decisiveness forces society to rank `a > b`; pair by pair, society copies voter `i`'s entire ranking. That is dictatorship, and it matches what the small-case enumeration found: the only rules that survived there were exactly the dictatorships, one per voter.

So the mild axioms do not merely rule out majority rule or Borda count. They rule out every nondictatorial social welfare function on an unrestricted domain with at least three alternatives. The mechanism is now concrete rather than slogan-shaped. IIA turns each pair into an independent binary problem; unrestricted domain lets me build profiles that place a third alternative wherever the argument needs it; Pareto supplies the unanimous link on a second pair; transitivity ties the supposedly independent pairwise answers together. The moment a single pairwise comparison flips, the third alternative lets that local pivot propagate to all pairs, and the only configuration that survives the propagation is one fixed individual supplying every verdict. Pairwise independence is too strong precisely because a coherent social ordering cannot be assembled from separately insulated pairwise judgments unless one voter supplies them all.
