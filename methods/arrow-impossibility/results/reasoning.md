I want a rule that turns many rankings into one ranking. Not a utility sum, not a compromise over intensities, just rankings in and a ranking out. With two alternatives this feels easy: look at how people compare the two and choose the side that wins, or use any other monotone pairwise rule. The trouble begins when the output has to rank three alternatives at once. I can decide `x` versus `y`, `y` versus `z`, and `x` versus `z` separately, but the three answers have to fit together as a transitive social ordering.

The first thought is pairwise majority, because it treats each pair cleanly and democratically. But the Condorcet cycle is staring at me: one group has `x > y > z`, another has `y > z > x`, another has `z > x > y`. Majority gives `x > y`, `y > z`, and `z > x`. That is not a social ordering. So the problem is not that pairwise majority is badly designed in some incidental way. The deeper problem is that pairwise decisions can be locally reasonable and globally inconsistent.

Borda count avoids that by letting the position of every alternative affect every comparison. If `z` moves around in the voters' rankings, the point totals for `x` and `y` can change. That is exactly what independence of irrelevant alternatives refuses to allow. IIA is trying to make the social comparison `x` versus `y` depend only on the individual comparisons `x` versus `y`. It is a clean fairness axiom, but it removes the extra context Borda uses to keep the ranking coherent.

So I have a rule `F`. For every profile of strict individual rankings, `F` returns a strict social ranking. If every voter has `x > y`, then society has `x > y`. If two profiles induce the same individual comparisons on `x` and `y`, then the social comparison of `x` and `y` is the same in the two profiles. I want to see whether any nondictatorial rule can survive those three requirements.

I should stop thinking in terms of formulas for scores and start thinking in terms of power over a pair. Say a coalition `S` is decisive for `x` over `y` if, whenever every voter in `S` ranks `x > y` and every voter outside `S` ranks `y > x`, society ranks `x > y`. Pareto says the full voter set is decisive for every ordered pair. The empty set cannot be decisive, because if everyone ranks `y > x`, Pareto gives society `y > x`.

The useful question is whether decisiveness for one pair spreads to other pairs. Suppose `S` is decisive for `x` over `y`. Can I force `S` to be decisive for `x` over `z` too? Build a profile where voters in `S` rank `x > y > z`, and voters outside `S` rank `y > z > x`. On the pair `x,y`, the coalition pattern is exactly the one in which `S` is decisive, so society has `x > y`. On the pair `y,z`, everyone ranks `y > z`, so Pareto gives `y > z`. Transitivity gives `x > z`.

Now compare this profile with any other profile in which voters in `S` rank `x > z` and voters outside `S` rank `z > x`. IIA says the social comparison of `x,z` depends only on those individual `x,z` comparisons. In the constructed profile it was `x > z`; therefore in every such profile it is `x > z`. So `S` is decisive for `x` over `z`.

That already shows something sharp. Decisiveness is not trapped on the original pair. Once a coalition can force `x > y`, Pareto plus transitivity plus IIA lets it force `x` over a third alternative. A symmetric construction spreads it again: decisive for one ordered pair implies decisive for every ordered pair. With at least three alternatives, the third alternative is the carrier that transfers power.

Now I need a decisive coalition that is as small as possible. The full voter set is decisive. Among all decisive coalitions, choose a minimal one, call it `S`. If `S` has exactly one voter, then that voter is decisive for every pair, hence a dictator. The only possible escape is that a minimal decisive coalition has at least two voters.

Assume `S` has at least two voters. Split it into a nonempty proper subset `A` and the rest `B = S \ A`. Pick three alternatives `x,y,z`. I want a profile that makes `A`, `B`, and everyone outside `S` pull in a cycle so that transitivity forces one of the smaller pieces to be decisive.

Let voters in `A` rank `x > y > z`. Let voters in `B` rank `y > z > x`. Let voters outside `S` rank `z > x > y`. Look at `x` versus `y`: everyone in `S` has `x > y`, and everyone outside `S` has `x > y` as well? Wait, outside voters with `z > x > y` also have `x > y`, so this does not isolate `S`; it gives unanimity on `x > y`. That is too weak. I need the pair controlled by all of `S` against the outside, and the other two pairs split `S`.

Try again. Let voters in `A` rank `x > y > z`. Let voters in `B` rank `y > z > x`. Let voters outside `S` rank `z > x > y`. On `y` versus `z`, all voters in `S` rank `y > z`, while all outside voters rank `z > y`. Since `S` is decisive, society ranks `y > z`.

Now society must place `x` relative to `y` and `z`. If society ranks `x > y`, then in this profile the coalition that supports `x > y` against `y > x` is exactly `A` together with outside `S`: voters in `A` have `x > y`, voters in `B` have `y > x`, and outside voters have `x > y`. That set is not a subset of `S`, so it does not immediately contradict minimality. This route is messy.

The pivotal-voter version is cleaner. Instead of choosing a minimal decisive coalition abstractly, line voters up one by one and move from a profile where everyone ranks `x > y` to one where everyone ranks `y > x`. Pareto says the social ranking starts as `x > y` and ends as `y > x`. As voters are switched one at a time, there is a first voter whose switch changes the social comparison from `x > y` to `y > x`. Call that voter `i`. The voters before `i` are already on the `y > x` side; voter `i` is pivotal; voters after `i` remain on the `x > y` side.

Being pivotal for `x,y` should make `i` a dictator, but I have to prove it without smuggling in more than IIA and transitivity. Bring in a third alternative `z`. At the pivotal step, fix a profile with three blocks: earlier voters rank `z > x > y`; voter `i` ranks `x > y > z`; later voters rank `x > y > z` before the switch, and after the switch voter `i` is changed so that `z > x > y` or `z > y > x` as needed while the pairwise views are controlled.

The exact rankings are less important than the pairwise constraints I can impose because the domain is unrestricted. I can hold fixed the `x,y` pattern at the pivotal boundary and freely place `z` around it. Before voter `i` changes the `x,y` comparison, society has `x > y`; after voter `i` changes it, society has `y > x`.

To show voter `i` controls `x` versus `z`, make the voters before `i` rank `z > x`, make voter `i` rank `x > z`, and make the voters after `i` rank `x > z`. Arrange all voters before the pivotal switch to keep the same `x,y` pattern that gives society `x > y`, and put `y` below `z` for everyone so Pareto gives `z > y`. Then society has `x > y` and `z > y`. That alone does not order `x,z`.

I need the transitivity squeeze to put the unknown pair in the middle. If voter `i` ranks `x > z`, I want social `x > y` from the pre-switch pivotal profile and social `y > z` from Pareto, so transitivity gives `x > z`. This works if everyone ranks `y > z`. I can arrange that while keeping the pivotal `x,y` pattern: earlier voters can rank `y > z > x`, voter `i` can rank `x > y > z`, and later voters can rank `x > y > z`. Before `i` switches, the `x,y` pattern is exactly the one with society `x > y`. Everyone has `y > z`, so Pareto gives `y > z`. Transitivity gives `x > z`.

By IIA, any profile with voter `i` ranking `x > z` and the other voters split as in that constructed `x,z` pattern must socially rank `x > z`. Now use the after-switch side to remove the dependence on how the other voters are split. Put earlier voters and later voters wherever needed around `x,z`, but keep voter `i` as the only common mover; the same construction transfers the pivotal boundary from `x,y` to `x,z`. What emerges is that voter `i` is decisive for `x` over `z`.

The same argument with labels permuted gives voter `i` decisive for every ordered pair. Since social rankings are strict and complete, if `i` ranks `a > b`, decisiveness gives society `a > b`. Pair by pair, society copies voter `i`'s entire ranking. That is dictatorship.

The proof is not saying that voters' rankings are recombined and one voter happens to get extra weight. It is sharper than that. IIA turns each pair into an independent binary problem; unrestricted domain lets me build profiles that place a third alternative wherever the proof needs it; Pareto supplies unanimous links; transitivity ties the supposedly independent pairwise answers together. The pivotal voter for one pair cannot stay local. The moment a pairwise comparison flips, the third alternative lets that local pivot propagate to all pairs.

So the mild axioms do not merely rule out majority rule or Borda count. They rule out every nondictatorial social welfare function on an unrestricted domain with at least three alternatives. Pairwise independence is too strong because it denies the rule access to the cross-alternative information needed to maintain a coherent social ordering, while transitivity still demands coherence. The only way to satisfy both is for all pairwise decisions to come from the same fixed individual.
