Let me start from the thing that actually hurts. I have a discrete problem — minimize c(x) over x in X — and the only practical handle I have is local search: define moves, define the neighborhood S(x) of a trial solution, and from x step to whichever neighbor lowers the cost. Travelling salesman with 2-opt, partitioning with swaps, 0/1 problems with bit flips — same skeleton, just swap the move. Steepest descent runs this to the floor: keep taking the best improving move until none exists. And there it stops. That stopping point is a local optimum, and in any combinatorial landscape worth the name it is generically not the global optimum. That's the whole pain. The method is held hostage by wherever it happened to start and by the shape of the neighborhood, with nothing to do once the improving moves run out.

The obvious reflex is multi-start: run descent from a hundred random starts, keep the best. But that throws away everything each run learned — a hundred independent descents into a hundred basins, no transfer between them, coverage left entirely to luck, and luck is exponentially thin when X is huge. I want a *single* search that keeps going past the first local optimum, not a portfolio of blind restarts.

So suppose that when descent stalls, instead of stopping, I take the best move anyway — the one that worsens the cost the least. Hansen's steepest-ascent/mildest-descent does exactly this for combinatorial programming: at a local optimum, climb out by the mildest possible worsening step rather than restarting. The primitive feels right. Keep moving by the *best available* move even when "best available" means "least bad." Then local optimality need never be detected or handled as a special case, because the rule "take the best move in S(x)" is well-defined whether or not an improving move exists; the search just keeps choosing.

But I should actually run this before trusting it, because something nags at me about what happens the instant I leave a local optimum. Let me take a small TSP instance — 8 random points in the unit square, 2-opt moves — descend to a genuine local optimum, then from there let the unconstrained "take the best move, worsening if necessary" rule run six steps and print the tour at each step. The local optimum it lands on is the tour (0,4,6,1,5,2,3,7) with length 3.2003. Continuing by best-available-move from there, the sequence of lengths is:

    3.2003   (0, 4, 6, 1, 5, 2, 3, 7)
    3.2885   (0, 4, 6, 1, 5, 2, 7, 3)
    3.2003   (0, 4, 6, 1, 5, 2, 3, 7)
    3.2885   (0, 4, 6, 1, 5, 2, 7, 3)
    3.2003   (0, 4, 6, 1, 5, 2, 3, 7)
    3.2885   (0, 4, 6, 1, 5, 2, 7, 3)

It is an exact two-cycle, and the tours match identically, not just in length — step 0 equals step 2 equals step 4. So the worry was real and it isn't a corner case: it's the *generic* behaviour. The reason is now obvious from the numbers. I leave the local optimum x* (3.2003) by the mildest-worsening move to its neighbor y (3.2885). At y, the cheapest move in S(y) is the one straight back to x* — x* was a local optimum, lower than all its neighbors, and y is one of those neighbors, so the single cheapest thing to do from y is return. I take it, I'm back at x*, the best move is again the mildest worsening step to y, and round it goes. So escaping the local optimum and not cycling back into it are not two separate problems — they're the same problem. Any rule loose enough to let me leave the basin is, by the same looseness, happy to walk me right back in. The mildest-descent primitive is useless without a companion that refuses the immediate undo.

What is the undo, precisely? It's the *reverse* of the move I just made. If I moved by s, the trap is the move s⁻¹ that satisfies s⁻¹(s(x)) = x. So the minimal discipline is: having just applied s, forbid s⁻¹ on the next step. Then from y the back-move to x* is off the table, and I'm forced to take the *second*-best move in S(y) — which carries me somewhere genuinely new.

Is forbidding only the last reverse enough? No. Picture a slightly bigger loop. I go x⁰ → x¹ → x², and from x² the cheapest move is back to x¹ (forbidden, good), but the *second* cheapest is back to x⁰ along a different move whose reverse I'm not blocking — a 3-cycle, or 4-cycle, sneaking around the single forbidden edge. The fix has to scale: forbid the reverse of not just the last move but the last several. If I keep the reverses of the last t moves forbidden, then for the next t steps I cannot directly undo any of them, which steadily pushes me *away* from the recent solution states. The intuition I'll lean on: the chance of cycling back to a past solution falls off with distance from it, where distance is measured in moves taken since I was there, *provided* no intervening move is allowed to reverse one of its predecessors. Forbid the reverses of the last t moves and that proviso holds over a t-step horizon, so short loops can't re-close and, for t large enough, the likelihood of return effectively vanishes. It's a likelihood argument, not an ironclad guarantee — with the cheap attribute proxy I'll introduce shortly, a longer loop could still sneak around the forbidden reverses; a hard no-revisit guarantee would need more elaborate bookkeeping than this. For escaping local optima and killing the short cycles, the recency window is what does the work.

So I'll carry a set T — the forbidden moves — and define it as the reverses of the moves made in the last t iterations:

    T = { s⁻¹ : s = s_h, h > k − t },

with k the current iteration. Maintaining it is trivial: it's a sliding window. At each step I add the reverse of the move I just made and drop the one that's now t iterations old — a circular list of length t. The selection rule becomes "best move in S(x) that is not in T," and the search never references local optimality at all; it just keeps choosing the best admissible move. I'll call T the *tabu* list and t its *tabu tenure*.

Now the parameter t. There's a real tension here and I should think it through rather than guess. Push t small: I forbid almost nothing, so I have the widest possible latitude to chase the genuinely best moves — but I barely suppress cycling; a loop a hair longer than t slips through. Push t large: I crush cycling hard, the window of forbidden reverses spans any short loop — but I've also forbidden a big chunk of S(x) on every step, which can starve the search of the good moves and force it through long detours of mediocre ones, and worse, can drive it into regions purely because everything attractive is tabu. So the goal is *not* "make t large." The goal is the smallest t that reliably prevents cycling, because every unit of tenure beyond that is latitude I'm giving up.

How long does a loop actually need to be blocked? The thing I'm fighting is short cycles — the 2-cycle I just watched re-close, and the small loops that re-close quickly. Those need a tenure on the order of a handful of steps, not hundreds. And there's an independent hint about that order of magnitude: a simple recency memory that holds about seven items is, per Miller, roughly the span a basic memory mechanism carries usefully — about seven chunks, give or take two. That's not a derivation, but it's a striking coincidence to keep in mind: the amount of recent history I need to remember to keep a combinatorial search from looping back on itself sits in the same small range as the amount of recent history a person holds in short-term memory. So on the simpler problems where each move toggles one element — covering, scheduling, partitioning — a tenure of around seven, somewhere in the 5-to-12 band, is the regime I expect to be both anti-cyclic and unconstraining. I'll treat t as a small constant there and not as something to be cranked up. I should be careful not to over-read the number, though: the right tenure scales with how the neighborhood and the move attributes are built. A move that records a coarse attribute (one edge of a 2-opt swap, where the same edge can recur in many tours) blocks a wider class per unit of tenure than a move that flips a single named variable, so a richer move structure like 2-opt over a large tour may want a longer, problem-size-dependent list rather than a flat seven. The principle is "smallest t that stops cycling," not the literal value — I'll want to confirm that a small fixed t actually does stop the cycling once the whole thing is assembled, rather than assume it.

Now a practicality that turns out to reshape the whole design. To check "is s in T?" I've been imagining storing whole moves, or whole solutions. For a TSP tour that's a permutation; storing and comparing full tours for the last t steps, across a neighborhood of size O(n²) candidates each step, is heavy. I don't actually need the full identity of a move to recognize its reverse. A 2-opt move is characterized by the two edges it deletes and the two it adds; to block the reverse it may be enough to record an *attribute* — say, one of the edges the move just put into the tour — and forbid any move that would touch that edge again until its tenure expires. So instead of a tabu set of moves, I keep tabu *attributes*, and a move is tabu if it touches a currently-tabu attribute. Cheap to store (an edge, a pair, a (set, weight) tag), cheap to check (look the attribute up in a small table), and one recorded attribute can forbid a whole class of moves at once.

But "one of the edges" is sloppy, and this is exactly the place to get something backwards, so let me work the bookkeeping out on a concrete move rather than hand-wave it. Take the tour [0,1,2,3,4] and the 2-opt that reverses positions i+1..j with (i,j)=(1,3); it yields the neighbor [0,1,3,2,4]. Comparing edge sets directly: the edges present in the original but gone from the neighbor are {1,2} and {3,4} — those are *removed*; the edges present in the neighbor but absent from the original are {1,3} and {2,4} — those are *added*. Now the decisive question: which edges does the *reverse* move — the one that takes [0,1,3,2,4] back to [0,1,2,3,4] — delete? Searching for the (i',j') on the neighbor that returns to the original gives (1,3) again, and the edges it removes are {1,3} and {2,4}. Those are precisely the edges my forward move *added*. So to block the undo I must make the *added* edges tabu, recorded as "do not delete these for the next t iterations"; then any later candidate that would delete a still-tabu edge is exactly an attempted reversal, and gets blocked. Had I instead tabu-ed the deleted edges {1,2},{3,4}, the reverse move — which doesn't delete those, it re-adds them — would sail straight through, and I'd be back in the two-cycle. That's the trap, and the explicit edge comparison is what rules it out; I would not have wanted to guess this one.

That cheapness of the attribute proxy is also a liability, and recognizing it forces the next piece. Attribute-based tabu over-forbids. If I make "don't delete edge e" tabu for t steps, I block *every* move that would delete e — including moves that would lead somewhere I have never been and that might be excellent. The attribute is a proxy for "you're undoing recent history," but the proxy is coarse: it sometimes flags moves that aren't undoing anything, that genuinely improve on everything I've ever seen. Forbidding those is just leaving quality on the table for no anti-cycling benefit.

So I need an escape valve — a condition under which a move's tabu status is overridden because the move is, on the merits, too good to pass up. Call it an *aspiration* criterion. The cleanest one falls right out of the worry I just raised: if a tabu move would produce a solution better than the best I have ever found, accept it regardless of its tabu status. And I can argue this never reintroduces cycling, which is the only thing tabu was protecting against — because a solution strictly better than my all-time best is, by definition, one I have never visited, and there is no loop to fear in stepping somewhere strictly new. So "aspiration by objective": override tabu when c(s(x)) < c(x*), the incumbent best. Tabu restrictions and aspiration are mirror images — a move is admissible if the tabu condition does *not* apply, and *also* admissible if the aspiration condition *does* apply — two faces of the same admissibility test, one constraining, one releasing.

I can push the aspiration idea further if I want finer control, and it's worth seeing the general shape even if the simple form is what I'll use. The thing I want to detect is "this transition has never been made before," because that's exactly the condition that makes overriding tabu safe. So key a record on the *objective value of the solution being left*: let BEST(q) record, for the objective value q, the best value that has previously been reached by a move *starting from* a solution of value q — i.e. the best already-seen transition out of the q-level. Initialize it pessimistically. When a move carries me from an x with c(x)=q to s(x), I update BEST(q) toward c(s(x)), the value just produced. Then a tabu move that leaves a (possibly different) solution of value q can override its tabu status if it would reach a value better than BEST(q): beating the best previously-recorded transition out of the q-level means I'm achieving a transition that has not occurred before, which is safe against cycling for the same reason as before. The all-time-incumbent rule is the blunt special case — "is this better than everything, ever?" — while the BEST(q) record lets aspiration fire on transitions that are new relative to their own objective class without being global records. For the core method, "better than the incumbent best" is enough and is what I'll code.

One subtlety I should pin down, because it's easy to get backwards: what exactly should the tabu list prevent — *reversal* of a move, or *repetition* of a move? They sound similar and they are not. Repetition-prevention says "don't make the same move s again." But that's both too weak and wrong for my purpose: right after applying s I could apply s⁻¹ and slide straight back to where I was — repetition isn't blocked, reversal is what I needed to block, and repetition-prevention permits exactly the 2-cycle I watched re-close above. A list built to prevent repetition rather than reversal fails to stop the immediate undo, which is the whole point. So the tabu list prevents *reversal*: don't undo what you recently did. (The strongest possible anti-cycling condition would be "never return to a solution visited before," but that requires remembering and checking every visited solution — too much memory and effort. Reversal-prevention is the tractable proxy that the attribute/list structure naturally supports, and aspiration patches its over-restriction.)

Let me also be honest about *why* I'm taking the best move aggressively rather than copying simulated annealing's slow randomized descent, because they're solving the same escape problem differently and I should know my reason. Annealing accepts worsening moves with probability exp(−Δ/Temp) and cools slowly, betting that a gentle, randomized descent lands in a good basin; all improving moves have equal status, worsening ones start acceptable and get extinguished, and crucially it keeps *no memory* — diversity comes only from randomization. I'm doing the opposite on both counts. On aggressiveness: many combinatorial problems are actually solved optimally by making the best available move at each step (shortest-path and matroid-intersection structures behave this way), so there's no general virtue in dawdling toward a local optimum — and since local optimality is no longer a barrier for me, I have no reason to slow down approaching it. I'd rather spend the bulk of my effort in regions where solutions are good than linger proportionately in poor ones. On memory: randomization buys diversity by *forgetting* the past, and that's the very thing that lets annealing re-explore ground it has already covered. I'm choosing to buy escape and diversity by *remembering* the past instead — the tabu list is memory used to be different from the recent past without relying on accident.

Which surfaces the realization that memory is the real engine here, and the tabu list is only its shortest-horizon use. The list is *recency* memory: it answers "what did I touch in the last t steps?" and uses that to escape local optima and prevent cycling. But a trajectory of good solutions carries more information than its last t moves, and I'm currently throwing it away. Two longer-horizon uses suggest themselves, and they pull in opposite directions.

First, intensification with intermediate-term memory. As the search runs it keeps finding good solutions, and good solutions tend to *share features* — particular edges that keep appearing in short tours, particular assignments that recur. If I record the features common to a pool of the best solutions seen and then bias the search to favor those features for a while — restricting or penalizing moves that abandon them — I concentrate effort in the promising region instead of drifting out of it. There's even a free, brutal version for big problems: after some iterations the good solutions only ever use a fraction of the available move-components (in TSP, only a fraction of all edges appear in any good tour), so I can simply discard the never-used components, shrink the problem, and run far more iterations per second on what remains.

Second, diversification with long-term *frequency* memory, which is roughly the reverse. Recency memory asks "what did I just do?"; frequency memory asks "what have I been doing *all along*?" Keep a count, over the entire run, of how often each attribute appears — how many tours each edge has been in. An attribute used constantly is a sign the search keeps circling the same kind of solution. So *penalize* frequently-used attributes: add to the move evaluation a term that taxes high-frequency components, pushing the search to assemble solutions out of pieces it has neglected, into territory it hasn't explored. This is a purposeful restart — a new region reached by reasoning about the search's own history — as opposed to annealing's restart-by-randomness, which can't learn anything from where it's been. Recency-based tabu and frequency-based penalties are the two ends of the same memory idea: forbid the immediate past to escape now; penalize the cumulative past to explore later.

There's one more thing nagging at me on the theoretical side, worth naming even though the simple method doesn't need it. With a fixed tenure t and the attribute proxy, can I *guarantee* I never revisit a solution, and that the whole thing terminates? Not in general — the attribute proxy and a constant t only make return *unlikely*, not impossible, and aspiration deliberately lets some "forbidden" moves through. If I wanted a hard finiteness guarantee I'd have to manage the tabu list more precisely — track exactly which attributes must remain forbidden so that the only way back to an old solution is along a trajectory never traveled, and let the tenure grow with the iteration count when forced, dropping the oldest restrictions first when everything is tabu. That exact bookkeeping is more machinery than I want to carry into the basic method; I'll build the version that relies on a small fixed tenure plus aspiration, and then check whether it suppresses cycling well enough on its own.

Now let me assemble the actual procedure. Start from any x, record it as the incumbent x*. Each iteration: scan the neighborhood S(x); for each candidate, compute its cost and identify the attributes it touches; skip it if any of those attributes is currently tabu *unless* it aspires (beats x*); among the survivors, take the best — improving or not. Apply it, mark its attributes tabu for the next t iterations, and if it beats x* update x*. Loop for a budget of iterations or until no admissible move exists. The skeleton is steepest descent with two changes: I never stop at a local optimum (I take the best *admissible* move, worsening if necessary), and I carry the tabu memory that forbids the recent reversals with an aspiration override.

Let me write it for TSP with 2-opt, since that makes every piece concrete. A move is "reverse the segment between positions i+1 and j"; from the edge-comparison above I know it deletes the two end edges and adds two new ones, and the tabu attributes have to be the two *added* edges, recorded as "do not delete these for the next t iterations" — so any later candidate that would delete a still-tabu edge is exactly an attempted undo. One guard on the loop indices: I take j ≥ i+2, because j = i+1 reverses a one-element segment, which is the same tour back — a degenerate no-op I don't want competing with real moves. Aspiration is "cost beats the global best."

```python
import random, math

def tour_len(tour, D):
    n = len(tour)
    return sum(D[tour[i]][tour[(i + 1) % n]] for i in range(n))

def two_opt_apply(tour, i, j):
    # neighbor: reverse the segment positions i+1..j
    return tour[:i + 1] + tour[i + 1:j + 1][::-1] + tour[j + 1:]

def removed_edges(tour, i, j):
    # the two undirected edges this move deletes (the segment's end edges)
    n = len(tour)
    return frozenset((tour[i], tour[i + 1])), frozenset((tour[j], tour[(j + 1) % n]))

def added_edges(tour, i, j):
    # the two edges this move creates; tabu-ing these is what blocks the reverse,
    # since the reverse move would have to delete them to undo me
    n = len(tour)
    return frozenset((tour[i], tour[j])), frozenset((tour[i + 1], tour[(j + 1) % n]))

def tabu_search(D, n, tenure=7, iters=400, seed=0):
    random.seed(seed)
    cur = list(range(n)); random.shuffle(cur)          # any starting solution
    best, best_cost = cur[:], tour_len(cur, D)         # the incumbent x*
    tabu = {}                       # edge -> iteration until which deleting it is forbidden
    history = [best_cost]
    for k in range(1, iters + 1):
        chosen, chosen_cost, chosen_add = None, math.inf, None
        for i in range(n - 1):
            for j in range(i + 2, n):                   # j>=i+2: skip degenerate no-op moves
                if i == 0 and j == n - 1:
                    continue
                cand = two_opt_apply(cur, i, j)
                cost = tour_len(cand, D)
                r1, r2 = removed_edges(cur, i, j)       # this candidate deletes r1, r2
                is_tabu = tabu.get(r1, 0) >= k or tabu.get(r2, 0) >= k  # undoing a recent add?
                aspire = cost < best_cost               # override: never-seen & better
                if is_tabu and not aspire:
                    continue
                if cost < chosen_cost:                  # best ADMISSIBLE move,
                    chosen, chosen_cost = cand, cost
                    chosen_add = added_edges(cur, i, j)
        if chosen is None:                              # all moves forbidden (demo stops here)
            break
        cur = chosen                                    # take it, improving or not
        for e in chosen_add:                            # forbid deleting the just-added edges
            tabu[e] = k + tenure
        if chosen_cost < best_cost:                     # update incumbent
            best, best_cost = cur[:], chosen_cost
        history.append(best_cost)
    return best, best_cost, history
```

With tenure 7 the incumbent now walks down through 3.9695 → 3.5064 → 3.2885 → 3.2003: it passes *through* the 3.2885 tour that the bare rule kept bouncing back from, instead of getting pinned in the two-cycle there — so the reversal-blocking plus aspiration does what the cycling demo showed was missing. To see how good 3.2003 actually is, I brute-force all 7! tours on this instance: the true optimum is 3.1861, so the search lands within 0.0142 of it. That's honest — a small fixed tenure plus aspiration escapes the cycle and gets very close, but it is *not* guaranteed to hit the global optimum on a single run, which is consistent with everything I argued: return is made unlikely, not impossible, and I held the exact dynamic-list machinery in reserve precisely for the cases where "close" isn't good enough. The causal chain, start to finish: steepest descent dies at local optima, so I let it keep moving by the best available step even when that worsens the cost — but I watched the unconstrained version cycle, snapping back along the reverse of the move it just made into an exact two-cycle, so I forbid the reverses of the last t moves with a sliding tabu list, which converts "escape" and "don't fall back" into one mechanism. A small tenure suffices and is preferable, because tenure beyond what kills short cycles only steals the latitude to chase good moves. Storing whole moves is wasteful, so I tabu cheap *attributes* of moves instead — and the explicit edge comparison fixed which attributes: the *added* edges, since those are exactly what a reversal would delete. Attributes over-forbid, occasionally blocking a move that reaches somewhere new and excellent, so I add an aspiration override that accepts a tabu move when it beats the incumbent, safe because a record-beating solution has never been visited and so cannot close a cycle. That recency memory is the short-horizon use of a more general idea: intermediate-term memory intensifies by favoring features shared among good solutions, and long-term frequency memory diversifies by penalizing over-used attributes to reach new regions — escape, intensify, diversify, all bought by remembering the trajectory rather than randomizing over it.
