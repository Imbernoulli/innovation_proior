## Research question

Test-time scaling — letting a model think in a long chain of thought before answering — has
turned competition math and coding into a proving ground for large language models, and the engine
behind the strongest reasoning models is large-scale reinforcement learning on top of a pretrained
base model. The recipe in principle is simple: sample completions from the current policy, score
each with a verifiable, rule-based reward (correct/incorrect on a math problem with a checkable
answer), and push the policy toward the high-reward completions with a clipped policy-gradient
update. In practice the published reasoning systems hide their training details, and teams trying
to reproduce them from the obvious starting point — Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) on a
strong base model — plateau far below the reported numbers and watch training go unstable in
characteristic ways. The problem is to make long-CoT RL scale under practical constraints: the
policy must keep exploring, minibatches must keep carrying useful gradient signal, responses can
range from a few hundred to tens of thousands of tokens, and length truncation must not corrupt the
verifiable reward. A solution has to be made of standard, auditable pieces — it must run inside an
existing RL framework, on rule-based rewards, with no critic — and it has to hold up over a long
training run, not just the first few steps.

## Background

The setting is policy-gradient RL applied to autoregressive generation. A prompt `q` (a math
problem) with reference answer `a` is drawn from a dataset `D`; the behavior policy `π_old` samples
a completion `o` token by token; a reward scores the completion; and the policy parameters `θ` are
updated to raise the expected reward. The per-token importance ratio

```
r_{i,t}(θ) = π_θ(o_{i,t} | q, o_{i,<t}) / π_old(o_{i,t} | q, o_{i,<t})
```

measures how much more (or less) likely the current policy is to emit token `o_{i,t}` than the
behavior policy that actually generated it; it is the object every clipped policy-gradient method
manipulates. The advantage `Â_{i,t}` says whether that token's continuation was better or worse
than a baseline. The update multiplies the two and ascends.

Several load-bearing facts about this regime are established before any new method:

- **The importance ratio is token-local, while the state drift accumulates along the sequence.**
  Each token's ratio is `exp(log π_θ − log π_old)` at that token's generated prefix. A full
  trajectory ratio would multiply the token ratios, but the clipped LLM objective acts on the
  token-local ratios. Later tokens are still fragile because they are conditioned on longer prefixes
  generated by the behavior policy, so small policy differences have more places to change the
  context before the token is scored. For numerical safety implementations clamp the log-ratio to a
  bounded range (e.g. `[−20, 20]`) before exponentiating.
- **Rule-based (verifiable) rewards avoid reward-model hacking.** On tasks with a checkable answer,
  the outcome reward is computed by a rule — `+1` if the predicted answer is equivalent to the
  ground truth, `−1` otherwise — rather than by a learned reward model, which is known to be
  exploitable (reward hacking). This has been shown effective for activating base-model reasoning
  in theorem proving, programming, and competition math.
- **Group-relative advantages remove the critic.** Instead of a learned value function, one can
  draw a group of `G` completions for the same prompt and use the group statistics as the baseline:
  the advantage of completion `i` is the standardized group reward `(R_i − mean) / (std + ε_num)`,
  broadcast to that completion's response tokens, with `ε_num` only guarding division by zero. This
  is what makes critic-free RL on LLMs cheap, and it is the baseline algorithm teams start from.
- **The KL leash to a frozen reference is a holdover from alignment.** In RLHF the goal is to align
  behavior without drifting far from the initial model, so a KL penalty to a frozen reference is
  added. But a reasoning policy is *expected* to drift far from the base model as it learns to think
  at length; the relevance of an alignment-era KL leash in this regime is an open question.

Diagnostic findings observed when running the critic-free, group-relative baseline at scale on a
strong base model:

- **Policy entropy collapses early.** The entropy of the policy's token distribution drops quickly
  as training progresses; sampled completions within a group become nearly identical. The policy
  becomes deterministic too soon, and exploration — the source of the long-CoT behaviors RL is
  supposed to reinforce — dries up.
- **The mean probability of up-clipped tokens is low** (below ~0.2). Among the tokens whose
  positive-advantage update gets clipped at the upper bound, the typical token has a *small*
  model probability.
- **Zero-advantage groups proliferate.** When every one of a prompt's `G` completions is correct
  (or every one wrong), the group reward has zero standard deviation, so the standardized advantage
  is zero for the whole group and contributes no gradient. The fraction of all-correct groups
  climbs steadily over training, so the number of prompts in a batch that actually carry a gradient
  shrinks, while the batch-gradient variance rises.
- **Response length and entropy drift upward unhealthily**, with the longest responses showing
  low-quality patterns (gibberish, repetition), under the standard sample-level loss reduction.
- **Truncated overlong samples inject reward noise.** When generation is capped at a maximum length
  and overlong completions are truncated and assigned a punitive reward, a sound but long reasoning
  chain is penalized purely for length, confusing the learning signal.

The natural smoothing fact in the background: a clipped surrogate (below) bounds how far the
update can move the ratio, which stabilizes off-policy reuse of sampled data, but the clip is a
blunt instrument — it is symmetric, applied identically per token, and it interacts with the
sign of the advantage.

## Baselines

**PPO — clipped surrogate objective (Schulman et al. 2017).** PPO maximizes a pessimistic,
clipped surrogate of the conservative-policy-iteration objective `L^CPI = E_t[r_t Â_t]`:

```
L^CLIP(θ) = E_t[ min( r_t Â_t,  clip(r_t, 1 − ε, 1 + ε) Â_t ) ],   ε = 0.2 (typical).
```

The clip removes the incentive to move `r_t` outside `[1−ε, 1+ε]`, and the outer `min` makes the
clipped objective a lower bound on the unclipped one, so the policy only "gets credit" for ratio
moves that don't make the objective look better than it should. To first order around `θ_old`
(`r=1`) it equals `L^CPI`; the two diverge as `θ` moves away. The advantage is estimated with a
learned value function via GAE, `Â_t = Σ_l (γλ)^l δ_{t+l}`, `δ_l = R_l + γV(s_{l+1}) − V(s_l)`.
**Gap / limitation.** PPO needs a critic the size of the policy — heavy on memory in the LLM
setting, and hard to train token-accurate when the reward lands only on the last token. The clip
is symmetric and applied per token, so the *upper* bound on a token's probability after an update
is `π_old · (1 + ε)`: for a token that was already probable this is loose, but for a token that was
improbable under the behavior policy it is a tight absolute cap. The behavior of the clip across
the token-probability spectrum, and its effect on a model's exploration over a long run, is not
something the symmetric PPO clip was designed around.

**GRPO — group-relative policy optimization (Shao et al. 2024, DeepSeekMath).** GRPO removes the
critic. For each prompt it samples a group `{o_i}_{i=1}^G` from `π_old`, scores them, and uses the
standardized group reward as the per-token advantage, `Â_{i,t} = (R_i − mean({R})) / (std({R}) + ε_num)`.
It keeps PPO's clipped ratio and adds a KL penalty to a reference policy:

```
J_GRPO(θ) = E[ (1/G) Σ_{i=1}^G (1/|o_i|) Σ_{t=1}^{|o_i|}
              ( min( r_{i,t} Â_{i,t}, clip(r_{i,t}, 1−ε, 1+ε) Â_{i,t} ) − β D_KL(π_θ ‖ π_ref) ) ].
```

The aggregation is **sample-level**: first average the per-token losses *within* a completion
(the `1/|o_i|`), then average across the `G` completions (the `1/G`). **Gap / limitation.** Because
each completion is averaged to a single per-sample number before the outer average, a completion's
tokens are weighted by `1/|o_i|` — a token sitting in a 10,000-token response counts a hundredth as
much as a token in a 100-token response. Over a long-CoT run the effects observed above appear:
entropy collapses under the symmetric clip; all-correct groups give zero standardized advantage and
zero gradient, so the effective batch shrinks; length and entropy drift up while the longest,
lowest-quality responses are under-penalized; and the alignment-era KL leash constrains a policy
that ought to move far from its base. The clip range, the loss reduction, the sampling, and the
length and reward handling in GRPO are each left at their RLHF-era defaults.

**Dual-clip PPO (Ye et al. 2020).** A lower clamp on the loss for negative-advantage tokens (a
constant `c > 1`), to keep a single bad token with a huge ratio from dominating the update. Used as
a stabilizer in some implementations. **Gap / limitation.** It guards the negative-advantage tail
but says nothing about exploration, zero-gradient groups, length weighting, or truncation noise.

## Evaluation settings

The yardsticks already in use for long-CoT math RL, all pre-existing:

- **Competition-math accuracy.** AIME 2024 (American Invitational Mathematics Examination) as the
  headline benchmark, reported as `avg@k` over multiple samples per problem to reduce variance on a
  small test set; broader math suites (e.g. MATH, AMC, GSM8K) as secondary checks.
- **Base policy.** A strong open base model (e.g. the Qwen2.5 family) trained from the pretrained
  checkpoint, not from an instruction-tuned one — the "from base" (R1-Zero-style) protocol.
- **Rollout / training protocol.** Per RL step, draw a batch of prompts and sample a group of `G`
  completions per prompt from `π_old`; verify each with the rule-based reward; compute
  group-relative advantages; run a small number of gradient updates on the clipped objective; copy
  `θ` into `π_old` for the next step. Generation length is capped, and overlong completions are
  truncated. The simpleRL / verifiable-math style datasets (problems with checkable integer or
  closed-form answers) are the training pool.
- **Monitored training-dynamics metrics** (used to detect instability, not as the final score):
  policy entropy, mean token probability, response length, reward, and the fraction of clipped
  tokens — watched over the course of training.

## Code framework

A candidate solution has to plug into an existing critic-free, group-relative RL trainer. The data
pipeline, rollout, reward computation, advantage standardization, optimizer, and outer training loop
already exist. The open slots are a policy-loss function that turns the old/new per-token log-probs
and the advantages into a scalar loss, reward post-processing that turns raw rule-based correctness
into the scalar reward fed to advantage estimation, the response-token mask used by the loss, and
the batch-construction rule used before the loss is evaluated. Two generic utilities exist: a
masked aggregator `agg_loss` that can reduce a per-token loss matrix to a scalar under a choice of
normalization mode, and a masked mean. The shapes are `(batch, response_length)`.
