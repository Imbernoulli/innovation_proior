# Andrew Wiles — self-account quotes (verbatim), with sources

## Source A: NOVA Online "The Proof" interview — https://www.pbs.org/wgbh/nova/proof/wiles.html
(saved: nova_wiles_interview.txt)

- Childhood dream: "I found this one book, which was all about one particular problem—Fermat's
  Last Theorem... Here was a problem, that I, a ten year old, could understand and I knew from
  that moment that I would never let it go. I had to solve it."
- Setting it aside as a researcher: "I realized that the only techniques we had to tackle it had
  been around for 130 years. It didn't seem that these techniques were really getting to the root
  of the problem."
- Ribet 1986 (the route opens): "Casually in the middle of a conversation this friend told me that
  Ken Ribet had proved a link between Taniyama-Shimura and Fermat's Last Theorem. I was
  electrified... this meant that to prove Fermat's Last Theorem all I had to do was to prove the
  Taniyama-Shimura conjecture. It meant that my childhood dream was now a respectable thing to
  work on."
- Method of work: "I used to come up to my study, and start trying to find patterns. I tried doing
  calculations which explain some little piece of mathematics... And sometimes I realized that
  nothing that had ever been done before was any use at all. Then I just had to find something
  completely new."
- The "dark mansion" metaphor: "You enter the first room of the mansion and it's completely dark.
  You stumble around bumping into the furniture, but gradually you learn where each piece of
  furniture is. Finally, after six months or so, you find the light switch, you turn it on, and
  suddenly it's all illuminated... Then you move into the next room and spend another six months
  in the dark."
- "Living in the wrong century": "It could be that the methods needed to take the next step may
  simply be beyond present day mathematics... even if I was on the right track, I could be living
  in the wrong century."
- 1993 breakthrough morning: "it was one morning in late May... I was sitting at my desk thinking
  about the last stage of the proof. I was casually looking at a research paper and there was one
  sentence that just caught my attention. It mentioned a 19th-century construction, and I suddenly
  realized that I should be able to use that to complete the proof."
- The error: "It was an error in a crucial part of the argument, but it was something so subtle
  that I'd missed it completely until that point... it can't really be described in simple terms."

## Source B: Simon Singh, "Fermat's Last Theorem" / BBC Horizon "The Proof" — the Sept 19 1994 fix
(via Wikipedia "Fermat's Last Theorem", quoting Singh; cross-checked with Nautilus)

> "I was sitting at my desk examining the Kolyvagin–Flach method. It wasn't that I believed I
> could make it work, but I thought that at least I could explain why it didn't work. Suddenly I
> had this incredible revelation. I realised that, the Kolyvagin–Flach method wasn't working, but
> it was all I needed to make my original Iwasawa theory work from three years earlier. So out of
> the ashes of Kolyvagin–Flach seemed to rise the true answer to the problem. It was so
> indescribably beautiful; it was so simple and so elegant. I couldn't understand how I'd missed
> it and I just stared at it in disbelief for twenty minutes. Then during the day I walked around
> the department, and I'd keep coming back to my desk looking to see if it was still there. It was
> still there. I couldn't contain myself, I was so excited. It was the most important moment of my
> working life. Nothing I ever do again will mean as much."

## Timeline (cross-checked, Wikipedia "Wiles's proof of FLT" + Singh)
- ten years old: finds the problem in Milton Road library (Eric Temple Bell, "The Last Problem").
- early teens / college: tries it as Fermat might, then 18th-19th c. methods; sets it aside.
- Summer 1986: hears (over iced tea) of Ribet's theorem; begins ~7 years of secret work.
- ~1988-1991: tries horizontal Iwasawa theory for the class-number-formula bound; gets stuck.
- Summer 1991: learns of the Flach-Kolyvagin / Kolyvagin-Flach Euler system; pivots to it; it
  looks "tailor made" for the inductive step.
- June 21-23 1993: announces at the Isaac Newton Institute, Cambridge.
- Aug-Sept 1993: refereeing; Nick Katz pinpoints the gap — the Euler-system bound on the relevant
  Selmer group is not established in general (the system isn't long enough / the bound fails for
  the cases needed).
- 1993-1994: a year of failed repair, alone, then with Richard Taylor (from spring 1994).
- 19 September 1994: the revelation — Kolyvagin-Flach failure is exactly what makes the old
  Iwasawa-theory approach work; the two combined give the class number formula. Taylor-Wiles
  patching (auxiliary primes -> complete intersection) is the technical engine.
- 24 October 1994: submits the two papers; published Annals 141(3), May 1995.
