I have carried this one equation around since I was ten years old. x^n + y^n = z^n has no solution in positive integers for n > 2 — Fermat scribbled that he had a proof and that the margin was too small, and then three hundred years of mathematicians failed to find one. For most of my life I treated it the way I first found it, as a single Diophantine statement to be attacked directly, and that is exactly why I, and everyone before me, got nowhere. The techniques that exist for it — Kummer's cyclotomic fields, regular primes, ideal class numbers — have been around for a hundred and thirty years, and they go after the equation head-on, prime by prime, never reaching infinity. The honest thing to admit is that there is no leverage in the equation itself. If I am ever going to move it, I need to stop looking at the equation and find a deeper object whose general theory, once proved, kills this equation as a by-product. So let me not start from "no solutions"; let me start from "suppose there is one" and ask what kind of object it manufactures.

Suppose a^p + b^p = c^p with p an odd prime and a, b, c positive integers, coprime. What can I build out of that? Frey's suggestion is the thing to seize: take the elliptic curve

    E:  y^2 = x(x - a^p)(x + b^p).

The roots are 0, a^p, -b^p, and the differences between them are a^p, b^p, and a^p + b^p = c^p — all perfect p-th powers. Let me compute its discriminant, because for a curve in this Legendre-type form the discriminant is, up to a power of 2, the product of squared root-differences. I get Delta = 2^{-8}(a^p b^p c^p)^2 = 2^{-8}(abc)^{2p}. Stare at that exponent. The discriminant is a perfect p-th power up to the prime 2. And the conductor — the arithmetic measure of bad reduction — works out to rad(abc), the squarefree radical, so E is semistable. Now what does this do to the Galois representation on the p-torsion? Let rho = rhobar_{E,p}: Gal(Qbar/Q) -> GL_2(F_p). The ramification of this representation at a prime q of bad reduction is governed by how p divides the valuation of Delta there. But every such valuation is a multiple of p — the discriminant is a p-th power away from 2 — so modulo p the ramification at each odd q ≠ p simply vanishes. The representation is unramified at every odd prime away from p, and finite flat at p. This is a wildly, suspiciously, *unnaturally* unramified representation. It is exactly the sort of object that, if it were forced to come from a modular form, would have to come from one of impossibly small level.

That is the lever. Serre made the mechanism precise as the epsilon conjecture — a level-lowering principle for mod-p modular representations — and Ribet proved it in the summer of 1986. The statement I need from it: if a mod-p representation is modular, unramified at q, and finite flat at p, and it occurs at level qN, then it already occurs at level N. Apply that repeatedly to rhobar_{E,p}: the only ramification left is at 2 and p, and the level-lowering strips everything down until, if the Frey curve were modular, its mod-p representation would have to arise from a weight-2 newform of level 2. But the space of weight-2 cusp forms for Gamma_0(2) is zero — X_0(2) has genus zero, there simply are no such forms. So a Fermat counterexample produces a semistable elliptic curve that *cannot* be modular.

Ribet's theorem changes the problem completely. The contrapositive is the whole game: if I can prove that *every* semistable elliptic curve over Q is modular, then no non-modular Frey curve can exist, so no Fermat counterexample can exist, and the Last Theorem falls out. My childhood equation has become a respectable, central conjecture — the modularity of elliptic curves, the conjecture that grew from Shimura and Taniyama. I no longer have to attack the equation. I have to prove modularity for semistable curves, and that is mainstream mathematics, the kind that generates worthwhile results even on the way to failing.

So: prove a semistable E/Q is modular. How do I even get a handle on "modular"? Going at the curve directly — its L-function, its covering by X_0(N) — gives me nothing to compute with. But there is an equivalent formulation that turns the curve into something with structure I can deform and count. By Eichler-Shimura and Deligne, modular eigenforms have Galois representations attached: to f of weight 2, level N there is rho_{f}: Gal(Qbar/Q) -> GL_2(O) with trace(rho(Frob_q)) = a_q(f), det(rho(Frob_q)) = q for q ∤ Np. And E is modular if and only if its own p-adic representation rho_{E,p}: Gal(Qbar/Q) -> GL_2(Z_p) on p-power torsion agrees with one of these rho_f — modularity of the curve is modularity of its Galois representation. So I will study Galois representations, not curves. That is the natural move for me anyway; I have spent years on p-adic representations attached to Hilbert modular forms in the Iwasawa setting, so the language is home ground.

Now which residual prime do I work with? My first instinct is ell = 2, because the Frey curve and the 2-torsion sit so close together; if ell = 2 were tractable it would directly serve the curves Frey uses. I spend several months on the 2-adic representation and it does not yield. The 2-adic case is too degenerate; there is no foothold. Then comes the first real breakthrough, and it is a change of prime. Take ell = 3. The reason is a theorem of Langlands, completed in the case I need by Tunnell: if a continuous odd irreducible complex representation has finite solvable image — dihedral, A_4, or S_4 projectively — then it comes from a weight-one modular form. Read mod 3: PGL_2(F_3) is isomorphic to S_4, so the projective image of an odd irreducible residual representation rho_0 = rhobar_{E,3} is a subgroup of a solvable group; lift its finite image to characteristic zero, apply Langlands-Tunnell, and then use the standard mod-3 weight adjustment to get the weight-2 residual modularity compatible with the local type of E. So in the irreducible mod-3 case I get the bottom of the ladder for free. The reducible mod-3 case is not covered by this; I will have to return to it later.

That changes the shape of the problem entirely. I am no longer trying to prove modularity from nothing. I have a *modular residual representation* rho_0, and I need to climb: show that the actual p-adic representation rho_{E,3}, which reduces to rho_0 mod 3, is itself modular. Inductively, show the GL_2(Z/3^n) representation is modular for every n, and pass to the limit. This is a lifting problem — given that the mod-p representation is modular, are all of its lifts modular? — and lifting problems I might be able to attack, because lifts of a fixed residual representation form a structured family.

How do I organize "all lifts of rho_0"? This is exactly what Mazur built his deformation theory for. Fix rho_0: Gal(Q_Sigma/Q) -> GL_2(k), k a finite field of characteristic p, Sigma a finite set of primes including p, and impose a deformation condition D — fix the determinant, and prescribe the local behavior at each prime (ordinary at p, or flat at p, with controlled ramification elsewhere). Then there is a universal deformation ring R_D, a complete Noetherian local W(k)-algebra, carrying a universal lift rho^univ: Gal -> GL_2(R_D), such that every deformation of type D to any ring A is the pushforward of rho^univ along a unique homomorphism R_D -> A. So R_D *is* the space of all lifts of rho_0 of type D; its tangent space, the reduced cotangent space p_R/p_R^2 dualized, is a cohomology group H^1 of Galois cohomology — a Selmer group — counting the first-order deformations.

On the other side sit the modular lifts. The cusp forms of the relevant level, weight 2, congruent to rho_0 mod p, are organized by a Hecke algebra T, localized at the maximal ideal m corresponding to rho_0. Each modular lift gives a homomorphism, and collectively the modular deformations of type D are parametrized by a quotient Hecke ring T_D. Crucially, every modular lift *is* a lift of type D, so by the universal property there is a canonical surjection

    phi:  R_D ->> T_D.

"Every lift of rho_0 of type D is modular" is *exactly* the statement that phi is an isomorphism: R_D = T_D. If the universal ring of all lifts equals the ring of modular lifts, then there are no non-modular lifts. Mazur had conjectured this kind of R = T in special cases; now it is the engine I must build in general. Prove R_D = T_D, and modularity of all lifts follows, and modularity of E follows.

So I need a criterion to prove a surjection of complete local rings is an isomorphism. Let me think about what would force phi: R ->> T to be injective too. Both rings sit over O = W(k) (or a finite extension), both have a distinguished prime p_R, p_T with quotient O — corresponding to the chosen modular point. A surjection is an isomorphism if it doesn't collapse anything, and the obstruction to collapsing lives in the tangent/cotangent spaces and in the relations. If phi induces an isomorphism on the reduced cotangent spaces p_R/p_R^2 = p_T/p_T^2, and if T is presented with the complete-intersection number of relations — r relations when the cotangent is O-torsion, or r-u after a free rank u is split off — then a clean commutative-algebra argument forces phi to be an isomorphism.

Let me see the commutative algebra in the torsion case first. Choose a regular local power series ring S = O[[x_1,...,x_r]] mapping onto R and T, so the surjection R ->> T corresponds to ideals I_R ⊂ I_T in S. Since T is a complete intersection, I_T is generated by r relations f_1,...,f_r. Choose generators g_1,...,g_s for I_R, and choose an r-tuple among them whose linear terms give the relevant Fitting ideal for p_R/p_R^2. Modulo the square of the maximal ideal of S, write f_i ≡ sum_j a_{ij} x_j and g_i ≡ sum_j b_{ij} x_j for that chosen r-tuple. The Fitting ideal of p_T/p_T^2 is generated by det(a_{ij}); the selected Fitting minor for p_R/p_R^2 is det(b_{ij}). The cotangent isomorphism forces these two determinant ideals to have the same valuation in O. But I_R ⊂ I_T, so each selected g_i has an expression g_i = sum_j r_{ij} f_j. Comparing linear terms gives b ≡ r a at the closed point, hence det(b) = det(r) det(a) up to a unit. Since det(a) and det(b) have the same valuation, det(r) is a unit. The matrix r is invertible, the f_j lie in the ideal generated by the selected g_i, and therefore I_T ⊂ I_R. The reverse inclusion came from the surjection, so I_R = I_T and R = T. If the cotangent has a free part of rank u, I split off u variables and apply the same determinant argument to the torsion quotient, with r-u relations. So: cotangent iso + T a complete intersection => R = T.

That reduces everything to two checkable things: (1) the map is iso on cotangent spaces, and (2) T is a complete intersection. The first is a Galois-cohomology computation — bounding a Selmer group. The second is a ring-theoretic property of the Hecke algebra. So I need a way to *test* whether the Hecke ring is a complete intersection, and a way to *bound* the Selmer group, and I need these two to meet.

For the complete-intersection test, I had already had to prove the Hecke rings are Gorenstein — that comes from the duality on the modular Jacobian, T ≅ Hom_O(T, O) as T-modules, which Mazur established for prime level and which extends. Gorenstein is weaker than complete intersection. I need a criterion that upgrades Gorenstein to complete intersection. Two invariants are floating around in my own calculations, and the question is whether they coincide. The first is the size of the Hecke cotangent space p_T/p_T^2 — call its length l(p_T/p_T^2); the deformation cotangent p_R/p_R^2 maps onto it and is measured by a Selmer group, the number of allowed first-order deformations. The second is a congruence ideal. Let beta: T -> T/p_T = O be the projection to the chosen modular point, and let beta-hat: O -> T be its adjoint with respect to the perfect O-pairings (the Gorenstein duality on T, and the trivial pairing on O). Define the principal ideal (eta_T) = (beta(beta-hat(1))). This eta is exactly the congruence ideal: it measures the congruences between the chosen eigenform f and the other forms in T — a special value, in the L-function tradition. The invariant I had been struggling with on the cohomology side, the Selmer group, should bound the deformation cotangent and therefore the Hecke cotangent; the invariant eta is controlled by congruences. Kunz's paper had struck me years earlier: it suggested using an invariant (this eta) to test for isomorphisms between Gorenstein rings, and a different invariant (the p/p^2 one) to test for complete intersections. Reading Tate's account of Grothendieck duality for complete intersections — buried in section 6 of a Tate paper — I learn that for such rings these two invariants are *equal*. And then it clicks the other way: the equality of the two invariants is not just a consequence of being a complete intersection, it is a *criterion* for a Gorenstein ring to be one.

Let me write the criterion exactly. For a Gorenstein O-algebra T, finite and free over O, with T/p_T = O and p_T/p_T^2 a torsion O-module:

    T is a complete intersection over O   <=>   l(p_T/p_T^2) = l(O/eta_T).

One always has the inequality l(p_T/p_T^2) >= l(O/eta_T). The Gorenstein pairing gives the Fitting-ideal containment Fitt_O(p_T/p_T^2) ⊂ (eta_T). Over a discrete valuation ring, if (pi^a) is contained in (pi^b), then a >= b; and for a finite O-module the valuation of its Fitting ideal is its O-length. So the congruence side eta gives a *lower* bound for the Hecke cotangent length, automatically. The complete-intersection property is precisely the statement that this inequality is an *equality*. The impact of this is enormous: the relationship between the deformation ring and the Hecke ring can be tested using just these two invariants. The deformation side (the Selmer group, the number of Galois representations) and the congruence/L-value side (eta) are forced to meet.

So now the whole proof has a clean architecture. The Selmer group is the cotangent space on the deformation side, p_R/p_R^2. The map R ->> T makes p_T/p_T^2 a quotient of p_R/p_R^2, so

    l(p_T/p_T^2) <= l(p_R/p_R^2) = l(Selmer group).

The automatic inequality gives l(O/eta_T) <= l(p_T/p_T^2). Therefore an upper bound on the deformation cotangent,

    l(p_R/p_R^2) = l(Selmer group) <= l(O/eta_T),

would trap all three lengths:

    l(O/eta_T) <= l(p_T/p_T^2) <= l(p_R/p_R^2) <= l(O/eta_T).

Everything would be equal. The cotangent map would be an isomorphism, T would be a complete intersection by the numerical criterion, and the commutative-algebra argument would give R = T. The entire weight of the proof now rests on one inequality: an *upper* bound on the Selmer group, matching the congruence number eta. This is a generalized class-number formula of the kind familiar in Iwasawa theory — and it is the place where I will spend years.

How do I bound the Selmer group from above by eta? My instinct, from my own work on the main conjecture for totally real fields, is Iwasawa theory. There I had a technique: instead of climbing the standard cyclotomic Z_p-tower of fields and controlling class groups level by level, I replaced it by a sequence of auxiliary primes q_i ≡ 1 mod p^{n_i} with n_i -> infinity, and used the extra ramification at those primes to do the limiting. The hope is to do the analogue here: build a tower, take the projective limit of the Hecke rings, and have that limit be a *power series ring* O[[x_1,...,x_r]] — because if the inverse limit is a power series ring, the complete-intersection property and the bound descend cleanly to each finite level. That is the natural object Iwasawa theory wants.

I hit a wall. Translated into the ring-theoretic, deformation language, the standard Iwasawa method seems to require unknown principles of base change. I need the exact relations between the Hecke rings for the different fields in the cyclotomic tower — not just up to torsion, but exactly — and I do not know how to control them. The projective limit refuses to become a power series ring. There are tenuous hints of a patching argument in the Iwasawa literature, and Hida's construction gives one step toward a power series ring in the ordinary case, but I search and search and cannot find the key. The cyclotomic tower, the very thing my totally-real-fields proof was built on, will not translate.

Then in August 1991 I learn of Flach's construction, and it looks like a way out that does not need the tower at all. Flach has the first step of what could be an Euler system — a Kolyvagin-style compatible family of cohomology classes. An Euler system, if it can be completed, gives the precise upper bound on the size of the Selmer group *directly*, by annihilating cohomology with the classes, no limiting process required. This seems tailor-made. I become convinced an extension of Flach's method is more plausible than my stuck Iwasawa approach, and I abandon the tower. I spend 1991 and 1992 extending Flach's construction into a full Euler system. By the fall of 1992 I believe I have it: the system is long enough, the Selmer bound holds.

There is still the case where rho_0 = rhobar_{E,3} is *reducible*, where Langlands-Tunnell's irreducibility hypothesis fails. For several months I try to repeat the deformation-ring/Hecke-ring machinery directly, and it resists. Then in May 1993, reading Mazur's construction of twisted forms of modular curves, I find the argument I now call the 3-5 switch. If rhobar_{E,3} is reducible, I cannot seed the ladder at 3. Before I move to 5, I need rhobar_{E,5} to be irreducible. If rhobar_{E,5} were reducible too, E would give a rational point on X_0(15); the rational points there are only the cusps and four non-cuspidal points, and those four correspond to non-semistable curves that are already modular for this purpose. So, apart from cases already finished, the semistable curve I still have to treat has irreducible mod-5 representation. Now I twist the full-level-5 modular curve X(5) by rhobar_{E,5}. The curve E itself gives a rational point on a genus-zero component C of this twist. A rational point of C gives an elliptic curve E' with E'[5] ≅ E[5] as Galois modules. I can use Hilbert irreducibility to choose such a point so that rhobar_{E',3} is irreducible, and choose it 5-adically close enough to the original point so that E' (or a quadratic twist) is semistable at 5. Then E' is modular by the p = 3 argument, hence rhobar_{E',5} is modular; because E'[5] ≅ E[5], rhobar_{E,5} is modular; and now the lifting theorem at p = 5 makes E modular. This uses deformations of the elliptic curve itself, not just of the Galois representation, and it is the only place I need that. With the 3-5 switch in hand, every semistable curve is covered, irreducible-at-3 or not. I believe the proof is complete, and I sketch the whole theory in three lectures at Cambridge in June 1993.

And then, in the fall of 1993, it comes apart. In refereeing, Katz presses on the Euler-system argument — patiently, question by question, reading the construction critically with Illusie — and the questions lead me to the problem. The Euler system extending Flach's first step is *incomplete*, and possibly flawed. It does not actually establish the upper bound on the Selmer group in the generality I need. The system is not long enough; the bound I claimed simply isn't there for the cases that matter. The one inequality the entire architecture rests on — the upper bound l(Selmer) <= l(O/eta) — is unproved. This is the most subtle and the most devastating place it could fail, because it is the load-bearing wall.

Now begins the worst year. I try, alone, to repair the Euler system, to make the construction long enough, and I cannot. Taylor joins me in January 1994 and we work on it together. In the spring, frustrated, we turn to a completely different attempt using p = 2, and by the end of August that has reached an impasse too. I am close to accepting that the Euler-system approach is irreparable. In September I decide to take one last look at my attempt to generalize Flach — not even to fix it, but just to understand precisely *why* it fails, to write down the obstruction cleanly.

And in doing exactly that — sitting at my desk examining the Kolyvagin-Flach method, trying only to explain its failure — I see it. The reason the Euler system fails is itself the missing piece. While extending Flach back in 1992 I had been forced to introduce a particular set of *special* auxiliary primes — primes q ≡ 1 mod p^n (not the q ≡ 1 mod p primes I had always used before), chosen so that the residual representation acquires extra deformations at exactly those primes. The Euler system needed them and couldn't make full use of them. But those special primes are *precisely* the primes my old abandoned Iwasawa approach needed and never had. De Shalit, I now realize, has a generalization of Hida's theory that works for these q ≡ 1 mod p^n primes — and Hida's theory was the one thing that gave a step toward a power series ring. Combine de Shalit's theory, generalized, with Galois duality (Poitou-Tate), and I can *glue* the Hecke rings T_{D_n} at the auxiliary levels Q_n together through compatible finite quotients. The patched object lives over a power series ring O[[x_1,...,x_r]] in the limit — the exact object the cyclotomic Iwasawa method demanded and could not produce, now built not by climbing a tower of fields but by stacking auxiliary primes at a fixed field. It is the old idea of picking q_i ≡ 1 mod p^{n_i} with n_i -> infinity to drive the limiting process, but the switch to these special primes is what finally makes it work. Out of the ashes of the failed Euler system rises the answer to the problem. It is so simple and so elegant I stare at it in disbelief.

For each n, choose a finite set Q_n of auxiliary primes q ≡ 1 mod p^n, of controlled size, at which I allow extra ramification in the deformation problem D_{Q_n}; the primes are chosen so that the dual Selmer obstruction is killed and the new local deformation directions are exactly counted. At level Q_n there is a deformation ring R_{Q_n}, a Hecke ring T_{Q_n}, and a Hecke module with an action of the diamond operators coming from the p-power quotients at the q's. I do not need honest transition maps between all these finite levels. I keep only congruent finite quotients modulo p^n and the auxiliary augmentation ideals, and I glue those quotients into a single patched object over a power series ring S_infty = O[[z_1,...,z_r]]. The Hida/de Shalit input gives freeness of the patched Hecke module over S_infty; Poitou-Tate gives the exact count of the variables and relations on the patched deformation side. A depth and dimension count then forces the patched deformation ring and patched Hecke ring to coincide with the expected complete-intersection shape. When I specialize by killing the auxiliary variables, this descends to the minimal Hecke ring T: it is a complete intersection, and the inequality chain gives l(p_T/p_T^2) = l(O/eta_T) and R = T at minimal level. The patching *is* the missing upper bound on the Selmer group, obtained structurally rather than by an Euler system.

With the complete intersection in hand at the minimal level, the criterion fires: T is a complete intersection, the cotangent map is an isomorphism, so R_D = T_D at the minimal level — every lift of the modular residual rho_0 at minimal level is modular. The level-raising and level-lowering machinery (the congruences between Hecke rings at different levels, Hida-Ribet, propagated through the same eta-invariant control) carries modularity from the minimal level up to all allowed levels. So every suitable lift of a modular rho_0 is modular. That is the modularity lifting theorem.

Now assemble the whole thing. Take a semistable elliptic curve E/Q. Look at rho_0 = rhobar_{E,3}. If it is irreducible, Langlands-Tunnell makes it modular; the lifting theorem (R = T) makes rho_{E,3} modular; so E is modular. If rho_0 is reducible, either the X_0(15) exceptional cases are already modular, or rhobar_{E,5} is irreducible and the 3-5 switch produces a curve E' modular via the p=3 argument with rhobar_{E',5} = rhobar_{E,5}; then rhobar_{E,5} is modular, and the lifting theorem at p = 5 makes rho_{E,5} modular, so E is modular. Either way, every semistable elliptic curve over Q is modular. And then the Frey-Ribet reduction closes it: if there were a Fermat counterexample a^p + b^p = c^p, the Frey curve would be semistable, hence modular, hence (by Ribet) give a weight-2 form of level 2, of which there are none. Contradiction. So u^p + v^p + w^p = 0 with p > 3 forces uvw = 0. The Last Theorem is proved.

The causal chain, start to finish: the lone equation gives no leverage, so I trade it — via Frey's curve, Serre's epsilon, and Ribet — for the modularity of semistable elliptic curves; modularity of a curve becomes modularity of its Galois representation, and choosing p = 3 lets Langlands-Tunnell hand me a modular residual representation in the irreducible case, turning the problem into a lifting problem; lifting becomes the assertion that a universal deformation ring equals a Hecke algebra, R = T, which follows when the Hecke ring is a complete intersection and the cotangent map matches, and complete intersection is detected by the numerical criterion that the Hecke cotangent length equals the congruence length, l(p_T/p_T^2) = l(O/eta_T); the automatic inequality l(p_T/p_T^2) >= l(O/eta_T) has to be matched by the Selmer upper bound l(p_R/p_R^2) <= l(O/eta_T), and that bound resisted the cyclotomic Iwasawa method (no base change) and then resisted the Euler-system method (the bound was never really there), until the realization that the special auxiliary primes the broken Euler system had forced on me were exactly what the abandoned Iwasawa approach needed — gluing the Hecke rings at those primes into a patched power-series object delivers the complete intersection structurally, hence R = T, hence the modularity lifting theorem; the 3-5 switch, with the X_0(15) check and the twisted X(5) family, removes the reducible mod-3 case, so all semistable curves are modular, and Fermat's Last Theorem follows.
