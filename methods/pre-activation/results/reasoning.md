Let me start from what's actually nagging me. Residual learning already won the depth argument once. The thing that was broken before — plain deep convolutional nets get *worse training error* as you stack more layers, even though a deeper net contains a shallower one by construction (just set the extra layers to identity), so it can't be a capacity problem, it has to be that SGD can't find the identity-preserving solution — that thing got fixed by the additive shortcut. Fit `F(x) = H(x) − x` instead of `H(x)`, output `F(x) + x`, and if the right answer is near identity the solver just drives `F` toward zero. That preconditioning is what let 100+-layer nets train at all. And yet when I push toward a thousand layers it starts hurting again: the very deep net's training loss falls painfully slowly at the start, and I've seen a 1202-layer net come out *worse* than a 110-layer one. So residual learning didn't fully solve the depth problem; it pushed the wall further out and the wall is still there. I want to understand precisely what is still in the way before I touch anything.

So let me write the unit down in the most general form I can, because I suspect the answer is in a detail I've been treating as fixed. A residual unit takes input `x_l`, and does

  y_l = h(x_l) + F(x_l, W_l),
  x_{l+1} = f(y_l),

where `h` is whatever sits on the shortcut, `F` is the residual branch (a couple of convs with their BNs and ReLUs), and `f` is whatever happens *after* the element-wise addition. In the unit I'm using, `h` is the identity and `f` is a ReLU. Those two choices — identity on the shortcut, ReLU after the add — are the two things I've never questioned. Let me question them by asking what the network would look like if *both* `h` and `f` were exactly the identity, and see what property that buys, because then I'll know what the ReLU might be costing me.

If `f` is identity, then `x_{l+1} = y_l` exactly, so `x_{l+1} = x_l + F(x_l, W_l)`. Now unroll. `x_{l+2} = x_{l+1} + F(x_{l+1}) = x_l + F(x_l) + F(x_{l+1})`, and continuing,

  x_L = x_l + Σ_{i=l}^{L-1} F(x_i, W_i),

for *any* deeper unit `L` and *any* shallower unit `l`. Stare at this. The feature at any deep layer equals the feature at any shallow layer *plus a sum of residuals*. It is additive, all the way through. Contrast that with a plain net, where `x_L` is a *product* of weight matrices applied to `x_0` — roughly `(Π W_i) x_0` ignoring the nonlinearities. A product of many matrices is exactly the thing that explodes or collapses with depth; a sum of terms with `x_l` sitting cleanly out front does not. That clean leading `x_l` is the whole point. Information from layer `l` reaches layer `L` undistorted, by a route that doesn't pass through a single weight layer.

Now the backward pass, because that's where depth really dies. Let `E` be the loss. By the chain rule,

  ∂E/∂x_l = (∂E/∂x_L) · (∂x_L/∂x_l) = (∂E/∂x_L) · (1 + ∂/∂x_l Σ_{i=l}^{L-1} F(x_i, W_i)).

Look at what that "1" does. The gradient at the shallow layer `l` decomposes into two added pieces: one is `∂E/∂x_L` itself, carried straight back from the deep layer with *no* weight-layer Jacobian multiplying it, and the other is the usual term that flows back through the residual branches' weights. The first piece is a gradient highway. And it can't be killed: for the gradient `∂E/∂x_l` to vanish you'd need the bracket to be zero, i.e. the `∂/∂x_l ΣF` term to equal exactly `−1`, and there's no reason for that sum-of-residual-Jacobians to be `−1` for every example in a mini-batch at once. So the gradient at any layer never vanishes even if all the weights are driven tiny. *This* is the property residual learning is really providing — an additive, never-vanishing gradient path between any two layers — and it holds *exactly* only when both `h` and `f` are identity. The moment I put a non-identity `h` or `f` in the way, I'm degrading this. So the question sharpens: how much am I degrading it with the identity shortcut already chosen (fine, `h` is identity) and with the ReLU I left sitting after the addition?

Let me check the shortcut side first, because I want to be sure identity `h` is really necessary and not just convenient — if a richer shortcut were strictly better I'd want it. Replace `h(x_l) = x_l` with the simplest non-identity thing, a scalar gate `h(x_l) = λ_l x_l`. Then `x_{l+1} = λ_l x_l + F(x_l)`, and unrolling the same way,

  x_L = (Π_{i=l}^{L-1} λ_i) x_l + Σ_{i=l}^{L-1} (Π_{j=i+1}^{L-1} λ_j) F(x_i, W_i).

The clean leading `x_l` just picked up a coefficient `Π λ_i`, a product over all the units in between. And the backward pass inherits it:

  ∂E/∂x_l = (∂E/∂x_L) · ((Π_{i=l}^{L-1} λ_i) + ∂/∂x_l Σ (...)).

Now the direct term is `(Π λ_i)·∂E/∂x_L` instead of `1·∂E/∂x_L`. For a deep net, `L−l` is large, so this product is the disaster I was trying to escape: if every `λ_i > 1` it blows up exponentially, and if every `λ_i < 1` it decays exponentially toward zero — which switches the gradient highway *off* and forces the signal back through the weight layers, the exact failure mode the additive shortcut was supposed to prevent. So a constant non-unit scale on the shortcut reintroduces the multiplicative trap. And it generalizes: if `h` is any smooth transform, the direct backward term becomes `Π_{i} h'_i`, a product of the shortcut's derivatives, and any consistent deviation of `h'` from 1 compounds multiplicatively over depth. A 1x1 convolution on the shortcut, a gate, dropout on the shortcut — they're all multiplicative manipulations on the one path that has to stay clean. They might add representational power — a gated or 1x1-conv shortcut *contains* the identity in its solution space, so in principle it can only help — but the issue isn't representation, it's optimization: the product factor makes the problem harder for SGD, and the training error goes up, not down. So identity `h` isn't a convenience, it's load-bearing. Good — `h` stays identity. Wall avoided on the shortcut side; the threat is real but I'm already on the right side of it.

That leaves the after-addition `f`, the ReLU I've been ignoring. My exact additive-highway derivation assumed `f` is identity. But `f` is ReLU, which is *not* identity. So everything I just wrote — the clean `x_L = x_l + ΣF`, the never-vanishing `1` in the gradient — is only an *approximation* of the unit I'm actually running. How bad is the approximation? ReLU after the addition means `x_{l+1} = max(0, y_l)`. Its derivative is 1 where `y_l > 0` and 0 where `y_l < 0`. So the gradient highway — that "1" I was so happy about — actually gets gated by `ReLU'(y_l)`: wherever the pre-addition sum `y_l` is negative, the highway is locally *severed*, the direct term is zeroed, and the gradient is forced back through the weights at that location. At one or two units that's a minor leak. But this is multiplicative across depth in the same way the shortcut scaling was: the highway from layer `L` back to layer `l` is open only where it was open at *every* intervening unit. Over a thousand units, even an occasional truncation per unit compounds, and the clean approximation `x_L ≈ x_l + ΣF` stops being good. That's exactly the symptom I started with — the 1000-layer net learning slowly at the beginning. The after-addition ReLU is the remaining obstruction. I need `f` to be the identity.

But I can't just delete the ReLU. The network needs its nonlinearities; a stack of convs with no activation between units collapses to a linear map and I lose all the representational power. So the real question is: can I make the *after-addition* operation an identity while keeping the same number of BNs and ReLUs in the network? I have a fixed budget of activations to place; I just want to place them somewhere other than on the merge point.

Right now the activation `f = ReLU` sits after the add, so it touches *both* branches of the *next* unit — write out two stacked units and you see `y_{l+1} = f(y_l) + F(f(y_l), W_{l+1})`, the `f(y_l)` appears as the shortcut input *and* inside `F`. It's symmetric: the same activation feeds both paths. What if I make it *asymmetric* — let an activation `f̂` touch only the residual branch `F` and leave the shortcut path bare? Then the next unit would be

  y_{l+1} = y_l + F(f̂(y_l), W_{l+1}).

Now the after-addition operation between `y_l` and `y_{l+1}` is a bare add — exactly the identity I wanted on `f` — and the activation `f̂` hasn't disappeared, it has moved *inside* the residual branch, in front of its weight layers. Rename `y` back to `x` and this is just a residual unit of the form

  x_{l+1} = x_l + F(f̂(x_l), W_l),

which is structurally identical to my clean case (`f` identity), so the exact additive forward recursion and the never-vanishing backward `1` both hold. And `f̂`, the activation I shifted, is now the *pre-activation* of the next unit's weight layers rather than the post-activation of the merge. So "make the after-addition activation asymmetric so it only hits the residual branch" is *the same thing as* "pre-activate the weight layers." That's the unlock: pre-activation isn't a cosmetic reordering, it's the unique way to keep all the activations while turning the merge point into a true identity. Worth noting why this only matters because of the addition: in a plain chain of `N` weight layers there are `N−1` activations between them, and it's meaningless to call any of them "pre" or "post" — they're just the activations between two weights. It's the branch-and-merge structure that breaks the symmetry and makes the *position* of the activation relative to the add matter.

So I'm going to pre-activate. But "pre-activate" still leaves a real choice — there are a BN and a ReLU per weight layer, and I have to decide which of them move in front of the conv and in what order. Let me not guess; let me walk the candidates and let each one's failure tell me what the next one has to fix.

Start by going the *wrong* way deliberately, to calibrate. What if I keep the activation after the add but make `f` the full BN-then-ReLU — "BN after addition"? Now BN sits directly on the merged signal, which means BN is on the clean shortcut path. That's a non-identity operation (it scales by `γ/sqrt(var)` and shifts) right on the highway — the exact thing I argued against on the shortcut side. So this should *break* the clean path, and indeed I'd expect the very thing I'm trying to fix to get worse: the training loss should be hard to reduce at the start because the shortcut is no longer clean. Rejected — it's moving BN onto the path I need bare.

Now go the pre-activation direction but the laziest version: take just the ReLU and move it to *before* the addition, so `f` becomes identity and the ReLU acts on the residual branch's output. "ReLU before addition." This does make the after-add op identity, good. But watch what it does to `F`: the residual branch now *ends* in a ReLU, so its output `F(x) ≥ 0` always. Then in the forward recursion `x_L = x_l + Σ F`, every term I'm adding is non-negative, so the feature is *monotonically non-decreasing* as I go deeper — it can only grow, component-wise, along the depth. But a residual ought to be free to be negative; subtracting is half the point of "fit `H(x) − x`." Forcing `F ≥ 0` cripples what the residual branch can express. So I've fixed the path but broken the branch. Rejected — and the reason is sharp: the residual must range over `(−∞, ∞)`, and a trailing ReLU forbids the negative half.

The lesson from those two: I need the activations *before* the weight layers (so the residual branch can start with a ReLU and *end* with a plain conv, leaving `F` free to be negative), and I must not put BN on the shortcut. So move the ReLU all the way to the front of the residual branch. "ReLU-only pre-activation": each residual branch is ReLU → conv → ... with BN left wherever it was (after the conv). Now the after-add op is identity, and `F` ends in a conv so it can be negative. This should at least match the baseline. But there's a subtlety that should leave performance on the table: that leading ReLU is now *not* paired with a BN in front of it. BN's job is to keep the input to each weight layer normalized; a ReLU on un-normalized activations doesn't get to ride on BN's normalization. So I'd expect this variant to be roughly baseline — it fixed the path but didn't get the normalization benefit. Close, but I haven't gained anything.

So pull the BN forward too. Put *both* the BN and the ReLU in front of the weight layer: each residual branch is BN → ReLU → conv → BN → ReLU → conv. "Full pre-activation." Check it against everything I've learned: the after-addition operation is a bare add, so `f` is identity and the additive highway holds exactly. The residual branch ends in a conv (not a ReLU), so `F` can be negative — branch is free. No BN sits on the shortcut — the path stays clean. And the leading op of each weight layer is now a BN, so *every* weight layer in the network receives a normalized input. This is the variant where every constraint I derived is satisfied at once, and I want to spell out the two distinct payoffs because they come from two different fixes.

The first payoff is ease of optimization, and it's the one I've been chasing: because `f` is now exactly the identity, the forward `x_L = x_l + ΣF` and the backward `∂E/∂x_l = ∂E/∂x_L (1 + ...)` are exact at *all* depths, not approximations that fray at a thousand layers. The gradient highway is never gated by an after-add ReLU. So I'd expect the extreme-depth net to stop crawling at the start of training — the 1000-layer training loss should drop quickly instead of slowly. And I can even see why the original after-add ReLU was only *mildly* bad at moderate depth: in the original unit, the input `x_l` is itself the output of the previous unit's ReLU, so `x_l ≥ 0`, which means the pre-add sum `y_l = x_l + F(x_l)` is negative only when `F` is sufficiently negative to overcome a non-negative `x_l`. That's relatively rare at 100-ish layers — the highway is open most of the time — but at 1000 layers even an occasional truncation per unit compounds along the chain, so the same defect that's invisible at moderate depth dominates at extreme depth. The fix's benefit therefore *grows* with depth, which is exactly the regime I care about.

The second payoff is regularization, and it's a genuinely separate effect from a different rearrangement — the BN moving to the front, not the merge becoming clean. Trace the normalization through the *original* unit: the residual branch ends in `conv → BN`, so BN normalizes `F`'s output, but that normalized output is immediately *added* to the un-normalized shortcut `x_l`. The sum `x_l + BN(F)` is no longer normalized, and that un-normalized sum is what feeds the *next* unit's first weight layer. So in the original design, the weight layers don't actually get normalized inputs — BN's normalization is undone by the addition. In full pre-activation, the BN is the *first* operation each weight layer sees, applied to that weight layer's own input, so every weight layer in the network genuinely receives a BN-normalized input. BN regularizes through the mini-batch noise it injects, and now that regularization is delivered to every weight layer's input properly. I'd expect the signature of regularization: slightly *higher* training loss at convergence (the network is more constrained) but *lower* test error. That's a different fingerprint from the optimization fix — optimization shows up as faster, lower training loss, regularization shows up as the train-test gap shrinking — and full pre-activation gets both, which is why it's the one to keep.

Let me also sanity-check the two failed pre-activation orderings against this normalization lens to make sure I'm not throwing away a simpler design. ReLU-before-addition put a ReLU at the *end* of the branch — wrong, kills the negative half of `F`. ReLU-only pre-activation put ReLU first but left BN after the conv — so the leading op is an un-normalized ReLU, no BN benefit at the weight-layer input. Only BN-ReLU-conv puts a BN at the very front of every weight layer. So the ordering is forced: BN, then ReLU, then conv. Not conv-BN-ReLU (that's post-activation, leaves a ReLU after the add), not BN-conv-ReLU or ReLU-BN-conv (those don't put a normalized input at the conv while keeping the branch able to go negative). BN → ReLU → Conv it is.

Now I have to make this concrete as a block, and a few practical details fall out of the construction rather than being decisions I get to make freely. The basic residual branch is two 3x3 convs, so the unit is: `BN → ReLU → conv3x3 → BN → ReLU → conv3x3`, then add the shortcut, with nothing after the add. The shortcut is a bare identity when the input and output have the same shape. When the stage changes — a stride for downsampling, or a jump in channel count — the identity can't add to a differently-shaped tensor, so I need a 1x1 conv to match dimensions, used *only* there. And here's the subtle part: in a pre-activation unit the very first thing the unit does is `BN → ReLU` on `x_l`. If I'm going to project the shortcut for a dimension change, what should it project — the raw `x_l`, or the pre-activated `ReLU(BN(x_l))`? The clean thing is to share that first BN-ReLU: compute `pre = ReLU(BN(x_l))` once, send it down the residual branch *and* feed the 1x1-conv shortcut from the same `pre`. That keeps the projection seeing a normalized input too, and it's the natural reading of "the activation has moved to the front of the unit and both paths in this unit start from it." So: `pre = ReLU(BN(x))`; residual = `conv2(ReLU(BN(conv1(pre))))`; shortcut = `pre` projected by 1x1 conv if dims change else `x`; output = shortcut + residual. No scaling on either path — the entire analysis says putting a multiplicative factor on the clean path is exactly the mistake, so the residual is added with coefficient 1, no learnable gate.

Two more units need special handling, and they're forced by the same shifting argument, not bolted on. When I derived pre-activation by moving each unit's after-add activation onto the *next* unit's branch, the chain has two loose ends. At the very start of the network there's a stand-alone conv (the stem) feeding the first residual unit; if the first unit begins with `BN → ReLU`, then the stem's conv output is the first thing getting pre-activated, which is fine — but I should make sure that first `BN → ReLU` happens right after the stem before the path splits, which it does in the shared-pre construction. At the very end, the last residual unit's output is a *pre-activation* — it's a raw sum `x_l + F` that hasn't been through a final BN-ReLU, because in pre-activation the activation that "would have" followed it got pushed forward to a next unit that doesn't exist. So before global average pooling and the classifier I need to add one extra `BN → ReLU` after the last unit's addition, to actually activate the final feature. Both special cases are just the natural boundary terms of the asymmetric shift; the interior units are clean.

Let me write the block as I'd actually ship it, filling the empty slot in the backbone's `CustomBlock`. The internal arrangement is the pre-activation ordering I derived; the shortcut is identity or a 1x1-conv projection from the shared pre-activation; nothing follows the add.

```python
import torch
import torch.nn as nn
import torch.nn.functional as F


class CustomBlock(nn.Module):
    """Full pre-activation residual block: BN -> ReLU -> Conv, twice, with a
    clean (identity) after-addition path so the additive forward/backward
    highway x_L = x_l + sum F holds exactly."""
    expansion = 1

    def __init__(self, in_planes, planes, stride=1):
        super().__init__()
        # Activations live BEFORE the weight layers (pre-activation), so the
        # residual branch ends in a conv (F can be negative) and every conv
        # receives a BN-normalized input.
        self.bn1 = nn.BatchNorm2d(in_planes)
        self.conv1 = nn.Conv2d(in_planes, planes, 3, stride=stride, padding=1, bias=False)
        self.bn2 = nn.BatchNorm2d(planes)
        self.conv2 = nn.Conv2d(planes, planes, 3, stride=1, padding=1, bias=False)

        # Shortcut: bare identity when shapes match; otherwise a 1x1 projection
        # (used ONLY for dimension changes), fed from the shared pre-activation.
        self.shortcut = None
        if stride != 1 or in_planes != planes * self.expansion:
            self.shortcut = nn.Conv2d(
                in_planes, planes * self.expansion, 1, stride=stride, bias=False)

    def forward(self, x):
        pre = F.relu(self.bn1(x))                 # shared first pre-activation
        out = self.conv1(pre)                     # BN-ReLU-Conv
        out = self.conv2(F.relu(self.bn2(out)))   # BN-ReLU-Conv (ends in conv: F unbounded)
        # clean add, nothing after it -> after-addition activation is identity
        shortcut = self.shortcut(pre) if self.shortcut is not None else x
        return shortcut + out
```

And the two boundary units, which the backbone owns rather than the block: a `BN → ReLU` right after the stem conv before the first unit, and an extra `BN → ReLU` after the last unit's addition before global average pooling and the linear classifier.

Stepping back to the causal chain. I started stuck: residual learning had pushed the depth wall out but it reappeared near a thousand layers, where the very deep net trains slowly. Writing the unit generally and assuming both the shortcut `h` and the after-add `f` are identity produced an *exact* additive structure — forward, the deep feature is a shallow feature plus a sum of residuals (not a product of weights, so it doesn't explode or collapse); backward, the gradient carries an irreducible `+1` term that is a never-vanishing highway between any two layers. Probing the shortcut with a scalar `λ` showed any non-identity `h` turns that `1` into a product `Π λ_i` that explodes or vanishes with depth, so identity `h` is necessary, not just convenient — and the same multiplicative trap condemns gating, 1x1-conv, and dropout shortcuts. The remaining obstruction was the after-add ReLU: it gates the highway wherever the pre-add sum is negative, harmless at moderate depth (the input is non-negative from the previous ReLU) but compounding to a real slowdown at a thousand layers. Making `f` identity without deleting any activation forced an asymmetric move — let the activation touch only the residual branch — which is identically pre-activation of the next unit's weights. Walking the pre-activation orderings, each failure pointed to the next: BN-after-add puts BN on the clean path (rejected); ReLU-before-add forces `F ≥ 0` and a monotone forward signal (rejected); ReLU-only pre-activation leaves the leading ReLU un-normalized (no BN benefit); full BN-ReLU-conv satisfies everything — identity `f` (exact highway, optimization eased, benefit growing with depth) and a BN at the front of every weight layer (normalized inputs everywhere, delivering BN's regularization that the original unit's post-add structure had been undoing). The block is BN-ReLU-conv twice, a clean add with nothing after it, an identity shortcut or a 1x1 projection from the shared pre-activation for dimension changes, no residual scaling, plus the two forced boundary activations at the stem and before the classifier.
