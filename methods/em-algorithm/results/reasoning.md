I start with the likelihood that is actually supposed to be maximized. The observation is `y`, but the model is easier to write for a richer complete datum `x`. The observed likelihood is therefore `g(y | phi)`, obtained by summing or integrating `f(x | phi)` over every complete datum compatible with `y`. The computational problem is that `log g(y | phi)` is not the log likelihood I would have if `x` were observed. It is the log of a marginal likelihood, and that marginalization is exactly where the easy complete-data sufficient statistics disappear.

The tempting move is to fill in the missing part and maximize as if the fill-in were true. That is not enough. A hard fill-in changes the target and gives me no reason to believe the observed likelihood rises. What I need is a way to use the complete-data likelihood while keeping the observed likelihood as the objective.

The current parameter gives a principled distribution over the missing complete data. If the current value is `phi_p`, then `k(x | y, phi_p)` is the conditional density of complete data given the observed data. I can average the complete-data log likelihood under this conditional distribution and get `Q(phi | phi_p) = E[log f(X | phi) | y, phi_p]`. This is the expected complete-data log likelihood, not a pseudo-complete-data likelihood from a single imputation.

Now I need to see why maximizing this expectation helps the observed likelihood. The key identity is to write `f(x | phi) = g(y | phi) k(x | y, phi)` on the fiber of complete data that maps to `y`. Taking the conditional expectation under the current parameter gives `Q(phi | phi_p) = L(phi) + H(phi | phi_p)`, where `L(phi) = log g(y | phi)` and `H(phi | phi_p) = E[log k(X | y, phi) | y, phi_p]`. So the observed log likelihood is `L = Q - H`.

At first this looks dangerous: increasing `Q` might be canceled by increasing `H`. Jensen's inequality removes that danger at the current conditional distribution. The expected log ratio between `k(. | y, phi)` and `k(. | y, phi_p)` is at most the log of its expected ratio, which is zero. Therefore `H(phi | phi_p) <= H(phi_p | phi_p)`. When I move from `phi_p` to `phi_{p+1}`, the change in observed log likelihood is the gain in `Q` plus a nonnegative correction from this conditional-density term. If the M-step raises `Q`, the observed likelihood rises.

This is the real structure of the method. The first step forms a latent complete-data posterior under the current parameter. The second step solves, exactly or approximately, the complete-data maximum-likelihood problem after replacing complete-data sufficient statistics by their conditional expectations. In an exponential family this becomes especially concrete: compute `E[t(X) | y, phi_p]`, then choose the parameter whose complete-data mean sufficient statistic matches that expected statistic.

For mixtures, the complete data would include one-hot component labels. The current parameter converts each hidden label into posterior probabilities, often called responsibilities. The maximization then treats those probabilities as fractional component membership: component weights become normalized expected counts, means become responsibility-weighted averages, and covariances become responsibility-weighted scatter matrices. This is not merely alternating bookkeeping. The fractional counts are conditional expectations of complete-data sufficient statistics, and the weighted M-step is justified because it increases the observed-data likelihood through the `Q - H` decomposition.

The modern lower-bound view says the same thing in different coordinates. Introduce a distribution over the missing data. The E-step sets it to the exact posterior so the bound touches the observed log likelihood at the current parameter. The M-step raises the bound with that distribution fixed. Because the bound is tight before the M-step, raising it raises the observed likelihood after the step. This view also explains generalized variants: an exact maximizer is convenient, but monotonicity only needs an increase in the auxiliary objective.

The limits follow from the same reasoning. The argument gives ascent, not global optimality. The method can converge to local optima or stationary points. It can be slow when the observed data contain little of the complete-data information, because each posterior expectation changes cautiously. The choice of complete data is therefore part of the method design, not a cosmetic modeling choice.
