Let me start from what actually blocks me. I want to minimize a smooth non-convex F(x) = E_xi[f(x;xi)] using only stochastic first-order oracle calls, and I want the optimal stationarity rate. I know the landscape: for general smooth non-convex objectives SGD gets E||grad F(xbar)|| down at O(1/T^{1/4}) and that's tight, but on the narrower class where each sampled f(.,xi) is itself L-smooth — expectation over smooth losses — the rate improves to O(1/T^{1/3}), also tight. The methods that reach 1/T^{1/3} are the variance-reduced ones, and there are two families. The SVRG/SARAH/SPIDER family does it with checkpoints: stop every so often, burn a mega-batch of N samples (N as big as O(T), never smaller than about T^{2/3}) to compute a low-noise gradient at an anchor point, then run cheap recursive steps off it. The thing I don't like about it is concrete: the mega-batch makes zero progress while it computes, and the batch size, the checkpoint frequency, and an L-tied step cap are all knobs I'd have to balance against constants I don't know. The other family is STORM, by Cutkosky and Orabona, which gets the optimal rate with no checkpoint and no mega-batch at all. On the dimension I care about — knobs I'd have to set blind — STORM is strictly less encumbered: no batch size, no checkpoint period. So let me take STORM as the starting point and see exactly why it works, because if I'm going to improve it I need to know what its machinery is buying and where it's still paying a price.

STORM keeps a running gradient estimate d_t and steps x_{t+1} = x_t - eta_t d_t, just like SGD, but d_t is not a raw gradient — it's a corrected momentum. Standard momentum would be d_t = a_t g_t + (1-a_t) d_{t-1}, a weighted average of past gradients, with g_t = grad f(x_t;xi_t). That's biased as an estimate of grad F(x_t) and, in the stochastic non-convex world, nobody has shown it improves the rate over SGD — the noise eats the benefit. STORM's move is to add one correction term, computed by spending a *second* oracle call on the *same* fresh sample xi_t but at the previous iterate:

  d_t = g_t + (1 - a_t) ( d_{t-1} - gtilde_{t-1} ),   where gtilde_{t-1} = grad f(x_{t-1}; xi_t).

Expand and it reads d_t = a_t g_t + (1-a_t) d_{t-1} + (1-a_t)(g_t - gtilde_{t-1}). The bracket g_t - gtilde_{t-1} is a within-sample gradient *difference* between x_t and x_{t-1}. Why would that help? Let me track the thing I actually care about, the error of the estimate, eps_t = d_t - grad F(x_t). Write gbar_t = grad F(x_t) for the true gradient. Subtract gbar_t from both sides of the recursion. Since d_{t-1} = gbar_{t-1} + eps_{t-1}, I get

  eps_t = (1-a_t) eps_{t-1} + a_t (g_t - gbar_t) + (1-a_t) Z_t,
  Z_t = ( g_t - gtilde_{t-1} ) - ( gbar_t - gbar_{t-1} ).

Stare at the three pieces. The first carries the old error forward, shrunk by (1-a_t) — that's the contraction that lets error decay. The second, a_t (g_t - gbar_t), is fresh noise: zero-mean, magnitude controlled by sigma, and crucially *premultiplied by a_t*, so I can squash it by choosing the momentum a_t small. The third is the payoff of the correction. Z_t is the difference between a *stochastic* gradient difference and the *true* gradient difference; it too is zero-mean given the past, and by L-smoothness of the per-sample loss, ||Z_t|| <= ||g_t - gtilde_{t-1}|| + ||gbar_t - gbar_{t-1}|| <= 2L||x_t - x_{t-1}|| = 2L eta_{t-1} ||d_{t-1}||. So Z_t is on the order of the *step I just took*. As I converge and the steps shrink, Z_t shrinks with them. That's the whole engine: the error obeys roughly ||eps_t|| ≈ (1-a_t)||eps_{t-1}|| + (something order a_t sigma) + (something order eta_{t-1}||d_{t-1}||), so it contracts toward a small equilibrium, and STORM never needed a checkpoint to make the noise decrease — the within-sample difference plus smoothness does it for free.

Now, how does STORM actually pick eta_t and a_t? Inspired by AdaGrad it sets eta_t = k/(w + sum_{i<=t} ||g_i||^2)^{1/3}, and then ties the momentum to the squared step: a_{t+1} = c eta_t^2. Here is where I want to look hard, because this is the part I'd have to set blind. The constants are not innocent. k = b G^{2/3}/L, c = 28 L^2 + (stuff)/L, w = max((4Lk)^3, 2G^2, ...). So to run STORM I have to *know the smoothness constant L and a bound G on the gradient norms*. The momentum a_{t+1} = c eta_t^2 is really a_{t+1} ≈ const · L^2 eta_t^2 — it has L^2 baked into it. Is that L cosmetic, or does the proof genuinely lean on it? Let me hold that question and check it against the proof in a moment, because the answer decides whether STORM is salvageable as a parameter-free method or whether the L-dependence is load-bearing. In practice L and G are exactly the things I never know. I can tune a step-size scale, sure, but a method that needs the true Lipschitz and smoothness constants is the same disease that made the checkpoint family painful: it's not parameter-free. The adaptive crowd — AdaGrad, and the analyses by Li and Orabona, by Ward, Wu and Bottou — already showed you can hit *SGD's* non-convex rate with no knowledge of L or sigma, just by reading the step size off the observed gradients. STORM's argument is a Lyapunov potential Phi_t = F(x_t) + z_t ||eps_t||^2 with a time-varying weight z_t ∝ 1/eta_{t-1}. The descent half is standard: smoothness of F gives, with x_{t+1} = x_t - eta_t d_t,

If I delete the L from a_t the smoothness term and the contraction no longer balance — the L isn't cosmetic, it's the balancing weight between two terms in the error recursion.   E||eps_{t-1}||^2 <= (1/a_t)(E||eps_{t-1}||^2 - E||eps_t||^2) + (8L^2/a_t)E[eta_{t-1}^2||d_{t-1}||^2] + 2 a_t sigma^2.

(Let me confirm the algebra: from E||eps_t||^2 <= (1-a_t)E||eps_{t-1}||^2 + R_t with R_t the two non-contraction terms, divide by a_t: E||eps_t||^2/a_t <= (1/a_t - 1)E||eps_{t-1}||^2 + R_t/a_t, i.e. E||eps_{t-1}||^2 <= (1/a_t)E||eps_{t-1}||^2 - E||eps_t||^2/a_t + R_t/a_t = (1/a_t)(E||eps_{t-1}||^2 - E||eps_t||^2) + R_t/a_t. Yes.) Now sum t = 1..T with eps_0 = 0:

  E sum_{t=1}^T ||eps_{t-1}||^2 <= A + B + 8L^2 C + 2 sigma^2 D,

with the four pieces, after Abel summation on the first:
  A = -E||eps_T||^2/a_T  (the boundary term; since a_T <= 1, A <= -E||eps_T||^2 <= 0, just drop it),
  B = sum_{t=1}^{T-1} (1/a_{t+1} - 1/a_t) E||eps_t||^2,
  C = E sum_t eta_{t-1}^2 ||d_{t-1}||^2/a_t,
  D = sum_t a_t.

Bound them. For B: with a_{t+1} = 1/t^{2/3} the increment 1/a_{t+1} - 1/a_t = t^{2/3} - (t-1)^{2/3}. Concavity of y^{2/3} gives (z_1 + z_2)^{2/3} - z_1^{2/3} <= (2/3) z_1^{-1/3} z_2; with z_1 = t-1, z_2 = 1, that's <= (2/3)(t-1)^{-1/3} <= 2/3 for t >= 2, and the t=1 increment (1/a_2 - 1/a_1 = 1 - 1 = 0). So B <= (2/3) E sum_t ||eps_t||^2 — it's a fraction of the very thing I'm bounding, so I can absorb it to the left later. For C: eta_{t-1}^2 ||d_{t-1}||^2/a_t = (||d_{t-1}||^2/a_t)/(sum_{i<=t-1}||d_i||^2/a_{i+1})^{2/3}, which is the b/(sum b)^{2/3} shape with b_t = ||d_t||^2/a_{t+1}, so by the p = 2/3 telescope C <= 3 E(sum_{t<=T-1}||d_t||^2/a_{t+1})^{1/3}, and since 1/a_t <= 1/a_{T+1} <= T^{2/3} (a non-increasing), C <= 3 T^{2/9} E(sum_t ||d_t||^2)^{1/3} <= 3 T^{2/9}(E sum_t ||d_t||^2)^{1/3} by Jensen on the concave z^{1/3}. For D: sum_t a_t = 1 + sum_{t=1}^{T-1} 1/t^{2/3} <= 1 + 3 T^{1/3} <= 4 T^{1/3} (the p=2/3 telescope on b_i = 1, or just the integral bound sum t^{-2/3} <= 3 T^{1/3}). Put them together and absorb the (2/3) from B:

  (1/3) E sum_t ||eps_t||^2 <= 24 L^2 T^{2/9}(E sum_t ||d_t||^2)^{1/3} + 8 sigma^2 T^{1/3},

i.e. E sum_t ||eps_t||^2 <= 72 L^2 T^{2/9}(E sum_t ||d_t||^2)^{1/3} + 24 sigma^2 T^{1/3}. So the accumulated error is controlled by the accumulated *estimate* energy E sum||d||^2 (to the 1/3 power) plus a noise floor. Good.

Part two: turn that into a bound on E sum_t ||gbar_t||^2. Split into two cases on whether the error dominates the signal. Case 1: E sum||eps||^2 >= (1/2) E sum||gbar||^2. Since d_t = gbar_t + eps_t, ||d_t||^2 <= 2||gbar_t||^2 + 2||eps_t||^2, so E sum||d||^2 <= 2E sum||gbar||^2 + 2E sum||eps||^2 <= 4E sum||eps||^2 + 2E sum||eps||^2 = 6E sum||eps||^2 (using the case condition to replace E sum||gbar||^2 by 2E sum||eps||^2). Plug into the part-one bound: E sum||eps||^2 <= 72 L^2 T^{2/9}(6 E sum||eps||^2)^{1/3} + 24 sigma^2 T^{1/3}. Let S = E sum||eps||^2. Then S <= c1 T^{2/9} S^{1/3} + c2 sigma^2 T^{1/3}; the S^{1/3} term, being sublinear in S, can be solved out — S <= max(2 c2 sigma^2 T^{1/3}, (2 c1 T^{2/9})^{3/2}) <= O((L^3 + sigma^2) T^{1/3}). (The (T^{2/9})^{3/2} = T^{1/3}, exactly the right power.) And by the case condition E sum||gbar||^2 <= 2 S <= O((L^3 + sigma^2)T^{1/3}). Case 2: E sum||eps||^2 <= (1/2)E sum||gbar||^2. Then E sum||d||^2 <= 2E sum||gbar||^2 + 2·(1/2)E sum||gbar||^2 = 3E sum||gbar||^2. Now I use the smoothness-plus-update bound exactly like the offline case but carrying eps: from F(x_{t+1}) - F(x_t) <= -eta_t gbar_t^T d_t + (L eta_t^2/2)||d_t||^2 = -eta_t||gbar_t||^2 - eta_t gbar_t^T eps_t + (L eta_t^2/2)||d_t||^2, Young on the cross term (-gbar^T eps <= (1/2)||gbar||^2 + (1/2)||eps||^2) gives ||gbar_t||^2 <= ||eps_t||^2 + (2/eta_t)(Delta_t - Delta_{t+1}) + L eta_t ||d_t||^2. Sum, telescope the (2/eta_t)(Delta_t - Delta_{t+1}) part by parts to 2B/eta_T (using Delta in [0,B], 1/eta non-decreasing), and telescope the L eta_t||d_t||^2 part by p=1/3 to (3/2)L(sum||d||^2)^{2/3}:

  sum_t ||gbar_t||^2 <= sum_t ||eps_t||^2 + 2B(sum_t||d_t||^2/a_{t+1})^{1/3} + (3/2)L(sum_t||d_t||^2)^{2/3}.

With a_{t+1} = 1/T^{2/3} the middle term is 2B T^{2/9}(sum||d||^2)^{1/3}. Take expectation, use E sum||d||^2 <= 3E sum||gbar||^2 and E sum||eps||^2 <= (1/2)E sum||gbar||^2 and Jensen on z^{1/3}, z^{2/3}:

  E sum||gbar||^2 <= (1/2)E sum||gbar||^2 + 2B T^{2/9}(3E sum||gbar||^2)^{1/3} + (3/2)L(3E sum||gbar||^2)^{2/3}.

Subtract the (1/2): (1/2)E sum||gbar||^2 <= (sublinear in E sum||gbar||^2) terms, solve out the 1/3 and 2/3 powers -> E sum||gbar||^2 <= O(L^3 + B^{3/2} T^{1/3}). Combining the two cases, E sum_t ||gbar_t||^2 <= O((L^3 + sigma^2 + B^{3/2})T^{1/3}). Random iterate + Jensen: E||grad F(xbar_T)|| <= sqrt(E sum||gbar||^2/T) <= O(sqrt(L^3+sigma^2+B^{3/2})/T^{1/3}) — the optimal 1/T^{1/3}, parameter-free in the algorithm (L, B, sigma only in the final constant). But I notice this simplified version does *not* recover 1/sqrt T when sigma = 0: the sigma^2 T^{1/3} term came from D = sum a_t with the non-adaptive a, which keeps adding t^{-2/3} regardless of whether there's any noise to fight. That's a concrete flaw, and it's exactly the reason the fixed-schedule a_t isn't good enough — I want the *adaptive* a_t precisely so the momentum notices when sigma = 0 and stops paying for noise that isn't there. But the spine is proven, and the offline check already showed the adaptive eta gives 1/sqrt T when the error is gone, so the fix is in the momentum.

Now the hard version: a_{t+1} = 1/(1+sum_{i<=t}||g_i||^2)^{2/3}, which is *random* (it depends on the noisy g_i). Two new headaches. First, a_t is no longer deterministic-given-past in the convenient way — it's H_{t-1}-measurable still (depends on g_1..g_{t-1}), good, but it's correlated with the noise, so I can't pull it out of expectations as a constant. Second, and worse, the reciprocal increments 1/a_{t+1} - 1/a_t are no longer bounded by 2/3: a single large ||g_t||^2 can make 1/a_{t+1} = (1+sum_{i<=t}||g||^2)^{2/3} jump a lot in one step. The term-B bound depended on 1/a_{t+1} - 1/a_t <= 2/3, so it's in danger. Let me check just how badly it breaks before deciding how to patch it, because if the increments stay near 2/3 anyway I'm worrying about nothing. Simulate a_t with constant per-step gradient energy ||g||^2 = c and watch the worst reciprocal increment:

```
||g||^2=0.25 (G^4=0.06): worst increment over the run = 0.150
||g||^2=1.0  (G^4=1):    worst increment = 0.493
||g||^2=4.0  (G^4=16):   worst increment = 1.403   <-- exceeds 2/3
```

So it genuinely breaks: with ||g||^2 = 4 the very first increments overshoot 2/3 (max 1.40), because early on a_t is still near 1 and (1+sum)^{2/3} is climbing steeply. The danger is real but it's confined to the *early* iterations, when the accumulated gradient energy is small. That tells me the fix: I don't need the 2/3 bound everywhere, only once a_t has gotten small. By concavity of y^{2/3}, 1/a_{t+1} - 1/a_t <= (2/3)||g_t||^2/(1+sum_{i<t}||g||^2)^{1/3} = (2/3) sqrt(a_t) ||g_t||^2, so the increment is small once sqrt(a_t)||g_t||^2 <= 1, i.e. a_t <= 1/||g_t||^4. With ||g_t||^2 <= G^2 (the gradient bound — used in the *analysis only*, not the algorithm), define beta = min{1, 1/G^4} and the stopping time tau* = max{t in [T]: a_t >= beta}. For t > tau*, a_t < beta <= 1/G^4, so sqrt(a_t)||g_t||^2 <= sqrt(beta)G^2 <= sqrt(1/G^4)·G^2 = 1, giving 1/a_{t+1} - 1/a_t <= (2/3)sqrt(beta)G^2 <= 2/3. Let me confirm against the same simulation that the bound really is restored after a_t crosses below beta: for ||g||^2 = 4 (beta = 1/16 = 0.0625), the worst increment *among the steps where a_t has already dropped below beta* is 0.657 — under 2/3, exactly as the concavity argument promises, even though the overall worst was 1.40. So the stopping time isolates the bad early steps and the 2/3 bound holds on the tail. And tau* is genuinely a stopping time because a_{t+1} is H_t-measurable. (tau* is finite and random; a_t is non-increasing so once it drops below beta it stays below.) Before tau*, a_t >= beta, so 1/a_t <= 1/beta is just bounded by a constant — I don't need the increment bound there, I can bound the whole prefix sum sum_{t<=tau*}||eps_t||^2 directly.

So the plan splits sum||eps_t||^2 = sum_{t<=tau*}||eps_t||^2 + sum_{t>tau*}||eps_t||^2, control the prefix, then control the whole. Prefix first. Take the eps recursion, square and sum to tau*, keeping the martingale piece explicit this time (because I can't take expectations naively with a random stopping time — I'll use optional stopping):

  sum_{t<=tau*}||eps_t||^2 <= sum_{t<=tau*}(1-a_t)||eps_{t-1}||^2 + 2 sum_{t<=tau*}||Z_t||^2 + 2 sum_{t<=tau*} a_t^2||g_t - gbar_t||^2 + sum_{t<=tau*} M_t,

where M_t = 2(1-a_t)eps_{t-1}^T((1-a_t)Z_t + a_t(g_t - gbar_t)) is a martingale difference (E[M_t|H_{t-1}] = 0, since the two factors inside are zero-mean given the past while (1-a_t)eps_{t-1} is given). Rearrange the contraction: sum(1-a_t)||eps_{t-1}||^2 = sum||eps_{t-1}||^2 - sum a_t ||eps_{t-1}||^2, and move terms so that, using a_t >= beta on the prefix,

  beta sum_{t<=tau*}||eps_t||^2 <= ||eps_{tau*}||^2 + sum_{t<tau*} a_{t+1}||eps_t||^2 <= 2 sum_{t<=T}||Z_t||^2 + 2 sum_{t<=T} a_t^2||g_t-gbar_t||^2 + sum_{t<=tau*}M_t.

(I extended the Z and noise sums to T, only enlarging them, and bounded the prefix's leftover error by the full sums.) Now I bound the three pieces.

For the first piece, sum ||Z_t||^2 <= 4L^2 sum eta_{t-1}^2||d_{t-1}||^2 = 4L^2 sum (||d_{t-1}||^2)/(sum_{i<t}||d_i||^2/a_{i+1})^{2/3}. Here I bound the denominator below by dropping the 1/a (eta_t = (sum||d||^2/a)^{-1/3} <= (sum||d||^2)^{-1/3}), giving the b/(sum b)^{2/3} shape with b = ||d||^2, and the p=2/3 telescope gives 12 L^2 (sum_t||d_t||^2)^{1/3}. For the second piece, E sum a_t^2||g_t - gbar_t||^2 is controlled by the raw-gradient energy. Since a_t is H_{t-1}-measurable and E[||g_t - gbar_t||^2|H_{t-1}] = E[||g_t||^2|H_{t-1}] - ||gbar_t||^2 <= E[||g_t||^2|H_{t-1}], I get E[a_t^2||g_t-gbar_t||^2] <= E[a_t^2||g_t||^2]. And a_t^2 = 1/(1+sum_{i<t}||g||^2)^{4/3}, so E sum a_t^2||g_t||^2 = E sum ||g_t||^2/(1+sum_{i<t}||g_i||^2)^{4/3}. This is exactly the delayed AdaGrad sum with exponent 4/3 > 1 that I checked numerically lands at a constant; let me now prove the constant rather than rely on the simulation, and it gives C_1 = 12 + 2G^2. For the third piece, sum_{t<=tau*}M_t disappears in expectation because tau* is a bounded stopping time and {M_t} is a martingale difference sequence, so Doob's optional stopping theorem gives E sum_{t<=tau*}M_t = 0. Therefore E[beta sum_{t<=tau*}||eps_t||^2] <= 2 C_1 + 24 L^2 (E sum_t||d_t||^2)^{1/3} (Jensen on z^{1/3}), i.e.

  E sum_{t<=tau*}||eps_t||^2 <= 2 C_1/beta + 24 (L^2/beta)(E sum_t||d_t||^2)^{1/3}.

The exponent-4/3 lemma deserves a real proof, not a citation — my numeric check said the sum sits around 2.5 for all-ones, but I want the worst-case constant. Claim: for a_1,...,a_n in [0, amax], sum_{i=1}^n a_i/(1 + sum_{j<=i} a_j)^{4/3} <= 12. Dyadic blocking. Define N_0 = max{i: sum_{j<=i}a_j <= 2}, and for k >= 1, N_k = max{i: 2^k < sum_{j<=i}a_j <= 2^{k+1}}. On the zeroth block the denominator (1+partial sum)^{4/3} >= 1, so its contribution is <= sum_{i<=N_0}a_i <= 2. On block k, every term has 1 + sum_{j<=i}a_j > 2^k, so denominator > (2^k)^{4/3}, and the numerators in the block sum to at most 2^{k+1} (the partial-sum increase across the block), so block k contributes <= 2^{k+1}/(2^k)^{4/3} = 2·2^{k(1 - 4/3)} = 2·(2^{-1/3})^k. Sum over k >= 1: 2 sum_{k>=1}(2^{-1/3})^k = 2·(2^{-1/3}/(1-2^{-1/3})) = 2/(2^{1/3}-1) < 10. Total <= 2 + 10 = 12. (The exponent 4/3 > 1 is exactly what makes the geometric series converge — this is the worst-case version of what I watched converge to ~2.5 numerically; with exponent 1 the ratio (2^{-1/3}) would become 2^0 = 1 and the sum over k would diverge linearly in log n, the harmonic leak I also saw. So the choice a_t ∝ (1+sum||g||^2)^{-2/3}, giving a_t^2 ∝ (.)^{-4/3}, is precisely tuned to land the variance term at a *constant*.) The "delayed" version with 1+sum_{j<i} instead of 1+sum_{j<=i}: split off at N_0 = min{i: sum_{j<i}a_j >= amax}; the prefix i < N_0 has 1+sum_{j<i} >= 1 so contributes <= sum a_i <= 2 amax, and after N_0 the floor 1+amax+sum lets me replace 1+sum_{j<i} by 1+a_i+sum_{j<i} = 1+sum_{j<=i} and apply the non-delayed lemma, total <= 2 amax + 12. With amax = G^2: C_1 = 12 + 2G^2.

Now the second part, bounding the *full* E sum_{t<=T}||eps_t||^2. Same eps recursion, but this time divide by a_t (so the algebra stays clean with the random a), square, and sum:

  (1/a_t)||eps_t||^2 <= (1/a_t - 1)||eps_{t-1}||^2 + 2||Z_t||^2/a_t + 2 a_t||g_t - gbar_t||^2 + Y_t,

where Y_t = 2((1-a_t)/sqrt(a_t))eps_{t-1}^T(sqrt(a_t)(g_t-gbar_t) + ((1-a_t)/sqrt(a_t))Z_t) is again a martingale difference (E[Y_t|H_{t-1}] = 0). (The 1/a_t - 2 + a_t coefficient on ||eps_{t-1}||^2 from expanding (1-a_t)^2/a_t I upper-bounded by 1/a_t - 1 using a_t in [0,1].) Rearrange to E sum||eps_{t-1}||^2 <= boundary + sum_t(1/a_{t+1} - 1/a_t)||eps_t||^2 + 2 sum||Z_t||^2/a_t + 2 sum a_t||g_t-gbar_t||^2 + sum Y_t. Name them A, B, C, D, E. A <= -E||eps_T||^2/a_T <= 0 (drop, since a_T <= 1). E: martingale, E sum Y_t = 0.

Term B is where the stopping time pays off. Split at tau*: B = sum_{t<=tau*}(1/a_{t+1} - 1/a_t)||eps_t||^2 + sum_{t>tau*}(1/a_{t+1} - 1/a_t)||eps_t||^2. On the prefix I can't bound the increment by 2/3 (the simulation showed exactly that), but I *can* bound 1/a_{t+1} by a constant: I'll show 1/a_{t+1} <= 1/betatilde for all t <= tau*, where 1/betatilde := (1/beta^{3/2} + G^2)^{2/3}. Indeed 1/a_{t+1} = (1 + sum_{i<=t}||g||^2)^{2/3}; raise to the 3/2: (1/a_{t+1})^{3/2} = 1 + sum_{i<=t}||g||^2 = (1/a_t)^{3/2} + ||g_t||^2. At t = tau*, (1/a_{tau*})^{3/2} <= (1/beta)^{3/2} since a_{tau*} >= beta, and ||g_{tau*}||^2 <= G^2, so (1/a_{tau*+1})^{3/2} <= 1/beta^{3/2} + G^2, i.e. 1/a_{tau*+1} <= (1/beta^{3/2}+G^2)^{2/3} = 1/betatilde, and since 1/a is non-decreasing in t this bounds 1/a_{t+1} for all t <= tau*. So the prefix part of B is <= (1/betatilde) sum_{t<=tau*}||eps_t||^2 (telescoping the increments to the largest reciprocal). On the suffix t > tau*, the increment is <= 2/3 (the part the simulation confirmed), so the suffix part is <= (2/3) sum_{t>tau*}||eps_t||^2 <= (2/3) sum_{t<=T}||eps_t||^2. Hence E B <= (1/betatilde) E sum_{t<=tau*}||eps_t||^2 + (2/3) E sum_t||eps_t||^2, and the first piece I already bounded by the prefix result: <= (1/betatilde)(2C_1/beta + 24(L^2/beta)(E sum||d||^2)^{1/3}) + (2/3)E sum||eps||^2.

For the unmultiplied term C = E sum ||Z_t||^2/a_t, the smoothness bound gives C <= 4L^2 E sum eta_{t-1}^2||d_{t-1}||^2/a_t = 4L^2 E sum (||d_{t-1}||^2/a_t)/(sum_{i<t}||d_i||^2/a_{i+1})^{2/3}. The p=2/3 telescope on b_t = ||d_t||^2/a_{t+1} gives C <= 12 L^2 (sum||d||^2/a_{t+1})^{1/3} <= 12 L^2 (1/a_T)^{1/3}(sum||d||^2)^{1/3} <= 12 L^2 (1+sum||g||^2)^{2/9}(sum||d||^2)^{1/3}. Now there's a 2/9 power of the gradient-energy multiplying a 1/3 power of the estimate-energy — I want to break it into a constant times (sum||g||^2)^{1/3} plus a small multiple of sum||d||^2, so it doesn't feed back uncontrollably. Young's inequality with exponents 3/2 and 3: for any rho > 0, a^{2/9}b^{1/3} = (a rho^{9/2})^{2/9}(b/rho^3)^{1/3} <= (2/3)a^{1/3}rho^{3/2} + (1/3)b/rho^3. Take a = 1+sum||g||^2, b = sum||d||^2, rho = (512 L^2)^{1/3}: then C <= 512 L^3 (1+sum||g||^2)^{1/3} + (1/128) sum||d||^2. The full error inequality carries 2C, so this contributes 1024 L^3 (1+sum||g||^2)^{1/3} and (1/64)sum||d||^2 after the outer factor of two.

Term D = 2 E sum a_t||g_t - gbar_t||^2 <= 2 E sum a_t||g_t||^2 (same conditional-variance trick) = 2 E sum ||g_t||^2/(1+sum_{i<t}||g||^2)^{2/3}. This is the delayed AdaGrad sum but with exponent 2/3 < 1 now (not 4/3), so it does *not* stay constant — and indeed my earlier numeric run at p=2/3 showed this one growing like n^{1/3} rather than flattening. It telescopes to a (sum||g||^2)^{1/3}: by the shifted technical lemma, sum b_t/(b_0 + sum_{j<t}b_j)^p <= b/b_0^p + (2/(1-p))(b_0 + sum b_t)^{1-p}; with b_0 = 1, p = 2/3, b = G^2: D <= 2(G^2 + 6(1+sum||g||^2)^{1/3}). (The shifted lemma is proved by the same delayed-blocking idea: split at T_0 = min{i: sum_{j<i}b_j >= b}; prefix gives b/b_0^p, suffix replaces b_0 + sum_{j<i} by (1/2)b + (1/2)sum_{j<i} >= (1/2)sum_{j<=i} and applies the non-shifted telescope with the factor 2^p <= 2 out front.) This is the term that *does* feel the noise — it grows with sum||g||^2, which keeps growing only as long as there are noisy gradients to accumulate — and it's the mechanism by which the adaptive a_t will give variance adaptivity, unlike the fixed-schedule D = sum a_t that grew like T^{1/3} regardless.

Collecting A,...,E (E and A drop), absorbing the (2/3) from B to the left:

  (1/3) E sum_t||eps_t||^2 <= (24 L^2/(beta betatilde))(E sum||d||^2)^{1/3} + 2C_1/(beta betatilde) + 2G^2 + (1024 L^3 + 12)(1 + E sum||g||^2)^{1/3} + (1/64) E sum||d||^2.

(The (1/64) is from C's (1/128) doubled by the factor-2 in front of C in the original sum.) Now the last part: convert to E sum||gbar||^2, two cases, exactly as in the simplified proof.

Case 1: E sum||eps||^2 >= (1/2)E sum||gbar||^2. Then E sum||d||^2 <= 6 E sum||eps||^2, and E sum||g||^2 = E sum||gbar||^2 + E sum||g - gbar||^2 <= E sum||gbar||^2 + sigma^2 T <= 2 E sum||eps||^2 + sigma^2 T. Plug E sum||d||^2 <= 6 E sum||eps||^2 and E sum||g||^2 <= 2 E sum||eps||^2 + sigma^2 T into the displayed bound. The dominant left term is (1/3)E sum||eps||^2 minus the (1/64)·6 E sum||eps||^2 from E sum||d||^2; (1/3 - 6/64) = (1/3 - 3/32) > 1/5, so (1/5)E sum||eps||^2 <= (const)(E sum||eps||^2)^{1/3} + (1024L^3+12)(1+sigma^2 T+2E sum||eps||^2)^{1/3} + constants. The right side is sublinear in E sum||eps||^2 (powers 1/3), with the only T-growth being the sigma^2 T inside a 1/3 power -> (sigma^2 T)^{1/3} = sigma^{2/3}T^{1/3}. Solving the sublinear inequality, E sum||gbar||^2 <= 2 E sum||eps||^2 <= O(1 + C_1/(beta betatilde) + (L^2/(beta betatilde))^{3/2} + G^2 + L^3 + L^{9/2} + L^3 sigma^{2/3}T^{1/3}).

Case 2: E sum||eps||^2 <= (1/2)E sum||gbar||^2. Take the worse of the two cases. Both have the same noise structure: a T-independent constant (the M^2 part) plus a sigma^{2/3}T^{1/3} term (the kappa^2 part). Reading off, E sum_t||gbar_t||^2 <= O(M^2 + kappa^2 sigma^{2/3}T^{1/3}) with kappa^2 = B^{3/2} + L^3 and M^2 = 1 + L^{9/2} + B^{9/4} + G^{10} + (L^2 G^8)^{3/2} (the betatilde-dependent pieces unpack into the G powers, since 1/betatilde involves G^2 and 1/beta involves G^4). Finally random-iterate + Jensen:

  E||grad F(xbar_T)|| = sqrt(E sum||gbar||^2/T) <= O(M/sqrt T + kappa sigma^{1/3}/T^{1/3}),

with kappa = O(B^{3/4} + L^{3/2}), M = O(1 + L^{9/4} + B^{9/8} + G^5 + (L G^4)^{3/2}). So I have the two-term bound. The rate is O(1/T^{1/3}) in the stochastic case — optimal for the expectation-over-smooth-losses class — and the crucial check is the limiting case sigma -> 0: the second term carries sigma^{1/3} as a factor, so it vanishes exactly when the noise does, leaving M/sqrt T = O(1/sqrt T). That recovers the offline rate I computed independently at the very start (where I solved the polynomial inequality and got the same 1/sqrt T from the step size alone) — the two routes agree, which is the consistency check I wanted: the noiseless limit of the stochastic bound matches the directly-computed noiseless bound. So the method *adapts to the variance* automatically: noiseless gets the faster 1/sqrt T, noisy gets the optimal 1/T^{1/3}, and I never told it which regime it's in. And L, G, B, sigma appear only in the constants M, kappa — *never in the schedules eta_t, a_t the algorithm computes*. That's the parameter-free claim, and it survived the proof. (Compared with the simplified non-adaptive a_{t+1}=1/t^{2/3} schedule, the adaptive a_t is exactly what removed the spurious sigma^2 T^{1/3} term that survived even at sigma = 0: here the noise enters only through term D = sum a_t||g||^2, which stops growing when the gradients stop being noisy. And compared with the constant-knowing STORM, the cleaner 1/sqrt T term sheds the (log T)^{3/4} factor.)

Let me also be honest with myself about what the analysis quietly assumed beyond smoothness and bounded variance, so I know the method's limits. The proof used a bound on the gradient norm G (in the stopping-time argument and the C_1 term) and a bound on the *range* of function values B = max|F(x)-F(y)| (in the smoothness-plus-update telescope, where Delta_t had to live in [0,B]). Both enter only the final constants, not the schedules — but the B assumption is genuinely needed by *this* argument; without it the (2B/eta_T)(Delta telescope) step has nothing to bound Delta_t by. I'll note that as the price of this particular proof route.

The method as I'd actually run it: at iteration t I already hold d_t, the current stochastic gradient g_t (with d_1 = g_1 from the warm start), and two running sums — the gradient energy sum_{i<=t}||g_i||^2 and the reweighted estimate energy sum_{i<=t}||d_i||^2/a_{i+1}. Each step (1) folds ||g_t||^2 into the gradient-energy sum and forms a_{t+1} from it; (2) folds ||d_t||^2/a_{t+1} into the estimate-energy sum and forms eta_t; (3) takes x_{t+1} = x_t - eta_t d_t; (4) draws a fresh sample and makes the two oracle calls g_{t+1} = grad f(x_{t+1};xi_{t+1}) and gtilde_t = grad f(x_t;xi_{t+1}); (5) recurses d_{t+1} = g_{t+1} + (1-a_{t+1})(d_t - gtilde_t), carrying g_{t+1} forward as the next iteration's current gradient. The norms are full-vector Euclidean norms over all parameters — one scalar step size and one scalar momentum per iteration, not per-coordinate. That five-step loop, filled into the corrected-momentum harness's one empty slot, is the whole construction: two oracle calls per step, no batch, no anchor, nothing to tune but an overall step-size scale, and L, G, B, sigma never appearing in anything the algorithm computes.
