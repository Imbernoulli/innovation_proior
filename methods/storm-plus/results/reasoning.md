Let me start from what actually blocks me. I want to minimize a smooth non-convex F(x) = E_xi[f(x;xi)] using only stochastic first-order oracle calls, and I want the optimal stationarity rate. I know the landscape: for general smooth non-convex objectives SGD gets E||grad F(xbar)|| down at O(1/T^{1/4}) and that's tight, but on the narrower class where each sampled f(.,xi) is itself L-smooth — expectation over smooth losses — the rate improves to O(1/T^{1/3}), also tight. The methods that reach 1/T^{1/3} are the variance-reduced ones, and there are two families. The SVRG/SARAH/SPIDER family does it with checkpoints: stop every so often, burn a mega-batch of N samples (N as big as O(T), never smaller than about T^{2/3}) to compute a low-noise gradient at an anchor point, then run cheap recursive steps off it. I hate this — the mega-batch makes zero progress while it computes, and the batch size, the checkpoint frequency, and an L-tied step cap are all knobs I have to balance against constants I don't know. The other family is STORM, by Cutkosky and Orabona, which is the one I actually like, because it gets the optimal rate with no checkpoint and no mega-batch at all. So that's where I'll build from. Let me make sure I understand exactly why STORM works, and then find what's still wrong with it.

STORM keeps a running gradient estimate d_t and steps x_{t+1} = x_t - eta_t d_t, just like SGD, but d_t is not a raw gradient — it's a corrected momentum. Standard momentum would be d_t = a_t g_t + (1-a_t) d_{t-1}, a weighted average of past gradients, with g_t = grad f(x_t;xi_t). That's biased as an estimate of grad F(x_t) and, in the stochastic non-convex world, nobody has shown it improves the rate over SGD — the noise eats the benefit. STORM's move is to add one correction term, computed by spending a *second* oracle call on the *same* fresh sample xi_t but at the previous iterate:

  d_t = g_t + (1 - a_t) ( d_{t-1} - gtilde_{t-1} ),   where gtilde_{t-1} = grad f(x_{t-1}; xi_t).

Expand and it reads d_t = a_t g_t + (1-a_t) d_{t-1} + (1-a_t)(g_t - gtilde_{t-1}). The bracket g_t - gtilde_{t-1} is a within-sample gradient *difference* between x_t and x_{t-1}. Why would that help? Let me track the thing I actually care about, the error of the estimate, eps_t = d_t - grad F(x_t). Write gbar_t = grad F(x_t) for the true gradient. Subtract gbar_t from both sides of the recursion. Since d_{t-1} = gbar_{t-1} + eps_{t-1}, I get

  eps_t = (1-a_t) eps_{t-1} + a_t (g_t - gbar_t) + (1-a_t) Z_t,
  Z_t = ( g_t - gtilde_{t-1} ) - ( gbar_t - gbar_{t-1} ).

Stare at the three pieces. The first carries the old error forward, shrunk by (1-a_t) — that's the contraction that lets error decay. The second, a_t (g_t - gbar_t), is fresh noise: zero-mean, magnitude controlled by sigma, and crucially *premultiplied by a_t*, so I can squash it by choosing the momentum a_t small. The third is the payoff of the correction. Z_t is the difference between a *stochastic* gradient difference and the *true* gradient difference; it too is zero-mean given the past, and by L-smoothness of the per-sample loss, ||Z_t|| <= ||g_t - gtilde_{t-1}|| + ||gbar_t - gbar_{t-1}|| <= 2L||x_t - x_{t-1}|| = 2L eta_{t-1} ||d_{t-1}||. So Z_t is on the order of the *step I just took*. As I converge and the steps shrink, Z_t shrinks with them. That's the whole engine: the error obeys roughly ||eps_t|| ≈ (1-a_t)||eps_{t-1}|| + (something order a_t sigma) + (something order eta_{t-1}||d_{t-1}||), so it contracts toward a small equilibrium, and I never needed a checkpoint to make the noise decrease — the within-sample difference plus smoothness does it for free.

Now, how does STORM actually pick eta_t and a_t? Inspired by AdaGrad it sets eta_t = k/(w + sum_{i<=t} ||g_i||^2)^{1/3}, and then ties the momentum to the squared step: a_{t+1} = c eta_t^2. And here is the thing that bothers me, the reason I can't just ship STORM. The constants are not innocent. k = b G^{2/3}/L, c = 28 L^2 + (stuff)/L, w = max((4Lk)^3, 2G^2, ...). So to run STORM I have to *know the smoothness constant L and a bound G on the gradient norms*. The momentum a_{t+1} = c eta_t^2 is really a_{t+1} ≈ const · L^2 eta_t^2 — it has L^2 baked into it. And it's not cosmetic: the convergence proof literally needs a_{t+1} proportional to L^2 eta_t^2 so that the smoothness term and the momentum term in the error recursion balance; remove the L and the proof doesn't go through. In practice L and G are exactly the things I never know. I can tune a step-size scale, sure, but a method that needs the true Lipschitz and smoothness constants is the same disease that made the checkpoint family painful: it's not parameter-free. The adaptive crowd — AdaGrad, and the analyses by Li and Orabona, by Ward, Wu and Bottou — already showed you can hit *SGD's* non-convex rate with no knowledge of L or sigma, just by reading the step size off the observed gradients. So the question that won't leave me alone: can I keep STORM's variance-reduction-via-corrected-momentum and its optimal 1/T^{1/3} rate, but set eta_t *and* a_t from observed quantities only — no L, no G, no sigma?

Let me first see precisely where the constants enter the proof, because that tells me what the data-driven versions have to reproduce. STORM's argument is a Lyapunov potential Phi_t = F(x_t) + z_t ||eps_t||^2 with a time-varying weight z_t ∝ 1/eta_{t-1}. The descent half is standard: smoothness of F gives, with x_{t+1} = x_t - eta_t d_t,

  F(x_{t+1}) - F(x_t) <= -eta_t gbar_t^T d_t + (L eta_t^2/2)||d_t||^2.

Substitute d_t = gbar_t + eps_t, so gbar_t^T d_t = ||gbar_t||^2 + gbar_t^T eps_t, and tame the cross term with Young's inequality z^T y <= (1/2)(||z||^2 + ||y||^2):

  F(x_{t+1}) - F(x_t) <= -eta_t ||gbar_t||^2 + (eta_t/2)||gbar_t||^2 + (eta_t/2)||eps_t||^2 + (L eta_t^2/2)||d_t||^2.

So a single descent step buys me -(eta_t/2)||gbar_t||^2 of progress, *minus* an error tax (eta_t/2)||eps_t||^2 and a curvature tax (L eta_t^2/2)||d_t||^2. The potential's job is to pay the error tax out of the contraction of ||eps_t||^2; that's why z_t ∝ 1/eta_{t-1} — the 1/eta makes the eps-bookkeeping telescope against the eta in the descent term. Fine. The place L and G sneak in is the eps recursion: to get the contraction you need the momentum a_t and step eta_t set so that the smoothness term 4L^2 eta_{t-1}^2 ||d_{t-1}||^2 and the variance term a_t^2 sigma^2 both stay summable, and STORM achieves that by hardwiring a_t ∝ L^2 eta_t^2 with eta_t ∝ (sum g^2)^{-1/3}.

Right. So I need new schedules. Let me reason about what they must do, term by term, and only afterward write them down. Two cumulative quantities are available to me at iteration t with no oracle of L or G: the running sum of squared *raw gradient norms*, sum_{i<=t} ||g_i||^2, and the running sum of squared *estimate norms*, sum_{i<=t} ||d_i||^2. AdaGrad teaches me the shape: make a schedule inversely proportional to a power of one of these sums, because the magic identity sum_i b_i/(sum_{j<=i} b_j)^p <= (1/(1-p))(sum_i b_i)^{1-p} — the one McMahan and Streeter use — turns "step inversely proportional to accumulated squares" into a summable, telescoping quantity. The exponent p is the only real freedom.

What exponent do I want on the *step size*? Look back at the curvature tax (L eta_t^2/2)||d_t||^2 and at the smoothness term in the eps recursion, both of which carry eta_{t-1}^2 ||d_{t-1}||^2. When I sum those over t, I want a telescope. If I set eta_t = 1/(sum_{i<=t} ||d_i||^2)^{1/3} — note: I'm using the *estimate* norms ||d_i||^2 in the denominator, not the raw ||g_i||^2 — then eta_{t-1}^2 ||d_{t-1}||^2 = ||d_{t-1}||^2/(sum_{i<t}||d_i||^2)^{2/3}, which is exactly the b_i/(sum)^{2/3} shape with p = 2/3, and the identity collapses sum_t of it to (1-2/3)^{-1}(sum||d||^2)^{1/3} = 3(sum||d||^2)^{1/3}. There's my cube root. Why ||d|| and not ||g|| here? Because the terms I'm trying to make summable are literally built from ||d_{t-1}||^2 (they came from ||Z_t||^2 ∝ ||x_t - x_{t-1}||^2 = eta_{t-1}^2||d_{t-1}||^2, and from ||d_t||^2 in the curvature tax). For the McMahan-Streeter telescope to fire, the thing accumulated in the denominator has to be the same thing sitting in the numerator. If I accumulated ||g||^2 in the eta denominator, the ||d||^2 numerators wouldn't cancel and I'd have no telescope. So eta must be tied to the d-history. And the exponent has to be 1/3, not 1/2 like vanilla AdaGrad: 1/2 would telescope these to (sum)^{1/2} = sqrt-scale, which is the SGD 1/T^{1/4}-flavored regime; 1/3 is what lands the variance-reduced 1/T^{1/3}. The power is forced by the rate I'm targeting.

Now the *momentum* a_t. In STORM a_t ∝ L^2 eta_t^2, and since STORM's eta ∝ (sum g^2)^{-1/3} ∝ t^{-1/3} when noise keeps ||g||^2 from vanishing, that means a_t ∝ t^{-2/3}. So the *shape* I'm imitating is a_t decaying like t^{-2/3}. I can manufacture that shape parameter-free with the same AdaGrad trick on the *gradient* norms: a_t = 1/(1 + sum_{i<t} ||g_i||^2)^{2/3}. When the noise floor keeps sum ||g_i||^2 growing like t, this is ≈ t^{-2/3}, the decay STORM wanted, but with no L^2 and no G anywhere. Why ||g|| here and not ||d||? Because the term a_t is fighting is the variance term in the eps recursion, a_t^2 ||g_t - gbar_t||^2, and I'll bound ||g_t - gbar_t||^2 in expectation by ||g_t||^2 (more on that in a second), so I want a_t to telescope against ||g||^2. Why the "+1" inside? Two reasons: it makes a_1 = 1 when there's no history yet (and a_1 = 1 is the honest "trust the first sample fully" momentum), and it guarantees a_t in (0,1] for all t, which the recursion needs (a_t is a convex weight). Why exponent 2/3 and not 1/2 or 1? The squared momentum multiplies the fresh-noise term, so a_t^2 becomes a denominator exponent 4/3 on the cumulative gradient energy; that is just above 1, which lets the dyadic sum stay bounded by a constant. With exponent 1/2 I would get only exponent 1 in that variance sum, a logarithmic/harmonic leak, and sum a_t would grow like T^{1/2} in the simplified proof instead of T^{1/3}. With exponent 1, a_t ∝ 1/t shrinks too fast relative to the eta^2 shape STORM's balance wanted, so the old error is not refreshed strongly enough while the estimator is still moving. The 2/3 power is the one that mimics STORM's L^2 eta^2 decay, makes a_t^2 land at the constant dyadic 4/3 lemma, and still gives reciprocal increments 1/a_{t+1} - 1/a_t <= 2/3 in the deterministic proxy.

But now I've created a circularity I need to face. STORM tied momentum to step (a ∝ L^2 eta^2) using its knowledge of L. I refuse to use L, so I can't write a in terms of eta that way. The two schedules still have to be *coupled* — the proof's balance between the smoothness term and the variance term depends on a relationship between them — but I have to forge that coupling out of observed quantities. So instead of making a depend on eta, let me make eta depend on a. Take my eta denominator and *reweight each ||d_i||^2 by 1/a_{i+1}*:

  eta_t = 1 / ( sum_{i=1}^{t} ||d_i||^2 / a_{i+1} )^{1/3}.

This is the heart of it. The factor 1/a_{i+1} ≈ (1 + sum||g||^2)^{2/3} ≈ t^{2/3} inflates the denominator, so eta shrinks faster than a plain (sum||d||^2)^{-1/3} would — fast enough to control the smoothness term Z_t even though I don't know L, because the extra t^{2/3} of shrinkage does the job that STORM's explicit L^2 did. The two schedules are now adaptively coupled, in the direction I *can* compute: the momentum schedule feeds the step schedule, not the other way around. One subtlety I should flag to myself: the reweighting uses a_{i+1}, the *next* momentum coefficient, not a_i. At iteration t I compute a_{t+1} = 1/(1 + sum_{i<=t}||g_i||^2)^{2/3} from gradients I already have (g_1..g_t), so a_{t+1} is available before I take the step — it's not really "the future," it's just an index bookkeeping that says "weight the t-th estimate by the momentum that will govern the t-th-to-(t+1) transition." It will make the analysis a little more delicate (a_{t+1} depends on the same noise as d_t), but it's perfectly computable.

Let me also note: the only safe initialization that stays no-checkpoint is d_1 = g_1 = grad f(x_1;xi_1), a single gradient on a single sample — no mega-batch. And eps_0 := 0 by convention. Good, that preserves the whole point.

  (1/3) E sum_t ||eps_t||^2 <= 24 L^2 T^{2/9}(E sum_t ||d_t||^2)^{1/3} + 8 sigma^2 T^{1/3},

i.e. E sum_t ||eps_t||^2 <= 72 L^2 T^{2/9}(E sum_t ||d_t||^2)^{1/3} + 24 sigma^2 T^{1/3}. So the accumulated error is controlled by the accumulated *estimate* energy E sum||d||^2 (to the 1/3 power) plus a noise floor. Good.

Part two: turn that into a bound on E sum_t ||gbar_t||^2. Split into two cases on whether the error dominates the signal. Case 1: E sum||eps||^2 >= (1/2) E sum||gbar||^2. Since d_t = gbar_t + eps_t, ||d_t||^2 <= 2||gbar_t||^2 + 2||eps_t||^2, so E sum||d||^2 <= 2E sum||gbar||^2 + 2E sum||eps||^2 <= 4E sum||eps||^2 + 2E sum||eps||^2 = 6E sum||eps||^2 (using the case condition to replace E sum||gbar||^2 by 2E sum||eps||^2). Plug into the part-one bound: E sum||eps||^2 <= 72 L^2 T^{2/9}(6 E sum||eps||^2)^{1/3} + 24 sigma^2 T^{1/3}. Let S = E sum||eps||^2. Then S <= c1 T^{2/9} S^{1/3} + c2 sigma^2 T^{1/3}; the S^{1/3} term, being sublinear in S, can be solved out — S <= max(2 c2 sigma^2 T^{1/3}, (2 c1 T^{2/9})^{3/2}) <= O((L^3 + sigma^2) T^{1/3}). (The (T^{2/9})^{3/2} = T^{1/3}, exactly the right power.) And by the case condition E sum||gbar||^2 <= 2 S <= O((L^3 + sigma^2)T^{1/3}). Case 2: E sum||eps||^2 <= (1/2)E sum||gbar||^2. Then E sum||d||^2 <= 2E sum||gbar||^2 + 2·(1/2)E sum||gbar||^2 = 3E sum||gbar||^2. Now I use the smoothness-plus-update bound exactly like the offline case but carrying eps: from F(x_{t+1}) - F(x_t) <= -eta_t gbar_t^T d_t + (L eta_t^2/2)||d_t||^2 = -eta_t||gbar_t||^2 - eta_t gbar_t^T eps_t + (L eta_t^2/2)||d_t||^2, Young on the cross term (-gbar^T eps <= (1/2)||gbar||^2 + (1/2)||eps||^2) gives ||gbar_t||^2 <= ||eps_t||^2 + (2/eta_t)(Delta_t - Delta_{t+1}) + L eta_t ||d_t||^2. Sum, telescope the (2/eta_t)(Delta_t - Delta_{t+1}) part by parts to 2B/eta_T (using Delta in [0,B], 1/eta non-decreasing), and telescope the L eta_t||d_t||^2 part by p=1/3 to (3/2)L(sum||d||^2)^{2/3}:

  sum_t ||gbar_t||^2 <= sum_t ||eps_t||^2 + 2B(sum_t||d_t||^2/a_{t+1})^{1/3} + (3/2)L(sum_t||d_t||^2)^{2/3}.

With a_{t+1} = 1/T^{2/3} the middle term is 2B T^{2/9}(sum||d||^2)^{1/3}. Take expectation, use E sum||d||^2 <= 3E sum||gbar||^2 and E sum||eps||^2 <= (1/2)E sum||gbar||^2 and Jensen on z^{1/3}, z^{2/3}:

  E sum||gbar||^2 <= (1/2)E sum||gbar||^2 + 2B T^{2/9}(3E sum||gbar||^2)^{1/3} + (3/2)L(3E sum||gbar||^2)^{2/3}.

Subtract the (1/2): (1/2)E sum||gbar||^2 <= (sublinear in E sum||gbar||^2) terms, solve out the 1/3 and 2/3 powers -> E sum||gbar||^2 <= O(L^3 + B^{3/2} T^{1/3}). Combining the two cases, E sum_t ||gbar_t||^2 <= O((L^3 + sigma^2 + B^{3/2})T^{1/3}). Random iterate + Jensen: E||grad F(xbar_T)|| <= sqrt(E sum||gbar||^2/T) <= O(sqrt(L^3+sigma^2+B^{3/2})/T^{1/3}). There it is — the optimal 1/T^{1/3}, parameter-free in the algorithm (L, B, sigma only in the final constant). This simplified version doesn't recover 1/sqrt T when sigma = 0 (the sigma^2 T^{1/3} term is from D = sum a_t with the non-adaptive a, which doesn't notice the absence of noise), which is exactly why I want the *adaptive* a_t. But the spine is proven.

Now the hard version: a_{t+1} = 1/(1+sum_{i<=t}||g_i||^2)^{2/3}, which is *random* (it depends on the noisy g_i). Two new headaches. First, a_t is no longer deterministic-given-past in the convenient way — it's H_{t-1}-measurable still (depends on g_1..g_{t-1}), good, but it's correlated with the noise, so I can't pull it out of expectations as a constant. Second, and worse, the reciprocal increments 1/a_{t+1} - 1/a_t are no longer bounded by 2/3: a single large ||g_t||^2 can make 1/a_{t+1} = (1+sum_{i<=t}||g||^2)^{2/3} jump a lot in one step. The term-B bound depended on 1/a_{t+1} - 1/a_t <= 2/3, so it's in danger.

Handle the increment problem with a stopping time. The increment 1/a_{t+1} - 1/a_t = (1+sum_{i<=t}||g||^2)^{2/3} - (1+sum_{i<t}||g||^2)^{2/3}; by concavity of y^{2/3}, this is <= (2/3)||g_t||^2/(1+sum_{i<t}||g||^2)^{1/3} = (2/3) sqrt(a_t) ||g_t||^2. So the increment is small *once a_t is small enough*: if sqrt(a_t)||g_t||^2 <= 1, i.e. a_t <= 1/||g_t||^4, the increment is <= 2/3. With ||g_t||^2 <= G^2 (the gradient bound — used in the *analysis only*, not the algorithm), define beta = min{1, 1/G^4} and the stopping time tau* = max{t in [T]: a_t >= beta}. For t > tau*, a_t < beta <= 1/G^4, so sqrt(a_t)||g_t||^2 <= sqrt(beta)G^2 <= sqrt(1/G^4)·G^2 = 1, giving 1/a_{t+1} - 1/a_t <= (2/3)sqrt(beta)G^2 <= 2/3. So *after* tau* the old increment bound is restored. And tau* is genuinely a stopping time because a_{t+1} is H_t-measurable. (Note tau* is finite and random; a_t is non-increasing so once it drops below beta it stays below.) Before tau*, a_t >= beta, so 1/a_t <= 1/beta is just bounded by a constant — I don't need the increment bound there, I can bound the whole prefix sum sum_{t<=tau*}||eps_t||^2 directly.

So the plan splits sum||eps_t||^2 = sum_{t<=tau*}||eps_t||^2 + sum_{t>tau*}||eps_t||^2, control the prefix, then control the whole. Prefix first. Take the eps recursion, square and sum to tau*, keeping the martingale piece explicit this time (because I can't take expectations naively with a random stopping time — I'll use optional stopping):

  sum_{t<=tau*}||eps_t||^2 <= sum_{t<=tau*}(1-a_t)||eps_{t-1}||^2 + 2 sum_{t<=tau*}||Z_t||^2 + 2 sum_{t<=tau*} a_t^2||g_t - gbar_t||^2 + sum_{t<=tau*} M_t,

where M_t = 2(1-a_t)eps_{t-1}^T((1-a_t)Z_t + a_t(g_t - gbar_t)) is a martingale difference (E[M_t|H_{t-1}] = 0, since the two factors inside are zero-mean given the past while (1-a_t)eps_{t-1} is given). Rearrange the contraction: sum(1-a_t)||eps_{t-1}||^2 = sum||eps_{t-1}||^2 - sum a_t ||eps_{t-1}||^2, and move terms so that, using a_t >= beta on the prefix,

  beta sum_{t<=tau*}||eps_t||^2 <= ||eps_{tau*}||^2 + sum_{t<tau*} a_{t+1}||eps_t||^2 <= 2 sum_{t<=T}||Z_t||^2 + 2 sum_{t<=T} a_t^2||g_t-gbar_t||^2 + sum_{t<=tau*}M_t.

(I extended the Z and noise sums to T, only enlarging them, and bounded the prefix's leftover error by the full sums.) Now I bound the three pieces.

For the first piece, sum ||Z_t||^2 <= 4L^2 sum eta_{t-1}^2||d_{t-1}||^2 = 4L^2 sum (||d_{t-1}||^2)/(sum_{i<t}||d_i||^2/a_{i+1})^{2/3}. Here I bound the denominator below by dropping the 1/a (eta_t = (sum||d||^2/a)^{-1/3} <= (sum||d||^2)^{-1/3}), giving the b/(sum b)^{2/3} shape with b = ||d||^2, and the p=2/3 telescope gives 12 L^2 (sum_t||d_t||^2)^{1/3}. For the second piece, E sum a_t^2||g_t - gbar_t||^2 is controlled by the raw-gradient energy. Since a_t is H_{t-1}-measurable and E[||g_t - gbar_t||^2|H_{t-1}] = E[||g_t||^2|H_{t-1}] - ||gbar_t||^2 <= E[||g_t||^2|H_{t-1}], I get E[a_t^2||g_t-gbar_t||^2] <= E[a_t^2||g_t||^2]. And a_t^2 = 1/(1+sum_{i<t}||g||^2)^{4/3}, so E sum a_t^2||g_t||^2 = E sum ||g_t||^2/(1+sum_{i<t}||g_i||^2)^{4/3}. This is a delayed AdaGrad sum with exponent 4/3 > 1, and a sum of b_t/(1+sum_{j<t}b_j)^{4/3} with b_t in [0,G^2] is bounded by a constant; I will prove it in a second, and it gives C_1 = 12 + 2G^2. For the third piece, sum_{t<=tau*}M_t disappears in expectation because tau* is a bounded stopping time and {M_t} is a martingale difference sequence, so Doob's optional stopping theorem gives E sum_{t<=tau*}M_t = 0. Therefore E[beta sum_{t<=tau*}||eps_t||^2] <= 2 C_1 + 24 L^2 (E sum_t||d_t||^2)^{1/3} (Jensen on z^{1/3}), i.e.

  E sum_{t<=tau*}||eps_t||^2 <= 2 C_1/beta + 24 (L^2/beta)(E sum_t||d_t||^2)^{1/3}.

The exponent-4/3 lemma deserves a real proof, not a citation. Claim: for a_1,...,a_n in [0, amax], sum_{i=1}^n a_i/(1 + sum_{j<=i} a_j)^{4/3} <= 12. Dyadic blocking. Define N_0 = max{i: sum_{j<=i}a_j <= 2}, and for k >= 1, N_k = max{i: 2^k < sum_{j<=i}a_j <= 2^{k+1}}. On the zeroth block the denominator (1+partial sum)^{4/3} >= 1, so its contribution is <= sum_{i<=N_0}a_i <= 2. On block k, every term has 1 + sum_{j<=i}a_j > 2^k, so denominator > (2^k)^{4/3}, and the numerators in the block sum to at most 2^{k+1} (the partial-sum increase across the block), so block k contributes <= 2^{k+1}/(2^k)^{4/3} = 2·2^{k(1 - 4/3)} = 2·(2^{-1/3})^k. Sum over k >= 1: 2 sum_{k>=1}(2^{-1/3})^k = 2·(2^{-1/3}/(1-2^{-1/3})) = 2/(2^{1/3}-1) < 10. Total <= 2 + 10 = 12. (The exponent 4/3 > 1 is exactly what makes the geometric series converge; with exponent 1 it would be a divergent harmonic-type sum — log, not constant. So the choice a_t ∝ (1+sum||g||^2)^{-2/3}, giving a_t^2 ∝ (.)^{-4/3}, is precisely tuned to land the variance term at a *constant*.) The "delayed" version with 1+sum_{j<i} instead of 1+sum_{j<=i}: split off at N_0 = min{i: sum_{j<i}a_j >= amax}; the prefix i < N_0 has 1+sum_{j<i} >= 1 so contributes <= sum a_i <= 2 amax, and after N_0 the floor 1+amax+sum lets me replace 1+sum_{j<i} by 1+a_i+sum_{j<i} = 1+sum_{j<=i} and apply the non-delayed lemma, total <= 2 amax + 12. With amax = G^2: C_1 = 12 + 2G^2.

Now the second part, bounding the *full* E sum_{t<=T}||eps_t||^2. Same eps recursion, but this time divide by sqrt(a_t) rather than a_t (so the algebra stays clean with the random a), square, and sum:

  (1/a_t)||eps_t||^2 <= (1/a_t - 1)||eps_{t-1}||^2 + 2||Z_t||^2/a_t + 2 a_t||g_t - gbar_t||^2 + Y_t,

where Y_t = 2((1-a_t)/sqrt(a_t))eps_{t-1}^T(sqrt(a_t)(g_t-gbar_t) + ((1-a_t)/sqrt(a_t))Z_t) is again a martingale difference (E[Y_t|H_{t-1}] = 0). (The 1/a_t - 2 + a_t coefficient on ||eps_{t-1}||^2 from expanding (1-a_t)^2/a_t I upper-bounded by 1/a_t - 1 using a_t in [0,1].) Rearrange to E sum||eps_{t-1}||^2 <= boundary + sum_t(1/a_{t+1} - 1/a_t)||eps_t||^2 + 2 sum||Z_t||^2/a_t + 2 sum a_t||g_t-gbar_t||^2 + sum Y_t. Name them A, B, C, D, E. A <= -E||eps_T||^2/a_T <= 0 (drop, since a_T <= 1). E: martingale, E sum Y_t = 0.

Term B is where the stopping time pays off. Split at tau*: B = sum_{t<=tau*}(1/a_{t+1} - 1/a_t)||eps_t||^2 + sum_{t>tau*}(1/a_{t+1} - 1/a_t)||eps_t||^2. On the prefix I can't bound the increment by 2/3, but I *can* bound 1/a_{t+1} by a constant: I'll show 1/a_{t+1} <= 1/betatilde for all t <= tau*, where 1/betatilde := (1/beta^{3/2} + G^2)^{2/3}. Indeed 1/a_{t+1} = (1 + sum_{i<=t}||g||^2)^{2/3}; raise to the 3/2: (1/a_{t+1})^{3/2} = 1 + sum_{i<=t}||g||^2 = (1/a_t)^{3/2} + ||g_t||^2. At t = tau*, (1/a_{tau*})^{3/2} <= (1/beta)^{3/2} since a_{tau*} >= beta, and ||g_{tau*}||^2 <= G^2, so (1/a_{tau*+1})^{3/2} <= 1/beta^{3/2} + G^2, i.e. 1/a_{tau*+1} <= (1/beta^{3/2}+G^2)^{2/3} = 1/betatilde, and since 1/a is non-decreasing in t this bounds 1/a_{t+1} for all t <= tau*. So the prefix part of B is <= (1/betatilde) sum_{t<=tau*}||eps_t||^2 (telescoping the increments to the largest reciprocal). On the suffix t > tau*, the increment is <= 2/3, so the suffix part is <= (2/3) sum_{t>tau*}||eps_t||^2 <= (2/3) sum_{t<=T}||eps_t||^2. Hence E B <= (1/betatilde) E sum_{t<=tau*}||eps_t||^2 + (2/3) E sum_t||eps_t||^2, and the first piece I already bounded by the prefix result: <= (1/betatilde)(2C_1/beta + 24(L^2/beta)(E sum||d||^2)^{1/3}) + (2/3)E sum||eps||^2.

For the unmultiplied term C = E sum ||Z_t||^2/a_t, the smoothness bound gives C <= 4L^2 E sum eta_{t-1}^2||d_{t-1}||^2/a_t = 4L^2 E sum (||d_{t-1}||^2/a_t)/(sum_{i<t}||d_i||^2/a_{i+1})^{2/3}. The p=2/3 telescope on b_t = ||d_t||^2/a_{t+1} gives C <= 12 L^2 (sum||d||^2/a_{t+1})^{1/3} <= 12 L^2 (1/a_T)^{1/3}(sum||d||^2)^{1/3} <= 12 L^2 (1+sum||g||^2)^{2/9}(sum||d||^2)^{1/3}. Now there's a 2/9 power of the gradient-energy multiplying a 1/3 power of the estimate-energy — I want to break it into a constant times (sum||g||^2)^{1/3} plus a small multiple of sum||d||^2, so it doesn't feed back uncontrollably. Young's inequality with exponents 3/2 and 3: for any rho > 0, a^{2/9}b^{1/3} = (a rho^{9/2})^{2/9}(b/rho^3)^{1/3} <= (2/3)a^{1/3}rho^{3/2} + (1/3)b/rho^3. Take a = 1+sum||g||^2, b = sum||d||^2, rho = (512 L^2)^{1/3}: then C <= 512 L^3 (1+sum||g||^2)^{1/3} + (1/128) sum||d||^2. The full error inequality carries 2C, so this contributes 1024 L^3 (1+sum||g||^2)^{1/3} and (1/64)sum||d||^2 after the outer factor of two.

Term D = 2 E sum a_t||g_t - gbar_t||^2 <= 2 E sum a_t||g_t||^2 (same conditional-variance trick) = 2 E sum ||g_t||^2/(1+sum_{i<t}||g||^2)^{2/3}. This is the delayed AdaGrad sum but with exponent 2/3 < 1 now (not 4/3), so it does *not* stay constant — it telescopes to a (sum||g||^2)^{1/3}: by the shifted technical lemma, sum b_t/(b_0 + sum_{j<t}b_j)^p <= b/b_0^p + (2/(1-p))(b_0 + sum b_t)^{1-p}; with b_0 = 1, p = 2/3, b = G^2: D <= 2(G^2 + 6(1+sum||g||^2)^{1/3}). (The shifted lemma is proved by the same delayed-blocking idea: split at T_0 = min{i: sum_{j<i}b_j >= b}; prefix gives b/b_0^p, suffix replaces b_0 + sum_{j<i} by (1/2)b + (1/2)sum_{j<i} >= (1/2)sum_{j<=i} and applies the non-shifted telescope with the factor 2^p <= 2 out front.)

Collecting A,...,E (E and A drop), absorbing the (2/3) from B to the left:

  (1/3) E sum_t||eps_t||^2 <= (24 L^2/(beta betatilde))(E sum||d||^2)^{1/3} + 2C_1/(beta betatilde) + 2G^2 + (1024 L^3 + 12)(1 + E sum||g||^2)^{1/3} + (1/64) E sum||d||^2.

(The (1/64) is from C's (1/128) doubled by the factor-2 in front of C in the original sum.) Now the last part: convert to E sum||gbar||^2, two cases, exactly as in the simplified proof.

Case 1: E sum||eps||^2 >= (1/2)E sum||gbar||^2. Then E sum||d||^2 <= 6 E sum||eps||^2, and E sum||g||^2 = E sum||gbar||^2 + E sum||g - gbar||^2 <= E sum||gbar||^2 + sigma^2 T <= 2 E sum||eps||^2 + sigma^2 T. Plug E sum||d||^2 <= 6 E sum||eps||^2 and E sum||g||^2 <= 2 E sum||eps||^2 + sigma^2 T into the displayed bound. The dominant left term is (1/3)E sum||eps||^2 minus the (1/64)·6 E sum||eps||^2 from E sum||d||^2; (1/3 - 6/64) = (1/3 - 3/32) > 1/5, so (1/5)E sum||eps||^2 <= (const)(E sum||eps||^2)^{1/3} + (1024L^3+12)(1+sigma^2 T+2E sum||eps||^2)^{1/3} + constants. The right side is sublinear in E sum||eps||^2 (powers 1/3), with the only T-growth being the sigma^2 T inside a 1/3 power -> (sigma^2 T)^{1/3} = sigma^{2/3}T^{1/3}. Solving the sublinear inequality, E sum||gbar||^2 <= 2 E sum||eps||^2 <= O(1 + C_1/(beta betatilde) + (L^2/(beta betatilde))^{3/2} + G^2 + L^3 + L^{9/2} + L^3 sigma^{2/3}T^{1/3}).

Case 2: E sum||eps||^2 <= (1/2)E sum||gbar||^2. Then E sum||d||^2 <= 3 E sum||gbar||^2, and I use the smoothness-plus-update bound from before, now with the general a_{t+1} so the middle term is 2B(sum||d||^2/a_{T+1})^{1/3} = 2B(1+sum||g||^2)^{2/9}(sum||d||^2)^{1/3} — and I apply the *same* Young's-inequality split (now with rho = (128B/3)^{1/3}, a = 1+sum||g||^2, b = sum||d||^2) to break it into 20 B^{3/2}(1+sum||g||^2)^{1/3} + (1/64)sum||d||^2. So sum||gbar||^2 <= sum||eps||^2 + (3/2)L(sum||d||^2)^{2/3} + 20 B^{3/2}(1+sum||g||^2)^{1/3} + (1/64)sum||d||^2. Take expectation, plug E sum||d||^2 <= 3E sum||gbar||^2, E sum||eps||^2 <= (1/2)E sum||gbar||^2, E sum||g||^2 <= sigma^2 T + E sum||gbar||^2, Jensen on z^{1/3},z^{2/3}: E sum||gbar||^2 <= (1/2 + 3/64)E sum||gbar||^2 + (3/2)L(3E sum||gbar||^2)^{2/3} + 20B^{3/2}(1+sigma^2 T + E sum||gbar||^2)^{1/3}. Move the (1/2+3/64) over: (~ 1/2)E sum||gbar||^2 <= 6L(3E sum||gbar||^2)^{2/3} + 80B^{3/2}(1+sigma^2 T+E sum||gbar||^2)^{1/3}, sublinear powers 2/3 and 1/3, solve: E sum||gbar||^2 <= O(1 + L^3 + B^{9/4} + B^{3/2}sigma^{2/3}T^{1/3}).

Take the worse of the two cases. Both have the same noise structure: a T-independent constant (the M^2 part) plus a sigma^{2/3}T^{1/3} term (the kappa^2 part). Reading off, E sum_t||gbar_t||^2 <= O(M^2 + kappa^2 sigma^{2/3}T^{1/3}) with kappa^2 = B^{3/2} + L^3 and M^2 = 1 + L^{9/2} + B^{9/4} + G^{10} + (L^2 G^8)^{3/2} (the betatilde-dependent pieces unpack into the G powers, since 1/betatilde involves G^2 and 1/beta involves G^4). Finally random-iterate + Jensen:

  E||grad F(xbar_T)|| = sqrt(E sum||gbar||^2/T) <= O(M/sqrt T + kappa sigma^{1/3}/T^{1/3}),

with kappa = O(B^{3/4} + L^{3/2}), M = O(1 + L^{9/4} + B^{9/8} + G^5 + (L G^4)^{3/2}). And there is the whole prize. The rate is O(1/T^{1/3}) in the stochastic case — optimal for the expectation-over-smooth-losses class — and when sigma = 0 the second term vanishes and I'm left with M/sqrt T = O(1/sqrt T), so the method *adapts to the variance* automatically: noiseless gets the faster 1/sqrt T, noisy gets the optimal 1/T^{1/3}, and I never told it which regime it's in. And critically, L, G, B, sigma appear only in the constants M, kappa — *never in the schedules eta_t, a_t the algorithm computes*. That's the parameter-free claim, earned. (Compared with the simplified non-adaptive a_{t+1}=1/t^{2/3} schedule, the adaptive a_t changes the accumulated noisy term from the cruder sigma^2 T^{1/3} dependence to the sigma^{2/3}T^{1/3} term that becomes kappa sigma^{1/3}/T^{1/3} after the random-iterate square root; and compared with the constant-knowing method, the cleaner 1/sqrt T term sheds the (log T)^{3/4} factor.)

Let me also be honest with myself about what the analysis quietly assumed beyond smoothness and bounded variance, so I know the method's limits. The proof used a bound on the gradient norm G (in the stopping-time argument and the C_1 term) and a bound on the *range* of function values B = max|F(x)-F(y)| (in the smoothness-plus-update telescope, where Delta_t had to live in [0,B]). Both enter only the final constants, not the schedules — but the B assumption is genuinely needed by *this* argument; without it the (2B/eta_T)(Delta telescope) step has nothing to bound Delta_t by. I'll note that as the price of this particular proof route.

So let me write the method as the loop I'd actually run, being careful about the index bookkeeping that the a_{t+1}-inside-eta coupling forces. At iteration t I already hold d_t, the corresponding current stochastic gradient g_t (with d_1 = g_1 from the warm start), and the running sums; I (1) fold this step's ||g_t||^2 into the gradient-energy sum and form a_{t+1} from it; (2) fold ||d_t||^2/a_{t+1} into the estimate-energy sum and form eta_t; (3) step; (4) draw a fresh sample and make the two oracle calls g_{t+1} = grad f(x_{t+1};xi_{t+1}) and gtilde_t = grad f(x_t;xi_{t+1}); (5) recurse d_{t+1} = g_{t+1} + (1-a_{t+1})(d_t - gtilde_t), and carry g_{t+1} forward as the next iteration's current gradient. The norms in the schedules are full-vector Euclidean norms over all parameters — a *scalar* step size and a *scalar* momentum per iteration, not per-coordinate. Filling the one empty slot of the corrected-momentum harness:

```python
import math
import torch


def grad(model, loss_fn, x, batch):
    """One stochastic gradient grad f(x; xi), flattened to a single vector."""
    set_flat_params(model, x)
    model.zero_grad(set_to_none=True)
    loss = loss_fn(model, batch)
    loss.backward()
    return flat_grad(model)                       # 1-D tensor over all parameters


class StormPlus:
    """STORM+ : fully parameter-free, no-checkpoint variance reduction.

    Corrected-momentum recursion (STORM template), with momentum a_t and step
    eta_t set from observed gradient/estimate norms only -- no smoothness L,
    gradient bound G, or variance sigma anywhere in the schedule.

        a_{t+1} = 1 / (1 + sum_{i<=t} ||g_i||^2) ^ (2/3)
        eta_t   = 1 / (sum_{i<=t} ||d_i||^2 / a_{i+1}) ^ (1/3)
        x_{t+1} = x_t - eta_t d_t
        d_{t+1} = g_{t+1} + (1 - a_{t+1}) (d_t - grad f(x_t; xi_{t+1}))
    """

    def __init__(self, model, loss_fn, x_init):
        self.model = model
        self.loss_fn = loss_fn
        self.x = x_init.clone()              # current iterate x_t
        self.g = None                        # current stochastic gradient g_t
        self.d = None                        # gradient estimate d_t
        self.sum_g2 = 0.0                    # sum_{i<=t-1} ||g_i||^2 (gradient energy)
        self.sum_d2_over_a = 0.0             # sum_{i<=t-1} ||d_i||^2 / a_{i+1} (estimate energy)

    def step(self, batch=None):
        if self.d is None:                                   # warm start: d_1 = g_1 (one sample)
            if batch is None:
                batch = draw_sample()
            self.g = grad(self.model, self.loss_fn, self.x, batch)
            self.d = self.g.clone()

        g_t = self.g                                         # grad f(x_t; xi_t)

        # a_{t+1} from cumulative gradient norms -> shape ~ t^{-2/3}, in (0,1]
        self.sum_g2 += g_t.pow(2).sum().item()
        a_next = 1.0 / (1.0 + self.sum_g2) ** (2.0 / 3.0)

        # eta_t from cumulative estimate norms reweighted by 1/a -> the lr<->momentum coupling
        self.sum_d2_over_a += self.d.pow(2).sum().item() / a_next
        eta_t = 1.0 / (self.sum_d2_over_a) ** (1.0 / 3.0)

        x_old = self.x.clone()
        with torch.no_grad():
            self.x = self.x - eta_t * self.d                 # x_{t+1} = x_t - eta_t d_t

        # refresh on a FRESH sample: two oracle calls, current and previous iterate (no checkpoint)
        next_batch = draw_sample()
        g_next = grad(self.model, self.loss_fn, self.x, next_batch)    # grad f(x_{t+1}; xi_{t+1})
        g_tilde = grad(self.model, self.loss_fn, x_old, next_batch)    # grad f(x_t;     xi_{t+1})
        self.d = g_next + (1.0 - a_next) * (self.d - g_tilde)          # corrected momentum
        self.g = g_next                                                # g_{t+1} for next iteration
        return eta_t, a_next
```

Let me trace the causal chain one more time. I wanted the optimal 1/T^{1/3} stationarity rate without checkpoints, which STORM already had — but STORM bought it by hardwiring the smoothness L and the gradient bound G into its step eta ∝ (sum g^2)^{-1/3} and its momentum a ∝ L^2 eta^2, and its proof collapses without them, so it wasn't parameter-free. I traced the proof and saw the constants enter only through the requirement that the step control the smoothness term 4L^2 eta^2||d||^2 and the momentum control the variance term a^2 sigma^2. AdaGrad told me how to set a schedule from observed squared norms with a summable telescope, and the exponents were forced by the target rate: 1/3 on the step (so the d-energy terms telescope to a cube root that lands 1/T^{1/3}, using ||d|| not ||g|| so the numerators actually cancel) and 2/3 on the momentum (so a_t mimics STORM's t^{-2/3} decay and the reciprocal increments stay bounded by 2/3). The circular L-dependence — a tied to eta via L — I broke by reversing the coupling: instead of a ∝ L^2 eta^2, make eta depend on a, eta_t = (sum||d||^2/a_{i+1})^{-1/3}, where the extra 1/a ≈ t^{2/3} of shrinkage does L's old job parameter-free. I verified the construction in three layers: offline gives 1/sqrt T from the step alone; simplified-momentum gives 1/T^{1/3} and exposes the error-then-signal two-case spine; and the fully adaptive a_t — random, with unbounded reciprocal increments — is tamed by a stopping time tau* that restores the 2/3 increment bound after a_t drops below beta = min{1,1/G^4}, a constant-bounded prefix before it, a martingale/optional-stopping argument for the cross terms, the exponent-4/3 dyadic-blocking lemma that pins the variance term at a constant, and Young's inequality to split the mixed g-energy/d-energy products. The result is E||grad F(xbar_T)|| <= O(M/sqrt T + kappa sigma^{1/3}/T^{1/3}): optimal in the noisy case, automatically 1/sqrt T when noiseless, with L, G, B, sigma confined to the constants and absent from every schedule the algorithm computes — two oracle calls per step, no batch, no anchor, nothing to tune but an overall step-size scale.
