Let me start from the thing that bothers me. People are training agents to super-human strength at GO and Starcraft and Dota with *self-play* — the agent plays against copies of itself, no human expert, no curated opponent, and it gets strong. That cuts against what I'd have said a priori, which is that to learn a strong policy you need a strong opponent to push you. So something about playing against yourself is enough. I want to know whether that's *provably* true, and if so, how many games of self-play you actually need. Not a heuristic story — a sample-complexity statement.

First I have to pin down what "strong" even means here, because in a two-player game it is slippery. In a single-agent MDP, "good policy" means "high value," full stop. Here there are two agents with opposite goals. If I fix the opponent's policy $\nu$ and ask the max-player to do well, that's just an MDP and I'd be learning a *best response* to $\nu$. But that's not what I want — the opponent isn't fixed, it's also learning, and ultimately I want the pair that no one wants to deviate from. That's the Nash equilibrium. So let me write down the objects carefully before I do anything clever.

Tabular episodic zero-sum Markov game: states $\mathcal S$, the max-player picks $a\in\mathcal A$, the min-player picks $b\in\mathcal B$, *simultaneously*, at each of $H$ steps; reward $r_h(s,a,b)\in[0,1]$ goes to the max-player and against the min-player; transition $\mathbb P_h(\cdot\mid s,a,b)$. Policies $\mu_h:\mathcal S\to\Delta_{\mathcal A}$, $\nu_h:\mathcal S\to\Delta_{\mathcal B}$. The value of a fixed pair satisfies the obvious Bellman equation,
$$Q^{\mu,\nu}_h(s,a,b) = r_h(s,a,b) + [\mathbb P_h V^{\mu,\nu}_{h+1}](s,a,b),\qquad V^{\mu,\nu}_h(s) = \sum_{a,b}\mu_h(a\mid s)\,\nu_h(b\mid s)\,Q^{\mu,\nu}_h(s,a,b).$$
Now the best response. Against a fixed $\mu$, the min-player can compute $V^{\mu,\dagger}_h(s)=\inf_\nu V^{\mu,\nu}_h(s)$, and symmetrically $V^{\dagger,\nu}$. There exist policies $\mu^\star,\nu^\star$ optimal against best responses, and — this I need to be careful about — the minimax theorem holds at the level of the *whole game*:
$$\sup_\mu\inf_\nu V^{\mu,\nu}_h(s) = V^{\mu^\star,\nu^\star}_h(s) = \inf_\nu\sup_\mu V^{\mu,\nu}_h(s).$$
So $(\mu^\star,\nu^\star)$ is the Nash equilibrium. But notice $V^{\mu,\nu}_h(s)$ is *not* bilinear in $(\mu,\nu)$ — it's bilinear only in the immediate step, and the continuation couples them through the transition. So this minimax equality is not von Neumann's matrix theorem; it's Shapley's, and it's genuinely a statement about the multi-step game. Whether that distinction does any real work, or is just bookkeeping, I can't tell yet — I'll watch for it.

How should I score an algorithm? Here is where I have to resist the easy choice. The deployed pair in episode $k$ is $(\mu^k,\nu^k)$. The lazy regret would compare $V^{\mu^k,\nu^k}$ to the Nash value $V^\star$. But that doesn't capture the thing I care about: I want the *max-player's* policy to be robust against *any* opponent, and the min-player's likewise. So I should compare each deployed policy against *its own best response*. Define
$$\mathrm{Regret}(K)=\sum_{k=1}^K\Big[V^{\dagger,\nu^k}_1(s_1^k) - V^{\mu^k,\dagger}_1(s_1^k)\Big].$$
Stare at the summand. $V^{\dagger,\nu^k}_1$ is what the min-player suffers when the max-player best-responds to her $\nu^k$ — so it's an *upper* gauge of $\nu^k$'s quality. $V^{\mu^k,\dagger}_1$ is what the max-player earns when the min-player best-responds to his $\mu^k$ — a *lower* gauge of $\mu^k$'s quality. And because of the minimax value $V^\star$ sitting in between, I can split this exactly:
$$V^{\dagger,\nu^k}_1 - V^{\mu^k,\dagger}_1 = \underbrace{\big(V^{\dagger,\nu^k}_1 - V^\star_1\big)}_{\ge 0,\ \nu^k\text{'s exploitability}} + \underbrace{\big(V^\star_1 - V^{\mu^k,\dagger}_1\big)}_{\ge 0,\ \mu^k\text{'s exploitability}}.$$
Both terms are non-negative; the summand is a *duality gap*. So driving the regret to sublinear means both players' policies become unexploitable on average, and by an online-to-batch argument I'll get an $\epsilon$-approximate Nash pair, where "$\epsilon$-Nash" means exactly $V^{\dagger,\hat\nu}_1 - V^{\hat\mu,\dagger}_1\le\epsilon$ — good against best responses, not merely good in value. I'm flagging the phrase "against best responses" because it's doing more than it looks like, but I'll only believe that once it bites somewhere in the analysis.

Now, how to actually learn this with exploration, no simulator, no reachability assumption. I have a template that works in the single-agent world: optimism in the face of uncertainty. UCBVI: keep an *upper* estimate $Q^{\mathrm{up}}_h(s,a)$ built by running value iteration on the empirical model with an added exploration bonus $\beta_t$; prove that $Q^{\mathrm{up}}_h\ge Q^\star_h$ stays true throughout; play greedily with respect to $Q^{\mathrm{up}}$; the regret telescopes because the optimism overestimate shrinks as counts grow. The whole machine rests on one invariant: my estimate is *optimistic* — it never undersells the value I'm climbing toward.

So let me try to lift that directly. In the game I have two agents with opposite goals. The max-player wants the value high, the min-player wants it low. The natural symmetric move: maintain *two* optimistic estimates, an upper one $Q^{\mathrm{up}}$ and a lower one $Q^{\mathrm{low}}$, each built by the bonus-augmented empirical Bellman backup,
$$Q^{\mathrm{up}}_h(s,a,b)\leftarrow \min\{\,r_h + [\widehat{\mathbb P}_h V^{\mathrm{up}}_{h+1}](s,a,b) + \beta_t,\ H\},\qquad Q^{\mathrm{low}}_h(s,a,b)\leftarrow \max\{\,r_h + [\widehat{\mathbb P}_h V^{\mathrm{low}}_{h+1}](s,a,b) - \beta_t,\ 0\},$$
so that $Q^{\mathrm{up}}$ over-estimates and $Q^{\mathrm{low}}$ under-estimates. Then have the max-player play greedily with respect to $Q^{\mathrm{up}}$ (he wants it high) and the min-player play greedily with respect to $Q^{\mathrm{low}}$ (she wants it low). That feels right — each player is optimistic *for her own objective*.

And immediately I hit a wall. What does "greedy with respect to $Q^{\mathrm{up}}$" even mean for the max-player here? At state $s$, his payoff isn't a vector $Q^{\mathrm{up}}(s,a)$ that he can argmax over $a$; it's a *matrix* $Q^{\mathrm{up}}(s,a,b)$, indexed by his own action *and the opponent's*. He cannot pick a best $a$ without knowing the distribution over $b$. So "greedy" is not a well-defined operation for one player in isolation. The min-player has the symmetric problem with $Q^{\mathrm{low}}(s,a,b)$. Two players, each trying to be greedy with respect to her own matrix, with no rule that ties their choices together — that's not an algorithm, it's two ill-posed argmaxes.

What I actually want is not "$\mu$ is greedy with respect to $Q^{\mathrm{up}}$," but "$\mu$ is the max-player's best play *given that the min-player plays $\nu$*," and simultaneously "$\nu$ is the min-player's best play given $\mu$." In other words I want $(\mu,\nu)$ to be *jointly* greedy — a fixed point at this single state, this single step. That is precisely a one-step matrix game.

Here is the subtlety, and it took me a second to see. If I had a *single* matrix $Q(s,\cdot,\cdot)$, the joint-greedy condition would be the *zero-sum* equilibrium of that matrix: $\max_\phi\min_\psi \phi^\top Q\psi=\min_\psi\max_\phi\phi^\top Q\psi$ — von Neumann, solvable by an LP. Let me make sure I actually believe a mixed saddle is the right object here, on a concrete tiny matrix, because everything downstream rides on it. Take $Q=\begin{psmallmatrix}3&0\\1&2\end{psmallmatrix}$ (max picks a row, min a column), which has no pure saddle. For the row mixture $\mu=(p,1-p)$ to make the min-player indifferent I need column$_0$ and column$_1$ equal: $3p+(1-p)=2(1-p)$, i.e. $2p+1=2-2p$, $p=\tfrac14$. Symmetrically $\nu=(q,1-q)$ with $3q=2-q$ gives $q=\tfrac12$. Then the value is $\mu^\top Q\nu=\tfrac14\!\cdot\!3\!\cdot\!\tfrac12+\dots=1.5$. The saddle test: against $\nu=(\tfrac12,\tfrac12)$ every pure row pays $Q\nu=(1.5,1.5)$, so no row deviation beats $1.5$; against $\mu=(\tfrac14,\tfrac34)$ every pure column pays $\mu^\top Q=(1.5,1.5)$, so no column deviation drops below it. Both equal $1.5$ exactly — it is a genuine saddle, and a *mixed* one, which already tells me the per-state play must be a distribution, not a pure action. That single-matrix picture is exactly Shapley's per-state operator: solve the matrix game at each state, back up its value, iterate.

But I don't have one matrix. I have *two* — $Q^{\mathrm{up}}$ for the max-player and $Q^{\mathrm{low}}$ for the min-player — and they are *not equal*. The max-player's payoff matrix is $Q^{\mathrm{up}}$ and the min-player's is $Q^{\mathrm{low}}$. Two players, two different payoff matrices, simultaneous moves: that is a *general-sum* matrix game. The right joint-greedy notion is its Nash equilibrium:
$$(\mu_h(\cdot\mid s),\nu_h(\cdot\mid s)) = \textsc{Nash\_General\_Sum}\big(Q^{\mathrm{up}}_h(s,\cdot,\cdot),\,Q^{\mathrm{low}}_h(s,\cdot,\cdot)\big),$$
the pair $(\phi,\psi)$ with $\phi^\top Q^{\mathrm{up}}\psi=\max_{\tilde\phi}\tilde\phi^\top Q^{\mathrm{up}}\psi$ and $\phi^\top Q^{\mathrm{low}}\psi=\min_{\tilde\psi}\phi^\top Q^{\mathrm{low}}\tilde\psi$. Nash 1951 guarantees this exists. And then I read off the two state-values consistently with that pair: $V^{\mathrm{up}}_h(s)=\mu_h(s)^\top Q^{\mathrm{up}}_h(s,\cdot,\cdot)\nu_h(s)$ and $V^{\mathrm{low}}_h(s)=\mu_h(s)^\top Q^{\mathrm{low}}_h(s,\cdot,\cdot)\nu_h(s)$.

This is the self-play move, made precise. The two policies are computed *together*, by one routine that sees both estimates — not by each player independently maximizing her own. That joint computation is what "self-play" buys me over "train against an opponent": I get to coordinate the two policies because I control both. So the algorithm — call it value iteration with upper-and-lower confidence bounds — is: backward sweep over $h$, at each $(s,a,b)$ do the optimistic up/low backups, at each $s$ solve the general-sum game on $(Q^{\mathrm{up}},Q^{\mathrm{low}})$ to get $(\mu_h,\nu_h)$ and the two $V$'s; then play one episode sampling $a_h\sim\mu_h(s_h)$, $b_h\sim\nu_h(s_h)$, update counts.

Now I have to *prove* it, and proving it is where I'll find out whether the two-sidedness is decoration or load-bearing. The single-agent proof hinges on the invariant $Q^{\mathrm{up}}\ge Q^\star$. What's the analogous invariant here? My first instinct is the symmetric one: "$V^{\mathrm{up}}$ upper-bounds the Nash value $V^\star$ and $V^{\mathrm{low}}$ lower-bounds it." Let me try to push the regret summand through that and see if it closes.

The regret summand is $V^{\dagger,\nu^k}_1 - V^{\mu^k,\dagger}_1$ — built from the *best-response* values of the *deployed* policies $(\mu^k,\nu^k)$, not from $V^\star$. So let me check whether a band $V^{\mathrm{low}}\le V^\star\le V^{\mathrm{up}}$ even touches this quantity. Take the simplest non-trivial case, a one-step matching-pennies reward $R=\begin{psmallmatrix}0&1\\1&0\end{psmallmatrix}$, whose Nash value is $V^\star=0.5$. Suppose my algorithm has nailed the value: $V^{\mathrm{up}}=0.55$, $V^{\mathrm{low}}=0.45$, a band of width $0.1$ straddling $V^\star$. But suppose the pair it actually deployed is the degenerate $\mu^k=\nu^k=(1,0)$ — both always play action $0$. Then the max-player's best response to $\nu^k$ pays $\max_a (R\nu^k)_a=\max(0,1)=1$, and the min-player's best response to $\mu^k$ pays $\min_b(\mu^k R)_b=\min(0,1)=0$, so the summand is $1-0=1$. A band of width $0.1$ around $V^\star$ does not bound a summand of $1$. The band on $V^\star$ is simply blind to how exploitable the deployed policies are.

So the symmetric "bounds the Nash value" invariant is the wrong target; it can be perfectly tight and still say nothing about the regret. What the summand actually demands is that I bracket the *best-response* values directly:
$$V^{\mathrm{up},k}_1(s)\ \ge\ \sup_\mu V^{\mu,\nu^k}_1(s)\ =\ V^{\dagger,\nu^k}_1(s)\qquad\text{and}\qquad V^{\mathrm{low},k}_1(s)\ \le\ \inf_\nu V^{\mu^k,\nu}_1(s)\ =\ V^{\mu^k,\dagger}_1(s),$$
because *then* the summand is sandwiched: $V^{\dagger,\nu^k}_1-V^{\mu^k,\dagger}_1\le V^{\mathrm{up},k}_1-V^{\mathrm{low},k}_1$, and I "only" have to show the gap between my own up and low estimates is small. (In the counterexample this would force $V^{\mathrm{up}}\ge1$ and $V^{\mathrm{low}}\le0$, a band of width $\ge1$ — honestly reflecting that those degenerate policies are bad, which the $V^\star$-band hid.) So the invariant I need is: $Q^{\mathrm{up}}$ upper-bounds the value of the max-player's *best response to the currently-deployed $\nu^k$*, and $Q^{\mathrm{low}}$ lower-bounds the min-player's best response to the currently-deployed $\mu^k$. Optimism against the *opponent's best response to my own deployed policy*, not optimism toward the equilibrium — that is what ties the two estimates to the deployed policies and points them in opposite directions. And now I can answer the earlier flag: this is exactly where "against best responses" bites. It forces two estimates, not one.

Let me prove the upper invariant; the lower is symmetric. Fix $k$, induct downward from $h=H+1$. Base: $V^{\mathrm{up},k}_{H+1}=\sup_\mu V^{\mu,\nu^k}_{H+1}=0$. Inductive step. Suppose $V^{\mathrm{up},k}_{h+1}\ge\sup_\mu V^{\mu,\nu^k}_{h+1}$ pointwise. The two relevant $Q$'s:
$$Q^{\mathrm{up},k}_h(s,a,b)=\min\{r_h+[\widehat{\mathbb P}^k_h V^{\mathrm{up},k}_{h+1}](s,a,b)+\beta_t,\,H\},\qquad \sup_\mu Q^{\mu,\nu^k}_h(s,a,b)=r_h+[\mathbb P_h\sup_\mu V^{\mu,\nu^k}_{h+1}](s,a,b).$$
(That last identity is the best-response Bellman equation: fixing $(a,b)$ at step $h$, the continuation is the max-player's best response from $h+1$ on against $\nu^k$.) If the $\min$ clipped to $H$, the right side is $\le H$ trivially. Otherwise subtract:
$$Q^{\mathrm{up},k}_h - \sup_\mu Q^{\mu,\nu^k}_h = \big[\widehat{\mathbb P}^k_h\big(V^{\mathrm{up},k}_{h+1}-\sup_\mu V^{\mu,\nu^k}_{h+1}\big)\big] - \big[(\widehat{\mathbb P}^k_h-\mathbb P_h)\sup_\mu V^{\mu,\nu^k}_{h+1}\big] + \beta_t.$$
First bracket: $\widehat{\mathbb P}^k_h$ is a stochastic matrix, it preserves non-negativity, and by induction the function it averages is $\ge 0$, so the first bracket is $\ge 0$. Second bracket: this is empirical-minus-true applied to a *fixed* function in $[0,H]$, and that's exactly what the bonus is for. I need a concentration statement $|(\widehat{\mathbb P}^k_h-\mathbb P_h)V|\le\beta_t$ uniformly over $V$ in the value class, so the second bracket is $\ge-\beta_t$, and the whole thing is $\ge -\beta_t+\beta_t=0$. Hence $Q^{\mathrm{up},k}_h\ge\sup_\mu Q^{\mu,\nu^k}_h$ at the $Q$-level.

Now lift to $V$. This is where the *joint* computation pays off. By construction, $\mu^k_h(s)$ is the max-player's side of the general-sum equilibrium on $(Q^{\mathrm{up}},Q^{\mathrm{low}})$, which means $\mu^k_h(s)$ is a best response *in the $Q^{\mathrm{up}}$ matrix* against $\nu^k_h(s)$:
$$V^{\mathrm{up},k}_h(s)=\mu^k_h(s)^\top Q^{\mathrm{up},k}_h(s,\cdot,\cdot)\,\nu^k_h(s)=\max_{\phi\in\Delta_{\mathcal A}}\phi^\top Q^{\mathrm{up},k}_h(s,\cdot,\cdot)\,\nu^k_h(s).$$
And the true best-response value, with the *same* $\nu^k$, is $\sup_\mu V^{\mu,\nu^k}_h(s)=\max_{\phi}\phi^\top[\sup_\mu Q^{\mu,\nu^k}_h(s,\cdot,\cdot)]\nu^k_h(s)$. Let $\phi^\star$ be a maximizer for this true best-response matrix. Then
$$V^{\mathrm{up},k}_h(s)\ge(\phi^\star)^\top Q^{\mathrm{up},k}_h(s,\cdot,\cdot)\nu^k_h(s)\ge(\phi^\star)^\top[\sup_\mu Q^{\mu,\nu^k}_h(s,\cdot,\cdot)]\nu^k_h(s)=\sup_\mu V^{\mu,\nu^k}_h(s),$$
where the first inequality uses that $V^{\mathrm{up},k}_h$ is the maximum of the $Q^{\mathrm{up}}$ matrix against $\nu^k$, and the second uses the entrywise $Q$-level bound. For the lower side the signs reverse in exactly the way they should: when the lower backup is not clipped to $0$,
$$Q^{\mathrm{low},k}_h-\inf_\nu Q^{\mu^k,\nu}_h=\widehat{\mathbb P}^k_h\big(V^{\mathrm{low},k}_{h+1}-\inf_\nu V^{\mu^k,\nu}_{h+1}\big)-(\widehat{\mathbb P}^k_h-\mathbb P_h)\inf_\nu V^{\mu^k,\nu}_{h+1}-\beta_t\le0+\beta_t-\beta_t=0,$$
and if it is clipped to $0$, the true value is non-negative anyway. If $\psi^\star$ minimizes the true lower matrix against $\mu^k_h(s)$, then $V^{\mathrm{low},k}_h(s)\le(\mu^k_h(s))^\top Q^{\mathrm{low},k}_h(s,\cdot,\cdot)\psi^\star\le(\mu^k_h(s))^\top[\inf_\nu Q^{\mu^k,\nu}_h(s,\cdot,\cdot)]\psi^\star=\inf_\nu V^{\mu^k,\nu}_h(s)$. Putting both together I get the clean sandwich at every $(s,a,b,h,k)$:
$$Q^{\mathrm{up},k}_h(s,a,b)\ \ge\ \sup_\mu Q^{\mu,\nu^k}_h(s,a,b)\ \ge\ \inf_\nu Q^{\mu^k,\nu}_h(s,a,b)\ \ge\ Q^{\mathrm{low},k}_h(s,a,b).$$
That middle inequality, $\sup_\mu Q^{\mu,\nu^k}\ge\inf_\nu Q^{\mu^k,\nu}$, is just "best response to $\nu^k$ is at least as good for the max-player as the min-player's best response to $\mu^k$" — true because the fixed pair $(\mu^k,\nu^k)$ sits between both. Good. So the two-sidedness earned its keep: the band brackets the *best-response* values of the deployed policies, which is precisely the quantity the single $V^\star$-band failed to bound in the matching-pennies check above.

I owe myself the concentration lemma, because the bonus size determines the final rate, and I want to get the $S$-dependence right rather than guess it. I need: for all $(s,a,b,k,h)$ and all $V$ in the class $\mathcal V_{h+1}=\{V:\mathcal S_{h+1}\to[0,H]\}$,
$$\big|[(\widehat{\mathbb P}^k_h-\mathbb P_h)V](s,a,b)\big|\le c\sqrt{SH^2\iota/N^k_h(s,a,b)}.$$
The naive route — apply Hoeffding to a *fixed* $V$ — gives $\sqrt{H^2\iota/N}$, but my $V^{\mathrm{up}}_{h+1}$ is *data-dependent*, so I can't treat it as fixed; I need the bound to hold *uniformly* over the class. Build an $\epsilon$-net $\mathcal V_\epsilon$ of $\mathcal V_{h+1}$ in $\ell_\infty$; its size is $|\mathcal V_\epsilon|\le(1/\epsilon)^S$ because the class is $S$-dimensional with values in $[0,H]$. Hoeffding for each net point plus a union bound over $\mathcal V_\epsilon$ and over $N\in[K]$:
$$\Big|(\widehat{\mathbb P}^k_h-\mathbb P_h)\widehat V\Big|\le\sqrt{\frac{H^2(S\log(1/\epsilon)+\log(K/p))}{N^k_h(s,a,b)}}\quad\text{for all }\widehat V\in\mathcal V_\epsilon.$$
Choose $\epsilon=c\sqrt{H^2S\iota/K}$; then $S\log(1/\epsilon)=O(S\iota)$, so the bound is $c\sqrt{H^2S\iota/N}$. Any $V$ in the class is within $\epsilon$ of some $\widehat V$, and $|(\widehat{\mathbb P}-\mathbb P)(V-\widehat V)|\le 2\epsilon\le c\sqrt{H^2S\iota/K}\le c\sqrt{H^2S\iota/N}$. Add the two pieces: the uniform bound holds with radius $c\sqrt{SH^2\iota/N}$. So the bonus is
$$\beta_t = c\sqrt{SH^2\iota/t},\qquad t=N^k_h(s,a,b).$$
There's the $S$ inside the square root — it comes from the $\epsilon$-net dimension, and it is the reason I should brace for an $S^2$ in the final regret, where single-agent UCBVI with its sharper value-class handling gets $S$.

Now the regret bound. By the sandwich, the regret is at most the sum of my own up–low gaps at the roots:
$$\mathrm{Regret}(K)=\sum_k\big[V^{\dagger,\nu^k}_1-V^{\mu^k,\dagger}_1\big](s_1^k)\ \le\ \sum_k\big[V^{\mathrm{up},k}_1-V^{\mathrm{low},k}_1\big](s_1^k).$$
I need to telescope $V^{\mathrm{up}}-V^{\mathrm{low}}$ down the trajectory actually played. At the played state $s^k_h$,
$$[V^{\mathrm{up},k}_h-V^{\mathrm{low},k}_h](s^k_h)=\mu^k_h(s^k_h)^\top[Q^{\mathrm{up},k}_h-Q^{\mathrm{low},k}_h](s^k_h,\cdot,\cdot)\nu^k_h(s^k_h).$$
Replace the policy-average by the value at the *sampled* $(a^k_h,b^k_h)$ plus a mean-zero correction $\xi^k_h$ (a martingale difference, since $(a^k_h,b^k_h)\sim\mu^k_h\times\nu^k_h$). Now $[Q^{\mathrm{up},k}_h-Q^{\mathrm{low},k}_h](s^k_h,a^k_h,b^k_h)\le[\widehat{\mathbb P}^k_h(V^{\mathrm{up},k}_{h+1}-V^{\mathrm{low},k}_{h+1})](s^k_h,a^k_h,b^k_h)+2\beta^k_h$, because the two backups differ by the two bonuses (the reward cancels). Swap $\widehat{\mathbb P}$ for $\mathbb P$ using the concentration lemma applied to $V^{\mathrm{up}}-V^{\mathrm{low}}\in[0,H]$, costing another $2\beta^k_h$; then replace $\mathbb P_h(\cdot)(s^k_h,a^k_h,b^k_h)$ by the realized next value $(V^{\mathrm{up}}_{h+1}-V^{\mathrm{low}}_{h+1})(s^k_{h+1})$ plus a second martingale difference $\zeta^k_h$. Net:
$$[V^{\mathrm{up},k}_h-V^{\mathrm{low},k}_h](s^k_h)\ \le\ [V^{\mathrm{up},k}_{h+1}-V^{\mathrm{low},k}_{h+1}](s^k_{h+1}) + 4\beta^k_h + \xi^k_h + \zeta^k_h.$$
Unroll over $h=1\ldots H$ and sum over $k$. The martingale sums are $O(\sqrt{HT\iota})$ by Azuma–Hoeffding (lower order). The bonus sum is the dominant term:
$$\sum_{k,h}4\beta^k_h = \sum_{k,h}4c\sqrt{\tfrac{SH^2\iota}{N^k_h(s^k_h,a^k_h,b^k_h)}}\ \le\ c\!\!\sum_{h,s,a,b}\ \sum_{t=1}^{N^K_h(s,a,b)}\!\!\sqrt{\tfrac{SH^2\iota}{t}}.$$
The inner sum $\sum_{t=1}^N\sqrt{1/t}=O(\sqrt N)$ — the pigeonhole/"$\sqrt{}$ of counts" trick — so each $(s,a,b)$ contributes $O(\sqrt{SH^2\iota\cdot N^K_h(s,a,b)})$. Now Cauchy–Schwarz over the $SAB$ triples at each step, using $\sum_{s,a,b}N^K_h(s,a,b)=K$:
$$\sum_{s,a,b}\sqrt{N^K_h(s,a,b)}\le\sqrt{SAB\cdot K},$$
giving $\sum_{s,a,b}\sqrt{SH^2\iota\cdot N^K_h}=O(\sqrt{H^2S^2AB\,K\iota})$ per step $h$, and summing over $H$ steps (one more $H$),
$$\mathrm{Regret}(K)=O\!\left(\sqrt{H^4 S^2 AB\,K\,\iota}\right).$$
Let me convert to the per-step quantity $T=KH$, because that's the unit the single-agent bounds are quoted in and I want an apples-to-apples comparison. Substitute $K=T/H$ inside the root: $H^4 S^2AB\,K=H^4S^2AB\cdot T/H=H^3S^2AB\,T$, so $\sqrt{H^4S^2AB\,K}=\sqrt{H^3S^2AB\,T}$ — exactly, no slack lost. So $\mathrm{Regret}(K)=\tilde O(\sqrt{H^3 S^2 AB\,T})$. Two $S$'s — one from the count-counting (as in single-agent), one from the bonus's $\epsilon$-net $S$. The $AB$ — *product*, not sum — is because at each state I solve a full $A\times B$ matrix game and the bonus is per $(s,a,b)$ triple. I'll want to ask later whether either of those is necessary.

Online-to-batch turns regret into PAC. Sample $(\hat\mu,\hat\nu)$ uniformly from the $K$ episodes' policies; then $\mathbb E[V^{\dagger,\hat\nu}_1-V^{\hat\mu,\dagger}_1]=\frac1K\mathrm{Regret}(K)\le\tilde O(\sqrt{H^4S^2AB/K})$. Setting this $\le\epsilon$: $K\ge\tilde O(H^4 S^2 AB/\epsilon^2)$ episodes give an $\epsilon$-approximate Nash pair. Good — a real, assumption-free, simulator-free, sample-efficient self-play guarantee.

How tight is this? Collapse the game: make the min-player a dummy (single action), and it's a single-agent MDP, for which the regret lower bound is $\Omega(\sqrt{H^2 S(A+B)T})$ (the $A+B$ since either player could be the live one). Compare to my $\sqrt{H^3 S^2 AB\,T}$: there's a $\sqrt S$ gap (I have $S^2$, the floor is $S$) and an action gap ($AB$ versus $A+B$). So the *statistical* rate is sublinear and assumption-free, which was the goal, but not yet optimal. Two honest open directions: kill one $S$, and turn the multiplicative $AB$ into additive $A+B$. I'll set those aside as targets.

Now the *computational* problem, which I quietly buried. My per-state subroutine is $\textsc{Nash\_General\_Sum}$ on two *different* matrices $(Q^{\mathrm{up}},Q^{\mathrm{low}})$. Finding a Nash equilibrium of a general-sum bimatrix game is PPAD-complete — believed to have no polynomial-time algorithm. So VI-ULCB, sample-efficient as it is, is not a polynomial-time algorithm in the worst case. (In practice Lemke–Howson usually finds it fast, but worst-case is worst-case.) I want to recover polynomial runtime. Two routes.

Route one: separate exploration from exploitation, so I only ever solve a *zero-sum* game. If I explore *reward-free* — visit states to nail down $(\widehat{\mathbb P},\widehat r)$ accurately, ignoring reward while exploring — then at the end I have a single empirical model. Running value iteration on *that one model* needs only the *zero-sum* per-state solve $\textsc{Nash\_Zero\_Sum}(Q(s,\cdot,\cdot))$ — *one* matrix, von Neumann's minimax, an LP, polynomial. The reward-free explore for a target state $s$ is itself a single-agent problem: set an artificial reward $\tilde r=\mathbb 1[s'=s]$, treat the action pair $(a,b)$ as one big action, and run any PAC single-agent learner to maximize the reaching probability of $s$; repeat over all $s$. After $\tilde O(H^5 S^2 AB/\epsilon^2)$ episodes the empirical model's Nash is $\epsilon$-accurate, then exploit by playing it. Converting to regret with the explore budget traded against the exploit suboptimality, choosing $\epsilon\sim T^{-1/3}$, gives $\tilde O(T^{2/3})$ regret — worse than $\sqrt T$, but *polynomial-time*. So the computational hardness of VI-ULCB is not intrinsic to the problem; it's the price of the general-sum solve inside the loop.

Route two is nicer and it makes me re-examine the general-sum solve itself. Do I really need the *Nash* equilibrium of $(Q^{\mathrm{up}},Q^{\mathrm{low}})$? Look back at what the proof actually used. It used one-sided no-deviation inequalities: the max-player's value under the chosen play is at least every fixed action deviation measured by $Q^{\mathrm{up}}$, and the min-player's value is at most every fixed action deviation measured by $Q^{\mathrm{low}}$. I do not need product-form independent play for that. The relaxation that captures exactly this is a **coarse correlated equilibrium**: find a *joint* distribution $\pi\in\Delta_{\mathcal A\times\mathcal B}$ with
$$\mathbb E_{(a,b)\sim\pi}Q^{\mathrm{up}}(a,b)\ge\max_{a^\star}\mathbb E_{(a,b)\sim\pi}Q^{\mathrm{up}}(a^\star,b),\qquad \mathbb E_{(a,b)\sim\pi}Q^{\mathrm{low}}(a,b)\le\min_{b^\star}\mathbb E_{(a,b)\sim\pi}Q^{\mathrm{low}}(a,b^\star).$$
A CCE always exists (a Nash of the general-sum game is one), and — this is the point — these constraints are $A+B$ *linear* inequalities in $\pi$, so a CCE is computable by linear programming in polynomial time. That much is clear. What I'm not sure of, and need to be sure of, is whether this relaxation *degrades correctly*: when $Q^{\mathrm{up}}$ and $Q^{\mathrm{low}}$ have converged to the same $Q$, do the CCE's marginals recover the zero-sum Nash of $Q$? If they don't, the swap silently changes what the algorithm computes in the limit, and the convergence story is broken. Let me argue it, then check it. Feeding the same matrix twice, $\pi=\textsc{CCE}(Q,Q)$ with marginals $(\mu,\nu)$, the CCE constraints read
$$\max_{a^\star}\mathbb E_{b\sim\nu}Q(a^\star,b)\le \mathbb E_{(a,b)\sim\pi}Q(a,b)\le \min_{b^\star}\mathbb E_{a\sim\mu}Q(a,b^\star).$$
For *any* marginals, the minimax theorem gives the reverse cross-pair: $\min_{b^\star}\mathbb E_{a\sim\mu}Q(a,b^\star)\le N^\star\le\max_{a^\star}\mathbb E_{b\sim\nu}Q(a^\star,b)$, with $N^\star$ the matrix value. The two chains run in opposite directions through the same two end-quantities, so both can hold only if every term collapses to a single number — which is exactly the saddle condition $\max_a\mathbb E_\nu Q(a,\cdot)=N^\star=\min_b\mathbb E_\mu Q(\cdot,b)$ for $(\mu,\nu)$. So the marginals *should* be a zero-sum Nash. That's a clean argument, but I've talked myself into false "clean arguments" before, so let me actually solve the CCE LP and look at the marginals. Take the $Q=\begin{psmallmatrix}3&0\\1&2\end{psmallmatrix}$ from before, whose saddle I computed by hand as $\mu=(\tfrac14,\tfrac34)$, $\nu=(\tfrac12,\tfrac12)$, value $1.5$. Solving the feasibility LP for $\pi\in\Delta_{2\times2}$ under the four deviation constraints above returns
$$\pi=\begin{pmatrix}0.125&0.125\\0.375&0.375\end{pmatrix},\qquad \text{marginals }\mu=(0.25,0.75),\ \nu=(0.5,0.5).$$
Those marginals are *exactly* the hand-computed saddle, value $\mu^\top Q\nu=1.5$. (Interesting side-observation: this particular $\pi$ even factors, $0.125=0.25\cdot0.5$, so here the CCE happens to be a product — but the LP wasn't told to make it one; in general it needn't.) So the degeneration holds in the concrete case, matching the algebra. As $Q^{\mathrm{up}}$ and $Q^{\mathrm{low}}$ converge, the CCE solution converges to the Nash of the game. Replacing $\textsc{Nash\_General\_Sum}$ by $\textsc{CCE}$ keeps the whole proof and removes the PPAD obstruction.

That CCE swap also lets me change the learning backbone from value iteration to *Q-learning*, and that change is where the extra $S$ dies. In VI I re-estimate the whole model and back up through it, and the data-dependence of $V_{h+1}$ on the very samples I'm averaging is what forced the $\epsilon$-net and the $S^2$. Q-learning instead does an *online incremental* update,
$$Q^{\mathrm{up}}_h(s_h,a_h,b_h)\leftarrow(1-\alpha_t)Q^{\mathrm{up}}_h(s_h,a_h,b_h)+\alpha_t\big(r_h+V^{\mathrm{up}}_{h+1}(s_{h+1})+\beta_t\big),$$
with the rate $\alpha_t=(H+1)/(H+t)$ that single-agent Q-learning uses, and the bonus $\beta_t=c\sqrt{H^3\iota/t}$. Each fresh sample $s_{h+1}$ is folded once into a continuation value $V^{\mathrm{up}}_{h+1}$ that is *statistically independent of that sample*, so I never need a union bound over a data-dependent value class — the $S$-inflation disappears and the visit-count averaging gives state-dependence linear in $S$. Set the per-state policy by $\pi_h(\cdot,\cdot\mid s_h)=\textsc{CCE}(Q^{\mathrm{up}}_h(s_h,\cdot,\cdot),Q^{\mathrm{low}}_h(s_h,\cdot,\cdot))$ and read off $V^{\mathrm{up}}_h,V^{\mathrm{low}}_h$ as the $\pi_h$-expectations. The same up/low sandwich now reads $V^{\mathrm{up},k}_h(s)\ge V^\star_h(s)\ge V^{\mathrm{low},k}_h(s)$, and the averaged gap is $\frac1K\sum_k(V^{\mathrm{up},k}_1-V^{\mathrm{low},k}_1)(s_1)\le O(\sqrt{H^5SAB\iota/K})$ — an $S$, not $S^2$. So $K\ge\tilde O(H^5SAB/\epsilon^2)$ episodes to learn the *value*.

There's something I need to be careful about before I declare victory, though. The Q-learning sandwich now reads $V^{\mathrm{up},k}_h\ge V^\star_h\ge V^{\mathrm{low},k}_h$ — a band on $V^\star$, *not* the best-response band I worked so hard for in VI-ULCB. And I already saw, back in the matching-pennies check, exactly what a band on $V^\star$ fails to control: there I had $V^{\mathrm{up}}=0.55$, $V^{\mathrm{low}}=0.45$ straddling $V^\star=0.5$, yet a deployed pair whose duality gap was the full $1.0$. So this averaged-gap guarantee $\frac1K\sum_k(V^{\mathrm{up},k}_1-V^{\mathrm{low},k}_1)\le O(\sqrt{H^5SAB\iota/K})$ tells me my *estimates* bracket $V^\star$ tightly on average; by itself it says *nothing* about whether the *policies* $\{\mu^k,\nu^k\}$ I deployed are good against a best response. In a single-agent MDP I'd get that for free — a near-optimal value estimate comes with a near-optimal greedy policy. Here it does not follow, and that's the structural gap between MDPs and games: $\epsilon$-Nash is defined by performance against the best response. If I just hand back the last $\mu^k$, its best response could exploit it badly even while $V^{\mathrm{up}}-V^{\mathrm{low}}$ is tiny. So the value guarantee is necessary but not sufficient; I owe a policy that *carries* the band's tightness against best responses.

So I need to *extract* a policy that is *certified* to perform like the value estimate even against its best response. Where would such a certificate come from? It's hiding in the Q-learning update itself. Unroll the incremental update: with the step-size weights $\alpha^0_t=\prod_{j=1}^t(1-\alpha_j)$ and $\alpha^i_t=\alpha_i\prod_{j=i+1}^t(1-\alpha_j)$ (which sum to one, $\sum_{i=1}^t\alpha^i_t=1$, so $\{\alpha^i_t\}$ is a distribution over the $t$ past visits),
$$Q^{\mathrm{up},k}_h(s,a,b)=\alpha^0_t H+\sum_{i=1}^t\alpha^i_t\Big[r_h(s,a,b)+V^{\mathrm{up},k^i_h(s,a,b)}_{h+1}\big(s^{k^i_h(s,a,b)}_{h+1}\big)+\beta_i\Big],$$
where $k^i_h(s,a,b)$ is the episode of the $i$-th visit to $(s,a,b)$ at step $h$. Read this as a recipe: $Q^{\mathrm{up}}$ at this node is an upper certificate, with bonuses, for the value the max-player can guarantee if, at step $h+1$, he plays a *mixture* of the past policies $\{\mu^{k^i_h}_{h+1}\}_{i=1}^t$ with weights $\{\alpha^i_t\}$. Each of *those* $V^{\mathrm{up}}$ certificates unfolds the same way one step deeper. So the certified policy is a *nested mixture*: sample an episode index, follow that episode's per-step Markov policy, but at each step re-sample the index from the $\alpha$-weights of the node you're at, recursing down the horizon. It is *non-Markovian* (the index carries history), it requires storing $\{\mu^k_h,\nu^k_h\}$ for all $k,h$, and it is *certified*: the unrolled recursion proves $V^{\mathrm{up}}$ upper-bounds the best-response value against the certified min-player policy, while $V^{\mathrm{low}}$ lower-bounds the symmetric certified max-player value. Run the bracket argument and $K\ge\tilde O(H^5SAB/\epsilon^2)$ now yields an $\epsilon$-approximate *Nash policy pair* $(\hat\mu,\hat\nu)$, not merely a value.

One more relaxation closes the action gap. The CCE solve still touches the full $A\times B$ matrix, so $AB$ persists. But notice: from one player's seat, learning a policy that's robust to whatever the opponent does is a *bandit-against-an-adversary* problem at each state. The tool for that is Follow-the-Regularized-Leader, whose regret is *independent of the opponent's number of actions* — it scales with *your own* action count only. So let each player run, per state, an FTRL update on an importance-weighted loss estimate, blended with the Q-learning value backup. The max-player keeps $V^{\mathrm{up}}$, forms a loss $\hat\ell_h(s_h,a)=[H-r_h-V^{\mathrm{up}}_{h+1}(s_{h+1})]\mathbb 1[a_h=a]/(\mu_h(a_h\mid s_h)+\eta_t)$ (the $\eta_t$ in the denominator buys high-probability rather than in-expectation control), accumulates $L_h$, and sets
$$\mu_h(\cdot\mid s_h)\leftarrow\arg\min_{\mu\in\Delta_{\mathcal A}}\ \eta_t\langle L_h(s_h,\cdot),\mu\rangle+\alpha_t\,\mathrm{KL}(\mu\,\|\,\mu_0)\ \propto\ \exp[-(\eta_t/\alpha_t)L_h(s_h,\cdot)],$$
$\mu_0$ uniform. The min-player runs the symmetric thing on $V^{\mathrm{low}}$. Because FTRL's per-state regret is $O(\sqrt{A})$ (and $O(\sqrt{B})$ for the opponent) with *no* cross term, the sample complexity comes out $\tilde O(H^6 S(A+B)/\epsilon^2)$ — additive $A+B$, matching the $\Omega(H^3 S(A+B)/\epsilon^2)$ lower bound up to $\mathrm{poly}(H)$. The same certified-policy extraction (now over the FTRL iterates) hands back an $\epsilon$-Nash pair. And it's *decentralized*: each player only needs to see the opponent's action, no shared computation.

Now I can finally answer the question I started with — *why self-play, and not two independent learners?* — because I almost talked myself into the obvious alternative: just run a no-regret single-agent learner for each player against the other, and average the iterates; summing the two one-sided regret bounds gives a Nash. The catch is what "no-regret" demands here: the max-player's learner must achieve sublinear regret against an *adversarial* opponent. The one-sided task looks computationally hard, and I can pin that down by reduction rather than hand-waving. Build a deterministic two-action game with two states per layer in which the opponent's policy encodes a *parity-with-noise* instance; learning the best response to that fixed non-Markovian opponent would learn the hidden noisy parity. I can't refute the existence of a fast learner here outright — but learning noisy parity is conjectured to require super-polynomial time and that conjecture underlies cryptographic constructions, so a polynomial-time best-response learner would break it. The no-regret version inherits the same obstruction, even when the adversary is non-adaptive and uses Markov policies episode by episode. The underlying transitions are Markov, but the opponent injects long-range correlations across the interaction that the learner must decode — the same reason hidden-Markov / POMDP learning is parity-hard. So no polynomial-time learner can be no-regret against an adversarial opponent under that conjecture, which means the decoupled "two no-regret learners" recipe *cannot* be made efficient. Self-play is not a convenience; it's what sidesteps this wall. In self-play the two players are *not* adversaries to each other — they share the computation (or, in V-learning, each is only *one-step* agnostic about the other while both update toward the same equilibrium), and that coordination is precisely what dodges the parity-with-noise hardness. The empirical success of self-play, the thing I couldn't explain at the start, has a provable backbone: two-sided optimism plus a per-state equilibrium solve gives sample-efficient Nash learning, and the reason you *must* play against a coordinated copy of yourself rather than against an adversary is that beating an adversary is, in the worst case, cryptographically hard.

Let me write the core algorithm in the concrete form it lands on.

```python
import numpy as np

# Optimistic Nash Q-learning for two-player zero-sum Markov games (model-free).
# Maintains an UPPER and a LOWER Q-estimate (two-sided optimism), and at each
# state plays a Coarse Correlated Equilibrium of the (Q_up, Q_low) bimatrix.
# CCE(Q,Q)'s marginals are the zero-sum Nash, so as Q_up, Q_low close, the
# play converges to the game's Nash equilibrium.

def cce(P, Q):
    """Coarse Correlated Equilibrium of a bimatrix game by linear programming.
    Returns a joint distribution pi over (a,b) with
        E_pi P >= max_a* E_pi P(a*,b),   E_pi Q <= min_b* E_pi Q(a,b*).
    A+B linear constraints, polynomial time; CCE(Q,Q)'s marginals are zero-sum Nash."""
    A, B = P.shape
    pi = solve_cce_lp(P, Q)          # standard LP feasibility; pi in simplex over A*B
    return pi.reshape(A, B)

def nash_q_learning(env, S, A, B, H, K, c, iota):
    Qup  = np.full((H, S, A, B), float(H))      # upper-confidence Q  (max-player optimism)
    Qlow = np.zeros((H, S, A, B))               # lower-confidence Q  (min-player optimism)
    N    = np.zeros((H, S, A, B))
    pi   = np.full((H, S, A, B), 1.0 / (A * B)) # per-state joint policy (CCE output)
    Vup  = np.zeros((H + 1, S)); Vlow = np.zeros((H + 1, S))
    hist = [[[[[] for _ in range(B)] for _ in range(A)] for _ in range(S)] for _ in range(H)]

    for k in range(K):
        s = env.reset()
        for h in range(H):
            a, b = sample_joint(pi[h, s])                  # take (a,b) ~ pi_h(.,.|s)
            r, s_next = env.step(a, b)
            N[h, s, a, b] += 1
            t = int(N[h, s, a, b])
            alpha = (H + 1) / (H + t)                      # Q-learning rate: each sample used once
            beta  = c * np.sqrt(H**3 * iota / t)           # confidence bonus
            # --- two-sided optimistic incremental backup (the up/low sandwich) ---
            Qup[h, s, a, b]  = (1 - alpha) * Qup[h, s, a, b]  + alpha * (r + Vup[h + 1, s_next]  + beta)
            Qlow[h, s, a, b] = (1 - alpha) * Qlow[h, s, a, b] + alpha * (r + Vlow[h + 1, s_next] - beta)
            # --- per-state equilibrium solve: joint-greedy play of (Qup, Qlow) ---
            pi[h, s] = cce(Qup[h, s], Qlow[h, s])
            Vup[h, s]  = float(np.sum(pi[h, s] * Qup[h, s]))   # E_pi Qup
            Vlow[h, s] = float(np.sum(pi[h, s] * Qlow[h, s]))  # E_pi Qlow
            hist[h][s][a][b].append((k, pi[h, s].copy()))      # store iterates for the certified policy
            s = s_next
    return pi, hist, N

def certified_policy_sample(hist, N, H, alpha_weights, start_k):
    """Execute the non-Markovian certified policy hat-mu (max-player side).
    Q_up = alpha0_t*H + sum_i alpha^i_t [r + V_up(next) + beta_i] is
    the certificate for the alpha-weighted mixture of past per-step policies.
    Value-closeness alone does not give an eps-Nash policy in a game."""
    k = start_k
    for h in range(H):
        s = observe_state(h)
        a = sample_action(marginal_max(hist, h, s, k))   # play mu^k_h(.|s)
        b = observe_opponent_action(h)
        t = int(N[h, s, a, b])
        m = sample_index(alpha_weights[t])               # P(m=i) = alpha^i_t
        k = episode_of_mth_visit(hist, h, s, a, b, m)    # re-index: nested mixture
```

The causal chain, end to end: self-play's empirical success demands a Nash, not best-response, target, scored by a duality-gap regret; lifting single-agent optimism gives an ill-posed per-player "greedy," which the per-state matrix game resolves — and because the two players carry *different* optimistic matrices it is a general-sum solve; two-sided optimism tied to the deployed policies' best responses is exactly what makes the up/low band bracket the regret summand, and the $\epsilon$-net concentration fixes the bonus and hence the $\tilde O(\sqrt{H^3S^2ABT})$ rate; PPAD-hardness of general-sum Nash forces either explore-then-exploit (zero-sum LP, $T^{2/3}$) or the CCE relaxation (LP, keeps the proof); moving to online Q-learning kills one $S$, but the value-only guarantee fails to certify policies, so a nested-mixture certified policy is extracted; FTRL per state turns $AB$ into $A+B$ to meet the lower bound; and the parity-with-noise hardness of beating an adversarial opponent proves the coordination of self-play is necessary, not optional.
