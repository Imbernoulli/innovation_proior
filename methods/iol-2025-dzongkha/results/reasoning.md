So System A is base twenty: ke X da Y means 20·X + Y, where X is a digit naming how many twenties and Y is whatever is left under twenty. The word ke marks the twenties place; da is the join. This is consistent everywhere I can test it, and it just handed me the units 4, 5, 6, 7, 9 with no contradictions. The units so far: ci=1, ɲi=2, sum=3, ʑi=4, ŋa=5, ɖu=6, dyn=7, ge=8, gu=9.

Two entries in the A column have no da at all: ke ʑi = 80 and ke ceŋa = 300. ke ʑi with ʑi=4 is just 20·4 = 80 — a bare twenties place with nothing added, so you drop the da Y tail. So the digit slot in ke X da Y can itself be a teen, not only a bare unit — the "number of twenties" is just a Dzongkha number, and you write it however you'd write that number. Good to know.

Now System B. Same values, different words: tsaɲi=22, tsaŋa=25, soʑi=34, ʑedyn=47, ŋaŋa=55, dønɖu=76, gepcu=80, ɟagu=89, sumɟa=300. So tsa- carries "twenty," and the tail is the unit added: tsa = 2-tens, ɲi=2, ŋa=5. So each digit has more than one shape. The unit shape (ci, ɲi, sum, ʑi, ŋa, ɖu, dyn, ge, gu) is one set; the tens-stem shape (tsa for 2, so for 3, ʑe for 4, ŋa for 5, døn for 7, ɟa for 8...) is another. The working summary of System B is base ten, with several stem-shapes per digit depending on whether the digit is a unit, a multiplier of ten standing alone, or a multiplier of ten with something after it.

Now I hit the entries that do not fit the clean base-twenty rule. ke pɟe-da ɲi = 30 and ke ko-da sum = 55. Let me not theorize too fast; let me collect every pɟe-da and every ko-da I can see, in System A, with their values, and stare at the pattern. Now I can read what pɟe and ko actually mean. Rewrite the formulas: ke pɟe-da X = 20·(X−1) + 10, and ke ko-da X = 20·(X−1) + 15. So pɟe = "half" and ko = "three-quarters," modifiers that say "halfway / three-quarters of the way into the next twenty." The system overcounts the twenties on purpose — it points at the twenty it is climbing through and tells you the fraction climbed. ke pɟe-da ɲi is "halfway into the 2nd twenty" = one full twenty plus ten = 30. ke ko-da sum is "three-quarters into the 3rd twenty" = two full twenties plus fifteen = 55. The fractions are literal. pɟe is the word for one-half and ko is the word for three-quarters, used to mark how far into the current twenty you have climbed. The overcounting is not an accident of the formula; it is the language saying "in the second twenty, halfway."
