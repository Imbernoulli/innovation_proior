Let me start from what I actually have in my hands when I want to know how far my policy `q` has drifted from a frozen reference `p`. I do not have the two distributions laid out over the whole support — the support is the entire vocabulary of continuations, far too big to sum over, and even when it is a continuous density there is no closed form for the integral I want. What I do have is the ability to evaluate `log p(x)` and `log q(x)` at any point, and a stream of samples `x ~ q`, because `q` is the thing I am sampling from anyway as I generate. So the whole game is: out of per-sample pairs `(log p(x), log q(x))`, build a number whose average over my samples is a good estimate of `KL[q, p]`.

The definition itself hands me the obvious estimator. `KL[q, p] = sum_x q(x) log(q(x)/p(x)) = E_{x~q}[log(q(x)/p(x))]`. It is already an expectation under `q`. So I just draw `x ~ q`, evaluate the integrand, and average. Let me name the recurring object: the likelihood ratio `r = p(x)/q(x)`, so that `log(q/p) = -log r`, and the estimator is

  k1 = -log r = log q(x) - log p(x).

This is unbiased by construction — `E_q[k1] = KL[q, p]` exactly, no approximation anywhere, it is literally the integrand of the expectation that defines KL. That should be the end of the story. But it is not, because of how it *behaves* sample to sample. Watch the sign. The true KL is always non-negative — that is forced, because `-log` is convex and Jensen gives `E_q[-log r] >= -log E_q[r]`, and `E_q[r] = sum_x q(x) (p(x)/q(x)) = sum_x p(x) = 1`, so `E_q[k1] >= -log 1 = 0`. Good, the mean is non-negative. But the *per-sample* value `-log r` is negative the moment `r > 1`, i.e. whenever the reference puts more mass on the sampled token than my policy does. And that happens on a huge fraction of samples — roughly half when the two distributions are close. So `k1` is a quantity that is negative on half its samples, positive on the other half, and only after the dust settles does the average crawl up to a small positive KL. With a finite batch, that cancellation is fragile: the estimate swings wildly, and a noisy KL estimate is poison downstream, because it becomes a noisy penalty and then a noisy gradient on the policy. So `k1` is correct in expectation but unusable in variance. Wall.

The instinct for the variance is to stop the estimator from ever going negative, since the true thing it estimates never is. The cheapest way to force non-negativity is to square the log-ratio: take

  k2 = 0.5 (log r)^2.

Every term is now `>= 0`, no destructive cancellation, and intuitively each sample is reporting *how far apart* `p` and `q` are at `x`, which feels like the right kind of signal. And empirically this is dramatically calmer than `k1`. So is this just the answer? Let me check whether it is unbiased, because I will not trade correctness for calm without knowing the price. Its expectation is `E_q[0.5 (log r)^2]`. Is that `KL[q, p]`? Not obviously. Let me look at both quantities as members of one family so I can compare them on the same footing. Both have the shape `E_{x~q}[f(r)]` for some convex `f`: KL is `f(x) = -log x`, and `k2` is `f(x) = 0.5 (log x)^2`. This is the f-divergence family, `D_f(p,q) = E_{x~q}[f(r)]`. And there is a fact about this family I can lean on: near coincidence, every differentiable f-divergence collapses to the *same* quadratic form up to the constant `f''(1)`. Expand `f(r)` about `r = 1`. Since these `f` all have `f(1) = 0` and the family is built so the linear term integrates away (more on that in a second), the leading surviving term is `0.5 f''(1) (r-1)^2`, and integrating over the local fluctuations of `r` gives `0.5 f''(1) theta^T F theta + O(theta^3)` with `F` the Fisher information. So two f-divergences agree to leading order near `p ~ q` exactly when their `f''(1)` agree. Compute them: for KL, `f(x) = -log x`, `f''(x) = 1/x^2`, `f''(1) = 1`. For `k2`, `f(x) = 0.5 (log x)^2`, `f'(x) = (log x)/x`, `f''(x) = (1 - log x)/x^2`, `f''(1) = 1`. They match. So `E_q[k2]` agrees with `KL[q, p]` to second order, which is why `k2`'s bias is astonishingly small when the policy is close to the reference. But "to second order" is the catch: it is an *approximation*, and as `p` and `q` separate, the higher-order mismatch grows and `k2` systematically over-estimates. `E_q[k2] != KL[q, p]` in general. So `k2` is biased, just gently biased near `p ~ q` and worse far from it. I have swapped `k1`'s variance problem for a bias problem. Wall again.

So now I can state the tension cleanly: the unbiased estimator (`k1`) is noisy, and the obvious de-noised one (`k2`) is biased. I want both — unbiased *and* low-variance. Squaring was a hack to force non-negativity; it threw away unbiasedness as collateral. Let me instead keep `k1`, which I *know* is unbiased, and attack its variance directly and surgically, without disturbing its mean.

There is a standard tool for exactly that: a control variate. If I have an unbiased estimator `X` and I can find some quantity `Y` whose expectation I know — say `E[Y] = 0` — then `X + lambda Y` has the same mean as `X` for *every* constant `lambda`, because I am only adding zero in expectation. And if `Y` is correlated with `X`, I can choose `lambda` to cancel part of `X`'s fluctuation: the variance of `X + lambda Y` is `Var(X) + 2 lambda Cov(X,Y) + lambda^2 Var(Y)`, minimized at `lambda* = -Cov(X,Y)/Var(Y)`, where it drops below `Var(X)` whenever `X` and `Y` are correlated at all. So I get variance reduction for free, paid for entirely by knowing one zero-mean quantity. The question is: what zero-mean quantity do I have lying around here?

I do not have many. I have `r`, and I know one nontrivial fact about it: `E_q[r] = 1`, exactly, because `p` is normalized. So `r - 1` has mean zero under `x ~ q`. That is the one simple, guaranteed-zero-mean object available — it costs me nothing extra to compute, since I already have `r`. So consider the family

  k1 + lambda (r - 1) = -log r + lambda (r - 1).

For any `lambda` this is an unbiased estimator of `KL[q, p]` — the control variate term contributes exactly zero to the mean. Now I should solve for the variance-minimizing `lambda*`. Let me try. `lambda* = -Cov(-log r, r-1)/Var(r-1)`. To evaluate that I need `E_q[(-log r)(r-1)]`, `E_q[-log r]`, `E_q[(r-1)^2]` — and every one of those is an expectation over `q` of some function of `r`, i.e. it depends on the actual distributions `p` and `q` in a way I cannot write down in closed form (that is the whole reason I am estimating in the first place — I cannot do these sums analytically). So the truly optimal `lambda*` is not computable. Dead end on optimality. But I do not need optimal; I need *good and simple*. Let me ask whether there is a value of `lambda` that gives me something I can guarantee without computing covariances.

Look at `lambda = 1`:

  -log r + (r - 1) = (r - 1) - log r.

Stare at this. `log r <= r - 1` for every `r > 0` — the log lies below its tangent line `y = x - 1` at `x = 1`, since `log` is concave, with equality only at `r = 1`. So `(r - 1) - log r >= 0` for every single sample, always, with equality exactly when `p(x) = q(x)`. The control variate at `lambda = 1` has not just lowered the variance — it has made the estimator *non-negative on every sample*, which is precisely the property `k1` failed and the property I was reaching for when I (wrongly) reached for the square. And it cost me no bias: it is still `k1` plus a zero-mean term, so still exactly unbiased. So

  k3 = (r - 1) - log r,   unbiased, and >= 0 on every sample.

Let me make sure I understand *why* `lambda = 1` lands on non-negativity and not just that it does. The expression `(r-1) - log r` is the vertical gap between the tangent line `r - 1` and the curve `log r` at the point `r`; both touch at `r = 1` where the gap is zero, and the gap opens upward on both sides because the curve is concave and the line is its tangent. That gap-between-a-convex-(here concave, mirrored)-function-and-its-tangent is a Bregman divergence — `B_f(r, 1) = f(r) - f(1) - f'(1)(r-1)` with `f = -log`: `(-log r) - 0 - (-1)(r-1) = -log r + (r-1) = k3`. So `k3` is exactly the Bregman divergence of `-log` between `r` and `1`, which is non-negative for the structural reason that convex functions sit above their tangents. The control-variate derivation and the Bregman picture are the same fact seen twice.

Now, is `lambda = 1` actually good for variance, or did I just get lucky on sign? Two checks. First, are `-log r` and `r - 1` negatively correlated, so that adding `+(r-1)` cancels fluctuation rather than amplifying it? As `r` rises above `1`, `-log r` goes *negative* (this was `k1`'s whole problem) while `r - 1` goes *positive*; as `r` drops below `1`, `-log r` goes positive while `r - 1` goes negative. They move oppositely, so `Cov(-log r, r-1) < 0`, which means `lambda* = -Cov/Var > 0` — the optimal sign is positive, and `lambda = 1` is a positive value on the right side of zero. The control variate is pulling in the variance-reducing direction. Second, let me see how `k3` behaves near `r = 1`, where most samples live when the policy hasn't drifted far, by Taylor-expanding. Write `r = e^s` with `s = log r`, so `k3 = e^s - 1 - s = (1 + s + s^2/2 + s^3/6 + ...) - 1 - s = s^2/2 + s^3/6 + ...`. To leading order `k3 ~ s^2/2 = 0.5 (log r)^2`, which is exactly `k2`. So near coincidence, `k3` *is* `k2` — it inherits `k2`'s low variance in the regime where `k2` was calm — but `k3` keeps the exact `k1` mean everywhere, so it does not pay `k2`'s bias when `p` and `q` separate. That is the best of both: `k2`'s calmness locally, `k1`'s correctness globally. I expect `k3` to have variance comparable to `k2` and zero bias, which would make it a strictly better estimator than `k2` — same calm, no bias. Exactly the unbiased-and-low-variance object I set out to find.

Before I lock it in, let me generalize, because I want to know whether `lambda = 1` is special or whether I have stumbled onto a recipe. The structure was: take an f-divergence `D_f = E_q[f(r)]` with convex `f`, and I want an always non-negative unbiased per-sample estimator of it. The Bregman construction does it for any such `f`: since `E_q[r] = 1` and `f` is convex, the per-sample quantity `f(r) - f'(1)(r - 1)` has expectation `E_q[f(r)] - f'(1)·E_q[r-1] = D_f - 0 = D_f` (unbiased), and it is `>= 0` on every sample because it is the Bregman gap `f(r) - f(1) - f'(1)(r-1)` (with `f(1)=0`) — the distance between `f` and its tangent at `1`, and convex functions lie above their tangents. So the recipe is: subtract off `f'(1)(r-1)`, the linear control variate that zeroes the tangent. For our KL, `f(x) = -log x`, `f'(1) = -1`, giving `f(r) - (-1)(r-1) = -log r + (r-1) = k3` — confirming `lambda = 1` was not arbitrary, it is `-f'(1) = 1`. And the same machine spits out the estimator for the *other* KL, `KL[p, q]`, which is the f-divergence with `f(x) = x log x`, `f'(1) = 1`: the estimator is `r log r - (r - 1)`. Same construction, different `f`. So I have not just found one estimator; I have found why it works and how to make its sibling.

Now let me put `k3` into the form I will actually compute, because I do not hold `r` directly — I hold log-probs. In the RL setting the current policy is the thing I am training and sampling from, so it plays the role of `q`, and the frozen reference plays `p`; I want `KL[q, p] = KL[policy, ref]`, sampling `x ~ policy`. The log-probs I get are `logprob = log q(x)` from the current policy (this one carries the trainable parameters) and `ref_logprob = log p(x)` from the frozen reference (detached). The ratio inside `k3` is `r = p(x)/q(x) = exp(log p - log q) = exp(ref_logprob - logprob)`. So define the log-ratio

  kl_logratio = ref_logprob - logprob = log(p/q) = log r,

then `r = exp(kl_logratio)`, and

  k3 = r - log r - 1 = exp(kl_logratio) - kl_logratio - 1.

Let me sanity-check the sign convention against the construction so I do not flip it. `k3 = (r-1) - log r` with `r = p/q`; here `log r = kl_logratio = ref_logprob - logprob`, and `r = exp(kl_logratio)`. So `k3 = exp(kl_logratio) - 1 - kl_logratio = exp(kl_logratio) - kl_logratio - 1`. Yes — and this is `>= 0` because `exp(s) - s - 1 >= 0` for all real `s` (the exponential lies above its tangent at `0`), the same Bregman non-negativity I derived, now written in `s = log r` instead of `r`. Good, the two forms agree.

Two numerical hazards in `exp(kl_logratio) - kl_logratio - 1` that I have to handle, because the log-ratio is unbounded. A rare token where the reference is far more confident than the policy gives a large positive `kl_logratio`, and `exp` of a large positive number overflows to `inf` — a single such token would blow up the whole batch. I need to bound the argument to `exp`. Clamp `kl_logratio` to a symmetric range before exponentiating; something like `[-20, 20]` is comfortable — `exp(20) ~ 4.85e8` is large but finite and well within float range, and a per-token log-ratio of magnitude 20 already corresponds to an astronomically different probability, so clamping there discards nothing real while killing the overflow. That handles the input side. On the output side, even with the input clamped, `k3` at the boundary is `exp(20) - 20 - 1 ~ 4.85e8`, which is an enormous per-token penalty that would dominate the aggregated KL loss and yank the optimizer around for one outlier token. So I also clamp the *output* `k3` to `[-10, 10]`. The upper cap is the part that matters for outliers; the lower side is just the same bounded-penalty convention, not a projection to non-negativity. Mathematically `k3 >= 0`, but I am going to mirror the implemented bounded range exactly rather than silently changing the estimator to `max(k3, 0)`.

One more thing I have to be honest about, because the estimator does not just feed a *value* — in the RL loss it is differentiated through the policy's log-prob, so its fixed-sample backpropagation gradient matters as much as its value. Value-unbiasedness is one property; gradient-correctness is a different one. Let me actually compute it. Let `theta` be the policy parameters, `s_theta = grad_theta log q_theta(x)` the score. With `r = p/q_theta`, `log q_theta` depends on `theta` and `log p` does not, so `grad_theta (log r) = grad_theta(log p - log q_theta) = -s_theta`. Then

  grad_theta k3 = grad_theta (r - log r - 1) = (grad_theta r) - grad_theta(log r) = r·(-s_theta) - (-s_theta) = (1 - r) s_theta,

using `grad r = r · grad(log r) = r·(-s_theta) = -r s_theta`. So the fixed-sample gradient of `k3` is `(1 - r) s_theta`. Now compare this to the score-function gradient of the true `KL[q_theta,p]`. Directly,

  grad_theta KL[q_theta,p] = grad_theta E_{q_theta}[log(q_theta/p)] = E_{q_theta}[(log(q_theta/p) + 1) s_theta].

The extra `+1` vanishes in expectation because `E_{q_theta}[s_theta] = grad_theta sum_x q_theta(x) = 0`, leaving

  grad_theta KL[q_theta,p] = E_{q_theta}[-(log r) s_theta].

That coefficient, `-(log r)`, is exactly what I get if I differentiate the squared-log surrogate `k2 = 0.5 (log r)^2` through the sampled log-prob: `grad_theta k2 = (log r)(-s_theta) = -(log r) s_theta`. The fixed-sample `k3` gradient uses `(1-r)` instead. These two agree only to first order near `r = 1`, since with `r=e^s`, `1-r = -s - s^2/2 - ...` while `-log r = -s`. So although `E_q[k3] = KL` exactly as a value estimator, differentiating the sampled `k3` value does not give the same gradient estimator as the true KL; value-unbiasedness and gradient-unbiasedness come apart. For the present construction, the object I set out to build is the divergence-value estimator — unbiased, low variance, non-negative — and that is `k3`. The gradient question is a downstream consideration I need to keep visible when this value is used as a differentiable loss.

So let me write `k3` into the slot in the harness — the per-token map from the two log-prob tensors to a per-token divergence, with both clamps in place and gradients flowing through `logprob`:

```python
import torch


def estimate_kl(
    logprob: torch.Tensor,      # log q(x): current policy, carries grad
    ref_logprob: torch.Tensor,  # log p(x): frozen reference, detached
) -> torch.Tensor:              # per-token k3 estimate of KL[policy || ref]
    # log-ratio s = log r = log(p/q) = ref_logprob - logprob
    kl = ref_logprob - logprob
    # clamp the argument to exp so a rare large log-ratio can't overflow
    kl = torch.clamp(kl, min=-20, max=20)
    # r = exp(s); the control-variate / Bregman estimator k3 = r - s - 1 >= 0
    ratio = torch.exp(kl)
    kld = (ratio - kl - 1).contiguous()
    # bound any single token's penalty so one outlier can't dominate the loss
    return torch.clamp(kld, min=-10, max=10)
```

That is the whole method. The causal chain: I needed a Monte-Carlo estimate of `KL[q,p]` from sampled log-probs, and the literal definition gives the unbiased `k1 = -log r`, but it is negative on half its samples and far too noisy. Squaring the log-ratio to force non-negativity gives `k2`, which is calm but biased — its expectation is an f-divergence that only matches KL to second order (same `f''(1) = 1`), so it over-estimates when the distributions separate. To keep `k1`'s exact unbiasedness while killing its variance, I added a control variate — the one guaranteed-zero-mean quantity available, `r - 1` — and discovered that the coefficient `lambda = 1` not only reduces variance (because `r-1` is negatively correlated with `-log r`) but makes the estimator non-negative on every sample, since `(r-1) - log r` is the gap between `log` and its tangent, a Bregman divergence of `-log`. That gives `k3 = (r-1) - log r = exp(s) - s - 1` with `s = log(p/q) = ref_logprob - logprob`, unbiased like `k1`, calm like `k2` (it equals `k2` to leading order near `r=1`), and non-negative. Clamping the log-ratio before `exp` and the output afterward makes it numerically safe, and the same Bregman recipe generalizes to any f-divergence by subtracting `f'(1)(r-1)`.
