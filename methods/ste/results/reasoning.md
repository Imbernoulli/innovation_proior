Let me start from the thing that actually blocks me. I want a unit inside my network whose output is a single bit — on or off — because a bit-valued gate lets me skip whole blocks of computation per example: compute a cheap gater first, and only pay for the expensive expert path where the gate is genuinely nonzero. If the gates are off most of the time, the cost per example collapses from "touch every parameter" to "touch the active subset," and I could train models far larger than my compute budget would otherwise allow. So I need the gate's forward output to be a real, hard zero a large fraction of the time — not a small continuous value that still forces me to do the multiply. The bit is the whole point.

But a bit-valued output is the indicator of a threshold being crossed. Write the unit as a sigmoid-parameterized Bernoulli: with `a = Wx + b` the affine pre-activation and `z ~ U[0,1]`, let `h = 1_{z < sigm(a)}`, so `h ∈ {0,1}` and `P(h=1) = sigm(a)`. The firing *probability* `sigm(a)` is a beautiful smooth function of `a`; the realized *bit* `h` is not. And it's the bit that goes downstream. Differentiate the bit with respect to `a`: it's flat everywhere `a` moves, because nudging `a` doesn't change which side of the threshold a fixed draw lands on — except at the single point where the threshold crosses that draw, where it jumps. So `dh/da = 0` almost everywhere, undefined at the jump. Plug that into the chain rule and it's fatal: `dL/da = (dL/dh)(dh/da) = (dL/dh)·0 = 0`. A zero factor annihilates the gradient. No signal reaches `W`, `b`, or anything feeding into this unit. Back-propagation, the engine I'd reach for, transmits nothing through a hard threshold.

This is exactly the wall that pushed everyone off formal hard-threshold neurons and onto sigmoids in the first place — Rumelhart, Hinton and Williams made backprop work precisely because the sigmoid gives a smooth, generally-nonzero relationship between parameters and loss, and a constant-by-parts graph kills gradient learning. So in some sense I'm trying to walk back into the room everyone left for good reason. Before I accept that, let me check whether the "must be smooth" belief is actually true, because if it's softer than it looks, there's room to move.

And it is softer than it looks. Rectifiers — `h = max(0, a)` — are non-smooth at the kink, and nets built on them are winning. Look at what backprop does with a rectifier: its subgradient is the indicator `1_{a>0}`, so on the backward sweep it zeroes exactly the units that were off on the forward sweep, and otherwise passes the gradient straight through with slope one. A unit whose *slope* is piecewise constant — zero in the dead region, one in the live region — trains fine. So a non-smooth nonlinearity is demonstrably trainable. That's encouraging, but it doesn't quite reach my case. The rectifier's slope is zero only on a half-line and one on the other half; my hard bit has slope zero *everywhere it's defined*. The rectifier gives me an existence proof that I can fudge the backward slope and survive; it doesn't yet tell me what slope to fake for a function whose true slope is zero a.e.

Noise is the other thing already working. Dropout multiplies a unit's output by binomial noise, denoising autoencoders corrupt the input, semantic hashing adds Gaussian noise before a sigmoid and lets the weights grow until the sigmoid saturates into near-binary codes. In every one of these, gradients flow as usual — and the reason is worth pinning down, because it's the trap. The noise enters a part of the graph that is *still a smooth function of the activation*: a multiplicative dropout mask is just a fixed scalar on the backward path, an additive noise is just an addend, the sigmoid after the added noise is still a sigmoid. So differentiation sails through. Noise even seems to help — dropout is a regularizer, it barely slows training, the induced sparsity and symmetry-breaking may ease ill-conditioning. So I'll take as a working principle that injecting stochasticity is cheap and often good. But none of these produce an actual 0/1 output; the moment I demand the realized output be a hard bit instead of a noisy continuous value, the smooth function I was riding disappears and the derivative collapses to zero again. Noise alone doesn't save me.

So let me lay out the honest options for getting *any* usable signal to the parameters of a hard unit, and find where each one breaks, because the right method is probably hiding in the gaps.

The brute-force option is to not differentiate at all — perturb and measure. Finite differences: nudge parameter `i` by `±ε`, measure `(L(u + ε e_i) - L(u - ε e_i))/(2ε)`. Correct, model-free, works through any black box including a hard threshold. But the cost is `O(N)` loss evaluations for `N` parameters, each evaluation itself `O(N)` — so `O(N²)`, which is `N` times slower than one backprop pass. At network scale that's dead on arrival. And it wants `ε` small to be accurate, the wrong regime for all-or-none events. Spall's simultaneous-perturbation trick rescues the cost: perturb *all* parameters at once with one random vector `z` and estimate `dL/du_i ≈ (L(u+z) - L(u-z))/(2 z_i)` — two function evaluations regardless of dimension, `O(N)` cheaper than coordinate-wise differences. Clever. But two problems for me. It's unbiased only as `z → 0`, the small-perturbation limit; and it *divides* by `z_i`. For genuine all-or-none spikes — a neuron fires or it doesn't — small perturbations are exactly the assumption that fails, and dividing by a perturbation that's supposed to be 0 or 1 is the wrong shape entirely. The spiking-neuron estimator of Fiete and Seung has the same small-perturbation disease. Whatever I build, I'd like it to *not* require the perturbations to be small, because my decisions are inherently large (0 vs 1).

The principled option is the likelihood ratio. If I treat the unit's output as a sampled stochastic action `h` drawn with probability `p_θ(h)`, and let `R` be the reward (negative loss), then the score-function identity — Williams' REINFORCE — says that for *any* baseline `b`, `E_h[(R - b)·∂ log p_θ(h)/∂θ] = ∂ E_h[R]/∂θ`. That's an unbiased estimate of the gradient of the expected reward, and crucially it works for hard 0/1 outputs because it never differentiates the sample — it differentiates the *log-probability*, which is smooth. Let me specialize it to my Bernoulli-logistic unit and actually derive the estimator I'd use, because I want to know its exact form and its cost. The probability mass is `g(h) = p^h (1-p)^{1-h}` with `p = sigm(a)`, so `log g = h log p + (1-h) log(1-p)`, and `∂ log g/∂a = (h/p - (1-h)/(1-p))·p'`, with `p' = sigm(a)(1-sigm(a)) = p(1-p)`. The bracket is `(h(1-p) - (1-h)p)/(p(1-p)) = (h - p)/(p(1-p))`, and multiplying by `p' = p(1-p)` cancels the denominator: `∂ log g/∂a = h - p = h - sigm(a)`. Lovely — the score with respect to the pre-activation is just `h - sigm(a)`. So a REINFORCE reward-ascent update is proportional to `(R - b)(h - sigm(a))`; for a loss, `(h_i - sigm(a_i))·L` estimates `∂E[L]/∂a_i`, and gradient descent subtracts it.

Let me prove this is actually unbiased rather than trust the general identity, because it's the one estimator here I can certify and I want the certification airtight. The loss depends on the unit's bit and on the rest of the system's noise. Split the noise into `c_i` (sources that influence `a_i`) and `c_{-i}` (those that don't). Condition on `c_i`. Because `h_i ∈ {0,1}`, the loss factors exactly: `L(h_i, ·) = h_i L(1, ·) + (1-h_i) L(0, ·)`. Taking the expectation over `z_i` and `c_{-i}`,
`E[L | c_i] = E_{c_{-i}}[ P(h_i=1)·L(1,·) + P(h_i=0)·L(0,·) ] = E_{c_{-i}}[ sigm(a_i) L(1,·) + (1-sigm(a_i)) L(0,·) ]`.
Now `a_i` doesn't touch `P(c_{-i})`, so I can differentiate under the expectation:
`g_i := ∂ E[L|c_i]/∂a_i = E_{c_{-i}}[ sigm'(a_i)(L(1,·) - L(0,·)) ] = E_{c_{-i}}[ sigm(a_i)(1-sigm(a_i))(L(1,·) - L(0,·)) ]`.
That's the truth I want to match. Now compute the expectation of my candidate estimator `ĝ_i = (h_i - sigm(a_i)) L(h_i,·)`. Use the bit identities `h_i² = h_i` and `h_i(1-h_i) = 0`. Expand `(h_i - sigm(a_i))(h_i L(1) + (1-h_i)L(0))`: the `h_i·h_i` term gives `h_i L(1)`, the `h_i(1-h_i)` term vanishes, and collecting,
`ĝ_i = h_i(1 - sigm(a_i)) L(1,·) - (1-h_i) sigm(a_i) L(0,·)`.
Take expectation, using `E[h_i] = sigm(a_i)` so `E[h_i(1-sigm(a_i))] = sigm(a_i)(1-sigm(a_i))` and `E[(1-h_i)sigm(a_i)] = (1-sigm(a_i))sigm(a_i)`:
`E[ĝ_i] = sigm(a_i)(1-sigm(a_i)) L(1,·) - (1-sigm(a_i))sigm(a_i) L(0,·) = sigm(a_i)(1-sigm(a_i))(L(1,·)-L(0,·))`,
in expectation over `c_{-i}` too — which is exactly `g_i`. Unbiased, confirmed, and it's a special case of REINFORCE with a Bernoulli-sigmoid action.

The baseline `b` (call it `L̄_i`) is free to subtract: `(h_i - sigm(a_i))(L - L̄_i)` is still unbiased, because `E[(h_i - sigm(a_i))] = 0` so the subtracted piece has zero expectation. And I can pick `L̄_i` to minimize variance. The centered variance differs from the uncentered one by `Δ = E[(h_i - sigm(a_i))²(L² - (L - L̄_i)²)]`, which I want to maximize, i.e. minimize `E[(h_i - sigm(a_i))²(L - L̄_i)²]`; setting the derivative in `L̄_i` to zero gives `E[(h_i-sigm(a_i))²(L̄_i - L)] = 0`, so `L̄_i = E[(h_i-sigm(a_i))² L]/E[(h_i-sigm(a_i))²]` — a per-unit weighted average of the loss. So the recipe is: keep two running averages per unit (numerator and denominator), form `L̄_i`, use `(h_i - sigm(a_i))(L - L̄_i)`.

Now I stare at what this costs me, and that's where it stops being attractive. It's unbiased, yes — but it's high variance, the classic disease of likelihood-ratio estimators, so without the baseline it learns slowly and unstably, and the baseline means extra running statistics for *every* unit. Worse, structurally: it needs the loss `L` *broadcast to every unit*. There's no local error signal — each unit multiplies the *global* (possibly delayed) loss by its own `(h_i - sigm(a_i))`. That's the opposite of what backprop gives me for free: backprop delivers a *structured*, local gradient to each unit, accounting for how that specific unit's change propagates through the specific downstream computation. REINFORCE throws that structure away and replaces it with "here's the global scalar loss, correlate it with your own noise." For a deep stack, that's a lot of variance and no credit assignment. So REINFORCE is the certified-correct-but-expensive option. Good to have in my pocket; not what I want to run.

Let me try the other family: don't fight the hard threshold, *smooth it* so backprop applies directly. The first idea: a noisy rectifier, `h = max(0, a + z)`. If the noise `z` has the logistic density `sigm(z)(1-sigm(z))`, I can derive nice properties. The probability the unit is on: `P(h>0) = P(z > -a) = 1 - sigm(-a) = 1 - 1/(1+e^{a}) = 1/(1+e^{-a}) = sigm(a)`. So it fires with the sigmoid probability, just like my hard Bernoulli unit — good. And the expected output is exact. Start with `E[h] = ∫_{-a}^{∞}(a+z) sigm(z)(1-sigm(z)) dz`. Set `x = a+z`, so `z = x-a` and the lower limit becomes `0`: `E[h] = ∫_0^∞ x sigm(x-a)(1-sigm(x-a)) dx`. Now `d sigm(x-a)/dx = sigm(x-a)(1-sigm(x-a))`, so integration by parts gives `lim_{t→∞}([x sigm(x-a)]_0^t - ∫_0^t sigm(x-a) dx)`. The antiderivative of `sigm(x-a)` is `softplus(x-a)`, hence the expression is `lim_{t→∞}(t sigm(t-a) - softplus(t-a) + softplus(-a))`. As `t→∞`, `t sigm(t-a) - softplus(t-a) → a`, so `E[h] = a + softplus(-a) = softplus(a)`. So in expectation the noisy rectifier is a softplus — a soft, differentiable surrogate. It has an insensitive flat state (output exactly 0) and a sensitive sloped state, and gradients flow whenever the unit is active, plus *occasionally* when a noise spike pulls an inactive unit above 0. That occasional signal is the only thing that can resurrect a unit sitting in its dead state. Which is also the problem: if a unit's basic state is inactive, it almost never gets a gradient, it can "die," and dead units stay dead a long time while live units sometimes wander into the death zone and stick. I'd need a bias-adjustment mechanism — push each unit's bias up when its moving-average firing rate falls below target — to keep units alive. Workable, but it's a patch, and it changes the forward unit away from the clean hard threshold I wanted.

The second smoothing idea is sharper: factor the output into a hard random part times a smooth part. Let `p = sigm(a)`, draw `b ~ Binomial(√p)` (so `b ∈ {0,1}` with `P(b=1) = √p`), and output `h = b·√p`. Call it stochastic-times-smooth. The random factor `b` delivers true zeros — the sparsity I need — while the smooth factor `√p` is differentiable in `a`. What's the catch? Let me check what gradient I actually get. First, `E[h] = E[b]·√p = √p·√p = p = sigm(a)` — the mean is the firing probability, clean. And `P(h>0) = P(b>0) = √p`. Now the key property: for any differentiable `f` of the output, what is `E[f(h)]` to leading order as `p → 0`? Only two outcomes: `h = √p` with prob `√p`, `h = 0` with prob `1 - √p`. So `E[f(h)] = √p·f(√p) + (1-√p)·f(0)`. Taylor-expand both around `p`: `f(√p) = f(p) + f'(p)(√p - p) + o(√p - p)` and `f(0) = f(p) + f'(p)(-p) + o(p)`. Substitute:
`E[f(h)] = √p[f(p) + f'(p)(√p - p)] + (1-√p)[f(p) + f'(p)(-p)] + o(√p)`.
Collect the `f(p)` terms: `√p f(p) + (1-√p)f(p) = f(p)`. Collect the `f'(p)` terms: `√p f'(p)(√p - p) + (1-√p)f'(p)(-p) = f'(p)[ (p - p√p) - (p - p√p) ] = 0`. The first-order term cancels exactly. So `E[f(h)] = f(p) + o(√p)` as `p → 0` (using `√p > p` near 0 to absorb the `o(p)` into `o(√p)`). That's elegant — the expected effect of this stochastic unit on any downstream differentiable `f` equals `f` evaluated at the smooth probability `p`, to first order, with the linear correction vanishing precisely because I expanded around `p = E[h]`. So I can train it by ordinary backprop through the smooth `√p`-path and, in expectation, I'm doing the right thing for the hard unit it represents. Genuinely nice. But notice what I paid: the forward output isn't the clean indicator anymore, it's `b·√p`, and the trick only buys first-order correctness near `p → 0`, with momentum and beta-distributed input noise acting as extra stabilizers to coax the sigmoids into decisive (not perpetually-0.1) behavior. It's a real method, but it's intricate, and it modifies the unit.

Step back. I have an unbiased-but-expensive route (REINFORCE: high variance, needs the loss broadcast, no local credit assignment) and two smoothing routes (noisy rectifier, stochastic-times-smooth: they back-propagate cleanly but only by *replacing* the hard threshold with a smooth surrogate that changes the forward behavior and needs auxiliary machinery — bias targeting, special noise). Both families work, both are fiddly. What I actually wanted was simpler than either: keep the *forward* exactly the hard threshold — the true bit, the true zeros — and somehow still get a gradient back. The forward has to stay hard because that's where the sparsity and the skipped computation come from. So the only place left to intervene is the *backward* pass.

So here's the question reduced to its bones: the true Jacobian of the hard threshold is `0` almost everywhere; the chain rule multiplies by it and annihilates everything; what if, on the backward pass *only*, I just refuse to multiply by zero? Replace the threshold's backward Jacobian with a proxy that carries a usable sign. The true derivative carries no learning signal here — it's zero — so the question is which nonzero proxy preserves the direction I need.

The recipe that nags at me from the rectifier is the simplest possible: pretend the slope was one. Back-propagate through the hard threshold *as if it had been the identity function*. Then the gradient arriving at the unit's hard output, `dL/dh`, is sent unchanged to its pre-activation `a`: `g_a = dL/dh`. The forward still computes the true bit `h = 1_{z < sigm(a)}`; only the backward edge lies, pretending `dh/da = 1`. Let me sanity-check the sign, because a proxy that points the wrong way is worse than useless. The threshold is monotone increasing in `a`: raise `a`, you raise `sigm(a)`, you raise `P(h=1)`. So an increase in `a` tends to increase `h`, which means the *true* sign of "how `h` responds to `a`" is positive. The identity proxy assigns slope `+1`, also positive. So for a single layer, the straight-through gradient has the *right sign*: if downstream wants `h` larger (`dL/dh < 0` for descent), it pushes `a` larger, exactly as it should. Right sign means descent in expectation for one layer. It's biased — the magnitude is fabricated — but the direction is correct where it matters most.

I should poke at whether a *less crude* proxy is better, since "slope = 1" feels too cheap. The natural candidate: the unit's firing probability is `sigm(a)`, smooth, with derivative `sigm(a)(1-sigm(a))`. So multiply the incoming gradient by that — back-propagate as if the hard threshold were its smooth sigmoid surrogate, `g_a = (dL/dh)·sigm(a)(1-sigm(a))`. That looks more principled: it's the derivative of the thing the bit is sampling from. Let me think about where it helps and where it hurts. The factor `sigm(a)(1-sigm(a))` peaks at `a = 0` (value `1/4`) and decays to ~0 as `|a|` grows. So it *attenuates the gradient in saturation* — when the unit is confidently on or confidently off. But a confidently-thresholded gate is exactly the regime I live in: I *want* decisive gates, mostly-off, deep in saturation. In that regime `sigm(a)(1-sigm(a)) ≈ 0`, so this "principled" proxy zeroes out the gradient for precisely the most decided units — it reintroduces, softly, the very vanishing I was trying to escape. The plain identity proxy doesn't do that: it keeps the signal alive in saturation, slope one everywhere, so a saturated-but-wrong gate still gets a full push to flip. So the cruder proxy is actually the better one here, and not by accident — it's better *because* it refuses to attenuate where I need signal most. I'll keep identity and drop the sigmoid-derivative factor. (And if I ever test the multiplied variant, I expect it to underperform — fewer gates getting unstuck.)

There's an honesty cost I should name. "Right sign" is a single-layer guarantee. Stack many of these hard units, and the identity proxy's error compounds: across several layers the fabricated `dh/da = 1` factors can combine into a backward direction whose sign no longer matches the true effect on the loss. So I shouldn't claim this is an unbiased gradient or that it's safe arbitrarily deep — it's a biased estimator that's reliable for one layer of hard units and degrades as I stack them. For my conditional-computation use — a gating layer, essentially one layer of bits — that's exactly the regime where it's most trustworthy. And the trade I'm making is deliberate: REINFORCE was unbiased with brutal variance and no locality; this is biased with essentially *zero* added variance (it's deterministic given the backward pass) and full locality (it uses the structured backprop signal, just sending it through one extra edge). For learning, low variance with the right sign tends to beat unbiased-with-huge-variance. That's the whole bet.

Let me also convince myself this isn't just a hack with no descent story. For one hard unit, condition on everything downstream and write the two loss values as `L(1)` and `L(0)`. The expected loss is `F(a) = p L(1) + (1-p)L(0)` with `p = sigm(a)`, so the honest gradient is `F'(a) = p(1-p)(L(1)-L(0))`. If I linearly extend the downstream loss across the bit, `L(h)=hL(1)+(1-h)L(0)`, the gradient arriving at the bit is `dL/dh = L(1)-L(0)`, and the identity straight-through estimator sends exactly that to `a`: `g_ST = L(1)-L(0)`. Therefore `F'(a) = p(1-p) g_ST`, or equivalently `g_ST = F'(a)/(p(1-p))` for finite `a`. The factor `p(1-p)` is positive, so the sign is the same. For many coordinates in one layer this is a positive diagonal rescaling: `g_ST = D^{-1}∇F` with `D_ii = p_i(1-p_i) > 0`, hence `∇F · g_ST = Σ_i (∂F/∂a_i)^2 / D_ii > 0` unless the true gradient is zero. So `-g_ST` is a descent direction for the expected loss in that one-layer setting. The proxy is "coarse" — it is not the gradient of the stochastic computation as written — but this particular coarse gradient correlates positively with the real gradient. A badly chosen proxy (wrong sign, or one that vanishes where the useful signal is needed) need not have that property. Identity is a good choice precisely because it is a strictly positive rescaling of the true one-layer gradient and it does not attenuate in saturation.

Now let me make it real and run it on every example of a large net, because "modify the backward Jacobian" has to become code. Two equivalent implementations. The explicit one is a custom autograd primitive: forward applies the hard op, backward returns the incoming gradient untouched.

```python
import torch


class _StraightThrough(torch.autograd.Function):
    @staticmethod
    def forward(ctx, a):
        # forward = the true hard decision (here a binary threshold; honest dh/da = 0 a.e.)
        return (a > 0).float()             # or 1_{z < sigm(a)} for the stochastic gate

    @staticmethod
    def backward(ctx, grad_output):
        # backward = identity: send dL/dh straight back as dL/da, slope 1, no attenuation
        return grad_output
```

The slicker one needs no custom class — it's a one-liner that the autodiff engine evaluates to the same thing. Write `h = a + (hard(a) - a).detach()`. The forward *value* is `a + hard(a) - a = hard(a)`, the true hard output. But `(hard(a) - a)` is detached, so it contributes no gradient; the only differentiable term left is the bare `a`, whose derivative is `1`. So the backward gradient is exactly the identity. Same straight-through behavior, no boilerplate:

```python
def straight_through(a, hard):
    # forward = hard(a); backward = identity (the detached term carries no gradient)
    return a + (hard(a) - a).detach()
```

That's the gate. The same backward convention also fits the hard decision I need for trained discrete weights: quantize a weight to a tiny set of levels for the forward computation, but learn the tensor that generated those levels. The structure is the same backward trick plus a piece of bookkeeping I have to get right. The quantizer `round(·)` and `clamp(·)` are both non-differentiable — round is a staircase, slope 0 a.e.; clamp is flat outside its range. So I apply straight-through through the whole quantize-dequantize. But there's a subtlety the single-bit gate hid: I can't store the learned weight at low precision, because each weight has to absorb a long run of tiny, noisy gradient steps, and averaging those out needs real resolution — coarse storage would round every infinitesimal SGD step to zero and nothing would move. So I keep a **full-precision shadow weight**, quantize it *on the fly* each forward pass, use the quantized value in the forward (and let straight-through carry the gradient back to the shadow), and accumulate the updates in the shadow. Only the shadow learns; the quantized value is recomputed every step. If the quantization grid has a fixed real range, the shadow must be clipped so it cannot drift past the range where the codes change; in the per-group max-abs version below, the code range is bounded and the scale is recomputed from the shadow each forward pass so the grid tracks the current group. This shadow-weight / on-the-fly-quantize / full-precision-accumulate pattern is the load-bearing structure for making weight quantization train at all.

Let me write the quantizer concretely for `B`-bit per-group symmetric signed quantization, because that's the format I'll actually ship. Signed `B` bits gives integer codes in `[qmin, qmax] = [-2^{B-1}, 2^{B-1} - 1]`. Group the input dimension into blocks of `group_size` so each block gets its own scale (finer than per-tensor, cheap to store). For each (row, group) the scale is max-abs over the group divided by the positive code count, `s = max|w| / qmax`, floored away from zero so I never divide by it. The fake-quant is `w_q = clamp(round(w/s), qmin, qmax)·s`, and the straight-through wrapper makes its backward the identity:

```python
import torch


def _qrange(num_bits):
    qmax = (1 << (num_bits - 1)) - 1        # +2^{B-1} - 1
    qmin = -(1 << (num_bits - 1))           # -2^{B-1}
    return qmin, qmax


def fake_quantize_weight(weight, num_bits, group_size):
    qmin, qmax = _qrange(num_bits)
    out_features, in_features = weight.shape
    assert in_features % group_size == 0
    w = weight.float().reshape(out_features, -1, group_size)
    # per-(row, group) max-abs scale; recomputed every forward so the grid tracks the shadow weight
    w_max = w.abs().amax(dim=-1, keepdim=True).clamp(min=1e-12)   # floor avoids /0
    scale = w_max / qmax
    # round + clamp are non-differentiable (slope 0 a.e.); both go through straight-through below
    w_q = torch.clamp(torch.round(w / scale), qmin, qmax) * scale
    # straight-through: forward value = w_q, backward gradient = identity to the shadow weight
    w_dq = w + (w_q - w).detach()
    return w_dq.reshape(out_features, in_features).to(weight.dtype)
```

The forward path of a quantized linear layer just calls this on its shadow weight and does the matmul; the gradient flows straight back into the shadow, and the optimizer accumulates it there at full precision. After training, I materialize the real integer model with a no-grad version of the same quantize-dequantize — no straight-through needed, because I'm done learning and only want the deployed weights:

```python
def quantize_dequantize_weight(weight, num_bits, group_size):
    qmin, qmax = _qrange(num_bits)
    out_features, in_features = weight.shape
    assert in_features % group_size == 0
    with torch.no_grad():
        w = weight.float().reshape(out_features, -1, group_size)
        w_max = w.abs().amax(dim=-1, keepdim=True).clamp(min=1e-12)
        scale = w_max / qmax
        w_q = torch.clamp(torch.round(w / scale), qmin, qmax) * scale
        return w_q.reshape(out_features, in_features).to(weight.dtype)
```

and the layer wrapper that holds the shadow weight and applies fake-quant in its forward:


I should be honest about why this quantizer is deliberately *plain* — round-to-nearest with a max-abs scale, no learned step size, no fancy grid. The contribution I'm making is the *gradient*: straight-through is the minimal thing that makes quantization-aware training possible at all, and nothing more. The scale here is fixed by a simple statistic, not learned; learning the step size jointly with the weights to place the levels more cleverly is a *different* idea, layered on top. Keeping the scale at the simple default isolates exactly what straight-through buys: the ability to send a gradient back through the round and clamp so the shadow weight reorganizes to be quantization-friendly, instead of being frozen by a zero Jacobian.

Let me trace the whole causal chain so I'm sure it holds together. I wanted hard bit-valued units — for conditional computation, the bits let me skip whole expensive blocks — but a hard threshold's honest derivative is zero almost everywhere, so the chain rule annihilates the gradient and ordinary backprop sends nothing through it. The "must be smooth" dogma said this was hopeless, but rectifiers (piecewise-constant slope, trains fine) and dropout (noise in a smooth graph, trains fine) showed the dogma was soft — though neither reaches a genuinely bit-valued unit. Perturbation methods (finite differences `O(N²)`; SPSA `O(N)` but only valid for small perturbations, wrong for all-or-none spikes) are too costly or ill-suited. REINFORCE gives a certified-unbiased estimator — I derived it as `(h_i - sigm(a_i))L`, with a per-unit variance-optimal baseline — but it's high variance, needs the loss broadcast to every unit, and discards backprop's local credit assignment. Smoothing the unit (noisy rectifier with `E[h] = softplus(a)`; stochastic-times-smooth with `E[f(h)] = f(p) + o(√p)`) lets backprop through cleanly but only by replacing the hard forward with a smooth surrogate plus auxiliary machinery. So I kept the forward exactly hard and intervened only on the backward edge: replace the threshold's zero Jacobian with a nonzero proxy. The identity proxy — back-propagate as if the hard op were the identity — has the right sign for one layer because the true one-layer gradient is `sigm(a)(1-sigm(a))` times the straight-through quantity, so straight-through is a positive rescaling of the true gradient; it keeps the gradient alive in saturation, where multiplying by the firing-probability derivative would wrongly attenuate it; and it is essentially zero-variance and fully local. It's biased and that bias compounds across stacked hard units, so it's most trustworthy for a single gating layer. In code it's either a custom autograd Function with identity backward or the one-line `a + (hard(a) - a).detach()`; and on weights it becomes quantization-aware training — a full-precision shadow weight, quantized on the fly with round-and-clamp, straight-through carrying the gradient back to the shadow, the shadow accumulating updates at full precision, with a final no-grad quantize-dequantize to materialize the deployed integer model.
