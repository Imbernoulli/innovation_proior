Let me start from what actually blocks me. I have a sample of data — pairs `(x, y)` measured from some system — and I want the formula behind it, in symbolic form, something I can read and reason about, like `log(x+1) + log(x²+1)` or `2·sin(x)·cos(z)`. The tools I reach for first don't even fit the question. Linear regression, quadratic regression, any polynomial regression: each of them hands me a *template* — `y = a₀ + a₁x + a₂x² + …` — and all they do is solve for the coefficients `aᵢ` that minimize `Σ(y − ŷ)²`. That's a beautiful, closed-form thing when I already know the shape of the answer. But I don't. The whole difficulty is that I don't know whether the truth is a polynomial, a sum of sinusoids, something with a logarithm, a ratio of things. If the data really came from `log(x+1) + log(x²+1)`, no polynomial template will ever recover it — a logarithm is not a finite polynomial — and least squares will just hand me a high-order polynomial that wiggles through my sample points and generalizes terribly. So the missing thing isn't a better coefficient solver. It's that I need to choose the functional *form* itself, and that choice is the answer, not an input.

So reframe it as a search. I want to search the space of *all expressions* for one that fits the data. Fine — but immediately I hit the thing that makes this not look like any optimization I know. The "things" I'm searching over don't have a fixed size. An expression might be three symbols (`x`) or three hundred; it might be a flat sum or a deeply nested composition. There is no fixed-dimensional vector of parameters here — the number of "knobs" isn't even constant from one candidate to the next. Gradient descent needs a fixed `θ` to move; coefficient fitting needs a fixed set of coefficients. I have neither. The objects are hierarchical, combinatorial, non-numeric. I can't differentiate my way through this space; I can't even write down a coordinate system for it. I need a search method that works on a weird combinatorial space where I can score a candidate but can't take its gradient.

What do I have for *that*? The genetic algorithm. Holland's idea: keep a whole population of candidate solutions, score each one with a fitness function, and breed the population forward like natural selection — let the fitter ones make more offspring, recombine pairs of them, sprinkle in a little mutation, repeat for generations, and watch average fitness climb. I love this for my situation because it asks almost nothing of the space: it never needs a gradient, never needs the objective to be smooth or even continuous. It only needs two things — that I can *evaluate* a candidate's fitness, and that I have operators to make new candidates from old ones. Evaluating fitness I can do: run the candidate expression on my data, measure the error. So the engine fits.

Let me be precise about the engine, because the details are going to matter. In Holland's setup each candidate is a fixed-length string of characters — a chromosome — over some small alphabet. Selection is fitness-proportionate: a string gets expected copies in proportion to its share of the total fitness, so the fitter it is the more it breeds. Crossover takes two parent strings, picks a random cut point, and swaps the tails to make two children. Mutation flips a character now and then to keep raw diversity from draining away. And there's real theory under it — Holland's schema theorem. A schema is a template, like `1**0`, matching a set of strings. Its order is the number of fixed positions, and its defining length is the distance between the outermost fixed positions. Selection gives an above-average schema more expected copies by the factor `f(H)/f̄`; one-point crossover disrupts it only when the cut falls inside its defining span, with probability roughly `δ(H)/(l−1)`; mutation disrupts it in proportion to its order. So short, low-order schemata whose average fitness beats the population average receive increasing trials over generations, because they are easy to preserve and repeatedly selected. The genetic algorithm is implicitly betting on above-average "building blocks" the way an optimal gambler spreads bets across a multi-armed bandit, and crossover's job is to splice good building blocks from different parents into better wholes. This is a genuinely good search engine for nasty landscapes.

Now here's the wall. Every bit of that — the schema counting, the building-block story, the clean theory — lives on the *fixed-length string*. The schema arithmetic is clean precisely because every chromosome has the same length and every position has a fixed, pre-assigned meaning. But my candidates are expressions of unknown and *varying* size and shape. If I force them into a fixed-length string, I have to decide the length up front — which is deciding, before I start, how big the answer is allowed to be. That's exactly the thing I'm trying to discover. And even setting that aside, what would each character of the string *mean*? An expression is nested — an operator applied to operands which are themselves expressions. Flattening that hierarchy into a flat string and assigning a fixed role to position 17 is unnatural and brittle; a crossover that cuts the string at a random byte will, almost always, slice through the middle of a nested subexpression and produce garbage that doesn't parse, let alone evaluate. So the string representation, which is the whole foundation the genetic algorithm and its theory stand on, is the one piece that doesn't reach my problem. The selection-and-recombination machinery is right; the chromosome is wrong.

So I shouldn't keep the engine and fight the encoding. I should keep the engine and *change what the individual is*. Let me look hard at what an expression actually is, structurally. Take `0.234·z + x − 0.789`. I'd write it flat, but it isn't flat — it's `+` applied to `(0.234·z)` and to `(x − 0.789)`, where `0.234·z` is `·` applied to `0.234` and `z`, and so on. That's a tree: `+` at the root, with a `·`-subtree and a `−`-subtree hanging off it, operators at the internal nodes, variables and constants at the leaves. And this isn't a representation I'm inventing — it's the *parse tree*, the thing every compiler already builds internally for an expression, and in the LISP family the tree (the symbolic expression, the S-expression) is a first-class object that a program can build and pick apart at run time. The crucial structural facts: a tree can be any size and any shape — there is no fixed length — and **any subtree of a valid expression is itself a valid expression**. That second fact is the one to hold onto.

So let the individual *be the expression tree itself*. Don't encode the candidate into some other alphabet and decode it back — the genotype and the phenotype are the same object. The population is a population of trees over a chosen *function set* `F` (the operators I'll allow — `+`, `−`, `·`, protected `/`, maybe `sin`, `cos`, `log`, `exp`) and a *terminal set* `T` (the input variables, plus constants). Now I just need Holland's two operators to act on trees instead of strings. Selection is unchanged — fitness is fitness, I can pick the fitter trees to breed regardless of their shape. The whole question collapses onto crossover: how do I recombine two parent trees?

The disease of string crossover was that cutting at a random point destroys structure. But with trees I have a unit of structure that is *always self-contained*: the subtree. So let me try the obvious thing — pick a node (a crossover point) in each parent, on copies so I don't damage the originals, and swap the two subtrees rooted at those nodes. Parent one's chosen subtree goes into parent two's spot and vice versa. Before I believe this, I want to actually run a swap and watch what comes out, including a deliberately nasty case, because the claim "any swap is valid" is exactly the kind of thing that sounds true and then isn't. Take `parent₁ = 0.234·z + (x − 0.789)` and `parent₂ = div(log(x), x − x)` — I chose `parent₂` on purpose so its denominator `x − x` is zero, the worst input it could carry. Cross them by grafting `parent₂`'s left subtree, `log(x)`, into `parent₁`'s right slot where the `(x − 0.789)` subtree sat. The result should be `0.234·z + log(x)`. Evaluating at `x = 2, z = 1`: the child gives `0.234·1 + log(2) = 0.234 + 0.6931 = 0.9271`, and computing `0.234 + math.log(2)` independently gives `0.9271471805599453` — the same number. The graft produced a tree that parses and evaluates, and it did so for *any* point I could have picked, because in every case I replaced a valid subexpression with another valid subexpression. (The donor parent's own `x − x = 0` never bit, because I only moved its `log(x)` subtree; but even if I'd grafted the `div(...)` whole, protected division would have caught the zero — I'll come back to that.) That worked example is what convinces me the closure problem that wrecked string crossover simply doesn't arise here: it's headed off by the choice of representation itself. And the swap does exactly what crossover is supposed to do in Holland's building-block picture: useful subexpressions — `log(x+1)`, `sin(x)`, a good constant subtree — are the building blocks, and swapping subtrees recombines them across individuals, with selection steering trials toward the parents that carry the good ones. The schema theorem itself was stated for fixed strings, so I shouldn't pretend I get the same theorem for free; what carries over is the building-block logic, now with subexpressions as the pieces selection protects and recombination moves. So the shape of the method is settling: run Holland's Darwinian loop directly on expression trees, recombining by subtree exchange.

Let me now actually assemble the loop and force every remaining choice to justify itself, because a sloppy detail anywhere will poison a run.

Start with evaluating a tree, because closure has a second, sneakier face — the one I dodged a moment ago. I have a population of *random* trees, and I'm going to keep cutting and splicing them. That means an arbitrary operator can end up applied to arbitrary operands. What happens when an evolved tree contains `div(something, x − x)` and `x − x` is zero — exactly the `parent₂` I built earlier? Or `log` of a negative number, or `exp` of a huge number? With ordinary arithmetic, `div(log(x), x−x)` raises `ZeroDivisionError` (or returns `inf`), and once one candidate produces `NaN`/`Inf` my fitness comparisons are meaningless and the run is poisoned. I can't forbid these subtrees — crossover will reconstruct them constantly; that very `parent₂` is one a random run would produce. So I have to make the *primitives themselves* total: every function must return a sane finite value for every possible input it could ever be handed. Protected division: if the divisor is essentially zero, return `1.0` instead of dividing. Let me check that this actually defuses the trap rather than just relocating it — evaluating `parent₂ = div(log(x), x − x)` at `x = 2` with protected division, the divisor `x − x` is `0`, so it returns `1.0` and the whole expression evaluates to `log(2)/1 → 1.0` in my trace, a finite number, no exception. Good: the operator absorbs the zero. Protected log: take `log(|a|)`, and return `0` when the argument is essentially zero (so `log(0)` doesn't explode and a sign flip doesn't kill it). Protected exp: clip the exponent into a safe range, say `[−10, 10]`, before exponentiating, so it can't overflow. And as a final net, after evaluating, replace any stray `NaN`/`Inf` with finite values and clip the whole output to a large finite range. This is the *evaluation-safety* half of closure, the partner to the *type-consistency* half. Type consistency: I make every function take and return the same type — real-valued — so any subtree can legally sit in any argument slot, which is exactly what unrestricted subtree crossover requires. Together: every function accepts any subtree's output and any input. So closure isn't decoration — the same crossover freedom that makes the search valid is what would hand me a non-finite fitness on the very next generation if the primitives weren't total.

While I'm choosing `F`, sufficiency. The function set has to be *able* to express a solution, or the search is doomed before it starts — the best it can do is approximate. If my target is `log(x+1) + log(x²+1)`, then `{+, −, ·, /}` alone can never represent it, because a transcendental like `log` is not any finite rational combination of those. So for transcendental benchmarks I must include `sin`, `cos`, `log`, `exp` in `F`. The flip side: throwing in a few extra primitives I might not need usually doesn't hurt the search much, while leaving out a necessary one is fatal — so I err toward including the operators the targets plausibly need. For a purely polynomial target like `x⁶ − 2x⁴ + x²`, `{+, −, ·}` already suffices, since that's just repeated multiplication and addition; the transcendental operators come along for free and the search can ignore them.

Now constants. Symbolic regression needs arbitrary *numbers* — coefficients like `0.234`, the `2` in `2 sin(x) cos(z)`. I can't put every real number into the terminal set `T`; that set has to be finite. So how does a real-valued constant ever enter a tree? The trick: add one special terminal that is an *ephemeral random constant*. Whenever, during the random construction of a tree (or when I generate a fresh subtree later), that terminal is selected, I draw a random number from some range and *freeze* it into that spot — that particular leaf now holds, say, `−0.402`, permanently. Different leaves get different draws, so the initial population already contains a scatter of random constants. From then on, crossover just *moves these constants around* and combines them: a `·` node might bring `0.90` next to a subtree and effectively scale it; a `+` might combine `−0.402` and `−0.583` into something that behaves like `−0.985`. So the relentless fitness pressure tunes constants not by any numerical optimizer but by selecting the recombinations that happen to land on better coefficients — a slight `×0.90` here, a `−0.08` there, whichever improves the fit gets selected and propagated. The constants are *evolved*, not solved.

Next, how do I seed generation zero? I need a population of *random* trees, and I want them varied — in both size and shape — because the initial spread is the raw material everything downstream recombines. The obvious two ways each have a flaw. The `full` method: at every node, keep choosing operators until I hit the depth limit, then choose terminals — this gives "bushy" trees with all leaves at the same depth, but they're all the same shape, so I get poor *shape* variety. The `grow` method: at each node, choose freely from operators *or* terminals (until the depth limit forces a terminal) — this gives irregular shapes, but its size distribution is wildly sensitive to the ratio of terminals to functions in my primitive set: if there are many more terminals than operators, `grow` keeps picking terminals early and almost always produces tiny stunted trees regardless of the depth limit. Neither alone gives me what I want. So combine them: **ramped half-and-half**. Pick a *range* of depth limits — ramp from shallow to deep, say 2 up to 6 — and at each depth build half the trees with `full` and half with `grow`. The ramp gives a spread of sizes; the half-and-half gives a spread of shapes within each size; together I get a genuinely diverse starting population. I keep the initial depth limit modest (around 6) so generation zero isn't full of enormous unwieldy random trees.

Now selection — which parents breed. Holland's default is fitness-proportionate: expected offspring proportional to fitness share. For symbolic regression my raw score is an error, so lower is better; if I want proportionate selection I first have to turn that into a bigger-is-better quantity, for example adjusted fitness `1/(1 + error)`, and then normalize those adjusted values into shares. Let me think about whether that's actually good here, because I can feel a failure mode. Proportionate selection is sensitive to the *scale* of those adjusted fitness values. Early in a run, suppose one lucky individual has far smaller error than everyone else — its adjusted-fitness share can be huge, so it grabs an enormous fraction of the reproductive opportunities and floods the next generation with its children. The population's diversity collapses around one tree, and now crossover has nothing to recombine *with* — every parent is a near-copy — so the search converges prematurely on a mediocre peak and dies. And the opposite failure shows up late: as the population improves, adjusted fitnesses can compress into a narrow band, the shares become nearly equal, and proportionate selection loses its ability to discriminate — selection pressure evaporates just when I still want to prefer the slightly-better trees. So proportionate selection has the wrong dependence on the *magnitudes* of fitness, at both ends of a run.

What I actually want is for selection to care only about *which* tree is better, not by how much — to be invariant to the scale and spread of the fitness numbers. So: draw a small number `k` of individuals uniformly from the population, with replacement, and let the single lowest-error individual among those `k` be the selected parent. A *tournament* of size `k`. Walk through why this fixes both problems. It only ever *compares* fitnesses; it never sums them or weights by magnitude, so a tree that is a thousand times better than the rest is treated exactly like one that is barely better — it just wins its tournaments. No single super-fit individual can swamp the next generation by taking an enormous numeric share, because to be selected it still has to be drawn into a tournament and there are only so many tournaments; its advantage is rank, not magnitude. That kills the early diversity-collapse. And late in the run, even when fitnesses are nearly equal, the better tree still wins its tournament whenever it appears against worse contenders, so selection pressure is controlled by `k` instead of by the accidental spread of raw scores. The size `k` is the single knob for how greedy selection is: `k = 1` is random drift (no pressure), large `k` is nearly always taking the population best (very greedy); a moderate value like 7 is a sensible default — strong enough to drive improvement, loose enough that average trees still occasionally win and keep diversity alive. It's also cheap: `O(k)` per selection, no global sum over the population, trivially parallel. Tournament selection it is.

Now the operators that build the new generation, and the rates. Crossover I already have — subtree exchange. But I should make it concrete and notice one more thing. To make a child by crossover: copy parent one (so I never mutate the original — the parents may be selected again and reused), copy parent two as the donor, pick a crossover point in each, and replace the copy's chosen subtree with the donor's chosen subtree. Now, *how* do I pick the crossover point? The naive choice is uniform over all nodes. Before I decide that's fine, let me count what fraction of nodes a uniform pick would actually land on a leaf, because if binary operators dominate the answer isn't obviously balanced. For a tree built only from binary internal nodes and leaves, the edge count gives `2·(internals) = (total nodes) − 1`, which solves to `leaves = internals + 1` — leaves always *outnumber* internals by one, so they're a hair over half the nodes regardless of size: 2 leaves of 3 nodes (0.667), 4 of 7 (0.571), 8 of 15 (0.533), 16 of 31 (0.516). Concretely, my `parent₁ = 0.234·z + (x − 0.789)` has 7 nodes, 4 of them leaves, so uniform point choice gives `P(leaf) = 4/7 ≈ 0.571` — most picks land on a single terminal, swapping one variable or constant for another. That's a trivial exchange; it barely moves me in program space, and the majority of my crossovers would be doing almost nothing structural. I want crossover to usually swap a *meaningful subtree*, an internal-node-rooted chunk with real structure. So bias the point selection: give function nodes much larger weight than terminals, the common 90/10 convention — weight each function node by `0.9` and each terminal by `0.1`, then normalize across the actual nodes present. Let me see what that does to the same `parent₁`: with three function nodes at `0.9` and four terminals at `0.1`, the normalizer is `3·0.9 + 4·0.1 = 3.1`, so `P(leaf) = 0.4/3.1 ≈ 0.129` — the leaf probability drops from 0.571 to about 0.13. That confirms what I wanted: the weighting doesn't make the aggregate probability exactly 90% (it's `0.871` here, because the tree happens to be small and leaf-heavy), but it flips the regime so the typical crossover now swaps a genuine subexpression and only occasionally fiddles with a single terminal — an internal node is nine times as likely as a terminal before normalization, correcting for the fact that terminals are so numerous.

But crossover has a built-in ceiling: it can only ever shuffle genetic material that's *already in the population*. If some primitive, or some constant value, is absent from every tree — never drawn in generation zero, or lost when a sub-population died out — then no amount of recombination can ever bring it back. Crossover splices existing pieces; it can't invent a new one. So if the answer needs a `cos` and `cos` has vanished from the gene pool, I'm stuck forever below the optimum. I need a source of *fresh* material. That's mutation. The natural tree-mutation, mirroring subtree crossover: pick a node, delete the subtree rooted there, and graft in a *newly generated random subtree* in its place. (Mechanically this is the same as crossing the tree with a freshly grown random tree — "headless chicken" crossover — but the point is that it injects primitives and constants the population may not currently have.) Mutation is disruptive, though — it throws away a working subtree and replaces it with random noise — so its rate has to be small; the search should lean overwhelmingly on crossover to assemble building blocks, and use mutation sparingly just to keep the gene pool from going extinct. A crossover rate around 0.9 and a mutation rate around a few percent.

And the third operator, the leftover probability: **reproduction** — just copy a selected individual into the next generation unchanged. This is the "survival" half of survival-of-the-fittest expressed at the operator level: a good tree should sometimes pass forward intact, not always be cut up. With crossover at 0.9 and mutation at, say, 0.05, reproduction takes the remaining 0.05. So building one new individual is: roll a random number; below 0.9, do crossover on two tournament-selected parents; between 0.9 and 0.95, mutate one tournament-selected parent; otherwise, copy one tournament-selected parent.

Two more things the dynamics force on me. First, **elitism**. My operators are stochastic — crossover and mutation can easily destroy the current best individual, and if they do, the next generation could be strictly worse than this one. I don't want the best-so-far to ever go backward across a generation boundary. So I copy the single best individual of the current generation into the next one unconditionally, before filling the rest by breeding. That guarantees the population's best is monotone non-worsening, and it costs one slot. (Whether I bake this into the generation or just separately track the best tree ever seen and report *that* at the end, the effect is the same — the result of a run is the best individual ever found.)

Second, **bloat**, and the depth limit. Run this loop for many generations and a nasty phenomenon shows up: programs grow in size without any matching gain in fitness — they accumulate inert subexpressions, "introns," chunks that don't change the output. Why does it happen? Because once the population is near a fitness plateau, larger trees are statistically more likely to survive crossover *unharmed* (a random crossover point is more likely to land in the inert padding than in the working core), so selection quietly rewards size for its own protective value. Left unchecked, trees balloon, eating memory and compute and turning my "interpretable symbolic model" into an unreadable monster — which defeats the entire purpose, since I wanted a *readable* formula. The simple, effective brake is a hard cap on tree depth — say a limit of 17, deep enough to hold any expression I'd actually want yet a firm ceiling. After crossover (or mutation), if the offspring's depth exceeds the cap, reject it and return a copy of a parent instead. The initial depth limit is smaller (around 6) so generation zero starts compact; the larger 17 gives crossover room to assemble structure during the run without letting it run away.

Now fitness — the number selection consumes. The task is a least-squares fit of the candidate's output to the targets `y` over the sample points (the "fitness cases"). The natural error is the squared error: take the candidate's predictions over the sample, subtract the targets, square, and average — mean squared error, `MSE = (1/n) Σ (ŷ − y)²`, lower is better. Squared error is the right choice here for the usual reasons — it's the least-squares objective, it's smooth in the residuals, it penalizes large misses heavily — and it maps cleanly onto what selection needs: tournament selection only needs to compare two candidates and say which is better, and "smaller MSE is better" is exactly such a comparison. (If I'd kept fitness-proportionate selection I'd have to transform raw error into a non-negative, bounded "adjusted" fitness where bigger means better — something like `1/(1 + error)` so a perfect fit maps to 1 and selection shares are well-defined; but tournament needs only the raw error and its ordering, so I can use MSE directly.)

Let me assemble the whole loop in my head one time to make sure it's coherent. Generation zero: a ramped-half-and-half population of random trees over `F` and `T`, with ephemeral random constants frozen into leaves. Each generation: evaluate every tree's MSE on the data; carry the best one forward by elitism; then repeatedly select parents by tournament and build children — 90% by subtree crossover (with function nodes weighted `0.9` and terminals `0.1` when choosing crossover points, depth-capped at 17), ~5% by subtree mutation (depth-capped), the rest by plain reproduction — until the new population is full. Repeat for a fixed number of generations, keeping track of the best individual ever seen, and report that tree as the discovered formula. Every piece is there: representation that makes the space searchable and recombination valid (trees, closure), an engine that needs no gradient (Darwinian selection), a recombination that exploits structure (subtree crossover with function/terminal point weights), a scale-free selector (tournaments), a diversity injector (subtree mutation), survival of the best (reproduction + elitism), a brake on bloat (depth cap), and a fitness that's just the least-squares error.

Let me write it as the code I'd actually run, filling the four empty slots in the harness.

```python
import random
import numpy as np

# `safe_evaluate`, `generate_random`, the tree object with
# .copy()/.depth()/.get_all_nodes(), and the protected operators are the fixed substrate.


def _is_function_node(node):
    return bool(getattr(node, "children", ()))


def _choose_subtree_index(tree):
    """Crossover point choice: function nodes weight 0.9, leaves 0.1."""
    nodes = tree.get_all_nodes()
    weights = [0.9 if _is_function_node(node) else 0.1
               for node, _parent, _child_idx in nodes]
    return random.choices(range(len(nodes)), weights=weights, k=1)[0]


def _replace_subtree(tree, point, replacement):
    """Replace the subtree at point in a copied tree, including the root case."""
    _node, parent, child_idx = tree.get_all_nodes()[point]
    if parent is None:
        return replacement.copy()
    parent.children[child_idx] = replacement.copy()
    return tree


def fitness_function(tree, X, y):
    """Least-squares fit error over the fitness cases. Lower is better."""
    y_pred = safe_evaluate(tree, X)            # run the candidate on the data (NaN/Inf sanitized)
    return float(np.mean((y - y_pred) ** 2))   # mean squared error


def selection(population, fitnesses, n_select, tournament_size=7):
    """Tournament selection: only RANK matters, so it is invariant to fitness scale/spread,
    keeping selection pressure constant and stopping any one tree from swamping the pool."""
    selected = []
    pop_size = len(population)
    k = max(1, min(tournament_size, pop_size))
    for _ in range(n_select):
        # sample k contenders with replacement, then take the single lowest-error one
        contenders = [random.randrange(pop_size) for _ in range(k)]
        best = min(contenders, key=lambda i: fitnesses[i])
        selected.append(population[best].copy())   # copy: parents may be reused, never mutated
    return selected


def crossover(parent1, parent2, n_features, max_depth=17):
    """Subtree crossover: swap a subtree of parent1 with a subtree of parent2. Because any
    subtree is itself a valid expression, every swap yields a valid offspring (closure)."""
    offspring = parent1.copy()
    donor = parent2.copy()

    off_point = _choose_subtree_index(offspring)
    don_point = _choose_subtree_index(donor)
    donor_subtree = donor.get_all_nodes()[don_point][0]
    offspring = _replace_subtree(offspring, off_point, donor_subtree)

    if offspring.depth() > max_depth:              # bloat brake: reject over-deep offspring
        return parent1.copy()
    return offspring


def mutation(parent, n_features, max_depth=17):
    """Subtree mutation: replace a random subtree with a freshly grown random subtree. This
    injects primitives/constants crossover alone could never reintroduce once they are gone."""
    offspring = parent.copy()

    # Headless-chicken mutation: build a fresh random donor and graft one of its subtrees.
    donor = generate_random('half and half', min(6, max_depth), n_features)
    donor_point = _choose_subtree_index(donor)
    new_subtree = donor.get_all_nodes()[donor_point][0]

    mut_point = _choose_subtree_index(offspring)
    offspring = _replace_subtree(offspring, mut_point, new_subtree)

    if offspring.depth() > max_depth:              # same depth cap
        return parent.copy()
    return offspring


def evolve_one_generation(population, fitnesses, X_train, y_train,
                          n_features, pop_size,
                          crossover_rate=0.9, mutation_rate=0.05,
                          max_depth=17):
    """One Darwinian generation: elitism, then breed by tournament selection +
    crossover / mutation / reproduction in the chosen proportions."""
    new_population = []

    # elitism: carry the single best individual forward so best-of-generation never worsens
    elite_idx = int(np.argmin(fitnesses))
    new_population.append(population[elite_idx].copy())

    while len(new_population) < pop_size:
        r = random.random()
        if r < crossover_rate:                                  # ~90%: subtree crossover
            parents = selection(population, fitnesses, 2)
            child = crossover(parents[0], parents[1], n_features, max_depth)
        elif r < crossover_rate + mutation_rate:                # ~5%: subtree mutation
            parents = selection(population, fitnesses, 1)
            child = mutation(parents[0], n_features, max_depth)
        else:                                                   # remainder: reproduction
            parents = selection(population, fitnesses, 1)
            child = parents[0]
        new_population.append(child)

    return new_population[:pop_size]
```
