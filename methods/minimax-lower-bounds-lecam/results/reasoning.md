I start from the minimax quantifiers. The estimator is chosen first, then nature may choose any distribution in the class. To prove a lower bound I do not need to understand the entire class at once. It is enough to find a small subproblem inside it, because the supremum over the full class is at least the supremum over any two selected points. This is the first economy in Le Cam's method: replace a continuous or infinite-dimensional estimation problem by two named worlds.

The two worlds have to pull in opposite directions. If their parameters are nearly identical, confusing them does not matter. So I choose `P_0` and `P_1` with `rho(theta_0, theta_1) >= 2s`. The number `s` is the accuracy radius I want to rule out. Any estimator that is within `s` of the truth under both worlds would necessarily reveal which world is true, because one estimate cannot lie within radius `s` of both targets when the targets are separated by at least `2s`.

This gives the reduction to testing. From any estimator `hat theta`, define a test that chooses the world whose parameter is closer to `hat theta`. If the test is wrong under `P_0`, then `hat theta` must be at least distance `s` from `theta_0`; otherwise it would have chosen `P_0`. The same argument holds under `P_1`. Therefore the estimation risk controls the testing error. A uniformly excellent estimator would create an excellent test.

Now the problem is purely probabilistic. For two simple hypotheses, the best possible sum of type I and type II errors is `1 - TV(P_0^n, P_1^n)`, equivalently the equal-prior Bayes error is `(1 - TV(P_0^n, P_1^n))/2`. No clever estimator can beat this because every estimator-induced classifier is just another test. Thus a typical Le Cam bound has the form: minimax loss is at least a constant times the parameter separation, multiplied by an indistinguishability factor such as `1 - TV(P_0^n, P_1^n)`.

Total variation is conceptually exact but can be hard to compute directly. KL and Hellinger enter as tractable upper bounds on distinguishability. If `KL(P_0^n || P_1^n)` is small, Pinsker-type inequalities keep total variation away from one. Hellinger distance is often even more convenient for products or mixture constructions because its affinity tensorizes cleanly. These divergences are not measuring estimation error; they measure how much evidence the sample can carry about which of the two worlds generated it.

The construction therefore has two opposing design constraints. The worlds must be far enough apart in the target metric to make the desired loss nontrivial. At the same time, they must be statistically close enough that the best binary test still fails with positive probability. The art of applying Le Cam is in tuning this pair. In a normal mean problem, that may mean choosing two means separated by the noise scale divided by `sqrt n`. In a nonparametric problem, it may mean placing two smooth functions whose features differ in loss but generate nearly overlapping distributions.

This explains why the argument is more fundamental than analyzing a specific algorithm. Algorithm analysis asks what a chosen procedure does with the data. Le Cam asks whether the data can support the decision at all. If the two worlds are indistinguishable, then every decision rule faces the same bottleneck: it must sometimes give nearly the same output on samples that require different correct answers. The lower bound follows from information loss in the experiment, not from computational weakness, poor tuning, convexity failure, or a loose proof of an estimator.

The method is deliberately small. It can be loose when the true difficulty is many-way ambiguity, where Fano or Assouad may capture dimension dependence better. But that smallness is also its clarity. A two-point lower bound is a certificate of impossibility: here are two admissible worlds; they demand different answers; the observation law does not let any rule tell them apart reliably. That is the core Le Cam insight.
