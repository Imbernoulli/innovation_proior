Let me start from what actually goes wrong when I try to solve a bilevel problem. I have an outer objective f(x,y) and I want to minimize it, but y isn't free — for the current x it has to lie in S(x) = argmin_y g(x,y), the minimizer set of an inner objective g(x,.). As x moves, that whole set moves with it, and that coupling is the entire difficulty. When g(x,.) is strongly convex, life is easy: S(x) collapses to a single point y*(x), the problem reduces to min_x f(x, y*(x)), and differentiating the lower stationarity equation grad_y g(x, y*(x)) = 0 hands me the hypergradient grad_x f - grad_xy g (grad_yy g)^{-1} grad_y f. Beautiful — but look at what it leans on. It needs grad_yy g invertible, which is exactly strong convexity, and it needs me to form a Hessian-inverse-vector product, which is second-order. The instant g(x,.) stops being strongly convex, grad_yy g can be singular and the formula is undefined; and if S(x) is a flat valley of many minimizers rather than a point, there is no single y*(x) to differentiate at all. That's the regime I care about — PL-but-nonconvex lower levels, possibly non-singleton S(x) — and the implicit-gradient machinery just doesn't reach it.

So what else is on the table. I could replace y*(x) by T steps of inner gradient descent, y_T(x), and backpropagate the outer loss through the whole unrolled trajectory — reverse-mode differentiation through the optimizer. That sidesteps the Hessian inverse, but it has its own troubles: I have to store the entire trajectory for the reverse pass, so memory and compute grow with T; a projection inside the inner loop is awkward to differentiate, so I'm pinned to the unconstrained case; and I don't get a clean finite-time relation to the true bilevel problem without restrictive assumptions on the unrolled map. It's still, secretly, second-order through the Jacobians of the inner update. I want a method that doesn't depend on a differentiable lower-solution map and doesn't store a long inner trajectory.

The natural next move is to fold the lower-optimality constraint into a penalty and descend one joint objective over (x,y). The first tempting penalty is the squared lower-gradient norm, ||grad_y g(x,y)||^2, because it's directly differentiable. So: min_{x,y} f + gamma||grad_y g||^2, gradient descent, done — let me test it on a tiny case before I trust it, because penalty methods love to fool you. Take g(y) = y^2 + 2 sin^2 y, f(y) = sin^2(y - 2pi/3); the only bilevel solution is y* = 0. Here grad_y g = 2y + 2 sin 2y, which vanishes exactly on the argmin set, so ||grad_y g||^2 = 4(y + sin 2y)^2 is a legitimate optimality metric. Penalize it and look at y = 2pi/3. Is it a critical point? The derivative of (y + sin 2y)^2 is 2(y + sin 2y)(1 + 2 cos 2y); at y = 2pi/3, cos(4pi/3) = -1/2 so the factor 1 + 2 cos 2y is zero, and the penalty gradient vanishes — for *every* gamma — even though grad_y g itself is nonzero there. So y = 2pi/3 is a local minimizer of the penalized problem at every penalty strength, yet it is neither a global nor even a local solution of the bilevel problem. Gradient descent on the naive penalty parks at junk. Wall. And the diagnosis matters: the penalty doesn't have the wrong zero set — its zeros are still the right ones — the trouble is that its *local stationarity* can be killed by a degenerate Hessian factor (the 1 + 2 cos 2y, which is a piece of grad_yy g) before lower feasibility is reached.

That failure tells me the right abstraction isn't "some optimality metric is zero" but "the metric gives a controlled *distance* to the lower solution set." The exact constraint y in S(x) is equivalent to d_{S(x)}(y)^2 = 0, the squared distance from y to the minimizer set. But that squared distance isn't computable — I'd have to know S(x) to evaluate it. So I want a computable, smooth p(x,y) that *behaves like* d^2: nonnegative, zero exactly on S(x), and with rho p(x,y) dominating d_{S(x)}(y)^2 for some constant rho. Call any such p a squared-distance bound. The domination is the part the naive penalty lacked: once p is a squared-distance bound, a small penalty residual isn't just a small number, it's a certificate that y is close to lower feasibility.

The generic global relation then follows from one scalar inequality, and it's worth doing carefully. Take any feasible (x,y), project y to y_x in S(x) so d_{S(x)}(y) = ||y_x - y||, and assume f(x,.) is L-Lipschitz, which controls how much the outer objective moves under that projection: f(x,y) - f(x,y_x) >= -L d_{S(x)}(y). Add gamma* p and use the squared-distance bound p >= d^2/rho on the penalty term:

  f(x,y) + gamma* p(x,y) - f(x,y_x) >= -L d_{S(x)}(y) + (gamma*/rho) d^2_{S(x)}(y).

The right side is a scalar function of z = d_{S(x)}(y): it's -Lz + (gamma*/rho) z^2. Minimize over z >= 0: derivative -L + 2(gamma*/rho)z = 0 at z* = L rho/(2 gamma*), value -L^2 rho/(2 gamma*) + L^2 rho/(4 gamma*) = -L^2 rho/(4 gamma*). So the whole expression is bounded below by -L^2 rho/(4 gamma*). If I want that floor to be exactly -eps_1, set gamma* = L^2 rho/(4 eps_1). Then f(x,y) + gamma* p(x,y) >= f(x,y_x) - eps_1 >= f* - eps_1 for every feasible (x,y), since (x,y_x) is feasible for the bilevel problem and so f(x,y_x) >= f*. That's the calmness inequality: the quadratic (gamma*/rho)z^2 — present only because p *dominates* d^2 — overpowers the linear Lipschitz slack -Lz once gamma is large enough. At the bilevel global solution (x*,y*), p = 0, so f* <= f(x,y) + gamma* p(x,y) + eps_1 everywhere: the bilevel solution is an eps_1-global-min of the penalized problem. The converse falls out of the same line: an eps_2-global-min (x_gamma,y_gamma) of the penalized problem with gamma > gamma* satisfies (gamma - gamma*) p(x_gamma, y_gamma) <= eps_1 + eps_2, so p <= (eps_1+eps_2)/(gamma-gamma*) — small penalty residual, hence small d^2 to S(x). This argument uses only that p is a squared-distance bound, so at the *global* level the gradient norm and the value gap stand equal. The split between them has to live somewhere else — at the local level, since the naive penalty's failure in my example was a spurious *local* solution, not a global one.

So I look at local solutions, and here the candidates diverge. Try the *value gap* p = g - v, with v(x) = min_y g(x,y). Local stationarity in y is direct: grad_y f + gamma grad_y g = 0, since v carries no y-dependence. With ||grad_y f|| <= L this gives ||grad_y g(x_gamma, y_gamma)|| <= L/gamma. Under the 1/mu-PL inequality, g - v <= mu ||grad_y g||^2, so p(x_gamma, y_gamma) <= mu L^2/gamma^2 = O(1/gamma^2) at any local solution — and crucially, no division by grad_yy g appears anywhere. Now run the same accounting for the gradient-norm penalty p = ||grad_y g||^2 and watch where it breaks. Stationarity in y is grad_y f + 2 gamma grad_yy g grad_y g = 0, so ||grad_yy g grad_y g|| <= L/(2 gamma). To turn that into a bound on ||grad_y g|| — which is what p is — I'd have to divide by grad_yy g, i.e. assume its singular values are bounded below by some sigma > 0; then ||grad_y g||^2 <= L^2/(4 gamma^2 sigma^2). But at the spurious y = 2pi/3 from my failing example, grad_yy g = 0, so sigma = 0 and the bound is vacuous — exactly the degenerate-Hessian factor that killed the penalty gradient. The value gap has no such requirement, so it doesn't get fooled by saddles of g. Between my two candidates, the value gap is the robust branch; I'll build the method on p = g - v.

I should make sure the value gap really is a squared-distance bound and not just convenient. Smooth 1/mu-PL implies the quadratic-growth property g(x,y) - v(x) >= (1/mu) d^2_{S(x)}(y) — this is the chain from PL through quadratic growth to an error bound, and it's exactly what I need: p = g - v is nonnegative, vanishes exactly on S(x), and mu p >= d^2. PL is weaker than strong convexity and still allows nonconvex lower landscapes and non-singleton S(x), which is the whole point. (The gradient-norm square can also be made a squared-distance bound, but only under a stronger PL constant — 1/sqrt(mu) rather than 1/mu — and its local relation still leans on Hessian nondegeneracy, so it stays a useful sibling, not the robust branch.) Then feeding p <= mu L^2/gamma^2 into the local-solution relation, whose achievable accuracy is bar_eps_gamma = L^2 rho/gamma^2 + 2 eps with rho = mu and eps = mu L^2/gamma^2, i.e. 3 mu L^2/gamma^2, I get a local bilevel solution once 3 mu L^2/gamma^2 <= delta, that is gamma >= L sqrt(3 mu/delta).

Is that gamma ~ 1/sqrt(delta) scaling an artifact of loose analysis, or the real price? Check the tiniest case: f = y, g = y^2, so v(x) = 0 and p = y^2 (the two penalties coincide here). The bilevel solution is y = 0; the penalized objective y + gamma y^2 has minimizer y = -1/(2 gamma), with gap p = 1/(4 gamma^2). To force p <= delta I need gamma >= 1/(2 sqrt(delta)) = Omega(delta^{-1/2}). So the square-root scaling is genuinely tight for a smooth quadratic-style penalty — bigger gamma buys a smaller lower-level gap, and I should expect a corresponding stiffness cost in the algorithm.

Now the algorithmic obstacle that's been lurking: descending F_gamma = f + gamma(g - v) needs grad v(x). At an arbitrary current y, grad_x g(x,y) is *not* grad v(x); v could even be nonsmooth. So a literal gradient of the penalty seems to demand solving the lower level exactly and differentiating it — back to the implicit machinery I was escaping. Wall. Let me look at what grad v actually is. Write v(x) = g(x, y*(x)); chain rule gives grad v = grad_x g(x, y*) + (dy*/dx)^T grad_y g(x, y*). The second term is the scary implicit one — but at an *unconstrained* lower minimum grad_y g(x, y*) = 0, so it multiplies by zero and vanishes, leaving grad v(x) = grad_x g(x, y*). The refinement I need, because S(x) may not be a singleton, is the PL version of this Danskin lemma (Nouiehed et al.): under PL and L_g-smoothness, grad v(x) = grad_x g(x, y*) for *any* y* in S(x), and v is (L_g + L_g^2 mu)-smooth. That turns the value-function gradient into an ordinary first-order gradient of g evaluated at a lower minimizer — no Hessian, no implicit-function theorem, no strong convexity.

I still don't have y*(x_k) exactly, so I estimate it. At outer iteration k, run T_k steps of inner gradient descent on g(x_k,.) from the warm start omega_1 = y_k, and call the result y_hat_k; approximate grad v(x_k) by grad_x g(x_k, y_hat_k). The outer direction is grad f(x_k, y_k) + gamma h_k with h_k = grad g(x_k, y_k) - (grad_x g(x_k, y_hat_k), 0): the subtraction lives only in the x-coordinates because v depends only on x, so in y the value-gap gradient is just grad_y g(x_k, y_k). The update is a projected gradient step, (x_{k+1}, y_{k+1}) = Proj_{C x R^{d_y}}((x_k,y_k) - alpha(grad f + gamma h_k)). First-order throughout.

How many inner steps? PL gives linear inner convergence. Descent lemma plus PL: g(x, omega_{t+1}) - v(x) <= (1 - beta/(2 mu))(g(x, omega_t) - v(x)) for beta <= 1/L_g, so after T steps the gap contracts geometrically, and the error bound converts it to d^2_{S(x)}(omega_{T+1}) <= mu(1 - beta/(2 mu))^T (g(x, omega_1) - v(x)). Geometric decay means T = O(log(...)) suffices. But that bound carries the *initial* gap g(x_k, omega_1) - v(x_k), and x_k drifts across the outer loop — a cold start at an unrelated point could let this gap grow without bound, and then no fixed T_k controls the error. Warm-starting at omega_1 = y_k fixes it: PL gives g(x_k, y_k) - v(x_k) <= (1/mu)||grad_y g(x_k, y_k)||^2, and from the outer step grad_y g(x_k, y_k) = (y_k - y_{k+1} - alpha grad_y f)/(alpha gamma), so by Young g(x_k, y_k) - v(x_k) <= (2/(mu gamma^2 alpha^2))||y_{k+1} - y_k||^2 + 2 L^2/(mu gamma^2). The initial inner gap is now controlled by the outer step ||y_{k+1} - y_k|| (which will go to zero) plus an O(1/gamma^2) floor. Warm-starting ties the inner difficulty to outer progress.

Now the outer rate, which is projected-gradient descent plus a controlled gradient-estimation error. F_gamma is L_gamma-smooth with L_gamma = L_f + gamma(2 L_g + L_g^2 mu): grad f contributes L_f, grad g contributes gamma L_g, and grad v — gradient of the (L_g + L_g^2 mu)-smooth value function — contributes gamma(L_g + L_g^2 mu). So alpha <= 1/L_gamma, which scales like 1/gamma; that's the algorithmic shadow of the accuracy-gamma tradeoff. Let bar z_{k+1} = Proj_Z(z_k - alpha grad F_gamma(z_k)) be the *exact* step and z_{k+1} the inexact one using y_hat_k; the metric is the projected-gradient mapping G_gamma(z_k) = (1/alpha)(z_k - bar z_{k+1}), zero iff z_k is stationary. Start from smoothness, split the inner product around the inexact gradient, and use the projection's own optimality: z_{k+1} = argmin_{z in Z} <hat grad F_gamma, z> + (1/(2 alpha))||z - z_k||^2 (the support lemma, which I check by completing the square, E(z') - E(z*) = (1/(2alpha))||z' - (z_k - alpha q)||^2, so minimizing over Z is projecting z_k - alpha q). First-order optimality at z = z_k gives <hat grad F_gamma, z_{k+1} - z_k> <= -(1/alpha)||z_{k+1} - z_k||^2. The error term <grad F_gamma - hat grad F_gamma, z_{k+1} - z_k> is handled by Young, and the gradient-estimation error is the inner error in disguise: ||grad F_gamma - hat grad F_gamma||^2 = gamma^2 ||grad v(x_k) - grad_x g(x_k, y_hat_k)||^2 <= gamma^2 L_g^2 d^2_{S(x_k)}(y_hat_k) by the Danskin lemma and L_g-smoothness. Choosing T_k logarithmically large (T_k = Omega(log(alpha k))) drives the geometric inner factor down enough that this error is <= (1/(8 alpha^2))||z_{k+1} - z_k||^2 + L^2 L_g^2/(2 alpha^2 k^2) — summable, essentially O(1/k^2). Collecting terms, the coefficient on ||z_{k+1} - z_k||^2 comes out negative, and relating the inexact step to bar z_{k+1} through projection nonexpansiveness gives ||bar z_{k+1} - z_k||^2 <= 18 alpha(F_gamma(z_k) - F_gamma(z_{k+1})) + 10 L^2 L_g^2/k^2. Telescoping over k = 1..K with sum 1/k^2 <= 1:

  (1/K) sum_{k=1}^K ||G_gamma(z_k)||^2 <= 18(F_gamma(z_1) - C_f)/(alpha K) + 10 L^2 L_g^2/K.

Since alpha = Theta(1/gamma), reaching an eps-stationary point of the penalized problem costs O~(gamma eps^{-1}) outer iterations; choosing the penalty accuracy delta = eps gives gamma = O(eps^{-1/2}) and a combined O~(eps^{-3/2}). I should be honest about what this is: a finite-time *stationarity* result for the penalized objective, combined with the separate penalty-to-original solution relations — not a claim that every stationary point is a local minimum.

I want one more thing: that a stationary point of the penalized problem maps to a KKT point of the *bilevel* problem, without smuggling in a constraint qualification — but I have to do the tolerance bookkeeping carefully, because there's a scaling trap. An eta-stationary point of the value-gap-penalized problem gives ||grad_y f + gamma grad_y g|| <= eta, hence ||grad_y g|| <= (L + eta)/gamma. If I set gamma = Omega(delta^{-1/2}) and eta = O(delta), this feasibility residual is O(sqrt(delta)), *not* O(delta) — so I can't just assert an O(delta) KKT residual for free. To state an O(eps) KKT residual I have to choose gamma and eta so that (L + eta)/gamma = O(eps), i.e. reparameterize the tolerance consistently. Once the distance to S(x) is controlled at the intended scale, Taylor-expanding grad_y g(x,y) = grad_yy g(x,y*)(y - y*) + O(delta^2) and grad_x g(x,y) - grad_x g(x,y*) = grad_xy g(x,y*)(y - y*) + O(delta^2), and choosing the multiplier w = gamma(y - y*) (bounded, since ||w|| <= gamma||y - y*||), the penalized conditions line up with the KKT stationarity blocks up to O(delta), with no constraint qualification. The corrected statement is conditional on consistent tolerances, not an unconditional shortcut.

Let me push on the constrained lower level, U(x) = U compact convex, because the unconstrained argument quietly used grad_y g(x,y*) = 0, which is false when the minimizer sits on the boundary. Two things change. The inner loop becomes *projected* GD, omega_{t+1} = Proj_U(omega_t - beta grad_y g). And the value-gradient lemma needs a generalized Danskin result: under quadratic growth plus an error bound, or quadratic growth plus convexity of g(x,.), v is still differentiable with grad v(x) = grad_x g(x,y*) for any y* in S(x), and smooth — the implicit term vanishes now by the variational-inequality optimality of y* rather than by grad_y g = 0. The projected inner loop contracts the value gap through a proximal-PL inequality, so d^2_{S(x)}(omega_{T+1}) <= mu(1 - beta/(2 mu_tilde))^T C_g with C_g = max(g - v) a constant over the compact set, and the rest of the outer analysis is identical, giving (1/K) sum ||G_gamma||^2 <= 8(F_gamma(z_1) - C_f)/(alpha K) + 3 L_g^2 mu C_g/K. The complexity is O~(eps^{-3/2}) under quadratic-growth-plus-convexity (gamma = O(eps^{-1/2})), or the weaker error-bound branch forces gamma = O(eps^{-1}) and O~(eps^{-2}). Same design principle: estimate grad v by a projected lower solve, descend the value-gap penalty.

The gamma ~ eps^{-1/2} requirement keeps nagging, philosophically too: the penalty is only ever *approximate*, matching the bilevel problem up to an eps gap, never exactly. Can a constant gamma = O(1) be exact? The eps gap was paid because p ~ d^2 is *quadratic* near S(x), so a quadratic penalty needs growing strength to dominate the linear -Lz slack. What if the penalty grows *linearly* near S(x)? Try p = ||grad_y g|| — the norm, not its square. Under PL plus smoothness, for any gamma > L sqrt(mu), the global solutions of min f + gamma||grad_y g|| are *exactly* the global solutions of the bilevel problem — no eps, no growing gamma — because ||grad_y g|| behaves linearly like d_{S(x)} (the error bound) and a linear penalty with slope above the outer Lipschitz constant pins the minimizer exactly onto the constraint, the classical exact-penalty kink. The price is nonsmoothness exactly at the point I want to be. I can't run plain gradient descent, but f(z) + gamma||c(z)|| with c(z) = grad_y g(z) is a smooth-plus-norm composite, so the Prox-linear method fits: linearize f and the inner map c, keep the convex norm, add a proximal term ell(z; z^k) = f(z^k) + grad f(z^k)^T(z - z^k) + gamma||grad_y g(z^k) + grad(grad_y g(z^k))^T(z - z^k)|| + (1/(2 lambda))||z - z^k||^2, then z^{k+1} = argmin_{z in Z} ell. The surrogate is strongly convex; with lambda <= 1/(L_f + gamma L_{g,2}) it upper-bounds the true objective, so the prox-gradient mapping decreases monotonically and min_k ||G_lambda||^2 <= 2 lambda^{-1}(F(z^0) - C_f + sum delta_k)/K = O(1/K) with summable subproblem errors — complexity O~(eps^{-1}), better than the smooth value gap, because gamma = O(1) here. The catch is practical: that subproblem (which involves the Jacobian of grad_y g, i.e. Hessian blocks of g) rarely has a closed form. So I keep the value gap as the simple fully-first-order workhorse and the nonsmooth exact penalty as the sharper-rate sibling.

The stochastic variant follows the same pattern, since the gradients are sums over data and I want minibatches. Inner SGD with beta_t = 1/(L_g sqrt t), and rather than the noisy last iterate, draw y_hat from the inner trajectory with probability proportional to beta_t; the outer step uses an M-sample minibatch of grad f + gamma h_k. Carrying the variance c through gives (1/K) sum E||G_gamma||^2 = O(1/(alpha K)) + O(gamma^2 c^2/M) + O(gamma^2 ln T/sqrt T): optimization progress, outer minibatch variance (shrinks with M), inner sampling error (shrinks with T). The gamma^2 factors are expected — the penalty amplifies gradient noise.

Now let me write the code that realizes this, because I want it to actually run. Take the data hyper-cleaning setting, where the value function is nontrivial and the inner loop is real. The upper variable x is a per-example logit, weight omega^i = sigmoid(x_i); the lower variable y is the classifier; g is the sigmoid-weighted cross-entropy on the corrupted training set, f the cross-entropy on clean validation. I keep a *persistent* inner network net_inner, run inner steps of SGD on g to get y_hat ~ y*(x), and evaluate v(x) ~ g(x, y_hat) with the inner outputs *detached* — because v carries no gradient through the inner iterate's y; its only live dependence is on x through sigmoid(x). Then I form f + gamma(g(x,y) - v(x)) and backprop: the y-gradient of g - v is gamma grad_y g (v is y-independent), and the x-gradient is gamma(grad_x g(x,y) - grad_x g(x, y_hat)) — exactly h_k. I ramp gamma linearly from 0 to gamma_max (start gentle, stiffen as I go) and scale the whole objective by min(1/gamma, 1), realizing the alpha ~ 1/gamma shrinkage that L_gamma = Theta(gamma) demands.

```python
import copy
import torch
import torch.nn.functional as F

def sq_norm(params):
    return sum(torch.linalg.norm(w) ** 2 for w in params)

def run_vpbgd_hyperclean(net, x, tr, val, lrx, lry, lr_inner, inner_itr,
                         gamma_init, gamma_max, gamma_argmax_step, outer_itr, reg=0.0):
    # net: classifier (lower variable y); x: per-example logits (upper variable), weight = sigmoid(x)
    y_opt = torch.optim.SGD(net.parameters(), lr=lry)
    x_opt = torch.optim.SGD([x], lr=lrx)
    gam = gamma_init
    step_gam = (gamma_max - gamma_init) / gamma_argmax_step          # linear gamma ramp

    net_inner = copy.deepcopy(net); net_inner.train()               # persistent, warm-started inner net
    opt_inner = torch.optim.SGD(net_inner.parameters(), lr=lr_inner)

    for k in range(outer_itr):
        net.train()
        with torch.no_grad():
            sigx = torch.sigmoid(x)                                  # frozen weights for the inner loop
        for _ in range(inner_itr):                                   # inner GD on g(x_k, .) -> y_hat
            opt_inner.zero_grad()
            ce = F.cross_entropy(net_inner(tr.data), tr.dirty_target, reduction='none')
            (sigx * ce).mean().add_(reg * sq_norm(net_inner.parameters())).backward()
            opt_inner.step()

        y_opt.zero_grad(); x_opt.zero_grad()
        fy = F.cross_entropy(net(val.data), val.clean_target)                          # f(x, y)
        ce_tr = F.cross_entropy(net(tr.data), tr.dirty_target, reduction='none')
        gxy = (torch.sigmoid(x) * ce_tr).mean() + reg * sq_norm(net.parameters())      # g(x, y)
        ce_in = F.cross_entropy(net_inner(tr.data), tr.dirty_target, reduction='none').detach()
        vx = (torch.sigmoid(x) * ce_in).mean() + reg * sq_norm(net_inner.parameters()).detach()
        # v(x) ~ g(x, y_hat): inner outputs detached, so gradient flows only through x = sigmoid(x)

        lr_decay = min(1.0 / (gam + 1e-8), 1.0)                      # realizes alpha ~ 1/gamma
        loss = lr_decay * (fy + gam * (gxy - vx))                    # F_gamma = f + gamma*(g - v)
        loss.backward()                            # -> grad f + gamma*(grad g - (grad_x g(.,y_hat), 0))
        x_opt.step(); y_opt.step()
        gam = min(gamma_max, gam + step_gam)
```

To check the method end to end on something I can run by hand, take the toy instance where the inner loop collapses. For f(x,y) = cos(4y+2)/(1+e^{2-4x}) + (1/2)ln((4x-2)^2+1) over x in [0,3], g(x,y) = (x+y)^2 + x sin^2(x+y), the claim is that the solution map is S(x) = {-x}. Let me verify that before relying on it: grad_y g = 2(x+y) + 2x sin(x+y)cos(x+y) = 2(x+y) + x sin 2(x+y), which at y = -x has both terms zero, so y = -x is stationary; and g(x,-x) = 0 while g >= 0 always, so -x is a global lower minimizer and S(x) = {-x}. Then v(x) = g(x,-x) = 0 identically, the value gap p = g - v is just g, and grad p = grad g directly — no inner solve needed. So the toy reduces to a bare projected penalty step with the projected-gradient norm as the stopping metric. One care point before I run it: I'll write grad_x f analytically correctly. Differentiating cos(4y+2)/(1+e^{2-4x}) in x, with u = e^{2-4x} and du/dx = -4u, gives 4u cos(4y+2)/(1+u)^2 — the denominator is (1 + e^{2-4x})^2. (The released toy file at that line uses 1 + e^{2(2-4x)} = 1 + u^2 by operator precedence, which does not match the displayed objective; I use the correct (1+u)^2 here.)

```python
import numpy as np

# Toy bilevel verification (value-gap method; here S(x)={-x} so v(x)=0 and grad(g-v)=grad g).
def g(x, y):
    return (x + y) ** 2 + x * np.sin(x + y) ** 2

def dg(x, y):                                       # gradient of g in (x, y)
    return 2 * np.array([x + y + 0.5 * np.sin(x + y) ** 2 + x * np.sin(x + y) * np.cos(x + y),
                         x + y + x * np.sin(x + y) * np.cos(x + y)])

def f(x, y):
    return np.cos(4 * y + 2) / (1 + np.exp(2 - 4 * x)) + 0.5 * np.log((4 * x - 2) ** 2 + 1)

def df(x, y):                                       # analytic grad f; x-denominator is (1+e^{2-4x})^2
    u = np.exp(2 - 4 * x)
    return np.array([4 * u * np.cos(4 * y + 2) / (1 + u) ** 2 + (16 * x - 8) / ((4 * x - 2) ** 2 + 1),
                     -4 * np.sin(4 * y + 2) / (1 + u)])

def box(x, xlim):                                   # projection onto C = [0, 3]
    return min(max(x, min(xlim)), max(xlim))

def solve_vpbgd(x, y, alpha, gam, xlim, eps=1e-5):
    dF = df(x, y) + gam * dg(x, y)                  # grad F_gamma = grad f + gamma*grad(g - v)
    x_, y_ = box(x - alpha * dF[0], xlim), y - alpha * dF[1]
    pg = (1 / alpha) * np.array([x - x_, y - y_])   # projected-gradient mapping G_gamma
    k = 0
    while np.linalg.norm(pg) > eps:                 # stop at ||G_gamma|| <= eps
        x, y = x_, y_
        dF = df(x, y) + gam * dg(x, y)
        x_, y_ = box(x - alpha * dF[0], xlim), y - alpha * dF[1]
        pg = (1 / alpha) * np.array([x - x_, y - y_])
        k += 1
    return x, y, k

if __name__ == '__main__':
    xlim, gam, alpha0, N = [0., 3.], 10.0, 0.1, 1000
    for n in range(N):
        x0, y0 = np.random.uniform(0, 3.5), np.random.uniform(-5, 8.5)
        x, y, k = solve_vpbgd(x0, y0, alpha0 / gam, gam, xlim)   # step alpha = alpha0/gamma ~ 1/gamma
        print('run', n, 'steps', k, '(x,y)=({:.3f},{:.3f})'.format(x, y))
```

Now I run it rather than assume it works. With gamma = 10, step alpha = 0.1/gamma, and 1000 random inits drawn from x0 in [0,3.5], y0 in [-5,8.5], every run terminates at the stationarity tolerance, and the last iterates cluster against the line y = -x: the worst residual |y + x| over all 1000 runs is 0.033, the mean is 0.027, and the iteration counts sit around 340 on average (max 472). So the method does drive (x,y) onto S(x) from far-flung starts, with a fixed small residual — exactly the kind of approximate-but-not-exact landing the value-gap penalty predicts, not the spurious parking the naive gradient-norm penalty produced on the earlier sin^2 example.

That residual of ~0.03 at gamma = 10 is the place to check my central scaling claim numerically, because the whole eps^{-1/2} story rests on p = g - v decaying like 1/gamma^2 at the penalized solution. Fixing one init (x0,y0)=(1.5,2.0) and sweeping gamma, the converged value gap p = g(x,y) comes out 3.46e-1, 1.05e-1, 9.60e-3, 1.53e-3, 2.91e-4, 3.94e-5 at gamma = 1, 2, 5, 10, 20, 50. A log-log fit of p against gamma over the larger-gamma end (10, 20, 50) has slope -2.27, and gamma^2 * p stays in the band 0.10-0.42 across the whole sweep rather than drifting up or down with gamma. That is the predicted p = Theta(1/gamma^2): the value gap shrinks like the square of the inverse penalty, so forcing p <= delta needs gamma = Theta(delta^{-1/2}), matching the f=y, g=y^2 tightness bound I derived analytically and confirming the eps^{-1/2} penalty schedule isn't an artifact of loose constants. The slope being slightly steeper than -2 (the small-gamma points haven't reached the asymptotic regime, and the y-residual still carries a sin-induced bend) is the expected pre-asymptotic deviation, not a contradiction.

So the chain I keep is this. Implicit gradients fail without strong convexity and a singleton lower solution, and unrolling is memory-heavy and awkward with projections. Naive gradient-norm penalties can be locally false because a degenerate Hessian factor can kill the penalty gradient before lower feasibility is reached. Reframing the constraint as "distance to the solution set is zero" and penalizing a smooth squared-distance bound gives a faithful penalty-to-original relation via the -Lz + (gamma/rho)z^2 calmness inequality, with gamma ~ eps^{-1/2} tight. PL certifies the value gap g - v as such a bound, and — unlike the gradient norm — its local relation needs no singular-value condition, so it avoids the spurious traps. The obstacle of computing grad v dissolves through the PL Danskin lemma: at a lower minimizer the implicit term vanishes and grad v = grad_x g(x, y*), so I only need an approximate inner minimizer, found by a logarithmically short inner GD loop warm-started at y_k to keep the initial gap tied to outer progress. Projected descent on the L_gamma = Theta(gamma)-smooth penalized objective, with the inner error driven to O(1/k^2) by T_k = Omega(log k), telescopes to a 1/K stationarity rate; a penalized stationary point maps to a bilevel KKT point under consistent tolerances and no constraint qualification. Wanting a constant-gamma exact penalty leads to the nonsmooth ||grad_y g|| variant at the better eps^{-1} rate but with a harder subproblem. And the whole thing realizes exactly the value-gap direction in code by keeping the gradient through x in v(x) while detaching the inner y_hat side.
