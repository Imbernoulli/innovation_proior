I start with the thing that ordinary volume says I am not allowed to do. If I cut a ball into finitely many decent pieces, move each piece rigidly, and put the pieces back together, the volume must be unchanged. That argument is too quick only because it hides a hypothesis: each piece has to be in the domain of the volume function. If I insist on Lebesgue-measurable pieces, finite additivity and invariance under rotations and translations end the discussion. So the only place the surprise can live is in arbitrary subsets, where choice can produce sets that volume is not allowed to measure.

I should keep the physical picture out of the proof. This is not a recombination of matter. There are no shaped fragments with boundaries that can be machined or moved in space. I am asking whether the set-theoretic relation "finite partition plus congruent motion" can behave differently once the pieces are allowed to be completely nonmeasurable.

What algebraic shape could force that behavior? If a group already contains a duplication pattern inside itself, an action of that group might carry the pattern out onto the set it acts on. The cleanest candidate is the free group on two generators, because there every nonidentity element is a unique reduced word and I can sort the elements by their structure without ambiguity. Let `F_2 = <a,b>`. Sort the reduced words by first letter into `W(a)`, `W(a^{-1})`, `W(b)`, `W(b^{-1})`, with the identity `e` standing apart. Left multiplication by `a` acts on a word: if the word starts with `a^{-1}` it cancels that letter, otherwise it prefixes an `a`. So `a` maps `W(a^{-1})` onto everything that does *not* start with `a^{-1}` — which is to say `a\,W(a^{-1}) = W(a) \cup W(b) \cup W(b^{-1}) \cup \{e\}`. Adding back `W(a)` I would seem to get all of `F_2`. But I have to be careful about the loose ends `e` and the positive powers `a, a^2, \ldots`: they start with `a`, so they sit in `W(a)`, yet the shift wants them too.

Let me actually pin down a clean partition and then check it numerically rather than trust the bookkeeping. Fold the dangling words into the `a^{-1}` block: define `W'(a^{-1}) = W(a^{-1}) \cup \{e, a, a^2, \ldots\}` and remove those positive `a`-powers from `W(a)` to get `W'(a)`. Now I claim the four sets `W'(a)`, `W'(a^{-1})`, `W(b)`, `W(b^{-1})` partition `F_2`, and that

`W'(a) \cup a\,W'(a^{-1}) = F_2`,  `W(b) \cup b\,W(b^{-1}) = F_2`.

I generated every reduced word up to length 7, formed these four sets, and tested it. The four sets are pairwise disjoint and their union is all of the words — a genuine partition. For the covering identities, restricting comparison to words of length at most 6 (so that the length-increasing shift cannot push a witness out past the horizon I enumerated), `W'(a) \cup a\,W'(a^{-1})` contains every such word, and likewise `W(b) \cup b\,W(b^{-1})` contains every such word. Nothing is missing in either case. So two disjoint blocks of `F_2`, namely `P_a = W'(a)` (together with its `a^{-1}` partner moved by `a`) and `P_b = W(b)` (with its partner moved by `b`), each reconstruct the whole group after a single generator-shift. The group really does contain two disjoint copies of itself.

If I tried the same idea with an amenable group it would collapse. A finitely additive invariant probability measure would assign the whole set mass `1`; two disjoint pieces that each move to cover the whole set would force `1 = 2`. So whatever group I push out onto the sphere has to fail to carry such a mean, and the free group on two generators is exactly the smallest place where that failure is visible.

Now geometry. I want this group to act by rotations of the sphere, because rotations are the congruences I am allowed. Two rotations only generate a free group if no nontrivial reduced word in them equals the identity rotation — every accidental relation would let a paradoxical block fold back and destroy the duplication. So I cannot just assert freeness; I should check it on a concrete pair. Take rotations about two perpendicular axes through an angle with `cos = 3/5`, `sin = 4/5`:

`a = [[1,0,0],[0,3/5,-4/5],[0,4/5,3/5]]`,  `b = [[3/5,-4/5,0],[4/5,3/5,0],[0,0,1]]`.

The denominators are what make this work: every entry is an integer over `5`, and multiplying reduced words keeps producing vectors of the form (integer vector)/`5^k` where the length `k` of the word controls the power, and the numerator is never divisible by `5` in the coordinate that the leading letter touches. That non-divisibility is exactly what prevents a word from returning to the identity, which has integer entries. Rather than carry the full induction by hand, I enumerated all `1456` reduced words of length up to `6` in `a, a^{-1}, b, b^{-1}` with exact rational arithmetic and multiplied out each one. Not a single one came out equal to the identity matrix. That is consistent with `<a,b>` being free of rank two; I would want the denominator induction to close it for all lengths, but the explicit check rules out any short relation, which is where accidental coincidences would most plausibly hide.

Let `F = <a,b>` be such a free subgroup of `SO(3)`. It acts on `S^2`. There is an annoyance: a nonidentity rotation fixes the two endpoints of its axis, so the action is not free everywhere. I remove the bad points. Let

`D = {x in S^2 : g x = x for some g in F, g != e}`.

The group `F` is countable, and each nonidentity rotation fixes exactly two points on the sphere, so `D` is a countable union of two-point sets, hence countable. Away from `D`, no nonidentity element fixes a point. That means if `g m = h m` for `m in S^2 \ D`, then `h^{-1} g m = m`, hence `h^{-1} g = e`, so `g = h`. On `S^2 \ D`, each orbit is a clean copy of the free group: distinct group elements move a representative to distinct points.

The next step is where ordinary constructive geometry runs out. The orbits of `F` partition `S^2 \ D`, and I need one seed point from each orbit. There is no canonical rule for selecting all of them — the orbits are an uncountable family with no order or distinguished member. By the axiom of choice, choose a set `M subset S^2 \ D` containing exactly one point from each orbit. Then every point of `S^2 \ D` has a unique form `g m` with `g in F` and `m in M`.

Now the algebraic partition of `F_2` becomes a geometric one. For `Q subset F`, define

`Q M = {q m : q in Q, m in M}`.

Because the representation `q m` is unique, disjoint subsets of `F` give disjoint subsets of the sphere outside `D`, and `(Q_1 \cup Q_2) M = Q_1 M \cup Q_2 M`. So the partition of `F` into `W'(a), W'(a^{-1}), W(b), W(b^{-1})` transports to a partition of `S^2 \ D` into the corresponding `M`-blocks, and the generator-shifts transport as actual rotations of the sphere. Concretely: applying the rotation `a` to the block `W'(a^{-1}) M` and leaving `W'(a) M` in place covers `(W'(a) \cup a\,W'(a^{-1})) M = F M = S^2 \ D`. The same computed covering identity I checked on words now says, after multiplying by `M`, that these two rotated sphere-pieces reassemble `S^2 \ D`. So `S^2 \ D` is equidecomposable with the union of the `a`-block alone — a copy of itself — and symmetrically with the union of the `b`-block alone. Two disjoint subsets of `S^2 \ D`, each rotation-equidecomposable with all of `S^2 \ D`.

I still have the countable set `D` sitting outside this. A countable subset of the sphere can be absorbed by a rotation trick, but I should make sure the trick is available and not just assert it. Choose an axis through no point of `D`, and look for a rotation `rho` around that axis with `D, rho D, rho^2 D, \ldots` pairwise disjoint. When can two of them collide? If `rho^n d = rho^m d'` for points `d, d' in D` and `n > m`, then `rho^{n-m} d = d'`, i.e. rotation by `(n-m)\theta` carries one specified point of `D` to another. For a fixed ordered pair `(d, d')` and a fixed nonzero integer `k = n-m`, the angle `k\theta` is forced (the two points must share a latitude about the axis, and then `k\theta` is determined modulo `2\pi`), so there are at most `|k|` offending values of `\theta`. The set of all bad angles is therefore indexed by triples `(d, d', k)`, a countable-times-countable-times-countable family, each contributing finitely many angles. A countable union of finite sets is countable, while `[0, 2\pi)` is uncountable — so a good `\theta` exists. Fix such a `rho`. Then `E = D \cup rho D \cup rho^2 D \cup \cdots` satisfies `rho E = E \setminus D`, so rotating `E` by `rho` and leaving `S^2 \setminus E` fixed turns `S^2` into `S^2 \setminus D`. Hence `S^2` is rotation-equidecomposable with `S^2 \ D`.

Combining these, one part of the sphere can swallow the exceptional set and the other can avoid it. Set

`S_1 = P_a M \cup D`,  `S_2 = P_b M`,

where `P_a M` and `P_b M` are the two disjoint copies built above. They are disjoint and cover `S^2`. The first is equidecomposable with `(S^2 \ D) \cup D = S^2`, because `P_a M` rebuilds `S^2 \ D` and `D` rides along untouched. The second rebuilds `S^2 \ D`, and the absorption I just verified makes `S^2 \ D` equidecomposable with `S^2`. So the sphere splits into two subsets, each equidecomposable with the entire sphere.

To pass from the sphere to the ball, extend radially. Every nonzero point of the unit ball is uniquely `r x` with `0 < r <= 1` and `x in S^2`. For `E subset S^2`, define the cone

`C(E) = {r x : 0 < r <= 1, x in E}`.

Rotations commute with radial scaling — `r(g x) = g(r x)` since `g` is linear — so a rotation that moves `E` to `E'` moves `C(E)` to `C(E')` by the same rotation, and a finite rotational equidecomposition of `E` becomes a finite rotational equidecomposition of `C(E)` piece by piece. The two cones `C(S_1)` and `C(S_2)` partition `B^3 \ {0}`, and each is equidecomposable with `B^3 \ {0}`. The missing center is one point; the ball is equidecomposable with the punctured ball by the same countable-shift idea (rotate a small circle through the center so that the orbit of the center under a fixed-angle rotation absorbs the puncture). Thus `B^3` is equidecomposable with `B^3 \ {0}`, and the full ball has a finite paradoxical decomposition: it can be partitioned into finitely many arbitrary subsets that, after rigid motions, form two balls congruent to the original.

The measure conclusion is now forced, and this is the one place where I can just compute the contradiction outright. Suppose there were a finitely additive volume `mu` defined on every subset of the ball, invariant under rigid motions, and agreeing with ordinary volume on balls. Let the original ball have volume `V > 0`. Each of the two disjoint parts `B_1, B_2` is equidecomposable with the whole ball; invariance sends every moved piece to a set of equal `mu`-measure, and finite additivity sums them, so `mu(B_1) = mu(B^3) = V` and likewise `mu(B_2) = V`. But `B_1 \sqcup B_2 = B^3`, so finite additivity also gives

`mu(B^3) = mu(B_1) + mu(B_2) = V + V = 2V`,

and `V = 2V` with `V > 0` is false. So no such `mu` exists, and at least some of the pieces in the decomposition must be nonmeasurable. The lesson is not that volume has been physically doubled. It is that ordinary volume cannot be extended to all subsets while keeping finite additivity and rotation invariance, precisely because the rotation group of the sphere is roomy enough to contain a free group, and a free group already duplicates itself.
