Let me start from what actually hurts. I have a van with a camera on it, and I want it to follow a road by itself. The standard way people do this is to hand-build a vision pipeline — write operators that find the road edges, fit a road model, run a control law off the geometry — and it works, sort of, on the exact road and lighting it was tuned for, and then falls over on the next road type, and getting it to that point took the vision group months. The processing is frozen at design time; the world isn't. So I don't want to keep hand-specifying what a road looks like. I want the *data* to tell the system what matters.

And there's a piece of luck sitting right in front of me. When a person drives this van, the steering wheel is, at every instant, the answer: for this sensor image, here is the command a competent controller should produce. That's a labeled example, delivered for free, continuously, in exactly the form a learner wants. So the move is almost forced: don't program the controller, *learn* it. Feed the current image in, train the network to output the steering the human just used, and let backprop figure out for itself which features of the image matter. Rumelhart, Hinton and Williams showed that's exactly what backprop does — the hidden units come to represent the regularities of the task, and the same machinery that learned to read handwritten digits and phonemes can learn this map. I've reduced a control problem to plain supervised function approximation. Input: the image. Target: the human's steering. Loss: how far the network's command is from the human's. That's the whole idea, and it's clean.

Now I have to actually shape the network and, more importantly, the readout, because steering isn't a class label, it's a continuous quantity and I want fine control. The cheap thing would be one linear output unit emitting a steering angle. But a single regressed scalar is twitchy and gives me no graceful way to express "I'm fairly sure it's a gentle left but it could be straight." Let me instead lay a whole bank of output units along the steering-curvature axis — far left at one end, straight ahead in the middle, far right at the other — and represent the command as a *bump* of activation over that bank. To drive, I read the command back as the location of the bump, its center of mass, not just the single most-active unit. That buys me resolution finer than the unit spacing: the peak can sit between two units. And for the *target* I don't want a one-hot spike on the single correct unit, because then every near-miss is scored as totally wrong and the gradients are brutal and uninformative. I want a soft target — a Gaussian hill centered on the correct curvature, tapering to its neighbors — so a unit one or two away from the true direction is told "you should be partly on," which gives graded credit and much better-behaved gradients than all-or-nothing. So: a small feedforward net, a low-resolution retina in, a hidden layer, and a population-coded steering output trained toward a Gaussian hill and read out by center of mass. Backprop trains it.

Before I had the real sensor I could only train on synthetic road images from a road generator — synthesize roads at many orientations and widths and lightings, label each with the geometrically correct steering, train on a big pile of those. That works under limited conditions, but it has an annoying cost: writing a generator rich enough to cover a *new* situation is itself a big engineering job, and synthetic roads never quite match the statistics of what the real camera sees. The whole point of learning was to stop hand-specifying the world, and a hand-written simulator quietly smuggles the hand-specification back in. The real teaching signal is right there on the steering wheel. So let me try the most direct thing: throw out the simulator and train *on the fly* — while the person drives, grab the live camera frame, pair it with their current steering, do a backprop step, repeat. Train on the real sensor, on the real road, with essentially no human effort beyond driving. This should also let the system adapt quickly to whatever situation it's dropped into.

Let me run that loop in my head before I trust it, because backprop has two failure modes I know about and I want to see whether this setup walks into either. It does. Walk into the first: the person is a good driver, so they keep the van near the center of the lane. Every single training image is therefore an on-center view of the road. The network learns, beautifully, to map "road centered ahead" to "go straight-ish." Then I let it drive. It's not perfect, so it drifts a little off center. Now it's looking at a view it has *never seen in training* — the road off to one side — and a supervised network on an input unlike anything in its training set has no constrained output; it does something arbitrary, drifts further, and there's no recovery because I never showed it a single example of being off-center and steering back. The human never produced those examples, because the human never got off-center. The training distribution is the distribution the *teacher* visits; the test distribution is the distribution the *learner* visits, and those are not the same the moment the learner makes its first small mistake. That's the wall. Pure on-the-fly cloning of a competent driver teaches the network to handle exactly the situations it won't be in.

The obvious patch: have the driver deliberately swerve toward the road edge and then steer back, so the network sees off-center views paired with recovery commands. Let me think about whether that actually works before I commit to it. It doesn't, for two reasons. First, half of each swerve is the *wrong* thing to imitate — the part where the driver is steering the van *off* the road. If I leave learning on during that, the network dutifully learns to drive off the road too. So I'd have to toggle learning *off* while the human heads for the ditch and *on* while they recover — manual intervention on every swerve, exactly the hand-holding I was trying to escape. Second, to generalize I'd need recoveries demonstrated in a great many circumstances — every road type, every curvature, every offset — which is slow to collect, and deliberately steering a van off real roads in traffic to collect it is dangerous. So swerving is out.

Now the second failure mode, which the on-the-fly loop also walks into. The examples arrive as a *temporal stream*, and backprop has no memory beyond the weights. Suppose at the end of a session the person drives a long straight stretch. The network now gets a long run of near-identical "straight road, go straight" frames, and with nothing to balance them it overlearns the recent input — the weights drift to fit the straights and it *forgets* how to take a curve, settling into "always steer straight." Sustained lack of diversity in the stream poisons it. Same disease as the first wall, really: the training set the backprop update sees is not representative of the full task.

Both walls are the same disease: I'm feeding backprop a training set that's missing pieces of the real task — missing off-center views in the first, missing variety in the second. I can't fix that by collecting more real frames (the human won't go off-center, and a monotonous stretch is monotonous no matter how many frames I grab). I have to *manufacture* the missing examples. And here's the thing I have that I haven't used: I know the geometry. The camera's position and orientation relative to the ground are known, and the ground is, locally, flat. Under a planar-ground assumption, perspective projection is a fixed, invertible map between a point on the ground and the pixel it lands on. So if I want to know what the camera *would have seen* had the van been sitting a little to the left, or rotated a few degrees, I don't have to drive there — I can compute it. Take the one real image, decide on a shifted/rotated virtual vehicle pose, work out which ground points are visible from that virtual pose, and for each, project it onto the ground and back-project into the *original* image to find the pixel to copy. The transformed image is just a resampling of the original.

That's exactly the off-center views I couldn't get from the human. From one real frame I synthesize a fistful of frames in which the van appears shifted left or right and rotated — including the off-center poses the network drifts into and must recover from. And the *same* trick fixes the second wall, because now even a monotonous straight stretch yields a spread of apparent vehicle placements per frame, so the training set stays varied. One mechanism, both walls. And it's nearly free: under the planar assumption the pixel-to-pixel sampling pattern for a given shift-and-rotation is *constant* — it doesn't depend on the image content — so I can precompute the sampling pattern for each transformation and fold it straight into the image-reduction step I already run to get the low-res retina. Making a transformed low-res image costs no more than reducing the image normally.

There's a subtlety the moment I shift, though. If I shift the apparent vehicle to the right, I'm asking to see ground that's a bit further left than the camera actually saw — and on the side I shifted away from, there's a strip of ground at the edge that simply wasn't in the original image. Those pixels have no source. I have to fill them in. The naive fill: for a missing pixel, find the nearest ground point that *is* visible in the original and copy from there. Let me picture what that does to a road. The lane line running down the side of the road is a near-vertical feature; nearest-point fill copies sideways, along rows, so the line gets *smeared* horizontally where it hits the edge of the original image. And here's why that's poison rather than just ugly: the length of that smear is correlated with how far I shifted, which is correlated with the correct steering — so the network, being a good feature-learner, happily learns to *read the smear* as its cue for how hard to steer. Then I put it in the van, where there's no smear because nothing was extrapolated, and it's lost. I'd have trained it on an artifact of my own data generation. So nearest-point fill is wrong.

I need to fill the missing strip in a way that matches how the scene really continues. What do I actually know about roads? Their interesting features — the edges, the painted lane lines — run *parallel to the road*, hence parallel to the vehicle's heading. So to fill a missing pixel, I shouldn't grab the nearest visible ground point; I should walk along the line through that pixel that runs *parallel to the vehicle's original heading* — toward the vanishing point — and sample the visible ground point on that line. That extends a lane line smoothly along its true direction instead of smearing it sideways, so the transformed image looks like a road really would from the shifted pose, and there's no artifact for the network to cheat on.

I've fixed the *image* of each synthetic example, but the example isn't done — it needs a *label*, the correct steering for that virtual pose, and the human's steering was the right answer for the *original* pose, not the shifted one. If I keep the human's command unchanged I'll teach the network to drive as if it were centered when it's actually off to the side, which is backwards. I need a model of how the correct steering *changes* when I move the vehicle. I'll use the simplest reasonable one: pure pursuit. The idea is that the right thing to do, from wherever you are, is to aim at a target point a fixed distance ahead — the road center, a lookahead `l` down the road — and steer the single circular arc that gets you there. So for each transformed pose I compute the arc to the same target the human was implicitly aiming at, and *that* arc is the label.

Let me actually work the geometry so the label is right, not hand-wave it. Put the original vehicle at A and the shifted-and-rotated virtual vehicle at B, with horizontal shift `s` and rotation `theta` between them. The human at A was steering some arc; call its radius `r_p` (the radius of the person's steering). The target T is the point that person's arc reaches a lookahead `l` ahead, sitting (by assumption) at the road center. First, how far off the straight-ahead direction is T as seen from A? If the vehicle at A drove straight for distance `l`, it would miss T by a lateral amount `d_p`. On the person's arc of radius `r_p`, the chord of length `l` subtends a sagitta — the perpendicular gap between the straight-ahead line and the arc at lookahead `l` — and that gap is `d_p = r_p - sqrt(r_p^2 - l^2)` by Pythagoras on the right triangle with hypotenuse `r_p`, one leg `l`, other leg `sqrt(r_p^2 - l^2)`. Good, that's how far T is from A's straight-ahead line.

Now move to B. B is shifted by `s` and rotated by `theta` relative to A. I want `d`, the lateral distance from T to B's straight-ahead line — the same kind of quantity as `d_p` but measured from the virtual pose. The shift `s` moves the line sideways by `s`; the rotation `theta` tilts B's straight-ahead line, so the lateral miss at lookahead `l` picks up an extra `l*tan(theta)`; and then the whole thing is measured along B's heading, which is tilted by `theta`, contributing a `cos(theta)` factor. Putting the three together, `d = cos(theta) * (d_p + s + l*tan(theta))`. Then the steering arc from B that reaches T is, by the same sagitta relation read the other way — chord `l`, lateral miss `d`, radius `r` — given by `d = r - sqrt(r^2 - l^2)`, which I solve for `r`: rearrange to `sqrt(r^2 - l^2) = r - d`, square to `r^2 - l^2 = r^2 - 2 r d + d^2`, the `r^2` cancels, and `r = (l^2 + d^2) / (2 d)`. That's the radius of the arc the network should be told to steer from the virtual pose B. So the label transform is: from the person's steering get `r_p`, compute `d_p`, then `d` from the shift and rotation, then `r`, and encode the curvature `1/r` as the Gaussian target hill.

Two things make me trust pure pursuit here rather than something fancier. It's *situation-independent* — it doesn't care whether this is a one-lane dirt road or a highway, it's pure geometry of "aim at a point ahead," which is exactly the kind of weak, general model I want so the same transform works everywhere. And it actually matches how people steer: measured human responses to a lateral displacement of about a meter at highway speed come out to steering radii in the several-hundred-to-thousand-meter range, and pure pursuit with the lookahead I'd pick lands inside that range. The only free knob is `l`, the lookahead distance, and I'll set it as the distance the vehicle travels in a couple of seconds — short enough to be responsive, long enough to be stable — which over the whole 5-to-55-mph speed range gives accurate control. One guard: when I roll the dice on a shift and rotation, I first compute the resulting label, and if it demands a turn sharper than the sharpest the output bank can represent (around a 20 m radius), I throw that transform out and redraw. No sense spending the network's limited capacity learning to handle a wildly off-road, hard-over pose it'll essentially never be in; better to keep the synthetic examples in the plausible range.

So now each real frame becomes a handful of synthetic training examples — I'll generate about fourteen of them, drawing the shift uniformly from roughly minus to plus six tenths of a meter and the rotation from about minus six to plus six degrees, each with its pure-pursuit-corrected label, plus the one real (image, steering) pair. A maximum shift around six tenths of a meter is calibrated to the camera's field of view so the road moves about a third of the way across the bottom of the retina at the extreme — enough to teach recovery, not so much that the road leaves the frame.

That fistful per frame already attacks the forgetting wall, but let me push on it, because a long monotonous stretch can still dominate if I only ever train on the most recent frame's batch. I'll keep a buffer of past training patterns — a couple hundred of them — and on each cycle add the new fifteen and drop some old ones, then run a backprop pass over the whole buffer. The buffer is a running, diverse training set instead of a sliding window of the present. But *which* patterns do I drop? This decision turns out to matter, so let me reason through the options by their failure modes.

Drop the oldest first? Then the buffer is a recent-history window, and during, say, a long right turn it fills up entirely with right-turn patterns — diversity gone, the very disease I'm fighting. Drop a random old one? Better, probability spreads it out, but a long monotonous stretch still biases the buffer toward whatever's being driven. Drop the pattern the network has the *lowest* error on — keep the hard ones, toss the easy ones? Tempting, but it has a nasty trap: the human occasionally has a lapse and steers slightly wrong for a moment, and that mislabeled pattern is one the network can *never* fit well, so it always has high error, so it never gets dropped — it festers in the buffer indefinitely, and the only way it would ever leave is if the network actually learned to reproduce the human's mistake, which is the last thing I want. I could add a small random drop probability on top so even a stuck high-error pattern eventually leaves, and that does fix the festering. But there's a cleaner idea that hits the real target directly.

What I actually want is for the buffer never to develop a *steering bias* — never to quietly fill with one turn direction and teach the network to favor it. The quantity I average has to be a signed steering scalar, not a raw radius: the sign is the difference between left and right, and straight is naturally zero steering rather than an infinite radius. So make that the replacement rule outright: insert the new pattern, then remove whichever pattern from the enlarged buffer leaves the *mean* signed steering closest to straight ahead. If the buffer is leaning right and a left-turn frame has just arrived, removing a right-turn pattern moves the average back toward straight; if the buffer is already balanced and a new pattern would tilt it, removing a pattern with a similar steering demand keeps it level. This bakes a real piece of prior knowledge into the training set: over any long run, left and right turns occur about equally often, so a controller should have no standing turn bias. By actively holding the buffer's mean signed steering at straight, the network can never learn to consistently favor one direction. That's the replacement scheme.

Let me assemble the whole cycle. While the person drives: digitize the current frame, reduce it to the retina; generate the fourteen transformed images by precomputed resampling, each with its pure-pursuit label, redrawing any whose label is too sharp; add those fourteen plus the one real pair to the buffer of two hundred using the mean-signed-steering replacement rule; then do one forward and one backward backprop pass over all two hundred patterns, learning rate around 0.01 and momentum around 0.8, which are the values that converge stably here without overshooting; repeat. Each cycle takes a couple of seconds of compute, and about a hundred cycles suffice to learn a new road, so the whole thing is roughly four or five minutes of someone just driving normally. The person drives at the speed the network will be tested at.

Let me write the training procedure I actually run; the network and backprop already exist:

```python
import numpy as np


def pure_pursuit_radius(r_person, shift_s, rot_theta, lookahead_l):
    """Correct steering for a virtual vehicle pose shifted by s and rotated by theta.
    Aim at the same target T (road center, lookahead l ahead) the person aimed at,
    and return the radius of the arc from the new pose that reaches T."""
    # sagitta of the person's arc at lookahead l: how far T sits off A's straight-ahead line
    d_p = r_person - np.sqrt(r_person ** 2 - lookahead_l ** 2)
    # lateral miss of T from the shifted+rotated pose B's straight-ahead line
    d = np.cos(rot_theta) * (d_p + shift_s + lookahead_l * np.tan(rot_theta))
    # invert d = r - sqrt(r^2 - l^2)  ->  r = (l^2 + d^2) / (2 d)
    return (lookahead_l ** 2 + d ** 2) / (2.0 * d)


def transform_image(retina, shift_s, rot_theta):
    """Resample the retina to the view from a vehicle shifted by s and rotated by theta,
    under a planar-ground assumption. The pixel-sampling pattern for a given (s, theta)
    is constant (precomputed), so this is as cheap as plain image reduction. Missing
    edge pixels are extrapolated ALONG the line parallel to the vehicle heading (toward
    the vanishing point), not from the nearest pixel, so road features (edges, lane
    lines) extend along their true direction instead of smearing across rows."""
    sampling = precomputed_sampling_pattern(shift_s, rot_theta)  # parallel-to-heading fill
    return resample(retina, sampling)


def train_while_human_drives(network, sensor_stream, human_steering_stream,

                             n_transforms=14, buffer_size=200,
                             max_shift=0.6, max_rot_deg=6.0, lookahead_l=...):
    buffer = []  # patterns: (retina, target_profile, signed_steering_scalar)
    sharpest_radius = 20.0  # sharpest turn the output bank can represent

    for raw_image, human_steering in zip(sensor_stream, human_steering_stream):
        retina = reduce_to_retina(raw_image)
        r_person = radius_of(human_steering)

        # 1) the one real demonstration
        new_patterns = [
            (retina, encode_steering_target(human_steering),
             signed_steering_scalar(human_steering))
        ]

        # 2) fourteen synthetic poses with pure-pursuit-corrected labels
        while len(new_patterns) < 1 + n_transforms:
            s = np.random.uniform(-max_shift, max_shift)
            theta = np.radians(np.random.uniform(-max_rot_deg, max_rot_deg))
            r = pure_pursuit_radius(r_person, s, theta, lookahead_l)
            if abs(r) < sharpest_radius:        # too sharp to represent -> redraw
                continue
            t_img = transform_image(retina, s, theta)
            t_steering = steer_from_radius(r)
            new_patterns.append((t_img, encode_steering_target(t_steering),
                                 signed_steering_scalar(t_steering)))

        # 3) buffer with mean-signed-steering-toward-straight replacement
        for pat in new_patterns:
            buffer.append(pat)
            if len(buffer) > buffer_size:

                # buffer's mean signed steering closest to straight ahead.
                signed_steering_sum = sum(p[2] for p in buffer)
                victim = min(range(len(buffer)),
                             key=lambda i: abs((signed_steering_sum - buffer[i][2]) / (len(buffer) - 1)))

        # 4) one forward + backward backprop pass over the whole buffer
        for retina_i, target_i, _ in buffer:
```

Let me trace the causal chain back. I started wanting a road follower that adapts instead of being hand-tuned per road, and noticed the steering wheel hands me labeled examples for free, so I reduced control to supervised learning: image in, human steering out, trained by backprop, with a population-coded Gaussian-hill output read by center of mass for smooth fine steering. Training the most direct way — on live frames as the person drives — walked into two walls, both the same disease of an unrepresentative training set: the human only ever shows centered views, so the network never learns to recover from the off-center poses it drifts into; and the temporal stream's monotonous stretches make it overlearn the recent input and forget. Swerving to demonstrate recoveries failed because half a swerve is wrong to imitate and would need constant learning-toggling and dangerous driving. The fix that resolved both was to manufacture the missing examples using known perspective geometry: resample each real frame into a handful of synthetic frames at shifted and rotated virtual poses — supplying exactly the recovery views and the variety that were missing — filling revealed edge pixels along the heading direction (not nearest-neighbor, which smears features into an artifact the network would cheat on), and relabeling each synthetic pose by a pure-pursuit model of how the correct steering changes with pose, with the arc radius derived from the sagitta geometry. A buffer of past patterns, inserting each new pattern and evicting whichever pattern leaves the buffer's mean signed steering closest to straight, locks in diversity and the left-right-symmetry prior so the network never acquires a turn bias. The result drops straight into the existing forward-net-plus-backprop loop and learns a new road in a few minutes of ordinary driving.
