I want a coordinate on a planar region that does not care how the boundary happens to be drawn. If `Omega` is simply connected and not the whole plane, it feels wasteful to classify it by its visual outline. Holomorphicity is too rigid for arbitrary outlines to survive as analytic invariants. At every point where the derivative is nonzero, a holomorphic map can only rotate and scale infinitesimal figures. It cannot shear them. So the real question is whether this local rigidity can be made global: does the absence of holes remove exactly the obstruction to choosing one conformal coordinate on the whole domain?

The disk is the right target because it has the three features I need: it is simply connected, bounded, and its automorphisms are completely controlled. If I choose a point `a in Omega`, then sending it to `0` removes two real degrees of freedom, and requiring `f'(a)>0` removes the remaining rotation. If a map exists under that normalization, it should be the canonical coordinate. That is already the shape of the theorem: topology plus one tangent normalization determines the complex coordinate.

Let me first look at what such a coordinate would force. Suppose `H: Omega -> D` is the desired map and `H(a)=0`. Near `a`, injectivity gives a simple zero, so `H(z)= (z-a) h(z)` with `h(a) != 0` and `h` never zero. Taking absolute values, `log|H(z)| = log|z-a| + log|h(z)|`. The boundary of the disk says `|H|` should tend to `1`, so `log|H|` should tend to `0` at the boundary. Therefore `log|h|` should solve a Dirichlet problem with boundary values `-log|z-a|`. This is the Green-function picture: build a harmonic function with a logarithmic pole at `a` and zero boundary value, take its harmonic conjugate, exponentiate, and the coordinate appears.

That route explains why the theorem should be true. Harmonic functions are flexible enough to absorb the boundary geometry, and simple connectivity is exactly what makes the conjugate single-valued. If I have `G(z)=log|z-a|+u(z)` harmonic away from `a` with boundary value `0`, and if `G*` is a harmonic conjugate of `G` off the pole with the right angular jump, then `exp(G+iG*)` has modulus tending to `1` at the boundary and a simple zero at `a`. The complex coordinate is not patched together from elementary maps; it is recovered from the potential whose boundary value is prescribed by the desired disk modulus. The hard parts are also visible. I need an existence theorem for the Dirichlet problem on rough domains, and after exponentiating I still owe a proof that the map takes every disk value exactly once. Riemann's geometric picture makes the construction feel inevitable, but the proof cannot rest on a mental picture of pushing a boundary curve inward.

So I switch to the function-theoretic proof because it keeps the same insight but packages existence as compactness. I will not try to guess the boundary. I will look at all holomorphic one-to-one maps from `Omega` into `D` that send `a` to `0`, rotate each one so its derivative at `a` is positive, and ask for the one with the largest derivative. The derivative is the conformal scale of the coordinate at the basepoint. If a candidate only maps `Omega` into a small part of the disk, it is wasting room; a fuller coordinate should allow a larger scale at `a`. The extremal derivative is a way to make "use the whole disk" into a testable condition.

I need the family not to be empty. Since `Omega` is a proper domain, choose `b` outside it. The function `z-b` has no zeros on `Omega`, and simple connectivity gives a holomorphic square root `h(z)=sqrt(z-b)`. This square root is one-to-one because equality of square roots gives equality after squaring. More importantly, the image cannot contain both `w` and `-w`: if `h(z1)=w` and `h(z2)=-w`, then `z1-b=w^2=z2-b`, so `z1=z2`, which would force `w=-w`, impossible because `b` is not in `Omega`. Since `h(Omega)` is open and contains a disk around `h(a)`, the reflected disk around `-h(a)` is omitted by `h(Omega)`. Then `1/(h(z)+h(a))` is bounded and still one-to-one. Subtract its value at `a`, scale it into the unit disk, and rotate it. That gives at least one normalized injective disk-valued map.

Now define `M = sup f'(a)` over all normalized injective `f: Omega -> D` with `f(a)=0` and `f'(a)>0`. This number is positive by the construction above, and finite by Cauchy's estimate on a small closed disk around `a` contained in `Omega`: every `f` is bounded by `1`, so `|f'(a)|` is bounded by the reciprocal of that disk radius. Choose maps `f_n` with `f_n'(a) -> M`. The family is locally bounded because all maps land in `D`, so Montel's theorem gives a subsequence converging uniformly on compact subsets to a holomorphic function `f`. Derivatives converge too, by the Cauchy integral formula on a small circle around `a`, so `f(a)=0` and `f'(a)=M>0`. The limit is not constant.

I have to be careful here. A uniform limit of injective functions can collapse if it becomes constant, but it cannot fold while staying nonconstant. Hurwitz's theorem gives exactly that: a locally uniform nonconstant limit of univalent holomorphic functions is univalent. So `f` is injective. Since `|f|<=1`, the maximum modulus principle also rules out an interior point with `|f|=1`; otherwise `f` would be constant. Thus the extremal map actually sends `Omega` into `D`, and it belongs to the same normalized family. The supremum is attained.

What remains is the issue that mattered from the beginning: why does this extremal map fill the disk? Suppose it misses a point `c in D`. It cannot miss `0`, because `f(a)=0`, so `c != 0`. The disk automorphism
`B_c(w)=(w-c)/(1-conj(c)w)` sends `c` to `0` and maps `D` to itself. Since `c` is omitted, `B_c(f(z))` is a holomorphic function on `Omega` with no zeros. Simple connectivity again supplies a holomorphic square root `g(z)=sqrt(B_c(f(z)))`. This is the same topological lever as before: no holes means the square root has no monodromy. Also `g` is injective, because equality of `g` gives equality of `g^2`, then equality of `B_c(f)`, then equality of `f`, then equality of points.

The new map `g` lands in the disk, but it no longer sends `a` to `0`. Let `alpha=g(a)`. Since `f(a)=0`, I have `alpha^2=B_c(0)=-c`, so `|alpha|=sqrt(|c|)`. Move `alpha` to `0` by the disk automorphism `Phi_alpha(w)=(w-alpha)/(1-conj(alpha)w)`, and rotate the result so the derivative at `a` is positive. This produces another admissible map. If the missed value really existed, its derivative at `a` should not exceed `M`. But the derivative calculation says otherwise.

Differentiate `g^2=B_c(f)` at `a`. Since `B_c'(0)=1-|c|^2`, I get
`2 alpha g'(a) = (1-|c|^2) f'(a)`. Taking moduli gives
`|g'(a)| = (1-|c|^2) f'(a)/(2 sqrt(|c|))`. The automorphism moving `alpha` to `0` multiplies derivative magnitude by `1/(1-|alpha|^2)=1/(1-|c|)`. Therefore the normalized new map has derivative
`f'(a) (1+|c|)/(2 sqrt(|c|))`, which is
`f'(a) (sqrt(|c|)+1/sqrt(|c|))/2`. For `0<|c|<1`, the arithmetic-geometric mean inequality makes this strictly larger than `f'(a)=M`. That is impossible. So the extremal image omits no point of the disk. It is onto.

This also explains why the square root keeps appearing. It is not an accidental trick. If the image misses a value, the disk has unused room. The automorphism turns the missed value into a missing zero; the square root opens that punctured disk-like image in a way that increases the conformal scale at the basepoint; simple connectivity is what permits that operation globally. A hole would allow monodromy and break the square root. The topological hypothesis is being used at the exact moment surjectivity is forced.

Uniqueness is then rigid in the smallest possible way. If `f` and `F` are two such normalized coordinates, `F o f^{-1}` is an automorphism of `D` fixing `0`. Schwarz's lemma says it must be a rotation `e^{i theta} z`. The derivative normalization is positive real for both maps, so the rotation is `1`. Hence the coordinate is unique. The final picture is clean: simple connectivity gives logarithms, square roots, and harmonic conjugates; Montel compactness produces an extremal coordinate; Hurwitz prevents folding in the limit; the omitted-value square-root argument forces the image to be the entire disk; Schwarz's lemma removes the last rotation. Arbitrary simply connected planar domains reduce to the disk because their complex structure has no remaining freedom once the basepoint and tangent direction are fixed.
