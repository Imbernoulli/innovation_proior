I need a matching whose failure mode is pairwise, not numerical. If I add up ranks or costs, I can get an assignment that looks globally optimized while one outside pair still wants to leave together. The real certificate has to talk about every outside pair. That makes early permanent commitments suspicious, because every early match creates a future obligation: I must know that no later person can tempt either endpoint away.

I first try to think like an assignment algorithm. There are rows, columns, and one entry in each row and column. But the cost-matrix view optimizes a scalar objective, and the blocking-pair test is not scalar. A pair blocks because two separate preference comparisons both point inward. So even if I can solve a minimum-cost assignment efficiently, I have solved the wrong object unless the objective somehow encodes every possible side deal.

Greedy matching has the same weakness in another form. If I match mutual first choices, the pair looks safe, but the rule has no answer when no mutual first choice exists, and it has no general certificate for the people left behind. Serial choice is also too thin. If one side simply picks in order, the other side's preferences enter only through availability. Someone chosen early may later prefer a later chooser, and the later chooser may prefer that person too. That is exactly the obstruction I need to rule out, and I want to see it actually happen before I trust the intuition. Take two proposers `A`, `B` and two receivers `x`, `y`, with `A: x>y`, `B: x>y`, and on the other side `x: B>A`, `y: A>B`. Serial choice in the order `A, B` gives `A` its top receiver `x`, then `B` falls to `y`, so the outcome is `A-x`, `B-y`. Now I check the pair `(B, x)`: `B` prefers `x` to `y`, and `x` prefers `B` to its current partner `A`. Both comparisons point inward, so `(B, x)` blocks. Serial choice does fail, and it fails precisely because it never lets `x` reconsider `A` once a better suitor shows up. So whatever rule I use has to keep that door open.

That points at the asymmetry I want. The safe irreversible action seems to be rejection rather than acceptance. If a receiver rejects a proposer while holding someone better, and if the receiver's held option can only improve afterward, then that rejected pair should never become a blocking pair. A permanent yes is risky; a justified no can become evidence.

So I let the left side move down its preference lists while the right side holds only tentative partners. A free proposer asks the most preferred receiver not yet asked. The receiver compares this proposal with the current held proposer, if any, and keeps the one she prefers. The rejected proposer remains free and moves on later. Nothing says the held pair is final until the process stops.

Before reasoning about it abstractly I want to run the rule by hand on a slightly bigger case to see what it does. Take three proposers and three receivers with `A: x>y>z`, `B: y>x>z`, `C: x>y>z`, and receivers `x: B>A>C`, `y: A>B>C`, `z: A>B>C`. Tracing the proposals: `A` proposes to `x`, `x` is empty and holds `A`. `B` proposes to `y`, empty, holds `B`. `C` proposes to `x`; `x` already holds `A` and prefers `A` to `C` (`A` is ranked above `C` on `x`'s list), so `x` rejects `C`. `C`, still free, proposes to `y`; `y` holds `B` and prefers `B` to `C`, so `y` rejects `C`. `C` proposes to `z`, empty, holds `C`. Now nobody is free. The result is `A-x`, `B-y`, `C-z`. That took five proposal events, and checking the blocking-pair condition over all pairs returns nothing, so this run produced a stable matching. Five events is also well under the three-times-three ceiling I will want to claim for termination, which is a small reassurance that the bound is loose rather than tight.

Now I have two monotone motions to lean on. Each proposer moves in one direction down a finite list and never repeats a receiver. Each receiver moves in one direction up her own ranking among the proposals actually received. This bounds the work: in a complete one-to-one instance with `n` agents on each side, there are at most `n^2` distinct proposer-receiver pairs, and each is used at most once, so the loop runs at most `n^2` times and stops. The trace above hit five of nine possible pairs, consistent with that.

I still need feasibility, because a terminated process with unmatched agents would not be the desired perfect matching in the equal-size complete case. Once a receiver has received a proposal, she always holds one. She may replace the held proposer with someone better, but she does not become empty again. If some proposer finishes unmatched after asking everyone, then every receiver has received a proposal and therefore every receiver is holding a distinct proposer. With the same number on both sides, that accounts for all proposers, contradicting the unmatched proposer. So the terminal matching is perfect in the complete equal-size case.

The stability argument now follows the history of any outside pair. Take a proposer `p` and receiver `r` who are not matched at the end. If `p` never proposed to `r`, then `p` reached the final partner before `r` on the preference list, so `p` does not prefer `r` to the final partner. If `p` did propose to `r`, then `r` rejected `p` either immediately or after holding `p` for a while. At that rejection moment, `r` held someone she preferred to `p`, and after that she only traded up. Thus at the end `r` does not prefer `p` to her final partner. In either case at least one of the two inward comparisons fails, so the pair cannot block.

I can see this in the small run too. The only rejected pairs there were `(C, x)` and `(C, y)`. At the end `C` is matched to `z`, and indeed `x` ends with `A` whom `x` ranks above `C`, and `y` ends with `B` whom `y` ranks above `C` — exactly the rejection certificates the argument promises. Running the blocking-pair checker over the whole matching returns an empty list, which is what I expect if no outside pair survives. What I like about this is that I never had to do a global search over unmatched pairs to be convinced; each tempting pair carries its own reason for failing, left behind by the construction.

But the rule is asymmetric, so I want to understand which stable matching it selects. The proposer side gets to try preferred receivers before falling back; the receiver side only chooses among proposals that arrive. I should expect a proposer-favorable outcome, not a neutral one. To pin this down I enumerate every matching of the three-by-three instance and keep the ones with no blocking pair. There are exactly two: `A-x, B-y, C-z` (the one the run produced) and `A-y, B-x, C-z`. Comparing them, each proposer weakly prefers the first: `A` ranks `x` above `y`, `B` ranks `y` above `x`, `C` is fixed to `z`. So the run did land each proposer on his better stable option. Looking at the receivers in the same two matchings tells the opposite story: `x` is paired with `B` in one and `A` in the other and ranks `B` above `A`, yet the run gives `x` the partner `A` — its worse stable option. So in this instance the proposing side gets its best feasible partner and the receiving side its worst. That is the asymmetry made concrete, not just expected.

Now I want to know whether that proposer-best behavior is a coincidence of this instance or a property of the rule. Call a receiver possible for a proposer if some stable matching pairs them. I ask whether a proposer can ever be rejected by a possible receiver. Suppose the first such rejection happens when receiver `r` rejects proposer `p` in favor of `p2`. Receiver `r` prefers `p2` to `p`. Since `p2` is proposing to `r`, proposer `p2` has already been rejected by every receiver preferred to `r`. By the choice of the first bad rejection, none of those preferred receivers can be possible stable partners for `p2`.

Now look at a stable matching that pairs `p` with `r`, which exists by the assumption that `r` is possible for `p`. In that matching, `p2` cannot be assigned to any receiver preferred to `r`, and `r` is assigned to `p`. Therefore `p2` prefers `r` to the assigned partner, while `r` prefers `p2` to `p`. That pair would block the supposed stable matching. The contradiction means the first rejection by a possible receiver never occurs.

Therefore a proposer never loses a receiver that could appear with that proposer in any stable matching. The final receiver must be the best possible stable partner for each proposer, because any better possible receiver would have been proposed to earlier and would have had to reject the proposer. That matches what the enumeration showed for the three-by-three case, where each proposer ended on the better of his two stable partners. The same asymmetry explains the other side's cost: among stable matchings, the accepting side is not being optimized by this run, which is exactly why `x` came out with its worse partner above. So stability holds and the proposing side does best, but nothing here says that every participant on both sides can safely report preferences truthfully; that would be a separate claim, and I would want to check it with its own argument or counterexample rather than read it off this proof.

For quotas, I do not think I need a new idea, though I should state the substitution carefully. A college holds up to its quota rather than one proposer. Each time new applications arrive, it keeps the top quota from the union of the old waiting list and new applicants, or all of them if fewer than the quota are present, then rejects the rest. A rejection now says the college is full with applicants it ranks above the rejected one; later replacements can only raise that cutoff, so the same rejection certificate that ruled out blocking pairs above still applies, now with a capacity threshold instead of a single held proposer. If a college ends under quota, it has not rejected any acceptable applicant who would rather be there, so no applicant-college pair blocks on the under-filled side either. The one-to-one trace is the clean core I checked by hand; the many-to-one version is that same rejection certificate carried up to capacity.
