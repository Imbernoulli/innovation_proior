I need a matching whose failure mode is pairwise, not numerical. If I add up ranks or costs, I can get an assignment that looks globally optimized while one outside pair still wants to leave together. The real certificate has to talk about every outside pair. That makes early permanent commitments suspicious, because every early match creates a future obligation: I must know that no later person can tempt either endpoint away.

I first try to think like an assignment algorithm. There are rows, columns, and one entry in each row and column. But the cost-matrix view optimizes a scalar objective, and the blocking-pair test is not scalar. A pair blocks because two separate preference comparisons both point inward. So even if I can solve a minimum-cost assignment efficiently, I have solved the wrong object unless the objective somehow encodes every possible side deal.

Greedy matching has the same weakness in another form. If I match mutual first choices, the pair looks safe, but the rule has no answer when no mutual first choice exists, and it has no general certificate for the people left behind. Serial choice is also too thin. If one side simply picks in order, the other side's preferences enter only through availability. Someone chosen early may later prefer a later chooser, and the later chooser may prefer that person too. That is exactly the obstruction I need to rule out.

The safe irreversible action seems to be rejection rather than acceptance. If a receiver rejects a proposer while holding someone better, and if the receiver's held option can only improve afterward, then that rejected pair should never become a blocking pair. A permanent yes is risky; a justified no can become evidence.

So I let the left side move down its preference lists while the right side holds only tentative partners. A free proposer asks the most preferred receiver not yet asked. The receiver compares this proposal with the current held proposer, if any, and keeps the one she prefers. The rejected proposer remains free and moves on later. Nothing says the held pair is final until the process stops.

Now I have two monotone motions. Each proposer moves in one direction down a finite list and never repeats a receiver. Each receiver moves in one direction up her own ranking among the proposals actually received. This is enough for termination: in a complete one-to-one instance with `n` agents on each side, there are at most `n^2` distinct proposals, so the loop cannot continue indefinitely.

I still need feasibility, because a terminated process with unmatched agents would not be the desired perfect matching in the equal-size complete case. Once a receiver has received a proposal, she always holds one. She may replace the held proposer with someone better, but she does not become empty again. If some proposer finishes unmatched after asking everyone, then every receiver has received a proposal and therefore every receiver is holding a distinct proposer. With the same number on both sides, that accounts for all proposers, contradicting the unmatched proposer. So the terminal matching is perfect in the complete equal-size case.

The stability proof now follows the history of any outside pair. Take a proposer `p` and receiver `r` who are not matched at the end. If `p` never proposed to `r`, then `p` reached the final partner before `r` on the preference list, so `p` does not prefer `r` to the final partner. If `p` did propose to `r`, then `r` rejected `p` either immediately or after holding `p` for a while. At that rejection moment, `r` held someone she preferred to `p`, and after that she only traded up. Thus at the end `r` does not prefer `p` to her final partner. In either case, the pair cannot block.

This is the core invariant: every outside pair is explained either by the proposer's own order or by a receiver's permanent rejection certificate. I do not need a final global search over all unmatched pairs, because the construction leaves behind the reason each tempting-looking pair fails.

But the rule is asymmetric, so I need to understand which stable matching it selects. The proposer side gets to try preferred receivers before falling back; the receiver side only chooses among proposals that arrive. I should expect a proposer-favorable outcome, not a neutral one.

Call a receiver possible for a proposer if some stable matching pairs them. I ask whether a proposer can ever be rejected by a possible receiver. Suppose the first such rejection happens when receiver `r` rejects proposer `p` in favor of `p2`. Receiver `r` prefers `p2` to `p`. Since `p2` is proposing to `r`, proposer `p2` has already been rejected by every receiver preferred to `r`. By the choice of the first bad rejection, none of those preferred receivers can be possible stable partners for `p2`.

Now look at a stable matching that pairs `p` with `r`, which exists by the assumption that `r` is possible for `p`. In that matching, `p2` cannot be assigned to any receiver preferred to `r`, and `r` is assigned to `p`. Therefore `p2` prefers `r` to the assigned partner, while `r` prefers `p2` to `p`. That pair would block the supposed stable matching. The contradiction means the first rejection by a possible receiver never occurs.

Therefore a proposer never loses a receiver that could appear with that proposer in any stable matching. The final receiver must be the best possible stable partner for each proposer, because any better possible receiver would have been proposed to earlier and would have had to reject the proposer. The same asymmetry explains the other side's cost: among stable matchings, the accepting side is not being optimized by this run. Stability is guaranteed, but universal truth-telling is not part of the same proof.

For quotas, I do not need a new idea. A college holds up to its quota rather than one proposer. Each time new applications arrive, it keeps the top quota from the union of the old waiting list and new applicants, or all of them if fewer than the quota are present, then rejects the rest. A rejection now says the college is full with applicants it ranks above the rejected one; later replacements can only raise that cutoff. If a college ends under quota, it has not rejected any acceptable applicant who would rather be there. The one-to-one proof is the clean core; the many-to-one version is the same rejection certificate with capacity.
