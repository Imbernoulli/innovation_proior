Let me start from what actually hurts. I have a pile of observational data — cell counts from a bunch of discrete variables — and I want the causal DAG behind them. No experiments, no interventions, and crucially no trustworthy time order on the variables. All I can really ask the data is: are these two variables independent, possibly after conditioning on some others? The first thing I have to be honest about is that I cannot recover a unique DAG, ever, from this kind of data. Two different DAGs can imply exactly the same set of conditional independences, and if they do, no CI test can tell them apart — the data are literally identical in everything I can measure. So the real target isn't "the DAG," it's the equivalence class of all DAGs that share the observed independence facts. I want to return that class, represented compactly: an edge wherever two variables are directly connected in every member, and an arrowhead wherever every member agrees on the direction. Anything the class disagrees about stays an undirected edge, honestly marking my ignorance.

What ties the data to the graph is two assumptions I'll lean on the whole way. The Markov condition says the graph's factorization holds — every variable is independent of its non-descendants given its parents, so `P(V) = ∏_{W∈V} P(W | Pa(W))` — and every independence the graph asserts is real in the distribution. And faithfulness is the converse: all and only the independences in the distribution are the ones the graph entails. The reason I need faithfulness and not just Markov is that I'm running the inference *backwards* — from independences to structure. Markov alone lets the distribution have *extra* independences from coincidental parameter cancellations, and those would be lies about the structure: I'd read an accidental zero correlation as a missing edge. Faithfulness rules that out. With both, I get a clean two-way bridge: a graphical separation in `G` holds exactly when the matching conditional independence holds in the distribution. And the graphical notion is d-separation — `X` and `Y` are d-separated given `W` when every path between them is blocked, where a non-collider in `W` blocks its path and a collider blocks unless it (or a descendant) is in `W`. So from now on, when a CI test says "independent given `S`," I'll read it as "d-separated given `S`" in the graph I'm hunting for, and vice versa.

Now, what is even identifiable? I should nail this before I write a line of search, because it tells me what to aim at and what to leave undirected. Take any DAG and look at what its independence pattern can possibly fix. Adjacency is fixed: if `X` and `Y` are directly connected, no conditioning set over the other variables can block the one-edge path between them, so under faithfulness they are dependent given every such set; if they are not connected, some set separates them. So the skeleton is identifiable. What about arrowheads? Here's the subtle part. Consider three variables on a path, `X — Y — Z`, with `X` and `Z` not adjacent. There are two shapes: a chain/fork where `Y` is a non-collider (`X → Y → Z`, `X ← Y ← Z`, or `X ← Y → Z`), and a collider `X → Y ← Z`. The non-collider shapes are all independence-equivalent to each other — conditioning on `Y` blocks the length-two `X`-`Z` path in every one of them — so the data can't distinguish them, and I must leave that orientation open. But the collider is *different*: in `X → Y ← Z`, the length-two path is blocked unless I condition on `Y` or a descendant of `Y`, and conditioning on `Y` opens it. That's an independence signature no non-collider shares. So unshielded colliders are identifiable, and ordinary non-collider triples are not. This matches the equivalence fact I can derive: two DAGs entail the same independences iff they share both their skeleton and their unshielded colliders. So my identifiable target is precisely: the skeleton, plus the unshielded colliders, plus whatever further orientations those two force by logic. That's the equivalence-class representative I'll return.

Good. So the plan has shape now: (1) find the skeleton with CI tests, (2) find the unshielded colliders, (3) propagate forced orientations. Let me attack the skeleton first, because it's the foundation.

The cleanest possible skeleton test comes straight from faithfulness: `X` and `Y` are adjacent iff they are dependent given *every* subset of the other variables. So to decide whether to delete the `X — Y` edge, search all subsets `S` of `V\{X,Y}`; if any one makes `X ⟂ Y | S`, they're non-adjacent. Do that for every pair, then orient. That's correct — given the true independence facts it returns the true skeleton. But let me count the cost, because I can feel it's bad. The exhaustive method searches subsets of all `p-2` other variables, but most of those variables are obviously irrelevant to whether `X` and `Y` are directly connected. But wait — I don't *know* the neighbors; the neighbors are what I'm trying to discover. This looks circular. Let me think about whether it actually bites. The reason it can't escape the neighborhood is structural, not lucky: parents are neighbors, removal-only keeps every true neighbor present, so the parent-set separator is always still on the table.

Now combine that with the cardinality problem. If I condition only on adjacency subsets, the size of the conditioning sets I ever test is bounded by the *degree* of the graph, not by `p`. On a sparse graph that's small, so my tests stay low-order and statistically sane, and the subset search stays cheap. But there's a sequencing subtlety: at the start, before any edges are removed, the "adjacencies" are still everybody, so a naïve subset-of-adjacencies search on the complete graph is still exponential. The fix is to make the order of testing do work for me. I should test small conditioning sets *first* and let them prune the graph before I ever attempt large ones. Test marginal independence (`|S|=0`) for every pair; delete what separates. Then first-order (`|S|=1`), drawing the single conditioning variable from the *now-thinned* adjacency sets; delete more. Then second-order, and so on, the conditioning-set size climbing by one each round. Each round shrinks the adjacency sets, which shrinks both the number and the order of the tests in the next round. On a sparse graph the whole thing terminates once the conditioning-set size exceeds the max degree, having mostly run cheap, reliable, low-order tests. This "thin from low order up, conditioning only on current neighbors" is the move that turns the exhaustive search into something feasible.

Now the third phase: propagate. After marking colliders I have a partially directed graph — some arrowheads, many undirected edges — and some of those undirected edges are actually forced, even though no collider directly touched them. They're forced by two facts I am not allowed to violate: the final graph must be acyclic, and it must not contain any unshielded collider that I *didn't* already find. That second one is the key and it's easy to under-appreciate, so let me dwell on it. The collider phase found *all* the unshielded colliders implied by the data. So if some orientation of a remaining undirected edge would create a *new* unshielded collider — one that wasn't in my list — that orientation is forbidden, because had it been real, the collider phase would have caught it.

Let me turn that into concrete rules by just chasing the two constraints. Rule one: suppose I have `A → B` and an undirected edge `B — C`, with `A` and `C` non-adjacent. What if I orient `C → B`? But are two rules *enough* to extract every forced orientation? Let me hunt for a configuration the first two miss. Let me run the closure on my four-node example to see what comes out, since the example is small enough to finish completely. When this closure halts in general, I expect the same property to hold — every arrowhead that *every* DAG in the equivalence class agrees on is present, and the undirected edges are exactly the ones the class genuinely disagrees about. That is the equivalence-class representative I set out to find.

Let me step back and look at the whole pipeline for soft spots, because I'll be running it on *sample* data, not an oracle, and sampling error will make some CI tests wrong. The first thing that worries me is the skeleton phase. I'm conditioning on subsets of the *current* adjacency set, and I update that set as I delete edges. But here's a subtlety I almost missed: within a single round `n`, the order in which I process the pairs changes the answer. Suppose at round `n` I delete edge `X — Y` early; that shrinks `Adj(C,X)`, so when I later test some other pair `(X, W)` at this same round, the subsets I draw for `W` are drawn from the *already-shrunk* neighborhood. A different processing order would have used the un-shrunk neighborhood and possibly run a different test, reaching a different verdict on `X — W`. With an oracle this doesn't matter — a separating set either exists in the neighborhood or it doesn't, regardless of order. But with noisy tests, the working adjacency set drifting mid-round makes the *output skeleton depend on the variable ordering*. That's a real defect: feed the same data with the columns permuted and I can get a different graph. On a small problem it's a minor wobble; on a big, dense-in-spots problem it can swing the result wildly, because an early wrong deletion ripples through every later neighborhood.

The fix is clean once I see the cause. The instability comes from *reading and writing the adjacency sets in the same round*. So separate the read from the write: at the *start* of each round `n`, snapshot every node's adjacency set, call it `a(X)`; during the round, draw all conditioning subsets from the frozen `a(X)`, and record which edges have separators at that level; when I leave the level, the next snapshot reflects those deletions. Now within a round, deleting `X — Y` no longer changes the conditioning sets used for any other pair, so the order of processing pairs is irrelevant — every ordering removes exactly the same edges, because an edge is removed iff *some* subset of the fixed `a(X)` or `a(Y)` separates the pair, and that condition doesn't care about order. The skeleton becomes order-independent. The oracle output is unchanged, so I lose nothing in the ideal case and gain robustness in the real one. I'll make this the default — frozen adjacency sets inside a level, updated adjacency sets only at the next level.

There's a parallel instability in the orientation phase under sampling error, and I want to handle the worst of it without overengineering. Two unshielded colliders can fight over a shared edge: sample noise might say `X1 → X2 ← X3` is a collider *and* `X2 → X3 ← X4` is a collider, but both can't fully orient the shared `X2 — X3` the way each wants. If I just blast every collider's arrowheads in regardless, the second one overwrites the first, and the outcome again depends on which triple I processed first — and worse, I can manufacture a bidirected edge that has no business in a DAG. The conservative move is: when orienting an unshielded triple `X — Y — Z` as a collider, only do it if I'm not contradicting an arrowhead already placed — orient `X → Y ← Z` only when neither `Y → X` nor `Y → Z` has already been directed. Prioritize the orientations that are already on the graph rather than overwriting them. It doesn't make collider orientation fully order-independent, but it stops the overwrite cascades and the spurious bidirected edges, which are the failure modes that actually hurt on real data.

Now let me make the conditional-independence test itself concrete for discrete data, because that's the engine and I've been treating it as a black box. I have integer-coded discrete columns. To test `X ⟂ Y | S`, I form, for each observed joint configuration (stratum) `k` of the variables in `S`, the contingency table of `X` against `Y` restricted to the rows in that stratum. Under conditional independence, the expected count in cell `(i, j)` of stratum `k` is the product of the row and column marginals of that stratum over the stratum total: `E(x_{ijk}) = x_{i+k} · x_{+jk} / x_{++k}`. The discrepancy between observed and expected counts, summed over all cells and strata, is Pearson's statistic `X² = Σ (O − E)² / E`. (There's a likelihood-ratio sibling `G² = 2 Σ O ln(O/E)`; both are asymptotically chi-squared, and they agree in large samples. I'll use the Pearson form.) Under the null of (conditional) independence, `X²` is asymptotically chi-squared, and the textbook degrees of freedom are `(Cat(X) − 1) · (Cat(Y) − 1) · ∏_{C∈S} Cat(C)` — one `(rows−1)(cols−1)` contribution per stratum. With sparse data some strata have empty rows or columns; an all-zero row or column carries no information and shouldn't cost a degree of freedom, so the implementation refines this per stratum as `(rows − 1 − #all-zero-rows)(cols − 1 − #all-zero-cols)` and sums those terms. If the total degrees of freedom comes out zero, there's nothing to test and I treat the pair as independent (p-value 1). The p-value is the upper tail of the chi-squared at `X²` with that df; a *large* p-value (above `α`) means I fail to reject independence — so I delete the edge. The threshold `α` isn't a real significance level here, since the search fires off thousands of these tests; it's a tuning knob where smaller `α` yields a sparser graph. The conventional default `α = 0.05` is the sensible starting point.

One more reason the low-to-high thinning matters, now visible from inside the test: the number of cells in the contingency table is `Cat(X)·Cat(Y)·∏_{C∈S} Cat(C)`, exponential in `|S|`. For reliable estimation you want the sample size to comfortably exceed the cell count (rule of thumb: several times more samples than cells), and that fails fast as `|S|` grows. By testing low-order conditioning sets first and only escalating on the locally-dense parts of the graph, almost every test I run stays in the regime where the table is well-populated and the chi-squared approximation is trustworthy. The structure of the search is doing statistical work, not just computational work.

Let me also be clear-eyed about where the whole approach is fragile, since I want to know its failure modes. An early wrong edge deletion in the skeleton phase can cause *later* errors, because removing `X — Y` shrinks neighborhoods and can leave a different false edge un-testable — a small input error can ramify. And the collider phase is the least stable: a single mis-decided collider sets arrowheads that the propagation rules then spread across the graph, so an orientation error can multiply. The freezing-per-round and the conservative-collider choices above are exactly the patches that contain these, but they don't eliminate them — on genuinely unfaithful or small-sample data this method can still disconnect a graph to satisfy one spurious independence, and for the worst cases a score-based repair pass would help. I'll note that as the honest boundary and move on; the method is asymptotically correct under faithfulness and, with the order-independence patch, about as robust as a pure constraint-based pipeline gets.

So let me assemble the whole thing into code, filling the one empty slot — the discovery procedure — with the three phases I derived: thin the complete graph into a skeleton with low-to-high adjacency-subset CI tests (frozen per round for order-independence), orient unshielded colliders from the recorded separating sets (conservatively), and close under the propagation rules. I'll build it on the discrete chi-squared CI oracle and a causal-graph structure that already exist.

```python
import numpy as np
from causallearn.graph.GeneralGraph import GeneralGraph
from causallearn.utils.PCUtils import SkeletonDiscovery, UCSepset, Meek
from causallearn.utils.cit import CIT


def run_causal_discovery(X: np.ndarray) -> GeneralGraph:
    """
    Input:  X of shape (n_samples, n_variables), integer-encoded discrete data.
    Output: estimated equivalence-class graph (CPDAG) as a causallearn GeneralGraph.
    """
    alpha = 0.05                       # CI-test acceptance threshold (tuning knob, not a real sig. level)
    indep_test = CIT(X, "chisq")       # discrete contingency-table test: p = indep_test(x, y, S)

    # Phase 1 — skeleton. Start complete; thin by CI tests of increasing
    # conditioning-set size, conditioning only on current neighbors, with the
    # adjacency sets frozen within each level so the skeleton is order-independent.
    cg_1 = SkeletonDiscovery.skeleton_discovery(
        X, alpha, indep_test, stable=True,        # stable=True: freeze a(X) per level
        background_knowledge=None, verbose=False,
        show_progress=False, node_names=None,
    )

    # Phase 2 — unshielded colliders. For each unshielded triple X — Y — Z
    # (X, Z non-adjacent), uc_sepset orients X -> Y <- Z only when Y is
    # absent from every stored sepset entry for X, Z. priority=2 preserves
    # an existing opposite orientation instead of overwriting it.
    cg_2 = UCSepset.uc_sepset(cg_1, 2, background_knowledge=None)

    # Phase 3 — propagate forced orientations. Repeatedly apply the rules that
    # avoid (a) creating an unshielded collider that phase 2 did not find and
    # (b) creating a directed cycle, until no edge can be oriented. Yields the CPDAG.
    cg = Meek.meek(cg_2, background_knowledge=None)

    return cg.G
```

Let me trace the causal chain one more time. I could never recover a single DAG from observational independences, so I aimed at the identifiable object — the skeleton plus unshielded colliders plus their forced consequences — because two DAGs are independence-equivalent exactly when they share those. The textbook way to get the skeleton, testing every subset of every pair, is both exponentially slow (and never short-circuits on the true edges that matter) and statistically empty (high-order discrete tests have no data per cell). The escape was the Markov-condition fact that a non-adjacent pair is separated by the parents of whichever endpoint has the other as a non-descendant, and parents are neighbors — so conditioning on subsets of the *current* adjacencies always suffices, since a removal-only search keeps the working graph a supergraph of the truth. Thinning from low conditioning-order upward keeps the tests small, reliable, and cheap, and bounds the whole cost by local degree rather than by all variables. The recorded separating sets then hand me the colliders for free, via the d-separation fact that the unshielded length-two path can be blocked by including a non-collider middle node but is opened by including a collider middle node. Two propagation rules (no new colliders, no cycles), plus a third kite rule whose wrong orientation would force a new collider after two acyclicity-rule applications, close the orientation maximally. Running this on samples instead of an oracle exposed an order-dependence — mid-round adjacency updates make the skeleton depend on column order — which I fixed by freezing the adjacency sets per round, and a collider-conflict instability, which I tamed by orienting colliders conservatively rather than overwriting. The discrete CI test underneath is the contingency-table chi-squared, expected counts from stratum marginals `x_{i+k}x_{+jk}/x_{++k}`, df from cardinalities minus degenerate rows/columns, p-value above `α` meaning delete the edge. And the whole thing drops into the harness as three calls — thin, orient colliders, propagate — returning the equivalence-class graph.
