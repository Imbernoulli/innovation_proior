Let me start from what actually goes wrong when I push a Transformer language model deeper. I stack identical blocks, each one writing the output of an attention or feed-forward sublayer back onto a shared residual stream, `x_{l+1} = x_l + F_l(x_l)`. The skip `x_l` is the highway: it's why deep nets train at all, because it gives gradients a clean path backward and lets each block begin as a small nudge to the identity. And yet two things break as the stack grows. Training gets jittery — loss spikes, gradient spikes, the deeper I go the more fragile the run, to the point where I'm dropping the learning rate or leaning on a warmup just to not diverge. And worse, when I finally do train a deep model and probe it, the deepest layers turn out to be doing almost nothing — I can lop them off with barely a change in loss. So I'm paying for depth twice over: it destabilizes the run, and the depth I bought is half-dead. I want both fixed at once, and I want it cheap: no new parameters to speak of, no surgery on attention or the FFN, and ideally it should work whether I'm in pre-norm, post-norm, or one of the hybrids.

Why are the deep layers dead, concretely? Let me trace the pre-norm recursion, since pre-norm is what I actually run. The block is `x_{l+1} = x_l + F(Norm(x_l))`. The normalization fixes the scale of the *branch input*, the thing fed into attention or the FFN, but it does nothing to the *stream* `x_l` that I keep adding onto. So the stream variance keeps accumulating with depth. Make that quantitative under the usual zero-mean normal-weight assumptions. The variance scale satisfies `σ²_{x_l} = σ²_{x_1}·Θ(∏_{k=1}^{l-1}(1 + 1/σ_{x_k}))` — every layer contributes a factor above one to the running stream scale. Bound it at depth `L`: the slowest growth is linear, `Θ(L) ≤ σ²_{x_L}`, and the dangerous upper branch is exponential, `σ²_{x_L} ≤ Θ(exp L)`. Either way the stream scale climbs with depth, and the exponential case is the one that makes the top layers useless.

Now here's why climbing variance kills the deep layers, and it's not obvious until I write the Jacobian. The block map is `Pre-LN(x) = x + f(Norm(x))`, so `∂Pre-LN(x)/∂x = I + (∂f/∂Norm)·(∂Norm/∂x)`. Look at that second term. `Norm` divides by the stream's own scale, so `∂Norm/∂x` carries a `1/σ_{x_l}` — when the stream variance is large, the normalization is dividing by a big number and its Jacobian is tiny. So the whole second term shrinks as `σ_{x_l}` grows, and the block Jacobian collapses toward `I`. Chain that across the whole stack and the end-to-end derivative `‖∂y_L/∂x_1‖` is bounded by a product `∏_l(1 + A/σ_{x_l} + B/σ²_{x_l})` for some constants `A, B`; when `σ²_{x_l}` is on the exponential branch, `A/σ + B/σ²` decays fast enough that the infinite product converges to a finite constant. A block whose Jacobian tends toward `I` is becoming locally identity-like: changing its input barely changes what the residual branch does. So the deepest layers in a deep pre-norm net are pushed toward identity behavior, which is exactly the redundancy the pruning probes keep finding: chop the top third and very little changes. The variance explosion isn't a cosmetic numerical annoyance; it's the mechanism that turns deep layers into dead weight.

So one school of thought says: kill the variance growth and the deep layers come back to life. That's the LayerNorm-Scaling move — divide the branch input by `√l` at layer `l`, `x_{l+1} = x_l + F(Norm(x_l)/√l)`, which deliberately down-weights deeper residuals so the variance curve flattens from exponential toward linear, keeping the deep Jacobians off `I`. It works, and the math above tells me *why* it works: shrink the per-layer increment with depth and the product `∏(1 + 1/σ)` can't run away. But sit with the shape of that fix. The factor is `1/√l` — it depends only on depth, and it is *static*, baked in for the entire run. As I make the model very deep, `1/√l` clamps the deepest branches hard, permanently. Early on that's helpful, it's controlling the explosion. But late in training, when everything has stabilized and I *want* those deep layers learning at full strength, the `1/√l` is still throttling them. I'm trading "dead because the variance exploded" for "dead because I clamped them." The cure is right but it's frozen in time, and that's the seam I want to pull on.

Let me look at the other school, the one that thinks the disease is instability of the *update* rather than variance of the *activation*. Fixup, T-Fixup, DeepNorm: they pin down that what blows up a deep Transformer is the *model update* — one optimizer step moves the function by an unbounded amount when the net is deep — and they bound that update by a constant. DeepNorm makes it concrete: `x_{l+1} = Norm(α·x_l + F_β(x_l))` with a *constant* skip scale `α` — `(2L)^{1/4}` for a decoder-only `L`-layer stack — plus a matching branch-weight scale `β = (8L)^{-1/4}`, and they prove the update is bounded at init; it trains past a thousand layers. Beautiful, but notice *where* the bound lives: it's derived from the network's behavior *at initialization*, and `α` and `β` are constants applied uniformly for all of training. The early chaotic phase is exactly when the update is worst, so a bound sized for that moment makes sense then. But training isn't one regime. There's a warmup where updates are large and chaotic, then a long stable phase of small gradual changes, then a decay — that's literally why people run Warmup-Stable-Decay learning-rate schedules. A constraint calibrated for the worst instant of training and held fixed through the stable phase is over-conservative once I'm in the stable phase; it caps the learning capacity of the layers it's protecting. Same frozen-in-time problem as `1/√l`, just from the update side instead of the activation side.

And then there's the scalar-on-the-branch family — ReZero, SkipInit — which is the closest in form to what I keep circling. ReZero puts one scalar on each branch and *learns* it: `x_{i+1} = x_i + α_i·F[W_i](x_i)`, every `α_i` initialized to 0. That zero init is lovely. At `t=0` the whole network is *exactly* the identity, `x_{l+1} = x_l` for every block, which is trivially well-conditioned — the toy stack `x_L = (1+αw)^L x_0` has input-output Jacobian `(1+αw)^L`, and `α=0` preserves the input signal exactly where `α=1` would send it to `(1+w)^L`, exploding. SkipInit is the same idea, a single learnable per-branch scalar init at 0, enough to train thousand-layer nets without any norm at all; and the reason being-near-identity-at-init is so valuable was spelled out by De and Smith — in a normalized residual net the variance into block `ℓ` grows like `Var(x_ℓ) ≈ Var(x_{ℓ-1}) + 1 ≈ ℓ`, so after the next residual add the new branch accounts for only a `1/(ℓ+1)` slice of the output variance at init. A healthy deep net *starts dominated by its skips*, hugging identity, and that's precisely what keeps its gradients conditioned. So "be identity at init" is a target I should engineer for on purpose.

But here's where ReZero and SkipInit leave me hanging, and it's the crux. Their `α_i` is *learned*, by gradient descent, *independently per layer*. Nothing says the shallow branches turn on before the deep ones. The optimizer is free to grow a deep `α` first if that locally lowers the loss — and given how entangled the stack is, that's not a hypothetical. Stare at the dependency structure for a second. A deep layer's input is the running output of every shallow layer beneath it; a shallow layer's gradient is back-propagated *through* every deep layer above it. So early in training, when a deep branch with freshly-random weights starts contributing, it does two bad things at once: it injects noise into the intermediate representation that all the layers above it now have to consume, and — because gradients flow back through it — it corrupts the gradient signal that the shallow layers below are using to figure out what *they* should compute. The shallow layers are trying to lay down a stable foundation, and the deep layers, firing early with garbage, are simultaneously eating that half-built foundation and poisoning the feedback it's built from. A *learned* scalar gives me no control over this; ReZero's only guarantee is "start at zero," and after step one it's the optimizer's call. The mechanism, like all the others, is fixed at initialization.

So let me name the common failure precisely, because all three families share it. Every one of these levers — depth-aware init, the constant DeepNorm `α`, the static `1/√l`, the zero-init learnable scalar — sets up the *first step* and then either freezes or hands the wheel to the optimizer. None of them is *training-phase-aware*. But I have two facts staring at me that are explicitly *about* the trajectory, not the init. One: training is staged — chaotic warmup, then stable, then decay. Two: the layers converge unevenly — shallow layers settle into their final representation earlier than deep ones; that's measured. Put those next to the dependency analysis and a different kind of fix suggests itself. The problem isn't only *how much* each branch contributes (a magnitude question, which the static factors answer) — it's *when*, and in *what order across depth*, each branch is allowed to start contributing. That's a question about the whole trajectory, and nobody's tool answers it because they all act at one instant.

What would I actually want? I want each layer to start at the identity — `α=0`, keep ReZero's good init — and then I want the contributions to *come on in order*: shallow first, deep last, with the deep ones held back until the shallow ones beneath them have had time to stabilize. And I want this to be something I *impose*, not something I hope the optimizer discovers, because the whole point is that the optimizer left to itself does *not* respect the order — that's the ReZero gap. So the scalar should not be a parameter at all. It should be a *predefined* function I set, that ramps from 0 up to 1 over the course of training, and whose ramp is *slower for deeper layers*. Let me write that as `x_{l+1} = x_l + α(l,t)·F(Norm(x_l))`, where `α(l,t)` depends on both the layer index `l` and the training step `t`, and is fixed by me, not learned. At `t=0`, `α(l,0)=0` for all `l` — exact identity, the property I wanted from ReZero. As `t→∞`, `α(l,t)→1` — the block becomes plain vanilla Pre-LN, so I lose nothing in the limit; the deep layers end up at full strength, which is exactly what the static `1/√l` fails to allow. In between, the *schedule* of how `α` climbs is where I encode "shallow first."

Now I have to pin down the shape of `α(l,t)`. Take the simplest monotone ramp from 0 to 1: linear in `t`, clipped at 1. `α(l,t) = min(t/τ_l, 1)`, where `τ_l` is the number of steps layer `l` takes to finish warming up — the step at which its scalar first reaches 1. The whole game is now in choosing the `τ_l`. I want deeper layers to take longer, so `τ_l` should increase with `l`. The cleanest choice that does that and has a single knob is to make `τ_l` linear in `l`: `τ_l = T·l`, where `T` is the warmup length of the *first* layer. Then layer 1 finishes at `T`, layer 2 at `2T`, layer `l` at `T·l`, and the deepest layer `L` finishes at `T·L`. That gives me

  α(l,t) = min( t / (T·l), 1 ),  l = 1,…,L.

Read what this does. At any fixed `t` during the ramp, the shallow layers (small `l`, small `T·l`) have already reached `α=1` while the deep layers are still partway up — the residual branches "switch on" in a wave that sweeps from shallow to deep. The total warmup, the step at which *every* layer has reached full strength, is `T·L`. After that, every `α(l,t)=1` and I'm running exactly vanilla Pre-LN — so whatever the schedule did, it did during the first `T·L` steps and then got out of the way. That's the property the static methods can't have: the constraint is real early and *gone* late.

Let me check this against the three things I said I wanted, and make sure each falls out rather than being asserted. Identity at init: `α(l,0)=min(0,1)=0`, so `x_{l+1}=x_l` exactly — yes, I get ReZero's clean start for free, and now the variance can't explode at init because nothing is being added to the stream yet. Bounded update across time *and* depth: early on, only the shallow layers have nonzero `α`, so only a few layers can move the function at all, and they move it by a fraction `α<1` — the model update is throttled, but as a function of *both* how deep we are and how far into training we are, not a single init-time constant. The constraint relaxes itself layer by layer as training proceeds, so it's tight when updates are chaotic (early) and fully released when they're stable (late) — exactly the temporal awareness DeepNorm's constant `α` lacks. And the ordering: a deep layer's `α` stays near 0 while the shallow layers below it are doing their early, large updates; by the time the deep layer's `α` has climbed enough to matter, the shallow layers beneath it have largely settled — so the deep layer builds on a stable input instead of amplifying random early-init noise, and crucially it isn't injecting garbage back through the gradient path while the shallow layers are still trying to find their footing. The sequential dependency of the stack is now respected by construction.

And the activation explosion? I claimed earlier that LayerNorm-Scaling cures it by shrinking deep increments. My schedule does the same thing but in time rather than statically: early in training the deep `α` are near zero, so they add almost nothing to the stream, so the variance can't run up the exponential branch during the chaotic early phase when it's most prone to. As `α` ramps, the increments come on *gradually*, so the variance grows gently — a slow linear-ish climb instead of an early exponential blowup — and by the time the deep branches are at full strength the stream has had time to organize. So I get the variance control of `1/√l` early, without its permanent late-phase clamp, because my factor goes to 1. Same disease, cured temporally, and the cure removes itself.

Let me make sure I'm not deceiving myself about the "predefined, not learned" choice, since ReZero's instinct was to learn it and learning is usually the safer bet. Could I just learn `α(l,t)`? No — that's precisely ReZero, and it has no mechanism to enforce shallow-before-deep; the optimizer would set the `α` to whatever minimizes the *current* loss, and there's no term in the loss that rewards "let the shallow layers settle first." The ordering is a *prior* I have about how training should proceed, drawn from the convergence-order and dependency facts, and a prior is something I impose, not something I ask the data to rediscover. Making `α` a fixed schedule with zero learnable parameters is the point: it costs nothing, it's deterministic, and it *guarantees* the order. The flip side — that a hand-set schedule might be wrong — I can address by checking that the right qualitative shape matters, which I can reason about by considering alternatives.

So let me consider the alternatives to convince myself the specific shape is load-bearing and not arbitrary. What if every layer warmed up *together*, same `τ` for all — `α(l,t)=min(t/T,1)`, no `l`-dependence? That still delays the chaotic init updates (good), but it ignores the ordering entirely: all the deep branches switch on simultaneously with the shallow ones, so I'm back to deep layers firing while shallow representations are unstable, just postponed by `T`. I'd expect this to help less than the depth-staggered version, and to be brittle — if I make the shared warmup long, I'm now scaling *all* residuals up at once after a delay, which can dump a coordinated burst of representation and gradient noise into the stack and even diverge. (As an aside, this "equal" schedule applied to a Post-LN block is essentially what BranchNorm does — a single global ramp — which is a known but weaker special case.) What if I went the *wrong* way, deep layers first — `τ_l = T·(L−l+1)`? That actively does the harmful thing: it lets the deep, randomly-initialized branches dominate optimization early while the shallow layers are starved, which is exactly the divergence pattern Post-LN is notorious for, where the last layers hog the update and the bottom layers get vanishing gradients and never escape. And a longer warmup would make *that* worse, by cementing the deep-dominant regime before the shallow layers ever get a say. So the *order* — shallow before deep — is doing real work; it's not just "delay everything." The linear-in-`l` stagger is the simplest schedule that encodes it.

I should also sanity-check the *static-vs-dynamic* distinction directly, because it's the whole thesis. Suppose I freeze the constraint instead of ramping it — e.g. hold every layer at a fixed fraction forever, `α(l)=1/√l` (which is just LayerNorm-Scaling) or `α(l)=1/L`. Compare that to the matching schedule that *starts* at that fraction and then relaxes it up to 1 — a "stagewise" ramp `clip((t−T(l−1))/T, 0, 1)` rescaled to begin at `1/√l` and climb to 1. Both impose the same gentle constraint at init; but the static one keeps strangling the deep layers through the entire stable phase, while the relaxing one hands them back their full capacity once they're past the dangerous early regime. If my reading is right — bounded update is needed *at init*, not forever — the relaxing version should win, and the static one should look needlessly conservative, especially for the architectures (like Post-LN) that most need the early protection. That's a clean, falsifiable consequence of the whole "training-phase-aware" idea, and it's why I trust the dynamic schedule over the frozen factor.

There's a small family of refinements on the basic linear ramp worth keeping in my pocket. The raw `α=min(t/(T l),1)` climbs at constant slope; I could make the early part gentler with `α = (min(t/(T l),1))²` — quadratic, so the branch comes on *very* softly at first, which is what I'd want for a fundamentally less-stable block like Post-LN, where easing in gently — the identity-at-init idea taken seriously — matters most — or faster with the square-root `(min(t/(T l),1))^{1/2}`. The total warmup `T·L` is the same for all three; only the curvature differs. The linear one is the natural default — it's the plainest interpolation from identity to vanilla — and I'd reach for the square only on the touchier normalizations. None of these change the parameter count: they're all just different arithmetic on `t`, `T`, `l`.

Now the one free number, `T`. It sets the timescale of the whole thing: layer 1 warms over `T` steps, the whole model over `T·L`. Two failure modes bracket it. If `T` is too small — say the warmup finishes in a few hundred steps — I've barely delayed anything past the chaotic init and I've thrown away most of the benefit; the branches snap on almost immediately. If `T` is too large — so `T·L` eats a big fraction of my total training budget — then I'm spending most of training with my deep layers artificially weakened, and they never get enough full-strength steps to actually learn; the run ends before the deep capacity is used. So `T` wants to be large enough to meaningfully outlast the warmup phase but small enough that `T·L` is a modest slice of training. For the model sizes I care about, `T=1000` lands there: at, say, 32 layers it gives a `T·L = 32{,}000`-step total warmup, comfortably inside a 100k-step run, and I don't even need to tune it — I can set it once and apply it everywhere. The only place I'd touch it is at extreme depth: at 96 or 120 layers, `T·L = 1000·120 = 120{,}000` would exceed a 100k-step run, so the deepest layers would *never* finish warming up — there I'd drop `T` to 500 so `T·L` fits inside training. That's the one principled adjustment: keep `T·L ≤` total steps so every layer actually reaches full strength.

A couple of choices I made without comment, now justified. I'm multiplying only the *branch* `F(Norm(x_l))` by `α`, and leaving the skip `x_l` at weight 1. That's deliberate: the skip is the identity highway and the gradient path; if I scaled *it* by `α` I'd be attenuating the signal and the gradient flow, and at `α=0` the network would collapse to zero output instead of to the identity. Keeping the skip at 1 and dialing the branch from 0 to 1 means `α=0` gives *exactly* identity and `α=1` gives *exactly* vanilla — the two endpoints I want — with a clean interpolation between. And this generalizes to every normalization variant the same way: wherever a block has a residual branch, I multiply that branch by `α(l,t)`. Pre-LN: `x_l + α·F(Norm(x_l))`. Post-LN: `Norm(x_l + α·F(x_l))`. Sandwich-LN: `x_l + α·Norm(F(Norm(x_l)))`. DeepNorm: `Norm(α_const·x_l + α(l,t)·F_β(x_l))` — its constant skip-scaling stays, and my time-scalar rides on the branch. LayerNorm-Scaling: `x_l + α(l,t)·F(Norm(x_l)/√l)` — its `1/√l` and my ramp compose. The principle — schedule the branch contribution — is architecture-agnostic, which is why I expect it to help across all of them, with the post-norm-flavored ones (Post-LN especially) benefiting most because they're the ones whose deep layers normally dominate and destabilize, exactly what the shallow-first ordering corrects.

Let me now turn this into code I'd actually run, because the implementation has one wrinkle worth getting right. The scalar `α(l,t)` depends on the global training step `t`, so I need to know the step before the residual write. The cleanest single-stream version carries the step as a non-trainable buffer on the model and computes the per-layer `α` right in the loop that runs the blocks. There's a second, smaller wrinkle: I want `x ← x + α·(branch contribution)`, but in the nanoGPT-style harness the block already folds the skip in and returns `x + branch`, i.e. `block_out = x + delta` where `delta` is the sublayer contribution. So rather than reach inside the block to multiply only the branch, I recover the contribution as `delta = block_out − x` and write `x ← x + α·(block_out − x)`, which scales exactly the branch and leaves the skip at weight 1. I read the current step before the block loop, so the first training forward is truly `t=0` and every branch is off; then I advance the buffer after this forward has used that step. For each block `i` (1-indexed layer `l = i+1`) I set `α = min(step/(T·l), 1)`; once a layer's `α` has reached 1 I just take `block_out` directly, which is identical to vanilla. Two extra lines of arithmetic, one integer buffer for the step, zero learnable parameters, and the optimizer is completely untouched.

```python
import torch
import torch.nn as nn
import torch.nn.functional as F


class Block(nn.Module):
    """Vanilla Pre-LN block, unchanged. The residual *schedule* lives in GPT.forward,
    so the block stays a pure 'skip + sublayer' map and the scalar can depend on the
    global step, which the block doesn't see."""

    def __init__(self, config):
        super().__init__()
        self.ln_1 = LayerNorm(config.n_embd, bias=config.bias)
        self.attn = CausalSelfAttention(config)
        self.ln_2 = LayerNorm(config.n_embd, bias=config.bias)
        self.mlp = MLP(config)

    def forward(self, x):
        x = x + self.attn(self.ln_1(x))
        x = x + self.mlp(self.ln_2(x))
        return x


class GPT(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, config):
        super().__init__()
        self.config = config
        self.transformer = nn.ModuleDict(dict(
            wte=nn.Embedding(config.vocab_size, config.n_embd),
            wpe=nn.Embedding(config.block_size, config.n_embd),
            drop=nn.Dropout(config.dropout),
            h=nn.ModuleList([Block(config) for _ in range(config.n_layer)]),
            ln_f=LayerNorm(config.n_embd, bias=config.bias),
        ))
        self.lm_head = nn.Linear(config.n_embd, config.vocab_size, bias=False)
        self.transformer.wte.weight = self.lm_head.weight

        # Residual warmup schedule: T sets the first layer's warmup length;
        # layer l finishes warming up at step T*l, so the whole model finishes
        # at T*L. The step counter is a non-trainable buffer, not a parameter.
        self.warmup_T = 1000
        self.register_buffer('_warmup_step', torch.zeros(1, dtype=torch.long))

    def forward(self, idx, targets=None):
        b, t = idx.size()
        pos = torch.arange(0, t, dtype=torch.long, device=idx.device)
        x = self.transformer.drop(self.transformer.wte(idx) + self.transformer.wpe(pos))

        step = self._warmup_step.item()
        T = self.warmup_T

        for i, block in enumerate(self.transformer.h):
            block_out = block(x)                       # block_out = x + delta (Pre-LN residual)
            if self.training and step < T * (i + 1):   # layer l = i+1 still warming up
                layer_idx = i + 1
                alpha = min(step / (T * layer_idx), 1.0)   # alpha(l,t) = min(t/(T*l), 1), 0 -> 1
                x = x + alpha * (block_out - x)        # x <- x + alpha * delta : scale only the branch
            else:
                x = block_out                          # alpha == 1: exact vanilla Pre-LN

        x = self.transformer.ln_f(x)
        if targets is not None:
            logits = self.lm_head(x)
            loss = F.cross_entropy(logits.view(-1, logits.size(-1)), targets.view(-1),
                                   ignore_index=-1)
        else:
            logits = self.lm_head(x[:, [-1], :])
            loss = None
        if self.training:
            self._warmup_step += 1
        return logits, loss

    def configure_optimizers(self, weight_decay, learning_rate, betas, device_type):
        # unchanged: the schedule adds no learnable parameters
        decay = [p for p in self.parameters() if p.dim() >= 2 and p.requires_grad]
        nodecay = [p for p in self.parameters() if p.dim() < 2 and p.requires_grad]
        optim_groups = [
            {'params': decay, 'weight_decay': weight_decay},
            {'params': nodecay, 'weight_decay': 0.0},
        ]
        return torch.optim.AdamW(optim_groups, lr=learning_rate, betas=betas)
```

Let me retrace the causal chain so I'm sure it holds together. I started with deep Transformers that are both unstable to train and wasteful — the deepest layers end up dead. Tracing the pre-norm recursion showed why the deep layers die: the stream variance accumulates with depth, `σ²_{x_l}` running up toward `Θ(exp L)`, which shrinks the normalization Jacobian and drives each deep block toward the identity map. The static cures — LayerNorm-Scaling's `1/√l`, DeepNorm's constant skip-scaling, the bounded-update inits — all work by controlling the per-layer contribution or the update, but every one of them is fixed at initialization and frozen for the whole run, so they over-constrain the deep layers in the stable phase where those layers should be learning. ReZero and SkipInit got the *zero-init, identity-at-start* piece right, but learning the scalar gives no control over the order in which branches turn on, and the entangled dependency structure of the stack means a deep branch firing early — with random weights — poisons both the representations the upper layers consume and the gradients the lower layers learn from. Two trajectory-level facts — training is staged, and shallow layers converge before deep — said the missing ingredient was *timing and order*, not just magnitude. So I made the branch scalar a *predefined* function `α(l,t)` of layer and step, zero at init (identity, no early variance blowup), one in the limit (full vanilla capacity, no permanent clamp), ramping linearly with a per-layer warmup length `T·l` that increases with depth, so the branches switch on in a shallow-to-deep wave over a total of `T·L` steps and then the schedule vanishes. The alternatives point to the same shape: warming all layers together ignores the order and can get brittle at long warmup; warming deep-first reproduces the Post-LN divergence pattern; freezing the constraint instead of relaxing it strangles the deep layers in the stable phase. The same scalar rides on the branch of every normalization variant, so the fix is general. And it lands as two lines of arithmetic plus one integer buffer, no learnable parameters, the optimizer untouched.
