# The AKS Primality Test

## Problem

Decide, for every integer `n > 1`, whether `n` is prime or composite by a deterministic algorithm whose worst-case running time is polynomial in `log n`, without randomness and without assuming ERH/GRH.

## Key idea

The full polynomial Fermat identity

`(X+a)^n equiv X^n+a mod n`

characterizes primes when `gcd(a,n)=1`, but checking the full identity costs too much because it has degree `n`. The method compresses the identity in the quotient ring

`(Z/nZ)[X] / (X^r - 1)`,

where only `r` coefficients remain. The quotient alone could hide errors, so `r` is chosen so that the multiplicative order of `n mod r` is larger than `(log n)^2`, and the identity is checked for enough small shifts `a`. If a composite passes all these compressed identities, the accepted shifts generate too many distinct residues in a finite field; a separate collision argument bounds the same group from above unless `n` is a prime power. A perfect-power precheck removes that last possibility.

## Algorithm

All logarithms are base 2. Let `ord_r(n)` be the multiplicative order of `n mod r`.

```text
Input: integer n > 1

1. If n = a^b for integers a > 1 and b > 1, output COMPOSITE.

2. Find the smallest r for which gcd(n,r) = 1 and ord_r(n) > (log n)^2.

3. For every integer a with 1 <= a <= r:
      if 1 < gcd(a,n) < n, output COMPOSITE.

4. If n <= r, output PRIME.

5. Let ell = floor(sqrt(phi(r)) log n).
   For a = 1,2,...,ell:
      if (X+a)^n != X^n+a in (Z/nZ)[X]/(X^r-1),
          output COMPOSITE.

6. Output PRIME.
```

The polynomial congruence in step 5 is computed by repeated squaring in the quotient ring: after every multiplication, reduce coefficients modulo `n` and fold `X^k` to `X^(k mod r)`.

## Correctness

**Prime inputs pass.** If `n` is prime, step 1 does not reject, step 3 finds no nontrivial gcd, and the binomial theorem gives `(X+a)^n equiv X^n+a mod n` for every `a`. Reducing further modulo `X^r-1` preserves the identity, so step 5 does not reject.

**Step 4 is sound.** If `n <= r` and `n` were composite, `n` would have a prime factor at most `sqrt(n) <= r`. Step 3 would have found a nontrivial gcd. Hence accepting in step 4 is correct.

**The searched `r` exists with polynomial size.** There is an `r <= max(3, ceil((log n)^5))` with `ord_r(n) > (log n)^2`. The proof uses the bound `lcm(1,...,m) >= 2^m` for `m >= 7`: if every small `r` failed, the lcm of all numbers up to `B=ceil((log n)^5)` would divide a product involving `n` and `n^i-1` for `i <= (log n)^2`, but that product is less than `2^B`, contradicting the lcm lower bound.

**Composite inputs cannot pass step 6.** Assume the algorithm reaches step 6. Since `ord_r(n)>1`, some prime divisor of `n` has order greater than one modulo `r`; choose such a divisor and call it `p`. Set `ell = floor(sqrt(phi(r)) log n)`. Step 3 and `n > r` imply `p > r`, and the choice of `r` gives `gcd(n,r)=1`. Since step 5 passed, for `1 <= a <= ell`,

`(X+a)^n equiv X^n+a mod (X^r-1,p)`.

Since `p` is prime, also `(X+a)^p equiv X^p+a mod (X^r-1,p)`, and the Frobenius map gives that both `p` and `n/p` are introspective for each checked `X+a`; the factor `X` is introspective for every exponent. A number `m` is introspective for `f` if

`f(X)^m equiv f(X^m) mod (X^r-1,p)`.

Introspective numbers are closed under multiplication, and for fixed `m`, the polynomials introspective for `m` are closed under multiplication. Hence every `(n/p)^i p^j` is introspective for every product `prod_{a=0}^ell (X+a)^{e_a}`.

Let `G_1` be the subgroup of `(Z/rZ)^*` generated by the residues of `(n/p)^i p^j`, and let `t=|G_1|`. Since `n=(n/p)p` lies in `G_1`, `t >= ord_r(n) > (log n)^2`. Let `h` be an irreducible factor of the `r`-th cyclotomic polynomial over `F_p`, and work in the field `F=F_p[X]/(h)`, where `X` is a primitive `r`-th root of unity. Because `ord_r(p)>1`, `deg h>1`, so none of `X,X+1,...,X+ell` is zero in `F`. Let `G_2` be the subgroup of `F^*` generated by those elements.

For the lower bound, any two distinct products of `X,X+1,...,X+ell` of total degree less than `t` remain distinct in `F`. Otherwise their difference would be a nonzero polynomial of degree less than `t` with the `t` distinct roots `X^m`, choosing one introspective representative `m=(n/p)^i p^j` for each residue class in `G_1`. Therefore

`|G_2| >= binom(t+ell, t-1) > n^sqrt(t)`.

For the upper bound, suppose `n` is not a power of `p`. The `(floor(sqrt(t))+1)^2` integers `(n/p)^i p^j`, with `0 <= i,j <= floor(sqrt(t))`, are distinct, so two of them are congruent modulo `r`; call them `m_1 > m_2`. Then every element of `G_2` is a root of `Y^(m_1)-Y^(m_2)` over `F`, whose degree is at most `n^sqrt(t)`. Hence `|G_2| <= n^sqrt(t)`, contradicting the lower bound.

So `n` must be a power of `p`. Step 1 has already rejected all nontrivial perfect powers, leaving only `n=p`. Thus every input that reaches step 6 is prime.

## Complexity

The search for `r` is polynomial because `r <= max(3, ceil((log n)^5))` by the elementary lcm argument, and the Fouvry-based estimate used in the primary paper gives `r = O((log n)^3)`. Step 5 dominates: each congruence is checked by `O(log n)` quotient-ring polynomial multiplications on degree `< r` polynomials with `O(log n)`-bit coefficients, for `floor(sqrt(phi(r))log n)` shifts. This yields a deterministic polynomial-time algorithm; with the Fouvry-based bound on `r`, the asymptotic time is `O~((log n)^(15/2))`, and the weaker elementary bound still gives `O~((log n)^(21/2))`.
