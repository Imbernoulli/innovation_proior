I start with the part of primality testing that almost works and keep asking what information it throws away. Fermat gives me a fast necessary condition: for prime `n`, `a^(n-1) equiv 1 mod n`. Repeated squaring makes this cheap. But it is too scalar. A Carmichael number can imitate a prime for every coprime base, so if I only look at one residue after exponentiation, I have collapsed too much structure.

The binomial theorem gives me a richer object without leaving the same universe. If `n` is prime, then in characteristic `n` the Frobenius map says `(X+a)^n = X^n + a^n`, and Fermat turns `a^n` into `a` when `gcd(a,n)=1`. Equivalently, all the middle binomial coefficients vanish modulo `n`. If `n` is composite, this full polynomial identity cannot hold: take a prime `q` with `q^k || n`; the coefficient of `X^q` is `binom(n,q)a^(n-q)`, and the `q^k` in `n` is not fully present in `binom(n,q)`, while `a` contributes no `q`. So the full identity `(X+a)^n equiv X^n+a mod n` really characterizes primes.

That is too large as an algorithm. I have replaced a scalar residue by `n+1` coefficients, which is worse than trial division. I need to keep the identity but stop it from expanding into degree `n`. The natural way to bound the degree is to work modulo a polynomial, so powers of `X` wrap around. If I reduce modulo `X^r-1`, every monomial `X^k` becomes `X^(k mod r)`, and repeated squaring on polynomials now keeps only `r` coefficients. This is the first compression: check

`(X+a)^n equiv X^n+a mod (X^r-1, n)`.

For primes this is still safe, because a true identity remains true after quotienting. The only danger is the reverse direction. A composite might have bad binomial coefficients that land in the same residue classes modulo `r` and cancel. A single shift `a` is not enough, and a random shift is not a deterministic proof. I need a quotient and a finite family of shifts arranged so that a composite cannot keep hiding.

What should `r` control? Since `X^r=1`, substituting `X -> X^m` only depends on `m mod r`. If `n` has small order modulo `r`, then the powers generated by repeatedly applying the exponent `n` create only a small orbit, and the quotient sees too little. I want `n` to move the primitive `r`-th roots through many distinct positions. So I search for an `r` with `ord_r(n) > (log n)^2`. This is not just a parameter trick; it gives the later counting argument room. I also need `r` itself bounded. Let `B = ceil((log n)^5)`, and look at the smallest positive integer that does not divide `n^floor(log B) prod_{i <= floor((log n)^2)} (n^i-1)`. Minimality forces it to be coprime to `n`, and if its order were at most `(log n)^2` it would divide one of the `n^i-1` factors. The whole product is smaller than `2^B`, while the lcm of the first `B` integers is at least `2^B` for large enough `B`. So some `r <= max(3, ceil((log n)^5))` has the order I need.

Before trusting the quotient, I strip away the obvious composite cases. If `n=a^b`, then a later proof may only be able to force `n` to be a prime power, so I must remove perfect powers at the start. If some `a <= r` has `1 < gcd(a,n) < n`, I already have a factor. If `n <= r` and no such factor appears, a composite `n` would have a prime factor at most `sqrt(n) <= r`, so it would have been caught; in that case I can safely accept.

Now suppose all these gates pass and the compressed identity holds for shifts `a=1,...,ell`, where I will choose `ell` soon. I want to prove this forces `n` to be a prime. Since `ord_r(n)>1`, at least one prime divisor of `n` has order greater than one modulo `r`; I choose such a divisor and call it `p`. The gcd gate and the `n>r` case give `p>r`, and the chosen `r` gives `gcd(n,r)=1`. Reducing the accepted congruences modulo `p` gives, for those checked shifts,

`(X+a)^n equiv X^n+a mod (X^r-1, p)`.

Because `p` itself is prime, it also satisfies

`(X+a)^p equiv X^p+a mod (X^r-1, p)`.

In characteristic `p`, Frobenius lets me peel off a factor of `p` from the exponent. The quotient by `X^r-1` is reduced modulo `p` because `p` does not divide `r`, so the `p`-th power map is injective there. Comparing the `n`-th power identity with the `p`-th power identity then forces `n/p` to behave in the same Frobenius-like way:

`(X+a)^(n/p) equiv X^(n/p)+a mod (X^r-1, p)`.

This suggests naming the behavior. A number `m` is introspective for a polynomial `f` if

`f(X)^m equiv f(X^m) mod (X^r-1, p)`.

The prime `p` and the cofactor `n/p` are introspective for each checked `X+a`; `X` itself is introspective for every exponent, so I get the `a=0` factor for free. The closure properties are exactly what I need. If `m` and `m'` are introspective for `f`, then

`f(X)^(mm') = (f(X)^m)^(m') = f(X^m)^(m') = f(X^(mm'))`,

with the final reduction valid because `X^r-1` divides `X^(mr)-1`. If one `m` is introspective for both `f` and `g`, then it is introspective for `fg` by multiplying the two identities. Therefore every number of the form `(n/p)^i p^j` is introspective for every product of `X` and the checked linear factors:

`prod_{a=0}^ell (X+a)^{e_a}`.

The set of exponents has a residue shadow modulo `r`. Let `G_1` be the subgroup of `(Z/rZ)^*` generated by residues of `(n/p)^i p^j`; it contains `n=(n/p)p`, so its size `t` is larger than `(log n)^2`. The tested linear factors have a polynomial shadow. Take an irreducible factor `h(X)` of the `r`-th cyclotomic polynomial over `F_p`; in the field `F=F_p[X]/(h)`, the image of `X` is a primitive `r`-th root of unity, and `deg h = ord_r(p) > 1` by the way I chose `p`. Let `G_2` be the subgroup of `F^*` generated by `X, X+1, ..., X+ell`.

I need `G_2` to be both too large and not too large if `n` is genuinely composite. The lower bound comes from the many small shifts. Consider two different products of the factors `X+a` whose total degree is less than `t`. If they were equal in `F`, call their difference `Q(Y)`. For each residue class in `G_1`, I choose an introspective representative `m=(n/p)^i p^j`; introspection gives equality after substituting `Y=X^m`, so `Q` has all `t` primitive roots `X^m` as roots. But `deg Q < t`, so `Q` cannot have `t` distinct roots unless it is zero, contradicting that the products are different. Thus all products of total degree less than `t` are distinct in `G_2`.

How many such products are there? They are counted by nonnegative exponent vectors `(e_0,...,e_ell)` with sum less than `t`, so there are `binom(t+ell, t-1)` of them. This is why I need enough shifts: if `ell = floor(sqrt(phi(r)) log n)`, and `t` divides `phi(r)`, then `ell >= floor(sqrt(t) log n)`. The binomial coefficient is then bigger than `2^(floor(sqrt(t)log n)+1)`, hence bigger than `n^sqrt(t)`. So `G_2` has more than `n^sqrt(t)` elements.

The upper bound appears if `n` is not a power of the chosen prime `p`. Look at the grid of exponents

`(n/p)^i p^j`, with `0 <= i,j <= floor(sqrt(t))`.

If `n` has a prime factor other than `p`, these integers are all distinct, because that other prime records the value of `i`, and then the power of `p` records `j`. There are more than `t` of them, but only `t` residues in `G_1`, so two of them, say `m_1 > m_2`, are congruent modulo `r`. Then `X^(m_1)=X^(m_2)` in the quotient. For every element `f` of `G_2`, introspection gives `f(X)^(m_1)=f(X)^(m_2)`, so every element of `G_2` is a root of `Y^(m_1)-Y^(m_2)` in `F`. The degree is at most `n^sqrt(t)`, so `|G_2| <= n^sqrt(t)`.

The lower bound says `|G_2| > n^sqrt(t)`, and the upper bound says `|G_2| <= n^sqrt(t)`, unless `n` is a power of `p`. That is exactly why the first line rejects perfect powers. Once perfect powers are gone, the only remaining prime power is `n=p`. So if all compressed identities pass, `n` is prime.

The final algorithm is now forced by the proof. Remove perfect powers, find a small `r` with `ord_r(n) > (log n)^2`, remove small gcd factors up to `r`, accept the tiny case `n <= r`, and then test the compressed polynomial identity for `a=1,...,floor(sqrt(phi(r))log n)`. The quotient by `X^r-1` turns the infinite-looking binomial identity into a polynomial-time computation; the order condition makes the quotient see many distinct root positions; the small shifts generate enough distinct linear-factor products; and the perfect-power gate closes the only escape left by the counting argument.
