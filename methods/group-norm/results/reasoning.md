OK, let me start from the thing that's actually bothering me, which is a curve I keep coming back to. I take a ResNet-50, train it on ImageNet, and I vary one knob: the number of images per device that the normalization layer pools its statistics over. At 32 images the validation error is about 23.6%. At 16, basically the same, 23.7%. At 8 it creeps to 24.8%. At 4 it jumps to 27.3%. At 2 it's 34.7%. That last number is enormous — more than eleven points worse than at 32, on the same architecture, same data, same everything except how many samples the normalization sees. The model is the same model. So whatever is breaking is not the network; it's the normalization layer, and specifically the way it gathers its mean and variance.

Let me remind myself what that layer is doing, because the failure has to be in the mechanics. For each channel c, it takes every feature value in that channel — across all N images in the batch and all H×W spatial positions — pools them, computes a mean μ and variance σ², and standardizes: x̂ = (x − μ)/√(σ²+ε). Then it puts back a learnable per-channel scale and shift, y = γx̂ + β, so we don't lose representational power by forcing every channel to zero-mean-unit-variance forever. Fine. The part that matters for the curve: the population it averages over, for channel c, is the set of positions {all k with the same channel index c}, which spans the batch axis N and the spatial axes H, W.

So when I shrink the batch from 32 to 2, the size of that population drops by a factor of 16 along N. The mean and variance are *estimated* quantities, sample statistics. With 2 images instead of 32, the estimate is noisier and more biased — it's a worse approximation of the true per-channel statistics of the data. And the curve says the model error tracks that estimate quality almost directly. That's the whole story of the degradation: the statistic I'm normalizing by is computed over the batch, so its quality is hostage to the batch size.

And the moment I phrase it that way — "the statistic is computed over the batch" — the obvious question is the one that actually matters: what statistic *doesn't* depend on the batch size? Because if I could normalize using a quantity estimated from something other than how many images happen to be on this device, the curve would just be flat. The batch size would stop being a knob that controls accuracy at all.

Let me push on why the batch dependence is even worse than that one curve suggests, so I'm sure I'm attacking the right thing and not just one symptom. There's a second problem that's structural, not statistical. At inference time there is no batch — or there's one image, or the batch is whatever the deployment hands me, and it's certainly not the training batch. So this layer can't actually compute its statistics the same way at test time. What it does instead is accumulate a running average of the per-channel mean and variance over the course of training, freeze those, and use the frozen population values at test time. Which means the function the network computes at test time is *not* the function it computed during training: during training each example was standardized by its own batch's statistics; at test it's standardized by these global frozen ones. There's a train/inference discrepancy baked in. And when I take a pretrained model and move it to a new task with a different data distribution, those frozen statistics can be flat wrong — so people often just freeze the whole thing into a fixed affine layer and effectively do no normalization during fine-tuning. And there's a third one: the batch isn't even always i.i.d. — if my "batch" is a few hundred regions cropped from a single image, those samples are correlated, and the batch statistic is corrupted for yet another reason. So "depend on the batch" is causing trouble at small batch, at inference, at transfer, and under non-i.i.d. sampling. It's the same root cause wearing four hats. I want it gone.

So the design goal sharpens to: a normalization that estimates its mean and variance from a population that lives entirely *inside a single sample*, never crossing the batch axis. If the population for a given feature is built only from other features of the *same image*, then it's computed identically whether the batch is 32 or 2 or 1, identically at train and test, no running average, no freezing, no discrepancy. The batch dimension just stops appearing in the definition.

Now, am I the first to want this? No — let me think about what's already on the table that avoids the batch axis, because the answer is going to teach me where the real difficulty is.

The first option pools, for each sample, over *everything* — all channels and all spatial positions, the whole (C, H, W) volume of that one image, one mean and one variance for the entire feature map of the sample. This is genuinely batch-independent: I never look at another image. It works well for recurrent nets. But let me think about whether it's right for a convolutional layer. It forces *every channel* of that image to be standardized by the *same* μ and σ. That's an assumption that all channels are interchangeable — that they make similar contributions and live on the same scale. For a fully-connected layer maybe. But the channels of a conv layer are different filters: one fires on horizontal edges, one on a color blob, one on a texture, one on high-frequency stuff. Their response distributions genuinely differ — different means, wildly different variances. Pooling all of them into one statistic is too coarse; I'm averaging together things that don't belong on the same scale, and the standardization that's right for the edge channel is wrong for the color channel. So this resolves the batch problem but over-shares. Call it the all-channels-together extreme.

The other option goes to the opposite extreme: pool, for each sample *and each channel separately*, over just the (H, W) spatial positions of that one channel. Each channel gets its own μ and σ, computed only from its own spatial map. Also batch-independent. Strong for style transfer and generative models, where decoupling per-channel statistics is exactly the point. But now think about what it threw away: it never lets two channels talk. If channels are correlated — and they are — it can't use that. Each channel is standardized in total isolation. That's the single-channel extreme, and it under-shares; it discards all the cross-channel structure.

So I have two batch-free options that are opposite failures: one pools all channels together (too coarse), one pools each channel alone (too isolated). Both miss. There's also the option of normalizing the *weights* of the filters instead of the features, which is batch-free too, but it's controlling the parameters rather than the feature distribution and it hasn't matched the batch layer's accuracy on recognition — so it doesn't address what I want, which is to control the activations. And the things that try to *rescue* the batch statistic — clipping how far the estimated mean/variance can drift, or pooling the batch statistics across many devices — are still fundamentally batch-dependent: the clipped version still degrades as the batch shrinks, and the cross-device version just buys a bigger batch with more hardware and breaks asynchronous training. None of these removes the batch axis from the definition; they patch around it. I want to remove it.

I'm describing all of these in words, one at a time, and I keep feeling like they're variations on one thing. Let me write them in a single notation and see. Every one of them does the *same* arithmetic: pick a set S of feature positions, compute the mean and variance over S, standardize each position in S by them, then a per-channel affine. Index a feature position by i = (i_N, i_C, i_H, i_W) — which image, which channel, which row, which column. The only thing that distinguishes the methods is which positions go into the set S_i that shares one (μ, σ) with position i. So:

- The batch layer: S_i is all positions with the same channel as i, S_i = {k : k_C = i_C}. The set spans the batch axis N and the spatial axes — that's the (N, H, W) pool, one statistic per channel.
- The all-channels-together layer: S_i is all positions with the same image as i, S_i = {k : k_N = i_N}. The set spans channels and spatial — the (C, H, W) pool, one statistic per sample.
- The single-channel layer: S_i is all positions with the same image *and* same channel, S_i = {k : k_N = i_N, k_C = i_C}. The set is just the (H, W) spatial pool, one statistic per sample per channel.

Stare at this. The three of them are *the same formula with three different choices of which axes the set S_i spans*. The batch layer is the only one whose set reaches across N — that's literally where its batch dependence comes from, right there in the membership condition. The other two pin k_N = i_N, "same image," and that's exactly the condition that makes them batch-independent. So "batch-independent" isn't some separate property I have to engineer; it's just "the membership rule includes k_N = i_N." Anything with that clause never crosses the batch axis, period.

And now the two batch-free options aren't a grab-bag of competing tricks; they're two settings of one dial. Both fix k_N = i_N. They differ only in how they treat the channel axis: the all-channels option puts *no* condition on k_C (all channels are in the same set), and the single-channel option puts the *strictest* condition, k_C = i_C (only the identical channel is in the set). One is "channels: all of them," the other is "channels: exactly one." Those are the two ends of a dial measuring *how many channels share a statistic*.

So the failures map cleanly onto the two ends. The all-channels end fails because it shares one statistic across *all* channels even though channels aren't interchangeable. The single-channel end fails because it shares with *no* other channel even though channels are correlated. If the two failures are "too many channels share" and "too few channels share," then the natural thing to try is the in-between: share a statistic across *some* channels — more than one, fewer than all. Group the channels, and pool within a group. That's a guess at this point, not a result; I'll have to check both that it actually reduces to the two known extremes at its limits and that the interior is better than either end, not just plausibly between them.

But before I commit to "in between is better," I should ask whether grouping is principled or whether I'm just splitting the difference because the two extremes happen to be bad. Where else have I seen normalization over groups of features? It turns up all over classical vision, and not by accident. A HOG descriptor is built from spatial cells, each cell an orientation histogram, and the histogram is normalized *within* a block — within the group of orientation bins. SIFT normalizes orientation histograms the same way. GIST, VLAD, Fisher Vectors are all stacks of sub-vectors — a group per orientation, per cluster — and you normalize within the sub-vector. The recurring idea is that the coefficients inside a group belong together and should be normalized together, while different groups are allowed their own scale. That's the same structure I'd be imposing on channels. And it lines up with how conv channels actually behave: a filter and its horizontal flip should have near-identical response distributions, so they're natural groupmates; orientation, frequency, texture, illumination each induce families of channels whose responses are interdependent. There's even the divisive-normalization picture from biology, where responses are normalized across a *population* of cells with related tuning — not one cell alone, not the whole cortex at once, a population. So grouping isn't an arbitrary compromise; it's the granularity these prior features have all converged on. That makes me more willing to test it, but it's still only motivation — I haven't yet shown the two extremes are limits of one grouping rather than two unrelated tricks.

So here's the rule. For position i, the set S_i is "same image, and same channel-group." Same image is k_N = i_N. Same channel-group: I split the C channels into G contiguous groups of C/G channels each, and two channels are in the same group when they fall in the same block. The clean way to write "same block" is by the floor of the channel index divided by the group size: channel c is in group ⌊c/(C/G)⌋. So

  S_i = { k : k_N = i_N, ⌊k_C/(C/G)⌋ = ⌊i_C/(C/G)⌋ }.

For a given image and a given group, that set is all C/G channels of the group across all H×W spatial positions: I pool over (H, W) *and* over the C/G channels of the group, and I get one (μ, σ) for that (image, group), shared by every position in the group. Then standardize, then the per-channel affine y = γx̂ + β with γ, β indexed by channel — I keep the affine per-channel even though the statistic is per-group, so each channel can still re-scale itself after the shared standardization and I don't lose representational power.

Now the claim I have to actually verify: do the two batch-free extremes fall out as G→1 and G→C? If they don't, then "grouping" is a third unrelated trick, not a dial connecting the two I already have, and the whole argument collapses. Algebraically it looks right — at G=1 the floor condition ⌊k_C/(C/G)⌋ = ⌊i_C/C⌋ is vacuous (C/G = C, every channel maps to group 0), so S_i = {k : k_N = i_N}, the all-channels pool; at G=C, C/G = 1 and ⌊k_C/1⌋ = k_C, so the condition becomes k_C = i_C alongside k_N = i_N, the single-channel pool. But algebra on a floor function is exactly the kind of thing I get wrong, so let me make it concrete and just run the reshape-and-reduce on a tiny tensor: one image, C = 4 channels, a 2×2 map, with x holding the integers 0..15 laid out channel-major. At G = 1, I standardize and compare against an independently computed all-(C,H,W) standardization of the same tensor; they match to within 1e-5 (the pooled mean used for every channel is 7.5, the mean of 0..15 — i.e. one statistic for the whole image, as the all-channels layer demands). At G = C = 4, I compare against an independently computed per-channel-over-(H,W) standardization, looping channel by channel; those match too. So both endpoints are genuinely recovered, not just claimed to be. While I'm in there, G = 2 is worth a look because it's the first *new* point: the four channels split into group 0 = {ch 0, 1} and group 1 = {ch 2, 3}. Group 0's eight values are 0..7, mean 3.5, biased variance 5.25; group 1's are 8..15, mean 11.5, variance 5.25. The output has channels 0 and 2 ending up with *identical* normalized values — which is right, because each sits in the same within-group position and each group is centered on its own mean, so the two groups standardize to the same pattern. That identical-pattern outcome is a thing I did not put in by hand; it's a consequence I can read off and check, and it's what confirms the per-group centering is actually happening. So grouping does interpolate between the two batch-free extremes, and any 1 < G < C is a genuinely new intermediate. The two ends I rejected are the boundary of this rule — I'm not inventing a separate thing, I'm filling in the interior of a dial whose ends were already known to be too coarse and too fine.

Why might an interior point beat both ends? At G=1 the model has exactly one (μ, σ) per image to describe a feature map with hundreds of channels of differing scales — one knob for all of them, too rigid, it can't standardize the edge channel and the color channel appropriately at once. At an interior G, the model gets G separate (μ, σ) per image, so it can give each group its own distribution — it has the flexibility the single-shared statistic lacked. Compared to the other end, at G=C each channel is standardized using only its own H·W spatial values and *nothing else*, blind to its correlated neighbors; at an interior G the statistic for a channel is pooled over its whole group, so correlated channels stabilize each other's estimate and the cross-channel structure is used rather than discarded. So the story I have is "flexible enough to separate unlike channels, pooled enough to share among like ones." I want to be honest that this is an argument about *why the extremes should be bad*, not a proof that the interior wins — both halves are about a failure mode each endpoint has and the interior avoids, which makes me expect a curve that's worst at G=1 and G=C and better in between, but the actual shape of that curve, and whether the gain over the better of the two ends is large or marginal, is something I can only get by training across G and reading off the accuracies. So I'd hold this as a prediction to test, not a settled result.

One thing I should actually check rather than wave at: is an interior-G statistic estimated from *enough* samples to be stable? The entire point was to escape the small-sample noise of tiny batches, so it would be embarrassing if grouping reintroduced it. Let me count the samples that go into a single (μ, σ) estimate, with concrete numbers, and put it side by side with the batch layer. Take a mid-network layer: C = 256 channels, a 14×14 feature map. The batch layer estimates one mean/variance per channel from N·H·W = N·196 values. At batch 32 that's 6272 values — comfortable. At batch 2 that's 392 values — and *that's* the regime where its error blew up to 34.7%. Now group normalization at G = 32: each group has C/G = 8 channels, and the pool is those 8 channels over all 196 spatial positions, so (C/G)·H·W = 8·196 = 1568 values per estimate. Two things jump out of that number. First, 1568 is independent of the batch — it's 1568 whether the batch is 32, 2, or 1 — because the spatial axes are always in S_i and N never is. Second, and I didn't expect the comparison to come out this strongly, 1568 is *four times larger* than the 392 values small-batch BN had to work with; so at the exact batch size where BN's estimate is starving, GN's per-group estimate is actually drawing on a bigger sample. So keeping (H, W) in the set is doing real work: it's what makes even one channel-group on one image a statistically comfortable sample, and the count confirms it's comfortable in absolute terms and not merely "better than nothing." The estimate is stable and it never touches N.

How many groups, then? The dial is continuous-ish in G (any divisor of C), and the argument above says the interior should be a broad plateau — not a knife-edge — because the failure is only at the two extremes. So I'd expect a wide range of G to work about equally, and I'd pick a round default in the middle of that range; 32 groups is a sensible default, with the understanding that I could instead fix the channels-per-group and let G vary by layer — both are reasonable, fixing G is just simpler to state. The thing I'd want to verify empirically is precisely that the curve over G is flat in the interior and only rises at G=1 (the all-channels end) and G=C (the single-channel end); that would confirm the dial is the right axis and that grouping per se — not some lucky G — is what's buying the accuracy.

And let me restate the payoff against the curve I started with, because it's now structural rather than hoped-for. Since S_i contains k_N = i_N, the statistic is computed within one image, so the per-device batch size never enters the computation: the layer computes the identical function at batch 32, 4, 2, or 1. Same function at training and at inference — there is nothing to accumulate into a running average and nothing to freeze, because the test-time statistic is just the test image's own group statistic, computed the same way as in training. No train/test discrepancy, nothing to break on transfer, and the non-i.i.d.-batch problem can't arise because there's no batch in the definition. Note exactly what I've earned here: it's not a claim about accuracy yet, it's that the *function* is provably the same across batch sizes — the sample count above was 1568 at batch 32 and 1568 at batch 2, identical — so the one variable that drove the 23.6→34.7% slide simply cannot appear in this layer's output. Whether the accuracy curve then comes out flat (rather than flat-but-uniformly-worse than batch normalization at large batch) is the empirical question I'd settle by running the batch sweep; but the *mechanism* that produced the slide is gone by construction, and that's the part I can be sure of from the definition alone.

Now let me land this on code, and here the framing as "the same formula, only the axis-set changes" pays off, because I don't have to implement bespoke group bookkeeping at all. The set S_i for group normalization is "fix the image, fix the group, pool over the C/G channels in the group and over H, W." If I reshape the feature tensor so that the group becomes its own axis, then "pool over the group's channels and spatial positions" is just "reduce over the within-group-channel axis and the spatial axes," which the autodiff library's moment primitive already does given a list of axes. So:

```python
import torch
import torch.nn as nn


def group_norm_forward(x, gamma, beta, G, eps=1e-5):
    # x: [N, C, H, W]; gamma, beta: per-channel scale/shift, shape [1, C, 1, 1].
    # G: number of channel groups.
    N, C, H, W = x.shape
    # Expose the group as its own axis: C channels -> (G groups) x (C/G per group).
    # Contiguous blocks, so channel c lands in group floor(c / (C/G)) -- exactly S_i.
    x = x.reshape(N, G, C // G, H, W)

    # S_i = {same image, same group}: reduce over (C/G, H, W), i.e. axes 2,3,4.
    # We do NOT reduce over axis 0 (N) or axis 1 (G): one (mu, sigma) per (image, group),
    # and nothing crosses the batch axis -- this is the batch-independence, made literal.
    mean = x.mean(dim=(2, 3, 4), keepdim=True)
    var = x.var(dim=(2, 3, 4), keepdim=True, unbiased=False)   # biased: m, not m-1
    x = (x - mean) / torch.sqrt(var + eps)                     # x_hat = (x - mu)/sigma

    x = x.reshape(N, C, H, W)                                  # fold groups back to channels
    return x * gamma + beta                                    # per-channel affine y = gamma*x_hat + beta
```

Let me read the reduction axes back carefully, because the entire correctness of the method is "which axes do I average over," and an off-by-one here would silently turn this into the wrong member of the family. After the reshape the axes are (N=0, G=1, C/G=2, H=3, W=4). I reduce over (2, 3, 4): the within-group channels and the two spatial axes. I keep axis 0 (N) — so different images get different statistics, never pooled together, no batch crossing. I keep axis 1 (G) — so different groups get different statistics, which is the whole point of grouping. That's S_i = {k_N = i_N, same group} precisely. If I had instead reduced over (0, 3, 4) — including N, excluding the group axis — I'd be back to the batch layer; if I reduced over (1, 2, 3, 4) I'd be at G=1, all-channels. So the axis list is the method, and (2,3,4) after this reshape is the one I derived.

Two details to keep honest. The variance is the biased one (divide by m, the size of S_i, not m−1) — it's a normalization statistic, not an unbiased estimator I'm reporting, and consistency with the standardization is what matters, so unbiased=False. And the affine γ, β stay per-channel (shape broadcasting over [1, C, 1, 1]) even though μ, σ were per-group: after I fold the groups back to C channels, each channel gets its own learnable scale and shift, recovering whatever per-channel distribution the shared standardization flattened. Wrapping it as a module is just bookkeeping around this same forward:

```python
class GroupNorm(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, num_groups, num_channels, eps=1e-5, affine=True):
        super().__init__()
        assert num_channels % num_groups == 0          # groups must tile the channels
        self.num_groups, self.num_channels = num_groups, num_channels
        self.eps, self.affine = eps, affine
        if affine:
            self.weight = nn.Parameter(torch.ones(num_channels))   # gamma, init 1
            self.bias = nn.Parameter(torch.zeros(num_channels))    # beta,  init 0
        else:
            self.register_parameter("weight", None)
            self.register_parameter("bias", None)

    def forward(self, x):
        N, C = x.shape[0], x.shape[1]
        spatial = x.shape[2:]                          # works for (H,W) and (T,H,W) alike
        x = x.reshape(N, self.num_groups, C // self.num_groups, *spatial)
        dims = tuple(range(2, x.dim()))                # within-group channels + all spatial
        mean = x.mean(dim=dims, keepdim=True)
        var = x.var(dim=dims, keepdim=True, unbiased=False)
        x = (x - mean) / torch.sqrt(var + self.eps)
        x = x.reshape(N, C, *spatial)
        if self.affine:
            shape = [1, C] + [1] * len(spatial)
            x = x * self.weight.reshape(shape) + self.bias.reshape(shape)
        return x
```

I notice the same code generalizes for free to video: if the feature is (N, C, T, H, W), `spatial` is (T, H, W), the reshape exposes the group, and I reduce over (C/G, T, H, W) — group normalization over the spatial-temporal volume, no change needed, still batch-free. That falls out because the method was never about "spatial" specifically; it was about "the axes inside one sample, grouped on the channel axis."

To recap the chain: the batch layer's error explodes at small batch because its per-channel statistic is estimated over the batch, and the batch is also why it needs frozen running stats at test and breaks on transfer — one root cause, the membership of the set S_i reaching across the batch axis. Demand a statistic that lives inside one sample (k_N = i_N) and the batch axis vanishes from the definition. The two existing batch-free choices are the two extremes of how many channels share a statistic — all of them (too coarse for unlike conv channels) or exactly one (too isolated given channels are correlated) — so the resolution is to share within *groups* of channels, the same group-wise normalization classical descriptors used, recovering both extremes at G=1 and G=C and giving a stable, batch-independent statistic for any G in between, computed by a one-line reshape that exposes the group axis and a moment reduction over everything except batch and group.
