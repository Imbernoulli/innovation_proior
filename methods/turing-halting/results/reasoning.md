The thing in front of me is Hilbert's decision problem. Given an arbitrary formula of the first-order functional calculus, is there a definite general method that decides, in finitely many steps, whether the formula is provable? By Gödel's completeness theorem provable and valid are the same here, so it's equivalently: is validity decidable by a uniform mechanical procedure? Hilbert wants the answer to be yes — that's the whole spirit of the program, no ignorabimus, every definite question settled by definite means. And I keep feeling that the answer is no. But I can't even begin to argue no, and the reason is embarrassing once I see it clearly.

To prove that a method exists, I just exhibit one. To prove that *no* method exists, I have to quantify over *all* methods — and I have no idea what the range of that quantifier is. "Definite general method," "mechanical procedure," "calculable by finite means," "effective" — these are words, not mathematics. There's nothing to range over. So the real problem, the one underneath Hilbert's problem, is to say exactly what an effective method *is*. Until I have that, an impossibility proof is literally unstatable.

There are candidates already. Church has the λ-calculus: a function is effectively calculable if it's λ-definable, computed by reducing formal terms. Herbrand and Gödel have the general recursive functions: closure of some base functions under composition, primitive recursion, and minimization. Both are perfectly precise, and Church is even willing to bet that "effectively calculable" just *is* these. I could try to build my impossibility proof on one of them. But it bothers me. Each of these is a particular formal calculus, chosen because it's technically convenient. Why should I believe λ-definability catches *everything* a person could compute by rote? If I prove "no λ-term decides provability," a skeptic shrugs: maybe provability is decidable by some method that isn't a λ-term. The identification of the calculus with all-possible-computation is exactly the thing in doubt, and I'd be assuming it. For an impossibility theorem I want a definition of "method" whose claim to be *all* methods is obvious on its face — argued from what computation actually is, not asserted by picking a formalism.

So forget the calculi for a moment and look at the only concrete example of effective computation I actually have: a person computing. A human being, pencil, paper, working a definite procedure. What does that consist of, stripped to the bone? Let me push on it until nothing is left but operations I couldn't possibly subdivide further.

He's writing symbols on a page. The page is two-dimensional, sure, but is the second dimension doing any *work*? In long multiplication we use columns, but that's just bookkeeping; I could write everything on a single line with markers and lose nothing. So the two-dimensionality is inessential. Reduce the page to a one-dimensional tape divided into squares, one symbol per square.

How many distinct symbols can he use? If I allowed infinitely many, there'd be two symbols differing by an arbitrarily small amount, and at some point he couldn't reliably tell them apart — the difference would stop being a difference he can act on. So the alphabet must be finite. That's not a loss: a long number like 9999999999999999 he treats as one "symbol" only because it's short enough to take in; he can always replace a rich symbol by a string of a few basic ones. Finitely many basic symbols, then: a blank, and S₁, S₂, …, S_m.

At any moment, what is he actually attending to? Some bounded patch of the tape — he can't take in unboundedly much at one glance, or again two enormous patches would blur together and he couldn't be sure which is which. So at each moment there's essentially one scanned square (or a bounded window), and the rest of the tape is just there, not being looked at.

And his behaviour at that moment — what determines it? Two things and only two: the symbol he's looking at, and his "state of mind" — where he is in the procedure, what he's keeping in mind to do next. Here too, finiteness is forced. If there were infinitely many states of mind, two of them would be arbitrarily close and he'd confuse them; effective rote-following requires that he be in one of finitely many distinguishable conditions. Anything he'd want an unbounded memory for, he can instead write down on the tape and read back — the tape carries the unbounded part, the head carries only finitely much.

Now break a step into the simplest pieces. In one elementary act he can't change much: alter at most one symbol on the scanned square (print, or erase), and/or shift his attention by a bounded distance — to a square not more than some fixed L away, because he can't instantly relocate his gaze across an unbounded stretch and still know where he landed. Any bounded shift can be broken into one-square shifts by adding intermediate states, so I lose nothing by making the elementary move left one square, right one square, or not at all. And the new condition he's in afterward, and which act he performs, are fixed by the pair (what he's looking at, what state he's in). Nothing else enters.

When I lay it out like that, the thing builds itself. A finite set of internal conditions — call them m-configurations, q₁, …, q_R. A tape, one symbol per square from a finite alphabet. At each moment a scanned square. And a *table*: for each pair (q_i, scanned symbol S_j) the table says — print/erase a symbol, move one square left or right or not at all, and pass into some m-configuration q_l. If the move at every moment is completely fixed by the configuration (no external choice), call it an automatic machine. That table *is* the machine; the machine *is* the procedure.

The point I care about isn't the gadget, it's that the analysis forced every clause. Finite states, finite alphabet, one-dimensional tape, bounded local action, behaviour determined by the scanned symbol and the state — each one came from "otherwise the human couldn't actually do it by rote." So this machine isn't one formalism among others I picked for convenience. Its claim is that it captures *exactly* what a human computer can do, because I read it off the human computer. That's the definition of "effective method" I was missing, and now an impossibility proof has something to quantify over.

Let me fix the bookkeeping so I can compute *with* this notion rather than just wave at it. A computing machine prints two kinds of symbols. The figures, 0 and 1, are the permanent output. The symbols of the second kind are scratch — rough notes to assist the memory, liable to erasure. The sequence of figures it prints is the sequence computed; read as a binary decimal it's the number computed. I'll keep figures on alternate squares — call them F-squares — and reserve the squares between them, the E-squares, for erasable scratch marks. That way I have unlimited working space that never disturbs the output digits, which is exactly the separation between "the answer" and "the rough work" the human had.

Now a distinction I didn't expect to need but the machines force on me. My machines compute *infinite* decimals — they're supposed to keep printing figures forever. So the *well-behaved* machine is the one that never stops producing figures. Some machines, though, after a while stop printing any new figure — maybe they reach a configuration with no move, maybe they loop forever fussing with scratch symbols and never emit another digit. A machine that prints only finitely many figures I'll call circular; one that goes on printing figures forever, circle-free. A sequence is computable exactly when some circle-free machine computes it. Notice the polarity, because it's the opposite of what intuition reaches for: "good" here is *runs forever, keeps going*; "bad" is *stops making progress*. The question "is this machine circle-free?" — does it keep producing output or does it stall — is going to be the crux.

Next I want to treat machines themselves as objects I can compute over, the way Gödel made a formal system talk about its own formulas by numbering them. A machine is its table, and a table is finite. Standardize it: arrange that every line's operation is one of a few shapes — erase, erase-and-move, print a symbol, print-and-move, just move — by adding extra m-configurations if needed. Then each line is "in m-configuration q_i scanning S_j, write S_k, move L or R or N, go to q_l." Encode it with a fixed finite alphabet: write q_i as the letter D followed by i copies of A, and S_j as D followed by j copies of C. The D starts each block and the count of A's or C's carries the index, so the code is self-delimiting — you can parse it back unambiguously. String the encoded lines together separated by semicolons. That's the standard description, the S.D, built entirely from A, C, D, L, R, N, and the semicolon. Replace A→1, C→2, D→3, L→4, R→5, N→6, semicolon→7 and the whole thing becomes a single integer: the description number, the D.N.

And there's the first real consequence, and it's a little vertiginous given where I think this is heading. Every computable sequence has at least one description number, and each description number names at most one sequence. So the computable sequences are *enumerable* — countable. There are only countably many machines, because there are only countably many finite strings over a finite alphabet. Hold onto that.

One more construction before the argument, and it's the one that makes "feed a machine to a machine" literal. I claim there's a single machine — a universal machine, U — that, given on its tape the S.D of any machine M, follows M's motion exactly, and therefore computes the same sequence when M is circle-free. How could that be? Because the rules of M are just letters on the tape, and so are the successive complete configurations of M, and letters are letters. U keeps M's S.D written on the tape, and beside it the current complete configuration of M (the tape contents, which square is scanned, which m-configuration — all spelled out as letters, with the m-configuration and scanned symbol marked so U can find them). One cycle of U: scan along the S.D to find the instruction whose m-configuration-block and scanned-symbol-block match the marked ones in the current configuration — that's just comparing two marked strings of letters, counting A's and C's — then write down the next complete configuration by applying that instruction's print/move/new-state, and print whatever new figure appears. It uses the E-squares for its own marking work, u, v, w, x, y, z and so on. The thing that makes it possible is exactly that program and data are the same kind of stuff: M's rules and M's running state are both just sequences of letters that U copies, marks, compares, erases. A *single* finite machine simulates *every* machine from its description. So my notion of effective method is closed under "run a given description," and I now have, in one object, the engine that will let a supposed decider act on all machines at once.

Now I have a list of all computable sequences and a way to run any of them. The instinct is immediate: Cantor. The reals aren't countable because of his diagonal — list binary sequences s₁, s₂, …, build s whose n-th digit flips the n-th digit of sₙ, and s is off the list because it disagrees with each sₙ exactly where sₙ would have to agree. So let me do that to my computable sequences. List them α₁, α₂, …; let φ_n(m) be the m-th figure of α_n; define β with n-th figure 1 − φ_n(n). β disagrees with every α_n at position n, so β is not any α_n. But β is built by a perfectly definite rule, so β looks computable, so β is some α_K, so at n = K I get φ_K(K) = 1 − φ_K(K), i.e. 1 = 2φ_K(K), 1 is even. Absurd. Conclusion: the computable sequences are not enumerable.

Except I just *proved* they're enumerable — every machine has a D.N. So something in the Cantor replay is wrong, and finding the exact false step is the whole game. Where did I cheat? I said "β is built by a definite rule, so β is computable." To actually compute β I'd need to compute the map n ↦ α_n: given n, produce the n-th computable sequence and read off its n-th figure. But the α_n aren't handed to me indexed; what I have is the integers, and among them the description numbers, and among *those* the ones whose machine is circle-free — only a circle-free machine computes a genuine sequence. To enumerate the computable sequences I'd have to walk the integers and pick out the ones that are description numbers of circle-free machines — I'd have to *decide*, of each number, whether it's the D.N of a circle-free machine. Call such a number satisfactory. The diagonal step silently assumed I can sort the satisfactory numbers from the rest by machine. The contradiction isn't telling me the sequences are uncountable; it's telling me that *sorting* can't be done.

So the diagonal hasn't failed — I've been pointing it at the wrong target. It can't refute countability, since finite descriptions are obviously countable. What it can do is refute the existence of the decider that the bad step assumed. Turn it on that.

Suppose, for contradiction, there is a machine D that does the sorting: given any candidate description number, D runs and halts with a verdict — "s" if that number is the D.N of a circle-free machine, "u" otherwise. "Otherwise" includes invalid descriptions and circular machines. D is total; it always reaches a verdict; that's the assumption. I'll combine D with the universal machine U to build a machine H that computes a diagonal sequence, and then ask H about itself.

The machine forced by this bookkeeping produces a sequence β′ whose n-th figure is the diagonal figure φ_n(n) — the n-th figure of the n-th computable sequence — and I'll take the *plain* diagonal, the figure itself, not one minus it. Call it H. H runs in sections. By the end of section N − 1 it has, among its scratch work, written down the integers 1, 2, …, N − 1 and run D on each; some number of them, say R(N − 1), came back satisfactory. In section N it forms the integer N and runs D on it. If N is unsatisfactory, R(N) = R(N − 1) and H moves on. If N is satisfactory, R(N) = R(N − 1) + 1, and N is the R(N)-th satisfactory number in order; so the machine with D.N N is the R(N)-th circle-free machine in my enumeration — it computes α with that index. H then uses U to simulate that machine far enough to obtain its first R(N) figures, and writes the R(N)-th of them down as the next figure of β′. Then on to section N + 1.

Is H circle-free? Each section terminates: D always halts (that's the assumption), so the test of N finishes in finite time; and when N is satisfactory, the machine it names is circle-free, so its R(N)-th figure really does get produced after finitely many simulated steps, and H writes it and the section ends. There are infinitely many satisfactory numbers — simple periodic printers already give infinitely many descriptions — so H goes on emitting figures forever. H is circle-free.

H is a machine, so it has a description number. Let K be the D.N of H. Now watch what H does in its own K-th section — the section where it tests the integer K, which happens to be itself.

H runs D on K and gets a verdict, "s" or "u." Suppose the verdict is "u," circular. But I just showed H is circle-free, and K is H's D.N, so D has misjudged a circle-free machine as circular — contradiction. So the verdict can't be "u."

Suppose instead the verdict is "s," circle-free. Then by H's own rule, K is satisfactory, K is the R(K)-th satisfactory number, so the machine it names — which is H — is the R(K)-th circle-free machine, and H must now compute the first R(K) figures of that machine and write the R(K)-th as the next figure of β′. But that machine *is H*. So the instruction H is now executing reads: "simulate H, compute its first R(K) figures, write down the R(K)-th." To produce its own R(K)-th figure, H must first run itself up to the production of its R(K)-th figure — which is precisely the act it is in the middle of attempting. It computes the first R(K) − 1 figures fine; the R(K)-th it can never reach, because reaching it requires having already reached it. H stalls at this figure — it stops producing figures. H is circular. But H is circle-free, and the verdict was "s." Contradiction on both counts.

Both verdicts are impossible. And the only thing I assumed was D. So there is no D. No machine decides, from a machine's description, whether that machine is circle-free. And now I see why I took the *unflipped* diagonal: it routes the contradiction straight through the meaning of "circle-free." H doesn't fail because its digit has to equal one minus itself. H fails because, told by D that it keeps going forever, it is thereby obligated to wait for its own output and so it stops going — the self-application turns "circle-free" into "circular" on the spot. Building β = 1 − φ_n(n) would also refute the assumed sorting process, but β′ exposes the exact failure: deciding non-stalling is what makes the machine stall.

I have undecidability of circle-free. But the decision problem I ultimately care about, Hilbert's, is about a logical formula, and a formula will naturally talk about *events* in a computation — a symbol appearing somewhere — not directly about the global "runs forever" property. So I should first convert the circle-free result into something about a print event, because that's what a formula can pin to.

No machine E can decide, given an arbitrary machine's S.D, whether that machine ever prints a particular symbol, say 0. Suppose E existed. From a machine M build M₁, which follows M except that where M's *first* 0 stands, M₁ prints a marked, non-figure 0̄; build M₂, which also strikes the second 0; and so on — M_k follows M but turns its first k zeros into the marked symbol so they no longer count as the figure 0. This construction is mechanical for each fixed k: keep a finite counter up to k, mark the first k zeros, then copy M's later behavior. Then "M prints 0 infinitely often" is exactly "every one of M₁, M₂, M₃, … eventually prints 0": if M has only finitely many zeros, some M_k has had all of them struck and never prints 0; if M has infinitely many, every M_k still has zeros left to print.

But I must not decide an infinite conjunction by waiting through infinitely many tests. I need E to compress it into one finite question. So I build a new machine X_M. It writes the S.D of M₁ and asks E about it; if E says M₁ never prints 0, X_M prints 0 and may stop, while if E says M₁ does print 0, X_M erases the work and goes on to M₂. Then it tests M₂ the same way, printing 0 exactly if E says M₂ never prints 0, and so on. Each stage is finite because E always gives a verdict. Now feed X_M itself to E. If E says X_M ever prints 0, then for some k, M_k never prints 0, so M had only finitely many zeros. If E says X_M never prints 0, then every M_k still eventually prints 0, so M prints 0 infinitely often. Thus E decides whether M prints 0 infinitely often. The same construction with 1 decides whether M prints 1 infinitely often. A machine prints infinitely many figures — is circle-free — exactly when it prints 0 infinitely often or 1 infinitely often, since the figures are only 0 and 1. So E would let me decide circle-free, which I just proved impossible. Hence no E: no machine decides whether an arbitrary machine ever prints 0.

Throughout I've been treating "there is a general process for determining…" as "there is a machine that determines…." That identification is the whole reason any of this bears on Hilbert, and it's licensed by exactly the human-computer analysis I started from: the machine captures all effective methods, so "no machine can" *is* "no effective method can." If that identification failed, the machines would be a curiosity. Because it holds, an undecidability about machines is an undecidability about methods, full stop.

Now to Hilbert. I want: no machine, given an arbitrary first-order formula 𝔄, decides whether 𝔄 is provable in the functional calculus K. I'll get it by a reduction — I'll attach to each machine M a single formula Un(M) and arrange that Un(M) is provable exactly when M ever prints 0. Then a decider for provability would decide "M prints 0," which I've just shown impossible.

The difficulty is that first-order logic has no built-in clock and no built-in tape; I must axiomatize the entire running of M as relations and let logical deduction carry out the computation. Set up predicates for the moving picture of M. Let R_{S_l}(x, y) mean "in complete configuration x, square y bears symbol S_l." Let I(x, y) mean "in complete configuration x, square y is the scanned square." Let K_{q_m}(x) mean "in complete configuration x, the machine is in m-configuration q_m." Let F(x, y) mean "y is the immediate successor of x" in one number-line relation, used both to step from one complete configuration to the next and to name neighboring tape squares. N(x) says x is a non-negative integer, and I need the basic facts that every number has a successor and anything related by F is a number.

Each instruction of M becomes one sentence. Take an instruction "in q_i scanning S_j, print S_k, move left, go to q_l." Write Inst as: for all complete configurations x and squares y, with x′ the successor configuration and y′ the square just to the left of y, *if* at x the machine has m-config q_i and scans y bearing S_j, *then* at x′ square y holds S_k, the scanned square is y′, the m-config is q_l, and *every other square keeps its symbol* — that last conjunct, "for all z other than y, the symbol at z is unchanged from x to x′," is the frame condition that makes the rest of the tape persist. The right-move and no-move instructions get the parallel sentences, using the right neighbor for R and keeping y scanned for N. Conjoin the Inst-sentences for all of M's instructions; call that conjunction Des(M), the description of M inside logic.

Assemble Un(M). Let A_M(u) gather the whole antecedent at a proposed initial index u: N(u); the successor facts (every number has a successor, and F only connects numbers); the blank initial tape, (y)R_{S₀}(u, y); the scanned square I(u, u); the initial m-configuration K_{q₁}(u); and Des(M). Then

`Un(M) = (∃u) A_M(u) -> (∃s)(∃t)[N(s) & N(t) & R_{S₁}(s, t)]`.

Reading R_{S₁} as "bears 0," the consequent says "at some numbered complete configuration s, on some numbered square t, the symbol 0 appears." Under the intended interpretation the antecedent is true — take u as the starting index — so Un(M) says exactly that M eventually prints 0.

Two things to prove, and they're tight inverses.

Lemma 1: if M actually prints 0 — if S₁ appears on some square in some complete configuration of M — then Un(M) is provable in K. I prove it by marching the logic in step with the machine. Let CC_n be the formula spelling out the *actual* n-th complete configuration of M: the finite run of nonblank or relevant squares with their symbols, followed by blanks, together with which square is scanned and which m-configuration holds, all indexed at instant u^{(n)} (the n-fold successor of u). Let F^{(n)} be the chain of successor facts from u to u^{(n)}, and let CF_n be `A_M(u) & F^{(n)} -> CC_n`. I show every CF_n is provable.

CF₀ is provable outright. The antecedent A_M(u) contains exactly the initial facts: every square is blank, the scanned square is u, and the m-configuration is q₁. But that is CC₀, so `A_M(u) -> CC₀` is immediate. For the step CF_n -> CF_{n+1}, CC_n tells me the scanned square y, the scanned symbol S_a, and the current m-configuration q_b. Exactly one table instruction of M matches that pair, and the corresponding Inst-sentence is a conjunct of Des(M). If the move is left, Inst uses the successor relation F(y', y) to name the left neighbour y'; from CC_n it derives that at the next instant the old scanned square y carries the printed symbol, y' is scanned, the new m-configuration holds, and every square z other than y keeps its old symbol by the frame conjunct. That is precisely CC_{n+1}. If the move is right, the same derivation uses F(y, y') to scan the right neighbour. If the move is N, the scanned square remains y and the frame conjunct again carries all other squares across. So in all three cases, `A_M(u) & F^{(n+1)} -> CC_{n+1}` is provable from CF_n and Des(M). By induction every CF_n is provable.

Now use the hypothesis: 0 appears, say at instant N on square K, so CC_N contains R_{S₁}(u^{(N)}, u^{(K)}). Take N' = max(N, K) so the needed successor chain supplies both terms as numbered objects. From A_M(u) and F^{(N')} I derive CC_N, hence R_{S₁}(u^{(N)}, u^{(K)}), hence `(∃s)(∃t)[N(s) & N(t) & R_{S₁}(s, t)]`. Therefore `(∃u)A_M(u)` implies that consequent, which is Un(M). Provable.

Lemma 2: the converse, and it's almost free by soundness. If Un(M) is provable in K, then substitute the intended interpretations — the real tape, the real successor of instants and squares, the real configurations of M — for the predicate symbols. Provable formulas come out true under every interpretation. In this interpretation the antecedent `(∃u)A_M(u)` is true, because M really does have its initial blank configuration and really does follow Des(M). So the consequent must be true: for some complete configuration s and square t, R_{S₁}(s, t). That is just S₁, the symbol 0, appearing on M's tape. So M does print 0.

Put the lemmas together: Un(M) is provable in K *if and only if* M ever prints 0. Suppose now, for contradiction, that the Entscheidungsproblem is solvable — that some machine decides, for every formula, whether it's provable in K. Apply it to the formulas Un(M): I'd have a machine deciding, for every M, whether M ever prints 0. But I proved no such machine exists. So no machine decides provability in K. The Hilbert Entscheidungsproblem has no solution.

I should be exact about how this differs from Gödel, because it's a different claim and I don't want it confused with his. Gödel showed that inside a fixed formal system there are sentences 𝔄 with neither 𝔄 nor ¬𝔄 provable — the system is incomplete — and that the system can't prove its own consistency. That's about the existence of undecided *sentences* in one system. What I've shown is that there is no general *method* that decides, of an arbitrary formula, whether it's provable — undecidability of the decision problem itself. The two even pull in instructive tension: if first-order logic were complete in the sense that for every 𝔄 either 𝔄 or ¬𝔄 were provable, then the proof-enumerator would decide provability — run it until 𝔄 or ¬𝔄 turns up, and since K is consistent, hitting ¬𝔄 means 𝔄 is unprovable. So it's precisely the failure of that kind of completeness that leaves room for undecidability — but the proof of undecidability doesn't go through Gödel's sentence; it goes through the machine and the diagonal.

One sharpening, because the strength of a negative result is in how little it needs. The formula Un(M) can be pushed into prenex form with all quantifiers up front, and the prefix that comes out is short — `(u)(∃x)(w)(∃u₁)...(∃u_n) B`, with u and w universal, B quantifier-free, and n = 6. With unimportant modifications the same essential construction gets n down to 5. So the undecidability already bites at a small, fixed quantifier-prefix class; it isn't a feature of wildly complicated formulas. Even there, no method decides provability.

Let me say the whole chain in one breath, because each link earns the next. I needed to define "effective method" before I could prove none decides provability, so I read the definition off the human computer and out fell the abstract machine — finite control, tape, scanned symbol, behaviour fixed by the pair — whose claim to capture all computation is the finiteness analysis itself. Finite tables encode as finite strings, so machines are countable and each gets a description number, and a single universal machine runs any of them from its description because program and data are the same letters. The naïve Cantor diagonal on the computable sequences contradicts that countability — and the false step is exactly the assumption that I can decide which descriptions are circle-free; so I aim the diagonal at *that* decider: a supposed satisfaction-decider D, fused with U into a circle-free machine H, must misjudge its own description number, because being told it runs forever forces it to wait for its own output and stall. Hence no D — circle-free is undecidable; hence an ever-prints-0 decider would decide infinite zeros by striking successive zeros and asking one finite question about the auxiliary machine X_M, and from infinite zeros or infinite ones it would decide circle-free; and "general process" means "machine" precisely because the machine captures effective method. Finally I encode a machine's entire run as one first-order formula Un(M) whose provability is equivalent, by an induction matching deduction to computation, to the machine's printing 0 — so deciding provability would decide printing-0, which can't be decided. The Entscheidungsproblem is unsolvable.

A concrete machine or two, just to keep the abstraction honest. The simplest circle-free machine prints 010101… forever:

```
# m-configurations b, c, e, f; alphabet {blank, 0, 1}; output on F-squares.
# (q, scanned) -> (operations) ; next m-config.   R = move right.
  b : blank -> P0, R        ; c     # print 0, step right
  c : blank -> R            ; e     # skip the E-square (scratch lane)
  e : blank -> P1, R        ; f     # print 1, step right
  f : blank -> R            ; b     # skip the E-square, loop
# It never reaches a configuration with no move and never stops emitting
# figures, so it is circle-free: it computes the sequence 0101...,
# i.e. the binary number .010101... . Its table, standardized and encoded
# (q_i -> D A^i, S_j -> D C^j, lines joined by ';', then letters -> digits),
# is a finite string, hence an integer -- its description number. That this
# always yields an integer is exactly why the machines are enumerable.
```

And the object the whole argument denies:

```
# D : the satisfaction-decider whose existence is refuted.
def D(candidate_description_number):
    # ASSUMED: always halts; returns 's' iff the number is the D.N of a
    #          circle-free machine, and 'u' otherwise.
    ...

# H : fuse D with the universal machine U into a circle-free machine that
#     emits the diagonal figure phi_n(n). Section N tests the integer N:
def H():
    R = 0                                  # count of satisfactory numbers so far
    for N in 1, 2, 3, ...:                 # each section ends in finite time
        if D(N) == 's':                    # N names a circle-free machine
            R += 1                         # N is the R-th satisfactory number
            digits = U(decode_SD(N), upto=R) # simulate that machine for R figures
            emit(digits[R])               # write its R-th figure as beta'[R]
        # else: N unsatisfactory, R unchanged, move to section N+1
# H is circle-free (every section finite, infinitely many satisfactory N).
```
