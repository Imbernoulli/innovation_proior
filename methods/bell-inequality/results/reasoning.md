Everyone tells me the hidden-variable question is closed. von Neumann proved, back in 1932, that quantum mechanics admits no hidden-variable completion — and that is recited as settled fact, the kind of thing one is not supposed to reopen if one wants to be taken seriously. And yet Bohm, in 1952, simply went ahead and *constructed* a hidden-variable theory that reproduces every quantum prediction. Particle positions as the hidden variables, guided by the wave function. It works. So a theorem says it cannot be done, and a working example does it. One of those two things is wrong, and it is not Bohm's arithmetic. Let me start by understanding exactly what von Neumann actually proved, because if I can see the crack in it, the whole landscape changes.

His proof rests on assuming that the expectation functional is additive: for any two observables R and S, Exp(R + S) = Exp(R) + Exp(S). And he requires this not only of ordinary quantum states but of the hypothetical dispersion-free states — the sharp, hidden-variable states whose ensembles are supposed to average back to the quantum statistics. Stare at the additivity demand. For quantum states it is true; I can check it. But is it a *reasonable* thing to demand of an individual dispersion-free state?

Take a spin-½ particle, the simplest possible case. I can write down a hidden-variable scheme for it explicitly. Let the state be the spinor ψ together with a real number λ uniformly distributed on [−1/2, 1/2]. An observable here has the form α + **β**·**σ**, with α a number and **β** a vector — and for a two-level system that is *every* observable, so reproducing these is reproducing all of them. Bring ψ to the standard up-spin form by rotation. Then the quantum expectation is α + β_z, while the two possible eigenvalues are α ± |**β**|. So assign the dispersion-free value α + |**β**| sign(λ + β_z/(2|**β**|)). The sign is positive on a fraction (1 + β_z/|**β**|)/2 of the interval and negative on the rest, so uniform averaging gives α + |**β**|(β_z/|**β**|) = α + β_z. Numerically, drawing two million λ for, say, α = 0.2 and **β** = (0.3, 0.4, 0.5), the model spits out only the two eigenvalues 0.907 and −0.507 (dispersion-free, as it must be — never an in-between value) and they average to 0.7006, against α + β_z = 0.7; for **β** = (0.6, 0.6, −0.2) the average is 0.001 against α + β_z = 0.0. The averages land on the quantum expectation. So this little model is consistent, it is dispersion-free at each λ, and averaged over λ it reproduces the quantum predictions for one spin. Hidden variables for a single spin plainly exist. Whatever von Neumann's theorem forbids, it cannot be forbidding *this*.

Now in my model, do the dispersion-free states have additive expectation values? Only for *commuting* observables. And of course they do not for non-commuting ones, and there is a perfectly good reason why they should not. The expectation value of σ_x for a magnetic particle is measured with a Stern–Gerlach magnet pointing along x. The expectation value of σ_y needs the magnet pointing along y. And σ_x + σ_y — which as an operator is √2 times a spin component along the diagonal — is measured with the magnet along a *third*, diagonal orientation. These are three different experiments. The dispersion-free state hands a definite ±1 to each, but the value it hands to the σ_x + σ_y experiment has no obligation to be the sum of the values it would have handed to the σ_x and σ_y experiments, because *those were never performed*; a different magnet was. The eigenvalues do not add, only the operators do. So additivity of allowed *values* fails, and rightly so.

Why then does additivity of *expectation values* hold for quantum states? Because the quantum state is a strange averaging device that happens to make the averages add even when the values do not. That is a special, almost magical property of quantum ensembles — not a law of thought. There is no reason on earth to impose it on the individual hidden states, whose only job is to reproduce the quantum averages *after* one averages over λ. von Neumann took a peculiar feature of the quantum averages and quietly demanded it of the very objects whose existence he was testing. The demand smuggles in the conclusion. The "impossibility" proof rules out a class of hidden-variable theories that no one had any reason to want.

I look at Jauch and Piron, and at the corollary one can squeeze out of Gleason's theorem, and it is the same disease in fancier clothing. They speak only of commuting projection operators, which sounds innocent. But the contradiction is driven by an *implicit* assumption: that the value a hidden state assigns to a projection is the same no matter which other compatible measurements it is bundled with — even though different bundles require different apparatus. Reject that, and these no-go theorems collapse too. So the field has been resting on the idea that hidden variables are mathematically impossible, and the idea is simply false. The mathematics forbids only hidden variables that are forced to be context-blind. The real question was never touched.

So which assumption *should* one impose on hidden variables, if not additivity, if not context-independence? The hidden variables ought to have some space-time meaning; they ought to evolve by some law; and — here is the thing Bohm's theory rubs my nose in — they ought, surely, to respect the independence of distant systems. Bohm's own construction is grossly nonlocal: when the joint wave function does not factor, the trajectory of particle 1 depends on the analyzing field set up for particle 2, however far away. He resolves the EPR situation in exactly the way Einstein would have liked least — by explicit action at a distance. Is that an accident of Bohm's particular construction, or is it forced? Is *every* hidden-variable account of quantum mechanics compelled to be nonlocal? Nobody has a proof either way. That is the question worth chasing — and it tells me what to put on the hidden variables in place of additivity: not a property of the quantum averages, but locality, the demand that each particle's outcome answers only to its own nearby apparatus. If I impose that and quantum mechanics still slips through, locality is safe; if I impose it and reach a contradiction, nonlocality is forced. Either way the assumption to interrogate is locality.

Let me get the EPR argument straight in its cleanest form, because I think locality is doing more work in it than people credit. Bohm's spin version: a pair in the singlet, flying apart to two Stern–Gerlach magnets. When the magnets are aligned, the outcomes are always opposite — perfect anticorrelation. Now the EPR step. I measure the spin of particle 1 along **a** and get, say, +1. Then I can predict with certainty that particle 2 along **a** will give −1. If I hold to local causality — that turning or reading magnet 1 cannot influence what happens at magnet 2, arbitrarily far away — then the result for particle 2 was not made by my measurement; it was already settled. And since I could have chosen **a** to be any direction, the result of measuring *any* component of particle 2 was settled in advance. The quantum state does not settle individual outcomes; therefore there is a more complete specification — call its content λ. 

I want to be careful about one thing, because it is the point everyone misreads. I am *not* assuming determinism. I am *deriving* it. The premises are local causality plus the perfect anticorrelation that quantum mechanics predicts. From those two, predetermination follows — because if the outcome on side 2 carried any residual randomness of its own, that randomness could not be coordinated with side 1 without a signal between them, and the perfect correlation would be spoiled. Determinism is the *conclusion* of the locality argument, not its premise. Hold onto that; it means the inequality I am about to look for is an inequality on *local* theories, and the determinism rides in for free.

Now I write the local hidden-variable theory down with no charity withheld. The result of measuring particle 1 along **a** is a function A(**a**, λ) taking values ±1. The result of measuring particle 2 along **b** is B(**b**, λ), also ±1. And the vital assumption, the one carrying all the physics: A depends on **a** and λ but *not* on **b**; B depends on **b** and λ but *not* on **a**. The setting of the distant magnet does not enter the local outcome. λ is whatever it needs to be — one variable, many variables, fields, functions; it makes no difference. It is distributed by some probability density ρ(λ), and ρ does not depend on **a** or **b**, because the source spits out the pair before, and independently of, how I choose to set the far-apart magnets.

The measurable thing is the correlation — the expectation of the product of the two outcomes:

  P(**a**, **b**) = ∫ dλ ρ(λ) A(**a**, λ) B(**b**, λ).

And I know what quantum mechanics says this should be for the singlet. Let me compute that, because it is the number the local theory has to match. The observable is (σ₁·**a**)(σ₂·**b**), and the singlet is |ψ⟩ = (1/√2)(|↑↓⟩ − |↓↑⟩). The clean way: the singlet is rotationally invariant, so ⟨ψ|(σ₁·**a**)(σ₂·**b**)|ψ⟩ can only depend on **a**·**b**. Better, ⟨ψ| σ₁^i σ₂^j |ψ⟩ = −δ_ij for the singlet — the two spins are perfectly anti-aligned in every component. Check the diagonal piece directly with both along z: (σ₁_z)(σ₂_z) acting on |↑↓⟩ gives (+1)(−1) = −1, and on |↓↑⟩ gives (−1)(+1) = −1, while the cross terms |↑↓⟩↔|↓↑⟩ get no contribution from the diagonal σ_zσ_z, so the expectation is (1/2)(−1)+(1/2)(−1) = −1 = −δ_zz. So

  ⟨(σ₁·**a**)(σ₂·**b**)⟩ = Σ_ij a_i b_j (−δ_ij) = −**a**·**b** = −cos θ.

So P(**a**, **b**) = −cos θ, with θ the angle between the magnets: at θ = 0, P = −1, the perfect anticorrelation; a smooth cosine in between.

Can a local theory of the form above produce −cos θ? Before I worry about whether it can, let me check that the form is not vacuous — that it reproduces at least the *easy* features people quote in arguments, the perfect anticorrelation at **a** = **b** and P = 0 at **a**⊥**b**. The most natural local model I can write: let λ be a unit vector uniform over the sphere, the common "axis" the source emits, and set A(**a**, λ) = sign(**a**·λ), B(**b**, λ) = −sign(**b**·λ) — each particle deflects according to whether the shared axis points more along or against its own local field, with the minus sign building in the anticorrelation. Honestly local: A sees only **a**, B sees only **b**.

What does this give? I do not want to guess the angle dependence, so let me actually average it. With **a** along z and **b** at angle θ, P = −⟨sign(cos α_a) sign(cos α_b)⟩ over λ uniform on the sphere, where α_a, α_b are the angles from λ to **a**, **b**. The product sign(**a**·λ)sign(**b**·λ) is +1 when λ falls in the two spherical caps where it agrees with both **a** and **b**, and −1 in the lune between the hemispheres **a**·λ>0 and **b**·λ>0. The disagreement lune has area proportional to θ, so the disagreement probability is θ/π and the agreement probability 1 − θ/π; then ⟨sign·sign⟩ = (1 − θ/π) − (θ/π) = 1 − 2θ/π, and P = −(1 − 2θ/π) = −1 + 2θ/π. Sampling it directly — drawing four million λ uniform on the sphere and averaging A·B at θ = 30°, 45°, 60°, 90°, 135° — gives −0.667, −0.500, −0.333, −0.000, +0.500, dead on −1 + 2θ/π = −0.667, −0.500, −0.333, 0.000, +0.500. So the model's correlation is exactly the straight line −1 + 2θ/π, a triangle wave in the angle. It hits −1 at θ = 0 and 0 at θ = π/2, just as required, and it is honestly local. So the local form easily mimics the headline correlations.

Now lay the straight line beside the quantum cosine. They agree at θ = 0, π/2, π — the three points the old debate fixed on. But in between they part, and not subtly: at θ = 45° the local model gives −0.500 while −cos 45° = −0.707; at θ = 30° it gives −0.667 against the cosine's −0.866. The trouble, if there is trouble, lives precisely in the interior, where the straight line sags below the cosine — and the interior is exactly what the discussion at 0, π/2, π never examined. This was the first model I dreamt up and it worked up to a point. The instinct now is to be cleverer — find a better local model that hugs the cosine all the way around, not just at three angles. Let me see whether that instinct can possibly succeed, instead of chasing models one at a time forever.

Go back to P(**a**, **b**) = ∫ dλ ρ A(**a**, λ) B(**b**, λ), and use what the perfect anticorrelation forces. P(**a**, **a**) = −1 with A, B = ±1 is only possible if A(**a**, λ) = −B(**a**, λ) for almost every λ — equality of a ±1 average to its extreme value pins down the integrand. So I may eliminate B in favor of A: B(**b**, λ) = −A(**b**, λ), and

  P(**a**, **b**) = − ∫ dλ ρ(λ) A(**a**, λ) A(**b**, λ).

Now bring in a *third* direction **c** and compare two correlations:

  P(**a**, **b**) − P(**a**, **c**) = − ∫ dλ ρ [ A(**a**,λ)A(**b**,λ) − A(**a**,λ)A(**c**,λ) ].

I want to factor the bracket. A(**b**, λ)² = 1 because A is ±1, so A(**a**,λ)A(**c**,λ) = A(**a**,λ)A(**b**,λ) · A(**b**,λ)A(**c**,λ). Pull A(**a**,λ)A(**b**,λ) out:

  P(**a**, **b**) − P(**a**, **c**) = − ∫ dλ ρ A(**a**,λ)A(**b**,λ) [ 1 − A(**b**,λ)A(**c**,λ) ].

Take absolute values. The prefactor |A(**a**,λ)A(**b**,λ)| = 1. The bracket [1 − A(**b**,λ)A(**c**,λ)] is non-negative, since A(**b**)A(**c**) is at most 1. So I can drop the prefactor and the absolute value bars on a non-negative quantity:

  |P(**a**, **b**) − P(**a**, **c**)| ≤ ∫ dλ ρ [ 1 − A(**b**,λ)A(**c**,λ) ] = ∫ dλ ρ − ∫ dλ ρ A(**b**,λ)A(**c**,λ).

The first integral is 1, because ρ is normalized. The second is −P(**b**, **c**) by the eliminated-B formula. Therefore

  |P(**a**, **b**) − P(**a**, **c**)| ≤ 1 + P(**b**, **c**).

Three directions, three correlations, and the chain that produced this never once chose a particular λ or a particular ρ — it used only A, B = ±1, the anticorrelation B = −A, and normalization. So whatever it constrains, it constrains every local theory of the singlet at once, no matter how λ is built or distributed. That is more than I get from testing models one at a time: it is a single bound that no amount of cleverness in designing λ can slip past.

Does quantum mechanics obey it? Substitute P = −cos θ and look at how the two sides scale near the perfect-correlation point. Hold **a** fixed, put **b** at angle φ from **a** and **c** a small angle ε beyond **b**, so **b** and **c** are ε apart. The left side is |cos(φ+ε) − cos φ| ≈ |sin φ| · ε — it grows *linearly* in ε. The right side is 1 − cos ε ≈ ε²/2 — it grows only *quadratically*. For small ε a linear term must overtake a quadratic one, so wherever sin φ ≠ 0 the inequality should fail, and fail harder the smaller ε gets. Taking φ = 45° and ε = 10°, 5°, 2°, 1°, the numbers are LHS = 0.1335, 0.0643, 0.0251, 0.0125 against RHS = 0.0152, 0.0038, 0.0006, 0.0002 — ratios LHS/RHS = 8.8, 16.9, 41.2, 81.7. The violation does not close as ε → 0; it *widens*, doubling each time ε halves, exactly the linear-over-quadratic signature. The geometric reason is plain once the numbers show it: the cosine is *stationary* at its minimum −1, coming in with zero slope, while the local correlation is *kinked* there, coming in at a finite slope. That mismatch in slope is the whole story, and it is exactly what the easy-angle discussion missed by only ever standing *at* the special points and never looking at the curvature through them.

A single clean instance pins it down. Put **b** and **c** at 90° and 45° from **a**, with **b** and **c** themselves 45° apart: **a**·**c** = 0, **a**·**b** = 1/√2, **b**·**c** = 1/√2. Then P(**a**,**b**) = −1/√2, P(**a**,**c**) = −cos90° = 0, P(**b**,**c**) = −1/√2, so |P(**a**,**b**) − P(**a**,**c**)| = |−1/√2 − 0| = 0.707, while 1 + P(**b**,**c**) = 1 − 1/√2 = 0.293. And 0.707 > 0.293 — a clean factor of 2.4 over the local ceiling. No local hidden-variable theory can reproduce the quantum cosine; the disagreement is not at the edges, it is gaping, in the ordinary interior of the angle range. The EPR completion that Einstein wanted — local, with predetermined values — is not merely inelegant. It makes a different prediction from quantum mechanics, and the prediction can be checked.

I notice the proof leaned on perfect anticorrelation to eliminate B = −A. That is fine for an ideal derivation but cruel in the laboratory. No real magnet pair gives exactly P(**a**,**a**) = −1; detectors miss particles, polarizers leak, geometry is imperfect. If the whole argument hangs on an idealization that no apparatus reaches, an experimenter can always wriggle out — "your inequality assumes a perfection I never had." I should redo the argument so that it never assumes perfect correlation, only the bare local form with bounded outcomes. If I can do that, the test no longer rests on exact anticorrelation.

Drop the elimination B = −A entirely. Keep only A(**a**,λ), B(**b**,λ) bounded by 1 in magnitude — I will not even insist they are exactly ±1, since a real "outcome" might be an imperfect detection. Use *four* settings: two on each side, **a** and **a′** for the first analyzer, **b** and **b′** for the second. Form the combination

  S = P(**a**,**b**) − P(**a**,**b′**) + P(**a′**,**b**) + P(**a′**,**b′**).

Write each as the integral and collect:

  S = ∫ dλ ρ(λ) { A(**a**,λ)[B(**b**,λ) − B(**b′**,λ)] + A(**a′**,λ)[B(**b**,λ) + B(**b′**,λ)] }.

Now look at the integrand pointwise, for a fixed λ, with the ±1 case to build intuition. B(**b**,λ) and B(**b′**,λ) are each ±1. So their sum B(**b**) + B(**b′**) is either ±2 or 0, and in the case it is ±2 the difference B(**b**) − B(**b′**) is 0, while in the case the sum is 0 the difference is ±2 — always *one* of the two brackets is zero and the other is ±2. Since A(**a**) and A(**a′**) are ±1, the whole integrand is ±2 at every λ. Checking all sixteen ±1 corner assignments of A, A′, B, B′: the integrand A(B−B′) + A′(B+B′) reaches a maximum magnitude of exactly 2, never more. More generally, with |A| ≤ 1 and |B| ≤ 1: A(**a**)[B(**b**)−B(**b′**)] + A(**a′**)[B(**b**)+B(**b′**)] is bounded in magnitude by |B(**b**)−B(**b′**)| + |B(**b**)+B(**b′**)|, and for any two numbers in [−1,1] that sum of the magnitudes of their sum and difference is at most 2 — I sample two hundred thousand random (A, A′, B, B′) in [−1,1]⁴ and the largest |integrand| I see is 1.986, edging up to but never reaching 2, the extreme living at the ±1 corners. So the integrand never exceeds 2 in absolute value, and

  |S| = |∫ dλ ρ · (integrand)| ≤ ∫ dλ ρ · 2 = 2.

  |P(**a**,**b**) − P(**a**,**b′**) + P(**a′**,**b**) + P(**a′**,**b′**)| ≤ 2.

No perfect correlation anywhere in this. No B = −A elimination. Just locality — A blind to **b**, B blind to **a** — bounded outcomes, and a shared λ. The bound of 2 is the wall every local theory must stay behind. Detector losses still have to be included honestly in the measured outcomes or rates, but exact anticorrelation is no longer part of the logic. This is the form an experiment can actually use: measure four correlations at two settings per side, add them with that one minus sign, and ask whether the total exceeds 2.

What does quantum mechanics give for S? Back to P = −cos(angle). I need to place four directions to push S past 2. Coplanar, by symmetry. Try **a** = 0°, **a′** = 90°, **b** = 45°, **b′** = 135°. The relative angles: (**a**,**b**) = 45°, (**a**,**b′**) = 135°, (**a′**,**b**) = 45°, (**a′**,**b′**) = 45°. So

  P(**a**,**b**) = −cos45° = −1/√2,
  P(**a**,**b′**) = −cos135° = +1/√2, because cos135° = −1/√2,
  P(**a′**,**b**) = −cos45° = −1/√2,
  P(**a′**,**b′**) = −cos45° = −1/√2.

  S = (−1/√2) − (+1/√2) + (−1/√2) + (−1/√2) = −4/√2 = −2√2.

|S| = 2√2 ≈ 2.828. Every contribution to S came out −1/√2 — the lone minus sign in S sits on the term whose angle is 135°, the one with the *positive* correlation +1/√2, so −(+1/√2) lines up with the other three negative pieces and they add coherently instead of cancelling. The geometry is doing exactly what it must: the four settings, each 45° from the next, arrange the cosines so that the combination piles up rather than averaging out. And 2√2 sails clean over 2, by a factor of √2, before any particular model for λ even enters.

It would be wrong to dignify the local-theory bound as a deep theorem; it is, line by line, almost embarrassingly trivial. Each member of the third correlation either does or does not have a certain property, and is thereby counted in one of the other two — the kind of inequality one proves about socks. That triviality is the point. From premises that any classical realist would sign without hesitation — that the two particles carry, in advance, properties fixing how they respond to each local measurement, and that the far setting does not reach across — one derives a constraint of utter banality, and quantum mechanics breaks it. So it is not that the local picture is complicated and quantum mechanics is simple; it is that the simplest, most reasonable local picture makes a quantitatively wrong prediction.

Nothing in the four-setting argument actually used spin-½, or particles, or magnets. Strip it down: a long box, a "go" signal, two distant ends; just before the outputs appear, a setting a is injected at one end and b at the other, too late for any light-speed signal to cross the box; out come two results A and B. The only assumption that the correlation is "locally explicable" is that there exist variables λ with P(A, B | a, b, λ) = P(A | a, λ) · P(B | b, λ) — the joint probability factors, with the first factor independent of b and the second of a — averaged over some ρ(λ). That factorization *is* local causality: holding λ fixed, the result at one end is statistically decoupled from the setting at the other. Define the correlation E(a, b) = P(yes,yes) + P(no,no) − P(yes,no) − P(no,yes); it equals ∫ dλ ρ(λ) Ā(a,λ) B̄(b,λ) with |Ā|, |B̄| ≤ 1, and the same four-term argument gives |E(a,b) − E(a,b′) + E(a′,b) + E(a′,b′)| ≤ 2. The inequality is about correlations in the visible outputs of conceivable experiments — nothing about spins, fields, or even the word "system." That generality is what makes it a *test of locality itself*, not of any model.

So the EPR debate is not a matter of taste after all. Locality plus predetermination — and the predetermination is itself a consequence of locality given the perfect correlations — forces an inequality on the correlations of distant measurements. Quantum mechanics predicts a number that breaks it; the experimenter's task is to measure the correlations without letting losses or setting choices reopen a local explanation. Either quantum mechanics is wrong in these critical configurations, which one can now go and check, or there is, in the world, a genuine non-locality: the result at one end depends on the setting at the other, however far apart, and — if the settings are switched during the particles' flight — faster than light could carry the news. The question Einstein, Podolsky and Rosen raised has been turned into one an experimenter answers, by setting two analyzers at the right angles and counting coincidences.
