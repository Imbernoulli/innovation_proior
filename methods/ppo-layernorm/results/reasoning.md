Let me start from what actually slows me down. I train deep nets with SGD and it takes days, and the two brute-force speedups — spread the data over machines, spread the model over machines — cost me communication and complicated software and stop paying off as I add workers. There's a cleaner lever sitting right in front of me: change the computation inside the forward pass so each step makes more progress. The reason that's even possible is a specific pain I keep hitting. The gradient on layer l's weights depends on the activations coming up from layer l-1, and while I train, those lower activations don't sit still — they drift, and worse, they drift in a *correlated* way, all sliding together, especially with ReLU units whose outputs can lurch by a lot. So every layer above is solving a moving-target problem: it adapts to a distribution of inputs that has changed by the time the adaptation lands. The usual band-aids — ReLUs, careful init, small learning rate — keep me out of the saturated regime but the price is that small learning rate, and that's exactly the thing throttling my training speed. What I want is a per-layer transform, cheap, in the forward pass, that holds the distribution of a layer's summed inputs steady as the weights below it move, so I can crank the learning rate without diverging.

The strongest thing on the table for this is batch normalization. Its move is to take each summed input a_i — the i-th coordinate of Wh, the pre-nonlinearity value for unit i — and standardize it across the training cases: subtract the mean of a_i over the data, divide by its standard deviation over the data, then put back a learned per-neuron gain and bias before the nonlinearity. In symbols, abar_i = (g_i/sigma_i)(a_i - mu_i) + b_i, with mu_i = E_x[a_i] and sigma_i = sqrt(E_x[(a_i-mu_i)^2]). Two things about this I want to keep, because they're genuinely good ideas, not accidents. First, the learned g_i and b_i aren't decoration: if I force every a_i to mean zero and variance one I've thrown away the network's freedom to choose the scale and offset of each unit, and the affine puts that freedom back — in fact setting g_i = sigma_i and b_i = E[a_i] recovers the original activations exactly, so the transform can always represent the identity and never costs me capacity. Second, and this is the part I really want to understand, BN is what lets you turn the learning rate up. Watch what scaling a weight does. Because the normalization divides by sigma, which scales with the magnitude of Wh, you get BN(Wu) = BN((aW)u) — multiply the weights by a and the normalized output doesn't move. Differentiate: d BN((aW)u)/d(aW) = (1/a) d BN(Wu)/dW. So if a weight grows, the layer's function is unchanged but the gradient flowing to that weight *shrinks* by 1/a. Big weights take smaller steps automatically. That's an implicit stabilizer — it's why a large learning rate that would normally blow the weights up instead settles, because the moment a weight gets large its own gradient backs off. I want that property in whatever I build.

But the way BN gets its mu and sigma is from the current minibatch, because the true data expectations are impractical to compute, and that single implementation choice is where everything cracks for me. Walk the constraints. If my minibatch is small, the minibatch mean and variance are noisy estimates and the normalization gets unreliable; at batch size one it's nonsense — a single number has no spread to divide by. So online learning is out, and so is the regime where a model is so large each worker only holds a handful of cases. Then there's train versus test: at test time I don't have a minibatch, so BN has to switch to running averages of mu and sigma accumulated during training. Now my train-time computation and my test-time computation are literally different functions, and I'm carrying stored statistics per layer to bridge them. And the one that kills it outright: recurrent nets. In an RNN the same weights act at every time step, and the summed input a^t to a recurrent unit depends on t. If I apply BN the obvious way I need separate statistics for each time step. But sequences have different lengths, and a test sequence longer than any training sequence has time steps for which I never collected statistics. That's not an edge case in language or handwriting, that's Tuesday. So BN is fast and principled but its statistic is computed over the wrong axis for everything I care about beyond fixed-depth feed-forward nets.

Is there a fix that already avoids the batch entirely? Weight normalization reparameterizes each weight vector as w = (g/||v||)v, splitting length from direction so the optimizer learns the magnitude g and the direction v separately. It touches only the weights, so it has no cross-case dependence at all — it works in RNNs, in RL, with tiny noisy batches, and it's cheap. In the common language where a normalization divides a_i by some sigma_i and subtracts some mu_i, weight norm is the choice mu_i = 0, sigma_i = ||w_i||_2. And that's exactly where it leaves something on the table for me: it never re-centers (mu = 0), and it normalizes by the *weight* norm, a quantity computed from the parameters alone. It is blind to the actual activations the layer is seeing. The whole reason I wanted normalization was to hold the *activation distribution* steady against the correlated drift from below; dividing by ||w|| doesn't measure that distribution at all. So weight norm gives me the batch-independence I need but throws away the activation-statistic stabilization that was the point.

So here's the tension, stated cleanly. BN measures the activation distribution and stabilizes it, but it measures it across the batch, and the batch axis is exactly what breaks for small batches, online learning, and RNNs. Weight norm escapes the batch axis but stops measuring activations. I want both: a statistic computed from the actual summed inputs, the way BN does it, but computed along an axis that has nothing to do with the batch.

Let me stare at BN's statistic and ask what axes are even available. For a given layer I have a tensor of summed inputs indexed by (training case n, unit i). BN computes, for each unit i, the mean over n — it averages down the batch axis, one statistic per unit, shared across cases. The complaint is entirely about that "over n." What's the other axis? For a single training case n, I have the whole vector of summed inputs across the units, (a_1, a_2, ..., a_H). What if I compute the mean and variance over *i* — over the units in the layer — for that single case? Then

  mu = (1/H) sum_{i=1}^H a_i ,   sigma = sqrt( (1/H) sum_{i=1}^H (a_i - mu)^2 ) ,

one mu and one sigma per training case, shared across all the units in the layer. This is the mirror image of BN: BN shares a unit's statistic across cases; this shares a case's statistic across units. And look what it costs me on the constraints. The statistic depends only on the H summed inputs at the current layer for this one case — no other training case enters, ever. So batch size is irrelevant: it's identical at batch size one, batch size four, batch size a thousand. There is no minibatch quantity to estimate, so there's nothing to replace with a running average at test time — the computation is the same function at train and test. And in an RNN I just compute mu^t, sigma^t from the summed inputs a^t at the current time step; nothing depends on t having appeared in training, so a longer test sequence is fine, and I keep one set of gain and bias shared over all time steps. Every single thing that broke about BN's batch axis is gone, because I moved the averaging onto the feature axis.

Now I have to be honest about whether this statistic is even *meaningful*, because it's not obvious that averaging across units is a sane thing to do — the units in a layer are different functions, why should their summed inputs share a mean? Here's the argument that rescues it, and it's the same correlated-drift observation I started from. When the layer below changes, the change in its output causes *highly correlated* changes in the summed inputs to all the units of this layer — especially with ReLUs, where a shift downstairs moves many of these a_i together and by a lot. So the thing I'm fighting — the drift — is largely a shared, layer-wide shift in the a_i. A single per-layer mu and sigma is exactly the right shape of object to absorb a shared, layer-wide shift. I'm not claiming the units are interchangeable; I'm claiming the *nuisance* I want to remove is common across them, and a common statistic removes a common nuisance. The per-unit gain and bias I'll add back are what let each unit re-individualize afterward.

So the transform is: take the summed inputs a, compute the single mu and sigma over the H features, standardize, then apply a per-neuron gain and bias before the nonlinearity,

  h_i = f( (g_i/sigma)(a_i - mu) + b_i ).

The gain and bias serve the same restore-capacity role they did in BN — forcing mean zero, variance one across features would otherwise strip the layer's ability to set per-unit scale and offset, and g, b give it back. I'll default them to g_i = 1, b_i = 0, so the transform starts life as pure normalization and the network learns away from it if it wants to. And I'll keep an epsilon inside the square root, sigma = sqrt((1/H) sum (a_i - mu)^2 + eps), because if a layer's summed inputs happen to be nearly constant across units then this variance collapses toward zero and I'd divide by it; the eps floors that. It's a numerical guard, so it wants to be small — something like 1e-5, well below any healthy spread of activations. One more small choice that I should pin down rather than wave at: the variance here is the plain (1/H) sum, dividing by H not H-1. The H-1 correction is for when you're estimating an unknown population variance from a sample and want an unbiased estimate of *that population*. That's not what I'm doing. I have the actual vector in hand and I literally want to rescale *this* vector to unit root-mean-square deviation; the population I'd be generalizing to doesn't enter. So the biased, divide-by-H form is the natural one, not a compromise.

There's a subtlety about *where* I insert this that I want to get right, because it's tempting to normalize the layer's input instead of its summed input. Suppose I normalized h (the input) before multiplying by W, rather than normalizing a = Wh after. Then a re-scaling or re-centering of the weight matrix would pass straight through and change the output — I'd lose the self-stabilizing weight-scale property I admired in BN. Normalizing the post-linear summed input a is what makes the transform see, and cancel, changes in the weights. So: apply it to Wh, after the linear map, before the nonlinearity. That's also the spot BN used, and for the same reason.

Let me make this concrete for the recurrent case, since that's the setting I most want to win. A vanilla RNN computes a^t = W_hh h^{t-1} + W_xh x^t, then h^t = f(a^t). I drop the transform straight onto a^t:

  h^t = f[ (g/sigma^t) ⊙ (a^t - mu^t) + b ] ,   mu^t = (1/H) sum_i a_i^t ,   sigma^t = sqrt((1/H) sum_i (a_i^t - mu^t)^2) ,

with one g and one b shared across all time steps. Now I want to check this actually does something good to the recurrent dynamics, not just that it's well-defined. In a plain RNN the average magnitude of the summed inputs to the recurrent units tends to either grow or shrink at every step, compounding over the sequence — that's the mechanism behind exploding and vanishing gradients in the first place. With this transform, scaling all of a^t by any positive scalar c leaves the normalized output unchanged: mu^t scales by c, sigma^t scales by c, and (c a^t - c mu^t)/(c sigma^t) = (a^t - mu^t)/sigma^t. So the layer is invariant to a uniform positive rescaling of all its summed inputs, which is precisely the runaway-or-collapse mode. The transform pins the scale of the hidden-to-hidden signal at every step, so the dynamics stay stable along the whole sequence. That's the recurrent payoff falling out directly from the rescaling invariance.

That invariance is interesting enough that I want to map it out fully and compare it against BN and weight norm, because the *pattern* of invariances is what really distinguishes these methods — they all look like "divide by sigma, subtract mu" but they're invariant to different things, and that's what predicts how they behave. Let me work through each transform under each transformation of weights or data.

Take a re-scaling of the entire weight matrix, W -> delta·W. Under my feature-wise transform, a -> delta·a, so mu -> delta·mu and sigma -> delta·sigma, and (delta a - delta mu)/(delta sigma) is unchanged: invariant. BN: same story per unit, mu_i and sigma_i both scale by delta, invariant. Weight norm: ||w|| scales by delta and a scales by delta, ratio unchanged, invariant. Everyone passes this one.

Now re-*centering* the whole weight matrix — add the same vector to every row, or equivalently W -> delta·W + 1·gamma^T with positive scale delta, so a_i -> delta·a_i + gamma^T x, a shared additive shift of every summed input by the same amount gamma^T x. Under my transform that shared shift is *exactly* what the per-feature mean removes: mu picks up the same gamma^T x, and (a_i - mu) is unchanged. Let me write it out to be sure. With W' = delta W + 1 gamma^T,

  h' = f( (g/sigma')( W' x - mu' ) + b ) = f( (g/sigma')( (delta W + 1 gamma^T) x - mu' ) + b ).

The added 1 gamma^T x is a constant vector across the units, so it shifts every (W'x)_i by gamma^T x and shifts mu' by the same gamma^T x; the difference (W'x - mu') = delta(Wx) + 1 gamma^T x - (delta mu + gamma^T x) = delta(Wx - mu). Then dividing by sigma' = delta·sigma gives (Wx - mu)/sigma, so h' = f((g/sigma)(Wx - mu) + b) = h. Invariant to re-centering the whole weight matrix. Does BN have this? BN's mu_i is per unit, computed across cases, and a per-unit shift gamma^T x that differs per case is not removed by a per-unit-across-cases mean — BN is *not* invariant to weight re-centering. Neither is weight norm (it never subtracts a mean). So I have an invariance the other two lack.

But there's a flip side I should not hide from. Re-scale a *single* weight vector w_i -> delta·w_i, just one neuron. Under BN and weight norm, that neuron's own sigma_i (its across-batch std, or its ||w_i||) scales by delta and cancels it — invariant per neuron. Under my transform, sigma is shared across the whole layer, computed from all the units; scaling one unit's weights changes that one a_i but the shared sigma is dominated by all the others, so it does *not* cancel — I am *not* invariant to scaling an individual weight vector. So I've traded per-neuron weight-scale invariance (which BN/WN have) for whole-matrix re-centering invariance and per-case invariance (which they don't). It's a genuine trade, not a free lunch, and it's worth stating plainly.

The two that really matter for me are about the *data*. Re-scale the whole dataset x -> delta·x by a positive scalar: every method has a -> delta·a one way or another and the ratio survives, all invariant. But now re-scale a *single training case*, x -> delta·x for this one example. Under my transform, mu and sigma for this case both scale by delta (they're computed from this case's own summed inputs), so

  h_i' = f( (g_i/sigma')( w_i^T (delta x) - mu' ) + b_i ) = f( (g_i/(delta sigma))( delta w_i^T x - delta mu ) + b_i ) = f( (g_i/sigma)( w_i^T x - mu ) + b_i ) = h_i.

Invariant to re-scaling individual training cases. BN cannot have this — its statistics are across cases, so rescaling one case doesn't get its own matched mu/sigma; it's *not* invariant to per-case rescaling. This is the property that says the transform is robust to one example arriving at a different overall scale than the rest, which is exactly the kind of nuisance variation I'd love to be immune to. So the picture, laid out: BN is invariant to dataset re-centering and per-unit weight rescaling but not to per-case rescaling; weight norm is invariant to weight/dataset rescaling but re-centers nothing; my transform is the only one invariant to whole-matrix weight re-centering and to single-case rescaling, at the cost of per-unit weight-vector rescaling. The methods are not the same dressed differently — they sit at different points in invariance space, and mine sits at the point that matches "stabilize a single case's activations regardless of batch."

Now I want the deeper "why does this help learning" story, not just "it's invariant to nuisances." The right tool is the geometry of the parameter space — how an update moves the *function*, measured by KL between output distributions, which to second order is delta^T F delta with F the Fisher information. I'll work it on a generalized linear model, because the result lifts to a deep net under a block-diagonal approximation to F where each block is one neuron's parameters. A GLM with summed input a = w^T x and bias b has log P(y|x;w,b) = ((a+b)y - eta(a+b))/phi + c(y,phi), mean E[y|x] = f(a+b), and the Fisher with respect to theta = vec([W, b]^T) for an H-dimensional output of independent GLMs is the expected Kronecker product of the data features with the output covariance,

  F(theta) = E_x[ Cov[Y|x]/phi^2 ⊗ [[x x^T, x],[x^T, 1]] ].

Now apply the normalization and add the gain parameters, theta = vec([W, b, g]^T), and call the resulting Fisher Fbar. The weight-weight block in the normalized GLM carries the gain and normalization factors as g_i g_j/(sigma_i sigma_j), with the derivative of the normalized input folded into the effective input vector. Here's the consequence I want, stated at the level the derivation actually supports. Suppose ||w_i|| doubles along a scale-invariant direction. The model output doesn't change — that's the weight-scale invariance I already proved — but the relevant sigma_i doubles with the summed-input scale, so the curvature along the w_i direction shrinks; the directional curvature changes by a factor of 1/2 in that doubling case. Same parameter update, but it moves the function *less* when ||w_i|| is large. That is an implicit learning-rate reduction tied to the norm of the weight vector: as a weight grows, it becomes harder to change its orientation, so learning naturally slows and stabilizes as it proceeds — an "early-stopping"-like effect baked into the geometry, the same self-stabilization I admired in BN's gradient-shrinking-with-scale, now read off the Fisher.

There's a second, cleaner geometric statement hiding in the gain direction, and I'll derive its punchline. I want to compare two ways of changing the magnitude of a neuron's incoming weights: in the normalized model I move the gain g_i; in the standard model I move ||w_i|| directly, by stepping along delta_gi · w_i/||w_i||. Project the Fisher onto each. For the standard GLM, the metric along the weight-magnitude direction comes out proportional to E_x[ Cov(y_i,y_j|x) · a_i a_j/(||w_i|| ||w_j||) ] — it depends on the *activities* a_i = w_i^T x, hence on both the current weights and the input magnitude. For the normalized model, project onto the gain: vec([0,0,delta_g]^T)^T Fbar vec([0,0,delta_g]^T), and because the gain direction multiplies the already-normalized (a_i - mu)/sigma, the input-and-weight-dependent pieces have been divided out — what's left is

  ds^2 = (1/2) delta_g^T ( (1/phi^2) E_x[ Cov(y_i,y_j|x) · (a_i-mu)(a_j-mu)/sigma^2 ] ) delta_g ,

where (a_i - mu)/sigma is order one by construction. So the metric along the gain depends essentially on the prediction-error covariance Cov[Y|x]/phi^2 and the *normalized* activities, not on the raw scale of the input or the weights. Learning the magnitude of the incoming weights is therefore robust to rescaling the input and the parameters — exactly the fragility the standard GLM has, where blowing up the input scale blows up the metric and detunes the effective learning rate. The normalization has decoupled "how big should this neuron's weights be" from "how big does the input happen to be." That's the real reason it trains more stably, stated at the level of the geometry rather than as an observation.

I should be candid about one regime where the core assumption frays, because it tells me where *not* to apply this. The whole justification for a single per-layer mu and sigma was that the units of a layer contribute similarly and the nuisance is shared across them. In a fully-connected layer that holds — all the units feed the next layer in comparable ways. In a convolutional layer it doesn't: a huge number of units are at receptive fields near the image boundary, they fire rarely, and their statistics are genuinely different from the units in the interior. Pooling all of them into one mu, sigma mixes populations that shouldn't be mixed, and I'd expect the feature-wise statistic to help less there than a batch statistic that keeps each feature map separate. So my honest expectation is: strong on fully-connected and recurrent layers where the share-a-statistic premise is true, weaker on convnets, and I'll keep the transform off the layers where the premise fails — which also tells me to keep it off the final output head, since the scale of the logits or the value is the prediction confidence itself and I have no business normalizing it to unit RMS.

Let me also handle how the transform threads through the gates of a real recurrent cell, since "apply it to a^t" is glib when a^t is a stack of gate pre-activations. For an LSTM the gate pre-activations are (f,i,o,g)^T = W_h h^{t-1} + W_x x^t + b. The clean way is to normalize the two contributions to the pre-activation separately and add them, so each linear projection's summed input is standardized on its own,

  (f,i,o,g)^T = LN(W_h h^{t-1}; g_1, b_1) + LN(W_x x^t; g_2, b_2) + b ,

then form the cell and hidden states as usual, c^t = sigmoid(f) ⊙ c^{t-1} + sigmoid(i) ⊙ tanh(g), and normalize the cell on the way out into the hidden state, h^t = sigmoid(o) ⊙ tanh(LN(c^t; g_3, b_3)). Each LN is the same map I derived — subtract the feature mean, divide by the feature std, apply gain and bias — just instantiated at several points in the cell, each with its own gain and bias. For a gated recurrent unit it's the same idea: normalize W_h h^{t-1} and W_x x^t separately inside the gate computation. The point is that the transform is a small reusable function over a vector, LN(z; g, b) = ((z - mu)/sigma) ⊙ g + b, and I drop it wherever a summed input is formed.

Now let me write it as the actual module I'd ship and the actual MLP, because the whole appeal is that it slots into the standard layer stack with no machinery around it. The transform is a per-feature module: given the post-linear vector, compute the mean and variance over the feature dimension, standardize with an eps floor, scale by a learned gain initialized to one and shift by a learned bias initialized to zero. The MLP builder inserts it after each hidden linear and before the activation, and crucially *not* after the output head. I'll build the actor and the critic the same way, with the policy's separate Gaussian std parameter sitting outside the stack.

```python
import torch
import torch.nn as nn
from torch.distributions import Normal


class LayerNorm(nn.Module):
    """Feature-wise normalization: standardize the summed inputs across the H features
    of a single example, then a learned per-feature gain and bias. Independent of batch
    size; identical at train and test time."""

    def __init__(self, num_features, eps=1e-5):
        super().__init__()
        self.eps = eps
        self.gain = nn.Parameter(torch.ones(num_features))   # g, init 1: start as pure normalization
        self.bias = nn.Parameter(torch.zeros(num_features))  # b, init 0

    def forward(self, x):                                     # x: (..., num_features) = the summed inputs Wh
        mu = x.mean(dim=-1, keepdim=True)                    # mu  = (1/H) sum_i a_i   (over features, per example)
        var = x.var(dim=-1, keepdim=True, unbiased=False)    # sigma^2 = (1/H) sum_i (a_i - mu)^2  (biased: /H)
        x_norm = (x - mu) / torch.sqrt(var + self.eps)       # (a - mu)/sqrt(sigma^2 + eps)
        return self.gain * x_norm + self.bias                # g * x_norm + b


def build_mlp(input_dim, hidden_dims, output_dim, activation=nn.ELU()):
    # linear -> LayerNorm -> activation on every hidden layer; NO norm on the output head.
    layers = []
    dims = [input_dim] + list(hidden_dims)
    for d_in, d_out in zip(dims[:-1], dims[1:]):
        layers.append(nn.Linear(d_in, d_out))
        layers.append(LayerNorm(d_out))                     # normalize the summed inputs, before the nonlinearity
        layers.append(activation)
    layers.append(nn.Linear(dims[-1], output_dim))          # output head: logits / value, left un-normalized
    return nn.Sequential(*layers)


class ActorCritic(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, num_obs, num_critic_obs, num_actions,
                 actor_hidden_dims=(512, 256, 128), critic_hidden_dims=(512, 256, 128),
                 init_noise_std=1.0, activation=nn.ELU()):
        super().__init__()
        self.actor = build_mlp(num_obs, actor_hidden_dims, num_actions, activation)
        self.critic = build_mlp(num_critic_obs, critic_hidden_dims, 1, activation)
        self.std = nn.Parameter(init_noise_std * torch.ones(num_actions))
        self.distribution = None

    def update_distribution(self, obs):
        mean = self.actor(obs)
        self.distribution = Normal(mean, mean * 0.0 + self.std)

    def act(self, obs):
        self.update_distribution(obs)
        return self.distribution.sample()

    def get_actions_log_prob(self, actions):
        return self.distribution.log_prob(actions).sum(dim=-1)

    def act_inference(self, obs):
        return self.actor(obs)

    def evaluate(self, critic_obs):
        return self.critic(critic_obs)
```

In practice I'd just use the framework's built-in `nn.LayerNorm(d_out)` in place of my hand-written module — it computes exactly this, mean and variance over the last dimension, biased variance, eps default 1e-5 inside the square root, gain initialized to one and bias to zero — so the whole change to the standard actor-critic is: after each hidden linear, insert a normalization of the summed inputs over the features, before the activation, and leave the output head alone. The existing linear layers keep whatever bias setting the scaffold already had; the method-specific affine freedom is the LayerNorm weight and bias.

Let me trace the causal chain back. I wanted to speed up training by holding each layer's summed-input distribution steady against the correlated drift from the layers below, so I could use a bigger learning rate. Batch normalization does that by standardizing each unit across the batch and adds the two ideas I kept — a learned gain/bias to restore capacity, and the self-stabilizing property that big weights get small gradients — but it measures along the batch axis, which fails at small batch, at batch one, at test time, and on RNNs with variable lengths. Weight normalization escapes the batch but normalizes by the weight norm, blind to the activations. The fix was to keep BN's activation statistic but compute it along the *feature* axis instead of the batch axis: one mean and one variance over all the units of a layer for a single case, justified because the drift I'm fighting is a shared, layer-wide shift that a per-layer statistic removes. That single change makes the transform independent of batch size, identical at train and test, and trivially applicable to RNNs at any sequence length, where it pins the hidden-to-hidden scale and tames the exploding/vanishing dynamics via its invariance to positive rescaling of all the summed inputs. Mapping the invariances showed it occupies a distinct point — uniquely invariant to whole-matrix weight re-centering and to per-case rescaling — and the Fisher-geometry argument showed why it stabilizes: the weight norm becomes an implicit per-weight learning rate, and learning the gain depends on the prediction error rather than the input scale. The premise frays where units don't contribute similarly, so I keep it on fully-connected and recurrent hidden layers and off convolutional layers and the output head. And it all lands as a tiny per-feature module — standardize over features, scale by a gain init to one, shift by a bias init to zero, eps inside the root — inserted after each hidden linear and before the nonlinearity in the standard actor-critic stack.
