I want to write a prime $p \equiv 1 \pmod 4$ as $a^2 + b^2$. Let me first remember why this is even supposed to be true, because the standard route is going to tell me what the hard kernel is, and then maybe I can find a way around it.

The usual argument has two stages. First, $-1$ is a quadratic residue mod $p$: the multiplicative group $(\mathbb Z/p)^\times$ is cyclic of order $p-1$, and since $4 \mid p-1$ there is an element of order $4$, whose square is an element of order $2$, which must be $-1$; so there is an integer $m$ with $m^2 \equiv -1 \pmod p$. Second, from that congruence one manufactures the representation — either by Thue's pigeonhole lemma (look at the values $u - mv$ for $0 \le u, v \le \lfloor\sqrt p\rfloor$, find two that agree mod $p$, and the difference gives $a^2 + b^2 = p$ after checking the size bounds), or by Fermat's descent (start from $m^2 + 1^2 = kp$ for some $k$ and descend $k$ down to $1$). Both stages work. But both stages are doing real labor: the first is a group-theoretic existence statement, the second is an inequality-chasing descent, and they don't talk to each other very gracefully — the residue $m$ is conjured, then fed into a separate machine.

So here is what I actually want. I want the *existence* of the representation to fall out of one clean structural fact, with the quadratic-residue content absorbed into that same fact rather than imported as a lemma. Let me think about what kind of structural fact can force "$p$ is a sum of two squares" without ever constructing $a$ and $b$.

The most primitive existence-from-counting tool I know is a parity argument. If I have a finite set $S$ and an involution $f \colon S \to S$ (a map with $f \circ f = \mathrm{id}$), then $S$ breaks into orbits of size $1$ and size $2$: the size-$2$ orbits are pairs $\{s, f(s)\}$ with $f(s) \ne s$, and the size-$1$ orbits are the fixed points. So $|S|$ and the number of fixed points have the same parity:
$$|S| \equiv \#\{s : f(s) = s\} \pmod 2.$$
This is completely elementary, but it's powerful in a specific way: it lets me prove a fixed point *exists* without exhibiting one. If I can build a set $S$ and an involution whose fixed points are exactly the objects I care about, and if I can independently pin down the parity of $|S|$, then parity *forces* a fixed point into existence.

Let me try to make "sum of two squares" into a fixed point. The representation $p = a^2 + b^2$ has an obvious symmetry: swapping $a$ and $b$. A representation with $a = b$ would be a fixed point of that swap — but $a = b$ gives $p = 2a^2$, which is even, so that's not what I want. I need a symmetry whose *fixed points are the representations themselves*, not the symmetric representations.

Suppose I parametrize things so that one coordinate is $a$ and the *other square* $b^2$ is split as a product. Write the second square as $b = 2y$ — I'll see in a moment why the factor $2$ helps — and think of $4y^2$ as $4 \cdot y \cdot y$. Then $p = a^2 + 4y^2 = a^2 + 4 y \cdot y$ is the "diagonal" case $y = z$ of the three-variable equation
$$x^2 + 4 y z = p.$$
Now I have room. Consider the set of all positive-integer triples solving this:
$$S = \{(x,y,z) \in \mathbb Z_{>0}^3 : x^2 + 4yz = p\}.$$
On $S$ there is a dead-obvious involution: swap the last two coordinates,
$$\sigma\colon (x,y,z) \mapsto (x,z,y).$$
It's clearly an involution. Its fixed points are exactly the triples with $y = z$, and a fixed point gives $p = x^2 + 4y^2 = x^2 + (2y)^2$ — a genuine sum of two squares! So the factor $2$ was for this: it makes the "diagonal" of $x^2 + 4yz$ land on $x^2 + (\text{even})^2$, which is what I can read as a square. (If I'd used $x^2 + yz = p$ the diagonal would only give $x^2 + y^2$ with $y$ unconstrained — that's actually fine too, but $4yz$ will turn out to interact more cleanly with the second, harder involution, so let me keep the $4$.)

This reframes the whole problem. I no longer need to construct anything. I just need to prove that the swap involution $\sigma$ has at least one fixed point. By the parity principle, $\sigma$ has a fixed point as soon as $|S|$ is *odd*:
$$|S| \text{ odd} \implies \#\mathrm{Fix}(\sigma) \text{ odd} \implies \#\mathrm{Fix}(\sigma) \ge 1 \implies p = x^2 + (2y)^2 \text{ for some } (x,y,y) \in S.$$
So the entire theorem has collapsed to a single combinatorial claim: $|S|$ is odd. Beautiful — and notice I haven't used $p \equiv 1 \pmod 4$ yet, nor primality. That content has to enter when I prove $|S|$ is odd. Good; at least I know where the real input lives now.

First let me make sure $S$ is finite and nonempty so the parity statement isn't vacuous. Finiteness: from $x^2 + 4yz = p$ with all of $x,y,z \ge 1$, each is bounded — $x \le \sqrt p$, and $4yz \le p$ forces $y, z \le p/4$. So $S$ is a finite set. Nonempty: I'd like at least one easy element. Try $x = 1$: then $4yz = p - 1$, and I need $p - 1$ divisible by $4$ to get $y = 1$, $z = (p-1)/4$. There it is — $p \equiv 1 \pmod 4$ makes $(1, 1, (p-1)/4)$ a legitimate element of $S$ with positive integer coordinates. That's promising; it might even be the key triple.

Now, how do I prove $|S|$ is odd? The same trick that worked for $\sigma$ should work again: find *another* involution on $S$, call it $\tau$, whose fixed points I can count exactly — and if $\tau$ has exactly **one** fixed point, then $|S| \equiv 1 \pmod 2$, i.e. $|S|$ is odd, and I'm done. So I need to design an involution on $S$ with a *unique* fixed point. The candidate fixed point should be that special triple $(1, 1, (p-1)/4)$, since it was the one place where $p \equiv 1 \pmod 4$ entered.

Let me think about what kind of map preserves $x^2 + 4yz = p$. I want a piecewise-linear surgery on $(x,y,z)$ that keeps the quantity $x^2 + 4yz$ equal to $p$, stays in the positive orthant, and is its own inverse. The conserved quantity is $x^2 + 4yz$, so I'm looking for substitutions that trade $x$ against $y$ and $z$ in a way that leaves $x^2 + 4yz$ alone.

Let me hunt for such a substitution by completing-the-square style algebra. I want a new triple $(x', y', z')$ with $x'^2 + 4y'z' = x^2 + 4yz$. One natural idea: shift $x$ by a multiple of one of the other variables and compensate. Suppose I set $x' = x + 2z$ (a shift by twice $z$). Then $x'^2 = x^2 + 4xz + 4z^2$, which overshoots $x^2$ by $4xz + 4z^2 = 4z(x + z)$. To restore the total I need $4y'z'$ to be smaller than $4yz$ by exactly $4z(x+z)$. If I keep one factor equal to $z$ — say $y' = z$ — then I need $4z \cdot z' = 4yz - 4z(x+z)$, i.e. $z' = y - x - z$. Let me check:
$$x'^2 + 4y'z' = (x + 2z)^2 + 4 z (y - x - z) = x^2 + 4xz + 4z^2 + 4yz - 4xz - 4z^2 = x^2 + 4yz = p. \checkmark$$
So the map
$$(x,y,z) \longmapsto (x + 2z,\ z,\ y - x - z)$$
stays on the surface. For it to land in $S$ I need all three new coordinates positive: $x + 2z > 0$ and $z > 0$ are automatic, and $y - x - z > 0$ means $x < y - z$. So this branch is only legal when $x < y - z$. Outside that region I'll need a different surgery.

This is encouraging but I'm worried about whether one such rule can possibly be an involution. Apply the rule once to $(x,y,z)$ and I get $(x+2z,\, z,\, y-x-z)$. Apply *the same rule* again: the new "$x$" is $x+2z$, the new "$z$" is $y - x - z$, so the new shift would be $x + 2z + 2(y - x - z) = 2y - x$, which is not going to bring me back to $x$. So this single rule is not its own inverse; it must be paired with a *different* rule that undoes it. Fine — that's the signal that the involution has to be genuinely piecewise, with branches that map into each other. Let me figure out the geometry of the branches.

The new triple from the first rule is $(x+2z,\, z,\, y-x-z)$. Where does it sit relative to the dividing inequalities? Its first coordinate is $x + 2z$ and twice its second coordinate is $2z$; since $x > 0$, we have $x + 2z > 2z$, i.e. the image satisfies $x' > 2 y'$. So images of the "$x < y - z$" branch all live in the region "$x > 2y$". That tells me the *partner* branch is whatever rule acts on the region $x > 2y$, and the two must be mutual inverses.

So let me build the rule on the region $x > 2y$ by literally inverting the first one. The first rule sends $(x,y,z) \mapsto (x+2z, z, y-x-z)$. I want the rule $R$ on $\{x > 2y\}$ with $R(x+2z, z, y-x-z) = (x,y,z)$. Rename the input as $(X, Y, Z) = (x+2z, z, y-x-z)$, so $z = Y$, $x = X - 2Y$, and $y = Z + x + z = Z + (X - 2Y) + Y = X - Y + Z$. Hence
$$R\colon (X, Y, Z) \longmapsto (X - 2Y,\ X - Y + Z,\ Y).$$
Let me sanity-check that $R$ preserves the surface directly, not just by construction:
$$(X - 2Y)^2 + 4(X - Y + Z)Y = X^2 - 4XY + 4Y^2 + 4XY - 4Y^2 + 4YZ = X^2 + 4YZ = p. \checkmark$$
And positivity on $\{X > 2Y\}$: $X - 2Y > 0$ by the region condition; $Y > 0$; and $X - Y + Z > 0$ since $X > 2Y \ge Y > 0$ and $Z > 0$. Good, $R$ maps $\{X > 2Y\} \cap S$ into $S$. I should check the two directions without hiding anything. Starting from $x < y-z$, the first rule gives $(x',y',z')=(x+2z,z,y-x-z)$, and $x'=x+2z>2z=2y'$, so it lands in the $x>2y$ region; then the third rule returns
$$(x+2z-2z,\ (x+2z)-z+(y-x-z),\ z)=(x,y,z).$$
Starting from $x>2y$, the third rule gives $(x',y',z')=(x-2y,x-y+z,y)$, and $y'-z'=x-2y+z>x-2y=x'$, so it lands in the $x<y-z$ region; then the first rule returns
$$(x-2y+2y,\ y,\ (x-y+z)-(x-2y)-y)=(x,y,z).$$
So the first and third branches are mutual inverses, not merely plausible partners, and there are no fixed points there because each branch moves a point into the other open region.

Now the only triples not yet covered are those in the *middle* region, $y - z < x < 2y$. The fixed point I'm chasing, $(1, 1, (p-1)/4)$, has $x = y = 1$, and $y - z = 1 - (p-1)/4 < 1 < 2 = 2y$, so it lives in the middle region — exactly where I haven't defined the map yet. So the middle branch is where the unique fixed point must come from, and it should map the middle region *to itself* (since the two outer regions already pair off with each other and are accounted for).

What surgery preserves $x^2 + 4yz$ and stays in the middle? The fixed point has $x = y$, so let me look for a rule that reflects $x$ about $y$: set $x' = 2y - x$. Then $x'^2 = 4y^2 - 4xy + x^2$, which differs from $x^2$ by $4y^2 - 4xy = 4y(y - x)$. To compensate, keep $y' = y$ and adjust $z'$: I need $4y z' = 4yz + 4y(x - y)$, so $z' = z + x - y = x - y + z$. Check:
$$(2y - x)^2 + 4y(x - y + z) = 4y^2 - 4xy + x^2 + 4xy - 4y^2 + 4yz = x^2 + 4yz = p. \checkmark$$
So the middle rule is
$$(x,y,z) \longmapsto (2y - x,\ y,\ x - y + z).$$
Positivity: $2y - x > 0 \iff x < 2y$ (upper bound of the middle region), $y > 0$, and $x - y + z > 0 \iff x > y - z$ (lower bound of the middle region). So *both* defining inequalities of the middle region are exactly what's needed to keep the image positive — the region was tailor-made for this rule. Does the image stay in the middle? Its first coordinate is $2y - x$ and twice its second is $2y$; since $x > 0$, $2y - x < 2y$, so $x' < 2y'$. And $y' - z' = y - (x - y + z) = 2y - x - z < 2y - x = x'$ because $z > 0$, so $x' > y' - z'$. Both middle inequalities hold for the image — it stays in the middle. Now is this rule its own inverse? Apply it twice: $(x,y,z) \mapsto (2y - x, y, x - y + z) \mapsto (2y - (2y - x),\ y,\ (2y - x) - y + (x - y + z)) = (x,\ y,\ z)$. Yes — the middle rule is an involution on the middle region all by itself.

Let me assemble the whole map. Define $\tau\colon S \to S$ by
$$\tau(x,y,z) = \begin{cases} (x + 2z,\ z,\ y - x - z) & \text{if } x < y - z, \\ (2y - x,\ y,\ x - y + z) & \text{if } y - z < x < 2y, \\ (x - 2y,\ x - y + z,\ y) & \text{if } x > 2y. \end{cases}$$
I've checked each branch preserves $x^2 + 4yz = p$ and maps positive triples to positive triples, that branch one and branch three are mutual inverses, and that branch two is an involution of the middle region. Since $y,z>0$, I have $y-z<2y$, so the three open inequalities are disjoint and ordered; they become exhaustive for a triple in $S$ once the two missing equalities $x=y-z$ and $x=2y$ are impossible. So $\tau$ is well-defined and $\tau \circ \tau = \mathrm{id}$ exactly when no point of $S$ sits on either boundary. I have to rule out those boundaries, and this is where the primality of $p$ is needed for the boundary exclusions.

Boundary $x = 2y$: then $p = x^2 + 4yz = 4y^2 + 4yz = 4y(y + z)$, so $4 \mid p$. But $p \equiv 1 \pmod 4$ makes $p$ odd, and a prime cannot be a positive multiple of $4$. No triple in $S$ has $x = 2y$.

Boundary $x = y - z$: then $y = x + z$, so $p = x^2 + 4yz = x^2 + 4(x + z)z = x^2 + 4xz + 4z^2 = (x + 2z)^2$, a perfect square greater than $1$. But a prime is not a nontrivial square. So no triple in $S$ has $x = y - z$ either. Both boundaries are excluded, the three regions partition $S$, and $\tau$ is a well-defined involution on all of $S$.

Now the fixed points of $\tau$. A fixed point can't be in branch one or three, since those branches swap the two outer regions ($\tau$ moves a point from $\{x<y-z\}$ into $\{x>2y\}$ and vice versa, so it can never return the same point). So any fixed point is in the middle, where $\tau(x,y,z) = (2y - x, y, x - y + z) = (x,y,z)$ forces $2y - x = x$, i.e. $x = y$ (and then $x - y + z = z$ is automatic). So fixed points are exactly the triples with $x = y$ in $S$. For such a triple, $p = x^2 + 4xz = x(x + 4z)$. Since $p$ is prime and $x + 4z > x \ge 1$, the factorization can only be $1 \cdot p$ with $x=1$ and $x+4z=p$. Then $p = 1 + 4z$, so $z = (p - 1)/4$, which is a positive integer exactly because $p \equiv 1 \pmod 4$. Conversely, that value gives $1^2+4\cdot1\cdot(p-1)/4=p$, so the point really is in $S$. Hence the *only* fixed point of $\tau$ is
$$(1,\ 1,\ \tfrac{p-1}{4}),$$
and it genuinely lies in $S$. So $\tau$ has exactly one fixed point.

By the parity principle, $|S| \equiv \#\mathrm{Fix}(\tau) = 1 \pmod 2$, so $|S|$ is **odd**. And now the first involution finishes everything: $|S|$ odd means the swap $\sigma(x,y,z) = (x,z,y)$ has an odd number of fixed points, in particular at least one. A fixed point of $\sigma$ has $y = z$, giving
$$p = x^2 + 4y^2 = x^2 + (2y)^2,$$
a sum of two squares. Done.

Now the chain is clean. I turn "is a sum of two squares" into "the swap involution $\sigma$ on $S = \{x^2 + 4yz = p\}$ has a fixed point." Parity reduces that to proving $|S|$ is odd. The second involution $\tau$ gives the oddness because its three completing-the-square branches cover all of $S$, the two outer branches reverse each other, the middle branch reverses itself, primality rules out the two missing boundary equalities, and a middle fixed point forces $p=x(x+4z)$, hence $x=1$ and $z=(p-1)/4$. Since $p \equiv 1 \pmod 4$, that unique fixed point exists, so $|S|$ is odd. Then $\sigma$ must have a fixed point, and a fixed point of $\sigma$ is exactly a triple $(x,y,y)$ with $p = x^2 + (2y)^2$. The quadratic-residue statement I was tempted to start with never has to be separated out; it is hidden inside the parity transfer from $\tau$ to $\sigma$. The argument is non-constructive, though: it proves that some fixed point of $\sigma$ exists without locating it.

The structure is the useful part: any two involutions on the same finite set have fixed-point sets of the same parity, because both parities equal $|S| \bmod 2$. Here one involution, $\tau$, has a fixed-point set I can count exactly, while the other involution, $\sigma$, has the fixed points I want but cannot count directly. Matching the two parities transfers existence from the countable fixed point to the desired one.

There's a picture that makes $\tau$ feel inevitable rather than pulled from a hat. Read a triple $(x,y,z)$ as a "windmill": a central $x \times x$ square together with four $y \times z$ rectangular arms attached pinwheel-fashion around it, with the side of length $y$ lying along the square. Its total area is $x^2 + 4yz = p$. The two outer branches of $\tau$ are the operation of re-cutting the windmill to the *largest* central square that the same four arms can frame — growing the square (and shrinking the arms' free length) when the current square is too small, trimming it when it's too big — which is exactly the completing-the-square trade I did with $x' = x \pm 2(\cdot)$. The middle branch, $x \mapsto 2y - x$, is the self-dual case where the square is already maximal. And the swap $\sigma$ is just flipping each arm's two side lengths: its fixed points, $y = z$, are the windmills whose four arms are *squares* of side $y$, so the whole figure is a big square of side $x$ flanked by four $y \times y$ squares — which reassembles into $x^2 + (2y)^2$. The unit windmill $(1,1,(p-1)/4)$ — a $1 \times 1$ center with four long thin arms — is the one shape $\tau$ pins, and parity says some *other*, square-armed windmill must exist to be pinned by $\sigma$.
