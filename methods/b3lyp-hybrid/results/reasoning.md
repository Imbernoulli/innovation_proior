I keep coming back to the same wall, and it bothers me. We have a Kohn–Sham machine that is cheap, self-consistent, and basis-set-free, and we have been feeding it better and better exchange-correlation functionals — the local spin-density approximation, then Becke's 1988 gradient correction for exchange, then a gradient correction for correlation on top — and the atomization energies of the G1 molecules just keep coming back too large. Not wildly: a few kcal/mol on average. But it is *systematic*, and it is worst for the non-hydrides, and no matter how I refine the gradient corrections it does not go away. The hydrides come out fine; CH₄, NH₃, H₂O sit close to experiment. But N₂, CO, O₂, F₂ — the molecules where two heavy atoms share electrons — those overbind. There is something structural here. If it were random, finer functionals would shrink it. It does not shrink. So the error is not in *how well* I model the density and its gradient; it is in *what* I am modeling.

Let me not reach for another gradient term. Let me go back to what E_xc actually *is*, in the exact theory, and stare at it until the error has a face.

The exact statement I trust is the adiabatic connection. Take the physical system, with the full 1/r₁₂ repulsion. Now imagine dialing the repulsion down with a coupling parameter λ, so the interaction is λ/r₁₂, and at each λ adjust the external potential v_λ so that the density stays nailed to its physical value n(r) all the way down. At λ = 1 I have the real system. At λ = 0 I have non-interacting electrons in some potential that reproduces n — that is exactly the Kohn–Sham reference system, a single Slater determinant. In between, a whole family of partially interacting systems, every one of them carrying the same density. Now the exchange-correlation energy is

  E_xc = ∫₀¹ U_xc^λ dλ,

where U_xc^λ is the exchange-correlation *potential* energy at coupling strength λ. Let me make sure I believe this and not just recite it. Write the energy of the λ-system as E_λ. By Hellmann–Feynman, dE_λ/dλ = ⟨Ψ_λ | dĤ_λ/dλ | Ψ_λ⟩, and dĤ_λ/dλ has two pieces: the explicit ∂/∂λ of the interaction, which is the bare V̂_ee, and the ∂/∂λ of the external potential. But the external potential is tuned to hold n fixed, so its contribution integrates to ∫ n δv_λ — and when I assemble E_xc by subtracting off the non-interacting kinetic energy, the external-potential and Hartree pieces are exactly what get removed, leaving the integral of the interaction expectation minus the Hartree term. So ∫₀¹ dλ (⟨V̂_ee⟩_λ − U_H) = E_xc, and the integrand at coupling λ, with the Hartree part divided out, is U_xc^λ. Good. And notice: the integrand is *only* potential energy, yet the λ-integration secretly produces the kinetic correlation energy too, because the wavefunction Ψ_λ rearranges as λ grows. That is the beautiful part — I get the full E_xc, kinetic correlation and all, from an integral over potential-energy integrands.

Now the two ends. At λ = 1, U_xc^λ is the potential energy of the deep, fully-developed exchange-correlation hole of the real system — a hole that is localized around each electron, normalized to remove exactly one electron (the sum rule ∫ n_xc = −1). At λ = 0 the system is a single Slater determinant of the Kohn–Sham orbitals; it has *no* correlation; its U_xc^λ is purely the exchange energy of that determinant. So

  U_xc^{λ=0} = E_x, the exact exchange energy of the KS determinant.

This is essentially the Hartree–Fock exchange energy (not exactly — it is evaluated on KS orbitals, not HF orbitals — but in value extremely close). The λ = 0 end of the integral is *nothing more and nothing less than exact exchange.* I want to sit with that for a moment, because it changes the problem. One endpoint of the integral I am trying to approximate is a quantity I can compute exactly. It is the exchange of a determinant — a Fock integral on the orbitals I already have.

Let me make sure that endpoint claim is not just words by pinning it down on a case I can do by hand: a closed-shell two-electron singlet, say He or H₂, where both electrons occupy one spatial orbital φ (one spin-up, one spin-down). Define the Coulomb self-energy of that orbital's density, J[φ] = ∫∫ |φ(r₁)|² |φ(r₂)|² /|r₁−r₂| dr₁ dr₂. The Fock exchange sums over same-spin orbital pairs, E_x = −½ Σ_σ Σ_{ij} (iσ jσ | jσ iσ); each spin channel has exactly one occupied orbital, so the double sum is the single term (φφ|φφ) = J[φ] for each of the two spins: E_x = −½ · (J[φ] + J[φ]) = −J[φ]. Now the Hartree energy of the full density n = 2|φ|²: E_H = ½ ∫∫ n(r₁) n(r₂)/r₁₂ = ½ · 4 · J[φ] = 2J[φ]. So at λ = 0, E_x = −J[φ] = −½ E_H — exchange cancels exactly *half* the Hartree energy of the two-electron system, with *no correlation*, and the number is fully computable from φ. That −½ is the right known answer for a two-electron closed shell, so the bookkeeping checks out. Two things fall out that I will need later: first, take the one-electron version (a single occupied spin-orbital, n = |φ|²), where E_H = ½ J[φ]... let me be careful — for one electron n = |φ|², so E_H = ½ ∫∫ |φ|²|φ|²/r₁₂ = ½ J[φ], and the single-spin Fock exchange is E_x = −½ (φφ|φφ) = −½ J[φ], so E_x = −E_H exactly: the exchange cancels the *entire* spurious self-repulsion of a one-electron density. That is genuine self-interaction freedom, something a semilocal exchange functional can never reproduce exactly. Second, the computation confirms operationally that the weak-coupling endpoint is a determinant property I evaluate from the orbitals, not something I have to model.

Now where does LSDA put its model? It replaces U_xc^λ at *every* λ — including λ = 0 — by the uniform-electron-gas value at the local density. So at the λ = 0 end, LSDA is using a *gas* exchange-correlation hole in place of the exact-exchange hole of the determinant. Is that a good substitution? Let me test it on the cleanest possible case, H₂ at equilibrium. The exact restricted exchange hole there is the negative of the σ_g bonding orbital density: it is *static*, spread uniformly over both atoms, and it does not move when I move my reference electron. Physically, at λ = 0 there is no left–right correlation in H₂ — the determinant has both electrons equally on both centers. Now what is the gas-model hole doing at the same point? The uniform-gas hole is reference-point *centered*: it sits around wherever my reference electron is, relatively localized, and it *follows* that electron as it moves. So the gas model is putting in a hole that trails the electron — which is a crude imitation of left–right correlation, an electron on one atom keeping the other electron on the other atom. That imitation is exactly what I *want* in the fully interacting λ = 1 system, where real left–right correlation exists. But at λ = 0 it is simply *wrong*: there is no such correlation in the determinant, and the gas model has manufactured some.

So now I can see the face of the error. The gas model is a hole that follows its electron, and that following-behavior is appropriate at strong coupling and inappropriate at weak coupling. LSDA applies it at *all* λ, so it injects fake left–right correlation at the λ = 0 end. Fake left–right correlation lowers the energy of the *molecule* (the electrons are being told to avoid each other across the bond), which makes the bond look stronger than it is — *overbinding*. And it is worst exactly where bonds form between heavy atoms, the non-hydrides, where left–right correlation across the bond is a big effect. The hydrides, with one heavy atom and protons, have less of this, so they come out fine. That matches what I see in the tables. The structural overbinding is a *λ = 0-end error*. And the exact value at that end I happen to know.

Let me also note a second symptom that points the same way. Gunnarsson and Jones argued that density-functional energy differences go conspicuously wrong whenever orbital nodes are created or destroyed in a process — for instance when an antibonding orbital gets occupied. The exact-exchange-energy differences tied to changes in orbital nodality are subtle and the local gas model represents them badly. And again the difficulty is sharpest at λ = 0, where the energy *is* exact exchange and nothing else. Two independent symptoms, same diagnosis: the local model is wrong at the exchange end, and exact exchange is the missing ingredient there.

So stop using the gas model at the λ = 0 end. Put the *exact* exchange there, since I know it. Keep the gas (and gradient) model at the strong-coupling end, where its following-hole correctly mimics left–right correlation and where, in fact, the whole error-cancellation that makes DFT work lives.

Let me try the crudest version of this first and see what breaks, because the cruder version will teach me what the parameters need to be. The integral E_xc = ∫₀¹ U_xc^λ dλ — approximate it by the simplest quadrature I can, a two-point average of the endpoints:

  E_xc ≈ ½ (U_xc^{λ=0} + U_xc^{λ=1}) = ½ (E_x^exact + E_xc^{LSDA}),

where I model the λ = 1 end by the LSDA value (its following-hole is right there). This is the half-and-half idea: half exact exchange, half LSDA exchange-correlation. Let me see how it does and, more importantly, where it fails, because the failures are the design spec for the next step.

It actually works surprisingly well on atomization energies — comparable to the gradient-corrected DFT, around 6 or 7 kcal/mol average. That it does no worse than the best semilocal functional while carrying real exact exchange at λ = 0 is at least consistent with the diagnosis; it is not proof, but it does not contradict the idea that the weak-coupling end was the problem. But now the problems, and they are sharp enough to kill this exact form.

First, the uniform-gas limit. This one stops me cold. Take a system that *is* a uniform electron gas — constant density. There, the exact theory must reduce to the gas exchange-correlation energy, by construction: E_xc → E_xc^{LSDA} = E_x^{LSDA} + E_c^{LSDA}, both pieces nonzero. Now evaluate the half-and-half on that density and see what comes out, term by term. The formula is E_xc ≈ ½ E_x^exact + ½ E_xc^{LSDA} = ½ E_x^exact + ½ E_x^{LSDA} + ½ E_c^{LSDA}. For the uniform gas the determinant *is* the gas Slater determinant, so its exact exchange equals the gas exchange exactly, E_x^exact = E_x^{LSDA}; substitute that into the first term: ½ E_x^{LSDA} + ½ E_x^{LSDA} + ½ E_c^{LSDA} = E_x^{LSDA} + ½ E_c^{LSDA}. Compare against the required answer E_x^{LSDA} + E_c^{LSDA}: the exchange matches, but the correlation comes out at exactly *half* its correct gas value. The error is −½ E_c^{LSDA}, and since the gas correlation energy is nonzero (and negative) at any finite density, this does not vanish — the half-and-half is wrong in the one limit where the exact answer is known analytically. That is a genuine formal embarrassment, and it is not a small numerical slip; it is a fixed factor-of-two on the whole correlation energy of the gas. I cannot accept that. Whatever I build next must recover the uniform-gas limit exactly, which means the correlation must enter at full weight (or with corrections that *vanish* for constant density), not at half.

Second, total energies are poor, and ionization potentials and proton affinities are poor — the half-and-half does atomization energies and little else. A single rigid coefficient, 50%, with no other knobs, cannot simultaneously fit atomization (an energy *difference* dominated by the λ = 0 overbinding) and total energies (which need the right amount of correlation everywhere) and IPs (which probe a different balance). One number is too few.

And third — stepping back — the half-and-half is a *terrible* quadrature of the λ-integral. A two-point trapezoid assumes the integrand is linear in λ. Is it? The integrand starts at exact exchange (λ = 0) and bends down toward the correlated value as λ grows; the curvature is real. A straight line between the endpoints is crude. The 50% weight on exact exchange is just the trapezoid weight, and there is no reason the true integral gives 50%.

Let me think harder about that third point, because it is the one that tells me how much exact exchange to use. Model the *integrand* as a function of λ rather than just averaging endpoints. I want a model U_xc^λ that (a) is exact at λ = 0, where I know it equals E_x, and (b) relaxes to the density-functional approximation (LSDA, or LSDA-plus-gradient) near λ = 1, where the gas model is trustworthy. A clean way to write that: let the *correction* to the DFA — the difference between exact exchange and the DFA exchange-correlation at coupling λ — decay as a power of (1 − λ):

  U_xc^λ ≈ E_{xc,λ}^{DFA} + (E_x^exact − E_x^{DFA}) (1 − λ)^{n−1},

for some integer n ≥ 1. Check the ends. At λ = 0, (1 − λ)^{n−1} = 1, so U_xc^0 = E_{xc,0}^{DFA} + (E_x − E_x^{DFA}) = E_x^exact — correct, exact exchange. As λ → 1, (1 − λ)^{n−1} → 0 (for n > 1), so U_xc → E_{xc,1}^{DFA}, the DFA value, where it should be. The exponent n controls how fast the exact-exchange correction dies off as the coupling turns on. Now integrate over λ. The only λ-dependent factor is (1 − λ)^{n−1}, and ∫₀¹ (1 − λ)^{n−1} dλ — substitute u = 1 − λ, du = −dλ, limits flip 1→0 — is ∫₀¹ u^{n−1} du = [u^n/n]₀¹ = 1/n. So

  E_xc = ∫₀¹ U_xc^λ dλ = E_xc^{DFA} + (E_x^exact − E_x^{DFA}) ∫₀¹ (1 − λ)^{n−1} dλ
       = E_xc^{DFA} + (E_x^exact − E_x^{DFA}) · (1/n).

So the fraction of exact exchange to mix in is a₀ = 1/n. Let me put numbers to the small integer values to feel out the relationship: n = 1 gives ∫₀¹(1−λ)⁰ dλ = ∫₀¹ 1 dλ = 1, so a₀ = 1 (100% exact exchange — a flat integrand, all the way exchange-like); n = 2 gives ∫₀¹(1−λ) dλ = ½, so a₀ = ½ (a linear integrand); n = 3 gives ∫₀¹(1−λ)² dλ = 1/3; n = 4 gives ∫₀¹(1−λ)³ dλ = 1/4. The whole question of "how much exact exchange" has collapsed to "what is n" — and n has a meaning: it is how high in λ-order I have to go before the integrand is well described. The n = 2 value, a₀ = ½, is precisely the half-and-half coefficient I just tried — and that is not a coincidence: a₀ = ½ from this argument *is* the assumption that U_xc^λ is *linear* in λ, which is exactly the two-point trapezoid I used. So the half-and-half, which I reached by averaging endpoints, is the n = 2 special case of this integrand model, and its 50% is the trapezoid weight in disguise. Seeing that the endpoint average and the (1−λ) model give the same number when both assume linearity is a small consistency check that the integrand model is the right generalization.

What is n for real molecules? n is the lowest order of perturbation theory in the coupling that gives a realistic picture of the λ-dependence. For a strongly closed-shell, exchange-dominated ion, n = 1 (exchange dominates, 100% exact exchange). For typical first-row molecules, the relevant perturbation series — Møller–Plesset in the interaction — needs to be carried to around fourth order (MP4) before atomization energies are accurate; so n ≈ 4, and a₀ ≈ 1/4. For systems with near-degenerate ground states — stretched H₂, ozone, where the λ = 0 reference is qualitatively wrong — the integrand has enormous curvature near λ = 0, effectively n → ∞, a₀ → 0, and I should fall back to the pure density functional (whose own approximate exchange happens to imitate the missing static correlation). So the *physical* amount of exact exchange for ordinary molecules is around one quarter, with the understanding that it should be smaller for the near-degenerate cases and larger for the exchange-dominated ones. Half-and-half overshot because a linear integrand is a worse model than a curved one for these molecules.

That settles the *order of magnitude* — around 0.2 to 0.25 — but I do not want to hardwire 1/4 from a perturbation-order argument and call it done, because the argument tells me a₀ varies by system and I am going to assume a single constant. So let me keep a₀ as a parameter to be fit, anchored by the knowledge that it should land near a quarter, and let me add the flexibility the half-and-half lacked.

Here is the form I want. Start from the LSDA exchange-correlation as the base — that guarantees I have the full uniform-gas energy in the leading term, which is the limit half-and-half broke. Then add a correction that swaps a fraction a₀ of the LSDA *exchange* for exact exchange:

  + a₀ (E_x^exact − E_x^{LSDA}).

At a₀ = 1 this would be full exact-exchange replacement; at a₀ = 0 it vanishes and I am back to LSDA. This term carries the λ = 0 fix. Now, gradient corrections. I still want the gradient improvements that B88 and the correlation GGA brought, because LSDA alone is too crude even at large λ. So add Becke's 1988 gradient correction for exchange and a gradient correction for correlation, each with its own coefficient:

  + a_x ΔE_x^{B88} + a_c ΔE_c^{GGA}.

Putting it together,

  E_xc = E_xc^{LSDA} + a₀ (E_x^exact − E_x^{LSDA}) + a_x ΔE_x^{B88} + a_c ΔE_c^{GGA}.

Three parameters. Let me check, before fitting anything, that this form fixes the things half-and-half got wrong.

The uniform-gas limit. Let me run the same constant-density test I used to kill the half-and-half, term by term on E_xc = E_xc^{LSDA} + a₀ (E_x^exact − E_x^{LSDA}) + a_x ΔE_x^{B88} + a_c ΔE_c^{GGA}. Term 1, E_xc^{LSDA}: this is the gas energy itself, so it gives exactly E_x^{LSDA} + E_c^{LSDA}, the required answer — already complete on its own. Term 2, a₀(E_x^exact − E_x^{LSDA}): for the gas the determinant exchange equals the gas exchange, E_x^exact = E_x^{LSDA}, so the bracket is E_x^{LSDA} − E_x^{LSDA} = 0; this term contributes nothing regardless of a₀. Terms 3 and 4, a_x ΔE_x^{B88} and a_c ΔE_c^{GGA}: both gradient corrections are built from |∇n|, and for constant density ∇n = 0 everywhere, so x_σ = |∇n_σ|/n_σ^{4/3} = 0 and the B88 integrand −β Σ n_σ^{4/3} x_σ²/(1 + 6β x_σ sinh⁻¹ x_σ) → 0 (the numerator carries x_σ²); likewise the correlation gradient correction → 0. So both vanish for any a_x, a_c. Summing: E_x^{LSDA} + E_c^{LSDA} + 0 + 0 + 0 = E_xc^{LSDA}, the exact gas energy — exactly, and independently of all three coefficients. That is the −½ E_c^{LSDA} defect of the half-and-half repaired: there the correlation came out at half weight; here the LSDA base carries the *full* correlation and every correction switches off. So this is, in fact, the *simplest* possible mixture of exact exchange, the LSDA, and gradient corrections of exchange and correlation type that recovers the uniform-electron-gas limit for arbitrary mixing coefficients — the limit is protected by construction, not by tuning.

The rigidity. I now have three knobs instead of one, so I can fit atomization energies, ionization potentials, proton affinities, and total energies *simultaneously* rather than atomization alone.

What should I expect for the coefficients, qualitatively, before I fit? a₀ should be near a quarter, from the λ-integrand argument — it is the fraction of exact exchange, the "rate of onset of correlation as λ grows from zero," which I am approximating as a single constant. a_x should be *less than one*: in the pure GGA I needed the full B88 correction, but now the exact-exchange term already supplies part of the exchange physics at the λ = 0 end, so I need less of the B88 gradient correction — its coefficient should drop below unity. a_c I will leave free for good measure; correlation gradient corrections do not double-count with exact exchange the way exchange ones do, so I expect a_c near one but I let the fit decide.

Now fit. The functional is *linear* in the three coefficients once I have computed, for each system, the four ingredients E_xc^{LSDA}, (E_x^exact − E_x^{LSDA}), ΔE_x^{B88}, ΔE_c^{GGA} on its converged post-LSDA density and orbitals. So a single linear least-squares over the whole G1 set — 56 atomization energies, 42 ionization potentials, 8 proton affinities, and the 10 first-row total atomic energies — gives the three coefficients in one shot. I run it. The optimum:

  a₀ = 0.20,  a_x = 0.72,  a_c = 0.81.

Now compare these against what the reasoning led me to expect, because the test of the whole picture is whether the unconstrained fit lands near the qualitative predictions or contradicts them. I had three soft predictions, none of them a number I hardwired: a₀ near 1/4 = 0.25 (the λ-integrand argument, n ≈ 4 for MP4-accurate molecules); a_x strictly less than 1 (exact exchange does part of the exchange job, so less B88 needed); a_c near 1 but free. The fit gives a₀ = 0.20: not 0.25, but the same order — a fifth rather than a quarter. The gap, 0.25 − 0.20 = 0.05, is in the direction the argument itself warned about: a₀ should be *smaller* for near-degenerate systems (n → ∞, a₀ → 0), and the G1 set contains some of those (O₃, stretched cases), so a single constant fit against the whole set is pulled below the well-behaved-molecule value of 0.25. So the discrepancy is not a failure of the picture; it is the picture predicting its own correction sign. a_x = 0.72 satisfies the inequality a_x < 1 cleanly — 28% of the B88 correction is no longer needed once exact exchange is present. a_c = 0.81 is in the "near 1, free" band, not full but close. None of the three contradicts the construction; one (a₀) is off by 0.05 in the anticipated direction. That is the most I can claim from the fit alone — agreement in sign and order of magnitude, not to the decimal.

Let me sanity-check the limit cases by actually substituting the corner coefficients into E_xc = E_xc^{LSDA} + a₀ (E_x^exact − E_x^{LSDA}) + a_x ΔE_x^{B88} + a_c ΔE_c^{GGA} and seeing what each reduces to. Set a₀ = 0, a_x = a_c = 1: the a₀ term drops, the gradient terms enter at full weight, E_xc = E_xc^{LSDA} + ΔE_x^{B88} + ΔE_c^{GGA} = (E_x^{LSDA} + ΔE_x^{B88}) + (E_c^{LSDA} + ΔE_c^{GGA}) = E_x^{B88} + E_c^{GGA} — that is exactly pure gradient-corrected (B88) DFT, the baseline functional I started from, recovered as a corner. Set a₀ = 1, a_x = a_c = 0: E_xc = E_xc^{LSDA} + (E_x^exact − E_x^{LSDA}) = E_x^exact + E_c^{LSDA} — full exact exchange plus LSDA correlation, the n = 1 exchange-dominated case. Set a₀ = ½, a_x = a_c = 0: E_xc = E_xc^{LSDA} + ½(E_x^exact − E_x^{LSDA}) = ½ E_x^exact + ½ E_x^{LSDA} + E_c^{LSDA} — the half-and-half exchange, but now carrying the *full* E_c^{LSDA} rather than ½ of it, so this is the half-and-half with its gas-limit correlation defect already repaired by the LSDA base. So all three things I tried sit at corners of a three-dimensional box, each recovered exactly by substitution, and the fit picks the interior point a₀ = 0.20, a_x = 0.72, a_c = 0.81 that does best on the data — none of the corners.

There is a deeper "why" sitting underneath the a₀ ≈ 0.2 that I should make explicit, because it is the same fact from another angle. Semilocal exchange carries a one-electron self-interaction error: the Hartree energy of a single electron, E_H[n], should be cancelled exactly by exchange, and Hartree–Fock exchange does cancel it, but B88 or any density-gradient exchange leaves a residual. That residual self-repulsion pushes density to spread out — it over-delocalizes, over-stabilizing stretched bonds and charge-transfer. Mixing in a fraction a₀ of exact exchange removes a fraction a₀ of the self-interaction error, because exact exchange is self-interaction free by construction. So a₀ is *also* the dial on self-interaction. And this immediately explains why I cannot just push a₀ to 1: full exact exchange is self-interaction free but has *no* correlation, and the magic of semilocal exchange-correlation is that the semilocal exchange functional quietly *imitates* the static (left–right) correlation that the correlation functional misses, and the two errors cancel. Take a₀ too large and I throw away that imitation and expose the missing static correlation — which is exactly why the near-degenerate, small-gap systems (stretched bonds, ozone, transition metals) get *worse* with too much exact exchange, while large-gap molecules get better. a₀ ≈ 0.2 is the sweet spot: enough exact exchange to kill the λ = 0 overbinding and a chunk of the self-interaction error, little enough to keep the static-correlation imitation and the X–C error cancellation intact.

So I have my functional, the three-parameter hybrid:

  E_xc = E_xc^{LSDA} + a₀ (E_x^exact − E_x^{LSDA}) + a_x ΔE_x^{B88} + a_c ΔE_c^{PW91},
  a₀ = 0.20, a_x = 0.72, a_c = 0.81.

One more thing I want to settle: the correlation piece. I wrote ΔE_c^{GGA} as a *gradient correction* to LSDA correlation, the Perdew–Wang form, and that is what the fit above used. But there is another correlation functional on the table that is not built on the gas at all: Lee, Yang, and Parr took the Colle–Salvetti correlation-energy formula — which expresses correlation through the curvature of the Hartree–Fock hole, with four constants fit to helium — and turned it into a pure density functional by a gradient expansion of the kinetic-energy density, giving E_c^{LYP}(n↑, n↓, ∇n↑, ∇n↓). LYP is a *complete* correlation functional, not a correction to LSDA, and it pairs unusually well with B88 exchange (both were calibrated against similar atomic correlation data). I want to be able to drop it in.

But LYP cannot enter the same way as ΔE_c^{PW91}, because it is not a *correction* — it is the whole correlation energy. If I want the analogous structure — mix a complete correlation functional with the LSDA correlation in the proportion a_c : (1 − a_c) — I should write the correlation part as a_c E_c^{LYP} + (1 − a_c) E_c^{LSDA}. That keeps the same idea (a fraction of the better correlation functional, the rest LSDA) and, crucially, still respects the uniform-gas limit if E_c^{LYP} also reduces sensibly there. Doing the same for exchange — the B88 *correction* term a_x ΔE_x^{B88} is, by definition of "correction," a_x(E_x^{B88,total} − E_x^{LSDA}), so the total exchange is (1 − a₀)E_x^{LSDA} from the base minus... let me just write the whole thing out cleanly in terms of *complete* functionals, collecting the LSDA exchange coefficient. The exact-exchange replacement contributes a₀ E_x^exact and removes a₀ E_x^{LSDA}; the B88 correction contributes a_x(E_x^{B88} − E_x^{LSDA}); the base contributes E_x^{LSDA}. Sum of LSDA-exchange coefficients: 1 − a₀ − a_x. So the exchange part is

  a₀ E_x^exact + a_x E_x^{B88} + (1 − a₀ − a_x) E_x^{LSDA},

and the correlation part, with LYP as the complete functional, is

  a_c E_c^{LYP} + (1 − a_c) E_c^{LSDA}.

Naming the exact-exchange fraction a, the B88 fraction b, and the LYP fraction c:

  E_xc^{B3LYP} = a E_x^exact + b E_x^{B88} + (1 − a − b) E_x^{LSDA} + c E_c^{LYP} + (1 − c) E_c^{LSDA},

with a = a₀ = 0.20, b = a_x = 0.72, c = a_c = 0.81. The exchange LSDA coefficient is 1 − a − b = 1 − 0.20 − 0.72 = 0.08, and the correlation LSDA coefficient is 1 − c = 0.19. Let me sanity-check those numbers do something sensible: 0.20 exact exchange + 0.72 B88 + 0.08 Slater (= LSDA) exchange — the three exchange pieces sum to 1, good, total exchange is conserved; and 0.81 LYP + 0.19 LSDA correlation. (One historical wrinkle to keep straight: the LSDA correlation used in practice is a specific uniform-gas parametrization — a Vosko–Wilk–Nusair fit — and one must be careful which VWN variant; the random-phase-approximation-based VWN was used, not the more accurate one. It is a detail of the E_c^{LSDA} term, not of the mixing.)

So the recipe, as a code would assemble it on a converged density and set of orbitals, is literally: take 0.20 of the exact (Fock) exchange, 0.08 of Slater (uniform-gas) exchange, 0.72 of the B88 exchange functional for the exchange energy; and 0.81 of LYP plus 0.19 of the uniform-gas (VWN) correlation for the correlation energy. The exact-exchange term makes the functional nonlocal — it needs a Fock exchange integral over the orbitals, evaluated through the generalized Kohn–Sham equations rather than a pure local potential — and that nonlocality is the price for fixing the λ = 0 end. It removes the structural overbinding and the three knobs let it fit atomization energies, ionization potentials, and proton affinities all at once. One honesty note on the gas limit for *this* (LYP) variant: I proved the gas limit holds exactly for the B3 form built on gradient *corrections* that vanish for constant density. The B3LYP form swaps the correlation correction for the complete LYP functional mixed as c:(1−c), so for the gas it gives c E_c^{LYP,gas} + (1−c) E_c^{LSDA}, and that equals the exact gas correlation only if E_c^{LYP} itself reduces to the gas value — which LYP does not do exactly (it was fit to atomic, not gas, data). So B3LYP's gas limit is approximate where B3's was exact; the exchange side still has its gas limit protected (0.20·E_x^{LSDA} + 0.72·E_x^{LSDA} + 0.08·E_x^{LSDA} = E_x^{LSDA} when ∇n = 0, since exact and B88 both reduce to Slater for the gas). That is a real, if small, concession I am making to get LYP's better molecular correlation. I would want to validate the whole thing back on the full G1 set, but the construction is done.

Let me write out the assembly as the concrete artifact.

```python
import numpy as np

# B3 / B3LYP three-parameter hybrid exchange-correlation energy.
# Born from the adiabatic connection E_xc = ∫_0^1 U_xc^λ dλ:
#   - the λ=0 end of the integrand is *exact exchange* (the KS determinant),
#     where the uniform-gas model is qualitatively wrong (H2 left-right argument);
#   - modeling the integrand correction as ~(1-λ)^{n-1} gives a₀ = 1/n exact
#     exchange (n≈4 for ordinary molecules → a₀≈1/4; fit gives 0.20);
#   - basing everything on LSDA + gradient corrections that vanish for the gas
#     keeps the exact uniform-electron-gas limit (the defect half-and-half had).

# fitted on the G1 set (atomization energies, IPs, proton affinities, total atomic E)
a0 = 0.20   # fraction of EXACT (Fock) exchange — fixes the λ=0 overbinding / self-interaction
ax = 0.72   # admixture of B88 gradient exchange (<1: exact exchange reduces the need for it)
ac = 0.81   # admixture of the gradient/complete correlation functional

def E_xc_B3(n_up, n_dn, grad_up, grad_dn, orbitals, grid):
    """Original B3 form: LSDA base + exact-exchange swap + gradient corrections.
    Each correction vanishes for the uniform gas ⇒ exact gas limit is preserved."""
    Exc_lsda = E_x_LSDA(n_up, n_dn, grid) + E_c_LSDA(n_up, n_dn, grid)
    dEx      = E_x_exact(orbitals, grid) - E_x_LSDA(n_up, n_dn, grid)   # λ=0 fix
    dEx_b88  = dE_x_B88(n_up, n_dn, grad_up, grad_dn, grid)             # exchange GGA correction
    dEc_gga  = dE_c_GGA(n_up, n_dn, grad_up, grad_dn, grid)            # correlation GGA correction
    return Exc_lsda + a0*dEx + ax*dEx_b88 + ac*dEc_gga

def E_xc_B3LYP(n_up, n_dn, grad_up, grad_dn, orbitals, grid):
    """B3LYP: same mixing, but the correlation correction is replaced by the
    *complete* Lee-Yang-Parr functional mixed with LSDA correlation as ac:(1-ac).
    Equivalent to the libxc recipe
        0.2*HF + 0.08*LDA + 0.72*B88 ,  0.81*LYP + 0.19*VWN
    since exchange-LSDA coeff = 1-a0-ax = 0.08 and correlation-LSDA coeff = 1-ac = 0.19."""
    # exchange:  a*HF + b*B88 + (1-a-b)*Slater
    Ex = ( a0 * E_x_exact(orbitals, grid)
         + ax * E_x_B88(n_up, n_dn, grad_up, grad_dn, grid)        # complete B88 exchange
         + (1.0 - a0 - ax) * E_x_LSDA(n_up, n_dn, grid) )
    # correlation:  c*LYP + (1-c)*LSDA(VWN)
    Ec = ( ac * E_c_LYP(n_up, n_dn, grad_up, grad_dn, grid)
         + (1.0 - ac) * E_c_LSDA(n_up, n_dn, grid) )
    return Ex + Ec
```

The causal chain, start to finish: gradient-corrected KS-DFT shows a systematic, structural overbinding of non-hydrides that no finer semilocal model removes → reading E_xc as the adiabatic-connection integral ∫₀¹ U_xc^λ dλ localizes the error to the λ = 0 end, where the integrand is *exact exchange* but LSDA substitutes a gas hole that fakes left–right correlation (the H₂ static-hole argument) → so inject exact exchange at λ = 0, which is computable → the crudest version, a two-point average (half-and-half), does no worse than gradient-corrected DFT (consistent with, not proof of, the diagnosis) but breaks the uniform-gas limit by a computed factor of two on the correlation and is too rigid → modeling the *integrand* as a (1 − λ)^{n−1}-decaying correction gives a₀ = 1/n exact exchange, ≈ 1/4 for ordinary molecules and self-interaction-reducing → base the functional on LSDA plus gradient corrections that vanish for the gas (restoring the gas limit) and open three knobs a₀, a_x, a_c → linear least-squares on G1 gives a₀ = 0.20, a_x = 0.72 (< 1, because exact exchange does part of the exchange job), a_c = 0.81 → and swapping the correlation gradient correction for the complete Lee–Yang–Parr functional, mixed with LSDA correlation as c : (1 − c), yields B3LYP, E_xc = a E_x^exact + b E_x^{B88} + (1 − a − b)E_x^{LSDA} + c E_c^{LYP} + (1 − c)E_c^{LSDA}.
