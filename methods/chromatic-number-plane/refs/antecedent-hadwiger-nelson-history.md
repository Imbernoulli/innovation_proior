# Hadwiger–Nelson problem — history and bounds
Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hadwiger%E2%80%93Nelson_problem (retrieved this run)

## Problem statement
How many colors are needed to color the plane so that no two points at unit distance are
the same color? Equivalent to the chromatic number of the unit-distance graph of the plane.

## History
- Origins (1950): Edward Nelson formulated the problem in 1950; first publication in
  Martin Gardner's 1960 Scientific American column. Hugo Hadwiger had published related
  work in 1945 on covering the plane with congruent sets.
- de Bruijn–Erdős reduction (1951): the problem is equivalent (under the axiom of choice)
  to finding the largest possible chromatic number of a FINITE unit-distance graph. This
  is why exhibiting one finite 5-chromatic unit-distance graph raises the lower bound.

## Bounds
Lower bounds:
- 1961: the Moser spindle (7-vertex graph) proves chromatic number ≥ 4.
- Same era: Solomon Golomb independently found a 10-vertex 4-chromatic unit-distance graph.
- 2018: Aubrey de Grey found a 1,581-vertex non-4-colorable unit-distance graph, raising
  the lower bound to 5.
- 2021: smallest known 5-chromatic unit-distance graph has 509 vertices.

Upper bound:
- Isbell's 7-coloring via hexagonal tessellation (hexagons of diameter slightly less than 1).

## Current status
Answer is 5, 6, or 7; may depend on set-theoretic axioms. Before 2018, bounds had been
4 ≤ χ ≤ 7 since 1950 with no improvement to either bound.
