We want to post-train a pretrained language-model policy $\pi_\theta$ to reason — to solve math and competition problems where a rule-based verifier scores any candidate solution as correct (1) or incorrect (0). The dominant recipe is "zero-RL": run on-policy RL with verifiable rewards (GRPO) directly on the base model. For a prompt $q$ it samples a group of $N$ rollouts $\tau_i \sim \pi_{\theta_{old}}$, verifies each, standardizes the rewards within the group into a critic-free advantage $A_i = (R(\tau_i) - \mathrm{mean}) / \mathrm{std}$, and pushes up the high-advantage rollouts through the PPO clipped surrogate. This works beautifully on a prompt the model can already *sometimes* solve, because the group then contains a mix of right and wrong rollouts, the within-group spread is nonzero, and the advantage says which to push up. But take a problem the model cannot solve at all. Every rollout is wrong, every reward is 0, the group mean is 0, the standard deviation is 0, and the standardized advantage is 0 for *every* rollout. The gradient on that prompt is exactly zero. The model learns nothing on precisely the problems where it most needs to learn — and this is structural, not a tuning issue: on-policy RL can only sharpen what the model can already occasionally do, it cannot bootstrap a capability the model never samples. That is why zero-RL stalls on weaker models and harder benchmarks, and why Pass@$k$ at large $k$ does not move: the capability boundary does not expand.

So we need an external signal — a correct solution the model itself cannot produce. Suppose we have one: an off-policy reasoning trace $\tau_{off}$ for the prompt, generated by a much stronger model $\pi_\phi$ (e.g. DeepSeek-R1). The obvious move is SFT, just maximizing the likelihood of $\tau_{off}$. But that triggers the other half of the well-known divide: SFT memorizes the teacher, narrows the model to the demonstration distribution, collapses its exploration, and degrades out of distribution, while RL with verifiable rewards is what generalizes. We do not want to *replace* RL with imitation; we want to *inject* the teacher into the RL signal exactly where on-policy RL has nothing, and keep RL everywhere else. SFT$\to$RL pipelines and RL-with-an-SFT-loss combine both signals but only in a fixed, multi-stage recipe whose order and coefficient must be re-tuned, and the SFT stage still narrows the model before RL recovers exploration. The question is how to fold an off-policy trace into the GRPO machinery in a *single* stage so it bootstraps the dead-gradient prompts without turning the run into SFT.

I propose LUFFY — Learning to reason Under oFF-policY guidance. Its first piece is Mixed-Policy GRPO. The cleanest idea is to not treat the off-policy trace as a separate loss at all, but as just another member of the rollout group. For a prompt let the on-policy group be $G_{on} = \{R(\tau_i): \tau_i \sim \pi_{\theta_{old}}\}$ and the off-policy set be $G_{off} = \{R(\tau_j): \tau_j \sim \pi_\phi\}$, and compute the group-relative advantage over the *union*:

$$\hat A_i = \frac{R(\tau_i) - \mathrm{mean}(G_{on}\cup G_{off})}{\mathrm{std}(G_{on}\cup G_{off})}.$$

Watch what this does on the dead-gradient prompt. The on-policy rollouts are all wrong (reward 0); the off-policy trace is correct (reward 1). Now the union has spread — a mean strictly between 0 and 1, a nonzero std — so the off-policy trace earns a large positive advantage and the failing on-policy rollouts a small negative one. The group computation *naturally* assigns high advantage to the teacher exactly when the model cannot produce a correct solution on its own. And on a prompt the model already solves well, its own rollouts are mostly correct, the teacher trace is no longer special, the on-policy rollouts take precedence, and the signal stays self-driven. The balance between imitation and exploration is not a hand-set coefficient — it falls out of the group statistics and self-adjusts with the model's competence. That is the core.

Optimizing over the union requires care, because the on-policy and off-policy members were sampled from different distributions and so need different importance ratios. The on-policy members use the GRPO ratio $r_{i,t}(\theta) = \pi_\theta(\tau_{i,t}\,|\,q,\tau_{i,<t}) / \pi_{\theta_{old}}(\tau_{i,t}\,|\,\cdot)$, calibrating to the pre-update sampler; the off-policy members would use $\hat r_{j,t}(\theta,\phi) = \pi_\theta(\tau_{j,t}\,|\,\cdot) / \pi_\phi(\tau_{j,t}\,|\,\cdot)$, dividing by the *teacher's* probability. The mixed objective is then the sum of two clipped surrogates over the union, normalized by the total token count $Z = \sum_j |\tau_j| + \sum_i |\tau_i|$:

$$J_{Mixed}(\theta) = \frac{1}{Z}\Big[\sum_{j\in off}\sum_t \mathrm{CLIP}(\hat r_{j,t}(\theta,\phi),\hat A_j,\varepsilon) + \sum_{i\in on}\sum_t \mathrm{CLIP}(r_{i,t}(\theta),\hat A_i,\varepsilon)\Big].$$

Both terms share the same $\hat A$ from the union group, and the off-policy denominator being $\pi_\phi$ rather than $\pi_{\theta_{old}}$ matters: since $\pi_\theta$ and $\pi_{\theta_{old}}$ are usually much closer than $\pi_\theta$ and the teacher $\pi_\phi$, the off-policy ratio tends to be smaller, calibrating the gradient from a genuinely distinct distribution.

But $\pi_\phi$ is a practical wall. To compute $\hat r_{j,t}$ I would need the teacher's per-token probabilities on its own trace — running the teacher at training time or storing dense logits — and worse, the teacher and my model may not even share a tokenizer, so $\pi_\phi$ on my tokens is ill-defined. So I set $\pi_\phi = 1$ on the off-policy tokens. Two things follow. First, the off-policy ratio collapses to $\hat r_{j,t} = \pi_\theta(\tau_{j,t}\,|\,\cdot)$ — just the model's own probability of the teacher token, no teacher density, no tokenizer matching, off-the-shelf datasets drop straight in. Second, the clip becomes ill-posed: a $[1-\varepsilon, 1+\varepsilon]$ range around 1 is meaningless for a quantity that is a raw probability in $[0,1]$, not a ratio centered at 1 — so I *omit the clip on the off-policy branch*, leaving $\sum_t \pi_\theta(\tau_{j,t}\,|\,\cdot)\cdot\hat A_j$. Setting $\pi_\phi = 1$ is biased — the honest ratio would use the real teacher density — but the convergence analysis goes through for any well-defined behavior policy, and $\pi_\phi = 1$ is its constant special case; the bias buys tokenizer-independence and the use of any off-the-shelf teacher data, the same trade-off offline RL methods accept. (I also drop the KL term, $\beta = 0$, and follow Dr.GRPO in removing the length and std-error normalizations so the advantage is clean and the only off-policy machinery is the ratio choice.)

Running this in my head over training, a new failure appears — the one the whole field warns about. With the off-policy term $\sum_t \pi_\theta(\tau_{j,t})\cdot\hat A_j$, the cheapest way for the optimizer to raise the objective is to push up the teacher tokens that *already* have high $\pi_\theta$ — the ones the model would have produced anyway — because the gradient $\pi_\theta\cdot\nabla\log\pi_\theta$ scales with $\pi_\theta$. The teacher tokens with *low* $\pi_\theta$ — precisely the deviating, surprising tokens that carry the new reasoning the model lacks — get a tiny gradient and are effectively ignored. The model converges fast onto the parts of the teacher trace it already agrees with and never learns the parts that would expand its capability; entropy collapses, exploration dies, and I am back to an imitator. Made precise: the off-policy per-logit gradient (predicted action equal to the off-policy token, $\pi_\phi=1$) is $f'(\pi)\cdot\pi\cdot\nabla\log\pi\cdot\hat A_j$ with the identity $f$, whose scale is bounded by $\pi(1-\pi)$ — near zero both as $\pi\to 0$ (the tokens I care about) and as $\pi\to 1$, peaking in the middle. The fix is now visible: I need a transformation $f$ of the off-policy ratio whose *derivative* is large where $\pi$ is small.

That is the second piece, policy shaping via regularized importance sampling: replace the raw off-policy ratio $\hat r_{j,t}$ with $f(\hat r_{j,t})$, so the off-policy term becomes $\sum_t f(\hat r_{j,t})\cdot\hat A_j$ and the on-policy term is unchanged,

$$J_{SHAPING}(\theta) = \frac{1}{Z}\Big[\sum_{j\in off}\sum_t f(\hat r_{j,t}(\theta,\phi))\,\hat A_j + \sum_{i\in on}\sum_t \mathrm{CLIP}(r_{i,t}(\theta),\hat A_i,\varepsilon)\Big],\qquad f(x) = \frac{x}{x+\gamma}.$$

I want $f$ increasing (more model-probability on a good teacher token is still good) but with $f'$ large at small $\pi$ and shrinking as $\pi$ grows — the opposite of the identity. The choice $f(x) = x/(x+\gamma)$ does exactly this: it is increasing and concave on $x>0$, saturating toward 1, with $f'(x) = \gamma/(x+\gamma)^2$ *largest at $x=0$* and decaying as $x$ grows. At a low-probability teacher token ($\hat r = \pi$ small) $f'$ is near its maximum and the gradient is amplified relative to the identity; at a high-probability token $f'$ is small and the easy agreement is downweighted. Concretely the shaped scale is $\gamma\,\pi(1-\pi)/(\pi+\gamma)^2$, which as $\pi\to 0$ behaves like $\pi/\gamma$ — relative to the identity's $\pi$, an amplification by $1/\gamma = 10$ for $\gamma = 0.1$. So surprising teacher tokens get an order of magnitude more gradient while easy ones are damped — the entropy-preserving emphasis I wanted — and an informal variance analysis shows the regularized weights also have smaller variance than the raw ratio, so training is steadier. The constant $\gamma$ tunes the strength: small $\gamma$ saturates fast (strong emphasis on low-probability tokens), large $\gamma$ tends back toward vanilla; a sweep over $\{0.01, 0.1, 0.2\}$ lands on $\gamma = 0.1$.

Assembled, the method is one single-stage mixed-policy GRPO. For each prompt, keep the off-policy teacher trace as one group member and the on-policy rollouts as the rest (in practice 1 off-policy + 7 on-policy, a group of 8), verify all of them, and compute the group-relative advantage over the union so the teacher earns high advantage when the model fails and low advantage when it succeeds. Optimize the on-policy members through the standard clipped GRPO surrogate with ratio $\pi_\theta/\pi_{\theta_{old}}$, and the off-policy members through the *unclipped, shaped* term $\sum_t f(\pi_\theta(\tau_{j,t}))\cdot\hat A_j$ with $f(x) = x/(x+\gamma)$, $\gamma = 0.1$, $\pi_\phi = 1$. Drop the KL ($\beta = 0$), use Dr.GRPO normalization, entropy coefficient 0.01, lr $1\mathrm{e}{-6}$ with AdamW, rollout temperature 1.0, a single pass over a 45k OpenR1-Math-220k subset whose off-policy traces come from DeepSeek-R1 (filtered to $\le 8192$ tokens, verified correct). Off-policy guidance bootstraps the prompts on-policy RL cannot, while the shaping keeps the model exploring rather than collapsing into the teacher — and the result beats on-policy GRPO in-distribution and gains markedly out-of-distribution.

from collections import defaultdict

import torch


def masked_mean(values: torch.Tensor, mask: torch.Tensor) -> torch.Tensor:
    return (values * mask).sum() / mask.sum().clamp_min(1.0)


def compute_mixed_group_advantage(rewards, index, epsilon=1e-6):
    """Eq. 4: standardize advantage over the UNION of on-policy and off-policy rollouts."""
    id2rewards = defaultdict(list)
    for i in range(rewards.shape[0]):
        id2rewards[int(index[i])].append(rewards[i])
    id2mean, id2std = {}, {}
    for uid, vals in id2rewards.items():
        g = torch.stack(vals)
        id2mean[uid] = g.mean()
        id2std[uid] = g.std() if g.numel() > 1 else torch.tensor(1.0, device=rewards.device)
    advantages = torch.zeros_like(rewards)
    for i in range(rewards.shape[0]):
        uid = int(index[i])
        advantages[i] = (rewards[i] - id2mean[uid]) / (id2std[uid] + epsilon)
    return advantages


def shaping_fn(x, gamma=0.1):
    """f(x) = x/(x+gamma); f'(x)=gamma/(x+gamma)^2 is largest at x->0 (emphasize low-prob tokens)."""
    return x / (x + gamma)


def mixed_policy_loss(log_prob, old_log_prob, advantages, response_mask, is_off_policy,
                      clip_ratio=0.2, gamma=0.1):
    """Eq. 5/6: clipped on-policy surrogate + unclipped shaped off-policy term (pi_phi = 1)."""
    adv_t = advantages.unsqueeze(-1)
    on_mask = ~is_off_policy
    ratio_on = torch.exp(log_prob - old_log_prob)
    surr1 = ratio_on * adv_t
    surr2 = torch.clamp(ratio_on, 1 - clip_ratio, 1 + clip_ratio) * adv_t
    on_term = torch.min(surr1, surr2)
    pi_theta = torch.exp(log_prob)                         # pi_phi = 1 -> off-policy ratio = pi_theta
    off_term = shaping_fn(pi_theta, gamma) * adv_t         # unclipped, shaped
    per_token = torch.where(on_mask.unsqueeze(-1), on_term, off_term)
    return -masked_mean(per_token, response_mask)
