Let me start from the wall, because it is sharp and it is exactly where zero-RL gives up. I run on-policy
RLVR — GRPO — on a math model: per prompt I sample a group of rollouts, the verifier scores each
correct or wrong, I standardize the rewards within the group to an advantage, and I push up the
high-advantage rollouts with the clipped surrogate. This works beautifully on a prompt the model can
already *sometimes* solve, because the group then contains a mix of right and wrong rollouts, the
within-group spread is nonzero, and the advantage tells me which to push up. But take a problem the
model cannot solve at all. Every rollout in the group is wrong, every reward is 0, the group mean is 0,
the standard deviation is 0, and the standardized advantage `A_i = (R(τ_i) − mean)/std` is 0 for *every*
rollout. The gradient on that prompt is exactly zero. The model learns nothing on precisely the problems
where it most needs to learn. And this is not a tuning issue — it is structural: on-policy RL can only
sharpen what the model can already occasionally do; it cannot bootstrap a capability the model never
samples. That is why zero-RL works on Qwen but stalls on weaker models or harder benchmarks, and why
Pass@k at large k does not move under RLVR — the capability boundary does not expand.

So I need an external signal: a correct solution the model itself cannot produce. Suppose I have one —
an off-policy reasoning trace `τ_off` for the prompt, generated by a much stronger model. The obvious
move is SFT: just maximize the likelihood of `τ_off`. But I know the other half of the divide. SFT
memorizes the teacher, narrows the model to the demonstration distribution, collapses its exploration,
and degrades out of distribution. RL with verifiable rewards is what generalizes. So I do not want to
*replace* RL with imitation of the teacher; I want to *inject* the teacher into the RL signal exactly
where on-policy RL has nothing, and keep RL everywhere else. The question is how to fold an off-policy
trace into the GRPO machinery so that it bootstraps the dead-gradient prompts without turning the run
into SFT.

The cleanest idea: do not treat the off-policy trace as a separate loss at all. Treat it as just another
member of the group. For a prompt, the on-policy group is `G_on = {R(τ_i) : τ_i ~ π_{θ_old}}` and the
off-policy set is `G_off = {R(τ_j) : τ_j ~ π_φ}` (the stronger model `π_φ` produced the teacher trace).
Compute the group-relative advantage over the *union*:

  Â_i = (R(τ_i) − mean(G_on ∪ G_off)) / std(G_on ∪ G_off).

Watch what this does on the dead-gradient prompt. The on-policy rollouts are all wrong (reward 0); the
off-policy trace is correct (reward 1). Now the union has spread — mean somewhere between 0 and 1, nonzero
std — so the off-policy trace gets a large positive advantage and the failing on-policy rollouts get a
(small) negative one. The group computation *naturally* assigns high advantage to the off-policy rollout
exactly when the model struggles to produce a correct solution on its own. And on a prompt the model
already solves well, its own rollouts are mostly correct, the off-policy trace is no longer special, the
on-policy rollouts take precedence, and the signal stays self-driven. The mixing between imitation and
exploration is not a hand-set coefficient — it falls out of the group statistics and self-adjusts with
the model's competence. That is the core of it.

Now I have to actually optimize over the union, and the on-policy and off-policy members are not
symmetric — they were sampled from different distributions, so they need different importance ratios.
For the on-policy members the GRPO ratio is `r_{i,t}(θ) = π_θ(τ_{i,t}|q,τ_{i,<t}) / π_{θ_old}(...)`,
calibrating the gradient to the pre-update rollout policy. For the off-policy members the analogous ratio
would be `r̂_{j,t}(θ,φ) = π_θ(τ_{j,t}|q,τ_{j,<t}) / π_φ(τ_{j,t}|q,τ_{j,<t})`, dividing by the *teacher's*
probability. So the mixed-policy objective is the sum of two clipped surrogates over the union,
normalized by the total token count `Z = Σ_j |τ_j| + Σ_i |τ_i|`:

  J_Mixed(θ) = (1/Z) [ Σ_{j∈off} Σ_t CLIP(r̂_{j,t}(θ,φ), Â_j, ε) + Σ_{i∈on} Σ_t CLIP(r_{i,t}(θ), Â_i, ε) ].

This is consistent — both terms use the same `Â` from the union group — and the off-policy ratio's
denominator being `π_φ` rather than `π_{θ_old}` matters: since `π_θ` and `π_{θ_old}` are usually much
closer than `π_θ` and the teacher `π_φ`, the off-policy ratio `r̂` tends to be smaller, which calibrates
the off-policy gradient from a genuinely distinct distribution.

But here I hit a practical problem with `π_φ`. To compute `r̂_{j,t}` I would need the teacher's per-token
probabilities on its own trace, which means either running the teacher at training time or storing dense
logits — and worse, the teacher and my model may not even share a tokenizer, so `π_φ` on my tokens is
ill-defined. Let me see whether I can avoid `π_φ` entirely. Set `π_φ = 1` on the off-policy tokens. Two
things happen. First, the off-policy ratio collapses to `r̂_{j,t} = π_θ(τ_{j,t}|...)` — just the model's
own probability of the teacher token, no teacher density needed, no tokenizer matching, off-the-shelf
datasets drop straight in. Second, the clip becomes ill-posed: a clip range `[1−ε, 1+ε]` around 1 makes
no sense for a quantity that is a raw probability in `[0,1]`, not a ratio centered at 1 — so I *omit the
clip on the off-policy branch*. The off-policy term becomes `Σ_t π_θ(τ_{j,t}|...) · Â_j`, unclipped. Is
`π_φ = 1` legitimate? It is a biased choice — the honest importance ratio would use the real `π_φ` — but
the convergence analysis still goes through for any well-defined behavior distribution, and `π_φ = 1` is
the constant special case; the bias buys tokenizer-independence and the ability to use any off-the-shelf
teacher data. I accept the bias for the practicality, the same trade-off offline RL methods make. (I also
drop the KL term, `β = 0`, and follow Dr.GRPO by removing the length and std-error normalizations, so the
advantage is clean and the only off-policy machinery is the ratio choice.)

Now run the mixed-policy objective in my head over training and a new failure appears — and it is the one
the whole field warns about. With the off-policy term `Σ_t π_θ(τ_{j,t}) · Â_j`, the cheapest way for the
optimizer to raise the off-policy objective is to push up the teacher tokens that *already* have high
`π_θ` — the ones the model would have produced anyway. Those contribute the most because `π_θ` is already
large and the gradient `π_θ · ∇log π_θ` scales with `π_θ`. Meanwhile the teacher tokens with *low* `π_θ`
— precisely the deviating, surprising tokens that carry the new reasoning the model does not yet have —
get a tiny gradient and are effectively ignored. So the model converges fast onto the parts of the
teacher trace it already agrees with and never learns the parts that would actually expand its
capability. Entropy collapses, exploration dies, and I am back to a fast-converging imitator. This is the
"hacking" of the mixed objective: it imitates the easy agreement and skips the hard, valuable
disagreement.

Let me make the failure precise by looking at the off-policy gradient. Write `π = π_θ(τ_{j,t})` for short.
The off-policy term is `f(r̂) · Â_j` with `f` the identity (vanilla mixed-policy is `f(x) = x`), and
`r̂ = π` (since `π_φ = 1`). Its gradient with respect to the logits, when the predicted action equals the
off-policy token, is bounded by something proportional to `π(1−π)`: it is `f'(π) · π · ∇log π · Â_j` and
with `f' = 1` the scale is `π(1−π)`, which is near zero both when `π → 0` (the low-probability tokens I
care about) and when `π → 1`, peaking in the middle. So the surprising teacher tokens — small `π` — get
the smallest gradient. That is the mechanism of the collapse, and now I can see what to fix: I need a
transformation `f` of the off-policy ratio whose *derivative* is large where `π` is small, so the gradient
emphasizes the low-probability tokens instead of suppressing them.

So replace the raw off-policy ratio `r̂_{j,t}` with `f(r̂_{j,t})` — *policy shaping via regularized
importance sampling*. The objective's off-policy term becomes `Σ_t f(r̂_{j,t}(θ,φ)) · Â_j`, the on-policy
term unchanged. What should `f` be? I want `f` increasing (more model-probability on a good teacher token
is still good), but with `f'` large at small `π` and shrinking as `π` grows — the opposite of the
identity's behavior. The function `f(x) = x / (x + γ)` does exactly this. It is increasing and concave on
`x > 0`, saturating toward 1 as `x` grows, and its derivative `f'(x) = γ/(x+γ)²` is *largest* at `x = 0`
and decays as `x` grows. So at a low-probability teacher token (`r̂ = π` small) `f'` is near its maximum
and the gradient on that token is amplified relative to the identity; at a high-probability token `f'` is
small and the already-easy agreement is downweighted. The shaping reweights the gradient toward the
low-probability, crucial actions — exactly the deviating reasoning steps the vanilla mixed objective
ignored. The constant `γ` controls the strength: small `γ` makes `f` saturate fast (strong emphasis on
low-probability tokens), large `γ` makes `f` more linear (back toward vanilla). A sweep over
{0.01, 0.1, 0.2} lands on `γ = 0.1` as the balance.

Let me confirm the shaping does the right thing to the gradient and does not just rescale everything. The
shaped off-policy gradient is `∇J_shaping-off = E_{τ~π_φ}[ f'(π) · (π/π_φ) · ∇log π · Â_j ]`; with
`π_φ = 1` and per-logit, when the predicted action is the off-policy token this is
`f'(π) · π · ∇log π · Â_j`, whose scale is bounded by `f'(π) · π(1−π) · |Â_j|`. With the identity `f` the
scale was `π(1−π)`, vanishing at small `π`. With `f(x) = x/(x+γ)`, `f'(π) = γ/(π+γ)²`, so the scale is
`γ·π(1−π)/(π+γ)²`; as `π → 0` this tends to `π/γ`·(1) which, relative to the identity's `π`, is amplified
by `1/γ = 10` for `γ = 0.1`. So small-`π` teacher tokens get an order of magnitude more gradient than they
would under the vanilla objective, while large-`π` tokens are damped. That is the entropy-preserving
emphasis I wanted, and an informal variance analysis shows the regularized weights also have *smaller*
variance than the raw ratio, so training is more stable as well.

Now assemble the full method. For each prompt: keep the off-policy teacher trace as one member and the `N`
on-policy rollouts as the rest (in practice 1 off-policy + 7 on-policy, group of 8). Verify all of them.
Compute the group-relative advantage over the *union* `G_on ∪ G_off` — so the teacher gets high advantage
when the model fails and low advantage when the model succeeds. Optimize the mixed-policy objective: the
on-policy members through the standard clipped GRPO surrogate with ratio `π_θ/π_{θ_old}`, and the
off-policy members through the *unclipped, shaped* term `Σ_t f(π_θ(τ_{j,t})) · Â_j` with `f(x) = x/(x+γ)`,
`γ = 0.1`, and `π_φ = 1`. Drop the KL (`β = 0`), use Dr.GRPO normalization (remove length and std-error
normalization), entropy coefficient 0.01, lr 1e-6 with AdamW, rollout temperature 1.0, single pass over
a mixed OpenR1 subset whose off-policy traces come from DeepSeek-R1. Together these make off-policy
guidance bootstrap the prompts on-policy RL cannot, while the shaping keeps the model exploring rather
than collapsing into the teacher.

Let me write the core as the real code I would ship: one function forms the union-group advantage, one
forms the shaped off-policy + clipped on-policy mixed loss, with `π_φ = 1` baked in so the off-policy term
is just `f(π_θ) · Â`.

```python
from collections import defaultdict

import torch


def masked_mean(values: torch.Tensor, mask: torch.Tensor) -> torch.Tensor:
    """Dr.GRPO-style aggregation over valid response tokens (no length normalization here)."""
    return (values * mask).sum() / mask.sum().clamp_min(1.0)


def compute_mixed_group_advantage(
    rewards: torch.Tensor,        # (B,) verifier score in {0,1} for each trajectory (on- and off-policy)
    index: torch.Tensor,          # (B,) prompt id, grouping on-policy + off-policy of the same prompt
    epsilon: float = 1e-6,
) -> torch.Tensor:
    """Eq. 4: group-relative advantage over the UNION of on-policy and off-policy rollouts.

    The off-policy (teacher) trajectory is just another group member, so when the model's own
    rollouts all fail (reward 0) and the teacher is correct (reward 1), the union has spread and the
    teacher gets a large positive advantage — bootstrapping exactly the dead-gradient prompts.
    """
    id2rewards = defaultdict(list)
    for i in range(rewards.shape[0]):
        id2rewards[int(index[i])].append(rewards[i])

    id2mean, id2std = {}, {}
    for uid, vals in id2rewards.items():
        g = torch.stack(vals)
        id2mean[uid] = g.mean()
        id2std[uid] = g.std() if g.numel() > 1 else torch.tensor(1.0, device=rewards.device)

    advantages = torch.zeros_like(rewards)
    for i in range(rewards.shape[0]):
        uid = int(index[i])
        advantages[i] = (rewards[i] - id2mean[uid]) / (id2std[uid] + epsilon)
    return advantages


def shaping_fn(x: torch.Tensor, gamma: float = 0.1) -> torch.Tensor:
    """Regularized-importance-sampling shaping f(x) = x / (x + gamma).

    f' = gamma / (x + gamma)^2 is largest at x -> 0, so the gradient is amplified on
    low-probability (surprising) teacher tokens and damped on already-likely ones.
    """
    return x / (x + gamma)


def mixed_policy_loss(
    log_prob: torch.Tensor,         # (B, T) under current pi_theta
    old_log_prob: torch.Tensor,     # (B, T) under pi_theta_old (on-policy sampler)
    advantages: torch.Tensor,       # (B,) from compute_mixed_group_advantage, broadcast over tokens
    response_mask: torch.Tensor,    # (B, T) 1 on response tokens
    is_off_policy: torch.Tensor,    # (B,) True for teacher-trace rows
    clip_ratio: float = 0.2,
    gamma: float = 0.1,
) -> torch.Tensor:
    """Eq. 5 + Eq. 6: clipped on-policy surrogate + unclipped shaped off-policy term.

    Off-policy uses pi_phi = 1, so the off-policy ratio is just pi_theta(token) = exp(log_prob);
    the clip is omitted on that branch (it is ill-posed for a raw probability) and replaced by the
    shaping f(.). On-policy uses the standard GRPO clipped surrogate with ratio pi_theta/pi_theta_old.
    """
    adv_t = advantages.unsqueeze(-1)                                  # (B, 1) broadcast over tokens
    on_mask = ~is_off_policy

    # on-policy: GRPO clipped surrogate, ratio = pi_theta / pi_theta_old
    ratio_on = torch.exp(log_prob - old_log_prob)
    surr1 = ratio_on * adv_t
    surr2 = torch.clamp(ratio_on, 1 - clip_ratio, 1 + clip_ratio) * adv_t
    on_term = torch.min(surr1, surr2)                                 # (B, T)

    # off-policy: pi_phi = 1 -> ratio = pi_theta(token); unclipped, shaped by f(x) = x/(x+gamma)
    pi_theta = torch.exp(log_prob)
    off_term = shaping_fn(pi_theta, gamma) * adv_t                    # (B, T)

    per_token = torch.where(on_mask.unsqueeze(-1), on_term, off_term)
    return -masked_mean(per_token, response_mask)                     # maximize -> minimize negative
```

So the causal chain, start to finish. On-policy RLVR contributes exactly zero gradient on the prompts the
model fails completely — every rollout wrong makes the group-relative advantage vanish — so it cannot
bootstrap a capability the model never samples, and the capability boundary (Pass@k) does not move.
Injecting an off-policy teacher trace as a separate SFT loss would memorize and collapse exploration, so
instead I make the teacher trace one more member of the group and standardize the advantage over the
*union* of on-policy and off-policy rollouts; the teacher then automatically gets high advantage exactly
when the model struggles and low advantage when it succeeds, so imitation-versus-exploration self-adjusts
with competence. Optimizing the union needs an off-policy importance ratio whose denominator is the
teacher policy; to avoid the teacher's densities and tokenizer I set `π_φ = 1`, collapsing the off-policy
ratio to the model's own probability and forcing me to drop the (now ill-posed) clip on that branch.
That bare off-policy term then *hacks* the objective — its gradient scales with `π_θ`, so it pushes the
teacher tokens the model already agrees with and ignores the low-probability surprising tokens that carry
the new reasoning, collapsing entropy. The fix is policy shaping: replace the off-policy ratio with
`f(r̂) = r̂/(r̂+γ)`, whose derivative is largest at small `π_θ`, amplifying the gradient on exactly the
low-probability crucial tokens and damping the easy agreement, with `γ = 0.1`. The result is one
single-stage mixed-policy GRPO with a shaped, unclipped off-policy term and a clipped on-policy term over
a shared union-group advantage — off-policy guidance that bootstraps beyond the model's own capability
while preserving the exploration that makes RL generalize.
