Let me start from the thing that keeps bothering me, before I reach for any machinery. So let me not lean on any single example. Let me ask: what do these events have in *common*? Take the binary-digit frequency event, `{ (1/n)∑_{k≤n} d_k → 1/2 }`. Suppose I reach in and flip the first ten digits of every outcome — `d₁,…,d₁₀` become whatever I like. Does that change whether the running average converges to `1/2`? It cannot. A finite change to the numerator is washed out by the `1/n`. The event is *exactly the same set of outcomes* after I tamper with the first ten coordinates. Now the convergence of `∑ Xₙ`: if the tail of the series converges, prepending finitely many fixed numbers still converges; if it diverges, prepending finitely many terms can't rescue it. Invariant under changing any finite prefix. The radius of convergence of `∑ Xₙ zⁿ` is `1/limsupₙ |Xₙ|^{1/n}`, and a `limsup` ignores any finite block of terms — invariant again. And `{Aₙ infinitely often}`: whether infinitely many of the `Aₙ` occur cannot be decided by the first hundred of them.

That's the common feature, and it's sharp: each of these events is insensitive to any finite prefix of the sequence. Whatever the first `n` coordinates do, membership in the event is unaffected; only the behaviour "arbitrarily far out" matters. So if there is one principle, the hypothesis has to be exactly this — the event depends only on the asymptotic part of the sequence, not on any finite initial segment.

Let me turn "depends only on the tail" into a clean measure-theoretic object, because vague phrasing won't let me prove anything. I have a sequence of random variables `X₁, X₂, …`. The events I can build from the *first* `n` of them form a σ-field — call it `𝔉ₙ = σ(X₁, …, Xₙ)`. The events I can build from coordinates *strictly after* `n` form `𝔗ₙ = σ(X_{n+1}, X_{n+2}, …)`. An event that doesn't depend on the first `n` coordinates lives in `𝔗ₙ`. An event that doesn't depend on *any* finite prefix — no matter how long — must live in `𝔗ₙ` for *every* `n`. So the right object is the intersection

`𝔗 = ⋂ₙ 𝔗ₙ = ⋂ₙ σ(X_{n+1}, X_{n+2}, …)`.

This is a σ-field (an intersection of σ-fields is again a σ-field), and it is precisely the class of events knowable from "stuff arbitrarily far into the future" of the sequence — the events I can still decide after throwing away any finite number of leading coordinates. Every one of my examples is a member of `𝔗`. Let me call its elements tail events. The claim I'm chasing now has a crisp form: *if* the `Xₙ` are independent, *then* every tail event has probability `0` or `1`.

I should ask straight away whether independence is really needed, because I'd love to drop it. The negative control says no. Take a dependent sequence — a Markov chain, where each step's distribution depends on the present state, `P_{𝔄⁽¹⁾…𝔄⁽ⁿ⁻¹⁾}(A⁽ⁿ⁾) = P_{𝔄⁽ⁿ⁻¹⁾}(A⁽ⁿ⁾)`, so the trials are *not* independent. The event "the chain eventually settles in state `a` rather than state `b`" is a tail event (absorption is asymptotic, untouched by finitely many early steps), yet for a chain started off-balance its probability sits squarely inside `(0,1)`. So dependence can give a tail event an intermediate probability; independence is not decoration, it is the hypothesis. Good — I know the shape of the theorem and that both pieces (independence, tail-membership) are load-bearing. The work is to find *why* they combine to forbid the open interval.

Take a tail event `A ∈ 𝔗`. Fix any `n`. Then `A ∈ 𝔗ₙ = σ(X_{n+1}, X_{n+2}, …)`. But being in the tail tells me more than `A`'s location: it tells me `A` is built entirely out of coordinates `X_{n+1}, X_{n+2}, …`, and those are *independent* of the front block `X₁, …, Xₙ`. So `A` should be independent of anything built from `X₁, …, Xₙ`, i.e. independent of the whole σ-field `𝔉ₙ = σ(X₁, …, Xₙ)`. Hold onto that: `A ⟂ 𝔉ₙ`, for every `n`.

Now stare at what `A ⟂ 𝔉ₙ` for *every* `n` is buying me. The front blocks grow: `𝔉₁ ⊆ 𝔉₂ ⊆ 𝔉₃ ⊆ ⋯`, and together they generate the whole σ-field of the sequence, `𝔉_∞ = σ(X₁, X₂, …)`. So `A` is independent of every finite-prefix σ-field, and those swell up to `𝔉_∞`. If independence survives that growth — if "independent of each `𝔉ₙ`" upgrades to "independent of `𝔉_∞`" — then `A` is independent of `𝔉_∞`.

And here is the thing that makes me sit up. `A` is a tail event, so `A ∈ 𝔗 ⊆ 𝔉_∞` — `A` is itself one of the events in `𝔉_∞`. If `A` is independent of `𝔉_∞`, and `A` belongs to `𝔉_∞`, then `A` is independent of *itself*.

What does it mean for an event to be independent of itself? Independence of `A` and `B` is `P(A ∩ B) = P(A) P(B)`. Put `B = A`. Then `P(A ∩ A) = P(A) P(A)`, i.e. `P(A) = P(A)²`. As *sets*, `A ∩ A = A` is a triviality. But independence converts that trivial set identity into a numerical equation, `P(A) = P(A)²`, which is not trivial at all: `t = t²` means `t(1 - t) = 0`, so `t = 0` or `t = 1`. The only fixed points of `t ↦ t²` in `[0,1]` are the two endpoints. The open interval is forbidden. `P(A) ∈ {0, 1}`.

That is the heart of it, and it explains the pattern in one stroke: a tail event is forced, by independence, to be independent of itself, and self-independence squeezes its probability onto the roots of `t = t²`. Every one of my examples is an instance.

But I've leaned on a step I only asserted: that "`A` independent of each `𝔉ₙ`" upgrades to "`A` independent of the union's σ-field `𝔉_∞`." Independence of `A` from each member of a *growing* family does not, on its face, give independence from everything in the σ-field they generate — generating a σ-field involves countable unions, complements, limits, and I have to check the product rule survives all of that. This is the place the whole argument can quietly fail. Let me build it carefully, and build it twice — once the way the foundations are actually laid out, using the uniqueness of measure extensions, and once in cleaner packaging — so I can see they are the same fact.

The first route uses the machinery already sitting underneath everything: the extension theorem. Recall how the foundations are organized. The finite-dimensional events — those describable through finitely many coordinates — form a *field* `𝔉`, closed under finite unions, intersections, complements. Its smallest enclosing σ-field is `B𝔉 = 𝔉_∞`, the Borel extension. And the extension theorem says a non-negative completely additive set function on the field `𝔉` extends to `B𝔉` in *exactly one way* — the uniqueness coming from the minimal property of `B𝔉`. The values on the generating field pin the measure down on the entire σ-field. That uniqueness is the lever.

Let me set it up as a comparison of two measures. Fix the tail event `A` and suppose `P(A) > 0` (if `P(A) = 0` the claim already holds, nothing to prove). With `A` fixed and of positive probability, the conditional probability `P_A(·) = P(A ∩ ·)/P(A)` is itself a probability measure: comparing its defining properties with the axioms shows `P_A(B) ≥ 0`, `P_A(E) = 1`, `P_A(B + C) = P_A(B) + P_A(C)`, and complete additivity is inherited from `P`. So I have two probability measures on the same Borel field: the original `P`, and the conditional `P_A`. I want to force them to agree everywhere, then read off the conclusion from their values at `A`.

Where do they agree to start with? On the field `𝔉` of finite-dimensional events. Take any `B ∈ 𝔉` — an event described by finitely many coordinates, hence `B ∈ 𝔉ₙ` for some `n`. Because `A` is a tail event it is independent of `𝔉ₙ`, so `A` and `B` are independent: `P(A ∩ B) = P(A) P(B)`, equivalently `P_B(A) = P(A)`. Now compute `P_A(B)` from the multiplication rule. By Bayes' form `P_A(B) = P(A∩B)/P(A) = P_B(A) P(B)/P(A)`, and substituting `P_B(A) = P(A)` gives `P_A(B) = P(A) P(B)/P(A) = P(B)`. So

`P_A(B) = P(B)`  for every `B ∈ 𝔉`.

The two measures `P_A` and `P` coincide on the entire generating field `𝔉`. Both are completely additive on `𝔉`. By the *uniqueness* half of the extension theorem, a completely additive set function on a field has only one completely additive extension to the Borel field `B𝔉`. Two extensions agreeing on `𝔉` cannot disagree anywhere on `B𝔉`. Hence

`P_A(B) = P(B)`  for every `B ∈ B𝔉 = 𝔉_∞`.

Now spend that equality at the one event that matters. `A` is a tail event, so `A ∈ 𝔗 ⊆ 𝔉_∞ = B𝔉`. Put `B = A`:

`P_A(A) = P(A)`.

But `P_A(A) = P(A ∩ A)/P(A) = P(A)/P(A) = 1`. Therefore `P(A) = 1`. Combined with the discarded case `P(A) = 0`, every tail event has probability `0` or `1`. And notice the engine: independence made `P_A` and `P` agree on all the finite-dimensional events, and *countable additivity* (the extension theorem rests on it) transported that agreement out to the tail event itself. The transport from "agree on finitely-described events" to "agree on the asymptotic event" is exactly the step countable additivity licenses and that Borel's finitely-additive scruples could not.

Let me dwell on that, because it's the deep reason the theorem couldn't have been stated a generation earlier. Everything in the argument is a transport of an equality from the field `𝔉` (events about finitely many coordinates) to a limiting event `A` that lives only in the σ-field. That transport *is* countable additivity — the unique extension of a measure from a field to its Borel field. On a merely finitely-additive set function the extension is not unique, the agreement on `𝔉` need not propagate, and a tail event could carry a value out of `{0,1}`. Borel, refusing to assume countable additivity, had the phenomenon — he found the first `0`-`1` result, the normal numbers — but not the lever; once probability is a completely additive measure with the continuity axiom, the lever is just sitting there. The law is a *consequence of the axioms*, not a separate miracle.

Now let me redo the upgrade step in cleaner packaging, because the extension-theorem phrasing, while honest, buries the independence structure inside "two measures agree." I want the version that tracks independence directly and uses the smallest possible generating sets. The tool: to check independence of two σ-fields, it suffices to check it on generating families closed under finite intersection — π-systems — and a monotone-class / Dynkin argument then extends it to the whole σ-fields. Let me see this carry the whole proof.

First, the front block against a finite tail block. Fix `n` and `k`. The σ-field `𝔉ₙ = σ(X₁, …, Xₙ)` is generated by the events `A = {X₁ ≤ x₁, …, Xₙ ≤ xₙ}` — finite intersections of coordinate-threshold events, a collection closed under intersection, so a π-system. Likewise `σ(X_{n+1}, …, X_{n+k})` is generated by the π-system `B = {X_{n+1} ≤ y₁, …, X_{n+k} ≤ yₖ}`. By the independence of the variables, the product rule factorizes the joint probability across the two disjoint index blocks, so `P(A ∩ B) = P(A) P(B)` for any such `A` and `B`: every generator of the first is independent of every generator of the second. Independence holding on two generating π-systems extends, by the π–λ theorem, to independence of the two generated σ-fields. So `𝔉ₙ ⟂ σ(X_{n+1}, …, X_{n+k})` for every `k`. Letting `k` grow: the events about finitely many of the later coordinates form a π-system generating `σ(X_{n+1}, X_{n+2}, …) = 𝔗ₙ`, and independence on that π-system extends, so `𝔉ₙ ⟂ 𝔗ₙ`. Since `𝔗 ⊆ 𝔗ₙ`,

`𝔉ₙ ⟂ 𝔗`,  for every `n`.

Now the upgrade I was worried about, done honestly. I have `𝔗` independent of each `𝔉ₙ`. The blocks `𝔉₁ ⊆ 𝔉₂ ⊆ ⋯` increase, and their union `⋃ₙ 𝔉ₙ` is itself a π-system: any two events in the union both sit in some common `𝔉ₙ` (take the larger index), whose intersection is again in `𝔉ₙ`, hence in the union. And `⋃ₙ 𝔉ₙ` generates `𝔉_∞ = σ(X₁, X₂, …)`. Independence of `𝔗` from every `𝔉ₙ` is exactly independence of `𝔗` from this union-π-system; by the π–λ theorem once more, `𝔗` is independent of the σ-field it generates:

`𝔗 ⟂ 𝔉_∞`.

That is the upgrade, now justified: independence from each finite prefix really does become independence from the whole sequence σ-field, because the prefixes form an increasing π-system and independence extends from a π-system to its σ-field. And the self-reference is back, sharp as before: `𝔗 ⊆ 𝔉_∞`, so `𝔗` is independent of a σ-field that *contains* `𝔗`. For any `A ∈ 𝔗`, both arguments of independence can be `A`:

`P(A) = P(A ∩ A) = P(A) · P(A) = P(A)²`,

hence `P(A)(1 - P(A)) = 0`, hence `P(A) ∈ {0, 1}`. Same conclusion, with the independence visible at every line and only π–λ uniqueness doing the heavy lifting. The two routes are the same fact wearing different clothes: "unique extension of a measure from a field" and "extension of independence from a π-system" are both the statement that countable additivity pins a measure down from its values on a generating, intersection-closed family.

Let me make sure I haven't smuggled anything past the negative controls. If the `Xₙ` are *not* independent, the first step breaks: the front block need not be independent of the tail blocks, so `𝔗 ⟂ 𝔉_∞` fails, `A` is not independent of itself, and `P(A) = P(A)²` is no longer forced — exactly leaving room for the Markov absorption event to sit in `(0,1)`. The hypothesis is used essentially. And an event like `{X₁ = 1}` for a fair bit, probability `1/2`: is it a tail event? No — it depends on the first coordinate, so it is not in `𝔗₁ = σ(X₂, X₃, …)`, hence not in `𝔗`. The theorem says nothing about it, correctly; the intermediate value is allowed precisely for non-tail events. The dichotomy bites only on the asymptotic events, which is what I wanted.

Now I want to push the conclusion further, because "the *event* has probability `0` or `1`" should imply something stronger about asymptotic *quantities*. Take a random variable `Y` measurable with respect to the tail σ-field `𝔗` — for instance `Y = limsup Sₙ/n`, or the radius of convergence `R = 1/limsupₙ |Xₙ|^{1/n}`, or the limit `lim (1/n)∑ dₖ` when it exists. Each is built only from the asymptotic behaviour, so `{Y ≤ y} ∈ 𝔗` for every real `y`. (That `limsup Xₙ` is even measurable is itself a small check: `sup_{k≥n} X_k` is measurable since `{sup_{k≥n} X_k ≤ x} = ⋂_{k≥n} {X_k ≤ x}`, and then `limsup Xₙ = inf_n sup_{k≥n} X_k` with `{limsup Xₙ ≤ x} = ⋂_m ⋃_n {sup_{k≥n} X_k ≤ x + 1/m}` — built by countable unions and intersections from the `{X_k ≤ x}`, so it is in the σ-field.) By the law just proved, the distribution function `F_Y(y) = P(Y ≤ y)` takes only the values `0` and `1`.

Let me extract the constant carefully rather than wave at it. `F_Y` is non-decreasing, right-continuous, with `F_Y(-∞) = 0` and `F_Y(+∞) = 1`, and now `{0,1}`-valued. Define `x₀ = inf\{ c : P(Y ≤ c) = 1 \}` and `x₁ = ` the smallest `c` with `P(Y > c) = 0`; these are the two ways to name the jump, and I should check they coincide. Suppose `x₀ < x₁`. Then there is `ε > 0` with `x₀ < x₁ - ε < x₁`. By definition of `x₁`, `P(Y > x₁ - ε) = 1` (it can't be `0`, since `x₁ - ε < x₁`). But `x₁ - ε > x₀`, so by definition of `x₀`, `P(Y ≤ x₁ - ε) = 1` as well. Then `P(E) = P(Y > x₁-ε) + P(Y ≤ x₁-ε) = 1 + 1 = 2`, impossible. Suppose instead `x₀ > x₁`. Then `P(Y ≤ x₁) = 1 - P(Y > x₁) = 1`, contradicting that `x₀` was the infimum of such `c`. So `x₀ = x₁ =: c`: `P(Y < c) = 0` and `P(Y > c) = 0`, hence `P(Y = c) = 1`. A tail-measurable random variable is almost surely a constant.

Let me feel the weight of that. The strong-law limit `lim Sₙ/n`, *before I compute its value*, is already a deterministic number with probability one — its randomness is gone; only "which number?" survives. The radius of convergence of a random power series with independent coefficients is, with probability one, a fixed constant, not a random radius. The long-run frequency of `1`'s in an independent bit stream is a sure constant — Borel's theorem then merely identifies it as `1/2`. The `0`-`1` law splits every such asymptotic question into a free part (it is deterministic) and a residual part (what the deterministic value is); only the residual needs real work.

Let me also reconcile this with how I'd phrase the hypothesis if I didn't yet have the tidy "tail σ-field" name in hand — because the structural content shouldn't depend on packaging, and I want the most general sufficient condition. Describe the event through a single function of *all* the coordinates: `A = { f(X₁, X₂, …) = 0 }`, where `f` is a Baire function — anything obtainable from polynomials by iterated limits, which is exactly the `𝔉_∞`-measurable functions. The property "insensitive to a finite prefix" becomes "the value of `f` is unchanged when only finitely many of the variables are altered." For such an `f`, knowing `X₁, …, Xₙ` tells you nothing about whether `f = 0`: the conditional probability of `{f = 0}` given the first `n` variables equals its unconditional probability, for every `n`. That conditional-equals-unconditional statement *is* the independence of `A` from each finite prefix — the same hypothesis, written about conditional probabilities rather than about a σ-field. And it suggests the cleanest generality: I never actually needed full independence of the `Xₙ`; I only needed, for the particular event `A = {f = 0}`, that

`P_{X₁,…,Xₙ}\{ f = 0 \} = P\{ f = 0 \}`  for every `n`.

Whenever that holds — `A` independent of every finite prefix — the extension-theorem argument runs verbatim: `P_A` and `P` agree on `𝔉`, hence on `B𝔉`, hence `P_A(A) = P(A)`, hence `P(A) = 1` (or `P(A) = 0`). Independence of the `Xₙ` together with finite-change-invariance of `f` is just the convenient sufficient condition that *guarantees* this hypothesis; the hypothesis itself is the bare minimum the proof consumes.

Let me state the result the way the proof actually earned it, and write the proof out in full so nothing is gestured at.

**Theorem (zero-or-one law).** Let `x₁, x₂, …` be random variables on a probability space with completely additive `P`, let `f` be a Baire function of `(x₁, x₂, …)`, and `A = { f(x) = 0 }`. Suppose that for every `n` the conditional probability of `f(x) = 0` given the first `n` variables equals its absolute probability,

`P_{x₁,…,xₙ}\{ f(x) = 0 \} = P\{ f(x) = 0 \}`,

i.e. `A` is independent of `σ(x₁, …, xₙ)` for each `n`. Then `P(A) ∈ {0, 1}`. In particular this holds whenever the `xₙ` are mutually independent and the value of `f` is unchanged by altering finitely many of the variables — that is, for every event in the tail σ-field `𝔗 = ⋂ₙ σ(x_{n+1}, x_{n+2}, …)` of an independent sequence. Moreover, any `𝔗`-measurable random variable is almost surely constant.

**Proof.** Let `𝔉` be the field of all events describable through finitely many of the variables (the cylinder field); its Borel extension is `B𝔉 = σ(x₁, x₂, …)`, and `A ∈ B𝔉` because `f` is a Baire function of all the coordinates.

If `P(A) = 0` we are done. Suppose `P(A) > 0`. Take any `B ∈ 𝔉`; it involves only finitely many coordinates, say the first `n` (enlarge `n` if needed), so `B ∈ σ(x₁, …, xₙ)`. The hypothesis gives `P_B(A) = P(A)`, equivalently `P(A ∩ B) = P(A) P(B)`. Hence
`P_A(B) = P(A ∩ B)/P(A) = P(A) P(B)/P(A) = P(B)`.
So the two measures `P_A` and `P` — both non-negative and completely additive, since `A` is fixed with `P(A) > 0` — coincide on the field `𝔉`. By the uniqueness of the extension of a completely additive set function from a field to its Borel extension, they coincide on all of `B𝔉`. Since `A ∈ B𝔉`, taking `B = A` gives `P_A(A) = P(A)`. But `P_A(A) = P(A ∩ A)/P(A) = P(A)/P(A) = 1`. Therefore `P(A) = 1`. In all cases `P(A) ∈ {0, 1}`.

For the special case: if the `xₙ` are mutually independent and `f` is unchanged by altering finitely many variables, then `A = {f = 0}` lies in `σ(x_{n+1}, …)` for every `n` (its membership ignores the first `n` coordinates), so `A` is independent of `σ(x₁, …, xₙ)` for each `n` — exactly the hypothesis. Equivalently, in σ-field language: the increasing prefix σ-fields `σ(x₁, …, xₙ)` are each independent of `𝔗`, since the variables' independence gives the product rule on the generating π-systems `{x₁ ≤ x_1', …, xₙ ≤ x_n'}` and `{x_{n+1} ≤ y_1, …, x_{n+k} ≤ y_k}`, which extends by the π–λ theorem to the generated σ-fields. The union `⋃ₙ σ(x₁, …, xₙ)` is a π-system generating `σ(x₁, x₂, …)`, so `𝔗 ⟂ σ(x₁, x₂, …)`; and since `𝔗 ⊆ σ(x₁, x₂, …)`, every `A ∈ 𝔗` is independent of itself, `P(A) = P(A ∩ A) = P(A)²`, so `P(A) ∈ {0, 1}`.

For the corollary: if `Y` is `𝔗`-measurable then `{Y ≤ y} ∈ 𝔗`, so `F_Y(y) = P(Y ≤ y) ∈ {0, 1}` for all `y`. Being non-decreasing and right-continuous with values in `{0, 1}` and limits `0` at `-∞` and `1` at `+∞`, `F_Y` equals `0` for `y < c` and `1` for `y ≥ c`, where `c = inf\{ y : F_Y(y) = 1 \}`; the case analysis above rules out a gap or a reversal, giving `P(Y < c) = P(Y > c) = 0`. This is the distribution of the constant `c`, so `Y = c` almost surely. ∎

The causal chain, start to end: I noticed several limiting probabilities pinned to `0` or `1`; I asked what those events share and found it is insensitivity to any finite prefix, which I made precise as membership in the tail σ-field `𝔗 = ⋂ₙ σ(x_{n+1}, …)`; under independence a tail event is independent of every finite prefix, the prefixes generate the entire sequence σ-field, independence extends from those generators to the whole σ-field (by the extension theorem's uniqueness, equivalently by the π–λ theorem — both incarnations of countable additivity pinning a measure down from a generating intersection-closed family), and that σ-field *contains* the tail event, so the tail event is independent of itself; self-independence turns the trivial `A ∩ A = A` into `P(A) = P(A)²`, whose only roots in `[0,1]` are `0` and `1`; and the same tail-measurability makes any asymptotic quantity almost surely a deterministic constant.
