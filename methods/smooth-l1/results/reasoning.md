Let me start from what actually goes wrong when I train this predictor. I have a target latent tensor from the encoder and a predicted latent tensor from the autoregressive roll-out, both `[B, C, T, H, W]`, and I need one scalar that says how far apart they are. The obvious thing ‚ÄĒ the thing sitting in the slot right now ‚ÄĒ is squared error, `¬Ĺ r¬≤` summed over every coordinate of the residual `r = target ‚ąí predicted`. And for the coordinates where the prediction is already decent it's fine. The trouble is the coordinates where it isn't. In an autoregressive roll-out the errors compound step to step; early in training the predictor is bad; some frames are just hard; and the encoder target itself is a moving, slightly noisy thing because it's being learned at the same time. So at any given moment a chunk of my residual coordinates are small and a few are large, and I have to ask what each kind contributes to the gradient.

What drives the parameter update is not `ŌĀ` itself but its derivative with respect to the residual, `Ōą(r) = ŌĀ'(r)` ‚ÄĒ that's the per-coordinate pull. For squared error `Ōą(r) = r`. Unbounded. A residual of size 10 pulls ten times as hard as a residual of size 1, a residual of size 100 pulls a hundred times as hard, forever, no ceiling. So a handful of large residuals ‚ÄĒ exactly the ones I expect from compounding roll-out and hard frames ‚ÄĒ can produce gradients that swamp the whole batch. The only lever I have to survive that is to drop the learning rate until the worst residual's gradient is tolerable, but then the bulk of small residuals barely move. I'm babysitting the learning rate to protect against a few coordinates. That's the squared-error failure mode in one sentence: its influence on the estimate is unbounded, so it has no defense against large residuals.

So flip to the other extreme: absolute error, `ŌĀ(r) = |r|`, `Ōą(r) = sign(r)`. Now the pull is `¬Ī1` no matter how large `r` is ‚ÄĒ bounded. A gigantic residual contributes exactly the same unit gradient as a moderate one, so no single coordinate can run away with the batch. That's the robustness I want in the tail. But watch what it does in the middle. `Ōą` is `¬Ī1` *all the way down to zero* ‚ÄĒ even when a coordinate is already nearly right, it still gets a full-magnitude, sign-only gradient ‚ÄĒ and at `r = 0` the loss has a kink, it's not differentiable at the very point I want the predictor to settle into. There's no quadratic basin near the optimum; the fit just rattles around the minimum at constant gradient magnitude instead of easing in. And statistically, if most of my residuals really are small and roughly Gaussian, `|r|` is fitting them inefficiently ‚ÄĒ it's the MLE for Laplace noise, the median, not the mean, and on clean-ish data the mean is the better estimate. So absolute error fixes the tail and breaks the middle. Wall, but a clarifying one: I now know exactly what I want from each region. Quadratic and smooth and efficient where `r` is small; bounded influence where `r` is large. The two baselines each give me one half. Neither gives me both.

Let me make the trade-off precise instead of hand-waving "small" and "large." Both `¬Ĺr¬≤` and `|r|` are M-estimators: I'm estimating something by `argmin_t ő£ ŌĀ(X_i ‚ąí t)`, with optimality condition `ő£ Ōą(X_i ‚ąí t) = 0`. And there's a clean fact about how good such an estimator is ‚ÄĒ its asymptotic variance is `E[Ōą¬≤] / (E[Ōą'])¬≤`. Let me convince myself which `Ōą` minimizes that for a *given* data density `f`. I want to minimize `‚ąę Ōą¬≤ f` subject to the normalization that comes out of `E[Ōą']`. Integrate `E[Ōą'] = ‚ąę f Ōą'` by parts: with `f` vanishing at the ends, `‚ąę f Ōą' = ‚ąí‚ąę Ōą f'`. So

  `E[Ōą¬≤]/(E[Ōą'])¬≤ = ‚ąę Ōą¬≤ f / (‚ąę Ōą f')¬≤  = ‚ąę Ōą¬≤ f / (‚ąę Ōą (f'/f) f)¬≤.`

By Cauchy‚ÄďSchwarz applied to the inner product `‚ü®Ōą, (f'/f)‚ü©` weighted by `f`,

  `(‚ąę Ōą (f'/f) f)¬≤ ‚Č§ (‚ąę Ōą¬≤ f)(‚ąę (f'/f)¬≤ f),`

so the ratio is `‚Č• 1 / ‚ąę (f'/f)¬≤ f`, with equality exactly when `Ōą ‚ąĚ ‚ąíf'/f`. So the variance-minimizing `Ōą` for data with density `f` is the maximum-likelihood score `Ōą = ‚ąíf'/f`. That re-says what I half-knew: choosing `ŌĀ` is choosing a noise model, and the best `ŌĀ` for density `f` is its MLE. For Gaussian `f` that's `Ōą = r` (squared error, the mean); for Laplace `f` that's `Ōą = sign(r)` (absolute error, the median). Fine ‚ÄĒ but my residuals are *neither* a clean Gaussian nor a clean Laplace. They're mostly Gaussian-ish with a contaminating minority of large ones. So the real question isn't "which fixed `f`," it's "what do I do when I only know `f` is *close* to Gaussian but a fraction of the mass is arbitrary?"

That's the right way to model it: the residual density is `F = (1 ‚ąí őĶ)ő¶ + őĶH`, where `ő¶` is the nominal Gaussian I'd see if the prediction were clean, `őĶ` is a small contamination fraction, and `H` is *anything* ‚ÄĒ the distribution of the gross residuals from compounding roll-out and hard frames, which I refuse to assume anything about. Now the variance `E[Ōą¬≤]/(E[Ōą'])¬≤` depends on `F`, and `F` is partly adversarial: `H` can be whatever is worst for me. So I shouldn't optimize `Ōą` for one density; I should pick the `Ōą` that is best against the *worst* `F` in this class. That's a minimax problem ‚ÄĒ a two-player game. I choose `Ōą` to minimize the asymptotic variance; an adversary chooses the contamination `H` (hence `F`) to maximize it. If this game has a saddle point `(Ōą‚āÄ, F‚āÄ)`, then `Ōą‚āÄ` is the robust estimator I'm after and `F‚āÄ` is the worst case it's protected against.

Let me think about what the adversary's worst `F‚āÄ` looks like, because the answer to the whole problem is going to fall out of it. The minimized variance for any fixed `F` is `1/‚ąę(f'/f)¬≤ f` ‚ÄĒ the reciprocal of the Fisher information of `f`. The adversary maximizes my variance, i.e. *minimizes the Fisher information* of `f` over the contamination class. So `F‚āÄ` is the **least-informative** density consistent with "`(1‚ąíőĶ)` of the mass is the nominal Gaussian `ő¶`, the rest is free." Where can the adversary dump the free `őĶ` mass to make `f` as uninformative as possible? Fisher information `‚ąę(f'/f)¬≤f` is a measure of how sharply the density's log changes ‚ÄĒ `(f'/f) = (log f)'`. A Gaussian has `log f = ‚ąít¬≤/2 + const`, so `(log f)' = ‚ąít`, which grows without bound in the tails: out there the Gaussian is *very* informative about location, because a point far out strongly says "the center is over here." That's precisely the informativeness the adversary wants to kill, because it's the same informativeness squared error exploits to let outliers dominate. So the adversary should flatten the tails ‚ÄĒ replace the steep `‚ąít¬≤/2` log-density in the tails with something whose slope `(log f)'` stops growing. The least-informative way to do that, while staying a valid density that still matches the Gaussian where it must, is to keep `f‚āÄ` exactly proportional to the Gaussian in some central interval `[t‚āÄ, t‚āĀ]` and splice on **exponential tails** outside it, because an exponential density `e^{‚ąík|t|}` has `(log f)' = ‚ąík¬∑sign(t)` ‚ÄĒ a constant-magnitude slope, the flattest non-trivial thing. Concretely, with the center symmetric, `t‚āÄ = ‚ąík`, `t‚āĀ = k`:

  `f‚āÄ(t) ‚ąĚ g(t)` for `|t| ‚Č§ k`  (the Gaussian middle),
  `f‚āÄ(t) ‚ąĚ g(¬Īk) e^{‚ąík(|t|‚ąík)}` for `|t| > k`  (exponential tails, matched in value and slope at `¬Īk`).

The crossover point `k` is pinned by the contamination level: after multiplying the middle-and-tail shape by `(1‚ąíőĶ)`, the whole density must integrate to one, which gives the condition `1/(1‚ąíőĶ) = ‚ąę_{‚ąík}^{k} g + 2g(k)/k`. So a larger `őĶ` (more contamination) means a smaller `k` (the Gaussian center shrinks, the robust tails take over sooner). I can also check this `f‚āÄ` is a *legal* contamination of `ő¶`: the implied contaminating density `H` is zero inside `[‚ąík,k]` and outside is proportional to the difference between the exponential splice and the original Gaussian. For `t ‚Č• k`, convexity of `‚ąílog g` gives `‚ąílog g(t) ‚Č• ‚ąílog g(k) + k(t‚ąík)`, hence `g(t) ‚Č§ g(k)e^{‚ąík(t‚ąík)}`; for `t ‚Č§ ‚ąík`, the tangent at `‚ąík` gives `‚ąílog g(t) ‚Č• ‚ąílog g(‚ąík) ‚ąí k(t+k)`, hence `g(t) ‚Č§ g(‚ąík)e^{k(t+k)}`. In both tails the splice dominates the original Gaussian, so `H ‚Č• 0`. Good, the adversary's choice is feasible.

Now the payoff. Against this least-favorable `f‚āÄ`, my best response is the MLE score I derived above: `Ōą‚āÄ = ‚ąíf‚āÄ'/f‚āÄ = ‚ąí(log f‚āÄ)'`. Read it straight off the pieces. In the middle `|t| ‚Č§ k`, `f‚āÄ ‚ąĚ g`, so `Ōą‚āÄ = ‚ąí(log g)'`; for the standard Gaussian `g(t) = e^{‚ąít¬≤/2}`, `(log g)' = ‚ąít`, so `Ōą‚āÄ(t) = ‚ąí(‚ąít) = t` ‚ÄĒ linear, exactly the squared-error score. In the tails `|t| > k`, `f‚āÄ ‚ąĚ e^{‚ąík|t|}`, so `(log f‚āÄ)' = ‚ąík¬∑sign(t)` and `Ōą‚āÄ(t) = ‚ąí(‚ąík¬∑sign(t)) = k¬∑sign(t)` ‚ÄĒ flat, exactly the absolute-error score scaled to `¬Īk`. So

  `Ōą‚āÄ(t) = t` for `|t| ‚Č§ k`,  `Ōą‚āÄ(t) = k¬∑sign(t)` for `|t| > k`,

which is just `Ōą‚āÄ(t) = clamp(t, ‚ąík, k)`. There it is ‚ÄĒ and I didn't choose this shape, the minimax problem *forced* it. The optimal robust influence function is the squared-error pull, capped at `¬Īk`. It's the MLE (efficient) wherever the data look Gaussian, and its influence saturates at a finite `¬Īk` so no single large residual can pull harder than that. The cap *is* the robustness: a bounded influence function is the formal statement that one gross coordinate can only move the estimate a bounded amount.

I should sanity-check that this `(Ōą‚āÄ, F‚āÄ)` is actually a saddle and not just a fixed point. Two directions. First, `Ōą‚āÄ` is optimal against `F‚āÄ` ‚ÄĒ yes, by construction it's the MLE for `f‚āÄ`, which minimizes variance for that density. Second, `F‚āÄ` is the adversary's best response against `Ōą‚āÄ` ‚ÄĒ I need `V(Ōą‚āÄ, F)` maximized at `F = F‚āÄ` over the class. Write `V(Ōą‚āÄ, F) = E_F[Ōą‚āÄ¬≤]/(E_F[Ōą‚āÄ'])¬≤` and split `F = (1‚ąíőĶ)G + őĶH`. The numerator `E_F[Ōą‚āÄ¬≤] = (1‚ąíőĶ)E_G[Ōą‚āÄ¬≤] + őĶE_H[Ōą‚āÄ¬≤]`, and since `|Ōą‚āÄ| ‚Č§ k` everywhere, `E_H[Ōą‚āÄ¬≤] ‚Č§ k¬≤`; the contamination can do no worse than put its mass where the score is saturated, so the implied `H‚āÄ` outside `[‚ąík,k]` reaches the upper bound. The denominator `(E_F[Ōą‚āÄ'])¬≤` ‚ÄĒ note `Ōą‚āÄ' = 1` on `(‚ąík,k)` and `0` outside, ignoring the boundary points ‚ÄĒ is `((1‚ąíőĶ)E_G[Ōą‚āÄ'] + őĶE_H[Ōą‚āÄ'])¬≤`, and putting the contamination mass in the flat tails where `Ōą‚āÄ' = 0` makes `E_H[Ōą‚āÄ'] = 0`, *minimizing* the denominator. Max numerator and min denominator simultaneously, both achieved by the same outside-the-core contamination, so `F‚āÄ` maximizes `V(Ōą‚āÄ, ¬∑)`. Saddle confirmed. `clamp(t, ‚ąík, k)` is genuinely the minimax-robust score.

Now I have `Ōą‚āÄ`; I want the loss `ŌĀ` itself, because that's what I implement, and I want it to be the integral of `Ōą‚āÄ` so the gradient I just derived is exactly `ŌĀ'`. Integrate piecewise. For `|t| ‚Č§ k`, `Ōą‚āÄ = t`, so `ŌĀ(t) = ¬Ĺt¬≤ + C‚āĀ`. For `|t| > k`, `Ōą‚āÄ = k¬∑sign(t)`, so `ŌĀ(t) = k|t| + C‚āā`. I want `ŌĀ` continuous at the join ‚ÄĒ and not just continuous, I want it `C¬Ļ`, value *and* slope matched at `|t| = k`, because a kink in `ŌĀ` means a jump in `Ōą`, and a discontinuous influence function would reintroduce exactly the non-differentiability I was trying to escape from `L1`. The slopes already match by construction: at `t = k‚ĀĽ` the slope is `t = k`, at `t = k‚Āļ` the slope is `k`. Same. Now match the values. Take `C‚āĀ = 0` (free choice of the loss's additive constant; I want `ŌĀ(0) = 0`). Continuity at `t = k`: `¬Ĺk¬≤ = k¬∑k + C‚āā`, so `C‚āā = ¬Ĺk¬≤ ‚ąí k¬≤ = ‚ąí¬Ĺk¬≤`. So

  `ŌĀ(t) = ¬Ĺt¬≤` for `|t| ‚Č§ k`,  `ŌĀ(t) = k|t| ‚ąí ¬Ĺk¬≤` for `|t| > k`.

Let me double-check the join numerically in my head at `t = k`: left gives `¬Ĺk¬≤`, right gives `k¬∑k ‚ąí ¬Ĺk¬≤ = ¬Ĺk¬≤`. Equal ‚ÄĒ continuous. Slopes both `k` ‚ÄĒ `C¬Ļ`. The `‚ąí¬Ĺk¬≤` constant is not a cosmetic tidy-up; the slope match already came from integrating a clipped `Ōą`, but this constant is forced if the value of `ŌĀ` is not allowed to jump at the crossover. This is exactly the loss whose interior is the quadratic Gaussian basin I wanted in the middle and whose exterior is the linear, bounded-gradient regime I wanted in the tail, with `k` the single knob that says "residuals bigger than `k` count as outliers." Quadratic-then-linear, derived rather than guessed.

So `k` is the crossover. In the statistical story it's pinned by the contamination level `őĶ` through that mass-matching equation, and the two limits are reassuring: `k ‚Üí ‚ąě` pushes the crossover to infinity, the loss is quadratic everywhere, I recover squared error and the mean ‚ÄĒ the right thing when `őĶ = 0` and the data are clean. `k ‚Üí 0` shrinks the quadratic region to nothing, the loss is linear everywhere, I recover absolute error and the median ‚ÄĒ the right thing as `őĶ ‚Üí 1`. So the new loss isn't a third competitor; it's the one-parameter family that *contains* both baselines as its endpoints and lives at the interior `0 < őĶ < 1` where my actual residuals are. That's the test that I found the right generalization and not just another option: the things I started from drop out as special cases.

For my JEPA prediction loss I don't have a clean `őĶ` to plug in ‚ÄĒ the contamination is the empirical mess of compounding roll-out error ‚ÄĒ so `k` becomes a hyperparameter for "what residual magnitude should I start treating as an outlier." The natural default is `k = 1`: the encoder produces latent features of order one, and after the variance regularizer keeps their spread normalized, a residual of order one is the natural scale at which to flip from "fit this carefully" to "cap its influence." With `k = 1` the loss is `¬Ĺt¬≤` for `|t| < 1` and `|t| ‚ąí ¬Ĺ` for `|t| ‚Č• 1`, and the gradient is `clamp(t, ‚ąí1, 1)` ‚ÄĒ saturating at unit magnitude. That's the form I'll ship.

One subtlety in how I parameterize the knob, because there are two natural conventions and I should know which I'm using. I can hold the quadratic fixed as `¬Ĺt¬≤` and scale the tail slope by `őī`, giving `ŌĀ_őī(t) = ¬Ĺt¬≤` for `|t| < őī` and `őī(|t| ‚ąí ¬Ĺőī)` for `|t| ‚Č• őī` ‚ÄĒ the tail gradient saturates at `¬Īőī`. Or I can normalize so the tail gradient always saturates at unit magnitude, which means dividing the quadratic by the threshold `ő≤`: `ŌĀ_ő≤(t) = ¬Ĺt¬≤/ő≤` for `|t| < ő≤` and `|t| ‚ąí ¬Ĺő≤` for `|t| ‚Č• ő≤` ‚ÄĒ gradient `clamp(t/ő≤, ‚ąí1, 1)`, always capped at `¬Ī1`. These are the same family written two ways. Relate them: in the tail the first has slope `őī` and the second has slope `1`, so `ŌĀ_őī(t) = őī ¬∑ ŌĀ_ő≤(t)` when `őī = ő≤`. The unit-saturating convention is the cleaner one to expose as a library default, because moving the threshold `ő≤` changes *where* the crossover is without rescaling how hard the tail pulls ‚ÄĒ the maximum per-coordinate gradient stays pinned at `1` regardless of `ő≤`, which is exactly the property I want when I'm using this to protect training from outlier residuals: I get to say "treat residuals past `ő≤` as outliers" without also changing the gradient ceiling. At `ő≤ = őī = 1` the two conventions coincide, which is the case I'm using anyway, so it's moot for the default ‚ÄĒ but I'll write the code in the unit-saturating form so the threshold and the gradient cap are decoupled.

Let me also be honest about the gradient at the boundary so the implementation is exactly right, not just morally right. The forward is `¬Ĺz¬≤/ő≤` for `z < ő≤` and `z ‚ąí ¬Ĺő≤` for `z ‚Č• ő≤`, with `z = |input ‚ąí target|`. Differentiate with respect to the signed kernel difference `x = input ‚ąí target`: inside, `d/dx (¬Ĺx¬≤/ő≤) = x/ő≤`; outside, `d/dx(|x| ‚ąí ¬Ĺő≤) = sign(x)`. At `|x| = ő≤` the inside slope is `x/ő≤ = sign(x)` and the outside slope is `sign(x)` ‚ÄĒ they agree, so the derivative is continuous and the kernel can put the boundary in the linear branch. The clean way to write the whole backward shape is `clamp(x/ő≤, ‚ąí1, 1)`: `x/ő≤` while `|x| < ő≤`, then flat at `¬Ī1`. With the mean reduction over the `[B, C, T, H, W]` coordinates there's an extra `1/N` factor, and if the trainable tensor is the second argument the sign flips, but the per-coordinate influence magnitude is still capped by `1/N`. No outlier residual, however large, can exceed that cap. That's the stability guarantee squared error never had.

Why mean over all the coordinates rather than sum? The loss is added to the variance‚Äďcovariance regularizer and the sum would scale with `B¬∑C¬∑T¬∑H¬∑W`, coupling the effective learning rate to the tensor size and to the model width (which changes across the small/base/large configurations); averaging makes the prediction term a per-coordinate quantity of order `ŌĀ(typical residual)`, comparable across model sizes and stable against the batch and resolution. So mean reduction, matching the default.

Now I land it in the slot. The cost is a function of the residual `r = state ‚ąí predicted`; I apply the piecewise `ŌĀ` elementwise and average. PyTorch already has this exact piecewise-quadratic-then-linear function as `F.smooth_l1_loss` with `beta` the crossover threshold ‚ÄĒ which is precisely `ŌĀ_ő≤` in the unit-saturating convention I settled on ‚ÄĒ so the faithful implementation is a single call with the default `beta = 1`:

```python
import torch
import torch.nn as nn
import torch.nn.functional as F


class CustomPredictionLoss(nn.Module):
    """Smooth L1 (Huber, beta=1) prediction loss for temporal JEPA.

    On the residual r = state - predicted it applies, per coordinate,
        rho(r) = 0.5 * r^2 / beta        if |r| <  beta   (Gaussian-efficient quadratic basin)
               = |r| - 0.5 * beta        if |r| >= beta   (bounded-influence linear tail)
    so the gradient with respect to r is clamp(r/beta, -1, 1): squared-error
    pull capped at unit magnitude past the crossover, robust to outlier residuals.
    """

    def __init__(self):
        super().__init__()

    def forward(self, state, predicted):
        # beta=1, mean over [B, C, T, H, W]: matches the derived rho exactly.
        return F.smooth_l1_loss(state, predicted)
```

The same loss written out elementwise is:

```python
    def forward_explicit(self, state, predicted, beta: float = 1.0):
        r = state - predicted                      # residual the cost acts on
        z = r.abs()
        quadratic = 0.5 * z * z / beta             # |r| <  beta : Gaussian-efficient basin
        linear = z - 0.5 * beta                    # |r| >= beta : bounded-gradient tail
        loss = torch.where(z < beta, quadratic, linear)
        return loss.mean()                         # mean over all [B,C,T,H,W] coordinates
```

So the chain closes. I started stuck between squared error ‚ÄĒ smooth and efficient in the middle but with an unbounded influence `Ōą = r` that lets a few large roll-out residuals blow up the gradient ‚ÄĒ and absolute error ‚ÄĒ bounded influence `Ōą = sign(r)` but kinked at the optimum and inefficient on the Gaussian bulk. Modeling the residuals as a mostly-Gaussian distribution contaminated by an arbitrary fraction `őĶ` of gross errors turned "which loss" into a minimax game: choose the influence function that minimizes asymptotic variance against the worst contamination. The worst contamination is the least-Fisher-information density in the class, which is Gaussian in the middle and exponential-tailed outside, and the MLE score for *that* density is `clamp(t, ‚ąík, k)` ‚ÄĒ linear where the data are Gaussian (efficient), saturating at `¬Īk` in the tails (robust). Integrating that score, with value-continuity forcing the `‚ąí¬Ĺk¬≤` constant, gives the quadratic-then-linear `ŌĀ`; squared error and absolute error fall out as the `k ‚Üí ‚ąě` and `k ‚Üí 0` limits, so it's the right one-parameter family rather than a third choice. For the JEPA latent residuals I fix the crossover at `k = ő≤ = 1`, average over the `[B, C, T, H, W]` coordinates so the term is comparable across model sizes, and the per-coordinate gradient is `clamp(r, ‚ąí1, 1)` ‚ÄĒ bounded by one, which is exactly the protection against outlier residuals that squared error lacked. That's `F.smooth_l1_loss(state, predicted)`.
