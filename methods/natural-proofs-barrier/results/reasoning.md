Let me start from the thing that actually bothers me, which is not any one lower bound but the pattern of where they stop. We can prove PARITY isn't in AC0. We can prove MOD-3 isn't in AC0[p]. We can prove inner-product needs huge depth-2 threshold circuits. And then — nothing. The whole program was supposed to be a staircase: random restrictions get you AC0, the polynomial method gets you AC0[p], surely you generalize the polynomial method to AC0[m], then to TC0, then NC1, then P/poly, and somewhere up there SAT falls out and P ≠ NP. The staircase has a missing step right above AC0[p] and nobody can find the next tread. Baker–Gill–Solovay already told us why diagonalization can't do this — it relativizes, the oracles flip the answer. But these combinatorial methods don't relativize; they grab the actual circuit and shake it. So BGS is silent here. Why are *these* stuck?

A nontechnical answer — "smart people tried and failed" — is worthless. A real answer would either be a technique provably different in kind from all of these, or a precise statement of what all of these have in common together with a proof that the common thing is exactly the wall. So let me try to say what they have in common, reading them as literally as I can, as proofs-on-paper rather than as slogans.

Take the parity proof. Strip it down. What does it actually use about parity? It uses that under a random restriction leaving the right number of variables free, parity does not become constant, whereas a small AC0 circuit almost surely does. So there is a property of the function — call it "robust under random restrictions," or in Fourier terms "a lot of weight on high-degree coefficients" — and the proof shows: any function with this property is not in AC0, and parity has it. Now the part I want to notice: this property is a property of the *truth table*. Given the 2^n bits of f, I can in principle check whether f stays non-constant under the relevant restrictions. And in fact I can check it fast — I enumerate the restrictions, each is a depth-2 test, and I AND them; that's an AC0 computation on the 2^n-bit input. The property is decidable, cheaply.

Smolensky's proof. Same skeleton. Any AC0[3] circuit is approximated by a low-degree polynomial over F_3; parity is far from every low-degree polynomial. The certificate, when you naturalize it, is: associate to f a linear map M(f) on a polynomial space and ask whether rank(M(f)) is large. That's a property of the truth table, and computing a rank is in NC2. Cheap again.

The threshold proof of Hajnal and the others. View f as a matrix; the certificate is "the matrix has low discrepancy"; you check it by feeding dot products into threshold gates and AND-ing — TC0. The perceptron proof of Aspnes–Beigel–Furst–Rudich: the certificate is "weak degree above a threshold," and you decide it by linear programming, which is in P. Andreev's formula-size bound, Razborov's switching-and-rectifier bound — same: in each one I can point at the truth table, name a property, and decide that property in time polynomial in 2^n, usually in some low class well inside it.

So that's the first shared feature, and it's robust across the whole list: the proof hands me an algorithm that, reading the truth table of f, decides whether f has the hardness-certifying property — and the algorithm runs in time polynomial in the truth-table size 2^n. Call that constructivity. I'll take "polynomial in 2^n" as the benchmark even though every actual proof does much better, because I want the *weakest* condition that still captures them all — if I can prove something negative even under this loose notion, the negative result is stronger.

Now the second feature, and this one I almost passed over. Look again at how each proof argues the explicit function is hard. It never says "parity is special." It says "parity has property C," and then — to even define the lower bound cleanly — it shows, by a counting argument, that *almost every* function has property C. The high-sensitivity property: a random function has overwhelming high-degree Fourier mass. The large-rank property: a random truth table gives a nonsingular M(f) with probability at least a half. Low discrepancy: a random matrix has it. High weak degree: a uniformly random function has weak degree above n/20 with probability 1 − 2^{−Ω(n)}. Every single time, the property the proof exploits is a generic property — it holds for a random function. So the second shared feature: a random function has the property with non-negligible probability, typically with probability close to one. Call that largeness; say the property is large if at least a 2^{−O(n)} fraction of all functions have it.

And the third thing, which is just what "the property proves a lower bound" means: if f has the property, then f is not in the target circuit class. Call that usefulness. Useful against AC0, against AC0[p], against — and here is the whole game — against P/poly, because a proof that SAT ∉ P/poly would exhibit a property useful against P/poly and possessed by SAT.

So I'll say a lower bound proof is *natural* if it works by exhibiting a property C_n of truth tables that is constructive, large, and useful against the class in question. And the empirical claim — which I've now checked function by function, parity through switching-and-rectifier — is that *every* known nonmonotone circuit lower bound is natural in this sense. The monotone bounds are an exception (no one has a clean notion of a "random monotone function," so largeness has no analogue there), and pure counting/diagonalization are exceptions for a reason I'll come back to. But the whole combinatorial wave is natural.

Fine. So what? Having a common form isn't a barrier. I need a reason the common form *fails*. Let me sit with the two conditions together, because individually they're innocuous and I suspect the trouble is in their conjunction.

Constructive: an efficient algorithm A that reads a truth table and outputs 1 (with non-negligible frequency) when the function is hard. Large: A outputs 1 on a non-negligible fraction of *all* functions. Useful against C: A never outputs 1 on a function that's *in* C.

Read that last line again with the first two. A says 1 often on random functions, and A never says 1 on functions in C. So A *separates random functions from functions in C*. A is a distinguisher. A distinguisher between truly random functions and… functions computable in C.

That's the thing. That's exactly the job description of an attack on a pseudorandom function family living in C. If C is strong enough to compute a pseudorandom function — a family of functions indistinguishable from random — then A, the algorithm the lower bound proof handed me for free, distinguishes that family from random, which means the family was never pseudorandom. The proof of hardness contains, as an automatic by-product, a break of any pseudorandom function in the class it's proving hardness against.

Let me make sure I have the direction right, because it's easy to flip. Usefulness says: C_n(f) = 1 ⟹ f ∉ C. Contrapositive: f ∈ C ⟹ C_n(f) = 0. So on every function that *is* in C, the test outputs 0. And largeness says on a random function the test outputs 1 with probability ≥ 2^{−O(n)}. So the test's acceptance probability is ≥ 2^{−O(n)} on uniform functions and exactly 0 on functions drawn from inside C. Non-negligible gap, zero on one side. That's a distinguisher with bias ≥ 2^{−O(n)}, and by constructivity it's computable in time 2^{O(n)}. Yes. The direction holds.

Now I have to turn "distinguishes a pseudorandom function from random" into "breaks a generator," quantitatively, because the cryptographic assumption I want to contradict is about generators. This is where I lean on Goldreich–Goldwasser–Micali. They take a length-doubling generator G : {0,1}^k → {0,1}^{2k}, split each output into its first and last k bits, G_0 and G_1, and build a function by walking a binary tree: for an n-bit input y = y_1 … y_n, set G_y = G_{y_n} ∘ … ∘ G_{y_1}, and let the function's value at y be the first bit of G_y applied to the seed. The seed indexes the family; each member is computable by a poly(2^n)-size circuit when G has polynomial-size circuits. And it's pseudorandom under query access, proved by a hybrid up the tree, with each adjacent change charged to one call to G. Perfect — this is a PRF whose security reduces to G's, and it lives in P/poly when G does.

So let me prove the thing for the default and most important setting: a property that is P/poly-constructive, large, and useful against P/poly. Suppose, for contradiction, one exists — a constructive large C_n useful against P/poly. (Without loss of generality take C_n to *be* its constructive large subset; if only a subset C* is constructive and large, rename it C_n.) Let G : {0,1}^k → {0,1}^{2k} be any P/poly-computable length-doubling generator, and let ε > 0 be arbitrary. Set n = ⌈k^ε⌉ — so n is a tiny power of k, the truth-table length 2^n is sub-exponential in the seed length, and "size 2^{O(n)}" will turn out to be "size 2^{O(k^ε)}," sub-exponential in k.

Build the GGM family f from G as above, with input length n. Each f(x) — the function indexed by seed x ∈ {0,1}^k — is computable by poly(2^n)-size circuits, hence the family lies in P/poly. I need the sequence quantifier in usefulness here: if for infinitely many lengths some generated function landed in C_n, I could choose one such seed at each length and get a P/poly sequence that has the property infinitely often, contradicting usefulness. So for all sufficiently large k, C_n(f(x)) = 0 for every seed x. In other words C_n has empty intersection with the entire seeded family {f(x) : x ∈ {0,1}^k}.

Meanwhile largeness says a uniformly random function f_n satisfies C_n with probability at least 2^{−O(n)}. So

  |Pr[C_n(f_n) = 1] − Pr[C_n(f(x)) = 1]| ≥ 2^{−O(n)} − 0 = 2^{−O(n)},

where on the left x is a uniform seed and f_n is a uniform function. And by constructivity the test C_n is computable by circuits of size 2^{O(n)}. So I have a 2^{O(n)}-size statistical test that separates a truly random function from a GGM-pseudorandom function with bias 2^{−O(n)}. That's a break of the PRF. Now I push it down to a break of G itself, with the hybrid.

The hybrid lives on the binary tree T of height n; it has 2^n − 1 internal nodes. Order them v_1, …, v_{2^n−1} so that a child comes after its parent. For hybrid i, take the forest made by the first i internal nodes together with all leaves. Each root of that forest gets an independent uniform k-bit label, and below that root the labels are generated by applying G along the tree edges. When i=0, every leaf is its own root, so the output bits at the leaves are independent uniform bits: this is a truly random f_n. When i=2^n−1, only the global root is random and every other label is generated from it, so this is exactly f(x). I have a test C_n separating the two endpoints with bias δ = 2^{−O(n)}, so telescoping across the 2^n − 1 steps gives an adjacent pair i, i+1 separated by at least δ/(2^n − 1). That is still 2^{−O(n)}; the extra 2^n factor only changes the hidden constant. This adjacent bias is an average over all labels outside the one node whose treatment changes, so I can fix those outside labels to a choice that preserves at least that adjacent bias. The only difference between hybrid i and hybrid i+1 is whether node v_{i+1}'s two child-subtree roots receive two independent uniform k-bit labels or the two halves of G(x_{v_{i+1}}) for an independent uniform parent label x_{v_{i+1}}. So the test, with the rest of the forest fixed, becomes a circuit C that distinguishes a uniform 2k-bit string from G(uniform seed) with bias 2^{−O(n)}, and C has size 2^{O(n)}.

Therefore the hardness of G, the smallest S admitting a size-≤ S circuit with distinguishing bias ≥ 1/S, satisfies

  H(G_k) ≤ 2^{O(n)} = 2^{O(k^ε)}.

And ε was arbitrary. For any fixed δ > 0, I can rerun the argument with ε < δ and then absorb the constant in O(k^ε) into k^δ for large k. So H(G_k) ≤ 2^{k^δ} for every fixed δ > 0, which is exactly H(G_k) ≤ 2^{k^{o(1)}}. That is the conclusion: a P/poly-natural property useful against P/poly forces *every* P/poly-computable length-doubling generator to be breakable by sub-exponential, in fact 2^{k^{o(1)}}-size, circuits. If there exist generators of hardness 2^{k^δ} for some fixed δ > 0 — equivalently, if 2^{n^ε}-hard one-way functions exist, which is widely believed (factoring, discrete log) — then no such natural property exists. No natural proof can prove SAT ∉ P/poly. The staircase doesn't just happen to stop; under a standard cryptographic assumption it *cannot* reach P/poly by any constructive, large argument.

The proof of hardness must, to be large, accept a random function; to be useful, it must reject functions in the class; so it must tell random functions apart from functions in the class — but "looking like a random function" is precisely what a pseudorandom function in the class achieves, so the proof's own discriminating power is exactly an attack on that pseudorandomness. We're trying to prove certain functions are hard, but deciding whether a function is hard is *itself* a hard problem, by the very conjectures we're trying to prove. The lower-bound proof has turned into the attack it was supposed to coexist with.

If the same explicit hard-bit function also supplies a generator, the two inequalities start pushing against each other. Usefulness against SIZE(t(n)) says the hard bit has circuit size at least t(k). The generator analysis says that hard-bit size is at most a polynomial in H(G)+k. But the GGM hybrid driven by the large constructive property says H(G) is at most 2^{O(n)} once I choose n just past t^{-1}(k^{C+1}). If t is half-exponential, those inequalities cannot all hold. That is the shape of the unconditional discrete-log result: the natural route cannot certify more than half-exponential circuit lower bounds for that hard bit.

Now I should check this picture against itself at the low end, because if it's right it must also explain why natural proofs *did* work for AC0 and AC0[p]. The resolution has to be that those classes are too weak to hide a pseudorandom function. For AC0: Nisan built a generator secure against AC0, fooling depth-d size-2^{O(n)} circuits, and its very construction sits low in the hierarchy. Run my argument relativized to AC0: a property that is AC0-constructive, large, and useful against AC0[2] would, against Nisan's AC0[2]-computable generator, give an AC0-size test distinguishing it from random — contradicting Nisan. So there is no AC0-natural proof against AC0[2]. But the parity lower bounds of Furst–Saxe–Sipser, Yao, Håstad *are* AC0-natural (their property C_n is in AC0). Hence those proofs provably cannot reach the AC0[p] bounds of Razborov and Smolensky — you genuinely need a property from a richer class, an NC2 rank rather than an AC0 restriction test, to climb that one rung. The obstruction isn't only at the top; it stratifies the staircase and tells me, rung by rung, that each step needs a strictly stronger certifying class, precisely because each class can hide generators against the weaker certifiers. And the plausible pseudorandom-function candidates already press down around TC0 and NC1, for example through subset-sum-based constructions. That's exactly where the lower bounds went quiet. The obstruction predicts the stall to the rung.

I'd like one more thing: a version with *no* cryptographic assumption at all, to show this isn't hostage to an unproven conjecture. The conditional theorem assumed generic hard generators exist. But discrete log gives me a specific explicit sequence to test against, because the Blum-Micali hard bit B_{p,g}(x) = 1 iff log_g x ≤ (p−1)/2 sits inside a generator whose security is tied back to the circuit complexity of that same hard bit. Say t(n) is half-exponential: nondecreasing, with t^{−1}(n^C) ≤ o(log t(n)) for every C — meaning the second iterate of t outruns the exponential, so t(n) = 2^{n^ε} qualifies but 2^{(log n)^C} does not. Claim: there's no large, P/poly-constructive property useful against SIZE(t(n)) that holds for infinitely many of the B_{p,g}.

Suppose one did. Take infinitely many primes p_ν and generators g_ν for which the hard-bit functions B_{p_ν,g_ν} land in the property, and let k_ν = ⌈log p_ν⌉. By usefulness against SIZE(t(n)), along an infinite subsequence these hard-bit functions have circuit size at least t(k_ν). Now build the standard Blum-Micali generator G_ν : {0,1}^{2k_ν} → {0,1}^{4k_ν} from B_{p_ν,g_ν}. The Blum-Micali analysis gives the reverse leverage I need: the circuit size of B_{p_ν,g_ν} is bounded by a polynomial in H(G_ν)+k_ν. Therefore

  t(k_ν) ≤ (H(G_ν)+k_ν)^{O(1)}.

Convert G_ν into a GGM pseudorandom function f_ν on n_ν bits exactly as before. There is a fixed C such that every function in its image has circuit size at most (k_ν+n_ν)^C. I choose

  n_ν = t^{−1}(k_ν^{C+1}) + 1.

The half-exponential condition forces t(k_ν) > k_ν^{C+1} for almost all ν; otherwise k_ν ≤ t^{−1}(k_ν^{C+1}) ≤ o(log t(k_ν)) ≤ o(log k_ν), which is impossible. Hence n_ν ≤ k_ν for almost all ν. Then every generated function has circuit size at most (2k_ν)^C ≤ k_ν^{C+1} < t(n_ν), so usefulness rejects the entire image at length n_ν. Largeness accepts a random n_ν-bit function with probability at least 2^{−O(n_ν)}, and the same GGM hybrid gives H(G_ν) ≤ 2^{O(n_ν)}. I also need to keep log k_ν under control: largeness makes C_n nonempty for almost all n, and usefulness would be impossible if SIZE(t(n)) already contained all n-bit functions, so t(n) ≤ 2^{O(n)} for almost all n. Since t(n_ν) > k_ν^{C+1}, this gives log k_ν = O(n_ν). Now the half-exponential inequality closes the loop:

  n_ν = t^{−1}(k_ν^{C+1}) + 1
      ≤ o(log t(k_ν))
      ≤ o(log H(G_ν) + log k_ν)
      ≤ o(n_ν).

That is impossible. So unconditionally, no natural proof shows discrete log needs more than half-exponential-size circuits; and the same goes for any function poly-time non-uniformly reducible to discrete log.

One loose end I want to nail, because it's the cleaner half of the pair of conditions and I claimed it but should prove it: that largeness is not an accident of these proofs but is *forced* by the way they're built. Almost all of them measure hardness with a formal complexity measure — an integer-valued function μ on Boolean functions with μ(f ∨ g), μ(f ∧ g) ≤ μ(f) + μ(g), and the input variables and their negations cheap; formula size is the prototype. Approximation-model measures sometimes have an extra +1 under ∨ or ∧, but shifting the measure by 1 puts them back into this formal form. I'll show: if μ takes a large value at even a single function, it takes a large value almost everywhere. So any property "μ(f) is big," which is how you'd state a hardness certificate, automatically has largeness — you couldn't make such a property *small* if you tried.

Precisely: if μ(f) = t for some f ∈ F_n, then for at least a 1/4 fraction of all g ∈ F_n, μ(g) ≥ t/4. The mechanism is purely about Boolean algebras and counting, so let me prove the algebra fact and read off the measure statement. Take a finite Boolean algebra B with N atoms and a subset S ⊆ B. Two facts. (a) If |S| > (3/4)|B|, then every element of B is a "depth-2" combination (s_1 ∧ s_2) ∨ (s_3 ∧ s_4) of four members of S. Why: fix b_0; for any b, write b_0 = (b ∧ (¬b ⊕ b_0)) ∨ (¬b ∧ (b ⊕ b_0)) — all four of b, ¬b ⊕ b_0, ¬b, b ⊕ b_0 are uniform over B, and since each lands outside S with probability < 1/4, the union bound leaves a positive-probability event where all four lie in S simultaneously, giving the representation. (b) If S contains all atoms and coatoms, every element is a short combination ⋁_{i≤ℓ} ⋀_{j} s_{ij} with ℓ small; this is the entropy-counting lemma — call an atom "good" if it lies below a random element of S with probability at most 2/3; an entropy bound caps the bad atoms at O(log(|B|/|S|)), and a random sub-collection of S of size O(log N) has meet hitting only bad atoms, so iterating builds any element with logarithmically many terms.

Now the measure statement from the algebra fact. Suppose, for the 1/4 claim, that fewer than 1/4 of the g have μ(g) ≥ t/4; let S = {g : μ(g) < t/4}, so |S| > (3/4)|F_n|. By (a), f = (s_1 ∧ s_2) ∨ (s_3 ∧ s_4) with each s_i ∈ S, so by the formal-measure inequalities

  μ(f) ≤ μ(s_1 ∧ s_2) + μ(s_3 ∧ s_4) ≤ μ(s_1) + μ(s_2) + μ(s_3) + μ(s_4) < t.

That contradicts μ(f) = t. So at least 1/4 of all g have μ(g) ≥ t/4: largeness, for free, from the subadditivity of the measure. The same with the sharper representation (b) upgrades 1/4 toward almost everywhere: for a 1−γ fraction, μ(g) stays at Ω(t/(n+log(1/γ))^2)−n. So the two natural-proof conditions aren't symmetric afterthoughts: largeness is *guaranteed* by the very algebra of formal complexity measures, and it's the *constructive* half that does all the work in the obstruction — exactly the half we have the least handle on outside exponential time.

Which, finally, tells me why counting and diagonalization slip through — the two acknowledged exceptions. A pure counting argument's property is "C_n(f) = 1 iff f ∉ P/poly," say "complexity above n^{log n}." That's large — most functions are hard. But is it constructive? Deciding "f has no polynomial circuit" from the truth table is presumably nowhere near exponential time; we have no efficient test for it, and the main theorem now says that if generators exist, no large constructive subset of it can exist *at all*. So counting violates constructivity. Diagonalization, conversely, zeroes in on a property that is *not* large — being EXP-complete, able to simulate all P-machines — a measure-zero idiosyncrasy, not a generic feature; that's how it dodges largeness, at the cost of relativizing. Each escapee escapes by failing exactly one of the two conditions, which is the strongest evidence I have that the two conditions are the right ones: they cut the known techniques at precisely the joint between what's blocked and what isn't.

So the duality I'm left holding is this. Any lower bound proof against a class C that is constructive and large is, simultaneously and unavoidably, a proof that C contains no strong pseudorandom function — and worse, an *algorithm* that breaks any such function. Working on lower bounds by natural methods is breaking a secret code keyed by the class you're working against. The way forward is not to give up but to build proofs that are *un*natural — that seize on a property too specialized to be large (the diagonalization escape), or too complex to be constructive — and the precise statement of the obstacle, with its proof, is exactly what's needed to aim research at those.

The result is now forced. A natural property for a class is a property of truth tables C_n with a subset C*_n that is constructive, meaning C*_n is decidable in time poly(2^n), or by poly(2^n)-size circuits in the P/poly-natural version; large, meaning |C*_n| ≥ 2^{−O(n)}·|F_n|; and useful against the target class, meaning any sequence hitting C_n infinitely often lies outside that class. There is no P/poly-natural property useful against P/poly unless every P/poly-computable generator G_k : {0,1}^k → {0,1}^{2k} has hardness H(G_k) ≤ 2^{k^{o(1)}}. Equivalently, if 2^{n^ε}-hard one-way functions exist for some ε>0, then no constructive, large property is useful enough to separate P from NP through P/poly. And without any generator-hardness assumption, for every half-exponential t, no large P/poly-constructive property useful against SIZE(t(n)) can contain infinitely many discrete-logarithm hard-bit functions B_{p,g}; so this route cannot prove more than half-exponential circuit lower bounds for discrete log. The whole chain is now tight: every known nonmonotone lower bound exhibits a truth-table property that is constructive and large; constructive plus large plus useful is an efficient distinguisher between random functions and functions in the class; if the class contains the GGM pseudorandom functions built from a generator G, the distinguisher breaks G through the forest hybrid with a 2^{O(n)}-size circuit; so a P/poly-natural proof against P/poly forces every generator down to 2^{k^{o(1)}}-size attacks, and the familiar combinatorial proofs stop being viable exactly when the circuit class becomes rich enough to hide pseudorandom functions.
