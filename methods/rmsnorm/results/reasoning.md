Let me start from the thing that actually nags me when I train a GRU-based translation model. I add layer normalization and the loss-versus-steps curve gets dramatically better — at the point where the unnormalized baseline's loss is still up around 7.0, the normalized one is already down near 5.4 after the same number of steps. That's a big win, and it's why everyone uses the layer. But then I plot the same two losses against wall-clock *time* instead of steps, and the gap shrinks: at equal time the normalized model is only at about 5.9. So a good chunk of the per-step gain is being handed back because each step now takes longer. The layer is doing real work for me, but I'm paying for it on every single forward pass, and in a recurrent net that means paying on every timestep of every layer. The question that won't leave me alone is: am I paying for something I don't need?

To answer that I have to be precise about what the layer actually computes, mechanically, because if part of the mechanism is dead weight I want to find it. For a layer with summed inputs a ∈ R^n — that's a_i = Σ_j w_ij x_j, the pre-activation to neuron i — it computes the mean μ = (1/n) Σ_i a_i and the standard deviation σ = √((1/n) Σ_i (a_i − μ)²), then standardizes ā_i = (a_i − μ)/σ · g_i with a learned per-neuron gain g, and finally y_i = f(ā_i + b_i). So mechanically there are two distinct operations bundled together. One: subtract the mean — recenter the vector to zero mean. Two: divide by the standard deviation — rescale it to unit spread. They feel like one move ("standardize") but they're not; they're two, and they cost differently and, more importantly, they buy different things.

Let me think about what each one buys, in terms of invariances, because that's the cleanest way to see what's load-bearing. Suppose a shift adds the same constant to every summed input, a → a + c·1; a recentered weight matrix can create exactly that kind of shift in the neuron vector. Because the layer subtracts μ, and μ shifts by exactly that same constant, the centered vector a − μ is unchanged. So the mean-subtraction is exactly what gives *re-centering invariance*: the output doesn't care about additive shifts of the summed-input vector. Now suppose instead I scale everything — a → α·a, say because I scaled the weight matrix or the inputs by α. Then μ → αμ, and σ → ασ, and (a − μ)/σ → (αa − αμ)/(ασ) = (a − μ)/σ, unchanged. So the division by σ is what gives *re-scaling invariance*: the output doesn't care about multiplicative scaling. Two operations, two invariances, cleanly separated — subtraction ↔ re-centering, division ↔ re-scaling.

The prevailing story credits both invariances for why the layer helps. But "credits both" is a story, not a measurement, and I have these two operations and I want to know which one is actually responsible for the stabilization, because if it's only one of them I can delete the other and stop paying for it. So let me ask: what does each invariance contribute to the thing that actually matters for optimization, which is keeping the variance of the activations and of the gradients under control?

The mean is the suspicious part. Stabilizing training is fundamentally about controlling *spread* — keeping hidden states and gradients from blowing up or vanishing as they propagate. Subtracting the mean recenters the vector, but it does nothing to its variance: var(a − μ) = var(a). Recentering moves the cloud of activations, it doesn't shrink or grow it. The thing that actually controls the spread — that pulls the variance to a fixed scale regardless of how big the weights got — is the *division by σ*. So if I had to bet on which of the two invariances is doing the stabilizing work, I'd bet on re-scaling, and I'd treat re-centering as the weaker suspect: it makes the activations tidy-looking, but it is not the direct mechanism keeping the optimization well-conditioned. That's a hypothesis I can act on: maybe I can drop the mean-subtraction entirely and keep only the scale normalization, and the training benefit survives while the cost drops.

If I drop the mean, what do I divide by? I can't use σ anymore, because σ = √((1/n) Σ (a_i − μ)²) is *defined* through μ — it's the spread *around the mean*. If I'm refusing to compute μ, I need a measure of scale that doesn't reference the mean at all. The natural candidate is the spread around *zero* instead of around the mean: the root mean square,

  RMS(a) = √( (1/n) Σ_{i=1}^n a_i² ).

This is just the quadratic mean of the entries — how big they are on average, measured from the origin rather than from their own center. And the normalization becomes

  ā_i = a_i / RMS(a) · g_i.

No μ anywhere. No subtraction. One statistic. Let me immediately check the degenerate case to make sure this is a sane generalization and not a different animal: if the summed inputs happen to have zero mean, μ = 0, then σ = √((1/n) Σ a_i²) = RMS(a), and my rule ā_i = a_i/RMS · g_i is *identical* to the standard layer. So RMSNorm coincides with the mean-subtracting layer exactly when the mean is already zero; the only thing I've changed is that I no longer *force* the mean to zero, I just leave it wherever it lands and normalize the magnitude. Good — that tells me this isn't a wild departure, it's the same layer with the recentering switched off.

Dropping the mean throws away an invariance, so I need to know exactly which one moves. The division by RMS still scales linearly with the input: RMS(αa) = √((1/n) Σ (αa_i)²) = |α|·√((1/n) Σ a_i²) = α·RMS(a) for α > 0. So re-scaling invariance survives — scale a by α and a/RMS(a) → αa/(αRMS(a)) = a/RMS(a), unchanged. That's the invariance I argued was the important one, and it's intact. But re-centering invariance is gone: if I shift a → a + c·1, RMS does not subtract anything, so a/RMS changes. So RMSNorm keeps exactly the invariance I bet was load-bearing and discards exactly the weaker one. That's the whole design thesis sitting in one line of algebra.

The differences from the neighbors matter, and I want to be sure I'm not quietly losing something BatchNorm or WeightNorm had. Write the layer in general form: y = f( (Wx)/RMS(a) ⊙ g + b ), with a = Wx and ⊙ elementwise. Scale the whole weight matrix, W' = δW. Then a' = δa, RMS(a') = δ·RMS(a) by the linearity I just showed, and (W'x)/RMS(a') = δWx/(δRMS(a)) = Wx/RMS(a). The output is untouched — invariant to weight-matrix re-scaling. Same computation for scaling the input, x' = δx: a' = δa again, same cancellation, invariant to input re-scaling, and that extends to scaling a whole batch or the whole dataset since each example cancels its own δ. So far so good, and these are the invariances the standard layer also had.

But what if I scale individual weight *vectors* by different factors — row i of W by δ_i? Then a_i → δ_i a_i, but RMS(a) = √((1/n) Σ_k a_k²) mixes *all* the neurons together, so it picks up a messy combination of the different δ_k and I cannot pull a single δ_i out of the ratio a_i/RMS(a). The cancellation fails. So RMSNorm is *not* invariant to per-weight-vector re-scaling. That's fine — the standard layer wasn't either, for the same reason (its σ also pools all neurons). It's WeightNorm that has that particular invariance, by construction, because it normalizes each weight vector's length on its own; but WeightNorm pays for it by *not* being invariant to input or dataset re-scaling, since it never looks at the activation distribution at all. So the methods carve up the invariance space differently, and I'm deliberately keeping the activation-side re-scaling invariances and dropping the recentering one.

And recentering: scale aside, is RMSNorm invariant to any shift? Shift the dataset, or shift the weight matrix by a constant — RMS has no μ to absorb it, so no. The standard layer *was* invariant to weight-matrix recentering (its μ ate the shift); RMSNorm gives that up. So if I lay the methods side by side: BatchNorm is invariant to weight-matrix re-scaling, dataset re-scaling and re-centering, but not single-case re-scaling and not weight-matrix re-centering; WeightNorm only to the two weight re-scalings; the standard layer to weight-matrix re-scaling, weight-matrix re-centering, dataset re-scaling and single-case re-scaling but not dataset re-centering; and RMSNorm matches the standard layer everywhere *except* it loses weight-matrix re-centering. One checkbox different, and it's the recentering checkbox — precisely the one I'm gambling doesn't matter.

There's a geometric reading of what RMS does that I want to hold onto, because it'll matter in a second when I consider an alternative. Normalizing by RMS forces a onto a sphere: a/RMS(a) has Σ_i (a_i/RMS)² = n, so it lives on the sphere of radius √n. RMS pins the *magnitude* of the summed-input vector to √n regardless of how the inputs and weights were scaled, which is exactly why the activation magnitude stays stable across layers and across rescalings of the data. The output distribution is held fixed in scale even as the input and weight distributions wander.

Now, that "sphere of radius √n" should make me pause, because there's an obvious cheaper-looking alternative staring at me: why not just divide by the plain Euclidean norm ‖a‖ = √(Σ a_i²)? It's the same thing without the 1/n inside the root — in fact ‖a‖ = √n · RMS(a), they differ only by a constant factor √n. WeightNorm already uses the L2 norm successfully (on weights), so it's a natural thing to try on activations. Let me think about whether the √n matters. ‖a‖ would force a onto the *unit* sphere, radius 1, independent of n. RMS forces it onto the radius-√n sphere, which *grows with the dimension of the layer*. Why would I want the target radius to depend on n? Because different layers, and the same layer at different times, have different effective sizes, and a fixed unit radius means a 1000-dimensional vector and a 10-dimensional vector both get squashed to length 1 — the per-coordinate scale then differs wildly between layers (each coordinate of the big vector is tiny, each coordinate of the small one is not). With the √n scaling, each *coordinate* is normalized to roughly unit scale regardless of layer width, so the normalization behaves consistently across vectors of different size. So the 1/n inside the root isn't cosmetic — it's what makes the scheme robust across layers of different dimension, and I'd expect plain L2 normalization of activations to not work as a drop-in the way RMS does. The factor I'd have been tempted to drop is the one carrying the robustness.

So the forward rule is settled: ā_i = a_i/RMS(a) · g_i, one reduction, no subtraction, with the gain g and no bias by default — no bias because the bias/shift only existed to restore a location after recentering, and I'm not recentering, so there's nothing to restore. Before I trust it I want to follow the gradient through, because the real test of a normalization layer isn't the forward invariances, it's whether it keeps the *gradients* well-behaved — the optimizer is what I'm trying to help, and stabilizing the backward pass is the point. Let me back-propagate.

Write v for the whole pre-nonlinearity expression, v = (Wx)/RMS(a) ⊙ g + b, so y = f(v). The easy parameters first. ∂L/∂b: b enters v additively, so ∂L/∂b = ∂L/∂v directly. ∂L/∂g: g multiplies the normalized inputs elementwise, so ∂L/∂g = ∂L/∂v ⊙ (Wx)/RMS(a) — that is, ∂L/∂v times the *normalized* summed inputs. Two things to notice. First, both of these are invariant to scaling x or W: scale by δ and (Wx)/RMS(a) is unchanged (that's the forward invariance I proved), so the g-gradient is unchanged, and the b-gradient never saw the scale at all. So the learned parameters' gradients don't move when the data or weights are rescaled — the optimizer sees a stable target. Second, the g-gradient is proportional to the *normalized* inputs, not the raw ones; the raw summed inputs could be huge or tiny but g always sees something of controlled magnitude, which keeps g's own scale from drifting. Good.

Now the weight matrix, which is where it gets interesting because RMS is quadratic in a and a = Wx, so W enters both through the numerator and through the denominator. Let me get ∂(a_j/RMS(a))/∂a_k carefully, treating RMS as a function of all the a's. Let S = RMS(a) = √((1/n) Σ_k a_k²). Then ∂S/∂a_k = (1/(2S))·(1/n)·2a_k = a_k/(n S). Quotient rule on a_j/S:

  ∂(a_j/S)/∂a_k = (δ_jk·S − a_j·∂S/∂a_k)/S² = δ_jk/S − a_j·a_k/(n S³) = (1/S)·( δ_jk − a_j a_k/(n S²) ).

So as a matrix in (j,k), the Jacobian of the normalized vector with respect to a is

  R = (1/RMS(a))·( I − (Wx)(Wx)ᵀ / (n·RMS(a)²) ),

writing a = Wx. Let me sanity-check this matrix makes sense: it's (1/S) times identity minus a rank-one outer-product correction. The outer-product term is the part that says "you can't move a in the radial direction without RMS moving too" — it projects out the component of any perturbation along a itself, which is exactly right, because scaling a (the radial direction) leaves a/RMS(a) fixed, so the Jacobian must annihilate that direction. Quick check: apply R to a itself. R·a = (1/S)(a − (aaᵀ)a/(nS²)) = (1/S)(a − a·(aᵀa)/(nS²)). But aᵀa = Σ a_k² = n S², so (aᵀa)/(nS²) = 1, giving R·a = (1/S)(a − a) = 0. The radial direction is in the null space, as it must be. The derivation is consistent.

Chaining to W is cleaner if I name the upstream gradient. Let u = ∂L/∂v and z = a/S. Since v = g ⊙ z + b, the gradient entering z is q = g ⊙ u. Since R is symmetric, the gradient entering a is Rq. And since a = Wx, one training case contributes the outer product

  ∂L/∂W = (R(g ⊙ u)) xᵀ.

For a batch I sum that expression over cases. The shape is the ordinary weight-gradient shape: a back-propagated feature vector times the input row. The structural fact I care about lives in R. How does R respond to scaling? Put a positive scale δ on either the input (x' = δx) or the weights (W' = δW); either way a → δa and RMS(a) → δ RMS(a). Then

  R' = (1/(δ·RMS(a)))·( I − (δWx)(δWx)ᵀ/(n·δ²·RMS(a)²) ) = (1/(δ·RMS(a)))·( I − (Wx)(Wx)ᵀ/(n·RMS(a)²) ) = (1/δ)·R,

because the δ² in the outer-product's numerator cancels the δ² in its denominator exactly, leaving the bracket untouched, and the leading 1/RMS picks up the extra 1/δ. So R scales as 1/δ — it's *negatively* correlated with the scale. Now feed that back into ∂L/∂W and watch the two cases come apart. If I scaled the *input*, x' = δx: the xᵀ factor in the gradient is multiplied by δ, while R is multiplied by 1/δ, so the product is unchanged — ∂L/∂W is invariant to input scaling. That's the smoothness I want: rescaling the data doesn't jolt the weight gradients. But if I scaled the *weights*, W' = δW: x is unchanged, so only the R factor moves, and ∂L/∂W gets multiplied by 1/δ — it shrinks when the weights grew. That's not a flaw, it's a feature: it means a layer whose weights have grown large automatically receives *smaller* gradients, which damps further growth and keeps the weight norm in check. It's an implicit, per-layer learning-rate adaptation falling straight out of the quadratic form of RMS — big weights, small steps; small weights, big steps — with no schedule and no extra parameters. So the backward pass not only stays stable under rescaling, it self-regulates the weight magnitudes. The recentering I dropped contributed none of this; all of it comes from the re-scaling structure I kept.

So I've got a layer that keeps the invariance I argued matters, throws away the one I argued doesn't, has a well-behaved and self-adapting gradient, and costs one reduction instead of two with no subtraction pass over the vector. That last part is the original motivation, so let me count the savings concretely. The standard layer needs a reduction to get μ, then a second reduction over the centered squares to get σ, plus an elementwise subtraction of μ across all n entries. RMSNorm needs a single reduction — sum of squares — then a reciprocal-square-root and an elementwise multiply. One fewer reduction, and the whole subtraction pass gone. In a feed-forward layer that's a modest constant saving; in a recurrent net where the layer fires at every timestep along the sequence, that constant is multiplied by the sequence length and by the number of layers, and it turns into a meaningful wall-clock speedup — exactly the regime where the time-versus-steps gap I started with was widest.

Can I push the cost down further? The reduction over all n entries is now the dominant cost. The n neurons in a layer tend to be, roughly, identically distributed — they're n draws from a similar pre-activation distribution. If that's so, I don't strictly need all n of them to estimate their root-mean-square; a subsample should give a usable estimate. So take only the first k entries, with k = ceil(n·p) in the mathematical description for a fraction p of the layer, and estimate

  RMS̄(a) = √( (1/k) Σ_{i=1}^k a_i² ),

then normalize *all* n entries by this partial RMS: ā_i = a_i/RMS̄(a) · g_i for every i. Call it partial, or p-RMSNorm. Does it keep the invariances? The only property the invariance proofs used was linearity, RMS(αa) = α·RMS(a), and the partial estimate has it too: RMS̄(αa) = √((1/k) Σ_{i=1}^k (αa_i)²) = α·RMS̄(a). So scaling x or W still cancels exactly, and p-RMSNorm inherits the same invariance table as full RMSNorm. The cost now scales with k instead of n, so the reduction shrinks roughly in proportion to k/n; if k is a small fraction of n, I touch only that fraction of the vector to set the scale for the whole thing.

I should be honest about what the subsample costs me, though. RMS̄ is not an unbiased copy of the full RMS: the sample mean of squares may track the second moment under the i.i.d. assumption, but the square root and the finite slice introduce bias and noise, and the smaller k is the worse both effects get. The danger is sharpest in the backward pass, because that 1/RMS sits in the denominator of the gradient, and a denominator estimated from very few samples can occasionally come out small, which makes the gradient spike. So I'd expect gradient instability — gradients tending to blow up — when k is pushed too small, and that's the practical limit on how aggressive p can be. The thing I'd want to verify empirically is how far down p can go before that instability bites.

Let me land it on code. The forward is almost nothing, which is the point.

```python
import torch
import torch.nn as nn


class RMSNorm(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, d, p=-1., eps=1e-8, bias=False):
        # d: layer width (number of neurons / summed inputs).
        # p: partial ratio; this implementation treats 0 <= p <= 1 as partial,
        #    so practical partial use should keep p > 0. p < 0 or p > 1 uses the full vector.
        # bias: off by default -- there is no mean-subtraction to undo, so no shift is needed.
        super().__init__()
        self.eps, self.d, self.p, self.bias = eps, d, p, bias
        self.scale = nn.Parameter(torch.ones(d))         # the gain g, init 1
        self.register_parameter("scale", self.scale)
        if bias:
            self.offset = nn.Parameter(torch.zeros(d))   # optional shift, only if asked for
            self.register_parameter("offset", self.offset)

    def forward(self, x):
        # x: [..., d], the summed inputs a to the layer.
        if self.p < 0. or self.p > 1.:
            norm_x = x.norm(2, dim=-1, keepdim=True)     # ||a|| = sqrt(sum a_i^2): ONE reduction
            d_x = self.d
        else:
            # p-RMSNorm: the implementation uses the first int(d*p) entries,
            # then normalize ALL d entries by it. Linearity of RMS keeps every invariance.
            partial_size = int(self.d * self.p)
            partial_x, _ = torch.split(x, [partial_size, self.d - partial_size], dim=-1)
            norm_x = partial_x.norm(2, dim=-1, keepdim=True)
            d_x = partial_size

        rms_x = norm_x * d_x ** (-1. / 2)                # full RMS or partial estimate = selected norm / sqrt(d_x)
        x_normed = x / (rms_x + self.eps)                # a_i / RMS(a): no mean subtracted, one division

        if self.bias:
            return self.scale * x_normed + self.offset
        return self.scale * x_normed                     # a_i / RMS(a) * g_i
```

Reading it back against the derivation: `x.norm(2, dim=-1)` is √(Σ a_i²) = ‖a‖ in the full branch, the single reduction over all features, and dividing it by √d turns the Euclidean norm into the root-mean-square (‖a‖ = √n·RMS, so ‖a‖/√n = RMS) — that's the √n-sphere scaling I argued was load-bearing for robustness across layer widths. There is no `mean`, no subtraction, one reduction where the standard layer had two. The gain `scale` is per-neuron and starts at 1, so the normalized vector is not distorted by a learned gain at initialization. The offset is created only when explicitly requested, because re-centering is the thing I'm deliberately not doing. The partial branch slices the first `int(d*p)` entries, converts that selected norm into RMS̄ with the same `d_x ** (-0.5)` factor, and then divides the full vector by it — exactly RMS̄ applied to all of a — and because the only property in play is the linearity of RMS, that branch carries the identical invariances.

The standard layer bundles two operations, mean-subtraction (which buys re-centering invariance) and division by σ (which buys re-scaling invariance), and I pay for both on every step. Subtracting the mean recenters but doesn't touch variance, so it is not the direct spread-control term; the scale division is the part that fixes the magnitude seen by the next layer and by the backward pass. So I drop the mean and normalize by the root mean square RMS(a) = √((1/n)Σ a_i²) instead of by σ; this coincides with the standard layer when the mean is already zero, keeps re-scaling invariance because RMS is linear in its argument, keeps the well-behaved, self-adapting gradient because the quadratic RMS makes ∂L/∂W invariant to input scaling and inversely proportional to weight scaling, and discards only re-centering. The 1/n inside the root is kept deliberately — it normalizes per-coordinate rather than per-vector, so the scheme is robust across layers of different width, where plain L2 normalization would not be. The code gives one reduction and no subtraction, and, exploiting that the neurons are roughly identically distributed, the scale can even be estimated from the first p-fraction of entries — partial RMSNorm — which keeps every invariance and cuts the reduction further, at the price of gradient instability if p is pushed too small.
