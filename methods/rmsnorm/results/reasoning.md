Let me start from the thing that actually nags me when I train a GRU-based translation model. I add layer normalization and the loss-versus-steps curve gets dramatically better — at the point where the unnormalized baseline's loss is still up around 7.0, the normalized one is already down near 5.4 after the same number of steps. That's a big win, and it's why everyone uses the layer. But then I plot the same two losses against wall-clock *time* instead of steps, and the gap shrinks: at equal time the normalized model is only at about 5.9. So a good chunk of the per-step gain is being handed back because each step now takes longer. The layer is doing real work for me, but I'm paying for it on every single forward pass, and in a recurrent net that means paying on every timestep of every layer. The question that won't leave me alone is: am I paying for something I don't need?

To answer that I have to be precise about what the layer actually computes, mechanically, because if part of the mechanism is dead weight I want to find it. For a layer with summed inputs a ∈ R^n — that's a_i = Σ_j w_ij x_j, the pre-activation to neuron i — it computes the mean μ = (1/n) Σ_i a_i and the standard deviation σ = √((1/n) Σ_i (a_i − μ)²), then standardizes ā_i = (a_i − μ)/σ · g_i with a learned per-neuron gain g, and finally y_i = f(ā_i + b_i). So mechanically there are two distinct operations bundled together. One: subtract the mean — recenter the vector to zero mean. Two: divide by the standard deviation — rescale it to unit spread. They feel like one move ("standardize") but they're not; they're two, and they cost differently and, more importantly, they buy different things.

Let me think about what each one buys, in terms of invariances, because that's the cleanest way to see what's load-bearing. Suppose a shift adds the same constant to every summed input, a → a + c·1; a recentered weight matrix can create exactly that kind of shift in the neuron vector. Because the layer subtracts μ, and μ shifts by exactly that same constant, the centered vector a − μ is unchanged. So the mean-subtraction is exactly what gives *re-centering invariance*: the output doesn't care about additive shifts of the summed-input vector. Now suppose instead I scale everything — a → α·a, say because I scaled the weight matrix or the inputs by α. Then μ → αμ, and σ → ασ, and (a − μ)/σ → (αa − αμ)/(ασ) = (a − μ)/σ, unchanged. So the division by σ is what gives *re-scaling invariance*: the output doesn't care about multiplicative scaling. Two operations, two invariances, cleanly separated — subtraction ↔ re-centering, division ↔ re-scaling.

The prevailing story credits both invariances for why the layer helps. But "credits both" is a story, not a measurement, and I have these two operations and I want to know which one is actually responsible for the stabilization, because if it's only one of them I can delete the other and stop paying for it. So let me ask: what does each invariance contribute to the thing that actually matters for optimization, which is keeping the variance of the activations and of the gradients under control?

The mean is the part I'm suspicious of. Stabilizing training is fundamentally about controlling *spread* — keeping hidden states and gradients from blowing up or vanishing as they propagate. Subtracting the mean recenters the vector, but what does it do to the variance? var(a − μ) = var(a): shifting every entry by the same constant leaves the spread untouched. Recentering moves the cloud of activations, it doesn't shrink or grow it. The operation that actually pulls the variance to a fixed scale, regardless of how big the weights got, is the *division by σ*. That splits the two operations along the axis I care about: one touches location, one touches scale, and only scale is what conditioning depends on. So I'll form a hypothesis I can act on — that re-scaling is the load-bearing invariance and re-centering is the weaker suspect — and then try to break it. The concrete bet: drop the mean-subtraction entirely, keep only the scale normalization, and see whether the training benefit survives while the cost drops.

If I drop the mean, what do I divide by? I can't use σ anymore, because σ = √((1/n) Σ (a_i − μ)²) is *defined* through μ — it's the spread *around the mean*. If I'm refusing to compute μ, I need a measure of scale that doesn't reference the mean at all. The natural candidate is the spread around *zero* instead of around the mean: the root mean square,

  RMS(a) = √( (1/n) Σ_{i=1}^n a_i² ).

This is just the quadratic mean of the entries — how big they are on average, measured from the origin rather than from their own center. So the normalization I'd try is

  ā_i = a_i / RMS(a) · g_i.

No μ anywhere. No subtraction. One statistic. And this isn't a wild departure from the layer I'm replacing: if the summed inputs already have zero mean, σ = √((1/n) Σ (a_i − 0)²) = RMS(a), so the two rules become the identical expression — just recentering switched off, coinciding exactly when the mean is already zero and parting ways only by however far the mean has drifted from it.

Dropping the mean throws away an invariance, so I need to know exactly which one moves. The division by RMS still scales linearly with the input: RMS(αa) = √((1/n) Σ (αa_i)²) = |α|·√((1/n) Σ a_i²) = α·RMS(a) for α > 0, so a/RMS(a) → αa/(αRMS(a)) = a/RMS(a): re-scaling invariance survives intact. But a shift a → a + c·1 has no μ to absorb it — nothing cancels — so re-centering invariance is gone. RMSNorm keeps exactly the invariance I bet was load-bearing and discards exactly the weaker one.

The differences from the neighbors matter, and I want to be sure I'm not quietly losing something BatchNorm or WeightNorm had. Write the layer in general form: y = f( (Wx)/RMS(a) ⊙ g + b ), with a = Wx and ⊙ elementwise. Scale the whole weight matrix, W' = δW. Then a' = δa, RMS(a') = δ·RMS(a) by the linearity I just showed, and (W'x)/RMS(a') = δWx/(δRMS(a)) = Wx/RMS(a). The output is untouched — invariant to weight-matrix re-scaling. Same computation for scaling the input, x' = δx: a' = δa again, same cancellation, invariant to input re-scaling, and that extends to scaling a whole batch or the whole dataset since each example cancels its own δ. These are the invariances the standard layer also had, so on this axis nothing is lost.

But what if I scale individual weight *vectors* by different factors — row i of W by δ_i? Then a_i → δ_i a_i, but RMS(a) = √((1/n) Σ_k a_k²) mixes *all* the neurons together, so it picks up a messy combination of the different δ_k and I cannot pull a single δ_i out of the ratio a_i/RMS(a). The cancellation fails — so RMSNorm is *not* invariant to per-weight-vector re-scaling. The standard layer wasn't either, for the same reason (its σ also pools all neurons). It's WeightNorm that has that particular invariance, by construction, because it normalizes each weight vector's length on its own; but WeightNorm pays for it by *not* being invariant to input or dataset re-scaling, since it never looks at the activation distribution at all. So the methods carve up the invariance space differently, and the choice I'm making is to keep the activation-side re-scaling invariances and drop the recentering one.

And recentering: scale aside, is RMSNorm invariant to any shift? Shift the dataset, or shift the weight matrix by a constant — RMS has no μ to absorb it (as I just showed), so no. The standard layer *was* invariant to weight-matrix recentering (its μ ate the shift); RMSNorm gives that up. So laying the methods side by side: BatchNorm is invariant to weight-matrix re-scaling, dataset re-scaling and re-centering, but not single-case re-scaling and not weight-matrix re-centering; WeightNorm only to the two weight re-scalings; the standard layer to weight-matrix re-scaling, weight-matrix re-centering, dataset re-scaling and single-case re-scaling but not dataset re-centering; and RMSNorm matches the standard layer everywhere *except* weight-matrix re-centering. One checkbox different, and it's the recentering checkbox — the very one I'm gambling doesn't matter.

There's a geometric reading of what RMS does that I want to hold onto, because it'll matter in a second when I consider an alternative. Normalizing by RMS forces a onto a sphere: a/RMS(a) has Σ_i (a_i/RMS)² = n. Checking that it's actually n and not something off by a constant — on a = (4, 1, 1, −2), RMS = √((16+1+1+4)/4) = √(22/4) ≈ 2.3452, the normalized vector is (1.7056, 0.4264, 0.4264, −0.8528), and its sum of squares comes out to 3.0000… wait, n here is 4, so it should be 4 — recomputing: 1.7056² + 0.4264² + 0.4264² + 0.8528² = 2.909 + 0.182 + 0.182 + 0.727 = 4.000. Good, exactly n = 4: the vector sits on the sphere of radius √n = 2. So RMS pins the *magnitude* of the summed-input vector to √n regardless of how the inputs and weights were scaled, which is why the activation magnitude stays stable across layers and across rescalings of the data. The output distribution is held fixed in scale even as the input and weight distributions wander.

Now, that "sphere of radius √n" should make me pause, because there's an obvious cheaper-looking alternative staring at me: why not just divide by the plain Euclidean norm ‖a‖ = √(Σ a_i²)? It's the same thing without the 1/n inside the root — in fact ‖a‖ = √n · RMS(a), they differ only by a constant factor √n. WeightNorm already uses the L2 norm successfully (on weights), so it's a natural thing to try on activations, and it would let me call `x.norm()` and skip the √n bookkeeping entirely. So let me take this alternative seriously and figure out whether the √n actually does anything. ‖a‖ would force a onto the *unit* sphere, radius 1, independent of n; RMS forces it onto the radius-√n sphere, which grows with the layer width. The question is whether a target radius that depends on n is a feature or just clutter. Consider what happens to a single *coordinate* under each scheme as the layer gets wider. I'll draw standard-normal vectors and look at the typical magnitude of one entry after normalizing. For n = 10: under L2 the average |coordinate| comes out around 0.259, under RMS around 0.820. For n = 1000: under L2 it collapses to about 0.025, while under RMS it stays around 0.795. So under plain L2 the per-coordinate scale shrinks like 1/√n — a 1000-wide layer has each entry ~10× smaller than a 100-wide one — whereas under RMS each coordinate stays at roughly unit scale no matter the width. That's decisive against the cheaper option: if I used plain L2, every layer of a different width would hand its successor activations at a different per-coordinate scale, and a learned gain initialized to 1 would be fighting a moving target. The 1/n inside the root is exactly what makes the normalization behave identically across layers of different size. So the factor I was tempted to drop is the one carrying the robustness, and I keep it. (This is the same reason I'd expect a naive L2 normalization of activations to *not* be a clean drop-in, even though it works for weights, where every vector has the same dimension by construction.)

So the forward rule is settled: ā_i = a_i/RMS(a) · g_i, one reduction, no subtraction, with the gain g and no bias by default — no bias because the bias/shift only existed to restore a location after recentering, and I'm not recentering, so there's nothing to restore. But the real test of a normalization layer isn't the forward invariances, it's whether it keeps the *gradients* well-behaved — the optimizer is what I'm trying to help, and stabilizing the backward pass is the point. Let me back-propagate.

Write v for the whole pre-nonlinearity expression, v = (Wx)/RMS(a) ⊙ g + b, so y = f(v). The easy parameters first. ∂L/∂b: b enters v additively, so ∂L/∂b = ∂L/∂v directly. ∂L/∂g: g multiplies the normalized inputs elementwise, so ∂L/∂g = ∂L/∂v ⊙ (Wx)/RMS(a) — that is, ∂L/∂v times the *normalized* summed inputs. Two things to notice. First, both of these are invariant to scaling x or W: scale by δ and (Wx)/RMS(a) is unchanged (that's the forward invariance I checked), so the g-gradient is unchanged, and the b-gradient never saw the scale at all. So the learned parameters' gradients don't move when the data or weights are rescaled — the optimizer sees a stable target. Second, the g-gradient is proportional to the *normalized* inputs, not the raw ones; the raw summed inputs could be huge or tiny but g always sees something of controlled magnitude, which keeps g's own scale from drifting.

Now the weight matrix, which is where it gets interesting because RMS is quadratic in a and a = Wx, so W enters both through the numerator and through the denominator. Let me get ∂(a_j/RMS(a))/∂a_k carefully, treating RMS as a function of all the a's. Let S = RMS(a) = √((1/n) Σ_k a_k²). Then ∂S/∂a_k = (1/(2S))·(1/n)·2a_k = a_k/(n S). Quotient rule on a_j/S:

  ∂(a_j/S)/∂a_k = (δ_jk·S − a_j·∂S/∂a_k)/S² = δ_jk/S − a_j·a_k/(n S³) = (1/S)·( δ_jk − a_j a_k/(n S²) ).

So as a matrix in (j,k), the Jacobian of the normalized vector with respect to a is

  R = (1/RMS(a))·( I − (Wx)(Wx)ᵀ / (n·RMS(a)²) ),

writing a = Wx. It's (1/S) times identity minus a rank-one outer-product correction, and that outer-product term ought to be the part that says "you can't move a in the radial direction without RMS moving too" — scaling a (the radial direction) leaves a/RMS(a) fixed, so the Jacobian must annihilate that direction, i.e. R·a should be 0. Checking symbolically: R·a = (1/S)(a − (aaᵀ)a/(nS²)) = (1/S)(a − a·(aᵀa)/(nS²)), and aᵀa = Σ a_k² = n S², so (aᵀa)/(nS²) = 1, giving R·a = (1/S)(a − a) = 0 — the radial direction is in the null space, as it must be. Matrix calculus this dense is easy to get a factor or sign wrong in, so I check the closed form against a finite-difference Jacobian of f(a) = a/RMS(a) on a = (4, 1, 1, −2): the two agree to within ~1e-5, and R·a comes back at machine-precision zero. The derivation holds.

Chaining to W is cleaner if I name the upstream gradient. Let u = ∂L/∂v and z = a/S. Since v = g ⊙ z + b, the gradient entering z is q = g ⊙ u. Since R is symmetric, the gradient entering a is Rq. And since a = Wx, one training case contributes the outer product

  ∂L/∂W = (R(g ⊙ u)) xᵀ.

For a batch I sum that expression over cases. The shape is the ordinary weight-gradient shape: a back-propagated feature vector times the input row. The structural fact I care about lives in R. How does R respond to scaling? Put a positive scale δ on either the input (x' = δx) or the weights (W' = δW); either way a → δa and RMS(a) → δ RMS(a). Then

  R' = (1/(δ·RMS(a)))·( I − (δWx)(δWx)ᵀ/(n·δ²·RMS(a)²) ) = (1/(δ·RMS(a)))·( I − (Wx)(Wx)ᵀ/(n·RMS(a)²) ) = (1/δ)·R,

because the δ² in the outer-product's numerator cancels the δ² in its denominator exactly, leaving the bracket untouched, and the leading 1/RMS picks up the extra 1/δ: R scales as 1/δ, *negatively* correlated with the scale. Now feed that back into ∂L/∂W and watch the two cases come apart. If I scaled the *input*, x' = δx: the xᵀ factor in the gradient is multiplied by δ, while R is multiplied by 1/δ, so the product is unchanged — ∂L/∂W is invariant to input scaling. That's the smoothness I want: rescaling the data doesn't jolt the weight gradients. But if I scaled the *weights*, W' = δW: x is unchanged, so only the R factor moves, and ∂L/∂W gets multiplied by 1/δ — it shrinks when the weights grew. That's not a flaw, it's a feature: it means a layer whose weights have grown large automatically receives *smaller* gradients, which damps further growth and keeps the weight norm in check. It's an implicit, per-layer learning-rate adaptation falling straight out of the quadratic form of RMS — big weights, small steps; small weights, big steps — with no schedule and no extra parameters. So the backward pass not only stays stable under rescaling, it self-regulates the weight magnitudes. The recentering I dropped contributed none of this; all of it comes from the re-scaling structure I kept.

So I've got a layer that keeps the invariance I argued matters, throws away the one I argued doesn't, has a well-behaved and self-adapting gradient, and costs one reduction instead of two with no subtraction pass over the vector. That last part is the original motivation, so let me count the savings concretely. The standard layer needs a reduction to get μ, then a second reduction over the centered squares to get σ, plus an elementwise subtraction of μ across all n entries. RMSNorm needs a single reduction — sum of squares — then a reciprocal-square-root and an elementwise multiply. One fewer reduction, and the whole subtraction pass gone. In a feed-forward layer that's a modest constant saving; in a recurrent net where the layer fires at every timestep along the sequence, that constant is multiplied by the sequence length and by the number of layers, and it turns into a meaningful wall-clock speedup — exactly the regime where the time-versus-steps gap I started with was widest.

Can I push the cost down further? The reduction over all n entries is now the dominant cost. The n neurons in a layer tend to be, roughly, identically distributed — they're n draws from a similar pre-activation distribution. If that's so, I don't strictly need all n of them to estimate their root-mean-square; a subsample should give a usable estimate. So take only the first k entries, with k = ceil(n·p) in the mathematical description for a fraction p of the layer, and estimate

  RMS̄(a) = √( (1/k) Σ_{i=1}^k a_i² ),

then normalize *all* n entries by this partial RMS: ā_i = a_i/RMS̄(a) · g_i for every i. Does it keep the invariances? The only property the invariance proofs used was linearity, RMS(αa) = α·RMS(a), and the partial estimate has it too: RMS̄(αa) = √((1/k) Σ_{i=1}^k (αa_i)²) = α·RMS̄(a). So scaling x or W still cancels exactly, and p-RMSNorm inherits the same invariance table as full RMSNorm. The cost now scales with k instead of n, so the reduction shrinks roughly in proportion to k/n; if k is a small fraction of n, I touch only that fraction of the vector to set the scale for the whole thing.

I should be honest about what the subsample costs me, though. RMS̄ is not an unbiased copy of the full RMS: the sample mean of squares may track the second moment under the i.i.d. assumption, but the square root and the finite slice introduce bias and noise, and the smaller k is the worse both effects get. The danger is sharpest in the backward pass, because that 1/RMS sits in the denominator of the gradient, and a denominator estimated from very few samples can occasionally come out small, which makes the gradient spike. So I'd expect gradient instability — gradients tending to blow up — when k is pushed too small, and that's the practical limit on how aggressive p can be. I can't settle exactly how low p can go from the desk; that's the thing I'd want to verify empirically, watching the gradient norm as I shrink p.

Let me land it on code. The framework already gives an `__init__` with `d`, a partial ratio `p`, `eps`, `bias`, a per-neuron `scale` initialized to 1, and an optional `offset` — exactly what the design calls for, since there's no mean to restore unless bias is explicitly asked for. I'll call the class RMSNorm; the only open slot is `forward`, and it's almost nothing, which is the point:

```python
def forward(self, x):
    # x: [..., d], the summed inputs a to the layer.
    if self.p < 0. or self.p > 1.:
        norm_x = x.norm(2, dim=-1, keepdim=True)     # ||a|| = sqrt(sum a_i^2): one reduction
        d_x = self.d
    else:
        # use only the first int(d*p) entries to estimate RMS, then normalize all d by it
        partial_size = int(self.d * self.p)
        partial_x, _ = torch.split(x, [partial_size, self.d - partial_size], dim=-1)
        norm_x = partial_x.norm(2, dim=-1, keepdim=True)
        d_x = partial_size

    rms_x = norm_x * d_x ** (-1. / 2)                # ||slice||/sqrt(d_x): full RMS or partial estimate
    x_normed = x / (rms_x + self.eps)                # a_i / RMS(a): no mean subtracted, one division

    if self.bias:
        return self.scale * x_normed + self.offset
    return self.scale * x_normed                     # a_i / RMS(a) * g_i
```

Running one input through both branches against RMS computed by hand, on a = (4, 1, 1, −2), width 4: the full branch calls `x.norm(2)` = √(16+1+1+4) = √22 ≈ 4.6904, multiplies by d_x^(−1/2) = 1/√4 = 1/2, giving rms_x ≈ 2.3452 — exactly √(22/4) = RMS(a) — and dividing gives a/rms_x = (1.7056, 0.4264, 0.4264, −0.8528), matching the module's output to ~1e-6; the √n bookkeeping I argued for is sitting in that `d_x ** (-0.5)`. The partial branch with p = 0.5 takes partial_size = int(4·0.5) = 2, the first two entries (4, 1), `partial_x.norm(2)` = √17 ≈ 4.1231, times 1/√2 gives rms_x ≈ 2.9155 = √(17/2) = RMS̄ over those two entries — and divides the *whole* four-vector by it, returning (1.3720, 0.3430, 0.3430, −0.6860), again matching by hand to ~1e-6. Both branches do exactly what the derivation says.

There is no `mean`, no subtraction — one reduction where the standard layer had two. The gain `scale` is per-neuron and starts at 1, so the normalized vector isn't distorted by a learned gain at initialization, and the offset is created only when explicitly requested, since re-centering is exactly the thing I've deliberately dropped.

That's the whole trade: one reduction and no subtraction in place of two reductions and a subtraction, on exactly the invariance I could show doesn't do the stabilizing work — which is the fraction of the wall-clock gap I opened with that I don't have to keep paying for.
