I start with the recursion because it tells me exactly where the difficulty is. I can predict by integrating the previous filtering distribution through the transition, and I can update by multiplying the predictive density by the new likelihood and normalizing. The symbols are simple: $p(x_k\mid y_{1:k-1})=\int p(x_k\mid x_{k-1})p(x_{k-1}\mid y_{1:k-1})dx_{k-1}$, then $p(x_k\mid y_{1:k})\propto g(y_k\mid x_k)p(x_k\mid y_{1:k-1})$. The problem is not the probability calculus. The problem is that, once the dynamics or observations are nonlinear or the noises are non-Gaussian, I do not have a tractable density to carry through these operations.

The Kalman case shows why this is hard rather than easy. In the linear-Gaussian model, the whole posterior remains Gaussian, so mean and covariance are a closed representation. Prediction and update are just algebra on those moments. But that closure is a property of a special family, not a property of filtering itself. If the likelihood bends the density into a ridge, or if a squared observation gives two modes, or if a bearing measurement leaves range ambiguous, two moments are no longer the distribution. A single Gaussian can be a confident summary of the wrong shape. A grid is more honest, but then I am paying for points wherever the grid happens to be, not where the probability mass moves.

So I change the representation first. I do not try to store a formula for the filtering density. I store a finite empirical measure: particles $x_k^1,\ldots,x_k^N$ with weights $W_k^1,\ldots,W_k^N$, so that for any test function $\varphi$ I read the posterior expectation as $\sum_i W_k^i\varphi(x_k^i)$. If all the weights are equal this is just an ordinary sample average. If the posterior is skewed or has several modes, the cloud can put mass in several places. This is the first important shift: the object I carry through time is not a state estimate, and not a Gaussian approximation, but an approximate filtering distribution.

Prediction now becomes almost embarrassingly direct. If the current particles approximate $p(x_{k-1}\mid y_{1:k-1})$, then to sample the predictive distribution I draw fresh process noises $w_{k-1}^i$ and apply the transition, $x_k^{i,*}=f_{k-1}(x_{k-1}^i,w_{k-1}^i)$. This is not a heuristic replacement for the Chapman-Kolmogorov integral; it is a Monte Carlo realization of it. The transition density itself is defined by pushing noise through a deterministic map, so sampling the old state and the new noise and applying the map gives a draw from the marginal predictive law. I have turned the prediction integral into one propagation per particle.

The update is the real wall. I have predicted particles from $p(x_k\mid y_{1:k-1})$, and Bayes asks me to tilt that predictive distribution by the likelihood $g(y_k\mid x_k)$. I can assign each predicted particle an unnormalized weight $\tilde W_k^i = W_{k-1}^i g(y_k\mid x_k^{i,*})$ and normalize across the finite cloud. The Bayesian normalizer, the integral over state space, disappears into the finite sum. At this point the weighted particles represent the filtering distribution, because posterior expectations are approximated by likelihood-weighted predictive expectations.

For a moment I am tempted to stop there. Weighted particles are a valid representation, and keeping them seems more accurate than adding another random resampling step. But this only works for one update. If I keep carrying the weights forward, the weight at time $k$ becomes a product of incremental likelihood or proposal-correction factors. In the general sequential importance sampling recursion, $w_k=w_{k-1}\alpha_k$, so after many observations each weight is the accumulated product of many random terms. Any mismatch between proposal and target compounds. Doucet and Johansen's simple Gaussian product example makes the danger precise: even when the proposal scale is close to the target scale, the relative variance grows exponentially with time. In practical terms, after enough updates nearly all normalized weight sits on one or a few trajectories. Then I am pretending to have $N$ particles while effectively using one.

I need a diagnostic for this collapse. The effective sample size, $\mathrm{ESS}=1/\sum_i(W_k^i)^2$, says how many equal-weight samples the weighted cloud is roughly worth. If all weights are $1/N$, ESS is $N$. If one weight is one and all others are zero, ESS is one. This is exactly the failure mode I need to fight: not a vague loss of quality, but a visible concentration of probability mass onto too few particles.

The Smith-Gelfand weighted bootstrap gives the missing move. Given samples from a distribution $g$ and a target proportional to $f$, resample the old sample with probabilities proportional to $f/g$ and the resampled values approximate the target. In the filtering update, the predicted particle cloud is the sample from the predictive distribution and the likelihood is the tilt. So after computing normalized weights, I draw $N$ offspring from the existing particles with probabilities $W_k^i$ and reset the surviving particles to equal weight. This is Bayes' rule implemented as selection and copying. Particles that explain the observation are likely to be copied; particles far from the likelihood are likely to disappear.

Now I can see why resampling is essential rather than decorative. It cuts the product of weights. Without resampling, today's small weight can keep haunting the particle forever, and yesterday's lucky high weight keeps multiplying. With resampling, I replace the weighted measure by an equally weighted empirical measure whose expected offspring counts are proportional to the weights. I add immediate Monte Carlo noise, but I stop carrying a long product of incremental weights. The next prediction starts from a cloud concentrated where the current posterior mass is, not from many dead particles with near-zero weights.

There is a price. Resampling creates duplicates, and repeated resampling destroys diversity in old path components. That matters for smoothing whole trajectories, because early states eventually have only a few ancestors. For filtering the current state, it is a tolerable price: each prediction step injects fresh process noise into the current state, and the target is the current marginal. I should not resample blindly, though. If the weights are already healthy, resampling only adds noise. The practical rule is to resample when ESS falls below a threshold such as $N/2$.

The proposal choice also matters. The lowest-variance proposal for the new state would sample from $p(x_k\mid y_k,x_{k-1})\propto g(y_k\mid x_k)f(x_k\mid x_{k-1})$, because then the incremental weight no longer depends on the sampled $x_k$. But sampling that distribution and computing its normalizer usually requires the same hard integral I am trying to avoid. The simple robust choice is to propose from the transition prior, $f(x_k\mid x_{k-1})$. Then the transition density cancels from the importance ratio, and the incremental weight is just the likelihood $g(y_k\mid x_k)$. This is why the algorithm only needs three model operations: sample the prior, propagate through the dynamics, and evaluate the observation likelihood.

I also have to account for finite sample impoverishment. If the likelihood barely overlaps the predictive cloud, only a few predicted particles receive meaningful weight, and resampling copies them many times. If the process noise is small, those copies remain nearly identical. Gordon, Salmond, and Smith handle this with roughening: after resampling, add small jitter with component scale proportional to the particle range times $N^{-1/d}$. The scale shrinks as the particle cloud becomes denser, but it breaks exact duplicates when the dynamics do not. Their prior-editing trick is another response to poor overlap, using a one-step lookahead to avoid spending particles in places the next likelihood will reject, but the core mechanism remains the same: represent the filtering distribution by weighted particles, watch degeneracy, and resample to keep the live mass represented.

The method that falls out is therefore not just SIR pseudocode. It is a particular answer to the filtering recursion. Prediction is simulation of the transition integral. Update is likelihood weighting of the predictive empirical measure. Resampling is the stability device that prevents sequential importance weights from collapsing over time. Estimates are read from the weighted cloud before or after resampling, and the method remains valid for nonlinear dynamics, nonlinear measurements, and non-Gaussian noise because it never assumes a closed parametric form for the posterior.
