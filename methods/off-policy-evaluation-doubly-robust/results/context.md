## Research question

We have logged data generated by some decision-making policy — a stream of (context, action, observed reward) tuples in the simplest case, or (state, action, reward, next-state) trajectories in the sequential case — and we want to know how *a different* policy would have performed, without ever running it. In Internet advertising we see only whether a user clicked the ad we showed, never what would have happened with the ad we did not show; in healthcare we observe outcomes only for the treatment a patient actually received; in robotics or dialogue, deploying an untested policy to measure its value is expensive, risky, or unethical. So the goal is **off-policy value evaluation**: given data from a *behavior* policy, estimate the value (expected total reward) of a *target* policy.

The core obstacle is that the logged data does not represent the action proportions the target policy would induce. We only ever observe the reward of the action that was taken, so we cannot simply replay the target policy on the logged contexts and read off its rewards. Any solution must somehow correct for this distribution mismatch. What we would want from a good estimator: that it be **unbiased** (or have bias we can actually control), that it have **low variance** so it converges fast from a limited, expensive dataset, and that — in the sequential case — its accuracy not collapse as the planning horizon grows.

## Background

**The contextual bandit / off-policy setting.** In the one-step (contextual bandit) formulation, the world draws (x, r⃗) from a distribution D, reveals only the context x; a policy picks an action a ~ p(a | x, h) (h = history); and only the chosen reward r_a is revealed, never r_{a'} for a' ≠ a. The value of a stationary target policy π is V^π = E_{(x,r⃗)~D}[ r_{π(x)} ]. In the sequential (MDP) formulation M = ⟨S, A, P, R, γ⟩, a length-H trajectory τ = (s_1,a_1,r_1,…,s_H,a_H,r_H,s_{H+1}) is generated by s_1 ~ μ, a_t ~ π_0(·|s_t), s_{t+1} ~ P(·|s_t,a_t); the target policy's value is v^{π_1,H} = E_{τ~(μ,π_1)}[ Σ_{t=1}^H γ^{t-1} r_t ], computable when the model is known by the Bellman recursion V^0 ≡ 0, Q^h(s,a) = E_{s'}[ R(s,a) + γ V^{h-1}(s') ], V^h(s) = E_{a~π_1}[ Q^h(s,a) ].

**Inverse-probability weighting.** The classical device for correcting a sampling mismatch is the Horvitz–Thompson estimator (Horvitz & Thompson 1952): to estimate a population total from a sample where unit i was included with known probability p_i, weight each observed value by 1/p_i. Reweighting by the inverse inclusion probability makes the sample-based average an unbiased estimate of the population quantity. Transplanted to policy evaluation, the "inclusion probability" is the behavior policy's probability of the action the target policy would have chosen, and the correction is the importance ratio π_1/π_0.

**Importance sampling for sequential off-policy evaluation.** Precup, Sutton & Singh (2000) carried importance weighting into reinforcement learning for off-policy evaluation, multiplying per-step ratios ρ_t = π_1(a_t|s_t)/π_0(a_t|s_t) along a trajectory. This is unbiased and makes no assumption about the size of the state space, but the cumulative ratio ρ_{1:t} = ∏_{t'≤t} ρ_{t'} is a product of per-step ratios, and its variance compounds — empirically the estimate is dominated by noise once the horizon is more than a handful of steps (a known failure mode for long-horizon problems, e.g. Mandel et al. 2014).

**Model-based / regression evaluation, and its un-auditable bias.** The other classical route is to fit a model — a reward function in the bandit case, an MDP in the sequential case — and evaluate the target policy against the fitted model. When an exact (tabular) representation is available and every relevant state-action pair is seen enough, such estimators have provably low variance and negligible bias (Mannor et al. 2007; Paduraru 2013). But real problems have large or infinite state spaces, so function approximation is unavoidable, and approximation error introduces a bias that in general *cannot be estimated from the data itself* — which is precisely what makes a regression-based value estimate untrustworthy as a guarantee.

**Augmented inverse-probability estimation in statistics (the missing-data / causal-inference line).** Independently of RL, statisticians studying estimation from incomplete data developed estimators that combine an inverse-probability-weighting term with an outcome-regression term. Cassel, Särndal & Wretman (1976) introduced a generalized-regression ("generalized difference") estimator of this combined form for survey sampling. Robins, Rotnitzky & Zhao (1994) and Robins & Rotnitzky (1995) developed the augmented inverse-probability-weighted (AIPW) estimators and the semiparametric-efficiency theory behind them; the resulting estimators carry a **double-protection** property: they remain consistent if *either* the propensity (selection-probability) model *or* the outcome-regression model is correctly specified — not necessarily both. The intuition recorded in expositions of this line (e.g. Bang & Robins 2005; standard AIPW treatments): the augmentation term carries the factor (treatment-indicator − propensity), which has mean zero when the propensity model is right, so it vanishes and the estimator collapses to pure IPW; and when the propensity is wrong but the outcome model is right, the augmentation corrects the misweighting. Most of this statistics analysis is asymptotic and conditioned on one model being exactly correct.

**Control variates.** A staple of Monte Carlo estimation: to estimate θ = E[X], if you have another variable Y with known mean E[Y] that is correlated with X, the estimator X − Y + E[Y] is still unbiased for θ but has variance Var(X) + Var(Y) − 2Cov(X,Y), which is smaller than Var(X) whenever 2Cov(X,Y) > Var(Y). Y is the control variate; the more correlated and the more its mean is known, the more variance it removes.

## Baselines

**Direct method / regression estimator (DM / REG).** Fit ρ̂_a(x), an estimate of the expected reward ρ_a(x) = E[r_a | x] (bandit), or fit an MDP and compute V̂(s) by the Bellman recursion (sequential). Then average the model's prediction for the target policy: V̂_DM = (1/|S|) Σ_x ρ̂_{π(x)}(x). *Core idea*: substitute a learned reward/value for the unobserved counterfactual reward. *Math*: V̂_DM unbiased iff ρ̂ unbiased; its bias equals |E_x[ Δ ]| with Δ = ρ̂ − ρ the model's error. *Gap*: the model is fit without knowledge of π, so it may be accurate in regions irrelevant to V^π and poor where it matters; and because the bias is the (data-unquantifiable) approximation error of the function class, the estimate comes with no trustworthy error guarantee. Its one virtue is low variance.

**Inverse propensity score / importance sampling (IPS / IS).** Reweight the observed reward by the inverse behavior probability of the target action. Bandit: V̂_IPS = (1/|S|) Σ r_a · I(π(x)=a) / p̂(a|x,h). Sequential (trajectory-wise and the lower-variance step-wise versions): V_IS = ρ_{1:H} · Σ_t γ^{t-1} r_t, V_step-IS = Σ_t γ^{t-1} ρ_{1:t} r_t. *Core idea*: correct the action-proportion mismatch with importance weights; IPS is the special case of the combined estimator with ρ̂ ≡ 0. *Math*: unbiased when p̂ = p; bias |E_x[ ρ_{π(x)} δ ]| with δ = 1 − p/p̂. *Gap*: variance. The reweighted variable's range explodes as p → 0, and in the sequential case the cumulative ratio is a product over H steps, so variance grows roughly exponentially in horizon. Since the behavior policy is usually known or well-estimable, IPS has small bias but is often dominated by variance — exactly when the target and behavior policies differ most.

**Weighted importance sampling (WIS).** A biased-but-consistent variant that self-normalizes by the average cumulative ratio w_t = (1/|D|) Σ_i ρ_{1:t}^{(i)}: V_WIS = (ρ_{1:H}/w_H) Σ_t γ^{t-1} r_t, and its step-wise form V_step-WIS = Σ_t γ^{t-1} (ρ_{1:t}/w_t) r_t. *Core idea*: trade a small bias for substantially lower variance than raw IS; the step-wise version is the standard practical point estimator in the IS family. *Gap*: still purely importance-sampling-based — it does not exploit a reward/value model at all, and remains the natural variance-reduction yardstick to beat.

**Offset Tree and benign-bandit reward-estimate tricks.** Earlier machine-learning work used a crude reward estimate as an "offset" (Beygelzimer & Langford 2009) or to bound regret by the bandit's variance (Hazan & Kale). These hint that a reward estimate can be folded into an importance-weighted procedure, but use only a coarse/constant estimate rather than a fitted model and a principled combination.

## Evaluation settings

Natural evaluation settings for this problem:

- **Multiclass classification with bandit feedback.** A k-class classification dataset is turned into a k-armed contextual bandit by revealing, for each example, the loss of a single uniformly-random action a ~ Unif(1..k); then p(a|x) ≡ 1/k is known exactly. UCI benchmark datasets serve as the testbed (ecoli, glass, letter, optdigits, page-blocks, pendigits, satimage, vehicle, yeast). A classifier is the policy; classification error is its expected loss. This lets one compare estimators of policy value against the fully-observed ground-truth error, and also use the estimators inside a learner for policy optimization.
- **Covariate-shift value estimation.** Estimating the average value of a quantity (e.g. average user visits to a web portal) when the data are sampled non-uniformly, with known sampling probabilities p_i — a two-arm special case of off-policy evaluation. Metrics: bias, standard deviation, and root-mean-squared error of the estimate against the true average; sweeping dataset size.
- **RL benchmark domains.** Finite-horizon control problems where ground-truth target-policy value can be computed: Mountain Car (2-D continuous state, 3 actions, deterministic, horizon ~100), the Sailing stochastic shortest-path grid (stochastic transitions, 8 actions), and a simulator fit to the KDD Cup 1998 donation dataset (stochastic, H = 22). Behavior policy uniformly random; target policy a mixture (1−α)π_train + α π_0 sweeping α to control the gap between behavior and target. Metrics: RMSE of the value estimate relative to the true value across many runs, as a function of how much data is allotted to evaluation versus model-fitting; and, for the safe-improvement use, the true value of the policy a confidence-bound-based selector recommends.
