I have a pile of logged data and a question that sounds simple and turns out not to be. The data was generated by some policy that was running in the wild — call it the behavior policy — and each record is a context, the action that policy happened to take, and the reward that came back for that one action. Nothing else. In an ad system: the user, the ad we showed, whether they clicked. I never see what would have happened with the ad I didn't show. Now someone hands me a *different* policy — a target policy — and asks: what is its value? What reward would it earn, on average, if I ran it? And I can't just run it, because running an untested policy on real users (or real patients, or a real robot) is exactly the expensive, risky thing I'm trying to avoid. I have to answer from the logs alone.

So let me be precise about the obstacle, because the obstacle is the whole problem. The target policy π wants to take action π(x) in context x. But in the logs, the action taken in context x was whatever the behavior policy chose, which is generally *not* π(x). And I only observed the reward of the action that was actually taken. If π(x) ≠ the logged action, I have no reward for π(x) at all in that record. I cannot replay π on the logged contexts and read off its rewards. The logged action proportions are wrong — they reflect the behavior policy, not π — and the rewards I'd need are mostly missing. The value I want is V^π = E_{(x,r⃗)~D}[ r_{π(x)} ], an expectation over the reward of the action π would pick, and the data won't hand me those rewards directly.

There are two obvious things to try, and I want to feel out exactly where each one breaks, because the failure modes are going to be the whole story.

First idea: build a model of the reward. Regress the observed reward on (context, action), get ρ̂_a(x) ≈ E[r_a | x]. Then I never need the missing rewards — for any context I just ask the model what π(x) would earn and average: V̂_DM = (1/|S|) Σ_x ρ̂_{π(x)}(x). Clean. Low variance, too — there's no reweighting, no dividing by small numbers, just an average of model predictions, so it concentrates fast. The trouble is bias. The model is fit to *some* function class, and if the truth isn't in that class — which, for any real problem with a big state space, it isn't — then ρ̂ ≠ ρ and the estimate inherits that error. Write Δ = ρ̂ − ρ. The bias of the direct method is E_x[Δ], the average model error along the target policy's actions. And here's the part that really bothers me: that bias is the approximation error of my function class, and I cannot measure the approximation error from the data. There's no held-out quantity that tells me how wrong my model is in the way that matters. Worse, the model was fit without knowing π — it spends its capacity fitting wherever the *behavior* data is dense, which may be exactly the regions π never visits. So the direct method gives me a number with low variance and an unknown, unauditable bias. As a value estimate I'm supposed to *trust* — say, before deploying a policy — that's disqualifying.

Second idea: don't model the reward, correct the proportions. This is the old survey-sampling move — Horvitz and Thompson, 1952: if I sampled unit i with known probability p_i, then weighting its value by 1/p_i makes the sample average unbiased for the population total, because the weighting exactly undoes the sampling. Here the "sampling probability" of the action I need is the behavior policy's probability of taking π(x). So: keep only the records where the logged action *happens* to equal π(x), and weight each by the inverse behavior probability. V̂_IPS = (1/|S|) Σ r_a · I(π(x)=a) / p̂(a|x,h). When my estimate p̂ of the behavior policy is correct, this is unbiased — and the behavior policy is usually something I know or can estimate well, since it's *my own* logging system. So bias is small. The bias, if p̂ is off, works out to E_x[ρ_{π(x)} δ] where δ = 1 − p/p̂ measures how wrong the propensity estimate is.

But now the variance is awful. Look at the random variable being averaged: r_a / p̂. Its range scales like 1/p̂. When the behavior policy rarely takes the action π wants — p small — that weight is enormous, and a single record with a tiny propensity dominates the whole average. The estimator is unbiased but it bounces around wildly, and it bounces hardest precisely when π and the behavior policy disagree a lot, which is the interesting case. So importance sampling has the opposite profile from the direct method: trustworthy in bias, terrible in variance.

And it gets worse the moment I leave the one-step bandit and go to sequential problems — full trajectories, an MDP. Precup, Sutton and Singh in 2000 carried importance weighting into reinforcement learning: along a trajectory you multiply the per-step ratios ρ_t = π_1(a_t|s_t)/π_0(a_t|s_t), and the cumulative weight at step t is ρ_{1:t} = ∏_{t'≤t} ρ_{t'}. Unbiased, and beautifully it doesn't care how big the state space is. But that cumulative weight is a *product* of H mean-one ratios, and the variance of a product of random variables compounds multiplicatively. Stretch the horizon to a few dozen steps and the variance is astronomical — the estimate is essentially all noise. So in the sequential case the variance doesn't just hurt, it grows exponentially in the horizon. Importance sampling alone is hopeless for any long-horizon problem.

So I'm stuck between two estimators that fail in complementary ways. The model is low-variance, biased, and the bias is un-auditable. Importance sampling is unbiased (given the behavior policy, which I have) but its variance explodes, worst exactly when the policies differ and worst of all over long horizons. And I keep wanting to say: I have *both* of these. I have a fitted reward model *and* importance weights. They're not rivals; I'm treating them as an either/or when I have both in hand. Can I use both at once and get the good half of each?

Let me think about what's actually wrong with each, mechanically, because that's where the combination has to come from. Importance sampling's variance comes from reweighting the *raw* reward r_a by a possibly-huge factor. The raw reward is a big number; multiply it by 1/p̂ and you get a giant number. What if the thing being reweighted were *small*? The model gives me ρ̂_a(x), a guess at the reward. If the model is any good, the *residual* r_a − ρ̂_a(x) is small — much smaller than r_a itself. So if I importance-weight the residual instead of the reward, the same 1/p̂ factor multiplies a small quantity, and the product is small, and a small random variable has small variance. That's the lever. Reweight the part the model got *wrong*, not the whole reward.

But if I only importance-weight the residual, I've thrown away the reward level — the residual averages to zero if the model is perfect, so this term alone estimates nothing. I need to add the level back. And I have it: the model's own prediction for the target action, ρ̂_{π(x)}(x). That's the direct-method estimate. So: use the model's prediction as a baseline, and add an importance-weighted correction for the residual on top.

Let me write it down and stare at it.

V̂_DR = (1/|S|) Σ_{(x,h,a,r_a)} [ (r_a − ρ̂_a(x)) · I(π(x)=a) / p̂(a|x,h) + ρ̂_{π(x)}(x) ].

The second term, ρ̂_{π(x)}(x), is the model's baseline — if I had no logged action matching π(x), this is all I'd have, and it's just the direct method. The first term is the correction: whenever the logged action *does* equal π(x), I look at how wrong the model was there, r_a − ρ̂_a(x), and importance-weight that residual to correct the baseline. If the model is good the residual is tiny and the correction barely moves the baseline — low variance. If the model is bad but the behavior weights are right, the correction inherits importance sampling's unbiasedness and does the heavy lifting. This feels right. Let me check whether it actually is.

The bias first, because the bias is where I claimed something I haven't earned. Let me define the two error quantities cleanly: Δ(a,x) = ρ̂_a(x) − ρ_a(x), the additive model error, and δ(a,x,h) = 1 − p(a|x,h)/p̂(a|x,h), the multiplicative propensity error. Δ = 0 means the model is right; δ = 0 means the weights are right. I'll assume ρ̂ and p̂ are fixed independently of the data S being averaged (in practice: fit them on a separate fold) — I'll come back to *why* that matters. Now take one term of the sum, expectation over (x, r⃗) ~ D and a ~ p(·|x,h), conditioning on the history h. Write I for I(π(x)=a), and abbreviate p, p̂ for the values at the target action.

E[ (r_a − ρ̂_a) I / p̂ + ρ̂_{π(x)} ].

Substitute ρ̂_a = ρ_a + Δ everywhere it appears:

= E[ (r_a − ρ_a − Δ) I / p̂ + ρ_{π(x)} + Δ ].

Now take the expectation over the reward noise first: E[r_a | x, a] = ρ_a, so r_a − ρ_a has mean zero and the (r_a − ρ_a) I/p̂ piece vanishes in expectation. I'm left with

= E_{x,a}[ −Δ I / p̂ + Δ ] + E_x[ ρ_{π(x)} ] = E_{x,a}[ Δ (1 − I/p̂) ] + V^π.

The ρ_{π(x)} term came straight out as V^π — that's the value I want, good. Now the leftover. The expectation of the indicator I = I(π(x)=a) over a ~ p(·|x,h) is just p, the behavior probability of the target action. So E_a[1 − I/p̂] = 1 − p/p̂ = δ. Therefore

= E_x[ Δ · δ ] + V^π.

So the bias of a single term — and summing over terms, of the whole estimator — is exactly **E_x[ Δ δ ]**. The bias is the *product* of the two errors. Now I see why the second idea kept tugging at me. The model error Δ multiplies the propensity error δ. If the model is right, Δ = 0 and the product is zero — unbiased, no matter how wrong the weights are. If the weights are right, δ = 0 and the product is zero — unbiased, no matter how wrong the model is. I only need *one* of the two to be correct, and the estimator is unbiased. That's the doubly robust property, and it isn't an assumption I'm imposing — it falls straight out of the algebra, because the two errors enter only as a product. Two independent chances to be right.

And it sharpens the comparison. The direct method is this estimator with the importance correction switched off; its bias is E_x[Δ], needing Δ ≈ 0. Importance sampling is this estimator with ρ̂ ≡ 0 (no model), so Δ becomes −ρ and its bias is E_x[ρ_{π(x)} δ], needing δ ≈ 0. The doubly robust estimator needs only Δ ≈ 0 *or* δ ≈ 0. Even when neither is exactly zero, its leading bias is a product Δδ rather than either factor alone, so if the model and the weights are both moderately accurate, the remaining bias is second-order in their errors.

Now the variance, because unbiasedness is worthless if the thing still bounces around like raw importance sampling. Take stationary policies so I can drop the history h, and analyze the second moment of one term, then subtract the square of the mean. Let me name the zero-mean reward-noise piece ε = (r_a − ρ_a) I / p̂; conditioned on x and a, E[ε] = 0. The single term, after the same substitution as before, is ε + ρ_{π(x)} + Δ(1 − I/p̂). Its second moment:

E[ (ε + ρ_{π(x)} + Δ(1 − I/p̂))² ].

Because ε has conditional mean zero once x and a are fixed, its cross terms with the non-reward pieces drop, and I get

= E[ε²] + E[ρ_{π(x)}²] + 2 E[ ρ_{π(x)} Δ (1 − I/p̂) ] + E[ Δ²(1 − I/p̂)² ].

Handle the indicator expectations. The cross term: E_a[1 − I/p̂] = δ, so 2 E[ρ_{π(x)} Δ (1−I/p̂)] = 2 E_x[ ρ_{π(x)} Δ δ ]. The square term: E_a[(1 − I/p̂)²] = 1 − 2p/p̂ + E_a[I]/p̂² = 1 − 2p/p̂ + p/p̂². I want to pull the clean δ² out of that, so split p/p̂² = p²/p̂² + p(1−p)/p̂². Then 1 − 2p/p̂ + p²/p̂² = (1 − p/p̂)² = δ², leaving an extra p(1−p)/p̂². So

E[ Δ²(1 − I/p̂)² ] = E_x[ Δ² δ² ] + E_x[ Δ² · p(1−p)/p̂² ].

Collecting, the second moment is

E[ε²] + E_x[ ρ_{π(x)}² + 2 ρ_{π(x)} Δδ + Δ²δ² ] + E_x[ Δ² p(1−p)/p̂² ] = E[ε²] + E_x[ (ρ_{π(x)} + Δδ)² ] + E_x[ Δ² p(1−p)/p̂² ].

Subtract the square of the mean, E_x[ρ_{π(x)} + Δδ]², to turn the middle bracket into a variance. And rewrite the last factor: p(1−p)/p̂² = ((1−p)/p) · (p/p̂)² = ((1−p)/p)(1 − δ)². So, summing over the |S| terms,

**Var[V̂_DR] = (1/|S|) ( E[ε²] + Var_x[ρ_{π(x)} + Δδ] + E_x[ ((1−p)/p) · Δ²(1−δ)² ] ).**

Three terms, and each one tells me something. The first, E[ε²], is irreducible reward noise — it's there in any estimator that touches the observed rewards. The second is the variance of the target value itself across contexts, perturbed by Δδ — small when the model and weights are decent. The third is the *importance-weighting penalty*: it carries the (1−p)/p factor that blows up when the behavior policy rarely takes the target action, p ≪ 1. This is the term that kills plain importance sampling. And here is the payoff of using the model as a baseline: that penalty is multiplied by **Δ²**, the squared *model error*. Let me derive the importance-sampling variance the same way to see the contrast — it's just ρ̂ ≡ 0, i.e. replace Δ by −ρ_{π(x)} in the last two terms:

Var[V̂_IPS] = (1/|S|) ( E[ε²] + Var_x[ρ_{π(x)} − ρ_{π(x)}δ] + E_x[ ((1−p)/p) · ρ_{π(x)}²(1−δ)² ] ).

Same first term, comparable second term when δ ≈ 0. But the third term has **ρ_{π(x)}²** where doubly robust has **Δ²**. The penalty is multiplied by the squared *reward magnitude* for importance sampling, versus the squared *model error* for doubly robust. Whenever the model is good enough that |Δ| < |ρ_{π(x)}|, the doubly robust penalty is smaller, and dramatically smaller when p is tiny. That's the variance reduction, made concrete: I've replaced "reweight the whole reward" by "reweight only what the model missed," and the variance term shrinks from ρ² to Δ². The direct method's variance, for completeness, is (1/|S|) Var_x[ρ_{π(x)} + Δ] — no reward-noise term, no 1/p penalty, so it's the lowest of the three, which is exactly why its low variance was never the problem; its bias was.

The baseline ρ̂_{π(x)}(x) I added back has an expectation I can compute under the target policy — and subtracting an importance-weighted version of something correlated with my noisy term, then adding back its known mean, is the textbook control-variate trick: to estimate E[X], use X − Y + E[Y] where Y is correlated with X and E[Y] is known; the variance becomes Var(X) + Var(Y) − 2Cov(X,Y), reduced whenever Y tracks X. The model is the control variate. The more it correlates with the reward — the better the model — the more importance-sampling variance it cancels. The double-robustness and the variance reduction are two faces of the same construction: the model enters additively as a baseline whose target-policy mean is known (giving unbiasedness, hence robustness) and as a correction to the reweighted term (shrinking ρ² to Δ², hence variance reduction).

I recognize the same skeleton in the missing-data statistics line. Cassel, Särndal and Wretman in 1976 wrote down a generalized-regression estimator of this combined inverse-probability-plus-regression shape for survey sampling; Robins, Rotnitzky and Zhao in 1994, and Robins and Rotnitzky in 1995, developed the augmented inverse-probability-weighted estimators and proved the double-robustness property in the missing-data / causal-inference setting: consistent if *either* the propensity model *or* the outcome-regression model is right. What I need for off-policy evaluation is the same device, but with a finite-sample accounting of how the bias E[Δδ] and the variance degrade as Δ and δ drift from zero, instead of assuming one model is exactly correct.

Now the harder thing: the sequential case, the long-horizon MDP where importance sampling's variance was exponential in H. I can't just slap the bandit estimator on; I have a whole trajectory and a value function with a Bellman structure. Let me look again at the step-wise importance sampling estimator, V_step-IS = Σ_t γ^{t-1} ρ_{1:t} r_t, and try to write it recursively, because recursion is how the Bellman structure usually reveals itself. Define V_step-IS^0 = 0 and, peeling from the front,

V_step-IS^{H+1-t} = ρ_t ( r_t + γ V_step-IS^{H-t} ).

Unfold it: at the outermost step it's ρ_1(r_1 + γ ρ_2(r_2 + γ(...))) = ρ_1 r_1 + γ ρ_1 ρ_2 r_2 + ... = Σ_t γ^{t-1} ρ_{1:t} r_t, which is exactly step-wise IS. The rewriting is trivial, but staring at the recursive form, something jumps out. At each step t I have a context s_t, an action a_t taken by the behavior policy, and a "reward" r_t + γ V_step-IS^{H-t} — the immediate reward plus the discounted continuation — that gets importance-weighted by the single-step ratio ρ_t. That is *a one-step bandit problem at every time step*. The context is s_t, the action is a_t, and the stochastic return r_t + γ·(future) has expected value, conditioned on (s_t,a_t), exactly the action-value Q(s_t, a_t). Step-wise IS is just doing single-step importance sampling on this per-step bandit, over and over down the trajectory.

And the instant I see it as a per-step bandit, I know what to do: apply the *bandit* doubly robust estimator at every step. The bandit version needed a reward model; here the per-step "reward" has mean Q(s_t,a_t), so I need an estimate Q̂(s_t,a_t) — fit from a separate dataset, by regression or by fitting an MDP and rolling out the Bellman recursion. The baseline I add back is V̂(s_t) = Σ_a π_1(a|s_t) Q̂(s_t,a), the model's value of s_t under the target policy — the analogue of ρ̂_{π(x)}(x). The correction importance-weights the per-step residual. So define V_DR^0 = 0 and

**V_DR^{H+1-t} = V̂(s_t) + ρ_t ( r_t + γ V_DR^{H-t} − Q̂(s_t, a_t) ),**

with the trajectory estimate V_DR = V_DR^H. Term by term this is the bandit estimator: V̂(s_t) is the baseline, ρ_t the per-step importance weight, and r_t + γ V_DR^{H-t} − Q̂(s_t,a_t) is the residual of the per-step return against the model — the part the model didn't predict, reweighted, recursively. And importantly, plug Q̂ ≡ 0 and it collapses back to V_step-IS, so step-wise IS is the special case with no model, exactly as IPS was the no-model special case in the bandit world.

Why is this unbiased? Same control-variate logic, one step at a time. The baseline V̂(s_t) is added with no weight, and I need to check that the importance-weighted Q̂ term doesn't bias it: E_{a~π_0}[ ρ_t Q̂(s_t,a) ] = Σ_a π_0(a|s_t) (π_1(a|s_t)/π_0(a|s_t)) Q̂(s_t,a) = Σ_a π_1(a|s_t) Q̂(s_t,a) = V̂(s_t), provided Q̂ doesn't depend on the sample — there's that independence requirement again. So the Q̂ part is a control variate with known mean V̂(s_t): subtracting ρ_t Q̂ in expectation cancels the V̂(s_t) I added, and what survives in expectation is ρ_t(r_t + γ V_DR^{H-t}), which by induction is unbiased for the value. Unbiased at every step ⇒ unbiased overall.

Now the variance, recursively, because I want to confirm the model actually buys me variance reduction here and to see *which* sources of randomness it can and can't touch. Let me compute the conditional variance V_t[V_DR^{H+1-t}] by induction down from t = H+1, where V_DR^0 = V(s_{H+1}) = 0 has zero variance. Inductive step: I expand V_t[V_DR^{H+1-t}] = E_t[(V_DR^{H+1-t})²] − (E_t[V(s_t)])². Add and subtract to write the inside as a sum of pieces whose cross terms I can kill. Inside the square, V̂(s_t) + ρ_t(r_t + γ V_DR^{H-t} − Q̂(s_t,a_t)); I split Q̂ = Q + Δ with Δ(s_t,a_t) = Q̂ − Q, and Q(s_t,a_t) = E[r_t + γ V(s_{t+1}) | s_t,a_t] = R(s_t,a_t) + γ E_{t+1}[V(s_{t+1})], to get

−ρ_t Δ(s_t,a_t) + V̂(s_t) + ρ_t( r_t − R(s_t,a_t) ) + ρ_t γ( V_DR^{H-t} − E_{t+1}[V(s_{t+1})] ).

The point of this regrouping: conditioned on (s_t,a_t), the term r_t − R(s_t,a_t) is zero-mean (reward noise), and V_DR^{H-t} − E_{t+1}[V(s_{t+1})] is zero-mean (the continuation is unbiased by induction), and these two are independent of each other and of the first two terms which are constants given (s_t,a_t). So the square of the sum equals the sum of squares in expectation — the cross terms vanish — and the conditional variance separates cleanly into

**V_t[V_DR^{H+1-t}] = V_t[V(s_t)] + E_t[ V_t[ ρ_t Δ(s_t,a_t) | s_t ] ] + E_t[ ρ_t² V_{t+1}[r_t] ] + E_t[ γ² ρ_t² V_{t+1}[V_DR^{H-t}] ].**

(The −ρ_tΔ + V̂(s_t) piece, conditioned on s_t with V̂(s_t) then a constant, contributes E_t[V_t[ρ_tΔ | s_t]], and V_t[V(s_t)] comes from the (E_t[V(s_t)])² subtraction.) Four terms, four sources of randomness. The first is the variance from the *state transition* into s_t. The second is the variance from *action stochasticity* in the behavior policy — and it is the only term that depends on the model, through Δ = Q̂ − Q. The third is *reward* randomness. The fourth recursively carries the variance of the future, weighted by ρ_t². So a good model shrinks Δ and squeezes the second term toward zero; step-wise IS is the special case Δ = −Q (because Q̂ ≡ 0), whose action term is Var_t[ρ_t Q(s_t,a_t) | s_t] instead of Var_t[ρ_t Δ(s_t,a_t) | s_t]. Exactly the bandit story — the action-weighting penalty is controlled by squared value-model error rather than squared value magnitude — now propagating down the trajectory.

This also tells me, soberingly, what doubly robust *cannot* fix. Even with a perfect model, Q̂ = Q, so Δ ≡ 0 and the second term is gone — but the first (transition) and third (reward) terms survive untouched, because they don't involve the model at all. If the MDP has substantial transition or reward stochasticity, the estimator still inherits that variance. The model only buys me the action-stochasticity variance.

Can I do better on the transition variance? It's worth a try. The transition variance entered because V_DR^{H-t} − E_{t+1}[V(s_{t+1})] is a noisy realization of the continuation around its mean. If I had a model of the transition dynamics P̂, I could use V̂(s_{t+1}) as a further control variate for that fluctuation. Concretely, modify the correction to also subtract a transition-importance-weighted value:

V_DR-v2^{H+1-t} = V̂(s_t) + ρ_t ( r_t + γ V_DR-v2^{H-t} − R̂(s_t,a_t) − γ V̂(s_{t+1}) · P̂(s_{t+1}|s_t,a_t)/P(s_{t+1}|s_t,a_t) ).

With the *true* P in that denominator this stays unbiased — same control-variate argument, the extra term has conditional mean γ Σ_{s'} P(s'|s_t,a_t) V̂(s') · (P̂/P) = γ Σ_{s'} P̂(s'|s_t,a_t) V̂(s'), which is what's needed to pair with the model reward R̂ and the model Bellman value — and it removes transition variance via a good P̂ and R̂. But it's useless as written: I don't *know* the true P to put in the denominator. The only escape is to assume P̂/P ≡ 1 — pretend my transition model is exact — which turns the last term into simply γ V̂(s_{t+1}). That's practical, and it reduces transition variance further, but it's no longer exactly unbiased; it buys variance with a little bias. How much bias? Let me bound it. Let β_t be the expected gap between V_DR-v2 with the approximation and the truly-unbiased V_DR-v2, conditioned on the history to step t; β_1 is the bias I want. Unrolling, β_t = E_t[ρ_t γ β_{t+1}] − E_t[ ρ_t γ V̂(s_{t+1})(P̂/P − 1) ]. The second expectation, integrating out s_{t+1}, becomes E_t[ ρ_t γ Σ_{s'} V̂(s')(P̂(s'|s_t,a_t) − P(s'|s_t,a_t)) ], whose magnitude is at most ρ_t γ V_max times the L1 transition-model gap for that state-action pair. With ε = max_{s,a} ‖P̂(·|s,a) − P(·|s,a)‖_1 and E[ρ_t | s_t] = 1, the recursion gives β_t ≤ γ(β_{t+1} + ε V_max), and with β_{H+1} = 0 this telescopes to **β_1 ≤ ε V_max Σ_{t=1}^H γ^t** — bias linear in the transition-model error ε, with no hidden 1/2 factor because ε is the L1 distance.

That leaves one nagging question. The plain doubly robust estimator can't reduce transition variance even with a perfect Q, and DR-v2 only does so by assuming the dynamics are essentially exact. Is that a failure of my estimator, or is the transition variance just *intrinsic* to the problem — a floor that no unbiased estimator can beat without extra assumptions? Let me try to find the floor, with a Cramér–Rao argument, in the cleanest setting: a discrete tree MDP, where the state is the full history h_t = o_1 a_1 ⋯ o_t, every trajectory is its own path, rewards come only at the end, and the whole MDP is parameterized by its transition probabilities θ_{hao} = P(o|h,a), subject to normalization Σ_o P(o|h,a) = 1. The Cramér–Rao bound for an unbiased estimator of v = v^{π_1,H} is K I^{-1} K^⊤ with the normalization constraints folded in — the constrained CRB is K U(U^⊤ I U)^{-1} U^⊤ K^⊤, where U spans the null space of the constraint Jacobian F and I is the Fisher information.

This is a real computation, so let me actually do it. The Fisher information: with P_0(h_{H+1}) = μ(o_1)π_0(a_1|o_1)P(o_2|o_1,a_1)⋯ the behavior-policy probability of a full history, ∂ log P_0/∂θ = θ^{∘-1} ∘ g, where g(h_{H+1}) is the indicator vector marking which (h,a,o) prefixes occur in the trajectory. So I = [θ_h^{-1}θ_{h'}^{-1}] ∘ E[g g^⊤]. The matrix E[g g^⊤] is zero off the prefix relation and equals P_0(hao) on the relevant entries, giving I_{(hao),(h'a'o')} = P_0(ha)/P(o'|h',a') when h'a'o' is a prefix of hao. This I is dense, so I can't invert U^⊤ I U directly. The trick: for any X, U^⊤ I U = U^⊤(F^⊤X^⊤ + I + XF)U because U ⟂ F, so I get to *choose* X to make D = F^⊤X^⊤ + I + XF diagonal. Setting X_{(h'a'o'),(ha)} = −P_0(ha)/P(o'|h',a') on the prefix entries cancels exactly the off-diagonal of I and leaves D diagonal with D_{(hao),(hao)} = P_0(ha)/P(o|h,a). Then choosing U block-diagonal over (ha)-blocks, each block of U(U^⊤ I U)^{-1}U^⊤ becomes (1/P_0(ha)) times the constrained CRB of a single multinomial P(·|h,a), which is the standard diag(p) − pp^⊤. The Jacobian of the value, K_{(hao)} = P_1(ha) V(hao) with P_1 the target-policy path probability. Assembling K U(U^⊤ I U)^{-1} U^⊤ K^⊤:

Σ_{ha} (P_1(ha)²/P_0(ha)) ( Σ_o P(o|h,a) V(hao)² − (Σ_o P(o|h,a) V(hao))² ) = Σ_{t} Σ_{|ha|=t} P_0(ha) (P_1(ha)/P_0(ha))² Var[ V(hao) | h,a ].

Now P_1(ha)/P_0(ha) is precisely the cumulative importance ratio ρ_{1:t}, and Σ_{|ha|=t} P_0(ha)(·) is an expectation over sample trajectories, so the bound collapses to

**Σ_{t=1}^{H+1} E[ ρ_{1:(t-1)}² · Var_t[V(s_t)] ].**

And now compare to my doubly robust variance with a *perfect* model, Q̂ = Q so Δ ≡ 0. Unfold the recursion: the second (action) term vanishes, the reward term V_{t+1}[r_t] vanishes for t < H in a tree MDP (rewards only at the end), and what's left, term by term, is exactly Σ_{t} E[ρ_{1:(t-1)}² Var_t[V(s_t)]] — the Cramér–Rao bound. So when no prior structural knowledge is available, doubly robust with a perfect value function is *statistically optimal*: it attains the minimum variance any unbiased estimator can have. The transition-stochasticity variance that doubly robust couldn't remove isn't a defect of the estimator — it's an information-theoretic floor of the off-policy evaluation problem itself. (The same argument goes through for layered DAG MDPs, where histories can re-merge into a shared state; the cumulative ratio ρ_{1:t-1} is just replaced by the state-action occupancy ratio P_1(s_{t-1},a_{t-1})/P_0(s_{t-1},a_{t-1}), and the problem is no harder.)

One last thing I glossed and need to nail down: the independence requirement. Every unbiasedness argument — bandit and sequential — used E[ρ Q̂] = V̂ or E[ρ ρ̂_a] = baseline, which silently assumed the model is *fixed* relative to the data being averaged. If I fit Q̂ (or ρ̂, or p̂) on the very same samples I then plug into the estimator, the weights and the model are correlated and the cancellation breaks — I'd reintroduce bias. So the model must be fit on data held out from the data the estimator averages over. In practice: split the data, fit the model on one part, run the estimator on the other; the target policy may even be computed from the same data used to fit the model, as long as the *evaluation* fold is independent of both. To avoid wasting the held-out data, I can rotate folds k-fold-cross-validation style — partition the evaluation set, for each part fit the model on the rest and apply the estimator on that part, then average; each part's estimate is unbiased so the average is too, and now every trajectory contributes to the final number.

Let me say the whole causal chain back to myself. I want a policy's value from off-policy logs. The model alone is low-variance but carries an un-auditable bias; importance sampling alone is unbiased but its variance explodes — multiplicatively over a horizon. They fail in complementary ways and I have both, so I combine them: use the model as a baseline and importance-weight only the model's *residual*. That single move does two things at once. The bias becomes the *product* of the model error and the propensity error, E[Δδ], so being right about *either* the model or the weights makes the estimator unbiased — doubly robust. And the variance penalty that wrecks importance sampling gets its reward magnitude ρ² replaced by the model error Δ², because the model is acting as a control variate whose target-policy mean is known — so a decent model slashes the variance. Writing step-wise importance sampling recursively exposes each timestep as a one-step bandit whose return has mean Q, so the same construction applies per step and gives a recursive sequential estimator; its variance separates into transition, action, reward, and future terms, of which only the action term the model can shrink. Pushing further, a transition control variate (DR-v2) trades a bias bounded by the dynamics-model error for reduced transition variance; and a Cramér–Rao calculation shows the leftover transition variance is the intrinsic floor, which doubly robust with a perfect model exactly attains.

Here is the bandit estimator in code, with the two existing approaches it subsumes:

```python
import numpy as np

def direct_method(q_hat, contexts, pi_target):
    # baseline only: average the model's prediction under the target policy
    return np.mean([np.dot(pi_target(x), q_hat(x)) for x in contexts])

def importance_sampling(rewards, actions, pscore, contexts, pi_target):
    # reweight the RAW reward by target/behavior probability -> unbiased, high variance
    return np.mean([r * pi_target(x)[a] / p
                    for r, a, p, x in zip(rewards, actions, pscore, contexts)])

def doubly_robust(rewards, actions, pscore, contexts, pi_target, q_hat, lam=np.inf):
    # baseline + importance-weighted RESIDUAL of the model
    est = []
    for r, a, p, x in zip(rewards, actions, pscore, contexts):
        pi_x = pi_target(x)
        q_hat_x = q_hat(x)
        iw = pi_x[a] / p
        if lam < np.inf:
            iw = min(iw, lam)                   # clipping trades bias for bounded weights
        baseline = np.dot(pi_x, q_hat_x)        # E_{pi_target}[q_hat]
        q_hat_factual = q_hat_x[a]
        correction = iw * (r - q_hat_factual)   # OBP form: baseline + iw * residual
        est.append(baseline + correction)
    # With lam == inf, bias = E[Delta * delta] and the variance penalty scales with Delta^2.
    return np.mean(est)
```

And the sequential estimator, the recursion built step by step, with step-wise importance sampling as its model-free special case:

```python
def step_wise_is(trajectory, gamma, pi_target, pi_behavior):
    # Q_hat == 0 special case of DR below, written out recursively
    v = 0.0
    for s, a, r in reversed(trajectory):           # V_step-IS^{H+1-t} = rho_t (r_t + gamma * V^{H-t})
        rho = pi_target(s)[a] / pi_behavior(s)[a]
        v = rho * (r + gamma * v)
    return v

def doubly_robust_sequential(trajectory, gamma, pi_target, pi_behavior, q_hat, v_hat):
    # v_hat(s) = sum_a pi_target(a|s) q_hat(s, a)  (the per-step baseline / control variate)
    # q_hat, v_hat must be fit on data INDEPENDENT of this trajectory (else bias)
    v = 0.0                                         # V_DR^0 = 0
    for s, a, r in reversed(trajectory):
        rho = pi_target(s)[a] / pi_behavior(s)[a]
        # V_DR^{H+1-t} = v_hat(s_t) + rho_t ( r_t + gamma V_DR^{H-t} - q_hat(s_t, a_t) )
        v = v_hat(s) + rho * (r + gamma * v - q_hat(s, a))
    # unbiased (E[rho * q_hat(s,a)] = v_hat(s) -> control variate cancels in mean)
    # variance separates into transition + action(only this shrinks with a good q_hat)
    #                         + reward + future;  Q_hat == 0  recovers step-wise IS
    return v
```
