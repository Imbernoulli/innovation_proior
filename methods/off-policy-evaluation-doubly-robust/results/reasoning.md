I have two families of estimators that fail in opposite ways, and I want to understand whether there is a single object that inherits the good behavior of each. So I start with the one-step off-policy problem, because whatever mechanism exists should be cleanest there before I worry about horizons.

The direct method predicts `q(x,a)` and averages the target-policy prediction. It is stable, but if the prediction is wrong on the action the target policy favors, the estimate inherits that model error directly with no correction. Importance sampling does the opposite. It keeps the observed reward and corrects the action distribution with `pi(a|x) / p(a|x)`, so it can be unbiased when the behavior propensity is right. But it reweights the raw reward, and small behavior probabilities create large random contributions.

The obvious thing to try is averaging the two estimates, maybe with a tuned weight. I do not think that helps, and I want to say why before I discard it: a convex combination `lambda * DM + (1-lambda) * IS` is unbiased only when one of the two pieces it averages is already unbiased, and it carries a `(1-lambda)^2` share of the IS variance no matter what. Blending cannot create a bias cancellation that was not in the ingredients; it just interpolates between two error profiles. So averaging is the wrong frame. I should look for a quantity that is *constructed* to cancel, not chosen to compromise.

That reframes the question. Instead of "which estimator do I trust," I ask "what is the smallest random quantity I can safely reweight?" Importance sampling reweights the whole reward `r`. But if the model is even moderately good, the residual `r - q_hat(x,a)` is smaller than the reward itself. Reweighting the residual rather than the raw reward should shrink the variance contributed by the weight. The residual alone has the wrong mean, so I add back the model's own target-policy mean `q_hat(x,pi) = sum_a pi(a|x) q_hat(x,a)`, which I can compute exactly because I control the model. The one-step contribution becomes

`q_hat(x,pi) + iw * (r - q_hat(x,a))`, with `iw = pi(a|x) / p_hat(a|x)`.

That is a guess shaped by intuition. Now I need to see what its bias actually is, because the whole point is whether the two error sources cancel or just sit side by side. Let me condition on `x`, fix the nuisance estimates independently of the evaluation sample, and take the expectation over the logged action `a ~ p(.|x)` and the reward with `E[r | x,a] = q(x,a)`.

The expected contribution is
`q_hat(x,pi) + sum_a p(a|x) * (pi(a|x) / p_hat(a|x)) * (q(x,a) - q_hat(x,a))`.

Write `Delta(a,x) = q_hat(x,a) - q(x,a)` for the model error and `delta(a,x) = 1 - p(a|x) / p_hat(a|x)` for the propensity error. I want to see whether the expression above minus the true value `V^pi(x) = sum_a pi(a|x) q(x,a)` reduces to something in the *product* `Delta * delta`. Pushing the algebra through, the `q_hat(x,pi)` baseline and the `(p/p_hat) q_hat` piece of the weighted term combine, the `(p/p_hat) q` piece supplies the importance-corrected true value, and the leftover should be `sum_a pi(a|x) Delta(a,x) delta(a,x)`. I am not fully sure I tracked every cross term correctly by hand, so I want to check it on numbers rather than declare it.

I take a single context with two actions: `q = (1.0, 2.0)`, behavior `p = (0.7, 0.3)`, target `pi = (0.25, 0.75)`, and deliberately wrong nuisances `q_hat = (1.5, 1.0)`, `p_hat = (0.5, 0.5)`. Computing the expected DR estimate directly from `q_hat(x,pi) + sum_a p[a] (pi[a]/p_hat[a]) (q[a] - q_hat[a])` gives `1.40`. The true target value is `sum_a pi[a] q[a] = 1.75`. So the actual bias is `1.40 - 1.75 = -0.35`. Then I evaluate the conjectured closed form `sum_a pi[a] Delta[a] delta[a]` with `Delta = q_hat - q = (0.5, -1.0)` and `delta = 1 - p/p_hat = (-0.4, 0.4)`, which gives `-0.35` as well. The two agree to floating-point. So the bias of this estimator really is `E[sum_a pi(a|x) Delta(a,x) delta(a,x)]`, and for a deterministic target policy it is just `E[Delta delta]`.

Now the structure is visible in a way it was not when I was thinking about averaging. The direct method has first-order bias in `Delta`. Importance sampling has first-order bias in the propensity error. This estimator has bias only in the *product* `Delta delta`. So if the reward model is right everywhere the target acts, `Delta = 0` and the bias vanishes; if the propensities are right, `delta = 0` and it vanishes. To make sure I am reading the identity correctly and not fooling myself, I set `q_hat = q` in the same numeric setup and recompute: the bias comes out `0.0`. Then I set `p_hat = p` instead and recompute: again `0.0`. Either correct nuisance kills the bias. That is the property I was hoping a constructed cancellation might give, and now I have actually watched it happen rather than asserted it.

The same residual structure is what controls the variance. Importance sampling multiplies the high-variance weight by the whole reward. Here the weight multiplies only `r - q_hat(x,a)`. The Dudik-Langford-Li variance expression makes the consequence precise: the dangerous factor containing `(1-p)/p` is multiplied by `Delta^2`, the squared model error, rather than by the squared reward or target-action value as in plain IPS. So a useful model reduces variance even when it is imperfect, because it shrinks the object being reweighted. This is the augmented-inverse-probability / control-variate structure from the missing-data literature, transcribed to policy value: the weighted factual model prediction and the model's target-policy mean have the same expectation when the propensity is correct, so the model term acts as a control variate. The estimator subtracts a correlated quantity from the noisy IS term and adds back its known mean; it does not blend two answers.

Now I want to carry this to a finite-horizon MDP, and the question is whether the one-step trick has a sequential analog or whether the horizon breaks it. Step-wise importance sampling can be written recursively as `V_step^{H+1-t} = rho_t (r_t + gamma V_step^{H-t})`, with `rho_t = pi_1(a_t|s_t) / pi_0(a_t|s_t)`. Read at a single time `t`, this *is* a one-step bandit: the context is `s_t`, the action is `a_t`, and the stochastic outcome is the immediate reward plus the discounted future estimate, whose conditional mean is `Q(s_t,a_t)`. So the one-step residual correction should drop in at every step. With a fitted `Q_hat` and `V_hat(s) = sum_a pi_1(a|s) Q_hat(s,a)`,

`V_DR^0 = 0`, `V_DR^{H+1-t} = V_hat(s_t) + rho_t * (r_t + gamma * V_DR^{H-t} - Q_hat(s_t,a_t))`.

Before trusting the recursion I check the boundary that has to hold by construction: setting `Q_hat = 0` (so `V_hat = 0`) should recover step-wise IS exactly. I trace both on the trajectory `[(s=0,a=1,r=1), (s=1,a=0,r=2)]` with `gamma = 0.9` and the policies `pi_t = {0:(.2,.8),1:(.6,.4)}`, `pi_b = {0:(.5,.5),1:(.3,.7)}`. Step-wise IS returns `7.36`; the DR recursion with `Q_hat = 0` returns `7.36` (agreeing to floating point). So the special case is right, and `Q_hat` only ever moves the estimate off this baseline by a residual.

The unbiasedness argument is then the one-step cancellation repeated by induction. Conditional on `s_t`, `E_{a_t ~ pi_0}[rho_t Q_hat(s_t,a_t)] = sum_a pi_0(a|s_t) (pi_1/pi_0) Q_hat = V_hat(s_t)`, so the added model term has zero net effect on the expectation; the weighted reward-plus-future term transports the behavior sample to the target distribution; and the future term is unbiased by the induction hypothesis. I would still like to see this hold end to end rather than only term by term, so I build a tiny deterministic-transition two-step MDP (action selects the next state, fixed rewards), where I can compute the true target value by exhaustive enumeration as `V_true = 2.672`. Rolling out 200k trajectories under the behavior policy and averaging the DR estimator: with `Q_hat = 0` the mean is `2.671`, and with `Q_hat = Q*` (the true target Q) the mean is `2.672`. Both land on the true value, so the model choice does not move the expectation — only the variance.

And the variance is where the model earns its place. The Jiang-Li decomposition splits the sequential variance into a transition term, an action-stochasticity term `E[Var(rho_t Delta(s_t,a_t) | s_t)]`, a reward-noise term, and a discounted future-variance term, with `Delta(s,a) = Q_hat(s,a) - Q(s,a)`. Only the action term carries `Delta`. So a better value model should shrink that one term and leave transition and reward noise untouched — it should not be able to drive variance to zero in general. In the same 200k-trajectory experiment the empirical variance of the `Q_hat = 0` estimator is `6.88`, and with the perfect `Q_hat = Q*` it drops to `0.17`, a 40x reduction. The residual `0.17` is finite-sample noise: this MDP has deterministic transitions and rewards, so the action term is the *only* reducible variance and the perfect model removes it, leaving essentially nothing here, while in a stochastic MDP the transition and reward terms would remain as a hard floor. That is exactly the behavior the decomposition predicts, and it stops me from overclaiming: a perfect value model removes the reducible action-mismatch variance and nothing more. Jiang and Li's lower-bound argument identifies what is left as intrinsic — in tree MDPs the perfect-model estimator reaches the Cramer-Rao bound for unbiased off-policy estimators.

There is one practical condition I cannot skip, and it is exactly the assumption I leaned on in the bias computation: the nuisance estimates must be independent of the evaluation fold. The identities `E[rho q_hat] = q_hat(pi)` and `E[rho Q_hat] = V_hat` treat the model as fixed while taking the expectation over the logged action. If I fit the model on the same sample I then evaluate, the model becomes correlated with the action and that conditional cancellation can fail, reintroducing first-order bias through the back door. Sample splitting or cross-fitting restores the independence while still using all the data across folds.

So what I arrive at is a residual-corrected importance estimator, not a blend. Direct modeling supplies a baseline whose target-policy mean I can compute; importance weighting corrects only the residual the baseline missed. The numerically confirmed product-bias identity `E[Delta delta]` is the reason one correct nuisance suffices, the control-variate reading explains why the variance penalty scales with model error rather than reward magnitude, and in sequential RL the whole thing recurs because each time step is a bandit whose outcome is reward plus discounted future return.
