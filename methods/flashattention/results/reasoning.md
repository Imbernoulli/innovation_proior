OK, let me think this through from scratch. The thing everyone complains about with Transformers is that attention is quadratic in the sequence length, and the instinctive reading of "quadratic" is "too many FLOPs." So the whole field has been chasing FLOP reduction: make the score matrix sparse so you only compute a fraction of its entries, or approximate the softmax with a low-rank factorization so the cost becomes linear. And yet — this is the thing that nags at me — a lot of these clever linear-attention methods, when you actually time them on a GPU, are not faster than plain dense attention. Some are slower. They reduce the FLOPs and the wall clock doesn't move. That's the surprise: if "quadratic FLOPs" were really the problem, cutting the arithmetic should cut the time, and it doesn't. So the time was never being spent on arithmetic in the first place.

So where is it being spent? Let me look at what attention actually does, step by step, as a sequence of GPU kernels. Inputs Q, K, V are each N×d, living in HBM, the big slow off-chip memory. Step one, S = QK^T: read Q and K from HBM, multiply, write S — and S is N×N — back to HBM. Step two, P = softmax(S): read the whole N×N S back from HBM, exponentiate and normalize row-wise, write the N×N P back to HBM. Step three, O = PV: read P and V from HBM, multiply, write O. Stare at this for a second. We wrote an N×N matrix to HBM, read it back, wrote another N×N matrix, read it back. For N=1K that's a million entries per matrix, round-tripped. The matmuls QK^T and PV have decent arithmetic intensity, but the softmax in the middle is a pure elementwise-plus-reduction over N² elements: almost no arithmetic per byte, it just reads and writes the giant matrix. That step is memory-bound. And masking and dropout, when present, are more elementwise passes over the same N×N matrix — more memory-bound traffic.

Now the hardware fact that turns this from an annoyance into the whole story: on a modern GPU, HBM is huge but slow — order 1.5–2 TB/s — and there's a tiny on-chip SRAM that's about ten times faster in bandwidth but only ~100 KB per multiprocessor. And crucially, over GPU generations compute has been getting faster relative to memory bandwidth. So a memory-bound op like the attention softmax is bottlenecked by the rate at which it can drag the N×N matrix across the HBM bus. That's the time. The FLOP-reduction methods don't touch this — they still materialize matrices in HBM, sometimes with messier access patterns, so the dominant cost is untouched. What I've been missing is the right thing to count: not FLOPs, but the reads and writes between HBM and SRAM. There's an old framework for exactly this, the external-memory / IO-complexity model — you count transfers between a small fast memory and a large slow one. Nobody's been applying it to attention. Let me adopt it as the objective and see where it leads. The thing to minimize is HBM accesses.

Let me get a number to anchor on. Standard attention's HBM traffic: QK^T reads Q,K (that's Θ(Nd)) and writes S (Θ(N²)); softmax reads S and writes P (Θ(N²)); PV reads P,V and writes O (Θ(N²) + Θ(Nd)). Total Θ(Nd + N²). The N² term is the killer and it's all from the intermediate matrices. So the goal sharpens into a concrete demand: *never write the N×N attention matrix to HBM, and never read it back.* Compute O = softmax(QK^T)V while keeping the big matrix on-chip, or better, never forming the whole thing at all.

The obvious move is kernel fusion — load Q, K, V once, do the matmul, the softmax, the second matmul all on-chip, write only O. That would collapse all those round-trips. But there are two walls. First wall: the softmax in the middle. Softmax over a row needs the whole row at once — it needs the max over the row for numerical stability and the sum over the row for the denominator. The row of S has length N. If N is large the row of S — let alone the whole matrix — doesn't fit in the little SRAM. So I can't just "load it all and fuse"; the reduction seems to demand the entire row be present. Second wall: this is for training, and the backward pass needs S and P to compute gradients. The standard fused-kernel trick still has to spill the intermediates to HBM precisely so the backward can read them — which puts the N² traffic right back. So naive fusion doesn't save training. Two challenges, then: (i) do the softmax reduction without ever holding a whole row; (ii) get through the backward pass without storing the N×N matrix.

Take the first wall. The softmax couples all the columns of a row through the max and the sum — that coupling is what seems to force materializing the whole row. Is there a way to compute a softmax incrementally, seeing the entries a chunk at a time, and still get the exact answer? Let me think about what a numerically-stable softmax even is. For a vector x, you take m = max_i x_i, form f_i = exp(x_i − m), let ℓ = Σ_i f_i, and softmax = f/ℓ. The subtraction of m is just for stability; mathematically softmax_i = e^{x_i}/Σ_j e^{x_j}. Now suppose I've only seen the first chunk x^{(1)} and computed its local max m^{(1)} and local sum ℓ^{(1)} = Σ exp(x^{(1)} − m^{(1)}). Then a second chunk x^{(2)} arrives with m^{(2)}, ℓ^{(2)}. The true max of the concatenation is m = max(m^{(1)}, m^{(2)}). But my ℓ^{(1)} was computed by subtracting the *old* max m^{(1)}, not the new global m. I can fix it: every term in ℓ^{(1)} was e^{x − m^{(1)}}; to re-base it to the new max I multiply by e^{m^{(1)} − m}, because e^{x − m} = e^{x − m^{(1)}} · e^{m^{(1)} − m}. So the corrected running sum is

  ℓ = e^{m^{(1)} − m} ℓ^{(1)} + e^{m^{(2)} − m} ℓ^{(2)}.

So if I carry just two extra scalars per row — the running max m and the running sum ℓ — I can fold in each new chunk by rescaling what I already have with e^{old_m − new_m} and adding the new chunk's contribution rescaled to the new max. The rescale factor e^{old_m − new_m} repairs the partial sum whenever the running max jumps. The coupling is broken: I never need the whole row, I need two running scalars. This is the single-pass-normalizer trick from online softmax, and it's exactly the lever I need against the first wall.

But wait — I don't actually want the softmax weights for their own sake. I want O = PV, the weighted sum of value vectors. So the running statistics had better let me build O incrementally too, not just the denominator. Let me carry, per query row i, an output accumulator o_i ∈ ℝ^d alongside (m_i, ℓ_i). When a new block of keys/values arrives, I compute its scores, its local max, its unnormalized exponentials, and the partial weighted sum of *that block's* values. The subtlety: the o_i I've accumulated so far was divided by the old ℓ_i and exponentiated relative to the old max m_i. When the max and the denominator both change, I have to re-scale the existing o_i the same way I re-scaled ℓ_i. So: un-normalize the old accumulator by multiplying back by the old ℓ_i, rescale it by e^{old_m − new_m} to move it onto the new max, add the new block's contribution (its exponentials, on the new max, times its V block), and finally re-normalize by dividing by the new ℓ_i. In symbols, with the new block giving local max m̃, local exponentials P̃ = exp(S̃ − m̃), and local sum ℓ̃:

  m^new = max(m, m̃),
  ℓ^new = e^{m − m^new} ℓ + e^{m̃ − m^new} ℓ̃,
  o^new = (1/ℓ^new) ( ℓ · e^{m − m^new} · o + e^{m̃ − m^new} · P̃ V_block ).

That last line is doing the real work: it says I can walk over the key/value blocks one at a time, never holding more than one block plus the running (m_i, ℓ_i, o_i), and — if it's right — at the end o_i is the attention output for row i, with no N×N matrix anywhere. It's the online-softmax recurrence promoted from a scalar denominator to a vector output. But I derived it by analogy, by "doing to o what I did to ℓ," and analogies are where I make sign and rescaling mistakes. Before I build a whole tiled kernel on top of it I want to actually run it on numbers and see the accumulator land on the dense softmax output, because if the rescaling of o is off by even a factor the whole method is wrong and the error would be invisible in the algebra.

Let me take a tiny case I can also do the slow way. N=3 keys, d=2, two query rows, scale 1. Pick Q = [[1,0],[0,1]], K = [[1,0],[0,2],[1,1]], V = [[1,0],[0,1],[1,1]]. The dense path: S = QK^T = [[1,0,1],[0,2,1]], softmax row 0 = (e¹,e⁰,e¹)/(e¹+e⁰+e¹) = (0.4223, 0.1554, 0.4223), so O row 0 = 0.4223·[1,0] + 0.1554·[0,1] + 0.4223·[1,1] = [0.8446, 0.5777]. Now run the recurrence on row 0 with block size 1, so every key is a separate block and the running max actually moves:

- key 0: s = q·k₀ = 1. m goes −∞→1, p = e⁰ = 1, acc = 0·e^{−∞−1} + 1·[1,0] = [1,0], L = 1·1 = e¹ ≈ 2.718.
- key 1: s = 0. m stays 1 (max(1,0)), p = e^{0−1} = 0.3679, acc = [1,0]·e^{1−1} + 0.3679·[0,1] = [1, 0.3679], L = 2.718 + 0.3679·... — tracking it in the lse form, L = 3.718.
- key 2: s = 1. m stays 1, p = e^{1−1} = 1, acc = [1,0.3679]·1 + 1·[1,1] = [2, 1.3679], L = 6.437.
- finalize: O row 0 = acc / L = [2, 1.3679] / e^{lse}... = [2/2.368, 1.3679/2.368] = [0.8446, 0.5777].

That matches [0.8446, 0.5777] exactly. And running the full thing in code over block sizes 1, 2, 3 against the dense softmax, the max elementwise discrepancy is 2.2e-16 — machine epsilon — for every block size. So the accumulator-rescale recurrence is not just plausible by analogy, it reproduces the dense output to floating-point. Good; the o-rescaling is correct as written.

Now make it concrete as a tiled algorithm. The SRAM has size M. I'll split K and V into column blocks of B_c rows each, and Q (and the output O and the stats) into row blocks of B_r rows each. The natural loop: outer loop over the K,V blocks j (load K_j, V_j into SRAM once), inner loop over the Q blocks i (load Q_i, O_i, ℓ_i, m_i). On chip compute S_ij = Q_i K_j^T, of size B_r×B_c — small, fits in SRAM. Row-max it to get m̃_ij, exponentiate to P̃_ij = exp(S_ij − m̃_ij), row-sum to ℓ̃_ij. Then apply the recurrence above to update m_i, ℓ_i, and O_i, and write the updated O_i, ℓ_i, m_i back. Initialize O = 0, ℓ = 0, m = −∞ so the first block just sets everything. Because the score block is only B_r×B_c and we never store a full row or matrix, the whole loop body is on-chip; the only HBM traffic is loading Q,K,V blocks and reading/writing O and the tiny stats. And because everything is now block-local, the matmul, the softmax, the masking, the dropout, the second matmul all live in one fused kernel — read inputs from HBM once, do all the work, write O once.

The tiny example checked specific numbers, but I'd like to know *why* it always lands exactly, for any block sizes and any data, before I trust it on N=64K. Let me prove it by induction on the outer-loop index j. Claim: after processing the first j blocks of K,V (call them K_{:j}, V_{:j}, the first jB_c columns/rows), the stored state is exactly the softmax statistics restricted to those columns:

  m^{(j)} = rowmax(S_{:, :j}),
  ℓ^{(j)} = rowsum(exp(S_{:, :j} − m^{(j)})),
  O^{(j)} = P_{:, :j} V_{:j} = softmax(S_{:, :j}) V_{:j},

where S_{:, :j} = Q K_{:j}^T. For j=0 it's the initialization: max −∞, sum 0, output 0 — vacuously correct. Now assume it for j and process block j+1, whose new column slab has row-max m̃ and ℓ̃ = rowsum(exp(S_{:, j:j+1} − m̃)). The max update gives m^{(j+1)} = max(m^{(j)}, m̃) = rowmax(S_{:, :j+1}), clearly. The sum update is the rescaled aggregation ℓ^{(j+1)} = e^{m^{(j)} − m^{(j+1)}} ℓ^{(j)} + e^{m̃ − m^{(j+1)}} ℓ̃, and by the same re-basing identity I derived above this equals rowsum(exp(S_{:, :j+1} − m^{(j+1)})). The interesting one is O. Start from the update line:

  O^{(j+1)} = diag(ℓ^{(j+1)})^{-1} ( diag(ℓ^{(j)}) e^{m^{(j)} − m^{(j+1)}} O^{(j)} + e^{m̃ − m^{(j+1)}} exp(S_{j:j+1} − m̃) V_{j:j+1} ).

Substitute the inductive hypothesis O^{(j)} = diag(ℓ^{(j)})^{-1} exp(S_{:, :j} − m^{(j)}) V_{:j}. The diag(ℓ^{(j)}) out front cancels the diag(ℓ^{(j)})^{-1} inside O^{(j)}. The first term becomes e^{m^{(j)} − m^{(j+1)}} exp(S_{:, :j} − m^{(j)}) V_{:j} = exp(S_{:, :j} − m^{(j+1)}) V_{:j}, the two exponentials combining because e^{m^{(j)} − m^{(j+1)}} · e^{−m^{(j)}} = e^{−m^{(j+1)}}. The second term's e^{m̃ − m^{(j+1)}} · exp(S_{j:j+1} − m̃) = exp(S_{j:j+1} − m^{(j+1)}) V_{j:j+1}, the m̃'s cancelling the same way. So

  O^{(j+1)} = diag(ℓ^{(j+1)})^{-1} ( exp(S_{:, :j} − m^{(j+1)}) V_{:j} + exp(S_{j:j+1} − m^{(j+1)}) V_{j:j+1} ).

The two column slabs glue back into the full first-(j+1) range: that's diag(ℓ^{(j+1)})^{-1} exp(S_{:, :j+1} − m^{(j+1)}) V_{:j+1} = softmax(S_{:, :j+1}) V_{:j+1}. Exactly the claim for j+1. So at j = T_c (all blocks processed) the stored O is softmax(QK^T)V — exact, not approximate, which is what the numeric check was already showing for the small case. And the cost: each inner step does two matmuls of size B_r×B_c×d, i.e. O(B_r B_c d) FLOPs, executed T_c T_r ≈ N²/(B_c B_r) times, totalling O(N²d) — same arithmetic order as standard attention. The only extra memory beyond inputs and output is the O(N) of statistics (m, ℓ). So: linear extra memory, exact result, all on-chip.

Now the second wall: the backward pass. Naively, gradients need S and P, the N×N matrices. If I store them I'm back to O(N²) memory and HBM traffic; if I don't store them I seem stuck. There's a known escape — gradient checkpointing: don't store the big intermediate, recompute it in the backward. The memory-efficient-attention line of work has already suggested doing this for attention to cap the footprint, by checkpointing the forward and letting autodiff recompute. But generic checkpointing has a reputation: it trades speed for memory — you pay extra FLOPs to recompute and you eat that as slower wall-clock. Let me think about whether that reputation holds *here*. In an IO-bound regime the cost I care about is accesses, not FLOPs, and the recompute happens on-chip in fast SRAM and saves the HBM round-trips of the N×N matrix that storing-and-reloading would cost. The extra FLOPs are cheap; the avoided HBM accesses are expensive. So in this regime recomputation might pay for itself or better. I'll hold that as a conjecture and check it once I've actually counted the backward's accesses — I don't want to assert it before the count is in. And to make the backward tight I don't want to lean on a generic autodiff-checkpoint of the forward — that would recompute too much and re-store per-block temporaries. Let me derive the backward by hand and see exactly the minimal thing I need to recompute and the minimal thing I need to save.

Set up: scalar loss φ, output gradient dO = ∂φ/∂O, want dQ, dK, dV. Let the raw dot-product score be R_{ij} = q_i·k_j and the score fed to softmax be S_{ij} = τR_{ij}. Write L_i = Σ_j e^{S_{ij}} for the (unshifted) normalizer, so o_i = Σ_j (e^{S_{ij}}/L_i) v_j and P_{ij} = e^{S_{ij}}/L_i. The easy one first: O = PV, so dV = P^T dO, i.e. dv_j = Σ_i P_{ij} do_i = Σ_i (e^{S_{ij}}/L_i) do_i. If I've kept L_i (or equivalently m and ℓ) I can recompute P_{ij} on the fly from q_i, k_j and accumulate dv_j with no extra storage. Next, dP = dO V^T, so dP_{ij} = do_i · v_j. Now the softmax derivative: each row P_{i:} = softmax(S_{i:}), and the Jacobian of y = softmax(x) is diag(y) − y y^T. So

  dS_{i:} = (diag(P_{i:}) − P_{i:} P_{i:}^T) dP_{i:} = P_{i:} ∘ dP_{i:} − (P_{i:}^T dP_{i:}) P_{i:},

with ∘ elementwise. The scalar P_{i:}^T dP_{i:} is a reduction over a length-N row — and that's the thing that, taken literally, would force me to hold a whole row of P and dP in SRAM, which doesn't fit. So compute it differently. Call it D_i := P_{i:}^T dP_{i:} = Σ_j P_{ij} dP_{ij} = Σ_j (e^{S_{ij}}/L_i) (do_i · v_j). Pull do_i out of the sum: = do_i · ( Σ_j (e^{S_{ij}}/L_i) v_j ) = do_i · o_i. The whole row-reduction collapses to a single dot product of two length-d vectors, do_i and o_i — and o_i is exactly the output I already have. If that identity is right it's the move that makes the backward fit on-chip: D_i computed per row from size-d vectors, never touching a length-N row. With D_i in hand, dS_{ij} = P_{ij}(dP_{ij} − D_i), where this dS is the gradient with respect to the scaled score. Since S_{ij} = τq_i·k_j,

  dq_i = τΣ_j dS_{ij} k_j = τΣ_j P_{ij}(dP_{ij} − D_i) k_j = τΣ_j (e^{S_{ij}}/L_i)(do_i·v_j − D_i) k_j,
  dk_j = τΣ_i dS_{ij} q_i = τΣ_i P_{ij}(dP_{ij} − D_i) q_i = τΣ_i (e^{S_{ij}}/L_i)(do_i·v_j − D_i) q_i.

This whole derivation is a chain of substitutions where I could easily have a sign wrong, a τ in the wrong place, or a transpose flipped — exactly the kind of thing that produces gradients that look reasonable and are quietly wrong. So before I commit it, I'll check the gradients numerically against finite differences. Take N=4, d=3, random Q,K,V, scale 1/√d, and a fixed loss φ = Σ(O ∘ G) for a random G so that dO = G. Compute dV = P^T dO, dS = P ∘ (dP − D), with D from the identity, and dQ = dS K τ, dK = dS^T Q τ. Then perturb each entry of Q, K, V by ±1e-6 and form the central-difference gradient of φ. The results: the max elementwise gap is 1.4e-10 for dQ, 2.3e-10 for dK, 1.1e-10 for dV — all at the level you'd expect from a 1e-6 finite-difference step, so the hand-derived backward agrees with the true gradient. And the identity itself: computing D_i both ways, as do_i·o_i and as the literal rowsum(P_{i:} ∘ dP_{i:}), the two agree to 1.7e-16. So D_i = do_i·o_i is exact, not approximately exact — the collapse from a length-N reduction to a length-d dot product is real. Every one of dV, dQ, dK is now a sum I can accumulate block by block, recomputing the needed P_{ij} on-chip from the saved (m, ℓ) and the inputs, and computing D_i as do_i·o_i. So the backward, like the forward, is O(N²) FLOPs and O(N) extra memory, and it never reads an N×N matrix from HBM. I save from the forward only O, the stats (m, ℓ), and, if dropout is on, the random-number-generator state (so I can regenerate the same dropout mask in the backward instead of storing the N×N mask — storing it would be O(N²), regenerating it is free in memory). This is tighter than checkpointing the forward generically: I recompute only the attention block S, P that I need, I don't recompute or re-store per-block temporary outputs, and I keep a single running copy of things.

This also sharpens a contrast with the prior linear-memory approach. That one also used online softmax and also recomputed in the backward, but it was aiming at the *footprint* — the peak bytes — and it summarized each block by a separate temporary output, then combined all the block outputs at the end, which means holding one output per block; and its backward leaned on generic gradient checkpointing. By instead incrementally updating one running O_i (the rescale-and-add recurrence above) I keep a single output copy, and by deriving the backward analytically I recompute only S and P. The deeper difference is the objective: minimizing HBM accesses, not bytes. And note minimizing accesses also bounds the footprint — if an algorithm makes A accesses its memory is at most A — so I get the footprint win for free, but the access count is what controls the wall clock.

So let me actually quantify the HBM accesses, both to confirm the win is real and to settle the backward-recomputation conjecture I parked above. Trace the forward kernel's traffic. Each element of K and V is loaded exactly once — that's the outer loop, Θ(Nd). The inner loop makes one pass over Q and O for each outer block, so T_c passes over Q and O, each pass moving Θ(Nd). Total HBM accesses ≈ Θ(Nd + Nd·T_c) = Θ(Nd·T_c). Now T_c = N/B_c, and B_c is set by how much fits in SRAM of size M. The constraints: K_j and V_j blocks are B_c×d, need B_c d = O(M), so B_c = O(M/d); Q_i, O_i are B_r×d, need B_r = O(M/d); and the score block S_ij is B_r×B_c, need B_r B_c = O(M). Push B_c as large as SRAM allows, B_c = Θ(M/d). Then T_c = N/B_c = Θ(Nd/M), and the total HBM accesses are

  Θ(Nd · Nd/M) = Θ(N² d² / M).

Compare to standard attention's Θ(Nd + N²). The ratio of the leading terms is (N² d²/M) / N² = d²/M. Let me put real numbers in rather than wave at "d² ≪ M." Head dim d is 64–128. SRAM is ~100 KB; counting it in fp32 elements that's about 100·1024/4 ≈ 25,600 elements. So d²/M is 64²/25600 ≈ 0.16 at d=64, and 128²/25600 ≈ 0.64 at d=128 — i.e. roughly a 6× reduction in leading-term traffic at d=64, but only about 1.6× at d=128. So the win is genuine but it is *not* uniformly "many-fold"; it shrinks as d grows toward √M, and at d ≈ √M ≈ 160 the asymptotic advantage in the leading term washes out entirely. That's a useful caution: the method helps most at the moderate head dims that are actually used, and the reported speedups should be read with the d-dependence in mind, not as an unconditional constant factor. For the d=64–128 regime that dominates practice it is a real reduction, and since attention is memory-bound that traffic reduction is the wall-clock speedup. Now the parked conjecture: the backward's traffic is the same Θ(N²d²/M) by the identical argument (each K,V element loaded once; T_c passes over Q, O, dO and over dQ). So recomputation in the backward costs more FLOPs but the *access* count is the same order as a hypothetical store-and-reload would pay just to move the N×N matrix — and since the clock follows accesses, not FLOPs, the recomputation is indeed not a net cost here. The conjecture holds, but only because the count came out this way, not because I assumed it.

Is this access count optimal, or could a cleverer schedule do asymptotically better? Let me see how low it could possibly go. Suppose some exact-attention algorithm achieved o(N²d²/M) accesses *for all* M in the meaningful range [d, Nd]. Look at the top of that range, M = Θ(Nd) — SRAM large enough to hold a constant number of the input matrices. There o(N²d²/M) = o(N²d²/(Nd)) = o(Nd). But Q, K, V and the output O each have Nd entries and they live in HBM to begin with and must end there; any algorithm that even reads its inputs and writes its output incurs Ω(Nd) accesses. So o(Nd) is impossible — contradiction. Hence no exact attention algorithm can beat Θ(N²d²/M) across all M; the tiling is asymptotically optimal in HBM accesses over the SRAM range, at least via this subrange argument. (A full M-parameterized lower bound would be nicer, but the subrange contradiction is the standard external-memory style and it pins optimality at M = Θ(Nd).)

A couple of constants I glossed need nailing down, since they decide whether the kernel actually fits. The block sizes I'll set to B_c = ⌈M/(4d)⌉ and B_r = min(⌈M/(4d)⌉, d). The 1/4 is a budget split: the kernel has to hold several d-wide blocks resident at once — a Q block, a K block, a V block, and the running O block — so I divide the M-element SRAM budget by about four to leave room for all of them (plus the small score tile and stats). Capping B_r at d is what enforces the third constraint B_r B_c = O(M): with B_c ≈ M/d, the product B_r B_c ≈ M·(B_r/d), so I need B_r ≤ d to keep that O(M). Asymptotically this is exactly B_c = Θ(M/d), B_r = Θ(min(M/d, d)), the same values the IO-complexity bound assumed.

One more pass to fold in the real-kernel niceties. The softmax scaling τ (typically 1/√d) just multiplies S_ij before everything; masking sets masked entries of S_ij to −∞ before the row-max (so they contribute zero weight); dropout multiplies P̃_ij elementwise by a mask whose entries are 1/(1−p) or 0, applied to the probabilities before the V multiply, and reproduced in the backward from the saved RNG state. And there's a tidy bookkeeping simplification real implementations use: during the forward kernel I still keep the temporary running max m_i because the accumulator is based at that max, but the statistic I need to save for the backward is only lse_i = m_i + log ℓ_i. Each block uses m_new = max(m_i, m_blk), folds the denominator as lse_i ← m_new + log(e^{lse_i − m_new} + ℓ_blk), and at the very end normalizes the accumulator once by o_scale = exp(m_i − lse_i). The backward reconstructs P_{ij} directly as exp(S_ij·τ − lse_i). This is the same (m, ℓ) recurrence I verified numerically above, just stored as the one scalar lse that's convenient to recompute from — the code below carries the lse form, and it's that form whose output I checked against the dense softmax to 2e-16.

And the extension that falls out almost for free: if I only care about a block-sparse attention pattern — a 0/1 mask over blocks of the score matrix — then in the loops I simply skip the blocks the mask zeroes out. Same kernel, an `if` around the inner body. The HBM accesses scale by s, the fraction of nonzero blocks, giving Θ(Nd + N²d²s/M) — the Nd term survives because I still have to write the whole output O — so for s = N^{−1/2} this is Θ(N√N) and for s = N^{−1}log N it's Θ(N log N) accesses. A natural pattern to use is a butterfly sparsity, since butterfly factorizations can express arbitrary structured patterns with near-optimal cost and are hardware-friendly. But the core is the dense exact algorithm; block-sparsity is just skipping iterations.

Let me write the forward as the kernel I'd actually run, mirroring the structure of a real fused implementation, with the running statistics and the rescale-and-add accumulation as the body — and folding (m, ℓ) into the log-sum-exp form since that's what the kernel stores.

```python
# FlashAttention forward, one query-block program. Streams over K/V blocks,
# keeps per-row running max m_i, running log-sum-exp lse_i, and output
# accumulator acc_o; rescales the accumulator each block; normalizes once at end.
def flash_attn_fwd_block(q, K, V, scale, BLOCK_N, is_causal=False):
    # q: (B_r, d) for this query block, already in SRAM.
    m_i   = full((q.shape[0],), -inf)     # running row max
    lse_i = full((q.shape[0],), -inf)     # running log-sum-exp
    acc_o = zeros((q.shape[0], q.shape[1]))  # running output accumulator

    for k_blk, v_blk in iter_blocks(K, V, BLOCK_N):     # load each K,V block once
        s = (q @ k_blk.T) * scale                       # S_ij block, on chip, B_r x B_c
        s = apply_mask(s, is_causal)                    # set masked entries to -inf

        m_ij  = maximum(m_i, rowmax(s))                 # tentative new max
        p     = exp(s - m_ij[:, None])                  # P~_ij = exp(S_ij - m), on chip
        l_ij  = rowsum(p)                               # this block's denominator piece

        acc_o = acc_o * exp(m_i - m_ij)[:, None]        # rescale old accumulator to new max
        acc_o = acc_o + p @ v_blk                       # add this block's P~ V contribution

        m_i   = m_ij                                    # advance running max
        lse_i = m_ij + log(exp(lse_i - m_ij) + l_ij)    # fold block into running log-sum-exp

    o = acc_o * exp(m_i - lse_i)[:, None]               # single final normalization by 1/L_i
    return o, lse_i                                     # save lse_i (O(N) stat) for backward
```

And the backward, as the hand-derived block recurrence — recompute P from the saved lse, get D_i as the do·o dot product, accumulate dV, dQ, dK:

```python
# FlashAttention backward. Recomputes P on-chip from lse; D_i = rowsum(O .* dO).
def flash_attn_bwd_block(q, K, V, o, do, lse, scale, BLOCK_N):
    D_i = rowsum(o * do)                                # D_i = do_i . o_i  (size-d dot, per row)
    dq  = zeros_like(q)
    for k_blk, v_blk, dk_blk, dv_blk in iter_blocks_bwd(K, V, BLOCK_N):
        s  = (q @ k_blk.T) * scale
        p  = exp(s - lse[:, None])                      # recomputed P_ij, no N x N stored
        dv_blk += p.T @ do                              # dV += P^T dO
        dp = do @ v_blk.T                               # dP_ij = dO V^T
        ds = p * (dp - D_i[:, None]) * scale            # dS_ij = P_ij (dP_ij - D_i), with tau
        dq += ds @ k_blk                                # dQ_i += dS_ij K_j
        dk_blk += ds.T @ q                              # dK_j += dS_ij^T Q_i
    return dq  # dk, dv accumulated in place
```

Stepping back over the whole chain. Attention turned out to be memory-bound, so its runtime is the HBM traffic of round-tripping the N×N score and probability matrices, not the FLOPs — which is the only thing that explains why FLOP-cutting approximations didn't speed it up. Making the objective "minimize HBM accesses" forces the demand to never write the N×N matrix, which forces fusing everything into one kernel; the obstacle that softmax needs a whole row dissolves into the online-softmax recurrence carrying a running max and denominator and rescaling partial results — promoted here to also rescale the running output accumulator, which I checked lands on the dense softmax output to 2e-16 and then proved exact by induction. The other obstacle, needing the N×N matrix for the backward, dissolves by recomputing the attention block on-chip from a saved O(N) statistic and the inputs — and the access count then showed this is a wash on the clock rather than a cost, because the avoided HBM accesses match what storing-and-reloading would pay — together with the identity D_i = do_i·o_i (verified exact, and the resulting dQ, dK, dV checked against finite differences to ~1e-10) that turns a length-N row reduction into a size-d dot product. Counting accesses gives Θ(N²d²/M), a reduction from Θ(N²) of order d²/M — about 6× at d=64, 1.6× at d=128, vanishing as d→√M — and a subrange argument shows that count is optimal for exact attention. Skipping zero blocks extends it to block-sparse with accesses scaled by the sparsity.
