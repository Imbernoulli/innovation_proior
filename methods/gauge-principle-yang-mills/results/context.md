# Context: a local principle for internal symmetry

## Research question

Strong interactions respect an internal symmetry — isotopic spin, the freedom to rotate the proton–neutron doublet into each other by an SU(2) transformation — to very good accuracy. As the symmetry is usually written, that rotation is a *global* one: I pick a single SU(2) matrix and apply the same one at every point of spacetime. But there is something arbitrary in that. The choice of "which internal direction is the proton" is, by the symmetry's own logic, physically meaningless; the symmetry says no internal direction is preferred. Yet once I fix the orientation of the isotopic-spin axes at one spacetime point, the global rule fixes it at every other point too — I am no longer free to choose differently over there. Why should a convention adopted here, with no physical content, propagate rigidly across the entire universe?

The question is whether one can demand the stronger thing: that the interactions be invariant under *independent* isotopic-spin rotations at every spacetime point, so that the relative orientation of the isospin frames at two separated points becomes a physically meaningless quantity, fixable freely. If such a *local* invariance can be imposed, what new structure does the world need in order to support it — and what would that structure predict?

## Background

**Internal symmetry: isotopic spin.** Heisenberg proposed treating the proton and neutron as two states of a single object, the nucleon, distinguished by an internal two-valued label formally identical to spin-½. Because the nuclear force barely distinguishes them — their masses are nearly equal, and Breit, Condon & Present and Cassen & Condon found proton–proton, neutron–proton and neutron–neutron forces equal in the same states — the strong interaction is, to good approximation, invariant under rotations of this internal "isotopic spin." Wigner made the total isotopic spin **T** a conserved quantum number. The internal rotation group is SU(2): a doublet ψ (proton, neutron) transforms as ψ → Sψ with S a 2×2 unitary matrix of determinant one, generated by the three isotopic "angular-momentum" matrices T^i = σ^i/2. The pion is known to carry isotopic spin one (its three charge states π⁺, π⁰, π⁻), and the comparison of p+p → π⁺+d with n+p → π⁰+d (Hildebrand) confirms that pions couple to the nucleon with isotopic spin conserved. Isotopic spin is, at this point, an exact-looking *global* symmetry of the strong interactions.

**The abelian gauge principle of electromagnetism.** Weyl tried to enlarge general relativity by allowing the scale ("gauge," *Eichinvarianz*) of length to be reset independently at each point, g_μν → e^{λ(x)} g_μν. As a theory of length it failed — the rescaling is path-dependent, so a clock's rate would depend on its history. After quantum mechanics, London, Fock, and Weyl revived the idea in the right setting: replace the real scale factor by a complex phase. A charged matter field is described by a complex wavefunction ψ, and its overall phase carries no physical meaning — ψ → e^{iα}ψ changes nothing. Weyl's move was to demand this hold *locally*: ψ(x) → e^{iα(x)}ψ(x), with α an arbitrary function of spacetime. The free derivative term ψ̄γ^μ∂_μψ is then no longer invariant, because ∂_μ acting on e^{iα(x)}ψ produces an extra ∂_μα term. The cure is to introduce a vector field A_μ and replace the ordinary derivative by a covariant one,

  D_μ = ∂_μ − ie A_μ,

with A_μ transforming inhomogeneously,

  A_μ → A_μ + (1/e) ∂_μ α,

so that the unwanted ∂_μα is absorbed and D_μψ transforms exactly like ψ. The combination ψ̄γ^μ D_μ ψ is then locally invariant; the field A_μ is the electromagnetic potential, its coupling e the electric charge, and the gauge-invariant field strength is

  F_μν = ∂_μ A_ν − ∂_ν A_μ,

which is unchanged under the gauge transformation (the ∂_μ∂_ν α terms cancel by symmetry). A mass term m²A_μA^μ is forbidden — it is not gauge invariant — so the photon is massless. This is the template: a *local* symmetry forces the existence of a compensating field with a fixed form of coupling. The known instance is abelian, U(1): the phases e^{iα} commute, so the order in which one applies two of them does not matter.

**Where the analogy is not obviously safe.** The phase group U(1) is abelian; the isotopic-spin group SU(2) is not — two finite isotopic rotations do not commute, S₁S₂ ≠ S₂S₁, and the generators obey [T^i, T^j] = i ε^{ijk} T^k. Whether Weyl's construction survives this non-commutativity is exactly the open question. The compensating field, if it exists, would now have to be matrix-valued (one component for each generator), and its transformation law would have to involve S(x) and its derivatives — objects that need not commute with the field itself. It is not clear in advance that the inhomogeneous term can be cancelled, nor that a field strength can be built that transforms cleanly.

**Localized fields.** A further pressure points the same way: the prevailing picture of field theory is that all fields are *localized* — built point by point, with no action at a distance. A symmetry that is only definable globally, requiring a single rotation applied identically everywhere at once, sits uneasily with that picture. A genuinely local field theory ought to permit the symmetry transformation to be chosen locally too.

## Baselines

**Weyl's abelian electromagnetic gauge invariance (with London and Fock).** Core idea and math as above: local U(1) phase invariance, compensated by A_μ with D_μ = ∂_μ − ieA_μ and A_μ → A_μ + (1/e)∂_μα, yielding F_μν = ∂_μA_ν − ∂_νA_μ and the QED Lagrangian −¼F_μνF^{μν} + ψ̄(iγ^μD_μ − m)ψ. *Gap it leaves:* it is built entirely on a commuting (abelian) group. The field strength is linear in A_μ, the photon is chargeless and does not couple to itself, and the source of the field is matter alone. Nothing in the construction tells you what to do when the symmetry group is non-commuting, and the linearity of F_μν is a feature special to the abelian case. Whether the principle generalizes is untested.

**Global isotopic-spin invariance (Heisenberg; Wigner; Cassen & Condon; Breit, Condon & Present).** Core idea: the strong interaction is invariant under a single, spacetime-independent SU(2) rotation of the nucleon doublet; total isotopic spin **T** is conserved. *Gap it leaves:* the invariance is only global. It treats the choice of internal orientation as fixed once and for all across spacetime, which is in tension both with the principle of localized fields and with the symmetry's own claim that no internal direction is physical. It also imposes only the *one* conservation law (total **T**); it says nothing about a dynamical field associated with the symmetry, because there is none — global symmetry forces no new field into existence.

**Heisenberg-Pauli canonical field quantization.** The standard machinery for quantizing a field theory from a Lagrangian: conjugate momenta, equal-time commutators, a Hamiltonian. *Role here:* it is the tool any new field theory must be passed through to extract its quanta (their spin, charge, statistics) and to check Lorentz invariance — but it presupposes a Lagrangian, which a new local-symmetry principle would first have to supply.

## Evaluation settings

The natural yardsticks are internal consistency and the known phenomenology of the strong interaction. A successful local-symmetry theory must (i) reduce to a sensible free theory and to ordinary global isotopic-spin conservation in the appropriate limit; (ii) reproduce the abelian electromagnetic gauge theory exactly when the internal group is taken to be U(1); (iii) be Lorentz invariant and quantizable by the canonical Heisenberg-Pauli procedure; (iv) yield a conserved total isotopic spin **T** = ∫ ℑ_4 d³x as the Noether charge of the local symmetry; and (v) predict the quantum numbers — spin, isotopic spin, electric charge, and mass — of whatever new quanta the compensating field carries, so they can be confronted with what is and is not seen experimentally. The relevant experimental backdrop is nucleon–nucleon and pion–nucleon scattering and the charge-independence data that established isotopic spin in the first place.
