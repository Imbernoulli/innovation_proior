Let me start from the thing that actually hurts. I want a super-polynomial lower bound on the size of arithmetic formulas computing the permanent or the determinant, and I cannot have it: nobody can prove a super-polynomial formula lower bound for any explicit polynomial at all. The record for the permanent and determinant is Ω(n³), Kalorkoti's, and that method is capped — for any partition of the n² matrix variables its transcendence-degree sum over the determinant is at most n⁴, so it physically cannot reach super-polynomial. So the general problem is a wall. If I want to make real progress I have to retreat to a restricted class of formulas — but the retreat is only meaningful if the class is *natural*, if the honest, hand-written formulas for the permanent and determinant already live inside it.

What is natural about the permanent and determinant? They are multilinear: each variable x_{i,j} appears to the first power in every monomial. And the formulas people actually write for them — cofactor expansion, Ryser's expansion — never cook up a high intermediate power of some variable only to cancel it later; every sub-expression along the way is itself multilinear. So the class to attack is *multilinear formulas*: formulas where the polynomial computed at every single gate is multilinear. This forbids exactly the "raise to a power, cancel later" trick. It's a real restriction, and yet the permanent and determinant and their obvious formulas all sit inside it. Good. That's the target class.

And here's the encouraging fact that makes it not hopeless and the discouraging fact that makes it not trivial, side by side: for this class, *nothing* is known — no lower bound at all, not even for constant depth. The only neighbour is set-multilinear constant-depth formulas, a weaker model where the variables are pre-partitioned into sets and each gate's monomials take exactly one variable from each set; an exponential bound is known there. So I'm in genuinely open territory but with a couple of techniques nearby that I should mine.

Every lower bound I know in this area has the same shape. You invent a complexity measure — a number you attach to the polynomial at each gate — that is small for anything a small formula can compute and large for your target. Then you bound how fast the measure can grow as you go up through `+` and `×` gates, conclude it stays small at the root of any small formula, and contrast that with its being large on the permanent. So the entire game is: find the right measure. Let me look hard at the two measures already on the table and figure out precisely where each one dies on multilinear formulas, because that's where the new idea has to come from.

Nisan–Wigderson's measure is Dim(f), the dimension of the linear span of all partial derivatives of f of all orders. It's sub-additive across plus, Dim(g+h) ≤ Dim(g)+Dim(h), and sub-multiplicative across times, Dim(g·h) ≤ Dim(g)·Dim(h), so you can run an induction up the formula. It nails homogeneous depth-3, it nails set-multilinear constant depth. Why doesn't it nail multilinear formulas? Let me actually think about what Dim does on a multilinear polynomial. Take something with a small formula and ask how many independent partial derivatives it has. The trouble is that a linear-size multilinear formula can already have a span of partial derivatives that's *enormous* — as large as, or larger than, the span for the permanent or determinant. If the measure is big for both the cheap thing and the target, it can't separate them. So Dim is just too coarse here; it's large on small multilinear formulas. Dead end, but an informative one: the measure has to be something that small multilinear formulas keep *small*, and Dim isn't it.

Now Nisan's non-commutative measure. This one I find more promising because of how cleanly it works, and I want to understand exactly why it works so I can see what breaks. In the non-commutative world, where xy ≠ yx, take a homogeneous degree-r polynomial and pick a split point k. Form the matrix M_k(f) whose rows are indexed by degree-k monomials, columns by degree-(r−k) monomials, and the entry is the coefficient in f of the row-monomial concatenated with the column-monomial. The magic is that every non-commutative monomial of degree r factors *uniquely* as a degree-k prefix times a degree-(r−k) suffix — so this matrix is well defined and every coefficient of f sits in exactly one cell. Then if you have a branching program with t vertices in layer k, you can write f = ∑_{i=1}^t h_i g_i, prefixes through the i-th vertex times suffixes from it, and each term contributes a rank-one matrix, so rank(M_k(f)) ≤ t. Find a polynomial with rank 2^{Ω(n)} and you've got an exponential lower bound. Beautiful, and it works because of unique factorization of monomials.

So the obvious question: can I take this coefficient-matrix idea into the commutative multilinear world? Here's the natural translation. I have multilinear polynomials. Split my variables into two halves, call them Y = {y_1,...,y_m} and Z = {z_1,...,z_m}. A multilinear monomial is just a choice of a subset of the y's times a subset of the z's. So index rows by multilinear Y-monomials (subsets of Y), columns by multilinear Z-monomials (subsets of Z), and let M_f(p,q) be the coefficient in f of the product monomial p·q. That's a 2^m × 2^m matrix. It's the multilinear analogue of Nisan's matrix, and it's well defined because a multilinear monomial over Y∪Z splits uniquely into its Y-part and its Z-part. Let me check the growth rules. Across a plus gate, M_{f+g} = M_f + M_g, so rank(M_{f+g}) ≤ rank(M_f) + rank(M_g): sub-additive, good. Across a product gate, if f uses only Y-and-Z-variables disjoint from g's, then the monomials of f·g split as (Y-part of f times Y-part of g) by (Z-part of f times Z-part of g), and M_{f·g} is the tensor product M_f ⊗ M_g, so rank multiplies: rank(M_{f·g}) = rank(M_f)·rank(M_g). And there's a trivial cap: M_v has nonzero columns only for Z-monomials all of whose variables appear in f, so if f mentions only a(v) of the z-variables (say z is the smaller side), rank ≤ 2^{a(v)}. The skeleton of an induction is right there.

So I write the dream statement: a small multilinear formula computes a polynomial whose M_f has low rank; the permanent's M_f has high rank; contradiction. And then I check the dream against the simplest possible example and it detonates.

Take f = (y_1 + z_1)(y_2 + z_2)···(y_m + z_m). This is multilinear. It has a *linear-size* formula — m additions and m−1 multiplications. So by my dream it should have low-rank M_f. What is M_f? Expand: f = ∑_{S ⊆ [m]} (∏_{i∈S} y_i)(∏_{i∉S} z_i). So for every Y-monomial p = ∏_{i∈S} y_i there is *exactly one* Z-monomial q = ∏_{i∉S} z_i with M_f(p,q) = 1, and every other entry in that row is 0. M_f is a permutation matrix. Its rank is 2^m — full. A linear-size formula, full rank. The dream is just false: "small multilinear formula ⇒ low rank" is wrong.

Stare at this counterexample, because the falseness has a *shape*. Why is the rank full? Because the variables are perfectly interleaved with respect to my chosen split: factor i contributes one y and one z, and they're paired up. The split Y/Z lines up with the structure of the formula in the worst possible way. The matrix isn't full-rank for some deep reason; it's full-rank because I happened to cut the variables exactly along the seams of the formula.

So the cut is the problem, not the measure. I chose Y and Z; what if I don't choose them — what if I *randomize* the split? In f = ∏(y_i+z_i), the variables came pre-labelled as y's and z's, but that labelling is part of the adversary's example, designed to align with the factors. If I instead take a set of variables and *randomly* partition them into the two halves Y and Z, then the kind of perfect pairing that made the rank full would have to survive a random cut — and it won't, for a *small* formula. Here's the intuition I want to make precise: a small formula is built from few gates; under a random split, somewhere inside it there will be a gate whose sub-formula sees far more Y-variables than Z-variables (or vice versa), and such a lopsided gate has a rank-deficient sub-matrix because of the 2^{a(v)} cap — its rank is bounded by 2 to the *smaller* side, which is much less than 2 to the average. And the plus/times rules then drag that local deficit all the way up to the root. The permanent and determinant, on the other hand — if I can arrange that under the random split they restrict to something like ∏(y_i ± z_i), which is full rank — will keep full rank. That's the asymmetry I need: small formulas can't avoid a lopsided gate, but the true permanent/determinant restriction is full-rank by construction.

Let me build this carefully. First a clean-up. The product rule rank(M_{f·g}) = rank(M_f)·rank(M_g) needed the variable sets of the two sons to be disjoint. A general multilinear formula doesn't guarantee that at every product gate — two sons could share a variable as long as the product stays multilinear (the shared variable just doesn't actually appear in one of them after expansion). Let me force disjointness syntactically. Call a formula *syntactic multilinear* if at every product gate v with sons v_1, v_2, the variable sets X_{v_1} and X_{v_2} are literally disjoint. Can I assume this for free? Suppose v is a product gate and both sons mention x_i. Since the gate's output is multilinear, x_i can appear in the product to power at most one, so it actually appears in at most one of the two son-polynomials — say it's absent from the polynomial computed by v_1. Then I can replace every occurrence of x_i in the *sub-formula* φ_{v_1} by the constant 0 without changing what v_1 computes (the variable wasn't contributing anyway), removing x_i from X_{v_1}. Repeat over all product gates and all shared variables. Same size, same output polynomial, now syntactic multilinear. So without loss of generality I work with syntactic multilinear formulas, and from now on the product rule is exact.

Now I need the local geometry of a node. For a node v in a formula over Y∪Z, let Y_v and Z_v be the y- and z-variables that appear in φ_v. Define b(v) = the average of |Y_v| and |Z_v|, a(v) = the minimum of the two, and d(v) = b(v) − a(v), which measures how lopsided the node is — it's half the gap between |Y_v| and |Z_v|. The three rank facts in this language: rank(M_v) ≤ 2^{a(v)} (the small-side cap); rank(M_v) ≤ rank(M_{v_1}) + rank(M_{v_2}) at a plus gate; rank(M_v) = rank(M_{v_1})·rank(M_{v_2}) at a product gate (syntactic multilinear). The small-side cap is the whole point: a lopsided node has a(v) = b(v) − d(v) much smaller than b(v), so its rank 2^{a(v)} = 2^{b(v) − d(v)} is a factor 2^{d(v)} below the "balanced" value 2^{b(v)}. Imbalance *is* rank deficit. So I want to call a node *k-unbalanced* if d(v) ≥ k, and I want to show that on the way from any leaf to the root I am forced to pass through a k-unbalanced node — that gives a 2^{−k}-deficit which I then propagate.

But "any path" is too strong — a formula could have many paths, some balanced. Which paths matter for getting the deficit to the root? At a product gate the rank multiplies, so a deficit at *one* son is enough — I should follow the son that carries the bulk, the heavier child. Since b(v) ≤ b(v_1) + b(v_2) always, at least one son u_1 has b(v) ≤ 2·b(u_1). Call a path from a leaf to v *central* if at each step the parent's b is at most twice the chosen child's b — the heavy-child path. By that inequality a central path to any node always exists. Now say v is *k-weak* if *every* central path reaching v is k-unbalanced (passes through a k-unbalanced node), and the formula is k-weak if its root is k-weak. The claim I'll prove is: k-weak forces a rank deficit at the root, and a small formula under a random split is k-weak.

The deficit propagation first. I claim: if v is k-weak then rank(M_v) ≤ |φ_v|·2^{b(v) − k/2}. Note before starting that k-weak forces b(v) ≥ k, since a central path reaching v exists and to contain a k-unbalanced node it needs at least k worth of variables to be unbalanced by. I'll induct on the size |φ_v|.

Leaf: rank(M_v) is at most 1, and 2^{b(v) − k/2} ≥ 2^{k/2} ≥ 1 because b(v) ≥ k, so the bound holds.

v itself is k-unbalanced: then a(v) = b(v) − d(v) ≤ b(v) − k, so by the small-side cap rank(M_v) ≤ 2^{a(v)} ≤ 2^{b(v) − k} ≤ |φ_v|·2^{b(v) − k/2}. Done — this is the base case where the deficit is realized locally.

v is a product gate and not itself k-unbalanced. Sons v_1, v_2, and by syntactic multilinearity b(v) = b(v_1) + b(v_2). Without loss of generality v_1 is the heavy child, b(v) ≤ 2·b(v_1). Every central path to v continues to v_1 (heavy child), and since each such path is k-unbalanced and v is not the k-unbalanced node on it, the k-unbalanced node lies in v_1's part, so v_1 is k-weak. Induction on v_1: rank(M_{v_1}) ≤ |φ_{v_1}|·2^{b(v_1) − k/2}. For the other son, the small-side cap: rank(M_{v_2}) ≤ 2^{a(v_2)} ≤ 2^{b(v_2)}. Multiply (product rule): rank(M_v) ≤ |φ_{v_1}|·2^{b(v_1) + a(v_2) − k/2} ≤ |φ_{v_1}|·2^{b(v_1) + b(v_2) − k/2} = |φ_{v_1}|·2^{b(v) − k/2} ≤ |φ_v|·2^{b(v) − k/2}. The key step is that the deficit −k/2 sits on the heavy child v_1, and the light child v_2 contributes at most its full 2^{b(v_2)} which the sum b(v_1)+b(v_2)=b(v) absorbs.

v is a plus gate and not k-unbalanced. Sons v_1, v_2. Now b(v) ≤ b(v_1) + b(v_2), and without loss of generality b(v) ≤ 2·b(v_1), so v_1 is k-weak as before. I split on the other son.
If also b(v) ≤ 2·b(v_2), then v_2 is k-weak too. Both b(v) ≥ b(v_1) and b(v) ≥ b(v_2), so the induction gives rank(M_{v_i}) ≤ |φ_{v_i}|·2^{b(v_i) − k/2} ≤ |φ_{v_i}|·2^{b(v) − k/2} for each i. Add (plus rule): rank(M_v) ≤ (|φ_{v_1}| + |φ_{v_2}|)·2^{b(v) − k/2} ≤ |φ_v|·2^{b(v) − k/2}.
If instead b(v) > 2·b(v_2), then b(v_2) < b(v)/2, and since b(v) ≥ k this gives b(v_2) < b(v) − b(v)/2 ≤ b(v) − k/2, so the small-side cap alone handles v_2: rank(M_{v_2}) ≤ 2^{a(v_2)} ≤ 2^{b(v_2)} ≤ 2^{b(v) − k/2}. And v_1 is k-weak with b(v) ≥ b(v_1), so rank(M_{v_1}) ≤ |φ_{v_1}|·2^{b(v) − k/2}. Add: rank(M_v) ≤ (|φ_{v_1}| + 1)·2^{b(v) − k/2} ≤ |φ_v|·2^{b(v) − k/2}. Every case closes. So k-weakness costs the rank a factor 2^{−k/2}, paid for by only the formula size out front. Pillar one stands.

Now the harder pillar: a small formula, under a random split, is k-weak with high probability. This is where the random partition has to do its work, and it's where I have to be honest about a tension I haven't resolved yet. I need two things from the partition that pull in opposite directions. First, it must make small formulas k-weak — that wants randomness, an unpredictable cut so the adversary's formula can't align with it. Second, the permanent and determinant must restrict to a *full-rank* polynomial under it — that wants structure, a cut engineered so that the permanent becomes something whose M_f is a permutation matrix. Let me design the partition to do both, then prove the k-weakness.

The matrix variables are X = {x_{i,j}}_{i,j∈[n]}. I'll only need m = ⌈n^{1/3}⌉ of the y's and z's. Here's the structured-random assignment A. Pick, uniformly, m disjoint row indices q_1,...,q_m and another m disjoint row indices r_1,...,r_m (all 2m distinct); similarly m disjoint column indices s_1,...,s_m and t_1,...,t_m (all 2m distinct). For each i, look at the 2×2 block of variables at rows {q_i, r_i} and columns {s_i, t_i}. Flip a coin: with probability ½ assign

  x_{q_i,s_i} ← y_i, x_{q_i,t_i} ← z_i, x_{r_i,s_i} ← 1, x_{r_i,t_i} ← 1,

and with probability ½ assign

  x_{q_i,s_i} ← y_i, x_{q_i,t_i} ← 1, x_{r_i,s_i} ← z_i, x_{r_i,t_i} ← 1.

Then take an arbitrary perfect matching π between the leftover rows and leftover columns and set x_{i,π(i)} ← 1; every remaining variable ← 0. Each y_i and each z_i is used exactly once, so after substitution the formula is a syntactic multilinear formula over Y∪Z of the same size.

Why this block design? Let me check what the permanent of the substituted matrix is, because that's the structure half. After substitution the matrix, up to permuting rows and columns, is block-diagonal: m blocks B_i, then a string of 1's on the diagonal, then 0's. Each B_i is a 2×2 matrix whose permanent I can read off from the two layouts. First layout: rows (y_i, z_i) and (1, 1); the permanent of [[y_i, z_i],[1,1]] is y_i·1 + z_i·1 = y_i + z_i. Second layout: [[y_i, 1],[z_i, 1]] has permanent y_i·1 + 1·z_i = y_i + z_i. Either way the block contributes y_i + z_i to the permanent. The diagonal 1's contribute 1, the 0's kill everything else, so the permanent of the whole substituted matrix is exactly ∏_{i=1}^m (y_i + z_i). And the determinant: det[[y_i, z_i],[1,1]] = y_i − z_i, and det[[y_i, 1],[z_i, 1]] = y_i − z_i, so the determinant restricts (up to a global sign from the row/column permutation) to ∏_{i=1}^m (y_i − z_i). Both are full-rank: for f = ∏(y_i + z_i), M_f is the permutation matrix I computed at the very start, rank 2^m; same for ∏(y_i − z_i). So the structure half is delivered — under A, the permanent and determinant become full-rank polynomials. The block design is exactly what manufactures the full-rank witness, and the two-layout coin plus the random row/column pairs are the randomness I'll use for the other half.

Now the k-weakness. I want: if |φ| ≤ n^{ε·log n} for a small enough constant ε, then with probability 1 − o(1) the substituted formula φ_A is k-weak, where I'll take k = n^{1/32}. Let me first feel out why the exponents should be what they are, then nail it.

The plan is to show that along any central path to the root, with overwhelming probability some node is k-unbalanced. A node v sees a fraction α(v) := |X_v|/n² of the matrix variables. Intuitively a node with a substantial α(v) receives many of the 2m special variables, and the coin in step three — which of each special pair becomes a y and which a z — makes the count of y's versus z's it sees a sum of independent fair coins, which fluctuates, so it's lopsided (k-unbalanced) with decent probability. The issue is that a single node is k-unbalanced only with some probability bounded away from 1, and there are up to n^{ε·log n} central paths, so one node per path isn't enough. But a central path is *long* — it passes through Ω(log n) nodes whose α grows geometrically from a leaf (α ≈ 1/n²) up to the root (α = 1) — and if I can find Ω(log n) nodes on it that fail to be k-unbalanced only with probability O(n^{−1/32}) each, *even conditioned on the earlier ones failing*, then the path is balanced only with probability O(n^{−1/32})^{Ω(log n)} = n^{−Ω(log n)}, which beats the n^{ε·log n} union bound over paths. So I need: per-node failure O(n^{−1/32}), conditionally, and Ω(log n) usable nodes per path. Let me make all three pieces real.

Let me set up the bookkeeping with a cleaner generation of the special variables. For each i, let W_i = {A^{-1}(y_i), A^{-1}(z_i)} be the pair of matrix-variables that carry y_i and z_i. Whether the formula ends up k-weak depends only on where the y's and z's land, i.e. on the W_i's and the y/z labelling — not on the 0/1 fillers. Generate the W_i's like this: for each i pick w_1^i uniformly from X; then with probability ½ pick w_2^i uniformly from X in the same row as w_1^i (distinct from it), and with probability ½ in the same column; set W_i = {w_1^i, w_2^i}. That captures "two variables sharing a row or a column," which is what the block design produces (the y_i, z_i pair shares either a row or a column depending on the layout coin). If any two of the chosen variables across different i's collide in a row or column, redo; but since the collision probability is O(1/n) and m = n^{1/3}, the redo happens with probability o(1), so I can analyze the simpler no-rejection distribution μ* (the w's i.i.d. as above) and pay an o(1) statistical-distance tax to transfer conclusions to the true distribution μ. Finally, step three: for each i flip a fair coin to decide which of the two is A^{-1}(y_i) and which is A^{-1}(z_i). Crucially, which paths are *central* depends only on the W_i's (steps one and two) — it's about the variable counts — while whether a path is *k-unbalanced* depends also on the y/z coin in step three. That separation is what will give me conditional independence.

Write W = ∪_i W_i, and for a node v write W_v = W ∩ X_v, and W_v^0 for the sub-collection of pairs W_i that meet X_v in exactly one of their two elements. (Under μ* I treat these as multisets.) Two concentration facts.

The first is just Chernoff. For a node v, the size |W_v| counts how many of the 2m special variables land in X_v; each w_b^i lands in X_v with probability α(v), and there are 2m of them, so |W_v| concentrates around α(v)·2m. Precisely, writing σ_b(v) = #{i : w_b^i ∈ X_v}, each is a sum of m = ⌈n^{1/3}⌉ indicators with success probability α(v), and Chernoff with relative deviation ½ gives, for any v with α(v) ≥ n^{−1/8}, Pr[|σ_b(v) − α(v)m| > ½·α(v)·m] < 2·exp(−n^{1/12}/2). Summing the two and union-bounding over the ≤ n^{ε·log n} nodes (the exponential beats n^{ε·log n} easily), with probability 1 − o(1): for every v with α(v) ≥ n^{−1/8}, 0.5·α(v)·2m ≤ |W_v| ≤ 1.5·α(v)·2m, and for every v with α(v) < n^{−1/8}, |W_v| ≤ 1.5·n^{−1/8}·2m. Call this Claim One.

The second fact is that for a node of moderate α, many pairs straddle the boundary of X_v — meet it in exactly one element — which is what I need for the imbalance argument. Claim Two: with probability 1 − o(1), for every v with 1/8 ≥ α(v) ≥ n^{−1/8}, |W_v^0| ≥ (1/16)·α(v)·m. To see it, call a row *dense* if at least half its entries are in X_v, and likewise for columns. Since |X_v| = α(v)·n², there are at most 2α(v)·n dense rows and at most 2α(v)·n dense columns, so at most 4α(v)²·n² variables lie in both a dense row and a dense column; because α(v) ≤ 1/8 that's at most α(v)·n²/2, so at least half the variables of X_v are *good* — not in a dense row, or not in a dense column. For a fixed i, estimate Pr[w_1^i ∈ X_v and w_2^i ∉ X_v]: with probability α(v), w_1^i ∈ X_v; given that, with probability at least ½ it's good, say not in a dense row; with probability ½ the layout picks w_2^i in the same row as w_1^i; and since that row isn't dense, with probability at least ½ that w_2^i lands outside X_v. Multiplying, the probability is at least α(v)/8. The number of such i is a sum of m independent indicators each at least α(v)/8, and since α(v) ≥ n^{−1/8} Chernoff gives it below (1/16)·α(v)·m with probability < 2·exp(−n^{1/12}/128). Each such i contributes a pair meeting X_v in exactly one element, so |W_v^0| is at least that count; union bound over nodes finishes Claim Two.

When both claims hold for every node I'll say the configuration W̃ = (W_1,...,W_m) is *good*; it's good with probability 1 − o(1). Note W̃ is determined by steps one and two, so once it's fixed I already know which paths are central; only the step-three coins remain random.

Now the core: fix a good W̃ and a path γ from a leaf to the root that is central for W̃. I claim Pr[γ is not k-unbalanced | W̃] ≤ n^{−Ω(log n)}. Pick spread-out nodes along γ as follows. α(v) is monotone increasing from leaf (≤ 1/n²) to root (= 1). Let v_1 be the first node with α(v_1) ≥ 100·n^{−1/8}; let v_{i+1} be the first node after v_i with α(v_{i+1}) ≥ 100·α(v_i); stop when no such node exists or α would exceed 1/8. Because γ is central, consecutive nodes can't jump α by too much: centrality says for a parent u and its chosen child u', |W_u| ≤ 2|W_{u'}|, and Claim One converts |W| back to α up to constant factors, so α(v_{i+1}) < 1000·α(v_i) for each step. Going from α ≈ n^{−1/8} up to 1/8 in geometric steps of ratio between 100 and 1000 takes Ω(log n) steps. So I get nodes v_1,...,v_l with l = Ω(log n), each satisfying 1/8 ≥ α(v_i) ≥ 100·n^{−1/8} and α(v_{i+1}) ≥ 100·α(v_i). For these, Claims One and Two give a usable lower bound on the straddling pairs: |W^0_{v_{i+1}}| ≥ 2|W_{v_i}| ≥ 200·m·n^{−1/8} ≥ 2·n^{1/8}.

Let E be the event that γ is not k-unbalanced, and E_i the event that the specific node v_i is not k-unbalanced. Since γ contains all the v_i, if no node of γ is k-unbalanced then certainly none of the v_i is, so E ⊆ ∩_i E_i, and Pr[E] ≤ Pr[∩_i E_i] = ∏_i Pr[E_i | ∩_{i'<i} E_{i'}]. So I need to bound each conditional factor by O(n^{−1/32}).

Fix i ≥ 2. Let the variables of W_{v_i} be x_1,...,x_r with r = |W_{v_i}|. Note W_{v_{i-1}} ⊆ W_{v_i}. Split the special variables in X_{v_i} into two parts:

  S = W^0_{v_i} \ W_{v_{i-1}},  and  T = W_{v_i} \ S.

The point of S: it's made of straddling pairs of v_i that aren't already in v_{i-1}, and since |W^0_{v_i}| ≥ 2|W_{v_{i-1}}|, I get |S| ≥ |W^0_{v_i}| − |W_{v_{i-1}}| ≥ 2|W_{v_{i-1}}| − |W_{v_{i-1}}| = |W_{v_{i-1}}| ≥ n^{1/8}. The point of T: it contains W_{v_{i-1}}, so anything about the earlier nodes lives inside T.

Now read off d(v_i). For each special variable x_j, let χ_j = 1 if A(x_j) is a y-variable and 0 if it's a z-variable — this is exactly the step-three coin. By definition, d(v_i) measures the imbalance between the count of y's and z's in X_{v_i}, i.e. d(v_i) = |∑_{j∈[r]} χ_j − r/2|. Split the sum over S and T: writing σ = ∑_{x_j ∈ S} χ_j and τ = ∑_{x_j ∈ T} χ_j, we have d(v_i) = |σ + τ − r/2|.

Here is where the careful construction of S pays off. The variables in S come from straddling pairs (size-1 intersections of distinct W_j's): for such a pair, exactly one of its two elements is in X_{v_i}, and which one is a y versus a z is decided by an *independent* fair coin in step three, and these coins for distinct pairs are mutually independent — and independent of the coins governing T's variables. So {χ_j : x_j ∈ S} are mutually independent fair coins, even conditioned on any event about {χ_j : x_j ∈ T}. Hence σ is Binomial(|S|, ½), even conditioned on the T-coins. A binomial random variable doesn't concentrate on any single value: by the standard anti-concentration, its maximum atom probability is O(|S|^{−1/2}), which is O(n^{−1/16}) since |S| ≥ n^{1/8}. So σ hits no specific value with probability more than O(n^{−1/16}), even conditioned on T; therefore σ + τ − r/2, and hence d(v_i), hits no specific value with probability more than O(n^{−1/16}), conditioned on any event about the T-coins.

And the conditioning I actually have, ∩_{i'<i} E_{i'}, is a statement about whether earlier nodes v_{i'} are k-unbalanced — which depends on the y/z coins of variables in W_{v_{i-1}} ⊆ T. So it's a T-event. Therefore the anti-concentration survives it. Now, v_i is *not* k-unbalanced exactly when d(v_i) < k. Since d(v_i) is an integer, it can be below k only by hitting one of the k integer values 0, 1, ..., k−1, each with probability O(n^{−1/16}), so by a union bound

  Pr[E_i | ∩_{i'<i} E_{i'}] = Pr[d(v_i) < k | (T-event)] ≤ k·O(n^{−1/16}) = O(n^{1/32}·n^{−1/16}) = O(n^{−1/32}),

using k = n^{1/32}. This is exactly the conditional per-node bound I wanted, and the choice k = n^{1/32} is forced: I need k·n^{−1/16} to be a negative power of n, i.e. k ≪ n^{1/16}, and n^{1/32} is the clean half-exponent that leaves a comfortable n^{−1/32} margin. Multiplying over the l = Ω(log n) nodes,

  Pr[E | W̃] ≤ ∏_{i} O(n^{−1/32}) = (O(n^{−1/32}))^{Ω(log n)} = n^{−Ω(log n)}.

To finish the pillar: the number of paths from a leaf to the root equals the number of leaves, which is less than n^{ε·log n} for the small ε. For a good W̃, union-bounding the per-path failure n^{−Ω(log n)} over fewer than n^{ε·log n} paths is still o(1) when ε is small enough — that's exactly the constraint that pins ε, balancing the Ω(log n) in the exponent against the ε·log n leaves. So for good W̃, with probability 1 − o(1) every central path to the root is k-unbalanced, i.e. the root is k-weak, i.e. φ_A is k-weak. Since W̃ is good with probability 1 − o(1), unconditionally φ_A is k-weak with probability 1 − o(1). Pillar two stands.

Now collide the two pillars. Suppose the permanent of the n×n matrix has a multilinear formula φ of size |φ| ≤ n^{ε·log n}. By the clean-up I may assume φ is syntactic multilinear. Apply the random assignment A. By pillar two, with probability 1 − o(1) the formula φ_A is k-weak with k = n^{1/32}; in particular such an A exists. By pillar one applied at the root,

  rank(M_{φ_A}) ≤ |φ_A|·2^{m − k/2} = n^{ε·log n}·2^{m − k/2}.

Now compare n^{ε·log n} with 2^{k/2}: n^{ε·log n} = 2^{ε·(log n)²}, and k/2 = n^{1/32}/2, and for large n the polynomial n^{1/32} dwarfs the poly-logarithm ε·(log n)², so ε·(log n)² < k/2 and hence n^{ε·log n}·2^{−k/2} < 1. Therefore

  rank(M_{φ_A}) < 2^{m − k/2}·2^{k/2} = 2^m,

so the rank is strictly less than 2^m. But φ computes the permanent, so φ_A computes the permanent of the substituted matrix, which I computed to be ∏_{i=1}^m (y_i + z_i), whose M_f is a permutation matrix of rank exactly 2^m. A polynomial cannot have rank both < 2^m and = 2^m. Contradiction. So no multilinear formula for the permanent has size ≤ n^{ε·log n}; every multilinear formula for the permanent has size n^{Ω(log n)}. The determinant is identical: φ_A computes ∏_{i=1}^m (y_i − z_i) up to sign, again a permutation matrix of rank 2^m, same contradiction.

There's one thing here I want to register because it sharpens what was really proved. The restricted polynomial ∏(y_i + z_i) is *itself* trivially easy — it has a linear-size multilinear formula, the very example that detonated my first dream. So the contradiction is not "this polynomial is hard." It's subtler: a small formula φ for the permanent, after the random substitution, becomes k-weak, and a k-weak formula provably cannot output a full-rank polynomial — even an easy full-rank one like ∏(y_i + z_i). The smallness of φ forces a rank deficit under the random cut that the genuine restriction of the permanent simply refuses to have. The full-rank-ness of the permanent's restriction, manufactured by the 2×2-block design, is the immovable object; the rank deficit forced by k-weakness is the irresistible force; and no formula of size ≤ n^{ε·log n} can host both.

So the chain is: the partial-derivative coefficient matrix M_f works in the non-commutative world but fails in the multilinear world on the interleaved example ∏(y_i+z_i), because the variable split was chosen to align with the formula; randomize the split, and a small formula is forced to contain a heavy gate that is lopsided — k-unbalanced — under it; the small-side cap turns that imbalance into a rank deficit, which an induction along central paths drags to the root with only a formula-size factor (the deficit-propagation pillar); a random structured assignment makes any small formula k-weak with high probability, via long central paths each of which is unbalanced because the y/z coin makes the imbalance of Ω(log n) spread-out nodes an anti-concentrated binomial (the k-weakness pillar); and the same structured assignment, by its 2×2-block design, makes the permanent restrict to ∏(y_i+z_i) and the determinant to ∏(y_i−z_i), both of full rank 2^m; the rank deficit of a small formula and the full rank of the true restriction cannot coexist, giving the bound n^{Ω(log n)}.

Theorem. Over any field, any multilinear arithmetic formula for the permanent or the determinant of an n×n matrix has size n^{Ω(log n)}. (And since a syntactic multilinear circuit of depth D unfolds into a multilinear formula of size 2^D, the corresponding circuit-depth corollary is Ω(log² n).)
