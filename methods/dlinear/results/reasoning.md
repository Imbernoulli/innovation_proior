Let me start from what actually bothers me about how long-horizon forecasters are built right now, not from any one architecture. I have a multivariate series — hourly electricity load across hundreds of clients, transformer-oil temperatures, weather indicators — and I want to predict a long stretch of the future, dozens to hundreds of steps out, given a window of recent history. Every recent model people reach for here is a Transformer of some flavor: LogTrans with its log-sparse mask, Informer with ProbSparse attention and a distilling encoder and a generative decoder, Autoformer swapping attention for auto-correlation and folding in a decomposition block, Pyraformer's pyramidal attention, FEDformer doing attention in the frequency domain with a mixture of decomposition kernels. Each is more intricate than the last, each reports beating the previous on the headline long-term benchmarks, and the accuracy keeps creeping up. And yet something nags at me, because I know what attention *is* and I know what a numeric time series *is*, and the two don't obviously fit.

Attention's whole job is to score pairs of tokens by content and aggregate them by a softmax over those scores: softmax(QK^T/√d)V. Before any positional signal is added, the operation is permutation-equivariant — if I shuffle the input tokens, I get the same multiset of pairwise scores and the same outputs up to the matching permutation. That's not a bug for language or vision; it's why attention works there. A sentence keeps its meaning if I jostle a couple of words, an image keeps its content if I shuffle a few patches, because each token — a word embedding, an image patch — carries a lot of standalone semantic content, and the model can recover ordering from positional encodings as a side channel. But stare at a single number in a load trace: 0.42. It means nothing by itself. There is no point-wise semantics in 0.42 the way there is in the word "cat." The only thing that carries the signal is the order — that this value sits between the previous hour and the next, that it's on the rising shoulder of the evening peak, that the same shape recurred 24 hours ago and 168 hours ago. So I'm being asked to believe that an architecture whose core operation is order-blind, and re-injects order only as an additive positional encoding bolted on top, is the right tool for data whose entire content is order. That's working against the grain.

Two things sharpen this from a hunch into a real doubt. The first is a diagnostic I cannot skip: feed a forecaster a *longer* look-back window — more context — and check whether its error actually drops. A model that genuinely extracts temporal structure from context should improve when handed more of it; error that stalls or climbs as the window grows would be the fingerprint of a model latching onto something local and being confused by the rest. The second is a confound in how these results are obtained. In each of those papers, the non-Transformer baselines they beat are iterated multi-step forecasters — learn a one-step model, feed its own prediction back in, roll it forward T times. Iterated prediction has a known disease over long horizons: each step is fed the previous step's error, and those errors compound as the roll-out lengthens, so by step 300 the input is mostly the model's own accumulated mistakes. Meanwhile the Transformers themselves nearly all use direct multi-step prediction — one map straight from the history to the whole horizon, no recursive feedback loop, no recursive compounding. So when a direct Transformer beats an iterated ARIMA or an iterated RNN, I genuinely cannot tell how much of the win is the attention and how much is just that one side was handicapped by recursive error accumulation and the other wasn't. The comparison conflates "Transformer" with "direct multi-step."

If that's the real situation, then the way to find out is not to build a better Transformer — it's to build the simplest possible thing that *removes the confound* and see how much of the reported gap survives. Strip away the attention entirely and keep only the part I suspect is doing the work: the direct multi-step strategy. What is the most minimal direct multi-step forecaster I can write down? It has to take a length-L history and emit a length-T horizon in one shot, no recursion. The absolute floor of that is a single affine map along the time axis. For one variate, predict the horizon as a weighted sum of the look-back: X̂_i = W X_i, with W a T×L matrix, X_i the look-back of variate i, X̂_i its predicted horizon. Row t of W says how to combine the L past values into the t-th future value. One linear layer, applied along time. That's it.

Let me make sure this minimal thing isn't trivially crippled before I take it seriously. The objection that comes first is "it's linear, it can't model anything." But think about what a single linear row can actually express. If the series has a period p — say 24 for daily, 168 for weekly — then the value at a future step is, to first order, well predicted by the value one period back in the look-back window; a row of W can place its weight on exactly that lag and read it off. If the series has a trend, a slow drift, then a future value is well predicted by an extrapolation of recent values, which is a weighted difference — also a linear combination of the look-back. Periodicity and trend are *exactly* the two kinds of structure a linear temporal map captures naturally, because both are, at heart, "the future is a fixed linear functional of the recent past." And here's the hypothesis that makes me think this is enough rather than a strawman: long-term forecasting is only feasible at all for series that *have* a relatively clear trend and periodicity. A genuinely chaotic series isn't long-horizon-predictable by anything. So if the only forecastable structure in the long-horizon regime is trend plus periodicity, and a linear map captures trend plus periodicity, then a linear map should already capture most of what is forecastable — and a high-capacity nonlinear sequence model would mostly be fitting noise that doesn't generalize across the train/test boundary. There's also a structural nicety: in this linear map, every input step connects directly to every output step with a learned weight, so the maximum signal path from any past observation to any future prediction is length one. There is no recurrence to forget through, no attention bottleneck — the long-range dependency is in the weight matrix itself, immediately.

So I'll commit to the minimal model: one linear layer, L→T, applied along the temporal axis, trained by direct multi-step MSE over the whole horizon. One more decision falls out immediately. There are C variates; do I learn a separate W per channel, or one shared W across channels? Within a single dataset the channels usually share temporal dynamics — all the electricity clients have the same daily and weekly rhythm, all the weather sensors share the same diurnal cycle — so a shared W along time, applied identically to each channel, both encodes that prior and slashes the parameter count from C·T·L to T·L, which matters for overfitting on the smaller datasets. So I'll share weights across variates by default and deliberately *not* model cross-variate (spatial) correlation at all. That's a real choice: the intricate models spend capacity learning which channels influence which, and I'm betting that on these benchmarks that cross-channel coupling is weak or spurious, and that not modeling it avoids overfitting. The point is a clean baseline, so I want the fewest moving parts that can still represent the forecastable structure.

Now I run the bare linear model in my head against the one dataset feature I know will bite it: distribution shift. On the hourly transformer-temperature data the *level* of the series drifts substantially between the training span and the test span — the mean is simply higher or lower later in time. A single linear map W·X is fit on the training-era levels; at test time the input window sits at a shifted baseline, and W faithfully maps that shifted baseline to a shifted-and-wrong horizon. The model has no way to recenter. I can see the fix without much agonizing: before applying W, subtract the last observed value of the look-back from the whole window, run the linear map on the de-leveled window, then add that last value back to the output. The last value of the look-back is a cheap, causal estimate of the series' current level; subtracting it puts every window on a common baseline so W only has to learn the *shape* of the continuation, and adding it back re-anchors the prediction to wherever the series currently is. That's a simple per-window normalization — subtract last, predict, add back — and it directly neutralizes the level drift that the plain linear map can't. I'll keep this as a sibling variant for the shift-heavy datasets; call it the normalized-linear.

But the more interesting weakness of the bare linear model is what happens when a series has a strong trend *together with* its seasonality, which is the common case. Picture the signal as a big slow ramp with a small daily oscillation riding on top. A single W now has to fit both at once, and these two structures want very different weight patterns. The trend wants weights that extrapolate a slow drift — broadly smooth across the look-back. The seasonality wants weights sharply concentrated at the periodic lags. Worse, the trend component is large in magnitude and the seasonal component is small, so in a squared-error fit the trend dominates the gradient: W spends itself getting the big ramp roughly right and under-fits the small oscillation that's actually the part carrying the fine structure. One weight matrix is being asked to be two different filters, and the loss makes it prioritize the loud one. So how do I let the two specialize?

Here's where a tool I already have lying around in this literature becomes obvious. Seasonal-trend decomposition is the oldest move in time-series analysis: write the series additively as a slow trend-cyclical part plus a seasonal/remainder part, because each piece, on its own, is more regular and more predictable than the sum. Classically that's STL; in the deep-learning setting Autoformer already wired a moving-average decomposition block into its network — estimate the trend by a moving average of the series, take the residual as the seasonal part. I don't need to invent any of that; I just need to *use* it as a fixed preprocessing in front of my linear maps. Decompose the input window once: trend = MovingAvg(x), seasonal = x − trend. Now the trend stream is the smooth ramp and the seasonal stream is the oscillation around it, cleanly separated and on comparable footing within each stream. Give each its own linear layer — W_trend and W_seasonal, both L→T — and let each specialize: W_trend learns to extrapolate a slow drift, W_seasonal learns to read off the periodic lags, neither contaminated by the other and neither's gradient swamped by the other's magnitude. Then sum the two predicted streams to recombine: X̂ = W_seasonal · seasonal + W_trend · trend.

I should be honest with myself about one thing here, because it looks like I just made the model "deeper" and that would betray the whole point. Is this still a linear model? Yes. Let the fixed moving-average operator be A. The recombined prediction is W_seasonal(I - A)x + W_trend A x, plus the two linear-layer biases. That is affine in the raw input. More than that, it does not enlarge the temporal affine function class beyond the single-layer map: for any affine map Mx + b, I can set W_seasonal = W_trend = M and split the bias across the two branches. What I add is not nonlinear representational power but *conditioning*: the fixed decomposition separates the loud trend from the quiet seasonality so that gradient descent on the squared error can fit each stream without letting the trend's magnitude swamp the seasonal residual. It's the same spirit as preconditioning an optimization problem — same affine solution set, a better-behaved parameterization. That's exactly what I want: not more nonlinear capacity, just a better-posed version of the same minimal map. And it pays off precisely when there's a clear trend, which is the case the bare linear model handled worst.

Let me pin down the moving average concretely, because the details decide whether the decomposition behaves at the window edges. I want trend = AvgPool over a sliding window of odd kernel size k, stride 1, so the trend has the same length as the input. A stride-1 average pool over a length-L signal with kernel k produces L - k + 1 outputs, shorter than the input, so I have to pad. Padding with zeros would drag the trend toward zero at the two ends, creating spurious dips exactly where I have least information; that's the wrong boundary behavior. Instead I replicate the endpoints: pad the front with (k - 1) // 2 copies of the first value and the back with (k - 1) // 2 copies of the last value, then average-pool. For the official odd kernel k = 25, that is 12 copied values on each side, and the output length is L + 24 - 25 + 1 = L. Replication means the moving average at the boundary just sees more of the boundary value, so the trend stays flat-but-faithful at the edges instead of being pulled to zero. I keep k = 25, the same decomposition scale used in the reference setup, because the comparison should reuse the established block rather than tune a new smoothing knob.

Now I want to check that the seasonal linear map really does learn periodicity, because that's the load-bearing claim. If I initialize W_seasonal not randomly but as the uniform average (1/L in every entry) and let it train, then visualize the learned weight matrix as a heatmap — forecast step t on one axis, look-back position on the other — I expect to see, on a periodic dataset, repeated high-weight patterns at the relevant phases. On hourly traffic data, for instance, a daily rhythm should show structure every 24 steps, and a weekly rhythm every 168 steps. That's a direct, interpretable readout that the linear map is doing exactly the temporal thing I claimed — pulling each future value from same-phase past values. The uniform 1/L initialization is a visualization option in the reference code, not the active default for ordinary training. The interpretability is a bonus, but the real point of the check is that it confirms the hypothesis: a one-layer linear map *is* representing periodicity directly.

Let me also sanity-check the shapes end to end, because the permute bookkeeping is where this kind of thing silently breaks. The input window is x with shape [B, L, C] — batch, time, channels. Decompose along time to get seasonal and trend, each still [B, L, C]. The linear layers act along the time axis, mapping L→T, so I need the time axis last: permute to [B, C, L]. Apply W_seasonal and W_trend, each an nn.Linear(L, T), giving [B, C, T] each. Sum them, [B, C, T], then permute back to [B, T, C] so the output is batch, horizon, channels — the same layout as the input, ready to be scored against the true horizon by MSE. With the shared non-individual path, W_seasonal and W_trend are single nn.Linear modules applied to all channels at once; with the per-channel variant they are lists of C linears looped over, but the default is the shared path.

That's the whole model, and I can write it as the forecasting architecture that fills the empty slot in the direct-multi-step harness. Two linear layers along time, a parameter-free moving-average decomposition in front, channel-shared, summed, trained by direct MSE over the full horizon:

```python
import torch
import torch.nn as nn


class moving_avg(nn.Module):
    """Trend extractor: length-preserving moving average along time."""
    def __init__(self, kernel_size, stride=1):
        super().__init__()
        self.kernel_size = kernel_size
        self.avg = nn.AvgPool1d(kernel_size=kernel_size, stride=stride, padding=0)

    def forward(self, x):                      # x: [B, L, C]
        # replicate-pad the endpoints so the trend stays faithful at the edges
        front = x[:, 0:1, :].repeat(1, (self.kernel_size - 1) // 2, 1)
        end = x[:, -1:, :].repeat(1, (self.kernel_size - 1) // 2, 1)
        x = torch.cat([front, x, end], dim=1)
        x = self.avg(x.permute(0, 2, 1))       # pool along time
        return x.permute(0, 2, 1)              # [B, L, C]


class series_decomp(nn.Module):
    """Split a window into trend (moving average) and seasonal (residual)."""
    def __init__(self, kernel_size):
        super().__init__()
        self.moving_avg = moving_avg(kernel_size, stride=1)

    def forward(self, x):                       # x: [B, L, C]
        trend = self.moving_avg(x)              # slow component
        seasonal = x - trend                    # residual: the oscillation
        return seasonal, trend


class Model(nn.Module):
    """Decomposition-Linear."""
    def __init__(self, configs):
        super().__init__()
        self.seq_len = configs.seq_len
        self.pred_len = configs.pred_len

        kernel_size = 25
        self.decompsition = series_decomp(kernel_size)
        self.individual = configs.individual
        self.channels = configs.enc_in

        if self.individual:
            self.Linear_Seasonal = nn.ModuleList()
            self.Linear_Trend = nn.ModuleList()
            for i in range(self.channels):
                self.Linear_Seasonal.append(nn.Linear(self.seq_len, self.pred_len))
                self.Linear_Trend.append(nn.Linear(self.seq_len, self.pred_len))
        else:
            self.Linear_Seasonal = nn.Linear(self.seq_len, self.pred_len)
            self.Linear_Trend = nn.Linear(self.seq_len, self.pred_len)

    def forward(self, x):                        # x: [B, L, C]
        seasonal_init, trend_init = self.decompsition(x)
        seasonal_init = seasonal_init.permute(0, 2, 1)   # [B, C, L]
        trend_init = trend_init.permute(0, 2, 1)         # [B, C, L]
        if self.individual:
            seasonal_output = torch.zeros(
                [seasonal_init.size(0), seasonal_init.size(1), self.pred_len],
                dtype=seasonal_init.dtype, device=seasonal_init.device)
            trend_output = torch.zeros(
                [trend_init.size(0), trend_init.size(1), self.pred_len],
                dtype=trend_init.dtype, device=trend_init.device)
            for i in range(self.channels):
                seasonal_output[:, i, :] = self.Linear_Seasonal[i](seasonal_init[:, i, :])
                trend_output[:, i, :] = self.Linear_Trend[i](trend_init[:, i, :])
        else:
            seasonal_output = self.Linear_Seasonal(seasonal_init)   # [B, C, T]
            trend_output = self.Linear_Trend(trend_init)            # [B, C, T]
        x = seasonal_output + trend_output                          # recombine the streams
        return x.permute(0, 2, 1)                                    # [B, T, C]
```

Let me trace the causal chain back. I started doubting that the booming attention-based forecasters were doing the temporal work credited to them because their core operation is order-blind before positional information is added while a numeric time series carries its signal almost entirely in order, and because the existing comparisons confound architecture with forecasting strategy. Their reported wins were against iterated baselines that compound error over long horizons, while the Transformer forecasters themselves use direct multi-step prediction. To separate those, I built the most minimal direct multi-step forecaster possible — a single linear map along time, L→T, shared across variates, no cross-channel modeling — reasoning that long-horizon forecasting is only feasible for series with clear trend and periodicity, both of which a linear temporal map captures directly with a length-one signal path from every past to every future step. Running it against distribution shift exposed that a fixed linear map can't recenter a drifting level, which the subtract-last / add-back normalization fixes; running it against trend-plus-seasonality exposed that one weight matrix gets dominated by the loud trend and under-fits the quiet seasonality. Reusing the existing moving-average seasonal-trend decomposition as a parameter-free preprocessing solved that without adding nonlinear representational capacity — it's preconditioning, not depth — by letting a trend linear and a seasonal linear specialize and then summing. The moving average uses replicate-padding to stay faithful at the window edges and the same kernel size as the established decomposition block for a clean comparison. The result is two linear layers over a decomposed window, trained by direct MSE over the whole horizon — an embarrassingly simple model whose entire purpose is to measure how much of the long-term forecasting accuracy the elaborate attention machinery was actually responsible for.
