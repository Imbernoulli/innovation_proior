Let me start from what I actually have and what hurts. I have a big pile of images, i.i.d., and I want a model that can both *make* new images like them and *encode* a given image into a compact code. The shape of model I keep coming back to is the simplest generative story I can tell: draw a latent code `z` from some prior `p(z)`, then paint an image with a neural network, `x ~ p_theta(x|z)`. If I believe in neural networks at all I should believe this is expressive enough — because a fact I trust is that any distribution in `d` dimensions can be obtained by taking `d` independent normals and pushing them through a sufficiently flexible function. Take a 2D Gaussian `z` and map it through `g(z) = z/10 + z/||z||`; the output lies on a ring. So I do not have to hand-design what each latent coordinate means or how they correlate; if `z ~ N(0,I)` and the decoder is deep enough, its early layers can manufacture whatever real latent structure the data needs — digit identity, stroke width, pose — and its later layers can render that into pixels. So I will fix the prior to the most boring thing imaginable, `p(z) = N(0,I)`, parameter-free, and let all the modeling happen in `p_theta(x|z)`. Expressiveness is not my problem.

My problem is learning and inference. To do maximum likelihood on `theta` I need `log p_theta(x)`, and that means the marginal `p_theta(x) = ∫ p(z) p_theta(x|z) dz`. The instant `p_theta(x|z)` is a neural net with a nonlinear hidden layer, that integral is intractable — I cannot evaluate it, let alone differentiate it. And by Bayes the posterior `p_theta(z|x) = p_theta(x|z)p(z)/p_theta(x)` is intractable for the same reason, because it has that same marginal in the denominator. So I cannot do EM either: EM's E-step *is* the posterior, and I do not have it. A per-image sampling loop could route around the posterior, but that gives me a costly inner loop for every datapoint, which is the wrong scale for minibatch image training. Two intractable integrals, the marginal and the posterior, blocking the two things I want.

What do people do when the posterior is intractable? Variational Bayes: replace the true posterior by an approximation `q(z|x)` from some tractable family and optimize a bound. Let me actually derive the bound, because I want to see exactly which pieces are tractable and which are not. For *any* `q(z|x)`, I write `log p_theta(x)` as an expectation under `q` (it does not depend on `z`), then multiply and divide inside the log: `log p_theta(x) = E_{q(z|x)}[ log( p_theta(x,z)/q(z|x) ) ] + E_{q(z|x)}[ log( q(z|x)/p_theta(z|x) ) ]`. The second expectation is `D_KL(q(z|x) || p_theta(z|x))`, and a KL divergence is `>= 0`. So the first expectation, `L(x) = E_{q(z|x)}[ log p_theta(x,z) - log q(z|x) ]`, satisfies `L(x) <= log p_theta(x)` — it is a lower bound on the evidence, and the slack is exactly that posterior KL. I like this a lot, because look at what maximizing `L` over `theta` and over the parameters of `q` does in one shot: since `log p_theta(x) = L(x) + D_KL(q||p_theta(z|x))`, pushing `L` up both raises the marginal likelihood I care about *and*, since `log p_theta(x)` for fixed `theta` is constant, drives the gap `D_KL(q||p_theta(z|x))` down, i.e. makes `q` a better approximation to the true posterior. Two birds. And I never needed the intractable posterior to *write* `L` — it only appears in the slack, which I am happy to leave implicit. Now split `L` the other way, peeling out the prior, because `p_theta(x,z) = p_theta(x|z) p(z)`: `L(x) = E_{q(z|x)}[ log p_theta(x|z) ] - D_KL( q(z|x) || p(z) )`.

This form is the one I want to *stare* at. The first term is the expected log-likelihood of the image given a code — in autoencoder language, a negative reconstruction error: encode `x` into a code distribution `q(z|x)`, draw a code, decode it, and ask how well it reproduces `x`. The second term is the divergence between the encoder's output distribution and the prior — a regularizer pulling `q(z|x)` toward `N(0,I)`. So the variational bound *is*, structurally, a regularized autoencoder, and the regularizer is not an ad-hoc penalty I bolted on (the way sparse or denoising autoencoders need a hand-set sparsity or corruption knob) — it falls out of the probability. That is already a better story than a plain autoencoder, which is known not to learn useful representations from reconstruction alone. And one sign discipline I will hold onto from the start, because it bites later: the *bound* contains `- D_KL`, since I maximize `L`; the *loss* I eventually minimize is `-L`, so it must contain `+ D_KL`. Maximize the negative-divergence, equivalently minimize the positive-divergence.

The question now is whether I can actually *optimize* `L` by gradient methods on a big dataset, and here is where the classical machinery breaks. Mean-field VB would factorize `q` and do coordinate ascent, but each coordinate update needs *analytic* expectations of `log p_theta(x,z)` under `q` — fine for a conjugate exponential family, hopeless the moment `p_theta(x|z)` is a neural network. And mean-field fits a *separate* variational distribution per datapoint, which is a non-starter for a large image set. So I cannot lean on conjugacy. I need to differentiate `L` and run plain stochastic gradient descent on minibatches.

Differentiate `L(x) = E_{q_phi(z|x)}[ log p_theta(x,z) - log q_phi(z|x) ]` with respect to the two parameter sets — call the decoder/generative parameters `theta` and the encoder/variational parameters `phi`. The `theta` gradient is easy: `theta` only appears *inside* the expectation, not in the measure `q_phi`, so I can move the gradient inside and Monte-Carlo it, `∇_theta L = E_{q_phi(z|x)}[ ∇_theta log p_theta(x,z) ] ≈ ∇_theta log p_theta(x,z)` with `z ~ q_phi(z|x)`. The `phi` gradient is the whole difficulty. `phi` appears in the *measure* I am integrating against, `∇_phi E_{q_phi(z|x)}[ f(z) ]` with `f(z) = log p_theta(x,z) - log q_phi(z|x)`. I cannot just swap `∇_phi` and the expectation, because the distribution I am averaging over moves as I change `phi`. This is the knot.

There is a standard trick for exactly this — the score-function estimator (the likelihood-ratio / REINFORCE estimator). Differentiate the integral directly and use `∇_phi q_phi = q_phi ∇_phi log q_phi`, which gives `∇_phi E_{q_phi}[f(z)] = E_{q_phi}[ f(z) ∇_phi log q_phi(z) ] ≈ (1/L) Σ_l f(z^l) ∇_phi log q_phi(z^l)`. It is unbiased, it is model-agnostic, and it even works for discrete `z`. So let me just try to use it. And it does run — but it is brutally noisy, and I want to be precise about *why*, because the reason will tell me what a better estimator must keep. Look at what the estimator actually consumes: it takes the *scalar* `f(z^l) = log p_theta(x,z^l) - log q_phi(z^l|x)` and multiplies it by the score `∇_phi log q_phi(z^l)`. That is all the information about `f` it ever uses — one number per sample. It is completely blind to *how* `f` changes as `z` moves; it never touches `∂f/∂z`. And `∂f/∂z = ∂/∂z [log p_theta(x,z) - log q_phi(z|x)]` is sitting *right there*, free, because backprop through my differentiable decoder and encoder hands it to me. The score estimator is funnelling the entire `phi`-gradient through a single scalar when there is a whole gradient *vector* available. That bottleneck is the variance: each sample's contribution is a noisy scalar times a noisy score, and with the scalar carrying the only signal, I need an enormous number of samples to average out the noise. Control variates help a little, but they cannot put back the directional information the estimator threw away. Wall. I do not want an estimator that ignores the pathwise derivative of my own differentiable network.

So the requirement crystallizes: I want a Monte-Carlo gradient that *flows through* the sampling step — one where the derivative of the integrand with respect to `z`, and then `z` with respect to `phi`, actually appears. The obstruction was that `z` is sampled from a `phi`-dependent distribution, so the randomness sits *between* `phi` and `f`, and I cannot backprop through a "draw a sample" operation; sampling has no gradient. Backprop handles a stochastic *input* fine, but not a stochastic *node in the middle* of the graph.

Then move the stochastic node to the input. What if I do not sample `z` directly from `q_phi(z|x)`, but instead sample some *fixed*, `phi`-independent noise `eps` from a distribution `p(eps)` that has nothing to do with `phi`, and then build `z` as a *deterministic, differentiable* function of that noise and the parameters, `z = g_phi(eps, x)` with `eps ~ p(eps)` independent of `phi`. If I can do that, then the randomness enters only through `eps`, which is a fixed input I draw once; everything downstream — `g_phi`, then `f` — is a deterministic differentiable function of `phi`, so backprop sails straight through. Let me check it is *legitimate*, i.e. that this re-expresses the same expectation. By the change of variables for the transform `z = g_phi(eps,x)`, the measures correspond, `q_phi(z|x) dz = p(eps) deps`, so `E_{q_phi(z|x)}[ f(z) ] = ∫ q_phi(z|x) f(z) dz = ∫ p(eps) f(g_phi(eps,x)) deps = E_{p(eps)}[ f(g_phi(eps,x)) ]`. Same expectation, but now written as an average over a `phi`-free measure. And *that* I can differentiate by pushing `∇_phi` inside: `∇_phi E_{q_phi(z|x)}[ f(z) ] = E_{p(eps)}[ (∂f/∂z)·(∂g_phi/∂phi) ]`. There it is — the `∂f/∂z` factor, the pathwise gradient, the exact thing the score estimator was blind to, now front and centre, multiplied by `∂g_phi/∂phi`. This is going to be far lower variance at the same sample count, because every sample now carries directional information about how to change `phi`, not just a scalar reweighting. The whole maneuver was just: relocate the stochasticity from a mid-graph node to an input node so backprop can cross it.

For which `q_phi` can I find such a `g`? Several families work — anything with a tractable inverse CDF, any location-scale family, or compositions — but the one I want is the cheapest useful one. Let me make `q_phi(z|x)` a diagonal Gaussian, `N(z; mu(x), sigma(x)^2 I)`, where the encoder network outputs the per-image `mu(x)` and `sigma(x)`. Why diagonal Gaussian and not something fancier? Three reasons that all matter for actually running this at scale. Its density is trivial to evaluate and differentiate; it is trivial to *sample* in parallel across latent dimensions, which is exactly what a GPU wants; and — I will use this below — its KL to a Gaussian prior is closed form. It is a simplifying choice, not a limitation of the method. For a Gaussian, the location-scale reparameterization is obvious: `z = mu + sigma ⊙ eps`, `eps ~ N(0, I)`, with `⊙` elementwise. `eps` is drawn from a fixed `N(0,I)`, and `z` is a smooth function of `mu`, `sigma`, and `eps`, so gradients flow into the encoder. One immediate sanity check on legitimacy: `∂z/∂eps = diag(sigma)`, a diagonal Jacobian, whose determinant is `∏_j sigma_j`, which is exactly what turns `p(eps)` into `q_phi(z|x)` under the change of variables — the transform is consistent, the densities line up. In code it is steadier to store `logvar = log sigma^2`, compute `std = exp(0.5 · logvar)`, and form `z = mean + std · eps`, which is precisely the operation the bottleneck distribution already performs when it draws a sample. So the encoding sample is handed to me; I do not resample inside the loss.

Note this trick *requires* a continuous, reparameterizable `q`. If `z` were discrete, then for fixed `eps` the map `g_phi(eps,x)` would have to jump between sample values as `phi` varies — a discontinuity, no gradient. So I am implicitly choosing the continuous-latent regime, which is fine: my codes are continuous. (Wake-sleep and the score estimator keep the discrete case; I am trading that generality for low variance, which is the right trade for images.)

Now the diagonal Gaussian also gives me the prior divergence without any Monte-Carlo noise, so let me compute it, because I want the exact term, not a hand-wave at "analytic." I need the negative-KL piece of the bound, `-D_KL = ∫ q(z)(log p(z) - log q(z)) dz`, for `q = N(mu, sigma^2 I)`, `p = N(0,I)`, latent dimension `J`. First, `∫ N(z;mu,sigma^2) log N(z;0,I) dz`: with `log N(z;0,I) = -(J/2)log(2π) - (1/2)Σ_j z_j^2` and `E_q[z_j^2] = mu_j^2 + sigma_j^2`, this is `-(J/2)log(2π) - (1/2)Σ_j (mu_j^2 + sigma_j^2)`. Second, the expected log-density of `q` itself: `log N(z;mu,sigma^2) = -(J/2)log(2π) - (1/2)Σ_j log sigma_j^2 - (1/2)Σ_j (z_j-mu_j)^2/sigma_j^2`, and `E_q[(z_j-mu_j)^2] = sigma_j^2` so the last sum averages to `(1/2)Σ_j 1 = J/2`, giving `-(J/2)log(2π) - (1/2)Σ_j (1 + log sigma_j^2)`. Subtract the second from the first; the `-(J/2)log(2π)` cancels, and `-D_KL(q||p) = (1/2) Σ_{j=1}^J ( 1 + log(sigma_j^2) - mu_j^2 - sigma_j^2 )`. This expression is never positive, and it reaches zero exactly at `mu_j = 0, sigma_j = 1` — the prior — as it must. Holding my sign discipline: the term in the *maximized bound* is this negative-KL; the term I *add* to the *minimized loss* is its negation, `D_KL(q||p) = (1/2) Σ_j ( mu_j^2 + sigma_j^2 - 1 - log sigma_j^2 )`. Rewritten with `logvar = log sigma^2`, that positive KL is `0.5 Σ_j ( mu_j^2 + exp(logvar_j) - 1 - logvar_j )`, which is exactly the per-sample quantity the bottleneck's `kl()` returns, summed over the latent channel and spatial dimensions for each image. So I will *add* `posterior.kl().mean()` to the loss — never subtract it there; the subtraction is the bound convention, the addition is the negative-bound (loss) convention.

Now the reconstruction term `log p_theta(x|z)`, and the choice of decoder likelihood is more consequential than it looks. For real-valued pixels the natural choice is Gaussian, `p_theta(x|z) = N(x; f_theta(z), sigma_x^2 I)`, with the decoder outputting the mean image `f_theta(z)`. Then `log p_theta(x|z) = -(D/2)log(2π sigma_x^2) - (1/(2 sigma_x^2)) Σ_d (x_d - f_theta(z)_d)^2`. The additive constant does not depend on the decoder, so maximizing this likelihood is *exactly* minimizing the squared (Euclidean, `L2`) reconstruction error between the decoded image and the target — the per-pixel mean-squared error. This is the "L2" in the loss: it is not a heuristic distance I picked, it is the negative log-likelihood of a fixed-variance Gaussian decoder. (A Laplace likelihood instead gives an absolute-error `L1` term, which tends to be sharper because it penalizes large residuals less; for binary pixels a Bernoulli likelihood gives a cross-entropy term and there is no free variance to set.)

And now look at where `sigma_x^2` sits. It multiplies *only* the reconstruction term, not the KL. So the full per-datapoint quantity to *minimize* (negative ELBO, constants dropped) is `(1/(2 sigma_x^2)) Σ_d (x_d - f_theta(z)_d)^2 + D_KL( q_phi(z|x) || p(z) )`, and `sigma_x^2` is precisely the knob weighing reconstruction against the prior regularizer. Something I should not gloss over: for continuous data this knob is *necessary*, not optional. Each continuous sample carries, in principle, infinite information, so without a scale the reconstruction and KL terms are in incommensurable units; `sigma_x^2` fixes how many bits I demand the reconstruction be accurate to, and only then does the trade-off make sense. (For Bernoulli/binary data both terms are honestly in bits and no such knob exists.) I should also notice what does *not* give me a free knob: rescaling the prior to `N(0, λI)` looks like it should, but it can be absorbed by reparameterizing `f`, `mu`, `sigma`, so it leaves the objective unchanged — the only real trade-off is the decoder variance. There is one more subtlety the implementation forces on me. The exact Gaussian NLL is a per-image *sum* of squared errors, but the convenient `mse_loss` averages over batch, channels, height, and width. Averaging over `D` pixels rescales the reconstruction term by `1/D` relative to the per-image-sum form, so the version I actually code, `mean_pixel_MSE + beta · mean_batch_KL`, equals the exact negative bound up to one positive global factor when `beta = 2 sigma_x^2 / D`. Practically, then, `beta` (the `kl_weight`) is best read as the *effective* KL coefficient under the chosen reduction, not as the raw decoder variance; and for a task whose whole goal is reconstruction quality I want it *small* — on the order of `1e-6` — so the objective is overwhelmingly reconstruction-driven, with the KL present mainly to keep the latent code well-behaved and near the prior rather than to compress hard.

How many noise samples `L` per datapoint? In principle the estimator averages over `L` draws of `eps`. But I am already doing stochastic gradient descent over a *minibatch* of images, so each step already averages over many `(x, eps)` pairs. As long as the minibatch is reasonably large — on the order of a hundred or more — a single `eps` per image (`L = 1`) is enough; the minibatch averaging supplies the rest, and the reparameterized estimator is low-variance to begin with. So the lower-bound estimator is `L̃(x) = -D_KL(q_phi(z|x) || p(z)) + (1/L) Σ_l log p_theta(x|z_l)` with `z_l = mu + sigma ⊙ eps_l`, `eps_l ~ N(0,I)`, `L = 1`; and the loss is its negative, constants dropped and the reduction applied, `loss = reconstruction_MSE + beta · D_KL(q_phi(z|x) || N(0,I))`.

Step back and check the gradient flows where I need it. The reconstruction term receives `recon`, the output of the fixed encode-sample-decode path, so `∇` flows through the decoder and *back through the sampled latent* into the encoder precisely because the sample was `mean + std ⊙ eps` — the reparameterization is what makes that path differentiable, carrying `∂ log p_theta/∂z`, the pathwise signal. The KL term is a closed-form differentiable function of `mean` and `logvar`, so its gradient also flows into the encoder. So one backward pass on one scalar objective updates both networks jointly. That is the thing wake-sleep could not do: there the two networks were trained on two phases that did not jointly bound `log p(x)`, so there was no guarantee the updates were consistent; here both `theta` and `phi` are moving uphill on the *same* lower bound, by construction. And it is the thing per-datapoint MCMC could not do at scale: no inner sampling loop per image, just one feedforward encode and one feedforward decode, because the recognition network *amortizes* inference — one shared set of weights predicts the posterior parameters for any image in a single pass. I never need the intractable marginal, the true posterior, or an MCMC chain.

One consistency check before I write it. If I strip the nonlinearity — linear decoder `p(x|z) = N(Wz, sigma_x^2 I)`, Gaussian prior — and push `sigma_x^2 -> 0`, the maximum-likelihood solution of this model is exactly PCA. So my reconstruction-plus-Gaussian-latent model is, in the linear limit, a known generative model in disguise; what I have built is the nonlinear, amortized, sampled generalization of that, with the KL term standing in for the latent prior that PCA's linear-Gaussian structure made implicit. That degeneration to something I already trust makes me believe the construction.

So let me write the training objective as code, filling the one empty slot in the harness. The harness has already encoded the image into a `posterior` (a diagonal-Gaussian bottleneck), drawn the code via the reparameterization inside `posterior.sample()`, and decoded it into `recon`; it also exposes `posterior.kl()` returning the per-sample positive `0.5 Σ_j (mu_j^2 + sigma_j^2 - 1 - log sigma_j^2)` I derived. So all my objective has to compute is the two terms of the negative ELBO — the squared-error reconstruction (Gaussian-decoder NLL up to a constant) plus the KL weighted by the small `beta` — and it must *not* resample, since the sample is already baked into `recon`:

```python
import torch.nn as nn
import torch.nn.functional as F


class VAELoss(nn.Module):
    """Negative ELBO for a KL-regularized autoencoder (the VAE objective):
    a per-pixel reconstruction term (Gaussian-decoder NLL = mean squared error;
    the L1 form is the Laplace-likelihood sibling) plus the closed-form KL of the
    diagonal-Gaussian posterior to the N(0, I) prior."""

    def __init__(self, device):
        super().__init__()
        # beta weights KL vs reconstruction; under the mean-reduction below it is the
        # effective KL coefficient (= 2*sigma_x^2 / D for the exact NLL). Small for a
        # reconstruction-driven task: the KL just keeps the code near the prior.
        self.kl_weight = 1e-6

    def forward(self, recon, target, posterior, step):
        # -log p_theta(x|z) up to a constant: fixed-variance Gaussian decoder -> squared
        # (L2) error. mse_loss averages over batch and pixels (the reduction folded into
        # beta). (Laplace decoder -> F.l1_loss; sharper.) recon already used one
        # reparameterized sample z = mean + std*eps, so do not resample here.
        rec_loss = F.mse_loss(recon, target)
        # + D_KL( q_phi(z|x) || N(0,I) ) = 0.5*sum_j (mu_j^2 + sigma_j^2 - 1 - log sigma_j^2),
        # averaged over the minibatch. ADD it (loss = -bound), never subtract.
        kl_loss = posterior.kl().mean()
        # negative ELBO; minimizing it maximizes the variational lower bound.
        loss = rec_loss + self.kl_weight * kl_loss
        return loss, {"rec_loss": rec_loss.item(), "kl_loss": kl_loss.item()}
```

The causal chain, end to end. I wanted a deep directed model `z ~ N(0,I)`, `x ~ p_theta(x|z)` that I could both learn and invert at scale, but the marginal and the posterior are both intractable for a neural likelihood, which kills EM and the conjugate mean-field machinery and makes per-datapoint MCMC too slow. The variational lower bound `L = E_q[log p(x|z)] - D_KL(q(z|x)||p(z))` rescued the formulation — maximizing it raises the likelihood and tightens the posterior approximation at once, and it never needs the intractable pieces — but I still had to differentiate an expectation whose measure depends on the encoder parameters. The score-function estimator does that unbiasedly yet throws away the pathwise gradient my own differentiable decoder hands me for free, so it is far too noisy. Relocating the randomness into a fixed input, `z = mu + sigma ⊙ eps` with `eps ~ N(0,I)`, made the whole graph differentiable and let the low-variance pathwise gradient through. A diagonal-Gaussian encoder gave a cheap, parallel, closed-form negative-KL `(1/2)Σ_j(1 + log sigma_j^2 - mu_j^2 - sigma_j^2)` — whose negation is the positive KL I add to the loss; a fixed-variance Gaussian decoder turned the reconstruction term into squared error, with the decoder variance `sigma_x^2` (folded with the mean-reduction into `beta`) reappearing as the single knob that weights reconstruction against the prior, small for a reconstruction-driven task. With `L = 1` sample inside a large minibatch, the whole thing is one feedforward encode, one decode, one backward pass, jointly training the amortized encoder and the decoder on one objective by ordinary SGD — exactly what wake-sleep and per-datapoint MCMC could not deliver. The final loss is the negative ELBO: reconstruction (`L2`/MSE, the Laplace `L1` sibling sharper) plus a small-weight KL.
