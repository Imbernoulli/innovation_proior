I have a large i.i.d. collection of images and want a single model that can learn to generate images like them, encode any new image into a compact code quickly, and reconstruct from codes. The natural shape is the simplest generative story: draw a latent $z$ from a prior $p(z)$, then paint pixels with a neural decoder $x \sim p_\theta(x\mid z)$. Expressiveness is not my worry — any distribution can be obtained by pushing independent normals through a flexible enough map, so I fix the prior to the most boring thing imaginable, $p(z) = \mathcal{N}(0, I)$, parameter-free, and let the decoder manufacture whatever latent structure the data needs. My problem is learning and inference. Maximum likelihood needs the marginal $p_\theta(x) = \int p(z)\, p_\theta(x\mid z)\, dz$, and the instant the decoder is a nonlinear network that integral is intractable; by Bayes the posterior $p_\theta(z\mid x) = p_\theta(x\mid z)\,p(z)/p_\theta(x)$ carries the same intractable marginal in its denominator. So EM is out — its E-step *is* the posterior I do not have. Conjugate mean-field variational Bayes is out too: its coordinate updates need analytic expectations of the complete-data log density, which a neural decoder destroys, and it fits a separate variational vector per datapoint, which does not scale to a large image set. Per-datapoint MCMC can route around the posterior but pays a costly inner sampling loop for every image, the wrong scale for minibatch training. And the score-function (REINFORCE) estimator, which would let me differentiate an expectation over a parameter-dependent measure, is unbiased but brutally noisy here. Wake-sleep gives the right computational shape — a recognition network for fast coding — but trains its two networks on two different criteria that do not jointly bound $\log p_\theta(x)$, so the encoder and decoder updates can point at different targets.

What I propose is the variational autoencoder (VAE) objective: the negative evidence lower bound of this deep directed latent-variable model, optimized with the reparameterized gradient estimator. The starting point is a variational lower bound. For any tractable approximate posterior $q_\phi(z\mid x)$, writing $\log p_\theta(x)$ as an expectation under $q$ and multiplying and dividing inside the log splits it as $\log p_\theta(x) = \mathcal{L}(x) + D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\big(q_\phi(z\mid x)\,\|\,p_\theta(z\mid x)\big)$, where the KL slack is nonnegative, so $$\mathcal{L}(x) = \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z\mid x)}\!\big[\log p_\theta(x\mid z)\big] - D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\big(q_\phi(z\mid x)\,\|\,p(z)\big) \le \log p_\theta(x).$$ Maximizing $\mathcal{L}$ does two things at once: it raises the marginal likelihood I care about and, since $\log p_\theta(x)$ is fixed in $\phi$, it drives down the gap $D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q\,\|\,p_\theta(z\mid x))$, making $q$ a better posterior approximation — and the intractable true posterior appears only in that slack, never in $\mathcal{L}$ itself. Structurally this bound *is* a regularized autoencoder: the first term is the expected log-likelihood of the image given its code (a negative reconstruction error), the second is a divergence pulling the encoder's output toward the prior — a regularizer that falls out of the probability rather than a hand-set sparsity or corruption knob. One sign discipline I hold throughout: the *bound* I maximize contains $-D_{\mathrm{KL}}$, so the *loss* I minimize, $-\mathcal{L}$, contains $+D_{\mathrm{KL}}$.

The difficulty is optimizing $\mathcal{L}$ by stochastic gradients. The $\theta$ gradient is easy because $\theta$ lives only inside the expectation, so I move $\nabla_\theta$ in and Monte-Carlo it. The $\phi$ gradient is the knot: $\phi$ parameterizes the *measure* $q_\phi$ I am integrating against, and I cannot swap $\nabla_\phi$ with the expectation because the distribution itself moves. The score-function estimator $\nabla_\phi \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi}[f(z)] = \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi}[f(z)\,\nabla_\phi \log q_\phi(z)]$ handles this unbiasedly, but it is blind to how $f$ changes as $z$ moves — it funnels the whole $\phi$-gradient through a single scalar $f(z)$ per sample and never touches $\partial f/\partial z$, even though backprop through my own differentiable decoder hands me that derivative for free. That discarded directional information is exactly its variance. What makes the VAE work is the reparameterization that puts it back. Instead of sampling $z$ directly from the $\phi$-dependent $q_\phi$, I draw fixed, $\phi$-independent noise $\varepsilon \sim p(\varepsilon)$ and build $z = g_\phi(\varepsilon, x)$ as a deterministic differentiable function. By the change of variables $q_\phi(z\mid x)\,dz = p(\varepsilon)\,d\varepsilon$, the expectation is unchanged, $\mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z\mid x)}[f(z)] = \mathbb{E}_{p(\varepsilon)}[f(g_\phi(\varepsilon,x))]$, but now the measure is $\phi$-free, so $\nabla_\phi$ goes inside and yields $\mathbb{E}_{p(\varepsilon)}[(\partial f/\partial z)(\partial g_\phi/\partial \phi)]$ — the pathwise gradient $\partial f/\partial z$ the score estimator threw away, now front and centre, every sample carrying a direction rather than a scalar reweighting. The maneuver simply relocates the stochasticity from a mid-graph node, which backprop cannot cross, to an input node, which it can. This needs a continuous, reparameterizable $q$: for discrete $z$ the map would have to jump between sample values as $\phi$ varies, killing the gradient. That is the right trade for continuous image codes.

I take $q_\phi(z\mid x) = \mathcal{N}(\mu(x), \sigma(x)^2 I)$, a diagonal Gaussian whose per-image mean and variance the encoder outputs — chosen because its density is trivial to evaluate and differentiate, it samples in parallel across latent dimensions the way a GPU wants, and its KL to a Gaussian prior is closed form. Its reparameterization is location-scale, $$z = \mu + \sigma \odot \varepsilon, \qquad \varepsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I),$$ with diagonal Jacobian $\partial z/\partial \varepsilon = \mathrm{diag}(\sigma)$ whose determinant $\prod_j \sigma_j$ is exactly what converts $p(\varepsilon)$ into $q_\phi(z\mid x)$ — the transform is consistent. In code this is $\texttt{std} = \exp(0.5\,\texttt{logvar})$, $z = \texttt{mean} + \texttt{std}\cdot\varepsilon$, precisely what the bottleneck's $\texttt{sample()}$ already performs, so the loss never resamples. The diagonal Gaussian also gives the prior divergence with no Monte-Carlo noise. Integrating the two Gaussian log-densities under $q$, with $\mathbb{E}_q[z_j^2] = \mu_j^2 + \sigma_j^2$ and $\mathbb{E}_q[(z_j-\mu_j)^2] = \sigma_j^2$, the $-(J/2)\log(2\pi)$ terms cancel and the negative-KL piece of the bound is $$-D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q\,\|\,p) = \tfrac{1}{2}\sum_{j=1}^J \big(1 + \log \sigma_j^2 - \mu_j^2 - \sigma_j^2\big),$$ never positive, zero exactly at $\mu_j=0,\ \sigma_j=1$ as it must be. Negating it for the loss and writing $\texttt{logvar} = \log\sigma^2$ gives the positive KL I *add*, $\tfrac{1}{2}\sum_j(\mu_j^2 + \exp(\texttt{logvar}_j) - 1 - \texttt{logvar}_j)$, exactly the per-sample quantity $\texttt{posterior.kl()}$ returns.

The reconstruction term and the decoder likelihood are more consequential than they look. For real-valued pixels I take a fixed-variance Gaussian decoder $p_\theta(x\mid z) = \mathcal{N}(x; f_\theta(z), \sigma_x^2 I)$, so $$\log p_\theta(x\mid z) = C - \frac{1}{2\sigma_x^2}\sum_d \big(x_d - f_\theta(z)_d\big)^2.$$ The constant does not depend on the decoder, so maximizing this likelihood is *exactly* minimizing squared (L2) reconstruction error — the "L2" is the negative log-likelihood of a Gaussian decoder, not a heuristic distance. (A Laplace likelihood would give an L1/absolute-error term, sharper because it penalizes large residuals less; a Bernoulli likelihood for binary pixels gives cross-entropy with no free variance.) Crucially, $\sigma_x^2$ multiplies only the reconstruction term, so the per-datapoint quantity to minimize is $\frac{1}{2\sigma_x^2}\sum_d (x_d - f_\theta(z)_d)^2 + D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\phi(z\mid x)\,\|\,p(z))$, and $\sigma_x^2$ is the single knob weighing reconstruction against the prior regularizer. For continuous data this knob is necessary, not optional — without a scale the two terms are in incommensurable units; rescaling the prior to $\mathcal{N}(0,\lambda I)$ does *not* give an independent knob because it can be absorbed by reparameterizing $f, \mu, \sigma$. The implementation forces one more subtlety: the exact NLL is a per-image *sum* over $D$ pixels, but $\texttt{F.mse\_loss}$ *averages* over batch and pixels, rescaling the reconstruction term by $1/D$. So $\text{MSE} + \beta\cdot\text{KL}$ equals the exact negative bound up to a positive global factor when $\beta = 2\sigma_x^2/D$; practically $\beta$ (the $\texttt{kl\_weight}$) is the effective KL coefficient under this reduction, and for a reconstruction-driven task I want it *small*, on the order of $10^{-6}$, so the objective is overwhelmingly reconstruction-driven with the KL present mainly to keep the latent code near the prior. One noise sample $L=1$ per image suffices because the large minibatch already averages over many $(x,\varepsilon)$ pairs and the reparameterized estimator is low-variance to begin with.

Putting it together, the loss is the negative ELBO with the reduction applied: reconstruction MSE plus the small-weight closed-form KL. The reconstruction gradient flows through the decoder and *back through the sampled latent* into the encoder precisely because the sample was $\mu + \sigma\odot\varepsilon$, carrying the pathwise $\partial \log p_\theta/\partial z$; the KL gradient flows into the encoder as a closed-form function of $\mu$ and $\texttt{logvar}$. So one backward pass on one scalar objective trains both the amortized encoder and the decoder jointly on the same lower bound — the consistency wake-sleep lacked — with no inner MCMC loop per image, just one feedforward encode and one decode. A reassuring degeneration: strip the nonlinearity to a linear decoder $\mathcal{N}(Wz, \sigma_x^2 I)$ and push $\sigma_x^2 \to 0$, and the maximum-likelihood solution is exactly PCA, so this is the nonlinear, amortized, sampled generalization of a model I already trust. Filling the one empty slot in the harness, which has already encoded, sampled, and decoded into $\texttt{recon}$:

```python
import torch.nn as nn
import torch.nn.functional as F


class VAELoss(nn.Module):
    """Negative ELBO for the fixed Gaussian-bottleneck reconstruction harness."""

    def __init__(self, device):
        super().__init__()
        self.kl_weight = 1e-6

    def forward(self, recon, target, posterior, step):
        rec_loss = F.mse_loss(recon, target)
        kl_loss = posterior.kl().mean()
        loss = rec_loss + self.kl_weight * kl_loss
        return loss, {
            "loss": loss.detach().item(),
            "rec_loss": rec_loss.detach().item(),
            "kl_loss": kl_loss.detach().item(),
        }
```
