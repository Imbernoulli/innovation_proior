Let me start from where the cost actually sits. In a decoder block I have two sublayers, attention and the position-wise feed-forward network, and the FFN is the heavier of the two by parameter count — it projects up to a hidden width of 4d, applies a pointwise nonlinearity, and projects back down, so its two matrices alone are 8d^2 parameters against attention's 4d^2. Everyone pours effort into attention. The FFN, meanwhile, is `act(x W1) W2` and the only thing in it that isn't a plain linear map is that one scalar function `act`, which I inherited without thinking — ReLU because the original Transformer used it, or GELU because BERT and GPT switched to it and the codebase followed. So if I want a cheap win in language-model pretraining, the most under-examined knob in the whole architecture is that single pointwise function. And the constraint I want to respect is strict: change only `act`, keep the `(B,T,d)->(B,T,d)` shape, don't touch attention or normalization or the optimizer or the data, and ideally add no parameters and no new hyperparameter, so the change is something anyone can drop into an existing codebase and just run. The question is whether the activation that got inherited is actually the best one for this slot, and I have no reason to believe it is.

So what are my options today, and what does each really do. ReLU is `max(0, z)`: kill the negatives, pass the positives through unchanged. The killing-negatives part is doing real work — it makes the layer sparse, only some units fire — but stare at the positive branch and it's literally the identity, `z`. So the FFN's entire nonlinearity is the hard gate at the origin; above zero it's a linear map, and a unit that's strongly on is treated on exactly the same linear scale as a unit that's barely on. The function is piecewise linear with a sharp kink. GELU smooths that kink — `z·Phi(z)` where `Phi` is the Gaussian CDF, derived as the expected value of `z` times a Bernoulli(`Phi(z)`) keep-mask, a deterministic stand-in for input-dependent dropout — and it adds a gentle dip just below zero. It's nicer to optimize. But what is GELU doing for a *strongly* activated unit? `Phi(z) -> 1` as `z` grows, so `GELU(z) -> z`: asymptotically it's the identity too. Swish, `z·sigmoid(beta z)`, came out of an actual reinforcement-learning search over scalar activation functions, which already tells me searching for `act` is worth it — but its winner is self-gated, smooth, and again `sigmoid(beta z) -> 1`, so `Swish(z) -> z` for large positive `z`. Every activation that's displaced ReLU lives in the same family: smooth around zero, and *asymptotically linear* on the positive side. They differ in how gently they treat the region near the origin and agree completely on what to do with a big positive signal — pass it through roughly unchanged. The unexamined assumption baked into all of them is that the positive branch should be (close to) linear.

That assumption is exactly the thing I should poke. Why should the positive branch be linear? When a unit is strongly activated, that's the network expressing high confidence in some feature; a linear positive branch says "twice the evidence, twice the output," which is fine, but it gives me no way to make a few strongly-firing units *dominate* the layer's output more than their linear share. If instead the positive branch grew faster than linearly, the loud units would pull ahead of the quiet ones, sharpening the layer into a more selective, more contrastive map — and the negatives are still killed, so I keep the sparsity. Is there any precedent for a faster-than-linear activation actually helping, or am I just speculating? There is, and it's an old one I half-remember from associative memory. Krotov and Hopfield studied dense associative memories with a rectified polynomial energy, `F(x) = x^n` for `x >= 0` and `0` for `x < 0`. The `n = 2` case is the standard quadratic Hopfield network; and the interesting claim is that as you crank `n` up, each term in the energy gets *sharper*, and a sharper energy can pack more memories into the same number of neurons — they work out a capacity `K^max = alpha_n N^{n-1}` that grows with `n`. They even pose the question I'm circling around in so many words: above the threshold, should the activation grow linearly, sub-linearly, or faster than linearly, and is there something better than ReLU? So the idea that a rectified, faster-than-linear nonlinearity has a real upside isn't mine — it's sitting in the energy-based-memory literature, where "spikier above threshold" buys capacity. The catch is that it lives in shallow associative-memory models and on toy classification, never in a deep Transformer FFN. Nobody has tried dropping a rectified polynomial into this slot. That gap is my opening.

Now, which polynomial. The Krotov-Hopfield family is `max(0, z)^n`. If "faster than linear" is the lever, I want the *smallest* step beyond linear, because I'm about to stack this thing through dozens of layers and I want to perturb the known-good architecture as little as possible. Linear is `n = 1`, which is just ReLU. The next integer is `n = 2`: `act(z) = max(0, z)^2`. That's the minimal faster-than-linear rectified polynomial — the candidate, if it survives a few angles of attack.

First, do I really need the rectification, or could I just square? Tempting to say `act(z) = z^2` and skip the `max`. But `z^2` is even — it forgets the sign of `z` entirely, so a strongly negative pre-activation and a strongly positive one produce the identical large output. That throws away the gating that made ReLU useful in the first place: the whole point was that negative pre-activations mean "this feature is absent, output nothing." A raw square turns "absent" into "very present," which is backwards, and it also makes the function symmetric and non-monotonic in a way that destroys the sparsity. So I can't square the raw pre-activation. I need to rectify first — zero out the negatives so off units stay off — and *then* square the surviving positive part. That's exactly `max(0, z)^2`, the `n = 2` rectified polynomial, not the bare parabola. Good: the rectification and the squaring are both load-bearing, and in that order.

Second, what does this function look like, precisely, so I'm not fooling myself about the asymptotics. For `z <= 0` it's flat at `0`. For `z > 0` it's `z^2`. Its derivative is `0` for `z < 0` and `2z` for `z > 0`, so it's continuous in value at the origin (both sides give 0) and, unlike ReLU, its derivative is *also* continuous at the origin — `2z -> 0` as `z -> 0+` matches the `0` from the left, so there's no hard kink in the slope, the function leaves zero smoothly with zero initial slope and then accelerates. Compare the growth: ReLU and GELU and Swish all satisfy `act(z)/z -> 1` as `z -> +inf`, they're asymptotically the identity. Here `act(z)/z = z -> +inf`: the function grows *quadratically*, without bound relative to the input. That is a genuinely different growth regime from everything in current use, not a cosmetic reshaping near zero. So a unit that's twice as activated produces four times the output, not twice — strongly-firing units dominate quadratically, which is precisely the "sharpen the layer, let the loud units pull ahead" behavior I was reaching for, and it's the deep-network analogue of the "spikier activation packs more capacity" story from the memory model.

Third, is `max(0, z)^2` secretly something the field already found valuable? If it is, I get its benefits without paying its costs. The other thing people put in this FFN slot is the GLU family. A Gated Linear Unit replaces one projection with a product of two: Dauphin's `(xW) ⊗ sigmoid(xV)`, with a bilinear variant `(xW) ⊗ (xV)` that drops the sigmoid, and Shazeer's Transformer version puts these in the FFN as `ReGLU(x) = max(0, xW) ⊗ xV`, plus `GEGLU` and `SwiGLU` for GELU/Swish gates. The reason GLU was supposed to help, per Dauphin, is gradient flow: the product `a ⊗ sigma(b)` has a linear path `a ⊗ sigma(b)` whose gradient `∇a ⊗ sigma(b)` isn't squashed by a saturating derivative the way a `tanh` gate's would be, so signal survives through depth. And separately there's the representational argument — a product of two learned quantities, a multiplicative interaction, strictly enlarges the function class a layer can express versus a purely additive one. ReGLU is the ReLU-gated member of that family. So let me write ReGLU out componentwise and compare it to my candidate. ReGLU's `i`-th hidden unit, before the down-projection, is `max(0, (xW)_i) · (xV)_i` — the ReLU-gated value of one projection times the raw value of a second, independent projection.

What happens to ReGLU if the two projections are forced to be the *same*, `V = W`? Then unit `i` is `max(0, (xW)_i) · (xW)_i`. Let me call the shared pre-activation `z = (xW)_i` and just check the two cases. If `z > 0`: `max(0, z) = z`, so the product is `z · z = z^2`. If `z <= 0`: `max(0, z) = 0`, so the product is `0 · z = 0`. Put them together: `max(0, z) · z = z^2` for `z > 0` and `0` for `z <= 0`, which is exactly `max(0, z)^2`. They're identical, for every `z`. So `max(0, z)^2 = max(0, z) · z`, and that means *squared ReLU applied after a linear projection `W` is precisely ReGLU with its two weight matrices tied to the same `W`*. The gate and the value are the same projection. My candidate isn't a separate idea from GLU — it's the tied-weight, single-matrix collapse of ReGLU. It inherits the GLU benefits: it's a genuine multiplicative interaction (the product of two copies of the pre-activation, the cheapest possible degree-2 term), and it has the same un-squashed linear path Dauphin cared about, because on the positive branch the "gate" `max(0, z)` is just `z`, an identity that doesn't saturate, so its derivative `2z` never vanishes from saturation the way a sigmoid gate's does. That's the same gradient-flow argument GLU made, except I'm getting it for free.

For free is the right phrase, and it's the whole reason this is better than the GLU variants rather than merely equivalent. ReGLU, GEGLU, SwiGLU each use *three* weight matrices — the gate projection `W`, the value projection `V`, and the down-projection `W2` — whereas the plain FFN uses two. To keep parameters and FLOPs matched against the two-matrix baseline, the GLU papers have to shrink the hidden width `d_ff` by a factor of two-thirds to pay for the extra matrix, which is an extra knob and an extra thing that can go wrong. Tying `V = W` collapses the gate and the value into one projection, so I'm back to *two* matrices — `c_fc` and `c_proj` — and I can keep the full 4d hidden width untouched. No third matrix, no width reduction, no new hyperparameter. I capture the multiplicative-gating benefit of ReGLU and the faster-than-linear sharpening of the rectified polynomial simultaneously, with the exact parameter count and shape of the activation I'm replacing. And it's *simpler* than all of them: no `erf`, no `exp`, no `sigmoid`, no second projection — just a rectify and a square, two cheap elementwise ops, cheaper per element than GELU or Swish which each need a transcendental.

Let me also be honest about why I stop at `n = 2` and don't chase the higher rectified polynomials the memory model says are even "sharper." Higher `n` does grow faster and would dominate even harder, but `max(0, z)^n` for larger `n` is numerically meaner — a moderately large activation raised to the fourth or sixth power overflows in low precision, and very small positive activations get crushed to zero, so the dynamic range gets ugly fast through a deep stack trained in bf16. The `n = 2` member is the minimal jump out of the linear-asymptote family, it's the one that coincides exactly with tied-weight ReGLU (the cubic and up don't have that clean single-product interpretation — they're products of three or more copies, a different and less-motivated object), and the squaring is the cheapest, most stable faster-than-linear choice. So `n = 2` isn't a compromise; it's the sweet spot where the asymptotic argument, the ReGLU-equivalence, and numerical stability all point at the same function. If I later wanted more I'd reach for `n = 3`, but I'd be trading stability for sharpness, and there's no reason to do that before I've even validated the cheap version.

One more design point to nail down so I don't leave a hole: where does the squaring sit relative to the rest of the FFN. It goes exactly where the old activation went — between the up-projection and the down-projection, applied pointwise to the 4d hidden state. The up-projection `c_fc` plays the role of `W` (it produces the pre-activation `z = xW` that gets both rectified and squared), and `c_proj` is the down-projection `W2`. Dropout, if the codebase has it, sits after the activation as before; the change is orthogonal to it. Bias terms I just inherit from whatever the harness uses — the activation swap doesn't care. So this really is a one-line edit to the forward pass: replace the inherited `act` with rectify-then-square.

Let me write it into the FFN, filling the single empty slot in the sublayer:

```python
import torch
import torch.nn as nn
import torch.nn.functional as F


class MLP(nn.Module):
    """Position-wise FFN with a squared-ReLU activation.
    Up-project to 4x width, apply max(0, z)^2 pointwise, project back down.
    Two matrices, full 4x width, no extra parameters vs a ReLU/GELU FFN."""

    def __init__(self, config):
        super().__init__()
        self.c_fc = nn.Linear(config.n_embd, 4 * config.n_embd, bias=config.bias)
        self.c_proj = nn.Linear(4 * config.n_embd, config.n_embd, bias=config.bias)
        self.dropout = nn.Dropout(config.dropout)

    def forward(self, x):
        x = self.c_fc(x)            # z = x W1 : up-projection to the 4x hidden state
        x = F.relu(x).square()      # max(0, z)^2 : rectify (keep sparsity), then square
                                    #   (= tied-weight ReGLU max(0,z)*z; quadratic positive branch)
        x = self.c_proj(x)          # down-projection back to n_embd
        x = self.dropout(x)
        return x
```

So the causal chain. I started from the observation that the FFN is the heavy sublayer and its one pointwise nonlinearity is the part of the Transformer nobody re-examines — inherited, not chosen. Looking hard at the incumbents, ReLU's positive branch is the bare identity and GELU and Swish, for all their smoothness near zero, are *also* asymptotically linear on the positive side; they all silently assume the positive branch should be roughly linear. Questioning that assumption pointed at faster-than-linear activations, for which the dense-associative-memory line already gives a reason — a sharper, faster-growing rectified nonlinearity packs more capacity — but only the smallest step beyond linear is safe to stack deep, so the `n = 2` rectified polynomial `max(0, z)^2`. Checking it can't be done by squaring the raw input (that's even and kills the gating); the rectify-then-square order is forced. Working out the function shows a genuinely different, quadratic growth regime, with a derivative `2z` that leaves the origin smoothly and never saturates. Then the unification: `max(0, z)^2 = max(0, z) · z` is exactly ReGLU with its two projections tied to one weight matrix, so this is the single-matrix collapse of the gated-linear-unit family — it inherits GLU's multiplicative-interaction expressivity and un-squashed gradient path, but with two matrices instead of three, the full 4x width instead of a 2/3-shrunk one, no extra parameters, no new hyperparameter, and no transcendental to evaluate. It drops into the existing FFN as a one-line replacement of the inherited activation: `x = F.relu(x).square()`.
