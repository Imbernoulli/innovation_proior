# Antecedents (load-bearing prior art, grounded this run)

## 1. The static-gene / beads-on-a-string dogma (the thing she reacts against)
Sturtevant 1913: genes lie in fixed linear order on the chromosome, each at a fixed locus ("beads on a string"). Morgan-school orthodoxy through the 1940s: a gene is an indivisible unit at a stable address; the chromosome is a static structure; heritable change = rare mutation of the bead itself. Limitation for McClintock's data: cannot explain a *burst* of new unstable loci, sharply-bounded somatic sectors that switch gene expression on a developmental timetable, twin reciprocal sectors, or elements that appear at different map positions in different crosses.

## 2. Breakage–fusion–bridge (BFB) cycle — her OWN prior establishment (1938, 1941, 1942)
Dicentric chromosome → at anaphase the two centromeres go to opposite poles → chromatin bridge → mechanical rupture at a variable point → a single broken end enters each telophase nucleus → after replication the broken end "fuses" with its sister copy → dicentric again → cycle repeats. In endosperm and gametophyte the broken end is NOT healed, so the cycle perpetuates (in the embryo a new telomere heals it). She can deliver a broken 9S end into the nucleus at will using a chromosome 9 carrying a reverse duplication of its short arm: a meiotic crossover yields a dicentric that starts the chromatid-type BFB. This is both the *tool* (controlled chromosome breakage) and, as it turns out, the *trigger* for the burst.

## 3. Maize pachytene cytogenetics
The 10 maize chromosomes are readable at pachytene: length, centromere position, individual chromomeres, and heterochromatic knobs. Break points and rearrangements can be located physically and tied to genetic markers. Marked telomeric knob on one homolog lets cytology confirm loss of a distal fragment. Maize was the premier organism for this before Drosophila salivary-gland chromosomes.

## 4. Markers
9S gene order: telomere – C (colored aleurone, dominant; c colorless) – Bz (Bronze; bz brownish) – Wx (Waxy; Wx+ stains blue with I2-KI, wx red) – centromere. a1 on chromosome 3 (A1 colored aleurone, a1 colorless recessive). Triploid endosperm/aleurone (one paternal + two maternal sets) lets dosage be read in the kernel directly; the kernel is a scorable record of one cross.

## 5. Rhoades 1938 (Genetics 23:377) — Dotted (Dt) / a1
First clear two-element instability system in maize: the stable recessive a1 mutates to A1 (pigment dots in plant + kernel) ONLY when a dominant element Dt is present elsewhere. Dt maps to the heterochromatic knob at the end of 9S. Frequency/pattern of dotting depends on Dt. This is the template McClintock generalizes: an unstable allele + a separable controlling factor. (Ac : its mutable loci :: Dt : a1.)

## 6. Drosophila position-effect variegation (PEV) + heterochromatin (Lewis 1950 Adv. Genet. 3:73)
In Drosophila, a rearrangement moving a euchromatic gene next to heterochromatin makes its expression variegated (mosaic on/off). Heterochromatin "controls" the expression of an adjacent gene, and the time/frequency of variegation is governed by the heterochromatic material. McClintock uses the homology: the maize variegation is "essentially the same" phenomenon, and the controlling material is suspected to be heterochromatin (Dt sits in the 9S knob).

## Field-state summary
By the late 1940s the orthodox gene is fixed and static; "unstable genes" (Drosophila, Oenothera, Rhoades' a1-Dt) are known curiosities usually filed under "position effect" or odd mutator genes. No one reads them as *mobile elements that physically relocate and govern neighbors*. McClintock's BFB tooling + pachytene cytology + the maize marker/dosage system are exactly what let her convert kernel patterns into a mechanism.
