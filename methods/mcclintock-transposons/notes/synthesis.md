## The pain point / research question (in-frame, ~1944–1950)
Prevailing dogma: genes are fixed, indivisible beads strung in linear order at fixed loci on the chromosome (Sturtevant 1913 line; Morgan school). A locus = a fixed address; mutation = a rare change in the bead itself. The chromosome is a static string. The goal McClintock actually has: explain the *burst* of unstable, variegated phenotypes — kernels and seedlings mosaic for gene expression — that appeared in the self-pollinated progeny of her 1944 breakage-fusion-bridge plants, far beyond anything the static-gene picture predicts.

## What tools / facts exist at the time
- **Breakage-fusion-bridge (BFB) cycle** — McClintock's own prior establishment (1938, 1941, 1942). A dicentric chromosome → anaphase bridge → mechanical rupture → broken end enters telophase nucleus → "fuses" with the replicated broken end next cycle → bridge again. A self-perpetuating breakage cycle in endosperm/gametophyte (where the broken end is NOT healed; in the embryo it heals = new telomere). She can *put a broken end of 9S into the nucleus on demand* via a crossover in a chromosome 9 with a reverse-duplication of its short arm (Fig 2 of lecture).
- **Maize pachytene cytology** — she can read the 10 chromosomes at pachytene; chromomeres, knobs (heterochromatic), centromere positions; she can map a break point physically and correlate it with genetics. Maize was the best material for this before Drosophila salivary chromosomes were used.
- **Marker genes on 9S**, order: telomere – C (colored aleurone, dom) – Bz (Bronze) – Wx (Waxy) – centromere. Plus a1 on chromosome 3, Dt on 9S knob.
- **Rhoades' Dotted (Dt), 1938:** a dominant element elsewhere that makes the otherwise stable recessive a1 mutate to A1, giving dots of pigment. A two-element instability system already known — but Dt thought of as a normal (if odd) gene at a locus.
- **Drosophila position-effect variegation:** moving a gene next to heterochromatin makes its expression variegated (Lewis 1950). Heterochromatin "controls" neighbor expression. McClintock leans on this homology.

## The discovery path (as her own accounts give it)
1. **1944 experiment, winter 1944-45 seedlings.** Self-pollinated 450 plants each starting from a zygote with a newly ruptured 9S end from BOTH parents (so BFB ran through gametophyte development). Expected: minute homozygous deficiencies segregating like recessives. Got instead: a flood of **variegated** seedlings/kernels — mosaic for type AND degree of gene expression, in sharply bounded sectors.
2. **Sector = clock.** A sector is sharply defined → the event that changed gene expression happened in the *ancestor cell* of that sector, many cell generations back. Sector size tells you *when in development* the switch happened: big sector = early event, small sector/many fine dots = late/frequent events. The pattern is a *clock and tally* of switching events.
3. **Twin sectors.** Side-by-side reciprocal sectors (one with more, the twin with fewer streaks) → from the two daughters of one mitosis: "one daughter cell had gained something that the other daughter cell had lost." So whatever changes gene expression is a *physical cell component unequally distributed at a mitosis* — not a change in the gene bead.
4. **The Ds locus = the Rosetta stone (1950 paper).** "A fortunate discovery": a locus on 9S where chromosome *breaks* recur in somatic cells. The time & frequency of breakage at this **Ds (Dissociation)** locus = the time & frequency of mutation at some mutable loci. So the mutable-gene phenomenon and a chromosome-breaking locus are the *same* underlying event readout. Breakage at Ds gives a dicentric + acentric fragment (BFB again) — loss of all distal dominant markers (C-I, Bz+, Wx+) = colored/waxy sectors, confirmed cytologically by loss of the marked telomeric knob.
5. **Ds moves (transposition).** Most variegated kernels break between Wx and centromere; occasional ones break between C and Bz instead, heritably — Ds is now at a NEW position, confirmed cytologically. "Transposition of Ds activity from one position to another in the chromosomal complement."
6. **Ds into a gene = a mutable allele.** A single gamete: Ds transposed to the C locus; C action *disappears* (phenotype = recessive c), and the disappearance is *correlated with the appearance of Ds at C*. Not a deficiency: because C action *reappears* (c→C mutations), and "when C action reappeared, the Ds action concomitantly disappeared." → Ds inserted next to/into C inhibits it; removal of Ds restores it. This is c-m1. "No gene mutations occur at the C locus; the restoration of its action is due to the removal of the inhibiting Ds chromatin." The gene is intact; the *insertion* is what mutates.
7. **Ac (Activator) = the separable controller.** Ds does nothing without a second factor Ac, inherited as a single unit, unlinked, mapping to different places in different crosses (Ac also transposes). "When no Ac is present in a nucleus, no mutation-producing events occur... nor are any chromosome breakage events detected at Ds." Ac **dosage controls timing**: "the higher the dose of Ac, the more delayed is the time of occurrence of mutations" — 1, 2, 3 doses give different timings. This is the quantitative key that turns spot/sector patterns into a readout of Ac dose & state.
8. **"Change in state."** Ds (and Ac) can change to a heritable new "state" that alters the *frequency* of its events — interpreted as change in quantity of the inserted material. Different states → different variegation patterns. Spot pattern = (Ac dose) × (Ds state).
9. **Origin: BFB activates silent elements.** The burst of mutable loci came from the BFB cycle. Test (1950 + lecture): take stocks homozygous a1 dt (no Dt action ever) and run a broken 9S through BFB → kernels show a1→A1 dotted patterns *indistinguishable from Dt*, created de novo. So BFB can activate a silent, potentially-transposable element (recreate Dt-like action) — Ac/Ds are such elements. Dt sits in the heterochromatic knob → suspicion that the controlling material is heterochromatin.
10. **The big leap.** The genome is not a static string of fixed beads; it carries mobile *controlling elements* that insert beside genes, switch them on/off, move, change state, and are mobilized by genome shock. The genome is a responsive organ. Field disbelief for ~two decades (until bacterial IS/transposons + cloning of Ac in 1983, Fedoroff/Wessler/Shure, ref 23 of lecture).

## Design-decision / why table (the non-obvious moves)
- **Why squaring on the variegation pattern as data at all?** Because a sector is a permanent clonal record of a single past event; pattern = developmental timeline of switching → you can read mechanism (timing, frequency, autonomy) off a static kernel. Alternative (count mutant offspring) loses the *timing* dimension that distinguishes the two-element control.
- **Why "twin sectors" force a physical, segregating element (not a gene mutation):** a gene mutation in one cell can't simultaneously give the *reciprocal* gain in the sister; only something physically partitioned (gained by one daughter, lost by the other) at that mitosis does. Rules out the static-bead mutation model on the spot.
- **Why Ds is "inserted material adjacent to the gene," not the gene mutating:** because gene action is fully *restored* (c→C, exactly wild type, stable) in lockstep with Ds *leaving* — a mutated bead doesn't un-mutate exactly and reproducibly; an excised insertion restoring the original sequence does. Plus a deficiency of C is excluded because the action comes back.
- **Why a *separate* Ac factor (two-element system) rather than Ds doing it alone:** Ds breakage/mutation is entirely absent when Ac is bred out and returns when Ac returns, and Ac segregates independently and maps elsewhere. The activity is *trans*-controlled. (Mirrors Rhoades' a1+Dt two-element system — McClintock explicitly builds on the analogy: Ac:its loci :: Dt:a1.)
- **Why Ac *dosage* matters (timing, not just on/off):** the same kernel/plant tissue shows later mutation timing with more Ac copies; this is reproducible across plant and triploid endosperm. Makes Ac a quantitative timer, explaining the whole spectrum of variegation patterns as dose×state.
- **Why "change in state" (an extra degree of freedom):** needed because the *same* Ds at the *same* place changes its event frequency heritably; interpreted as change in amount of inserted material. Without it the patterns wouldn't be heritably tunable.
- **Why heterochromatin / BFB as the origin mechanism:** the burst followed BFB; BFB preferentially breaks/fuses at knobs and centromeres (the heterochromatin); Dt lives in the 9S knob and Dt-like action is recreated by running a knobbed 9S through BFB. So genome shock at heterochromatin mobilizes/creates these elements.
