# Transposable controlling elements in maize (Ac/Ds)

## The problem
Maize plants whose chromosome 9 had been run through the breakage-fusion-bridge (BFB) cycle gave, in their self-pollinated progeny, a burst of *mutable loci*: many genes whose expression changes clonally during development, producing sharply sectored, variegated kernels and leaves - far more instability, at far more loci, than the static "gene = fixed bead at a fixed locus" picture can explain. The task is to identify the physical event that changes a gene's expression in a single ancestor cell and is inherited by its clone, and the factor that governs when and how often it happens.

## The key idea
A mutable locus is not an unstable gene. It is an ordinary, intact gene with a **mobile element inserted next to it** that controls its expression. Two elements form the system:

- **Ds (Dissociation)** - inserted chromatin whose altered state can (i) cause sister-chromatid fusion at its own position, producing a dicentric chromatid and a distal acentric fragment that is lost; (ii) **transpose** to a new chromosomal position; and (iii) when inserted beside a gene, **inhibit that gene**, then later be removed and **restore the gene intact** (no gene mutation). Ds also undergoes heritable **changes in state** that alter the relative frequency of its consequences (read as changes in the amount of inserted material).
- **Ac (Activator)** - a separable, autonomously mobile element, inherited as a single unit, segregating independently of Ds, required *in trans* for Ds mutation-producing and breakage events: with no Ac, no such events occur. Ac is **dose-dependent in timing** - higher Ac dose gives a more delayed time of occurrence for mutations at Ac-controlled mutable loci - and also undergoes changes in state.

The visible variegation pattern is the legible output of these elements operating during development: a sector is a clone descended from the cell in which an event occurred, so **sector size encodes timing** (large/early vs. small/late) and the **frequency of visible sectors** reflects which consequences of the element's event are being produced; **reciprocal twin sectors** mark a mitosis at which the two daughters received reciprocal regulatory changes. The genome is therefore not a static string of fixed beads but a responsive system carrying mobile elements that insert beside genes and govern them - and genome shock (the BFB cycle, acting on heterochromatin) can activate or create such elements de novo.

## The mechanism, stated cleanly
On the short arm of chromosome 9, gene order telomere - C - Bz - Wx - centromere:

1. **Ds breaks the chromosome at a definite site.** An event at Ds yields a dicentric chromatid by sister-chromatid fusion at Ds, plus an acentric distal fragment composed of the sister segments from Ds to the telomere; that fragment is lost. Genetic signature: a clonal sector that simultaneously loses every dominant marker distal to Ds (C, Bz, Wx in the chromosome-9 marker system). Cytological confirmation: the marked telomeric knob is lost in the same sectors.
2. **Ds transposes.** The breakage site, normally between Wx and the centromere, is found heritably relocated (e.g. between C and Bz), confirmed cytologically by the shifted break point. The locus changes its address.
3. **Ds beside a gene makes a mutable allele.** When Ds transposes to the C locus, C action disappears (phenotype c) - but this is **not a deficiency**, because C action *reappears* (c -> C reversion to full, stable wild type), and the return of C is locked to the *loss of Ds activity* at that site. Hence Ds inserted beside C inhibits it; removal of Ds restores the intact gene. This is the mutable c-m1 allele. c-m2 is another C-derived mutable locus with quantitative color states and twin sectors; wx-m1 is a Wx-derived mutable locus whose behavior is strikingly similar to c-m2, with the same kind of transposition suspected but not directly proven in that case.
4. **Ac is required and sets the timing.** Bred away from Ac, Ds-controlled mutable loci produce no mutation events and Ds produces no breakage events; restoring Ac restores activity. Ac dosage controls *when*: in the triploid endosperm, 1, 2, or 3 doses of a dosage-responsive Ac state give progressively *later* mutation timing, registered identically in plant and kernel - so Ac dose places the events on the developmental clock that sector size reads out.
5. **Origin by genome shock.** The burst of mutable loci traces to the BFB cycle, which breaks and fuses preferentially at heterochromatic knobs and centromeres. Direct test: a1 (chromosome 3) is stably colorless without Rhoades' Dt; running a knobbed chromosome 9 through the BFB cycle subjects the terminal heterochromatic knob - the kind of material in which Dt resides - to breakage and fusion, and generates de novo a1 -> A1-like dotting indistinguishable from Dt, with no Dt present. Genome shock at heterochromatin mobilizes or creates controlling elements.

## The evidence chain
- **Physical, segregating agent, not a mutating bead** - from reciprocal twin sectors: one daughter appears to have gained something the other lost.
- **A definite, breaking, relocatable site** - from the somatic breakage locus whose breakage time/frequency matches the mutation time/frequency of mutable loci, plus marker-loss and knob-loss cytology, plus the heritably shifted break point.
- **Insertion-and-removal, gene intact** - from full, stable restoration of C action in lockstep with Ds departure, which a deficiency cannot give.
- **A separable, mobile, trans-acting controller** - from the complete dependence of Ds mutation-producing and breakage events on Ac, Ac's independent segregation, and its wandering map position (built on Rhoades' 1938 a1/Dt two-element precedent).
- **Quantitative timing control** - from the reproducible higher-Ac-dose -> later-event relationship in triploid endosperm.
- **Heterochromatin / genome-shock origin** - from de novo recreation of Dt-like (a1 -> A1-like) action by subjecting the chromosome-9 knob to the BFB cycle.

## A worked reading of a kernel
Given Ds beside a color gene and Ac in the complement:
- **No Ac** -> the inhibited gene remains uniformly off and no Ds-controlled mutation-producing or breakage events occur.
- **Ac present in a dosage-responsive state** -> the gene can switch; **more Ac doses -> later, smaller sectors; fewer doses -> earlier, larger sectors.**
- **Reciprocal twin sectors** -> one mitosis at which the two daughters received reciprocal regulatory changes.
- **A colored bronze, waxy sector in a colorless starchy kernel** -> Ds breaking the arm and losing the distal marked fragment, not clean restoration of a gene.
- **Colorless kernel throwing colored spots that revert to stable full color** -> Ds inserted adjacent to the gene, then being removed and leaving it intact.

Reading backward from the pattern recovers the position of Ds, the presence and dose of Ac, and the state of each - the genome's own developmental activity, written in pigment.
