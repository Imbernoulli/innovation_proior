Let me start from what is actually on the seedling bench in front of me this winter, because it is not what I planted for. I grew about 450 plants whose history traces back to a zygote that received, from each parent, a chromosome 9 with a freshly broken short arm - I arranged that on purpose, so that the broken end would run through the breakage-fusion-bridge cycle in the pollen and the embryo sac before fertilization, and I self-pollinated each plant expecting to recover little homozygous deficiencies: clean recessive-looking segregants, minute pieces of the short arm gone. Some of those did show up. But they are buried under something I did not order. Progeny after progeny is variegated - mosaic for the expression of a gene, leaves and kernels broken into patches where a character is altered in one sector and different in the next, and not just on/off but graded, different intensities side by side. And it is not one gene. It is many. Forty-odd different loci behaving this way, affecting all sorts of characters, the majority of them loci that were perfectly quiet in the stocks these plants came from - stocks I have grown for years without this. A burst of instability, out of nowhere, at gene after gene. That is the thing I have to explain, and the orthodox gene gives me nothing to explain it with.

Because the orthodox gene is a fixed bead at a fixed address. That is how we all draw the chromosome: a string, beads in fixed places, each bead changing only by a rare mutation of itself. On that picture a "mutable locus" is just a bead flipping forward and back. Fine — but a bead mutating in a cell changes that bead in that cell and its descendants, and that is all. It does not tell me why forty quiet beads should all start flickering at once in these particular plants, and it does not begin to touch the most striking thing in front of me, which I want to look at hard before I theorize.

Look at the sectors. They are sharp. A patch of altered expression has a clean boundary, and everything inside it is alike, and it sits inside tissue that is different. A sharp clonal patch means one thing: the event that changed the gene's expression happened once, in a single ancestor cell, and every descendant of that cell inherited the changed state. So each sector is a record of a single past event, frozen into the tissue. And here is what that buys me: the *size* of the sector tells me *when* the event happened. A big sector means the ancestor cell was early in development, so its clone is large. A scatter of tiny dots means the events happened late, near the end of development, each founding only a few cells. So the kernel is not just showing me that a gene switched; it is showing me, in the geometry of its patches, the developmental *timetable* of switching. The pattern is a clock. If I can read it, the time of the changes and the frequency of visible consequences are written right there on the aleurone.

Now the observation that will not fit a bead at all. Twin sectors. Side by side, two patches that are reciprocals of each other - one with more of the streaks, the other with correspondingly fewer, as if what one gained the other lost. Two adjacent clones, reciprocal, means they are the two daughters of one mitosis, and at that mitosis one daughter got something the other did not. A point mutation of a bead cannot do this. A bead mutating in one cell does not simultaneously hand the sister cell the opposite change; mutation is not a partition. But *unequal segregation of a physical thing* is exactly a partition: at the division, a discrete component went preferentially to one daughter, and the other daughter is short of it. So whatever is regulating the expression of this gene is not the gene-bead changing - it is some physical cell component being distributed, sometimes unequally, at mitosis. I keep coming back to the same phrasing because the twins force it: one cell gained what the other lost. I am not watching a gene mutate. I am watching something move.

So I have a physical, segregating, clonally inherited agent that switches a gene's expression, on a developmental clock I can read off sector size, and the same kind of behavior erupting at many loci at once. I need a handle - one locus where I can pin the physical event to something I can actually see down the microscope, and then transfer the lesson to the rest. And I get a piece of luck. Among these cultures there is a locus on the short arm of chromosome 9 where chromosomes are *breaking* in somatic cells - recurrent breaks, at one definite spot. I can study breakage; breakage I understand, I have spent years on broken ends. So I look at the time and frequency of breakage at this locus, reading it the same way I read the variegation clock. And the time and frequency of the breaks at this one locus match the time and frequency of the mutation events at some of the mutable loci. The breaking locus and the switching genes are ticking to the same clock. That cannot be coincidence; it tells me the breaking locus is a window onto the same underlying event that drives the variegation. I will name it for what it first showed me - Dissociation, Ds - because what I first caught it doing was dissociating the chromosome.

What does the breakage event at Ds actually do? The change at Ds can make the sister chromatids sticky at that site. Fusion there gives a dicentric chromatid, and the sister segments from Ds out to the telomere are left as an acentric fragment. Acentric means no centromere, so that distal fragment is lost as the cell divides. In the clone descended from that cell, every marker distal to Ds on that chromosome is gone. I can test that directly with my markers. The short arm carries, reading out from the telomere, C for colored aleurone, then Bz, then Wx, then the centromere. Put the dominant markers on the chromosome that has Ds, and where Ds acts between Wx and the centromere, the clone loses C, Bz, and Wx together. In the cross built for this, a colorless starchy kernel gives colored bronze, waxy sectors when the marked distal fragment is lost. And I can do better than infer it: I marked the telomere end of that chromosome with a heterochromatic knob, the other homolog with a normal end, and in the sectors that lost the dominant markers, the knob is gone too. The cytology and the genetics lose the same piece together. Breakage at Ds is real, it is at a definite place, and it throws away the distal acentric fragment.

Here is where it stops being an oddity and starts being the mechanism. The position of Ds is not fixed. Most of my variegated kernels in that test behave as if breakage is between Wx and the centromere, so the sectors are colored, bronze, and waxy. But an occasional kernel gives a different heritable pattern: purple sectors, yet uniformly starchy. That only makes sense if breakage is now between C and Bz, closer to the telomere, and I confirm under the microscope that the break point has moved up the arm. The breaking locus has relocated. Ds is at a new position, and it stays there in the progeny. A bead does not do this. A locus is supposed to be an address. This one has changed its address. Transposition - Ds moves from one position in the chromosome to another.

And if Ds can move, then the thing my twins told me - a physical component handed unequally to one daughter - and the thing my breaking locus shows me - a definite site that relocates - are the same kind of thing. Now I have a way to make a *mutable gene* out of all this, and I find the case I need almost handed to me. A single gamete shows up carrying a chromosome 9 that looks morphologically normal but in which Ds has transposed to the location of C. And at that instant C stops working: the aleurone is colorless, the phenotype of recessive c. So far that could be a deficiency - a piece carrying C lost. But in the descendants, C *comes back*. Colorless tissue throws colored spots; c reverts to full C. A deficiency is a hole; a hole does not refill with the exact wild-type gene. And when I watch carefully, the return of C action is locked to the *departure* of Ds: when C works again, Ds activity has concomitantly vanished from that location, and the restored C is now stable - no more Ds events there. So the gene was never mutated. C sat intact the whole time. What happened is that Ds inserted itself adjacent to C and, sitting there, inhibited it; and when the Ds chromatin is removed, C is uncovered and resumes, unchanged. The "mutation" of the gene is the arrival and removal of an inserted element. That is the first mutable c locus - c-m1 - and it rewrites what a mutable locus is. The locus is not an unstable gene. It is a stable gene with a movable inhibitor parked beside it.

The next mutable loci keep me honest because they do not all look like c-m1. Another C locus arises from a previously normal C and I call it c-m2; it does not simply jump from colorless to full stable C. It gives a range of quantitative expressions: intermediate color, full wild type, even stronger than the wild type from which it came, and the sectors often come as twins with one sister deeper in color than the other. Breakage can occur at this locus too. Then a mutable Wx locus appears in a gamete from a plant carrying c-m2. Wx is proximal to C on the same short arm, and this wx-m1 pattern is strikingly like c-m2. I cannot prove in that case that the very same inhibiting substance has transposed from c-m2 to Wx, so I should not say more than the evidence gives me. But the capacity of this material to transpose, and the shared dependence on the same activator, make the suspicion the natural one. c-m1 shows clean inhibition and restoration; c-m2 and wx-m1 show that different inserted states can give quantitative expression, twins, and a different visible balance of consequences.

That single reinterpretation pays off everywhere. The reason instability erupted at forty loci at once is not forty beads going bad; it is this insertable, removable material landing next to gene after gene. The reason a sector is a sharp clone is that insertion, removal, breakage, or a change in state is a discrete event in one ancestor cell. The reason for twins is that the event can involve sister chromatids or a physical component partitioned at a division. The reason the controlling locus wanders the map is that it transposes. And the variegation pattern - the clock I read off sector size - is the record of *when* this material acted at that gene during development.

But there is a piece missing, and the cleanest experiments insist on it. The inserted Ds can keep C inactive, but without another factor in the nucleus it does not produce the visible mutational events. I can grow c-m1 generation after generation with no such factor and it stays flatly colorless - no spots, no c-to-C events, no breaks at Ds anywhere. The instability only appears in certain crosses. So there is a second factor, somewhere else, whose presence is required for any Ds event. When I breed it out, every Ds event stops - no mutation at c-m1, c-m2, or wx-m1, no breakage at Ds; when I cross it back in, instability returns. It is inherited as a single unit, it segregates independently of Ds, and - this is the tell - it maps to different positions in different crosses, just as Ds does. It moves too. I will call it the Activator, Ac, because that is its job: Ds and the mutable loci are inert without it, and it activates them. This is the same architecture Rhoades found in 1938 with his Dotted: the stable recessive a1 stays stable until the separable dominant Dt is present, and then a1 gives colored dots. A controlling factor elsewhere, required in *trans*. He had the two-element shape. What I can now add is that both elements move, that the "unstable allele" is really a stable gene with an inserted element beside it, and that the controller does something quantitative that Dt's story never spelled out.

Because Ac does not merely switch the system on. Ac sets the *timing*, and it does it by *dosage*. I can read Ac dose in the triploid endosperm, where I can have one, two, or three copies, and for Ac states with marked dosage effects the effect is clean and reproducible in both the plant and the kernel: the higher the dose of Ac, the more delayed the time of occurrence of mutations at the Ac-controlled mutable loci. One dose, two doses, three doses can give progressively delayed timing of the c-to-C events and the Ds breakage events. And late events make small late clones, early events make big early ones - so Ac dosage is precisely the knob that sets where on my sector-size clock the events fall. Suddenly the whole zoo of variegation patterns resolves: a pattern is the state of the inserted material (which sets the relative frequency of possible consequences) together with the dose and state of Ac (which sets the timing). High Ac dose, later and smaller sectors; low Ac dose, earlier and larger sectors. The clock I noticed on day one is being driven, and Ac is the drive.

I need one more degree of freedom, because the same Ds at the same place does not always behave identically across generations. Its event frequency changes, heritably — it shifts to a new setting and the new setting breeds true for a while, until it shifts again. I will call this a change in *state* of Ds, and read it as a change in the amount of the inserted material: more of the material, more breakage and gross rearrangement; less of it, more often a quiet loss of activity. Ac shows the same thing — changes in state that alter its dose-like action and its timing. So the pattern on any kernel is two dials: the state of the element at the gene, and the dose-and-state of Ac. Between them they generate every variegation pattern I have catalogued, and they do it without ever mutating a single gene.

Now the origin, which is where I started and where this all has to close. Why did the burst happen in *these* plants - the ones whose chromosome 9 went through the breakage-fusion-bridge cycle? The cycle does its breaking and fusing preferentially at the heterochromatic knobs and centromeres; I have the pachytene pictures of dozens of these plants showing nonrandom rearrangement of exactly that material. So my suspicion is that the cycle, by wrenching the heterochromatin around, either created or woke up these controlling elements. I can test that, and the test ties directly back to Rhoades. Dt sits in the heterochromatic knob at the end of the short arm of chromosome 9, and Dt makes a1 give colored A1-like dots. Take a stock that is homozygous a1 with no Dt at all - a1 dead stable, never dots, I have grown it for years and it never gives a single A1-like spot - and run a knobbed chromosome 9 through the breakage-fusion-bridge cycle in those kernels, so the knob is subjected to the breaks and fusions. And kernels come out dotting a1 to A1-like expression, some of them in a pattern indistinguishable from Dt's, with no Dt stock anywhere in the field to contaminate them. The cycle, acting on the knob, has recreated Dt-like controlling action where there was none. So genome shock at the heterochromatin can create or activate these elements de novo - which is exactly why my 450 plants, every one of them derived from a history of ruptured chromosome-9 ends and rearranged knob and centromere regions, erupted with controlling elements at forty loci. Ac and Dt act alike, Dt lives in the knob, the knob's action can be recreated by the cycle, and Ac appeared in stocks that had been through the cycle - all of it points to heterochromatin as the stuff these controlling elements are made of, and to genome shock as what mobilizes them.

So here is the picture I am forced to, and it is not the picture I was taught. The genome is not a fixed string of beads sitting at permanent addresses. It carries elements - Ac, the autonomous activator, and Ds, which depends on Ac - that can leave one position and insert at another, including adjacent to an ordinary gene. When such an element inserts beside a gene, it takes control of that gene: it can shut the gene off, and when the inserted chromatin is later removed it can hand the gene back, intact and working, with no mutation of the gene at all. Ac is required for Ds to act, and the *dose* of Ac sets the *time* in development when these mutation-producing events and breaks occur, while heritable changes in *state* of the elements set their relative frequencies and dosage responses. The visible variegation - the sharp clonal sectors, the size that reads as a clock, the reciprocal twins from unequal partition or sister-chromatid involvement at mitosis, the controller that maps to a different place in every cross - is the legible output of these mobile, controlling elements operating during development. And the whole system can be switched into existence by shocking the genome, by running the heterochromatin through the breakage-fusion-bridge cycle, which is why it all poured out of these particular plants. The chromosome is not a static catalogue. It is a responsive system, and the units that respond to its own ruptures by moving, inserting, and governing their neighbors are the controlling elements I have been chasing in the kernels.

To pin it as a reading rule for the kernel, the way I now use it: take a kernel of known genotype with Ds beside a color gene and Ac somewhere in the complement. If Ac is absent, the inhibited gene stays uniformly off - Ds is parked and no mutation-producing events occur. If Ac is present in a dosage-responsive state, Ds acts, and the gene switches; the *number of Ac doses* tells me how late the switching starts, so heavier Ac dose gives later, smaller sectors and lighter Ac dose gives earlier, larger sectors; a reciprocal twin marks one mitosis where the two daughters received reciprocal regulatory changes; a colored bronze, waxy sector in a colorless starchy kernel is Ds breaking the arm and losing the distal marked fragment rather than clean restoration of a gene; and a colorless kernel throwing colored spots that revert to stable full color is Ds sitting adjacent to the gene and then being removed, leaving it whole. Read backward from the pattern to the elements, and the kernel tells you where Ds is, whether Ac is present and in what dose, and the state of each - the genome's own activity, written in pigment.
