Four points $A,B,C,D$ sit on a line at equal spacing, $|AB|=|BC|=|CD|$, and a thin lens has thrown three of their images $A',B',C'$ onto the page. I have to produce $D'$, the image of the fourth point — and I am told almost nothing about the lens: not its focal length, not its position, not its tilt. The only crumb is that the optical axis lies in the plane of the figure. My instinct is to set up the thin-lens equation, place the lens somewhere, solve for object distances, and trace the fourth ray. But I can't place the lens — three unknowns about it are missing on purpose. So the brute-force route is blocked before it starts; whatever pins $D'$ has to survive my ignorance of the lens entirely.

Let me look harder at what the three given points actually are. They are the images $A',B',C'$ of three collinear, equally spaced points. First question: are $A',B',C'$ themselves collinear? Eyeballing the figure they look like they lie on one straight line, and that can't be a coincidence — it must be a property of imaging. So let me ask the cleaner question: what does a thin lens do to a whole straight line?

Take the original line $L=AB$ and imagine a single light ray that travels *along* $L$ — the line is, physically, the path of a ray. If $L$ is parallel to the lens plane, all points of $L$ have the same object distance, so the thin-lens equation gives one image distance and one constant transverse magnification; that easy case is already a straight image line. The interesting case is when the ray along $L$ actually reaches the lens. It refracts there, and by the construction rules of geometrical optics the outgoing part of a refracted ray is again a straight line. Now think about what that outgoing ray is. Every object point $P$ on $L$ emits, among all its rays, the one that runs along $L$ itself; after the lens that particular ray has the same outgoing straight line, and the image $P'$ of $P$ must lie on that outgoing line or its straight extension. So *every* image point $P'$ of a point on $L$ lies on that single outgoing straight line. The image of the whole line $L$ is exactly that refracted line, $L'$. The image of a straight line is a straight line. No algebra, no lens placement — just the ray construction, with the parallel case as the limiting affine version.

And there is a bonus hiding in the same picture. In the nonparallel case the refraction happens *at the lens plane*. The incoming straight piece is $L$ and the outgoing straight piece is $L'$, and they are joined at the point where the ray crosses the lens. So $L$ and $L'$ meet on the lens. In the parallel case this same statement is the point-at-infinity version: the two lines have their common direction on the lens plane. I file that away — the original line and its image intersect on the lens, finitely or projectively. It tells me the lens plane passes through $L\cap L'$, which is the kind of constraint that pins the geometry if I ever need it.

So $A',B',C'$ are collinear — they lie on this image line $L'$ — and $D'$ must lie on $L'$ too. Good, that's one coordinate of $D'$ gone: $D'$ is somewhere on the known line $A'B'C'$. Now I need the *position* of $D'$ along that line. Equal spacing of $A,B,C,D$ has to translate into some relation among $A',B',C',D'$. But what relation? Equal spacing is an affine fact (it's about ratios of lengths), and a lens does not preserve lengths or even ratios of lengths — a thin lens magnifies, and the magnification depends on object distance, so the three image gaps $A'B'$, $B'C'$ are visibly *unequal* in the figure. So whatever is conserved, it is weaker than "ratio of segments."

Let me figure out exactly how strong the imaging map is. Take any object point $P$ and its image $P'$. One of the standard rays from $P$ is the one straight through the optical center $O$ of the lens — that ray is undeviated, it goes straight through. The image $P'$ lies on that ray. So $P$, $O$, $P'$ are collinear: $O$, $P$, $P'$ on one line, for *every* point $P$. That is a strong statement. Restrict to the line $L$ and its image $L'$. The map sending $P\in L$ to $P'\in L'$ has the property that the line $PP'$ always passes through the one fixed point $O$. A point-to-point map between two lines whose connecting lines all pass through a single fixed point — that is exactly a central projection from one line to the other, a *perspectivity* with center $O$.

That reframes everything. A perspectivity is the most basic projective transformation of a line. And the thing every projective transformation of a line preserves — not lengths, not ratios of lengths, but the *cross-ratio* of four points — is exactly one rung weaker than the ratio I was hoping for. That has to be the invariant. The equal spacing of $A,B,C,D$ gives them a definite cross-ratio, the perspectivity through $O$ carries it to $A',B',C',D'$, and that cross-ratio plus the known $A',B',C'$ will pin $D'$.

A perspectivity preserves the cross-ratio — the whole answer leans on that, so I want the derivation, not just the name. Set the center $O$, and let four lines through $O$ — call the pencil $OA,OB,OC,OD$ — cut a transversal at $A,B,C,D$. With directed segments the cross-ratio is
$$(A,B;C,D)=\frac{CA}{CB}\cdot\frac{DB}{DA},$$
and I want to show it depends only on the four directions of the pencil, not on which transversal I cut with. Drop the cross-ratio's segments into triangles sharing the vertex $O$. In triangle $OCA$, the law of sines gives $CA=OA\,\dfrac{\sin\angle COA}{\sin\angle OCA}$, and in triangle $OCB$, $CB=OB\,\dfrac{\sin\angle COB}{\sin\angle OCB}$. The angles $\angle OCA$ and $\angle OCB$ are the same angle (both measured at $C$ along the transversal, between it and $OC$) — or supplementary, which has the same sine — so when I form $CA/CB$ those factors cancel:
$$\frac{CA}{CB}=\frac{OA}{OB}\cdot\frac{\sin\angle COA}{\sin\angle COB}.$$
Do the identical thing for $D$:
$$\frac{DB}{DA}=\frac{OB}{OA}\cdot\frac{\sin\angle DOB}{\sin\angle DOA}.$$
Multiply, and the $OA,OB$ side lengths cancel completely:
$$(A,B;C,D)=\frac{\sin\angle COA}{\sin\angle COB}\cdot\frac{\sin\angle DOB}{\sin\angle DOA}.$$
The right-hand side is built only out of angles *at $O$* — the directions of the four pencil lines — and contains nothing about the transversal. So any other transversal cutting the same four lines through $O$ gives the same cross-ratio. A perspectivity through $O$ preserves the cross-ratio. That is the law I needed.

The physical construction of $P'$ ordinarily invokes two special rays — through the center and through the focus — so it's worth checking that the center ray alone is enough to pin the map down. It is: for each $P\in L$, the center-ray fact places $P'$ on line $OP$, and $P'$ is already known to lie on $L'$, so $P'$ is exactly the intersection $OP\cap L'$ (barring the usual point-at-infinity limits). That is the perspectivity from $L$ to $L'$ with center $O$. The focal ray is what makes the same point also satisfy the lens equation, but it does not change the projective form of the map.

Now the arithmetic that turns "cross-ratio preserved" into a number. The four originals are equally spaced in order $A,B,C,D$. Put coordinates on $L$: $A=0$, $B=1$, $C=2$, $D=3$. Then with $(A,B;C,D)=\dfrac{CA}{CB}\cdot\dfrac{DB}{DA}$ and directed lengths,
$$CA=A-C=-2,\quad CB=B-C=-1,\quad DB=B-D=-2,\quad DA=A-D=-3,$$
$$(A,B;C,D)=\frac{-2}{-1}\cdot\frac{-2}{-3}=2\cdot\frac23=\frac43.$$
So equally spaced points in this order have cross-ratio $4/3$. By the perspectivity,
$$(A',B';C',D')=(A,B;C,D)=\frac43.$$
$A',B',C'$ are given on $L'$, the cross-ratio is fixed at $4/3$, and a cross-ratio determines the fourth point uniquely — so $D'$ is *the* point on $L'$ with $(A',B';C',D')=4/3$. The unknown lens has completely dropped out: I never needed $O$, $F$, the focal length, or the tilt. Any lens consistent with $A',B',C'$ produces the same $D'$, because $D'$ is fixed by this projective invariant.

Now I need a geometric construction, not a coordinate solve — I am not allowed to compute $D'$ numerically and drop in a point. So I need to *construct* the point on $L'$ whose cross-ratio with $A',B',C'$ is $4/3$ using the permitted ruler-and-compass operations. The clean idea: I know one concrete quadruple with cross-ratio $4/3$ — four *equally spaced* points. So let me manufacture an equally spaced range somewhere convenient and use a perspectivity to carry its cross-ratio onto $L'$, with $A',B',C'$ fixed and the fourth point landing on $D'$.

Pick a generic point $X$ not on $L'$, off to the side, avoiding the few degenerate placements where the auxiliary intersection would run to infinity. Draw the ray from $A'$ through $X$, and on it step off two more equal gaps: mark $Y$ with $XY=A'X$, and $Z$ with $YZ=A'X$. So $A',X,Y,Z$ are four equally spaced points on this auxiliary line $\ell$, and by the same computation as above $(A',X;Y,Z)=4/3$. Now I want a perspectivity that fixes $A'$, sends $X\mapsto B'$, $Y\mapsto C'$, and therefore $Z\mapsto D'$. A perspectivity between $\ell$ and $L'$ that fixes their common point $A'$ has some center $P$, and $P$ must lie on the line joining $X$ to its image $B'$, and on the line joining $Y$ to its image $C'$. So define
$$P=\,\text{(line }B'X)\cap(\text{line }C'Y).$$
That single point $P$ is forced to be the center of the perspectivity carrying $A',X,Y,Z$ to $A',B',C',\bullet$ (it already sends $A'\to A'$ since $A'$ is on both lines, $X\to B'$, $Y\to C'$ by construction). The image of $Z$ is then where the line $PZ$ meets $L'$. So
$$D'=\,(\text{line }PZ)\cap L'.$$
Because the perspectivity through $P$ preserves the cross-ratio, $(A',B';C',D')=(A',X;Y,Z)=4/3$, which is exactly the condition $D'$ had to satisfy. I use the compass only to step off the equal triple; after that, intersecting $B'X$ with $C'Y$ gives $P$, and intersecting $PZ$ with $L'$ gives $D'$. No number is ever entered.

Against the actual data — coordinates read off the figure, $A'\approx(1.166,1.180)$, $B'\approx(4.824,2.236)$, $C'\approx(6.310,2.666)$ — the chain checks out: these are collinear, consistent with $L'$ being the image line, and measuring along $L'$ from $A'$ the signed positions come out near $0$, $3.81$, $5.35$. Imposing $(A',B';C',D')=4/3$ and solving for the position of $D'$ pushes it past $C'$ to about $6.19$ along $L'$, i.e. to roughly $(7.12,2.90)$ — matching where the geometric construction lands, since the construction *is* the cross-ratio condition realized with intersections. The gaps come out as $A'B'\approx3.81$, $B'C'\approx1.55$, $C'D'\approx0.84$ — each one *shorter* than the last, the equally spaced object compressing as I march along $L'$ toward a vanishing point, exactly the look of a perspective range.
