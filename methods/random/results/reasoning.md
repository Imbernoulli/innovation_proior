Let me start from what the situation actually forces on me. I have a huge pile of inputs sitting there for free — `N` of them, call the pool `x_1, ..., x_N` — and getting a label on any one of them costs real money or real expert time. I can afford only a tiny budget of labels, and I have to spend it in rounds: pick some inputs, pay to label them, fold them into the training set, retrain, repeat. Everything else is fixed — the model family, the optimizer, the retraining loop. The only knob I own is the rule that decides, on each round, which `n` of the still-unlabeled inputs I send out to be labeled. So the whole problem collapses to: design `query(n)` returning `n` pool indices. That's it. And before I reach for anything clever, I should be honest about what "clever" would have to beat, and what I can actually *prove* about whatever I choose. The thing I'm building right now is the reference rule — the one whose behavior I understand completely and whose guarantees hold no matter what — because without that I can't even tell whether a fancier rule is helping.

So what do I actually know with certainty about supervised learning? The one theorem that makes the whole enterprise work is the bridge from training error to test error, and it has a precondition I keep coming back to. Write the thing I really care about — the test error of a hypothesis `c` — as the probability it disagrees with the true labeling on a fresh input drawn from the deployment distribution `P`:

  err(c) = Pr_{x ~ P}[ c(x) != t(x) ].

I never get to see `err(c)` directly; I only ever see the *empirical* error on my labeled training set. The reason fitting the training set tells me anything about `err(c)` is uniform convergence: empirical risk converges to true risk, and a hypothesis consistent with `m` labeled examples has `err(c) <= eps` with confidence `1 - delta` once

  m = O( (1/eps) ( d ln(1/eps) + ln(1/delta) ) )   (d = VC dimension),

and that bound — Valiant's PAC formulation, made quantitative for general classes by Blumer, Ehrenfeucht, Haussler and Warmuth — rests on exactly one assumption about *where the training examples come from*: they are an independent sample from the very same `P` I'll be tested on. The sample is the bridge. If the training points are distributed like deployment, low training error transfers to low test error; if they aren't, the bridge is gone and the guarantee evaporates. So whatever `query(n)` I pick, I'd better be able to say what distribution my labeled set ends up following, because that distribution is the thing the entire generalization story depends on.

That immediately tells me what the *safest* possible rule is. If I want the labeled training set to be distributed like `P`, the cleanest way to keep that premise is to not use the model, the features, or the observed labels to tilt the draw. The pool itself is a sample from `P` (that's where it came from). Conditional on the realized pool, I can only promise representativeness with respect to that finite pool; unconditionally, if the pool was drawn from `P` and my index selection is independent of the data values, the selected examples still have distribution `P`. What does "doesn't distort" mean precisely? It means every still-available input in the pool should be equally likely to be selected — no input preferred over another. Let me make sure that's the right formalization and not just a vibe.

Here's the survey-sampling way to see it, because this is an old solved problem in a different guise — Cochran's *Sampling Techniques* is entirely about picking a subset of a finite population so that quantities computed on the subset estimate the whole population well. The foundational design there is simple random sampling without replacement: choose `n` of the `N` units so that every size-`n` subset is equally likely. Let me check what that buys me. Define the inclusion probability `pi_i = Pr[unit i is in the sample]`. By symmetry, since every size-`n` subset of the `N` is equally likely and unit `i` appears in `C(N-1, n-1)` of the `C(N, n)` possible subsets,

  pi_i = C(N-1, n-1) / C(N, n) = n/N,

the same for every `i`. So under simple random sampling every unit in the current finite population has identical inclusion probability `n/N`. Now I can say precisely why that gives me an unbiased, faithful sample of the pool. Take any statistic I'd want the labeled set to estimate about the pool — the fraction of inputs in some region, the class balance, anything expressible as a population average of some `g(x_i)`. The general unbiased estimator under unequal inclusion is Horvitz-Thompson: weight each sampled unit by `1/pi_i`, forming `(1/N) sum_{i in S} g(x_i)/pi_i`. Its expectation is exactly the population average, because each unit `i` contributes `g(x_i)/pi_i` with probability `pi_i`, so `E[contribution] = g(x_i)` for every `i`, and summing then dividing by `N` gives the true `(1/N) sum_i g(x_i)`. And when all `pi_i = n/N` are equal, that Horvitz-Thompson estimator collapses to the plain sample mean `(1/n) sum_{i in S} g(x_i)` — the obvious thing — and it's unbiased for the population mean. So equal inclusion probability is not an aesthetic preference; it's the exact condition under which the plain unweighted labeled subset is an unbiased miniature of the pool for every population-average quantity. Push that to the limit: as the sampled portion grows, the empirical distribution of the labeled set approaches the empirical distribution of the pool, and as the original pool grows that empirical pool distribution approaches `P`. A selection rule that is independent of the data values therefore preserves the distributional bridge that the PAC bound demanded, while the finite-population formulas tell me exactly what dependence remains inside a without-replacement batch. The two solved problems line up: the design that survey sampling calls "representative" is the design that learning theory needs for its guarantee to hold. Uniform selection is the rule that keeps the bridge from training error to test error standing.

Now, should I sample with replacement or without? Sampling with replacement would let me draw the same pool input twice in a round. But a labeled input I already hold teaches me nothing new the second time — the label is the same, I've paid for it once — so a repeat in the batch is a wasted slot of my budget. Beyond the obvious waste, without-replacement is *also* statistically better, and I can quantify it. For estimating the population mean, the variance of the sample-mean estimator under simple random sampling without replacement is

  Var_WOR = (sigma^2 / n) * (N - n)/(N - 1),

versus `Var_WR = sigma^2/n` with replacement, where `sigma^2` is the finite-population variance with denominator `N`. The extra factor `(N - n)/(N - 1) <= 1` is the finite-population correction: without replacement strictly reduces variance, and the reduction is largest when `n` is an appreciable fraction of `N` (and it drives the variance to zero when `n = N`, since then I've labeled everything and there's nothing left to estimate). So without replacement dominates on both counts — no wasted budget, and a tighter estimate. The rule is taking shape on its own: draw `n` *distinct* pool inputs, each pool input equally likely. That's simple random sampling without replacement, and I want it over the *unlabeled* part of the pool only, because the labeled inputs are already bought — spending the new round's budget on them would, again, buy zero information.

Let me sanity-check that this is implementable as exactly "uniform without replacement over the unlabeled set" and not something subtly different. The unlabeled indices are the pool indices where the labeled mask is false. I want `n` distinct ones, uniformly. A permutation of the unlabeled indices, truncated to its first `n`, does exactly that: a uniformly random permutation makes every ordered prefix equally likely, so every size-`n` subset comes up with equal probability and they're automatically distinct. Inclusion probability of each unlabeled input is `n / (number unlabeled)` — equal across them, which is the property I just argued I need. Good; that's the whole rule.

So the rule I've landed on is: `idxs_unlabeled = pool indices where not labeled; return a uniformly random size-n subset of them`. It is almost embarrassingly simple, and I want to resist the urge to immediately complicate it, because its simplicity is the point — it is the rule with no free parameters, no dependence on the current model, no dependence on the labels seen so far, nothing that could go wrong in a data-dependent way. That's exactly what makes it the reference. But "reference" only means something if I also understand precisely where it's weak, so that I know what a smarter rule would have to fix and what I'd be giving up to get there. Let me stare at the failure modes, because they're knowable in advance, just from the structure of the rule.

The first one bites hard when a class is rare. Suppose the positive class is 1 in 1000 inputs. My uniform draw faithfully reproduces that 1-in-1000 balance in the labeled subset — that's the whole "representative" property I was just celebrating — so a budget of, say, 500 labels gives me in expectation about half a positive example: roughly 500 negatives and essentially no positives. A classifier trained on 500 negatives and zero positives can't learn to recognize the positive class at all. The very faithfulness that makes the rule unbiased is what makes it useless here: representativeness reproduces the imbalance, and when the rare class is the one I care about, reproducing the imbalance is the opposite of what I want. So uniform sampling is going to be label-inefficient exactly when the interesting signal is rare — which is a lot of the time.

The second weakness is subtler and shows up as the budget grows. Think about how much a freshly drawn random point is actually worth as I accumulate labels. Picture the set of hypotheses still consistent with everything I've labeled so far; somewhere in input space there's a region where these surviving hypotheses still disagree about the label — the region of uncertainty `R`. An input that lands outside `R` is already determined by what I know: every surviving hypothesis labels it the same way, so labeling it can't change my hypothesis and teaches me nothing. Only inputs inside `R` can move the model. Let `alpha = Pr_{x~P}[x in R]` be the probability a fresh input falls in that still-useful region. Early on `R` is large and `alpha` is healthy, so a random draw frequently lands somewhere informative. But as I label more, `R` shrinks — every informative label carves a piece out of it — so `alpha` decreases toward zero. And here's the trouble with uniform sampling specifically: it draws from all of `P`, oblivious to `R`, so the probability that any given drawn point falls in the useful region is exactly `alpha`, which is decaying. Late in training, most of my random draws land in already-resolved territory and re-confirm what the model already knows. The information per labeled point — and so the marginal accuracy gain per dollar — decays toward zero. Cohn, Atlas and Ladner make this concrete: as you draw at random, `alpha` and hence the efficiency of learning both approach zero, so eventually most examples you draw provide essentially no information about the concept. Uniform sampling never stops spending labels on the easy, already-settled parts of the space.

And I can put a sharp number on how much that non-adaptivity costs in the cleanest possible case. Take the textbook problem of locating a single threshold on the unit interval — learn a point `theta` in `[0,1]` such that the label is "left of `theta`" vs "right of `theta`." If I only get random labeled examples, pinning `theta` to within error `eps` takes `O((1/eps) ln(1/eps))` of them: the labeled points fall all over the interval, and I have to wait for ones that happen to bracket `theta` tightly. But a learner that could *choose* where to ask — binary search, halving the candidate interval each time — needs only `O(ln(1/eps))` queries. That's an exponential gap, `1/eps` versus `ln(1/eps)`, and it's the formal statement of how much the mere act of choosing what to label can buy. So the non-adaptivity of uniform sampling isn't a small constant-factor inefficiency; in the most favorable case for adaptivity it's the difference between linear and logarithmic in `1/eps`. That's the prize a smarter rule is chasing.

Now I should be careful, because all of that might tempt me to throw uniform sampling away and reach straight for a rule that targets `R` — only ask about uncertain points, chase the boundary, exploit the binary-search-style savings. And there are such rules. But the moment I bias selection toward `R` instead of toward `P`, I break the exact property that gave me my only unconditional guarantee: the labeled set is no longer an i.i.d. sample of the deployment distribution, so the PAC bridge from training error to test error no longer applies, and any improvement becomes conditional rather than certain. Worse, a boundary-chasing rule depends on the *current* model to decide what's uncertain, and early in training that model is fit on almost no data and is probably wrong, so it can define `R` badly and steer the whole budget into an unrepresentative corner of the space — a self-reinforcing sampling bias the uniform rule structurally cannot have, precisely because it ignores the model. There's also the membership-query route — synthesize the single most informative input and ask about it, which is what gets you the `ln(1/eps)` rate — but that assumes I can label arbitrary synthesized points, and in the pool setting I can't: I'm choosing among real, fixed inputs, and a synthesized "most informative" input may be an uninterpretable artifact a human oracle can't label at all (the classic failure where a learner asked for the most informative handwritten digit produces gibberish hybrids). So every cleverer rule trades away something the uniform rule keeps for free: the unbiasedness, the assumption-free guarantee, the immunity to a bad early model.

That settles what role this rule plays. It is the *passive* learner — it uses no model, no labels-seen-so-far, no notion of uncertainty; it just draws representatively. Precisely because it does none of those things, it is the one rule whose statistical behavior I can state without conditions, and that is exactly what makes it the yardstick. Any adaptive rule that claims to be smart has to be measured against the learning curve this rule produces — accuracy as a function of labels spent — and it only earns the label "better" if its curve sits above this one across the budget range. The fact that this rule throws away information is not a bug to be apologized for; it's what makes it the honest, assumption-free baseline against which the value of using information can be measured. And in the regimes where its weaknesses don't bite — balanced classes, a smooth boundary, a budget that's a healthy fraction of the pool so the finite-population correction is actively helping — a representative i.i.d. sample is genuinely hard to beat, because it's the sample the generalization theory is written for.

One more structural point before I write it down, about batches. The harness retrains in rounds and asks me for a batch of `n` at a time rather than one input at a time, to amortize the cost of retraining. For an adaptive rule, batching is a real complication — if you greedily pick the `n` most uncertain points they're often near-duplicates of each other and you waste the batch on one spot, so batch active methods have to engineer diversity into the batch. For the uniform rule there's no such concern at all: a uniform draw of `n` distinct points is already spread across the pool in proportion to the pool, and because the rule consults no model, drawing `n` at once has the same law as drawing one, marking it labeled, retraining, and drawing again until the batch is filled. The selections inside the batch are not independent — they are deliberately without replacement — but that dependence is exactly what prevents duplicates. So the batch size is free here: one size-`n` SRS-WOR draw per round, with none of the diversity headaches. That's another small gift of being model-free.

So let me write the rule into the one empty slot, `query(n)`. Find the pool indices that are still unlabeled, take a uniformly random subset of `n` distinct ones, return them:

```python
import numpy as np


class CustomSampling(Strategy):
    """Passive / random-sampling baseline. Selects n inputs uniformly at random,
    without replacement, from the currently-unlabeled pool. Uses no model, no labels
    seen so far, and no notion of uncertainty -- which is exactly what makes it the
    unbiased, assumption-free reference every adaptive rule is measured against."""

    def __init__(self, X, Y, idxs_lb, net, handler, args):
        super(CustomSampling, self).__init__(X, Y, idxs_lb, net, handler, args)

    def query(self, n):
        # pool indices that are still unlabeled (labeled ones are already bought,
        # spending budget on them buys zero information)
        idxs_unlabeled = np.arange(self.n_pool)[~self.idxs_lb]
        # uniformly random size-n subset, WITHOUT replacement:
        # a random permutation makes every size-n subset equally likely and the
        # picks distinct, so every unlabeled input has inclusion probability
        # n / (#unlabeled) -- equal across them. Equal inclusion makes the plain
        # sample mean unbiased for pool averages; because the draw is value-
        # independent, a pool drawn from P yields selected examples distributed
        # like P up to finite-pool effects. Without replacement also cuts
        # estimator variance by the finite-population correction (N-n)/(N-1).
        return idxs_unlabeled[np.random.permutation(len(idxs_unlabeled))][:n]
```

Let me trace the causal chain back so I'm sure of why this is the rule and not an accident of laziness. I started with the only knob I own — which `n` of the unlabeled pool to label — and asked what I can prove about any choice. The single theorem that makes supervised learning work, the bridge from training to test error, is stated for examples drawn from the deployment distribution `P`. The way to keep that premise intact is to make the selection independent of the data values, which means every still-unlabeled pool input should be equally likely to be picked; when the current unlabeled pool has size `N`, survey-sampling theory confirms the inclusion probability exactly as `C(N-1,n-1)/C(N,n)=n/N`. With that equal `pi_i`, Horvitz-Thompson collapses to the plain sample mean, so ordinary unweighted averages on the selected set are unbiased for finite-pool averages. Without replacement follows because a repeated label buys nothing and the variance formula `(sigma^2/n)(N-n)/(N-1)` is the with-replacement variance times the finite-population correction; restricting to the unlabeled set follows for the same no-information-from-repeats reason. That gives one line of code: a uniform without-replacement draw from the unlabeled indices. Then I located its weaknesses precisely — it reproduces a rare class's imbalance and starves the minority, and its information per label decays toward zero as the region of uncertainty shrinks while it keeps drawing obliviously from all of `P`, an inefficiency that in the cleanest case is the exponential `1/eps`-vs-`ln(1/eps)` gap between random examples and chosen queries. But the rules that fix those weaknesses all pay for it by biasing the sample away from `P` — losing the unconditional guarantee, risking a self-reinforcing bias from a bad early model, or assuming an oracle for un-labelable synthesized points. So this rule's refusal to use any information is not a flaw but its defining virtue: it is the unbiased, model-free, assumption-free passive learner whose learning curve is the honest reference any adaptive query strategy must dominate to justify the information it spends.
