I start with the wrong picture on purpose: a sphere in `R^{n+1}`, a continuous map into `R^n`, and the wish to find `x` with `g(x)=g(-x)`. If I stare at it in local coordinates, it looks like I have `n` equations in something like `n` degrees of freedom, so maybe this is just a disguised intermediate value theorem. But that is too local. A coordinate chart sees one patch of the sphere and forgets that the point I am comparing with `x` is not nearby at all; it is the antipodal point on the other side. The condition is not local equality of nearby values. It is a global symmetry condition.

The one-dimensional case tells me what survives. If `g : S^1 -> R`, then `h(x)=g(x)-g(-x)` satisfies `h(-x)=-h(x)`. If `h` were never zero, its sign would be a continuous map from the connected circle to `{+1,-1}`, hence constant, but the antipodal rule forces the sign to flip. So some zero exists. That is not merely a sign trick; it says that the negation of the theorem produces an odd object. In one dimension the odd object has nowhere to live without crossing zero.

Now in higher dimension the same difference is vector-valued: `h(x)=g(x)-g(-x) in R^n`, still with `h(-x)=-h(x)`. If no antipodal pair has the same image, then `h(x)` never vanishes. I can divide by its length and get `F(x)=h(x)/|h(x)|`, a continuous map `S^n -> S^{n-1}` with `F(-x)=-F(x)`. So a coincidence-free `g` is the same data as a continuous odd map from `S^n` down to `S^{n-1}`. The theorem I want to prove is that no such map exists.

Let me be honest about what is and is not forbidden here. A collapse from `S^n` to `S^{n-1}` is easy if I ignore antipodes; projection followed by normalization works away from two poles and can be patched in many non-symmetric ways. So dimension-lowering by itself is not the obstruction. The extra constraint is that every pair `{x,-x}` must map to a pair of opposite outputs, all the way around the sphere. I do not yet know that this is impossible — I only know it is the feature I have to detect. I need an invariant that responds to the antipodal involution and not just to dimension.

My first instinct is to reach for degree, since degree is the global count a chart cannot see. But degree is defined for a self-map `S^m -> S^m`, and `F` goes `S^n -> S^{n-1}` with mismatched dimensions. Let me check whether I can force the dimensions to match. The cheapest fix would be to compose `F` with the inclusion `S^{n-1} \hookrightarrow S^n` and read off a degree of `S^n -> S^n`. But that composite misses the top of the target: its image sits inside an equatorial `S^{n-1}`, so it is not surjective, and a non-surjective map of `S^n` to itself has degree `0`. That tells me nothing — degree `0` is consistent both with `F` existing and with `F` not existing, because I built the zero by hand when I lowered into a proper subsphere. So pushing `F` straight into a degree computation is a dead end; the dimension mismatch is not cosmetic.

The mismatch points to a different move. Instead of inflating the target, restrict the domain. Inside `S^n` there is an equatorial `S^{n-1}` bounding a hemisphere. Restrict `F` to that equator. Now I have an odd self-map `u : S^{n-1} -> S^{n-1}`, and this one has matching dimensions, so degree applies to it.

So the question becomes: what is `deg(u)` for an odd self-map of a sphere? I do not want to assert an answer; I want to compute on cases I can actually hold in my hand. Take `m=1`, the circle, and use the power maps `z |-> z^k` in complex coordinates, whose degree is `k`. Such a map is odd exactly when `(-z)^k=-z^k`, i.e. `(-1)^k=-1`, i.e. `k` is odd. Running that out: `k=1` gives the identity, odd, degree `1`; `k=3` is odd, degree `3`; `k=-1` is odd, degree `-1`; `k=2` is `not` antipode-preserving and indeed `(-1)^2=1`. So among power maps, the antipode-preserving ones are precisely those of odd exponent, and their degree is that odd exponent. Every odd power map of the circle I can write down has odd degree, and every even-degree power map fails to be odd. That is the first real signal that oddness of the map is forcing oddness of the degree, not a coincidence of one example.

Let me push the pattern with the antipodal map itself, in every dimension. On `S^m` the antipodal map `a(x)=-x` is odd by definition and has degree `(-1)^{m+1}`. For `m=0` that is `-1`; `m=1` gives `+1`; `m=2` gives `-1`; `m=3` gives `+1`. Each of these is an odd integer. So the two most basic odd maps available to me, identity-like power maps and the antipodal map, both land on odd degree across the dimensions I can check. The conjecture I now want to prove is: every continuous odd self-map of `S^m` has odd degree.

The cases are evidence, not a proof, and I cannot reach all odd maps by writing formulas. I need the structural reason oddness controls parity. Oddness means `u` commutes with the antipodal involution, so it descends through the quotient by antipodes, `S^m -> RP^m`, giving `bar u : RP^m -> RP^m`; together `u` and `bar u` form a map of the double cover to itself. The natural place where the involution leaves an algebraic trace is `Z/2` homology, where the two-sheeted covering `S^m -> RP^m` has a transfer exact sequence

`... -> H_i(RP^m; Z/2) -> H_i(S^m; Z/2) -> H_i(RP^m; Z/2) -> H_{i-1}(RP^m; Z/2) -> ...`.

Because `u, bar u` form a map of covers, they induce a commutative map of this exact sequence to itself. In dimension `0` the induced map is the identity on `H_0(=Z/2)`, an isomorphism. The intermediate groups `H_i(S^m;Z/2)` vanish for `0<i<m`, and the surviving `RP^m` groups are single copies of `Z/2`, on which a nonzero self-map has no choice but to be the identity. Chasing the diagram, exactness drags the isomorphism upward one step at a time, so `u_* : H_m(S^m;Z/2) -> H_m(S^m;Z/2)` is again an isomorphism, i.e. nonzero. On top homology with `Z/2` coefficients `u_*` is multiplication by `deg(u) mod 2`, so `deg(u)` is `1 mod 2`. The general statement matches every case I computed by hand, which is the check I wanted before trusting the diagram chase: the identity (degree `1`), the odd power maps (odd degree), and the antipodal map (degree `(-1)^{m+1}`) all have odd degree, and the argument now explains why this is forced rather than lucky.

So `u`, the equatorial restriction of `F`, has odd degree. Now I use the other thing I know about `u`: it does not just sit on the equator, it extends across a hemisphere, because `F` is defined on all of `S^n`. Let `D^n` be one closed hemisphere with boundary the equator `E \cong S^{n-1}`. Then `u=F|_E` and `F|_{D^n}` extends `u` over the disk. On top homology the induced map of `u` factors through the homology of the disk:

`H_{n-1}(S^{n-1}) -> H_{n-1}(D^n) -> H_{n-1}(S^{n-1})`,

and `H_{n-1}(D^n)=0` because a disk is contractible. So `u_*` is the zero map on `H_{n-1}`, which means `deg(u)=0`.

That is the collision: `deg(u)` is odd, and `deg(u)` is `0`. Zero is even, so the two are inconsistent and the map `F` cannot exist. Let me confirm the collision is real and not an artifact of the homological machinery by re-running it concretely in the smallest interesting case, `n=2`, where degree is just winding number and I can compute it numerically. There `F : S^2 -> S^1` would restrict to an odd loop `u : S^1 -> S^1`. Taking explicit odd loops `u(\theta)=e^{ik\theta}` with `k` odd, the winding numbers come out exactly `k`: `1, 3, 5, -1, -3` for `k=1,3,5,-1,-3`, each verified to satisfy `u(\theta+\pi)=-u(\theta)` so each is genuinely odd. So an odd equatorial loop is forced into winding number from the set `{...,-3,-1,1,3,...}`. But a loop that bounds a hemisphere, i.e. extends to `D^2 -> S^1`, is nullhomotopic and has winding number `0`. The set of odd integers and `{0}` have empty intersection, so in `n=2` the contradiction is explicit and computed, not merely asserted. The general-`n` argument is the same statement with winding number replaced by degree mod `2`.

This also explains, after the fact, why the proof wanted the normalized difference rather than `g` itself. The original values live in `R^n`, and equal antipodal values are exactly what I am trying to force; subtracting `g(-x)` from `g(x)` converts the equality I want into a zero, and the assumed absence of that zero is what lets me normalize. Normalizing strips away magnitude and leaves the only topological content: a symmetry-respecting direction assigned to each point of the sphere. The local-coordinate instinct asked where `n` scalar functions vanish at once; the invariant instead asks what would have to be true if they never did, and that hypothetical object cannot exist.

The low-dimensional cases now sit inside the same picture rather than apart from it. For `n=1` the target of the normalized odd map is `S^0`, two points, and a connected circle cannot map continuously onto two points while flipping antipodes — the same connectedness argument as the very first sign computation. For `n=2` the contradiction is the winding-number clash I just computed. For larger `n`, winding number becomes degree, oddness still forces odd degree through the transfer sequence, and bounding a hemisphere still forces degree `0`.

So the proof lands in a compact form. Suppose `g : S^n -> R^n` is continuous and no antipodal pair has equal image. Then `h(x)=g(x)-g(-x)` is nonzero and odd, hence `F(x)=h(x)/|h(x)|` is an odd map `S^n -> S^{n-1}`. Choose an equatorial `S^{n-1}` in `S^n` and call the restriction `u`. Since `u` is odd, `deg(u)` is odd. Since `u` extends over the hemisphere by `F`, `deg(u)=0`. That is impossible. Therefore the assumption was false, and some antipodal pair satisfies `g(x)=g(-x)`.

What carried the argument was not fixed-point folklore but the choice of an invariant that can hear the antipodal involution. The local search for a coincidence point became a global question about whether the sphere admits an antipode-respecting collapse to one lower dimension, and degree mod `2` answered that it does not.
