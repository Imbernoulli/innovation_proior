Garwin is in my office again, prodding. He keeps describing this problem — find the periodicities in the spin orientations of helium-3 in a three-dimensional crystal, $N$ a power of two along each axis — and he won't let it drop, even though I've got other things going and it doesn't strike me as urgent. What he keeps circling back to is a way of computing Fourier transforms that he has picked up from John Tukey at President Kennedy's Scientific Advisory Council: take the number of points $N$, write it as a product of factors, and break the one big transform into many small transforms on those factors; carry the factoring all the way down and a transform of $N = 2^k$ points costs on the order of $N \log N$ operations instead of the usual count. I know Tukey — I programmed spectral analysis of wind velocities for him years back, the correlation-first Blackman–Tukey way, which was built precisely to *dodge* doing a long Fourier transform — so when Garwin lays out an idea for doing the long transform itself fast, it doesn't come from nowhere. Garwin is vague about why this helium calculation matters so much, but his urgency is plain, and the same arithmetic wall would be even sharper for the test-ban problem he and Tukey have been discussing: spectra of seismometer time series from stations around the Soviet Union, so underground nuclear tests can be detected without on-site inspection. For the calculation in front of me, the visible fact is already enough: the brute-force cost is the wall, and Garwin is convinced that if I just program Tukey's factoring it falls. Fine. Let me actually sit down and work out whether it does, and what exactly the program has to be.

Start from what I have to compute. There are $N$ samples $A(0), \dots, A(N-1)$ and I want all $N$ Fourier coefficients

$$X(j) = \sum_{k=0}^{N-1} A(k)\, W^{jk}, \qquad W = e^{-2\pi i/N}.$$

Written out, this is just a matrix times a vector: the matrix $F$ has $(j,k)$ entry $W^{jk}$, and $X = F A$. A dense $N \times N$ matrix-vector product is $N^2$ complex multiplications. For a few hundred points that's fine; but the sizes Garwin keeps gesturing at are nothing like a few hundred — three-dimensional grids, long records — and at $N = 10^6$ it's $10^{12}$ multiplies, which on the machines I have is the difference between a coffee break and weeks of time the computer simply doesn't have to give. That's why the count matters so much to him: the brute-force transform is already past what the hardware can chew through, so unless the *order* drops, the scheme has no practical path. So the game Tukey is pointing at is concrete: beat $N^2$. The naive method treats every entry of $F$ as if it were an arbitrary number. But it isn't. Every entry is a power of one number, $W$, and Tukey's factor-$N$ idea has to be exploiting exactly that. Let me stare at the structure and see if I can rebuild his reduction from the ground up, because I'd rather understand it than transcribe it.

What do I actually know about $W$? Two things, and they're cheap. First, $W^N = 1$ — the exponents only matter mod $N$, so the matrix entries $W^{jk}$ wrap around; there are at most $N$ distinct values among the $N^2$ entries, not $N^2$. Second, and this is the one that smells useful: if $N$ is even, $W^{N/2} = e^{-2\pi i \cdot (N/2)/N} = e^{-\pi i} = -1$. And $W^2 = e^{-2\pi i \cdot 2/N} = e^{-2\pi i/(N/2)}$, which is *itself* a primitive root of unity of order $N/2$. So squaring $W$ gives me the root of unity for a half-length problem. That can't be a coincidence I should ignore — it says a length-$N$ transform has a length-$N/2$ transform hiding inside it.

So the redundancy is real. The question is how to convert "the same value appears many times" into "I do the arithmetic once and reuse it." Grouping equal entries the way the hand-computers do — collect all the terms that multiply the same $\cos$ value, add them first, multiply once — saves a constant factor, sure. But the savings there are bounded: you remove duplicate *multiplications by equal values*, but you never actually build a shorter transform, so the count stays $\Theta(N^2)$. That's a dead end if I want the *order* to drop, and the order is the whole point — it's the only thing that moves a million-point spectrum from out of reach on this machine to feasible. To change the order I need to express the length-$N$ DFT in terms of genuinely shorter DFTs. That is exactly what Tukey's "write $N$ as a product of factors" is telling me to do, and the squaring observation is pointing right at the simplest version of it. The crystal problem is a power of two in each dimension, so the factor I most need to make work is $2$ — split $N$ into $2 \times (N/2)$ and recurse. Let me chase the radix-2 case first and worry about general factors after.

How do I get a length-$N/2$ transform to fall out of the sum? The sum runs over all $k$. If I split the index $k$ into two classes and the exponent simplifies on each class, I might get two half-sums each of which is a clean shorter DFT. The squaring fact involved $W^2$, i.e. *even* powers, so let me split $k$ by parity: $k = 2m$ for the even-indexed samples and $k = 2m+1$ for the odd-indexed ones, each with $m = 0, \dots, N/2 - 1$.

$$X(j) = \sum_{m=0}^{N/2-1} A(2m)\, W^{j(2m)} + \sum_{m=0}^{N/2-1} A(2m+1)\, W^{j(2m+1)}.$$

Now pull the structure out of the exponents. $W^{j(2m)} = (W^2)^{jm}$, and I just noted $W^2 = e^{-2\pi i/(N/2)}$ — call it $W_{N/2}$, the half-length root. So the first sum is $\sum_m A(2m)\, W_{N/2}^{\,jm}$ — that is *exactly* a length-$N/2$ DFT, of the even subsequence, evaluated at index $j$. For the odd sum, $W^{j(2m+1)} = W^{j}\,(W^2)^{jm} = W^j\, W_{N/2}^{\,jm}$, so I can factor the single $W^j$ out front:

$$X(j) = \underbrace{\sum_{m} A(2m)\, W_{N/2}^{\,jm}}_{E(j)} \;+\; W^{j}\underbrace{\sum_{m} A(2m+1)\, W_{N/2}^{\,jm}}_{O(j)}.$$

There it is. $E$ is the length-$N/2$ DFT of the even-indexed inputs, $O$ is the length-$N/2$ DFT of the odd-indexed inputs, and $X(j) = E(j) + W^j O(j)$. The single factor $W^j$ that splices the two halves together — I'll keep calling that the twiddle factor, the thing that "corrects" the odd half for being shifted one sample over.

But wait — something is off if I read this too fast. $E(j)$ and $O(j)$ are defined for a length-$N/2$ transform, so they only naturally make sense for $j = 0, \dots, N/2 - 1$. Yet $X(j)$ runs over the full range $j = 0, \dots, N-1$. How do I get $N$ outputs out of two half-length transforms that each only have $N/2$ values? For a moment this looks like it can't close — I'm short by half the outputs. Stare at it. $E$ and $O$ are DFTs of length $N/2$, so they are *periodic* in $j$ with period $N/2$: $E(j + N/2) = E(j)$, same for $O$. So the formula $X(j) = E(j) + W^j O(j)$ does extend to $j \ge N/2$ — I just reuse $E(j \bmod N/2)$ and $O(j \bmod N/2)$. The only thing that changes between $j$ and $j + N/2$ is the twiddle. And here the halving fact pays off: $W^{j + N/2} = W^j \cdot W^{N/2} = W^j \cdot (-1) = -W^j$. So

$$X(j) = E(j) + W^{j} O(j), \qquad X(j + N/2) = E(j) - W^{j} O(j), \qquad j = 0, \dots, N/2-1.$$

This is much better than I even hoped. I don't recompute anything for the upper half. I compute $E(j)$ and $O(j)$ once, form the product $W^j O(j)$ *once* — that's a single complex multiply — and then I get *two* outputs, $X(j)$ and $X(j+N/2)$, by adding and subtracting. One multiply and two adds, yielding two of the $N$ answers. That little add/subtract pair sharing one twiddle multiply — the butterfly — is the unit of work, and the sign flip is just $W^{N/2} = -1$ doing its job. So a full level of recombination over all $j < N/2$ costs $N/2$ complex multiplies and $N$ adds, i.e. $O(N)$, to merge two half-transforms into the whole.

Now the recursion, because $E$ and $O$ are the same kind of object I started with, only half as long. Computing each of them naively would be $(N/2)^2$, two of those is $N^2/2$ plus the $O(N)$ merge — already half the work, but I haven't recursed yet. The point is I don't compute $E$ and $O$ naively: I apply the *same* split to each. $E$, the even subsequence's transform, splits into its even-even and even-odd quarter-length transforms; $O$ likewise. Let me just write the cost recurrence. If $T(N)$ is the cost of a length-$N$ transform, then

$$T(N) = 2\,T(N/2) + cN,$$

two subproblems of half the size plus a linear merge. Unrolling: at the top level I pay $cN$; that spawns two problems each paying $c(N/2)$, total $cN$ again; four problems each $c(N/4)$, total $cN$ again; and so on. Every level costs $cN$, and the number of levels is the number of times I can halve $N$ down to $1$, which is $\log_2 N$. The leaves add only another linear term, so $T(N) = O(N) + cN \log_2 N = O(N\log N)$. That's the whole prize: $N \log_2 N$ instead of $N^2$. Put numbers on it at $N = 2^{20} = 1{,}048{,}576 \approx 10^6$: the naive cost is $N^2 \approx 1.10 \times 10^{12}$, and $N\log_2 N = 1{,}048{,}576 \times 20 \approx 2.10 \times 10^7$. The ratio is $N^2 / (N\log_2 N) = N/\log_2 N = 1{,}048{,}576 / 20 \approx 52{,}400$ — better than four orders of magnitude, the difference between "the machine cannot do this" and "the machine does this before lunch." The recursion bottoms out at $N = 1$, and the transform of a single sample is just that sample copied to the output — the one-point DFT is the identity.

I should make sure the recursion is clean, which means I want $N$ to halve exactly every time, all the way down. That's true precisely when $N$ is a power of two. If an application lets me choose a longer record, I can append zeros and transform that longer record, but that is not the same object as the exact length-$N$ transform; for an exact transform at the given length, I have to factor $N$ itself. Power-of-two first; it makes every split exact and every level uniform, and it is exactly the shape of the three-dimensional helium calculation Garwin has put in front of me.

Now there's a bookkeeping question that I'd better nail before writing code, because the recursion shuffles the data in a specific way. At each level I separate even-indexed from odd-indexed entries. Even index means the least-significant bit of $k$ is $0$; odd means it's $1$. So the first split sorts by bit 0 of $k$. Within the even half, the next split sorts by the next bit; within the odd half, likewise. After $\log_2 N$ splits, each input has been routed by all its bits, *least-significant first*. Follow any leaf down the tree — a path of "even/odd, even/odd, …" choices — and the sample sitting there is the one whose bits, read in the order the splits tested them (LSB first), match that path. Read the path most-significant-first instead and you get the reversed bit pattern. So the sample that ends up at leaf position $p$ is the input $A(k)$ where $k$ is the bit-reversal of $p$. Concretely for $N=8$, position $001$ holds input $100$, position $011$ holds $110$, and so on — reverse the bits.

That's a gift. It means I don't need the recursion's call stack at all. If I first permute a working array into bit-reversed order, then the leaves are already laid out as adjacent one-point transforms, and I can build the answer bottom-up with plain loops. Adjacent pairs combine into 2-point transforms; adjacent pairs of pairs into 4-point transforms; and so on, doubling the block length each pass until the two halves of the whole array combine into the length-$N$ result. There are $\log_2 N$ passes, each touching all $N$ elements once — the same $N \log_2 N$, now with no recursion and with the butterflies themselves in place: each butterfly reads two slots and writes the two sums back into those same two slots. The bit-reversal permutation can also be done by swapping element $p$ with element $\mathrm{rev}(p)$; since reversal is an involution ($\mathrm{rev}(\mathrm{rev}(p)) = p$), the swaps are disjoint, and there are $N$ of them — cheap relative to the transform itself. In code I can still make the working array as a fresh list so the caller's input is left alone.

Let me make the iterative passes precise so the twiddles come out right. Pass $s$ builds transforms of length $L = 2^s$, for $s = 1, \dots, \log_2 N$. Within a length-$L$ block, the relevant root of unity is $W_L = e^{-2\pi i/L}$, because a length-$L$ transform's twiddles are powers of *its own* root, and the butterfly at offset $r$ inside the block uses twiddle $W_L^{\,r}$ for $r = 0, \dots, L/2 - 1$, pairing element $r$ with element $r + L/2$. So within each block I march a running twiddle $w$ starting at $1$ and multiplying by $W_L$ each step: $u = a[\text{start}+r]$, $t = w \cdot a[\text{start}+r+L/2]$, then $a[\text{start}+r] = u + t$ and $a[\text{start}+r+L/2] = u - t$, then $w \leftarrow w\,W_L$. That add/subtract pair is exactly the $E(j) \pm W^j O(j)$ I derived, just localized to one block at one pass. The only subtlety worth flagging: I compute the root $W_L$ once per pass (a single transcendental evaluation) and propagate $w$ by multiplication through the inner loop, rather than calling sine/cosine inside the loop — $\log_2 N$ transcendental calls total instead of $O(N \log N)$. (Repeated complex multiplication does drift in floating point over a long block; the practical fix is the trigonometric recurrence that updates $(\cos, \sin)$ stably, but the algorithm is the same.)

Let me sanity-check the whole thing on the smallest non-trivial case, $N = 2$. Bit reversal of two elements is the identity. One pass, $L = 2$, $W_2 = e^{-\pi i} = -1$, one butterfly with $w = 1$: $X(0) = A(0) + A(1)$, $X(1) = A(0) - A(1)$. That's the correct 2-point DFT. Good.

I don't trust that the two-pass composition is right until I run it once with actual numbers, because the place a derivation like this goes wrong is the indexing — the bit-reversal routing and which twiddle lands where. So take $N = 4$ and a definite input, $A = (1, 2, 3, 4)$, and grind both the iterative procedure and the direct sum and see if they agree. Direct first: $W_4 = e^{-2\pi i/4} = -i$, and $X(j) = \sum_k A(k)(-i)^{jk}$. $X(0) = 1+2+3+4 = 10$. $X(1) = 1 + 2(-i) + 3(-1) + 4(i) = (1-3) + (-2+4)i = -2 + 2i$. $X(2) = 1 + 2(-1) + 3(1) + 4(-1) = -2$. $X(3) = 1 + 2(i) + 3(-1) + 4(-i) = -2 - 2i$. So the target is $(10,\ -2+2i,\ -2,\ -2-2i)$.

Now the iterative side. Bit-reverse the indices ($2$ bits): position $00$ holds input $00$, position $01$ holds input $10$, position $10$ holds input $01$, position $11$ holds input $11$ — so the working array is $(A_0, A_2, A_1, A_3) = (1, 3, 2, 4)$. Pass $L=2$: $W_2 = -1$, $w=1$, two blocks. Block $(1,3)$: $u=1$, $t=1\cdot 3=3$, giving $1+3=4$ and $1-3=-2$. Block $(2,4)$: $2+4=6$, $2-4=-2$. Array is now $(4, -2, 6, -2)$. Pass $L=4$: one block, $W_4=-i$, $w$ runs $1$ then $-i$. At $r=0$: $u=4$, $t=1\cdot 6 = 6$, giving slot $0 = 4+6 = 10$ and slot $2 = 4-6 = -2$. At $r=1$: $w=-i$, $u=-2$, $t=(-i)(-2)=2i$, giving slot $1 = -2+2i$ and slot $3 = -2-2i$. Final array $(10,\ -2+2i,\ -2,\ -2-2i)$. That is the direct answer, entry for entry. The pieces lock together — and crucially the bit-reversal routing and the per-block twiddle schedule were exactly what made the two columns line up, which is the part I most doubted.

Now — is power-of-two really essential, or did I just pick it for convenience? Go back to the split. Nothing about it needed the factor to be $2$. Suppose $N = r_1 r_2$. To keep the parity split as the special case, I should group the input by its residue modulo $r_1$: write $k = k_0 + r_1 k_1$, where $k_0 = 0,\dots,r_1-1$ and $k_1 = 0,\dots,r_2-1$. The matching output split is $j = j_0 + r_2 j_1$, where $j_0 = 0,\dots,r_2-1$ and $j_1 = 0,\dots,r_1-1$. I should check this is actually a relabeling and not a lossy one, because the whole reduction is worthless if two distinct $(k_0,k_1)$ pairs collide onto the same $k$. The map $(k_0, k_1) \mapsto k_0 + r_1 k_1$ with $0 \le k_0 < r_1$ is just division with remainder: $k_0$ is $k \bmod r_1$ and $k_1$ is $\lfloor k/r_1\rfloor$, so it is a bijection from the $r_1 r_2 = N$ pairs onto $\{0,\dots,N-1\}$ — every $k$ hit once. The $j$ split is the same statement with the roles of $r_1, r_2$ swapped, so it is a bijection too. (Take $N=6$, $r_1=2$: the pairs $(k_0,k_1)$ give $k = 0,2,4$ for $k_0{=}0$ and $1,3,5$ for $k_0{=}1$ — all six, no repeats.) Substitute into $W^{jk}$ and expand the exponent $jk = (j_0 + r_2 j_1)(k_0 + r_1 k_1)$:

$$jk = j_0 k_0 + j_0 k_1\,r_1 + j_1 k_0\,r_2 + j_1 k_1\,r_1 r_2.$$

The last term has the factor $r_1 r_2 = N$, so $W^{j_1 k_1 N} = (W^N)^{j_1 k_1} = 1$ — it vanishes. The term $W^{j_0 k_1 r_1}$ is $(W^{r_1})^{j_0 k_1} = W_{r_2}^{\,j_0 k_1}$, the $r_2$-th root inside each residue class. The term $W^{j_1 k_0 r_2}$ is $(W^{r_2})^{j_1 k_0} = W_{r_1}^{\,j_1 k_0}$, the $r_1$-th root used to combine the residue classes. And the leftover cross term $W^{j_0 k_0}$ couples the two stages — there's the twiddle again, now in general form. So, grouping the sum,

$$X(j_0 + r_2 j_1) = \sum_{k_0=0}^{r_1-1} W_{r_1}^{\,j_1 k_0}\; W^{\,j_0 k_0} \Big[ \sum_{k_1=0}^{r_2-1} A(k_0 + r_1 k_1)\, W_{r_2}^{\,j_0 k_1} \Big].$$

Read inside out: the bracketed inner sum, for each fixed residue $k_0$, is a length-$r_2$ DFT over $k_1$ — there are $r_1$ of them. Multiply each result by the twiddle $W^{j_0 k_0}$. Then the outer sum over $k_0$ is a length-$r_1$ DFT — there are $r_2$ of them, one for each $j_0$. Counting with direct subtransforms for one split: $r_1$ transforms of length $r_2$ plus $r_2$ transforms of length $r_1$ gives roughly $r_1 r_2^2 + r_2 r_1^2 = N(r_1 + r_2)$ operations instead of $N^2$. And the radix-2 butterfly is exactly this with $r_1 = 2$: $k_0=0$ is the even subsequence, $k_0=1$ is the odd subsequence, $j_1=0$ gives $E(j_0)+W^{j_0}O(j_0)$, and $j_1=1$ contributes the factor $W_2^{1\cdot 1}=-1$ to give $E(j_0)-W^{j_0}O(j_0)$.

The recursion now generalizes: if $r_1$ or $r_2$ is itself composite, factor it again. Carried to the prime factorization $N = p_1 p_2 \cdots p_L$, the cost is $N(p_1 + p_2 + \dots + p_L)$ — $N$ times the sum of the prime factors, up to the linear twiddle work that sits between stages. When all the primes are $2$ ($N = 2^L$) that sum is $2L = 2\log_2 N$, recovering $2N\log_2 N$ with the same order as the radix-2 recurrence. The lesson is that the method loves *highly composite* $N$, and conversely this factorization alone is helpless when $N$ is prime: there is no nontrivial factorization, the sum-of-prime-factors is just $N$ itself, and the exact length-$N$ computation is back at $N^2$. If the scientific question tolerates a zero-padded longer record, I choose a composite length; if it demands exactly the prime length, this decomposition has no smaller pieces to expose.

This general-factor statement is exactly what Tukey is pointing at from the start — "any set of factors will do" — and the radix-2 even/odd split I chased first is just its smallest instance. When I compare the algebra with the older arithmetic, the doubling rules line up. Runge builds a $2N$-point transform from two $N$-point ones with about $N$ extra operations — the $r_1 = 2$ merge, but stated only in the doubling direction, $N \to 2N$, so it is tied to repeated doubling rather than a single recursion. Stumpff adds a tripling rule ($r=3$) and hints at a general multiple. The Danielson-Lanczos halving identity says a length-$N$ DFT is the sum of two length-$N/2$ DFTs on the even and odd samples, applied recursively — precisely the radix-2 case, prized in hand work because the redundant recomputation doubles the length for barely more than the half-length labor and doubles as an accuracy check. What I still need for Garwin's computation is the general composite-$N$ statement with the explicit twiddle $W^{j_0 k_0}$ between the two stages and the index reordering that makes it a uniform machine procedure for any factorization, because I am handing this to a computer that wants one regular loop nest, not a different hand-recipe per $N$.

There is a different factorization worth distinguishing when I check related schemes, because it looks similar and is not the same. If $r_1$ and $r_2$ are *coprime*, the Chinese Remainder Theorem gives an index map under which the cross term disappears entirely — no twiddle at all, just a genuine two-dimensional $r_1 \times r_2$ transform. That is a real, multiplication-saving scheme, but the coprimality is essential to it, and that is exactly why it cannot do what I want here: $N = 2^L$ factors as $2 \cdot 2 \cdots 2$, and those factors are *not* coprime, so the CRT route cannot recurse on a power of two at all. The twiddle-factor version I derived has no such restriction — it works for *any* factorization, coprime or not, including the repeated factor of $2$ — and that generality, plus the in-place butterfly bookkeeping, is what turns the splitting idea into a single algorithm I can just run.

Let me write it as one self-contained program: read $N$ (a power of two) and the $N$ complex samples from stdin, run the loop form — the same butterflies on a bit-reversed working array — and print the $N$ coefficients. I keep `dft_direct` alongside as the small-$N$ oracle and `inverse_transform` as the conjugate-root wrapper.

The real footgun is the bit-reversal routing plus the per-block twiddle schedule, so if I cannot verify those indices within budget, I fall back to the simpler recursive even/odd Cooley-Tukey form I already traced.

The chain, end to end: Garwin hands me the helium-3 computation and Tukey's factor-$N$ hint while the same arithmetic wall is sitting inside test-ban seismometer spectra; the DFT is a matrix-vector product whose matrix is all powers of one root of unity and is therefore drowning in redundancy; the redundancy is exposed by splitting the sum on the index, where $W^2$ being the half-length root makes each half a genuine shorter DFT and the cross factor $W^j$ — the twiddle — splices them; the periodicity of the half-transforms plus $W^{N/2} = -1$ lets one twiddle multiply produce two outputs, the butterfly; recursing gives $T(N) = 2T(N/2) + O(N) = O(N\log N)$; and the leaf ordering being bit-reversal turns the recursion into doubling passes with in-place butterflies on a working array — which generalizes from radix 2 to any composite $N = r_1 r_2$ via the same index reindexing and the twiddle $W^{j_0 k_0}$, at cost $N$ times the sum of the prime factors. That last generality is the part that makes it a program rather than a hand recipe: one routine, any highly composite $N$, the crystal's power-of-two grid as the case I run first.
