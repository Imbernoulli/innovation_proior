Let me start from what actually breaks when I make a network deep, because depth is the thing I keep wanting and keep being punished for. The expressive power of a stack of `L` layers grows exponentially in `L` but only polynomially in the width — Poole, Montufar, and that whole line make this precise — so if I want a more powerful function for a fixed parameter budget, I add depth, not width. But every time I add depth, training gets harder, and I want to be exact about the mechanism rather than waving at "vanishing gradients." A signal enters at `x_0`, propagates forward through `L` layers to `x_L`, the loss is read off `x_L`, and then the gradient has to propagate all the way back to update the early weights. If each layer multiplies the size of a perturbation by some factor `r` — and generically it does, because each layer is a linear map composed with a nonlinearity — then a perturbation at the input is scaled by `r^L` by the time it reaches the output, and symmetrically the gradient that comes back is scaled by `r^L` too. Any `r` that isn't exactly 1 gets raised to the power `L`. For `r > 1` the forward signal and the backward gradient explode; for `r < 1` they collapse to nothing. So the entire game at large depth is: keep the per-layer factor `r` pinned at 1.

The mean-field people made "`r ≈ 1`" into a sharp statement, and I should lean on their object directly. Linearize the whole network: the input-output Jacobian is `J = ∂x_L/∂x_0 = ∏_{l=1}^L D^l W^l`, where `D^l = diag(phi'(h^l))` carries the activation derivatives and `W^l` the weights. The growth rate of an average perturbation is controlled by the mean squared singular value of `J`, which works out to `chi^L` for a single-layer quantity `chi = (1/N)⟨Tr (DW)^T DW⟩`. There's a phase boundary: `chi > 1` is the chaotic phase, gradients explode; `chi < 1` is the ordered phase, gradients vanish; right at `chi = 1`, the edge of chaos, on average signal strength is preserved layer to layer. And the conventional wisdom — Glorot/Bengio, He — is exactly "rescale the weights so `chi ≈ 1`." That gets the *average* perturbation to survive.

But I keep reading Pennington, Schoenholz and Ganguli, and they make a point that I think is the real crux and that the `chi = 1` recipe misses. A unit *mean* squared singular value is necessary but not sufficient. The mean can be 1 while the *distribution* of singular values is awful — a few enormous ones, a lot of tiny ones. If the singular vectors attached to the large or the vanishing singular values happen to line up with the perturbations that actually live in my data, then those data perturbations get blown up or annihilated even though the average is fine, and training is still slow. The condition that actually buys efficient deep training is the stronger one Saxe et al. called *dynamical isometry*: not just `mean(sigma_i^2) = 1`, but *all* the singular values of `J` clustered near 1. Then every perturbation of the input — not just the average one — propagates through the network preserved, and the same holds for the gradient on the way back. They showed for deep linear networks that orthogonal initialization achieves exactly this and makes the number of epochs to train essentially *independent of depth*, while Gaussian init does not. Depth-independent training time is a strong enough claim that I want to treat isometry, not merely `chi = 1`, as the property worth chasing.

This is where I hit the wall. For *nonlinear* networks the achievability of dynamical isometry depends on the nonlinearity, through the distribution of `phi'`. For tanh or sigmoid with orthogonal weights I can tune it. But for ReLU it looks impossible. A ReLU has derivative 0 wherever its input is negative, so on a typical input roughly half the diagonal of each `D^l` is exactly 0. Those exact zeros pass straight through to `J = ∏ D^l W^l`: a product where one factor has rank deficiency `k` has rank at most `N − k`, so `J` itself carries vanishing singular values no matter what I choose for the orthogonal `W^l`. Orthogonality of `W` controls the *conditioning* of the rotation part but cannot refill the zeros that `D` punched out. So the obstruction is real and structural, and essentially everything I train is ReLU-based, including the convolutional ResNets I care about. The cleanest known path to dynamical isometry — pick an orthogonal init for the weights — is closed to me by the very nonlinearity I'm committed to. And it's worse for the self-attention stacks that just became dominant: LayerNorm is invariant to any shift of an input's mean or variance, which puts two exact zero singular values into the Jacobian per token per layer; and a softmax that's near-uniform (which it is at small initial logits) collapses all `n` embedding vectors onto one direction, killing all but `d` of the `n×d` singular values. The diagnostic on randomly initialized Transformer encoders says the same thing: shallow stacks can have Jacobian singular values peaked near 1, but by 64 layers a huge fraction have vanished to machine precision. So both of the architectures I want to go deep with are, by construction, barred from dynamical isometry under any clever weight initialization.

Let me also pin down, concretely, what goes wrong at *initialization* in a residual stack specifically, because residual connections are supposed to be the cure and I want to know precisely how they still fail. A residual net accumulates `x_l = x_0 + sum_{i<l} F_i(x_i)`. Take the blocks zero-mean at init, `E[F_i(x_i)|x_i] = 0`, which is what a sensibly initialized random branch gives. Then the branch and the skip are uncorrelated and their variances add: `Var[x_{l+1}] = Var[x_l] + Var[F(x_l)]`. And He-style initialization is designed so a block roughly *preserves* its input variance, `Var[F(x_l)] ≈ Var[x_l]`. Substitute: `Var[x_{l+1}] ≈ 2 Var[x_l]`, so `Var[x_l] ≈ 2^l`. The hidden activations' variance grows *exponentially* with depth at initialization. That's the explosion, derived. And it has a second, subtler face: since the branch contributes a variance equal to the skip's, the block's output is half skip and half branch — the function it computes is *nowhere near* the identity. So a deep residual stack at init is both numerically blown up and far from the identity it would like to fall back on.

This also explains, mechanically, why batch normalization "made deep ResNets trainable." De and Smith make it quantitative: batch norm, sitting in the residual branch, downscales that branch by a factor on the order of `sqrt(depth)` at initialization. Plug a `1/sqrt(d)` factor on `F` into the variance recursion — `Var[x_{l+1}] = Var[x_l] + (1/d)Var[F(x_l)] ≈ Var[x_l](1 + 1/d)` — and over `d` blocks the product `(1 + 1/d)^d` stays `O(1)` instead of exploding as `2^d`. (Concretely at `d = 50`: `(1 + 1/50)^50 = 2.69`, versus `2^50 ≈ 10^15` for the un-downscaled stack — the downscaling collapses fifteen orders of magnitude to a factor under 3.) So the branch is suppressed, the skip path dominates, and each block sits *close to the identity* at init. That's the actual reason normalization rescued depth. So the lesson I extract is not "use batch norm" — batch norm costs compute, adds hyperparameters (and for Transformers the entire question of *where* to put LayerNorm, Post vs Pre vs GPT2-style, is unsettled and each placement converges differently, and the original Post-Norm Transformer flat-out diverges without learning-rate warm-up). The lesson is the *mechanism*: what makes a deep residual stack trainable is that, at initialization, each block is near the identity and the skip path carries the signal, so the input-output map starts near `I`.

So what is the right target? "Near the edge of chaos" was too weak — it's the `chi = 1` condition that lets the *average* perturbation through while the tails misbehave. "Near the identity" is what batch norm achieves and what makes deep stacks trainable. The two suggest pushing the second one to its limit: if a block could start *at* the identity — not biased toward it, not approximately, but exactly — then at initialization the whole network would be the identity map, its Jacobian would be `J = I`, and *all* its singular values would be exactly 1. That is dynamical isometry, and the appeal is that it would hold *regardless of `F`*. The Pennington obstruction was about engineering `J` of a stack of *nontrivial, fully-contributing* ReLU/attention layers to be isometric; the zeros that `D` punches in are unavoidable when the layers are doing work. But if at initialization each layer contributes *nothing*, then `J` shouldn't depend on `F`'s nonlinearity at all. Take a stack of dimension-preserving residual blocks `x_{i+1} = x_i + alpha F_i(x_i)` with a shared scalar `alpha`, and compute `∂x_L/∂x_0` honestly by the chain rule. The per-block Jacobian is `∂x_{i+1}/∂x_i = I + alpha · ∂F_i/∂x_i`, and the full one is the ordered product `∏_{i=L-1}^{0} (I + alpha F_i')`. At `alpha = 0` every factor is `I + 0 = I`, so the product is `I` — and nothing in that line touched what `F_i` is or whether `F_i'` has rank-deficient pieces, because `F_i'` is multiplied by zero. The ReLU zeros, the LayerNorm invariances, the softmax collapse: all of them live inside `F_i'`, and all of them are annihilated by the `alpha = 0` prefactor. I built a stack of `L = 8` blocks of width 4 with a random nonlinear branch `F(x) = W^T tanh(Wx + b)` per layer and finite-differenced the input-output Jacobian:

```
alpha=0.0: ||J - I||_F = 9.1e-11, singular values = [1. 1. 1. 1.]
alpha=0.5: ||J - I||_F = 4.0e+00, singular values = [4.529 2.673 1.806 1.279]
alpha=1.0: ||J - I||_F = 1.6e+01, singular values = [13.289 10.754 2.902 2.092]
```

At `alpha = 0` the Jacobian is the identity to numerical precision and every singular value is exactly 1 — exact dynamical isometry, confirmed on an actual nonlinear stack and not just asserted. And it degrades exactly as the spread of singular values predicts: at `alpha = 1` the largest singular value is already 13 against a smallest near 2 on this tiny example, the kind of dispersion that, raised to a real depth, is the vanishing/exploding problem. So the route out of the impossibility theorem is not to make a fully-active nonlinear stack isometric — that's the wall — but to make every block *silent* at init, which buys `J = I` for free for any `F`.

How do people try to make a block start near the identity? Let me walk the options, because the right answer is going to be the cheapest thing that achieves *exactly* identity, and I want to see why each existing approach overshoots or undershoots. Highway Networks were first to hundreds of layers: `y = H(x)·T(x) + x·(1 - T(x))`, with an input-dependent transform gate `T(x) = sigma(W_T^T x + b_T)`, and the carry bias `b_T` initialized negative so the gate leans toward passing the input through. When `T(x) = 0` the layer is the identity, Jacobian `I`; when `T(x) = 1` it's a plain layer. That's the right idea — gate the layer so it can become identity — but look at the cost. `T` is a *whole weight tensor* `W_T` acting nonlinearly on the signal: a gating sub-network bolted onto every layer, with its own parameters and its own forward cost. And `sigma` never actually reaches 0 or 1, so the network is only *biased* toward identity, never initialized *at* it; the `2^l` variance problem is softened but not removed at init. Too heavy, and it doesn't hit identity exactly.

Savarese, Mazza and Figueiredo saw the same overshoot and stripped the gate down to a single scalar: `u = g(k) f_r(x, W) + x`, where `k` is one scalar per block and `g` is a scalar gate, reasoning — correctly — that learning a single `k → 0` to switch a layer off is far easier than learning a whole tensor `W_g → 0`. One parameter per block, input-independent. That's much closer to what I want, and it confirms the intuition that a *scalar* is enough to control whether a block contributes. But two things still bother me. First, they initialize `k` so that `g(k) = 1` — the block *starts as a standard residual block*, fully contributing. Which means at initialization I'm right back in the `Var ≈ 2^l`, Jacobian-far-from-`I` regime (exactly the `alpha = 1` column I just measured); the layer has to *unlearn* a fully-active random branch to get back toward identity. Second, the scalar is wrapped in a gate function `g(·)` (a clamped ReLU and so on), which constrains the range it can take. And there's zero-`gamma` — if a residual branch ends in a normalization layer, initialize that layer's scale `gamma` to 0 so the branch outputs 0 and the block begins as identity — which *does* start at exact identity, but it zero-initializes many parameters (one per channel) and only works when there's a normalization layer at the end of the branch to hijack. And FixUp trains deep ResNets with no normalization at all by a careful rescaling: zero the last layer of each branch and the classifier, scale the branch's weight layers by `L^{-1/(2m-2)}`, add scalar multipliers and biases — it works, and the rescaling rule is the heart of it, but the exponent depends on the depth `L` *and* on the number of layers per block `m`, and several pieces must be assembled correctly. And SkipInit, concurrent, says: replace the branch's normalization with a single learnable scalar `alpha` initialized to `1/sqrt(d)` (or 0), exactly reproducing the `sqrt(depth)` downscaling batch norm was doing — but it's framed as a *replacement for a normalization layer that's already there*, and `1/sqrt(d)` needs me to know the block count.

Reading them side by side, the constraints narrow. I want a single scalar (Savarese was right about that), input-independent (the gate's job is purely to control the init, which has nothing to do with the data), with *no gate function* wrapping it (let it be any real number — small, large, even negative — don't clamp away expressivity I might want), that starts the block at *exactly* the identity (so I'm at exact dynamical isometry, not biased-toward, not "standard residual that must unlearn"), that needs no knowledge of the depth (so not `1/sqrt(d)`), and that works whether or not there's a normalization layer (so not zero-`gamma`, not SkipInit's framing). Strip all of those away and what's left is almost embarrassingly simple. Put a single scalar `alpha` directly multiplying the residual branch, leave the skip path a pure identity, and initialize `alpha = 0`:

  x_{i+1} = x_i + alpha_i F[W_i](x_i),    alpha_i = 0 at init.

At `alpha = 0` this is exactly the stack I just finite-differenced: every block is `x_{i+1} = x_i`, `J = I`, every singular value exactly 1 — for *any* `F`, ReLU or attention or whatever, because `F` is multiplied by zero. No orthogonal-init scheme, no impossibility to fight, no normalization required, no depth-dependent constant, one parameter per branch and no extra forward cost beyond a scalar multiply.

But wait — if I multiply `F` by zero at init, doesn't that kill learning entirely, freezing the network at the identity forever? Compute the gradients at init rather than guess. The block is `x_{i+1} = x_i + alpha F(x_i)`. The gradient flowing back to the *branch weights* `W` is `∂L/∂W = (∂L/∂x_{i+1}) · alpha · (∂F/∂W)`, and at `alpha = 0` that's *zero* — so yes, `F`'s parameters get no gradient on the very first step, confirming the fear. But now compute the gradient to `alpha` itself: `∂L/∂alpha = (∂L/∂x_{i+1}) · F(x_i)`, and that is *not* zero — `F(x_i)` is some nonzero vector (the random branch evaluated on the data), and the upstream gradient `∂L/∂x_{i+1}` is well-behaved precisely because the rest of the network is the clean identity. So on the first step `alpha` moves off zero, and the instant `alpha ≠ 0` the branch weights `W` start receiving gradient `∝ alpha`. The network *dynamically grows its own depth*: it starts as the identity, then each layer's `alpha` ramps up from zero, turning on its branch's contribution gradually, exactly as fast as the optimizer finds it useful. Nothing is frozen; the order of events is just "turn on the gate, then learn the branch," and the gate turns on immediately.

That dissolves the freezing fear in symbols, but I still want to *see* why starting at zero beats starting at one in the actual training dynamics — and a toy I can fully solve is the way to check it. Take the crudest possible deep net: `L` single-neuron layers, no bias, all sharing one weight `w`, all sharing one gate `alpha`. Then each layer does `x_{i+1} = x_i + alpha w x_i = (1 + alpha w) x_i`, and the whole net is `x_L = (1 + alpha w)^L x_0`. The input-output Jacobian is `J = (1 + alpha w)^L`. Fixing `alpha = 1` is the toy model of a vanilla residual network; treating `alpha` as a learnable parameter initialized at 0 is the toy model of what I'm proposing. Look at `alpha = 1`, `w ≈ 1`: `J = 2^L`, the output is exponentially sensitive to the input — the explosion, in one line. At `w ≈ -1`, `J ≈ 0`, vanishing. At `alpha = 0`, `J = 1` no matter what `w` is — the input magnitude is exactly preserved. The toy is too simple to have a genuine order/chaos transition, but it reproduces the vanishing/exploding gradient behavior faithfully, so it should tell me what I need about the *training dynamics*.

Let me run gradient descent on it by hand. With cost `C` read off `x_L`, the update to `w` is `w ← w − lambda · (∂C/∂x)|_{x_L} · ∂x_L/∂w`, and `∂x_L/∂w = L alpha x_0 (1 + alpha w)^{L-1}`. So

  w ← w − lambda L alpha x_0 (1 + alpha w)^{L-1} C'(x_L).

Now stare at the `alpha = 1` case, `w > 0`. The gradient carries a factor `(1 + w)^{L-1}`, and the loss curvature carries `(1 + w)^{2(L-1)}`. For the step to be stable I'd need `lambda` to shrink like `(1 + w)^{-2(L-1)}` — exponentially small in depth. Now `alpha = 0`. The update to `w` carries the factor `alpha = 0`, so `w` gets *no* gradient on the first step — the thing I worried about. But the update to `alpha` is `alpha ← alpha − lambda · ∂x_L/∂alpha · C'(x_L)` with `∂x_L/∂alpha = L w x_0 (1 + alpha w)^{L-1}`, which at `alpha = 0` is just `L w x_0` — finite, no exponential factor at all because `(1 + 0·w)^{L-1} = 1`. So:

  alpha ← alpha − lambda L w x_0 C'(x_L).

`alpha` takes a clean, polynomial-in-`L` step off zero. The predicted contrast is stark — exponentially small admissible `lambda` for vanilla versus a depth-free one for ReZero — so I implemented both updates literally (`x_0 = 1`, target `y = 1`, cost `C = ½(x_L − y)^2`) and searched for the largest learning rate at which each one converges, as a function of depth. The vanilla update, starting from `w = 0.1` with `alpha` fixed at 1:

```
L= 4: largest stable lr ~ 1.0e-01     L=12: largest stable lr ~ 6.3e-03
L= 8: largest stable lr ~ 2.5e-02     L=16: largest stable lr ~ 1.6e-03
```

— the stable learning rate falls by roughly a factor of 4 for every 4 layers added, i.e. geometric decay in `L`, matching the `(1 + w)^{-2(L-1)}` prediction. (And when I start vanilla deeper into the explosive region, `w = 0.9`, no learning rate down to `1e-12` converges at all for `L ≥ 10`: the ridge is simply unreachable by gradient descent.) The ReZero update, training `alpha` from 0 with `w` fixed, converged to the right answer at `lambda = 1.0` for *every* depth I tried (`L = 5, 10, 20, 30`) — same task, same architecture, the only difference being where `alpha` starts. So the toy doesn't just illustrate the point, it quantifies it: starting at one drops me onto a ridge whose admissible learning rate is exponentially small in depth, while starting at zero lets the gate step off into a well-conditioned region and converge at an `O(1)` rate independent of depth. That is the concrete reason for `alpha = 0` over `alpha = 1`.

I want to double-check the `alpha = 0` versus `alpha = 1/sqrt(d)` question too, because SkipInit's analysis points at `1/sqrt(d)` and I'm claiming plain zero is better. `1/sqrt(d)` is the value that makes the *forward variance at init* exactly `O(1)` instead of `2^d` — it's the minimal nonzero downscaling that tames the explosion while still letting the branch contribute a little. It's a fine choice, but it has two costs zero doesn't: it requires me to know `d`, the number of blocks, so the init is depth-dependent and I have to recompute it per architecture; and it still leaves the branch contributing a nonzero (if small) amount at init, so I'm at "near identity," not "exactly identity" — which means `J` carries a small nonzero `(1/sqrt d)·F'` correction and is no longer exactly isometric. Zero needs no knowledge of `d`, is identical across every architecture and depth, and gives *exact* `J = I` (the `9e-11` I measured). The only thing zero gives up relative to `1/sqrt(d)` is the tiny initial branch contribution — and I just showed the gate turns on by itself on the first step, so I don't need to seed it with a nonzero value. For the initialization problem, zero is simpler and exact.

One more decision to nail: the scalar goes on the *branch*, and the skip stays a pure, unmodulated identity. I could imagine gating the skip instead, or gating both. But the pre-activation ResNet result is precisely that the cleanest gradient flow comes from leaving the identity path *unmodulated* — moving the activation off the merged path so the shortcut is a bare `+x`. The skip path is my highway for the gradient; I don't want anything — no activation, no gate, no norm — sitting on it. (And it matters for the `J = I` argument: with a bare skip the per-block Jacobian is `I + alpha F'`, which is exactly `I` at init; gate the skip too and the identity term itself picks up a factor, breaking the clean `J = I`.) So the scalar modulates only `F`, and the merged path is `x_i + alpha_i F(x_i)`, skip untouched. And `alpha` should not be wrapped in a gate function `g(·)` the way Savarese clamps it: I *want* `alpha` free to become anything the optimizer needs — small, order-one, even negative — and a clamp would only remove expressivity for no benefit, since the init-to-identity job is already done by setting the raw value to 0.

Let me make sure this generalizes off the convolutional case to the architecture that's most painful, the Transformer, because if the scalar is genuinely architecture-agnostic it should drop into a self-attention stack with no special handling. The original Transformer layer is `x_{i+1} = LayerNorm(x_i + sublayer(x_i))` — a post-normalization residual, where `sublayer` is self-attention or the feed-forward, and I showed earlier that the LayerNorm and the softmax both inject vanishing singular values that make a deep stack non-isometric. The same scalar should handle it: drop the LayerNorm and rescale the sublayer,

  x_{i+1} = x_i + alpha_i sublayer(x_i),

with `alpha_i = 0` at init — and I'll share one `alpha_i` across the attention/feed-forward pair within a layer, since they're the two sublayers of one Transformer block and there's no reason to give them separate gates. The `J = I` argument I checked carries over unchanged: the per-sublayer Jacobian is `I + alpha · ∂(sublayer)/∂x`, and the LayerNorm invariances and softmax collapse all live inside that derivative, so at `alpha = 0` they are multiplied away and `J = I` exactly — the pathologies never enter the Jacobian at init. As `alpha_i` grows during training the network can model complex functions again, but the bad initial signal-propagation problem has already been removed. The thing I'd still want to confirm beyond the init argument is whether a deep stack actually *trains well* once the gates start moving — that's an empirical claim about the trajectory, not the init, and I'd want the enwiki8 BPB curves to settle it rather than claiming it here.

There's a practical wrinkle I should record before I write code, because it follows from the same gradient I computed. The scalar `alpha` sits *in front of the entire branch*, so the branch's contribution to the output is linear in `alpha` and the loss is roughly linear in `alpha` near init — which means a too-large step on `alpha` swings the whole branch's contribution wildly and can destabilize training. (This is the same linearity the toy exposed: the `alpha`-update had no exponential factor precisely because the dependence is linear, which is good for stability of small steps but means a *large* step on `alpha` is unbuffered.) When I use an aggressive schedule (the one-cycle superconvergence ramp, with a huge peak learning rate as a regularizer), the body of the network tolerates it but the residual weights `alpha` plausibly do not — they likely need a *small, steady* learning rate of their own. So in that regime I'd hold the `alpha`'s at a small constant learning rate (around 0.1) while the rest of the network rides the big schedule; I'd want to confirm the exact value empirically. With the ordinary step-down or cosine schedule this isn't an issue; it's specifically the extreme-learning-rate regime where the gate's linearity bites.

I can now write the block directly: one added parameter, `self.resweight = nn.Parameter(torch.zeros(1))`, and the merge line becomes `out = self.shortcut(x) + self.resweight * out` in place of the stub's bare addition. The two convolutions, the two batch norms, and the shortcut stay exactly as given — the only change is the one gate and the one line that uses it.

The same edit drops into a Transformer encoder layer with no extra machinery: strip the `LayerNorm` calls, add one `resweight` scalar shared by both sublayers, and multiply each sublayer's output by it before the residual add — `src = src + self.dropout1(self.self_attn(src, src, src)[0] * self.resweight)`, and the identical pattern for the feed-forward sublayer. The attention module, the two linear layers, and dropout are untouched; only the normalization is replaced by the gate.
