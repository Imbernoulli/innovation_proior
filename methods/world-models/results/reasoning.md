I start with the training bottleneck, not with the architecture. I want a large predictor because the environment is a stream of images and actions, and a recurrent net can carry the temporal context that a single frame does not contain. But I do not want to train that whole recurrent net from sparse reward. The reward signal answers only whether a whole behavior worked, while the prediction problem gives me a dense supervised target at every time step. So I want to separate what can be learned cheaply from prediction from what must be learned from reward. The large part should learn to compress and predict experience; the reward-optimized part should be as small as I can make it. Whether "as small as I can make it" can really be small enough for a different optimizer is something I will only know once I count parameters near the end.

The image stream gives me the first split. Predicting directly in pixel space is too expensive and too tied to irrelevant visual detail, so I first need a compact latent state. A plain autoencoder would compress frames, but its latent space can be jagged and unconstrained. Since the temporal model will later sample latent vectors and feed them back as if they were observations, I need those off-training-point latent samples to remain meaningful. A variational autoencoder gives me that. It maps each frame to a Gaussian posterior, samples \(z=\mu+\exp(\log\sigma^2/2)\epsilon\), reconstructs the frame, and pays a KL penalty toward \(N(0,I)\). The reconstruction term preserves the visible state; the KL term keeps the code smooth and capacity-limited enough that generated latent points are not immediately nonsensical.

Now I need the temporal model. If I let an RNN predict a single next latent vector, it will average distinct futures. That is exactly wrong for the survival task: "a projectile is launched" and "no projectile is launched" are separate futures, not two noisy samples around one mean. I need a distribution, and more specifically I need a multimodal one. A mixture density output layer gives the recurrent model that ability. Given the current latent, the action, and the recurrent state, it outputs mixture weights, means, and log standard deviations for the next latent. Training is then negative log likelihood of the observed next latent under the predicted mixture.

I have to be precise about what this likelihood means, because there are two natural readings and I want to pick the one I can actually defend. One reading is a full diagonal-Gaussian mixture: a single component index for the whole latent vector, with each component a product of per-coordinate Gaussians, so the likelihood is \(\sum_k \pi_k \prod_d N(y_d;\mu_{k,d},\sigma_{k,d})\). The other is a factorization where each scalar coordinate carries its own \(K\)-component one-dimensional mixture and I average the scalar losses across coordinates, time, and batch. The per-coordinate scalar loss for a target \(y\) is
\[
-\log \sum_{k=1}^K \pi_k \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\sigma_k}
\exp\left(-\frac12\left(\frac{y-\mu_k}{\sigma_k}\right)^2\right),
\]
with \(\log \pi\) normalized by log-sum-exp and \(\sigma_k=\exp(\mathrm{logstd}_k)\).

I should not just assert these are different; I want to see how they differ before I commit. Take a toy two-dimensional latent with two components, equal weights, \(\sigma=0.5\), and means at \((0,0)\) ("both coordinates low") and \((3,3)\) ("both coordinates high"). Under the joint reading, the component index is shared, so the model says the two coordinates move together. Evaluate the implied density at two points. At the matched corner \((3,3)\) the joint density is \(0.318\) and the factorized density is \(0.159\). At a mixed point \((3,0)\) — coordinate 0 high, coordinate 1 low — the joint density is essentially \(0\), while the factorized density is still \(0.159\), exactly what it gave at the matched corner. So the factorization cannot represent the correlation: it lets each coordinate pick its own component independently, and it spreads equal mass onto the "mixed" corners that the joint model rules out. That is a real loss of expressiveness, but it is also much cheaper, and for feeding latents back into the RNN one coordinate at a time it is the natural shape. I will take the factorized version, knowing precisely what I gave up.

Before trusting the scalar loss I want to confirm the formula evaluates to the mixture log-likelihood I think it does, because the log-sum-exp normalization plus the \(-\mathrm{logstd}-\log\sqrt{2\pi}\) term is easy to get wrong by a constant. Take \(K=2\), unnormalized logits \((0,\log 3)\) so \(\pi=(0.25,0.75)\), means \((0,2)\), unit variances, target \(y=1\). Running the code path — normalize the logits by their log-sum-exp, add \(-\tfrac12((y-\mu_k)/\sigma_k)^2-\mathrm{logstd}_k-\log\sqrt{2\pi}\) per component, then take \(-\log\sum_k\) — gives \(1.41894\). The direct reference \(-\log(0.25\,N(1;0,1)+0.75\,N(1;2,1))\) gives \(1.41894\) as well. They agree, so the loss is the honest mixture NLL and not off by a normalization constant.

The controller can now stay tiny. It does not need to own the visual and temporal capacity; it can read the current latent and the recurrent model's state. The latent tells it what the present frame contains, while the recurrent state summarizes the predictive context that the density model uses for the next latent. A single linear map is the smallest thing that could work, so I try that first: map those features to an action and bound the action to the environment's range. Now I can finally check the claim I deferred at the start — is this small enough to matter? The car case uses \(z\) and the LSTM output \(h\), so the input is \(32+256=288\); a \(288\times 3\) weight plus 3 biases is \(288\cdot 3+3=867\) parameters. The survival case exposes \(z\), the LSTM cell state \(c\), and the LSTM output \(h\), so the input is \(64+512+512=1088\), and the one-output linear map with no bias is \(1088\) parameters. Both are in the hundreds-to-thousand range, against a VAE and RNN with millions. The split really did concentrate almost all capacity on the prediction side.

That number is small enough that I do not need gradients to optimize it. CMA-ES needs only a scalar return from each rollout, and each candidate policy can be evaluated independently, so a population of a few dozen vectors of length ~1000 is well within its comfortable range. That keeps reward credit assignment away from the large VAE and recurrent model. The large models get dense unsupervised losses; the controller gets black-box selection pressure through average rollout return. Had the count come out in the tens of thousands I would have had to reconsider, but at 867 and 1088 a derivative-free search is unproblematic.

The same recurrent density model can also be used as a latent environment. In the survival task, the model predicts both the next latent and a restart-or-done signal. A virtual environment can sample the next latent from the mixture, feed that latent back into the RNN, return a reward of one for each step survived, and terminate when the restart logit crosses zero or the maximum episode length is reached. Training a controller inside this virtual environment is cheap because there is no pixel renderer and no game engine.

But that creates a hazard I have to take seriously. A controller optimized inside an approximate simulator will search the simulator's weaknesses. If the recurrent model is too deterministic, the policy may discover hidden-state trajectories where projectiles disappear or never launch — not a real survival skill, but a simulator exploit. The mixture model gives me a control knob here. Sampling divides the mixture logits by a temperature \(\tau\) and multiplies the Gaussian noise by \(\sqrt{\tau}\). I want to be concrete about what \(\sqrt{\tau}\) on the noise does: it scales each sampled coordinate's standard deviation by \(\sqrt{\tau}\), so the variance scales by \(\tau\) exactly. At \(\tau=1.15\) the per-coordinate variance is inflated by 15% and the noise std by about \(1.072\). Low temperature collapses toward deterministic modes and makes the dream easy to exploit; higher temperature makes generated futures noisier, forcing the controller to survive a harder and less predictable simulation. Too much temperature would destroy useful structure, so the knob trades realism against exploitability, and I would expect the best transfer to sit slightly above \(\tau=1\) rather than at it — which is something I can only settle by sweeping \(\tau\) and measuring real-environment survival, not by reasoning alone.

Putting the pieces together: learn a frame VAE from random-policy observations; encode recorded frames into latent statistics and resample \(z\) while training the recurrent density model so it does not overfit one latent draw; train a small linear controller with CMA-ES, either in the real environment using \(z\) and recurrent state as features, or in the learned latent environment for the survival task. The constraint that made all of this hold together is the one I checked at the end — the predictive model is trained from dense observation data, while the reward-trained search space stays in the hundreds of parameters, small enough that black-box optimization is the natural fit.
