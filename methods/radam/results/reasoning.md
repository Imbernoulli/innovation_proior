I start from the nuisance that does not fit the usual story about Adam. The first moment is just a smoothed gradient and the second moment is just a smoothed squared gradient, yet in a Transformer run the optimizer can be fine with warmup and fail badly without it. The failure is not slow: the absolute-gradient histogram is already distorted in roughly the first ten updates, and the loss then stays in the bad regime. So I should not look for a late-time convergence pathology first. I should ask what the adaptive denominator is doing when it has only one or two observations behind it.

Adam's update can be written coordinatewise as a momentum term times an adaptive rate. The momentum term is \(m_t=(1-\beta_1)\sum_{i=1}^t \beta_1^{t-i}g_i/(1-\beta_1^t)\). The adaptive rate, ignoring the tiny numerical epsilon for the moment, is \(\psi_t=\sqrt{(1-\beta_2^t)/((1-\beta_2)\sum_{i=1}^t\beta_2^{t-i}g_i^2)}\). Warmup multiplies the whole update by a small scalar early on. If warmup is curing something intrinsic to Adam, the thing being cured is most likely the random size of \(\psi_t\), because that is the part built from very few squared-gradient samples.

At the first step this is stark. With the usual early-training approximation \(g_1\sim N(0,\sigma^2)\), \(\psi_1=1/|g_1|\). Then \(g_1^2/\sigma^2\sim\chi^2_1\), so \(1/g_1^2\) is scaled inverse chi-square. The second moment of \(\psi_1\) is \(E[1/g_1^2]\), and that integral diverges at zero because a Gaussian has nonzero density near zero. Even the mean \(E[1/|g_1|]\) diverges. This is enough to identify the opening hazard: a coordinate with a small sampled gradient receives an enormous adaptive rate, and a few such rates can push the parameters into a bad region before the denominator has had time to become a stable statistic. A small learning rate helps because \(\operatorname{Var}[\alpha X]=\alpha^2\operatorname{Var}[X]\), so warmup is plausibly suppressing the variance of the adaptive rate while the denominator is sample-starved.

The controlled observations fit that diagnosis. If I freeze the parameters and the first moment while still collecting second-moment samples for two thousand iterations, Adam no longer falls into the bad region; the denominator has been allowed to become a better statistic before it is trusted. If I instead make epsilon large, the denominator is effectively floored and the adaptive rate cannot explode as easily; that also avoids the worst failure, but it biases the adaptive scaling and hurts the quality of the optimization. These two interventions point to the same cause: too much early variance in the adaptive learning rate. The first fixes it by adding samples, the second by imposing a blunt cap. I want the first kind of fix without an explicit waiting schedule.

To turn that into a rule, I need a scalar that measures how many samples the second-moment estimate effectively contains. I first replace the exponentially weighted average by a simple moving average with the same center of mass. For a long EMA with decay \(\beta_2\), the mean age is \(\beta_2/(1-\beta_2)\), while an SMA window of length \(f\) has mean age \((f-1)/2\). Matching them gives the limiting effective length \(\rho_\infty=2/(1-\beta_2)-1\). For finite \(t\), carrying the truncated geometric sum through gives \(\rho_t=\rho_\infty-2t\beta_2^t/(1-\beta_2^t)\). This has the right endpoints: \(\rho_1=1\), and \(\rho_t\) rises toward \(\rho_\infty\).

Under the same Gaussian approximation, a flat average of \(f\) squared gradients gives \(f/\sum_{i=1}^f g_i^2\sim\operatorname{ScaleInv}\chi^2(f,1/\sigma^2)\). I can therefore model \(\psi_t^2\) as \(x\sim\operatorname{ScaleInv}\chi^2(\rho_t,\tau^2)\), with \(\tau^2=1/\sigma^2\). The density is \((\tau^2\rho/2)^{\rho/2}x^{-1-\rho/2}\exp[-\rho\tau^2/(2x)]/\Gamma(\rho/2)\). Its moments give \(E[x]=\rho\tau^2/(\rho-2)\) for \(\rho>2\), and \(E[\sqrt{x}]=\tau\sqrt{\rho/2}\,\Gamma((\rho-1)/2)/\Gamma(\rho/2)\) for \(\rho>1\). Thus the exact variance of \(\psi=\sqrt{x}\) is finite once \(\rho>2\), not only after \(\rho>4\):
\[
\operatorname{Var}[\psi]=\tau^2\left(\frac{\rho}{\rho-2}-\frac{\rho\,2^{2\rho-5}}{\pi}B((\rho-1)/2,(\rho-1)/2)^2\right).
\]
The beta-function form has the constants I need, and they check out by Legendre duplication. The important qualitative fact is that this exact variance decreases as \(\rho\) grows, so additional effective samples make the adaptive rate less volatile.

For implementation, though, the exact beta-function expression is the wrong object to compute. It is numerically fragile at large \(\rho\), and the stable surrogate comes from a delta-method expansion of \(\sqrt{x}\). That surrogate uses \(\operatorname{Var}[x]\), and \(\operatorname{Var}[x]\) for the scaled inverse chi-square exists only for \(\rho>4\):
\[
\operatorname{Var}[x]=\frac{2\rho^2\tau^4}{(\rho-2)^2(\rho-4)}.
\]
Then
\[
\operatorname{Var}[\sqrt{x}]\approx \frac{\operatorname{Var}[x]}{4E[x]}
=\frac{\rho}{2(\rho-2)(\rho-4)\sigma^2}.
\]
This is the source of the practical threshold. It is not that the exact variance of \(\psi\) is mathematically undefined for every \(\rho\le4\); rather, the simple rectification formula is derived from an approximation whose ingredients are not finite until \(\rho>4\), and the approximation is least trustworthy near that boundary. So the branch should be conservative there.

Now I can choose the rectifier. The late-time target variance is the floor reached at \(\rho_\infty\). I want \(\operatorname{Var}[r_t\psi_t]\) to equal that floor, so \(r_t=\sqrt{\operatorname{Var}[\psi]_{\rho_\infty}/\operatorname{Var}[\psi]_{\rho_t}}\). Substituting the stable approximation cancels \(\sigma^2\) and gives
\[
r_t=\sqrt{\frac{(\rho_t-4)(\rho_t-2)\rho_\infty}{(\rho_\infty-4)(\rho_\infty-2)\rho_t}}.
\]
The direction of the ratio matters. Since early variance is larger, \(r_t\) must be at most one and must rise toward one. The formula above has that behavior. The inverted ratio would amplify the very phase I am trying to tame, so it would be wrong even before checking code.

The optimizer therefore keeps Adam's two moving averages, computes \(\rho_t\), and uses the adaptive denominator only once the rectifier is in its valid region. In the active branch the update is \(\theta_t=\theta_{t-1}-\alpha_t r_t \hat m_t \sqrt{1-\beta_2^t}/(\sqrt{v_t}+\epsilon)\), with \(\hat m_t=m_t/(1-\beta_1^t)\). In the inactive branch the mathematical algorithm takes an unadapted momentum step \(\theta_t=\theta_{t-1}-\alpha_t\hat m_t\). When I translate this to the PyTorch implementation, there is a further practical choice: it activates the adaptive branch at `N_sma >= 5`, not merely at the real-valued condition `rho_t > 4`, and it exposes `degenerated_to_sgd`. With that flag true, the inactive branch is the momentum fallback; with the canonical default false, those early inactive updates are skipped instead. For the default \(\beta_2=0.999\), `N_sma` is about 4.996 at step 5 and about 5.994 at step 6, so the first adaptive update occurs at step 6 under the `>= 5` check.

This also clarifies the edge case \(\beta_2\le0.6\). Then \(\rho_\infty\le4\), so the stable rectification branch never becomes valid. With the momentum fallback enabled, the method stays as momentum SGD; with the canonical default disabled, the reference implementation would skip all steps in that degenerate setting. That is not a useful practical setting for this optimizer, but it is an important case for code faithfulness because it follows directly from the branch condition and the default flag.

So the finished idea is not a new per-coordinate statistic and not a learned schedule. It is Adam with a scalar variance rectifier determined entirely by \(t\) and \(\beta_2\), plus a conservative early branch before the approximate variance formula is safe to use. Warmup and the rectifier have the same rising shape because both reduce early adaptive-rate variance, but the rectifier ties the shape to the second-moment EMA rather than to a hand-picked warmup length.
