I start from the nuisance that does not fit the usual story about Adam. The first moment is just a smoothed gradient and the second moment is just a smoothed squared gradient, yet in a Transformer run the optimizer can be fine with warmup and fail badly without it. The failure is not slow: the absolute-gradient histogram is already distorted in roughly the first ten updates, and the loss then stays in the bad regime. So I should not look for a late-time convergence pathology first. I should ask what the adaptive denominator is doing when it has only one or two observations behind it.

Adam's update can be written coordinatewise as a momentum term times an adaptive rate. The momentum term is \(m_t=(1-\beta_1)\sum_{i=1}^t \beta_1^{t-i}g_i/(1-\beta_1^t)\). The adaptive rate, ignoring the tiny numerical epsilon for the moment, is \(\psi_t=\sqrt{(1-\beta_2^t)/((1-\beta_2)\sum_{i=1}^t\beta_2^{t-i}g_i^2)}\). Warmup multiplies the whole update by a small scalar early on. The momentum term is a convex combination of past gradients, so its magnitude is controlled by the gradients themselves; the adaptive rate is the part with a sum of squares in the denominator, built from very few samples in the opening updates. If warmup is curing something intrinsic to Adam, the random size of \(\psi_t\) is where I should look first.

At the first step this is stark. With the usual early-training approximation \(g_1\sim N(0,\sigma^2)\), \(\psi_1=1/|g_1|\). Then \(g_1^2/\sigma^2\sim\chi^2_1\), so \(1/g_1^2\) is scaled inverse chi-square. Is \(\psi_1\) even integrable? \(E[1/|g_1|]=\int (1/|g|)\,(2\pi\sigma^2)^{-1/2}e^{-g^2/2\sigma^2}\,dg\), and near \(g=0\) the density tends to a positive constant while \(1/|g|\) blows up, so the integrand looks like \(c/|g|\), whose integral is logarithmic and divergent. To make sure I am not fooling myself I cut the integral off at a small \(\varepsilon\) (with \(\sigma=1\)) and watch it as \(\varepsilon\to0\): from \(\varepsilon=10^{-1}\) I get \(1.89\), at \(10^{-2}\) it is \(3.72\), at \(10^{-3}\) it is \(5.56\), at \(10^{-4}\) it is \(7.39\), at \(10^{-6}\) it is \(11.07\). It grows by a roughly constant amount each time \(\varepsilon\) drops by a decade — the logarithmic divergence I expected. So even the mean of \(\psi_1\) is infinite, and \(E[1/g_1^2]\) is worse. A coordinate with a small sampled gradient receives an arbitrarily large adaptive rate, and a few such rates can push the parameters into a bad region before the denominator has had time to become a stable statistic. A small learning rate helps because \(\operatorname{Var}[\alpha X]=\alpha^2\operatorname{Var}[X]\), so warmup is plausibly suppressing the spread of the adaptive rate while the denominator is sample-starved.

The controlled observations fit that reading. If I freeze the parameters and the first moment while still collecting second-moment samples for two thousand iterations, Adam no longer falls into the bad region; the denominator has been allowed to become a better statistic before it is trusted. If I instead make epsilon large, the denominator is effectively floored and the adaptive rate cannot explode as easily; that also avoids the worst failure, but it biases the adaptive scaling and hurts the quality of the optimization. These two interventions point to the same cause: too much early spread in the adaptive learning rate. The first fixes it by adding samples, the second by imposing a blunt cap. I want the first kind of fix without an explicit waiting schedule.

To turn that into a rule, I need a scalar that measures how many samples the second-moment estimate effectively contains. I replace the exponentially weighted average by a simple moving average with the same center of mass. For a long EMA with decay \(\beta_2\), the mean age of the weighted samples is \(\beta_2/(1-\beta_2)\), while an SMA window of length \(f\) has mean age \((f-1)/2\). Matching them gives a limiting effective length \(\rho_\infty=2/(1-\beta_2)-1\). For finite \(t\), carrying the truncated geometric sum through gives \(\rho_t=\rho_\infty-2t\beta_2^t/(1-\beta_2^t)\). I want to be sure this has sane endpoints before I build on it, so I tabulate it for \(\beta_2=0.999\) (where \(\rho_\infty=1999\)): \(\rho_1=1.000\), \(\rho_2=2.000\), \(\rho_3=2.999\), \(\rho_4=3.997\), \(\rho_5=4.996\), \(\rho_6=5.994\), and it keeps climbing toward \(\rho_\infty\). So \(\rho_1=1\) exactly — one sample at the first step, as it must be — and \(\rho_t\) increases by almost exactly one per step early on, which is the right rate when each new gradient is a fresh sample.

Under the same Gaussian approximation, a flat average of \(f\) squared gradients gives \(f/\sum_{i=1}^f g_i^2\sim\operatorname{ScaleInv}\chi^2(f,1/\sigma^2)\). I therefore model \(\psi_t^2\) as \(x\sim\operatorname{ScaleInv}\chi^2(\rho_t,\tau^2)\), with \(\tau^2=1/\sigma^2\). The density is \((\tau^2\rho/2)^{\rho/2}x^{-1-\rho/2}\exp[-\rho\tau^2/(2x)]/\Gamma(\rho/2)\). Its moments give \(E[x]=\rho\tau^2/(\rho-2)\) for \(\rho>2\), and \(E[\sqrt{x}]=\tau\sqrt{\rho/2}\,\Gamma((\rho-1)/2)/\Gamma(\rho/2)\) for \(\rho>1\). So the exact variance of \(\psi=\sqrt{x}\) is \(E[x]-E[\sqrt x]^2\), which is finite as soon as \(\rho>2\):
\[
\operatorname{Var}[\psi]=\tau^2\left(\frac{\rho}{\rho-2}-\frac{\rho\,2^{2\rho-5}}{\pi}B((\rho-1)/2,(\rho-1)/2)^2\right).
\]
The beta-function form is just a rewriting of \(E[\sqrt x]^2\): \(B((\rho-1)/2,(\rho-1)/2)=\Gamma((\rho-1)/2)^2/\Gamma(\rho-1)\), and Legendre duplication, \(\Gamma(z)\Gamma(z+1/2)=2^{1-2z}\sqrt\pi\,\Gamma(2z)\) with \(z=(\rho-1)/2\), turns \(\Gamma(\rho-1)\) into a power of two times \(\Gamma(\rho/2)^2\). I do not trust that I rearranged it correctly, so I evaluate both sides at a few \(\rho\): with \(\tau^2=1\), \(E[\sqrt x]^2\) and the beta second term agree to machine precision — \(\rho=3\) gives \(1.909859\) for each, \(\rho=5\) gives \(1.414711\), \(\rho=20\) gives \(1.080687\), all matching to \(10^{-16}\). So the two forms are the same object, and the exact variance really is finite for \(\rho>2\).

I should check the whole variance against a simulation, because the chi-square algebra is easy to get wrong. I draw four million sets of \(f\) standard normals, form \(x=f/\sum g_i^2\), take \(\psi=\sqrt x\), and compare its sample variance to the formula. The exact expression tracks the Monte Carlo across the board: \(f=3\) gives MC \(1.100\) vs formula \(1.090\), \(f=5\) gives \(0.252\) vs \(0.252\), \(f=8\) gives \(0.106\) vs \(0.106\), \(f=20\) gives \(0.0305\) vs \(0.0304\), \(f=100\) gives \(0.00519\) vs \(0.00519\). The variance is finite and well-defined at \(f=3\) and \(f=5\), and it falls steadily as \(f\) grows — more effective samples make the adaptive rate less volatile, exactly the lever I want.

That tempts me to activate the adaptive step the moment \(\rho_t>2\), since that is where the exact variance becomes finite. But the exact beta-function expression is not a good thing to compute in an optimizer inner loop: it involves \(\Gamma\) of large arguments and the \(2^{2\rho-5}\) factor overflows for the \(\rho\) of order a thousand that I will actually see, with massive cancellation against the beta term. The stable surrogate is a delta-method expansion of \(\sqrt{x}\) around its mean, \(\operatorname{Var}[\sqrt x]\approx\operatorname{Var}[x]/(4E[x])\). That uses \(\operatorname{Var}[x]\), and for the scaled inverse chi-square \(\operatorname{Var}[x]=2\rho^2\tau^4/((\rho-2)^2(\rho-4))\) exists only for \(\rho>4\). Plugging in,
\[
\operatorname{Var}[\sqrt{x}]\approx \frac{\operatorname{Var}[x]}{4E[x]}
=\frac{\rho}{2(\rho-2)(\rho-4)\sigma^2}.
\]
Before I lean on this I should ask how good the approximation is near the boundary, because that is the regime — small \(\rho\) — where it matters. Against the same Monte Carlo: at \(f=5\) the approximation gives \(0.833\) while the truth is \(0.252\), off by more than a factor of three and in the wrong direction; at \(f=8\) it gives \(0.167\) vs \(0.106\); only by \(f=20\) (\(0.0347\) vs \(0.0304\)) and \(f=100\) (\(0.00531\) vs \(0.00519\)) does it close in. So activating at \(\rho>2\) on the strength of the *exact* variance would be using a *surrogate* that is wildly wrong exactly there. The honest reading is that the rectifier I am about to build is only as trustworthy as this approximation, so its valid region is \(\rho>4\), and even just above 4 it is shaky. The branch should be conservative near that boundary rather than fire the instant the formula is real-valued.

Now I can choose the rectifier. The late-time target spread is the floor reached at \(\rho_\infty\). I want \(\operatorname{Var}[r_t\psi_t]\) to equal that floor, so by the scaling fact \(r_t^2=\operatorname{Var}[\psi]_{\rho_\infty}/\operatorname{Var}[\psi]_{\rho_t}\), i.e. \(r_t=\sqrt{\operatorname{Var}[\psi]_{\rho_\infty}/\operatorname{Var}[\psi]_{\rho_t}}\). Substituting the stable approximation cancels \(\sigma^2\) and gives
\[
r_t=\sqrt{\frac{(\rho_t-4)(\rho_t-2)\rho_\infty}{(\rho_\infty-4)(\rho_\infty-2)\rho_t}}.
\]
Two things to verify by hand. First the direction of the ratio: early spread is larger, so \(r_t\) should be at most one and rise toward one. I compute it for \(\beta_2=0.999\): at the first active step \(t=6\), \(r_t=0.026\); then \(t=7\) gives \(0.033\), \(t=10\) gives \(0.049\), \(t=50\) gives \(0.148\), \(t=500\) gives \(0.476\), \(t=5000\) gives \(0.983\), approaching one. It is small early and climbs monotonically — the shape warmup imposes by hand, now derived. The inverted ratio would instead start huge and shrink, amplifying the very phase I am trying to tame, so the numerator and denominator are oriented the right way. Second, this \(r_t\) has the same rising-toward-one profile as a warmup schedule, but tied to \(\beta_2\) and \(t\) instead of a hand-picked length.

The optimizer therefore keeps Adam's two moving averages, computes \(\rho_t\), and uses the adaptive denominator only once the rectifier is in its valid region. In the active branch the update is \(\theta_t=\theta_{t-1}-\alpha_t r_t \hat m_t \sqrt{1-\beta_2^t}/(\sqrt{v_t}+\epsilon)\), with \(\hat m_t=m_t/(1-\beta_1^t)\). In the inactive branch the mathematical algorithm takes an unadapted momentum step \(\theta_t=\theta_{t-1}-\alpha_t\hat m_t\). When I translate this to the PyTorch implementation, the early branch needs a concrete cutoff. The approximation is least trustworthy just above \(\rho=4\), so rather than fire at the real-valued condition \(\rho_t>4\) I activate at the integer-ish guard `N_sma >= 5`, and I expose a `degenerated_to_sgd` flag for what the inactive branch does. With that flag true, the inactive branch is the momentum fallback; with the canonical default false, those early inactive updates are skipped instead. For the default \(\beta_2=0.999\), my table above has `N_sma` about 4.996 at step 5 and 5.994 at step 6, so the first adaptive update lands at step 6 under the `>= 5` check — the first five steps are non-adaptive, which is the built-in warmup.

I should make sure the code I write actually realizes the math I derived, since the implementation folds the bias corrections into a single `step_size`. The reference computes `step_size = sqrt((1-beta2^t)*(N_sma-4)/(N_sma_max-4)*(N_sma-2)/N_sma*N_sma_max/(N_sma_max-2)) / (1-beta1^t)`, and then steps by `-step_size * lr * exp_avg / (sqrt(exp_avg_sq)+eps)`. Comparing term by term with my update, that `step_size` should equal \(r_t\sqrt{1-\beta_2^t}/(1-\beta_1^t)\). I evaluate both for \(\beta_1=0.9,\beta_2=0.999\): at \(t=6\) the code's `step_size` is \(0.004263\) and \(r_t\sqrt{1-\beta_2^t}/(1-\beta_1^t)\) is \(0.004263\); at \(t=10\) both are \(0.007506\). They agree, so the single-scalar fusion in the code is the derived update, not a different one.

This also clarifies the edge case \(\beta_2\le0.6\). Then \(\rho_\infty=2/(1-\beta_2)-1\le4\): at \(\beta_2=0.6\) it is exactly 4, at \(\beta_2=0.5\) it is 3. So the stable rectification branch never becomes valid — \(\rho_t\) can never exceed 4. With the momentum fallback enabled the method stays momentum SGD; with the canonical default disabled the reference would skip all steps in that degenerate setting. Not a useful practical configuration, but it falls straight out of the branch condition and the default flag, so it matters for code faithfulness.

What this leaves me with is not a new per-coordinate statistic and not a learned schedule. It is Adam with a scalar variance rectifier determined entirely by \(t\) and \(\beta_2\), plus a conservative early branch held off until the approximate variance formula is safe to use. Warmup and the rectifier share the rising shape because both reduce early adaptive-rate variance — but the rectifier ties the shape to the second-moment EMA rather than to a hand-picked warmup length.
