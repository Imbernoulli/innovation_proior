Let me start from the thing that actually bothers me. Everyone in RL uses model-free methods — Q-learning, and the deep variants riding on it — because they are online, they store almost nothing, and they don't force you to commit to a parametric model of the world's dynamics. And everyone "knows" that you pay for this with sample efficiency: model-based methods, which estimate the transition kernel and then plan, are supposed to learn from fewer interactions. But is that a theorem or a folklore? In the cleanest setting I can imagine — a finite tabular episodic MDP, $S$ states, $A$ actions, horizon $H$, no simulator to query arbitrary pairs, no resetting mid-episode — I cannot point to a single model-free algorithm with a $\sqrt T$ regret bound. The model-based people have $\sqrt T$. The best model-free thing I know, delayed Q-learning, gives $T^{4/5}$. That gap is exactly the folklore made quantitative, and I want to know whether it is real or whether Q-learning has just never been analyzed correctly.

So let me set the stage precisely. An episode is $H$ steps; at step $h$ the agent in state $x$ picks $a$, collects $r_h(x,a)\in[0,1]$, and moves by $\mathbb P_h(\cdot|x,a)$. Crucially I will let the kernels $\mathbb P_1,\dots,\mathbb P_H$ differ across steps — that's the honest, harder case. Optimal values satisfy Bellman: $Q_h^\star(x,a)=r_h(x,a)+[\mathbb P_h V_{h+1}^\star](x,a)$, $V_h^\star=\max_a Q_h^\star$, $V_{H+1}^\star\equiv 0$. Over $K$ episodes, $T=KH$, I measure $\mathrm{Regret}(K)=\sum_k[V_1^\star(x_1^k)-V_1^{\pi_k}(x_1^k)]$, and the adversary even gets to pick the start state $x_1^k$. I want this sublinear in $T$, ideally $\sqrt T$.

Why doesn't plain Q-learning already do this? Two separate things go wrong, and I should be careful to keep them apart, because conflating them is probably why this has stayed open.

The first is exploration. Take $\varepsilon$-greedy. Picture the combination-lock instance: there's a special start state $s^\star$, and for the first $H/2$ steps exactly one special action $a^\star$ keeps you at $s^\star$; any deviation dumps you uniformly into the other states, from which you can never get back. All the reward sits at the end of the all-$s^\star$ path. A learner initialized at zero, dithering with random actions, only ever sees the rewarding prefix $(s^\star,a^\star,\dots,s^\star,a^\star)$ with probability $A^{-H/2}$. Until it does, every observed return is zero, so *no Q-value ever moves* — the algorithm has literally no signal. It must suffer $\Theta(H)$ regret per episode unless it plays $\Omega(A^{H/2})$ episodes. That's exponential in $H$, and it's not a fixable constant — it's the structure of undirected exploration. So whatever I do, the exploration has to be *directed*: I need to deliberately seek out the state-action pairs I'm uncertain about, not stumble onto them. That smells like the bandit answer — optimism, upper confidence bounds. Inflate the value of a pair by how unsure I am of it, act greedily under that inflation, let it deflate as I gather data. Fine, I'll carry a bonus.

But the second thing that goes wrong is subtler and I think it's the real reason the horizon dependence has always blown up, and it has nothing to do with exploration. It's the *learning rate*. The Q-learning update is $Q(x,a)\leftarrow(1-\alpha_t)Q(x,a)+\alpha_t[\text{target}]$. The textbook stochastic-approximation choice, the one that makes $Q$ converge to the right fixed point almost surely, is $\alpha_t=1/t$ on the $t$-th visit. Why $1/t$? Because then $Q$ is literally the *uniform average* of all $t$ targets I've ever seen for that pair — that's the unbiased estimator, the Robbins–Monro sweet spot. So $1/t$ should be the "correct" rate.

Let me stare at that for a second, because something is off. Even-Dar and Mansour analyzed exactly this — convergence *rates* as a function of the step-size form — and found something sharp and, at first glance, surprising. With the linear rate $1/t$, the time to converge is *exponential* in the effective horizon; with a polynomial rate $1/t^\omega$, $\omega\in(1/2,1)$, it's *polynomial*. And they pin the mechanism on the partial sums: $\sum_{t=1}^T (1/t)=O(\ln T)$, growing painfully slowly, whereas $\sum_{t=1}^T t^{-\omega}=O(T^{1-\omega})$, growing like a power. The "correct" unbiased rate is the slow one.

Why would unbiasedness hurt? Here's the picture in my setting. The target for $(x,a)$ at step $h$ is $r_h+V_{h+1}(x')+\text{bonus}$, and $V_{h+1}$ is *not fixed* — it's being learned by the next layer, and early on it's badly wrong. If I uniform-average all $t$ targets, I am giving the very first target, computed against a garbage $V_{h+1}$ from episode one, the same weight $1/t$ as the most recent target computed against a nearly-correct $V_{h+1}$. The stale next-layer error gets baked in and only washes out as $1/t\to 0$, which is glacial. And this error doesn't just sit there: it propagates *down* the $H$ layers. Layer $h$ inherits layer $h+1$'s staleness, multiplies it, passes it to $h-1$. If each layer amplifies the previous one's error by even a constant factor strictly above one, after $H$ layers I get that factor to the $H$ — exponential in the horizon. *That's* where the $A^{H/2}$-flavored blow-up comes from even after I've fixed exploration. The $1/t$ rate is too forgetful-slow to track a moving target across a deep MDP.

So I want a rate that *forgets the past faster* — that weights recent targets, the ones computed against a better $V_{h+1}$, more heavily. The Even-Dar–Mansour story says "go polynomial." But let me think about what scale I actually need, because I have a specific structure: $H$ layers, and I want the cross-layer amplification to be a *constant*, not just polynomial. Let me make the weighting explicit and then design it.

Unfold the update. On the $t$-th visit to $(x,a)$ at step $h$, with previous visits at episodes $k_1<\dots<k_t$,
$$Q_h^{k}(x,a)=(1-\alpha_t)Q_h^{k_t}(x,a)+\alpha_t[\,r_h+V_{h+1}^{k_t}(x_{h+1}^{k_t})+b_t\,],$$
and recursing all the way back to the initial value $H$, this is a weighted sum. Let me name the weights: define $\alpha_t^0=\prod_{j=1}^t(1-\alpha_j)$ and $\alpha_t^i=\alpha_i\prod_{j=i+1}^t(1-\alpha_j)$. Then
$$Q_h^k(x,a)=\alpha_t^0\,H+\sum_{i=1}^t\alpha_t^i\big[r_h(x,a)+V_{h+1}^{k_i}(x_{h+1}^{k_i})+b_i\big].$$
For $t\ge1$, $\alpha_1=1$, so $\alpha_t^0=0$ and the visit weights satisfy $\sum_{i=1}^t\alpha_t^i=1$; for the unvisited case $t=0$, $\alpha_0^0=1$ leaves the optimistic prior $H$ in place. So once a pair has been seen, $Q$ is exactly a weighted average of past next-state values, and the *shape* of $(\alpha_t^1,\dots,\alpha_t^t)$ is the whole ballgame. With $\alpha_t=1/t$ these weights are flat, all $1/t$ — that's the disease. With $\alpha_t=1/\sqrt t$ they'd pile almost all the mass on the last handful of visits — which forgets aggressively, good for tracking, but then I'm averaging over essentially $O(\sqrt t)$ samples and my variance is too high. I want something in between: enough recent weight to track $V_{h+1}$, enough breadth to average down noise.

Let me think about what controls the layer-to-layer blow-up directly. When I eventually telescope the regret across layers, a single $\phi_{h+1}^{k'}=(V_{h+1}^{k'}-V_{h+1}^\star)(x_{h+1}^{k'})$ from layer $h+1$, episode $k'$, gets re-used by layer $h$ every time the same layer-$h$ pair is revisited. If that episode was the $i$-th visit to the pair, its total possible re-use weight is $\sum_{t=i}^\infty\alpha_t^i$. If that's $1+c/H$, then across $H$ layers I pay $(1+c/H)^H\le e^c$ — a constant. *That's* my design target: a rate whose tail sum $\sum_{t\ge i}\alpha_t^i$ equals $1+\Theta(1/H)$.

Let me try $\alpha_t=\frac{H+1}{H+t}$. It's $\Theta(H/t)$ — polynomial-ish, between $1/t$ and $1/\sqrt t$ in aggressiveness, and the $H$ in the numerator is suspicious in exactly the right way. Let me just compute the three properties I need and see if it threads the needle.

First, the tail sum. I want $\sum_{t=i}^\infty\alpha_t^i$. Let me be careful with the product. $\alpha_j=\frac{H+1}{H+j}$ so $1-\alpha_j=\frac{j-1}{H+j}$. Then
$$\alpha_t^i=\frac{H+1}{H+i}\prod_{j=i+1}^t\frac{j-1}{H+j}=\frac{H+1}{H+t}\cdot\frac{i\,(i+1)\cdots(t-1)}{(i+H)(i+1+H)\cdots(t-1+H)}.$$
Now I need $\sum_{t=i}^\infty$ of this. There's a clean identity: for $n\ge k$,
$$\frac{n}{k}=1+\frac{n-k}{n+1}+\frac{(n-k)(n-k+1)}{(n+1)(n+2)}+\cdots,$$
which I can verify by checking that the partial-sum remainder $\frac nk-\sum_{m=0}^r x_m=\frac{n-k}{k}\prod_{m=1}^r\frac{n-k+m}{n+m}\to 0$. Set $n=i+H$, $k=H$. The bracket I need is exactly the right-hand side of this identity:
$$\sum_{t=i}^\infty\alpha_t^i=\frac{H+1}{H+i}\Big(1+\frac{i}{H+1+i}+\frac{i(i+1)}{(H+1+i)(H+2+i)}+\cdots\Big)=\frac{H+1}{H+i}\cdot\frac{i+H}{H}=\frac{H+1}{H}=1+\frac1H.$$
There it is, exactly $1+1/H$, for *every* $i$. So the across-layer amplification is $(1+1/H)^H\le e$. That is the property that kills the exponential-in-$H$ blow-up. The $1/t$ rate has no analogue of this — its tail sum is unbounded in $H$. This single line is, I think, the crux of the entire result, and it's a direct consequence of putting that $H$ in the rate.

Second, I need the weights to be small enough that the *noise* in the update is controlled. The relevant quantities are $\max_i\alpha_t^i$ and $\sum_i(\alpha_t^i)^2$. From the product form,
$$\alpha_t^i=\frac{H+1}{t+H}\cdot\frac{i\cdots(t-1)}{(i+H)\cdots(t-1+H)}\le\frac{H+1}{t+H}\le\frac{2H}{t},$$
since each factor $\frac{j-1}{j-1+H}\le 1$ and $H+1\le 2H$, $t+H\ge t$. So $\max_i\alpha_t^i\le 2H/t$. And because the weights sum to one, $\sum_i(\alpha_t^i)^2\le(\max_i\alpha_t^i)\sum_i\alpha_t^i\le\frac{2H}{t}$. Good — the "effective number of samples" is $\sim t/(2H)$, so noise decays like $\sqrt{2H/t}$. That confirms the intuition: the rate averages over roughly the last $1/H$ fraction of visits. Not the last handful ($1/\sqrt t$ would over-concentrate, variance too high), not all of them ($1/t$ averages stale targets) — the last $1/H$ fraction.

Third, a bookkeeping bound I'll need to control the *bonus* contribution: $\sum_{i=1}^t\frac{\alpha_t^i}{\sqrt i}$. By induction on $t$, using $\alpha_t^i=(1-\alpha_t)\alpha_{t-1}^i$ for $i<t$, I get $\frac{1}{\sqrt t}\le\sum_{i=1}^t\frac{\alpha_t^i}{\sqrt i}\le\frac{2}{\sqrt t}$. The base case $t=1$ gives $\alpha_1^1=1$. For the upper bound the induction step is $\frac{\alpha_t}{\sqrt t}+(1-\alpha_t)\frac{2}{\sqrt{t-1}}\le\frac{H+1}{\sqrt t(H+t)}+\frac{2\sqrt{t-1}}{H+t}\le\frac{2}{\sqrt t}$ once I use $H\ge1$; the lower bound is similar with the $1$. So a bonus of size $b_i$ contributes $\sum_i\alpha_t^i b_i$, which if $b_i\propto 1/\sqrt i$ stays $\propto 1/\sqrt t$. That's what I want — the accumulated bonus tracks the current per-visit bonus, it doesn't pile up.

Now the bonus itself. What scale must it be? The point of the bonus is optimism: I want $Q_h^k\ge Q_h^\star$ at all times, so that the greedy policy is never fooled into ignoring a genuinely good action. Subtract Bellman from the unfolded $Q$. Using $Q_h^\star=\alpha_t^0 Q_h^\star+\sum_i\alpha_t^i[r_h+(\mathbb P_h-\hat{\mathbb P}_h^{k_i})V_{h+1}^\star+V_{h+1}^\star(x_{h+1}^{k_i})]$ (legal because $\sum_{i=0}^t\alpha_t^i=1$, and $\hat{\mathbb P}_h^{k_i}V_{h+1}^\star$ is just $V_{h+1}^\star(x_{h+1}^{k_i})$, the single-sample empirical Bellman backup), I get
$$(Q_h^k-Q_h^\star)(x,a)=\alpha_t^0(H-Q_h^\star)+\sum_{i=1}^t\alpha_t^i\Big[(V_{h+1}^{k_i}-V_{h+1}^\star)(x_{h+1}^{k_i})+[(\hat{\mathbb P}_h^{k_i}-\mathbb P_h)V_{h+1}^\star](x,a)+b_i\Big].$$
The middle term $\sum_i\alpha_t^i[(\hat{\mathbb P}_h^{k_i}-\mathbb P_h)V_{h+1}^\star]$ is the only stochastic obstacle to optimism. Each summand is, conditioned on the past, mean zero — it's the deviation of a single next-state sample of $V_{h+1}^\star$ from its expectation — and it's bounded by $H$ since $0\le V^\star\le H$. So $\{I[k_i\le K]\cdot[(\hat{\mathbb P}_h^{k_i}-\mathbb P_h)V_{h+1}^\star]\}_i$ is a martingale difference sequence with respect to the natural filtration (the stopping times $k_i$ are when the pair is visited). Azuma-Hoeffding on the $\alpha$-weighted sum: with probability $1-p/(SAH)$, uniformly over $\tau$,
$$\Big|\sum_{i=1}^\tau\alpha_\tau^i\,I[k_i\le K]\,[(\hat{\mathbb P}_h^{k_i}-\mathbb P_h)V_{h+1}^\star](x,a)\Big|\le \frac{c}{2}H\sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^\tau(\alpha_\tau^i)^2\,\iota}\le c\sqrt{\frac{H^3\iota}{\tau}},$$
where $\iota=\log(SAT/p)$ is the union-bound log factor and I used $\sum(\alpha_\tau^i)^2\le 2H/\tau$ — so $H^2\cdot(2H/\tau)=2H^3/\tau$ under the root. Since this holds for all fixed $\tau$, it holds at the random $\tau=t=N_h^k(x,a)$ too.

So the fluctuation is $c\sqrt{H^3\iota/t}$, and *that* is exactly what the bonus must dominate to make $Q-Q^\star\ge 0$. Hence $b_t=c\sqrt{H^3\iota/t}$. Let me read off where the $H^3$ comes from, because it's not arbitrary: one $H^2$ is the squared magnitude of the value range (each backup of $V^\star$ swings over $[0,H]$), and the extra $H$ is from $\sum(\alpha_t^i)^2\le 2H/t$ — the price of the recent-weighting rate. The $\sqrt{1/t}$ is the ordinary visit-count decay. With this choice, define $\beta_t:=2\sum_i\alpha_t^i b_i$; by the $\sum\alpha_t^i/\sqrt i\in[1/\sqrt t,2/\sqrt t]$ bound, $\beta_t\in[2c\sqrt{H^3\iota/t},\,4c\sqrt{H^3\iota/t}]$. Plugging the concentration bound and $\beta_t$ back in, and inducting on $h=H,H-1,\dots,1$,
$$0\le (Q_h^k-Q_h^\star)(x,a)\le \alpha_t^0 H+\sum_{i=1}^t\alpha_t^i(V_{h+1}^{k_i}-V_{h+1}^\star)(x_{h+1}^{k_i})+\beta_t.$$
The left inequality is optimism; the right says the gap at layer $h$ is the prior leftover plus a weighted sum of *next-layer* gaps plus the bonus accumulator. That's a recursion waiting to be telescoped.

Now I assemble the regret. Optimism gives $V_h^k\ge V_h^\star$, so $\mathrm{Regret}(K)\le\sum_k(V_1^k-V_1^{\pi_k})(x_1^k)=\sum_k\delta_1^k$, where $\delta_h^k:=(V_h^k-V_h^{\pi_k})(x_h^k)$ and I'll also need $\phi_h^k:=(V_h^k-V_h^\star)(x_h^k)\ge0$. I want a recursion from $\sum_k\delta_h^k$ to $\sum_k\delta_{h+1}^k$. For a fixed $(k,h)$ with $t=N_h^k(x_h^k,a_h^k)$ and prior visits $k_1<\dots<k_t$,
$$\delta_h^k\le (Q_h^k-Q_h^{\pi_k})(x_h^k,a_h^k)=(Q_h^k-Q_h^\star)+(Q_h^\star-Q_h^{\pi_k}).$$
The first piece I bound by the optimism lemma; the second is $[\mathbb P_h(V_{h+1}^\star-V_{h+1}^{\pi_k})](x_h^k,a_h^k)$ by Bellman. So
$$\delta_h^k\le \alpha_t^0 H+\sum_{i=1}^t\alpha_t^i\phi_{h+1}^{k_i}+\beta_t+[\mathbb P_h(V_{h+1}^\star-V_{h+1}^{\pi_k})](x_h^k,a_h^k).$$
Replace the expectation $\mathbb P_h$ by its single realization at $x_{h+1}^k$ and absorb the difference into a martingale term:
$$[\mathbb P_h(V_{h+1}^\star-V_{h+1}^{\pi_k})]=(V_{h+1}^\star-V_{h+1}^{\pi_k})(x_{h+1}^k)+\xi_{h+1}^k,$$
where $\xi_{h+1}^k:=[(\mathbb P_h-\hat{\mathbb P}_h^k)(V_{h+1}^\star-V_{h+1}^{\pi_k})](x_h^k,a_h^k)$ is mean-zero given the past and bounded by $H$. And $(V_{h+1}^\star-V_{h+1}^{\pi_k})(x_{h+1}^k)=\delta_{h+1}^k-\phi_{h+1}^k$ by definition. So
$$\delta_h^k\le \alpha_t^0 H+\sum_{i=1}^t\alpha_t^i\phi_{h+1}^{k_i}+\beta_t-\phi_{h+1}^k+\delta_{h+1}^k+\xi_{h+1}^k.$$
Now sum over $k$. Three pieces. The prior-leftover term: $\sum_k\alpha_{n_h^k}^0 H=H\cdot\#\{k:n_h^k=0\}\le SAH$, since each $(x,a)$ at this layer can contribute the unvisited prior only once. The weighted next-layer term is the one that needs the magic. Regroup:
$$\sum_k\sum_{i=1}^{n_h^k}\alpha_{n_h^k}^i\phi_{h+1}^{k_i}.$$
Every fixed $\phi_{h+1}^{k_i}$, created on the $i$-th visit to some layer-$h$ pair, can be reused only when that same pair is later selected; as the later visit count ranges over $t=i,i+1,\dots$, the total weight it can collect is $\sum_{t=i}^\infty\alpha_t^i=1+\frac1H$. So this whole double sum is at most $(1+\frac1H)\sum_k\phi_{h+1}^k$. Therefore
$$\sum_k\delta_h^k\le SAH+\Big(1+\tfrac1H\Big)\sum_k\phi_{h+1}^k-\sum_k\phi_{h+1}^k+\sum_k\delta_{h+1}^k+\sum_k(\beta_{n_h^k}+\xi_{h+1}^k).$$
Since $\phi_{h+1}^k\le\delta_{h+1}^k$ (because $V_{h+1}^\star\ge V_{h+1}^{\pi_k}$), the two $\phi$ terms combine to $\frac1H\sum_k\phi_{h+1}^k\le\frac1H\sum_k\delta_{h+1}^k$, and I get the clean recursion
$$\sum_k\delta_h^k\le SAH+\Big(1+\tfrac1H\Big)\sum_k\delta_{h+1}^k+\sum_k(\beta_{n_h^k}+\xi_{h+1}^k).$$
Recurse over $h=1,\dots,H$ with $\delta_{H+1}\equiv 0$. The amplification compounds to $(1+1/H)^H\le e$, a constant, so
$$\sum_k\delta_1^k\le O\Big(H^2SA+\sum_{h=1}^H\sum_{k=1}^K(\beta_{n_h^k}+\xi_{h+1}^k)\Big).$$
The $H^2SA$ is $H$ layers of the $SAH$ first-visit cost, times the $O(1)$ amplification. Now the two remaining sums. For the bonus accumulator, $\beta_{n_h^k}=O(\sqrt{H^3\iota/n_h^k})$, and by a pigeonhole over visit counts, for each $h$,
$$\sum_{k=1}^K\beta_{n_h^k}=O(1)\sum_k\sqrt{\frac{H^3\iota}{n_h^k}}=O(1)\sqrt{H^3\iota}\sum_{x,a}\sum_{n=1}^{N_h^K(x,a)}\frac{1}{\sqrt n}\le O\big(\sqrt{H^3SAK\iota}\big)=O\big(\sqrt{H^2SAT\iota}\big),$$
using $\sum_{x,a}N_h^K(x,a)=K$ so that $\sum_{x,a}\sqrt{N_h^K(x,a)}$ is maximized at the uniform split $N=K/SA$, and $K=T/H$. Over all $H$ layers this is $O(\sqrt{H^4SAT\iota})$. The martingale sum is controlled by Azuma-Hoeffding: $|\sum_{h,k}\xi_{h+1}^k|\le cH\sqrt{T\iota}$ with high probability, lower order. So
$$\sum_k\delta_1^k\le O\big(H^2SA+\sqrt{H^4SAT\iota}\big).$$
Let me check the additive $H^2SA$ folds into the root term. If $\sqrt{H^4SAT\iota}\ge H^2SA$ the root dominates and there's nothing to do. The complementary case is $T<SA/\iota$; in the nontrivial high-probability regime $\iota\ge1$, the trivial regret bound $\sum_k\delta_1^k\le HK=T$ satisfies $T\le SA\le H^4SA\iota$, hence $T^2\le H^4SAT\iota$. Either way the $H^2SA$ disappears, and

$$\boxed{\;\mathrm{Regret}(K)\le O\big(\sqrt{H^4SAT\,\iota}\big)\;=\;\tilde O\big(\sqrt{H^4SAT}\big)\;}$$

with probability $1-p$. That settles it: a model-free algorithm — just Q-learning with a UCB bonus and the right rate, storing only the Q-table and a count per entry — has $\sqrt T$ regret without a simulator. The folklore is not a theorem. The two practical morals are exactly the two design choices: use a directed UCB bonus, not $\varepsilon$-greedy; and use $\alpha_t=\Theta(H/t)$, not $1/t$, so the update tracks the moving next-layer value.

Can I push the $H$ down? My bonus paid $\sqrt{H^3}$ because Hoeffding treats every next-state backup as swinging over the full range $H$. But that's pessimistic — the value doesn't actually vary by $H$ at every single step. Along a fixed policy, the increments $r_h(x_h,\pi(x_h))+V_{h+1}^\pi(x_{h+1})-V_h^\pi(x_h)$ are martingale differences, their sum is the realized return minus its mean, and the return lies in $[0,H]$. Orthogonality of martingale differences gives
$$\mathbb E\sum_{h=1}^H \mathbb V_h V_{h+1}^\pi(x_h,\pi(x_h))=\mathbb E\sum_{h=1}^H\big(r_h+V_{h+1}^\pi-V_h^\pi\big)^2\le H^2.$$
So the total variance in one episode is $O(H^2)$, not the naive $H\cdot H^2=H^3$; with concentration across episodes this becomes $\sum_{k,h}\mathbb V_hV_{h+1}^{\pi_k}(x_h^k,a_h^k)\le O(HT+H^3\iota)$. If I scale the bonus by the *empirical* variance $W_t$ of the observed next-state values — Bernstein instead of Hoeffding — the relevant fluctuation becomes $\sqrt{\frac{H}{t}\mathbb V V^\star\,\iota}$ rather than $\sqrt{\frac{H^3}{t}\iota}$, and the $O(H^2)$ per-episode variance budget buys back one factor of $H$ in the leading term. The empirical variance is computable online from running sums $\mu_h=\sum V_{h+1}$, $\sigma_h=\sum V_{h+1}^2$, no extra storage. I have to be careful — $V^\star$ is unknown so I use $V^k$'s empirical variance and bound the gap (its true-vs-empirical variance discrepancy, and the $V^k$-vs-$V^\star$ slack, are small once optimism holds by induction) — and the bonus has to be written so that its $\alpha$-weighted accumulator $\beta_t=2\sum_i\alpha_t^i b_i$ still telescopes; taking $\beta_t=\min\{c_1(\sqrt{\frac Ht(W_t+H)\iota}+\frac{\sqrt{H^7SA}\,\iota}{t}),\,c_2\sqrt{\frac{H^3\iota}{t}}\}$ and back-solving $b_t=\frac{\beta_t-(1-\alpha_t)\beta_{t-1}}{2\alpha_t}$ does it. Running the same recursion with an Azuma-*Bernstein* concentration ($\sqrt{\sum(\alpha)^2\mathbb V V^\star\iota}+\max\alpha\cdot H\iota$) and the total-variance lemma in the pigeonhole step gives
$$\mathrm{Regret}(K)\le O\big(\sqrt{H^3SAT\,\iota}+\sqrt{H^9S^3A^3}\,\iota^2\big),$$
shaving the leading term to $\tilde O(\sqrt{H^3SAT})$ at the cost of a more delicate bonus and a lower-order additive term.

How close is this to optimal? The lower bound: take $H$ independent two-state JAO chains in series, each with its own hidden good action $a_h^\star$ that beats the alternative by an $\varepsilon$ bias in the transition. To identify the right action at a given step $h$ you only get $T/H$ observations of that step (not $T$), so you need $T/H\approx\delta/\varepsilon^2$ to tell the two actions apart; per-step regret of order $\varepsilon/\delta$ accumulated over the steps you can't yet resolve forces $\Omega(H\sqrt{SAT})=\Omega(\sqrt{H^2SAT})$. So Hoeffding is off by a factor $H$ and Bernstein by $\sqrt H$ from the information-theoretic floor — model-free, nearly model-based-optimal, with a Q-table.

To recap the causal chain in one breath: undirected exploration fails exponentially, so I add an optimistic UCB bonus and act greedily under it; a $1/t$ rate uniform-averages stale next-layer targets and amplifies error multiplicatively across $H$ layers into an exponential-in-$H$ disaster, so I switch to $\alpha_t=(H+1)/(H+t)$ whose induced weights satisfy $\sum_{t\ge i}\alpha_t^i=1+1/H$, making the cross-layer blow-up $(1+1/H)^H\le e$; the bonus must be exactly $c\sqrt{H^3\iota/t}$ to dominate the Azuma-Hoeffding fluctuation of the $\alpha$-weighted single-sample Bellman backups (the $H^3$ being value-range-squared $H^2$ times the rate's $\sum(\alpha_t^i)^2\le 2H/t$), which forces optimism $Q\ge Q^\star$; telescoping the per-step regret across layers with that constant amplification and a pigeonhole on the bonus yields $\tilde O(\sqrt{H^4SAT})$; and replacing Hoeffding by a variance-scaled Bernstein bonus, riding on the $O(H^2)$ total-variance-per-episode budget, sharpens the leading term to $\tilde O(\sqrt{H^3SAT})$ — a model-free method within a single $\sqrt H$ of the $\Omega(\sqrt{H^2SAT})$ floor.

```python
import numpy as np

# Q-learning with UCB-Hoeffding.
# State (h,x,a) Q-table + visit counts only, O(SAH) space.

def q_learning_ucb_hoeffding(env, K, c=1.0, p=0.1):
    S, A, H = env.S, env.A, env.H
    iota = np.log(S * A * (K * H) / p)              # union-bound log factor log(SAT/p)
    Q = np.full((H, S, A), float(H))                # optimistic init: every value starts at H
    V = np.zeros((H + 1, S))                         # V_{H+1} == 0
    V[:H] = H                                        # consistent with Q init, clipped to [0,H]
    N = np.zeros((H, S, A), dtype=int)

    for k in range(K):
        x = env.reset()
        for h in range(H):
            a = int(Q[h, x].argmax())               # greedy under the optimistic Q -> directed exploration
            r, x_next = env.step(h, x, a)
            N[h, x, a] += 1
            t = N[h, x, a]
            alpha = (H + 1) / (H + t)                # the recent-weighting rate: sum_{t>=i} alpha_t^i = 1+1/H
            b = c * np.sqrt(H**3 * iota / t)         # UCB bonus dominating the Azuma-Hoeffding fluctuation
            target = r + V[h + 1, x_next] + b
            Q[h, x, a] = (1 - alpha) * Q[h, x, a] + alpha * target
            V[h, x] = min(H, Q[h, x].max())          # value clipped to its valid range [0,H]
            x = x_next
    return Q
```
