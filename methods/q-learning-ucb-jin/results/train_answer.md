Most of the reinforcement learning that works in practice is model-free — Q-learning and the deep variants riding on it — because such methods are online, store almost nothing, and never commit to a parametric model of the world's dynamics. The folklore, backed by empirical comparisons, is that you pay for these conveniences with sample efficiency: model-based methods that estimate the transition kernel and then plan are supposed to learn from fewer interactions. I want to know whether that gap is a theorem or just a gap in our analysis. In the cleanest possible setting — a finite tabular episodic MDP with $S$ states, $A$ actions, horizon $H$, step-dependent kernels $\mathbb P_1,\dots,\mathbb P_H$, rewards in $[0,1]$, no simulator to query arbitrary pairs and no mid-episode reset — I cannot point to a single model-free algorithm with $\sqrt T$ regret in the total number of steps $T=KH$. Model-based methods have it: UCB-VI reaches the information-theoretic $\tilde O(\sqrt{H^2SAT})$ floor, but it stores and updates the full $S^2A$ transition model, paying $\tilde O(TS^2A)$ time and $O(S^2AH)$ space. UCRL2 and optimistic posterior sampling are also model-based and loose in $S$ and $H$. The best model-free guarantee, delayed Q-learning, batches $m=\tilde O(1/\varepsilon^2)$ experiences per update and translates to only $\tilde O(T^{4/5})$ regret. And plain Q-learning with $\varepsilon$-greedy is hopeless: on the combination-lock MDP, where a length-$H/2$ prefix of special pairs hides all the reward, dithering reaches the rewarding prefix only with probability $A^{-H/2}$, and until it does no observed return is nonzero and no Q-value moves — linear regret unless one plays $\Omega(A^{H/2})$ episodes.

Two distinct things go wrong with naive Q-learning, and conflating them is, I think, why this stayed open. The first is exploration: it must be *directed*, not undirected. The bandit cure is optimism in the face of uncertainty — inflate each estimate by a confidence width, act greedily under the inflated value, let the inflation deflate as visit counts grow. The second is subtler and has nothing to do with exploration: it is the *learning rate*. The textbook choice $\alpha_t=1/t$ on the $t$-th visit makes $Q$ the uniform average of all past targets — the unbiased Robbins–Monro estimator, supposedly correct. But the target $r_h+V_{h+1}(x')+b$ is computed against a $V_{h+1}$ that is itself still being learned and is badly wrong early on. Uniform averaging gives the first, garbage-$V_{h+1}$ target the same weight $1/t$ as the latest, nearly-correct one; the stale next-layer error washes out only as $1/t\to0$, glacially, and worse, it propagates *down* the $H$ layers, each layer inheriting and amplifying the one above. A constant amplification strictly above one, raised to the $H$-th power, is exponential in the horizon — the same flavor of blow-up as undirected exploration, but caused entirely by the rate. This matches the Even-Dar–Mansour dichotomy: $\sum_{t=1}^T 1/t=O(\ln T)$ converges in time exponential in the effective horizon, whereas a polynomial rate $1/t^\omega$ with $\sum t^{-\omega}=O(T^{1-\omega})$ converges in polynomial time. The "correct" unbiased rate is exactly the wrong one because it cannot track a moving target across a deep MDP.

I propose Q-learning with UCB exploration. It is plain tabular Q-learning with two coupled fixes: an optimistic UCB bonus added to the Bellman target, and a recent-weighting learning rate. Concretely, initialize every $Q_h(x,a)\leftarrow H$ (optimistic, so unvisited pairs look maximally attractive) and counts $N_h(x,a)\leftarrow0$, set $\iota=\log(SAT/p)$, and on each visit perform
$$Q_h(x_h,a_h)\leftarrow(1-\alpha_t)\,Q_h(x_h,a_h)+\alpha_t\big[r_h(x_h,a_h)+V_{h+1}(x_{h+1})+b_t\big],\qquad V_h(x_h)\leftarrow\min\{H,\max_{a'}Q_h(x_h,a')\},$$
acting greedily $a_h\leftarrow\arg\max_{a'}Q_h(x_h,a')$, with $t=N_h(x_h,a_h)$ incremented first, and the two design knobs being
$$\alpha_t=\frac{H+1}{H+t},\qquad b_t=c\sqrt{\frac{H^3\iota}{t}}.$$
Why exactly these forms? Unfold the update over the $t$ past visits at episodes $k_1<\dots<k_t$. Writing $\alpha_t^0=\prod_{j=1}^t(1-\alpha_j)$ and $\alpha_t^i=\alpha_i\prod_{j=i+1}^t(1-\alpha_j)$, the recursion telescopes to $Q_h^k(x,a)=\alpha_t^0 H+\sum_{i=1}^t\alpha_t^i[r_h+V_{h+1}^{k_i}(x_{h+1}^{k_i})+b_i]$. Because $\alpha_1=1$, for any $t\ge1$ we have $\alpha_t^0=0$ and $\sum_{i=1}^t\alpha_t^i=1$; for $t=0$, $\alpha_0^0=1$ leaves the optimistic prior $H$ in place. So once visited, $Q$ is a *weighted average* of past next-state values, and the entire behavior is dictated by the shape of $(\alpha_t^1,\dots,\alpha_t^t)$. With $1/t$ these weights are flat — the disease. With $1/\sqrt t$ almost all mass piles on the last few visits — too few effective samples, variance too high. I want the in-between rate that puts enough mass on recent visits to track $V_{h+1}$ while averaging over enough to suppress noise.

The choice $\alpha_t=(H+1)/(H+t)$ is $\Theta(H/t)$, and that suspicious $H$ in the numerator is the load-bearing design decision. The thing that controls the cross-layer blow-up is the *tail* re-use weight: a value $\phi_{h+1}^{k_i}$ created on the $i$-th visit to a layer-$h$ pair gets re-used by layer $h$ every later time the pair is revisited, with total weight $\sum_{t=i}^\infty\alpha_t^i$. If that tail sum is $1+c/H$, then over $H$ layers I pay only $(1+c/H)^H\le e^c$, a constant. So I need the tail sum to be exactly $1+\Theta(1/H)$. Computing it: since $1-\alpha_j=\frac{j-1}{H+j}$, we get $\alpha_t^i=\frac{H+1}{H+t}\cdot\frac{i(i+1)\cdots(t-1)}{(i+H)(i+1+H)\cdots(t-1+H)}$, and summing over $t\ge i$ via the identity $\frac nk=1+\frac{n-k}{n+1}+\frac{(n-k)(n-k+1)}{(n+1)(n+2)}+\cdots$ with $n=i+H,\,k=H$ yields
$$\sum_{t=i}^\infty\alpha_t^i=\frac{H+1}{H+i}\cdot\frac{i+H}{H}=\frac{H+1}{H}=1+\frac1H$$
for *every* $i$. This single identity caps the per-layer error amplification at $(1+\frac1H)^H\le e$ and is the crux that kills the exponential-in-$H$ disaster; the $1/t$ rate has no such bound, its tail sum being unbounded in $H$. Two further properties of the same rate keep the noise and the bonus in line. From the product form each factor $\frac{j-1}{j-1+H}\le1$, so $\max_i\alpha_t^i\le\frac{H+1}{t+H}\le\frac{2H}{t}$, and since the weights sum to one, $\sum_i(\alpha_t^i)^2\le(\max_i\alpha_t^i)\sum_i\alpha_t^i\le\frac{2H}{t}$ — the effective sample size is $\sim t/(2H)$, the last $1/H$ fraction of visits. And by induction $\frac1{\sqrt t}\le\sum_{i=1}^t\frac{\alpha_t^i}{\sqrt i}\le\frac2{\sqrt t}$, so a per-visit bonus $\propto1/\sqrt i$ accumulates to a weighted total $\propto1/\sqrt t$ rather than piling up.

The bonus scale $c\sqrt{H^3\iota/t}$ is forced by optimism. Subtracting the Bellman fixed point from the unfolded $Q$ — legal because $\sum_{i=0}^t\alpha_t^i=1$ and the single-sample empirical backup $\hat{\mathbb P}_h^{k_i}V_{h+1}^\star$ is just $V_{h+1}^\star(x_{h+1}^{k_i})$ — gives
$$(Q_h^k-Q_h^\star)(x,a)=\alpha_t^0(H-Q_h^\star)+\sum_{i=1}^t\alpha_t^i\Big[(V_{h+1}^{k_i}-V_{h+1}^\star)(x_{h+1}^{k_i})+\big[(\hat{\mathbb P}_h^{k_i}-\mathbb P_h)V_{h+1}^\star\big](x,a)+b_i\Big].$$
The only stochastic obstacle to $Q\ge Q^\star$ is the middle term: each summand is, conditioned on the past, a mean-zero deviation of a single next-state backup of $V_{h+1}^\star\in[0,H]$, so the $\alpha$-weighted sum is a martingale-difference sequence, and Azuma–Hoeffding with $\sum_i(\alpha_t^i)^2\le2H/t$ bounds it by $\frac c2 H\sqrt{\sum_i(\alpha_t^i)^2\iota}\le c\sqrt{H^3\iota/t}$. The $H^3$ is not arbitrary: one $H^2$ is the squared value range (each backup swings over $[0,H]$), the extra $H$ is the price $\sum(\alpha_t^i)^2\le2H/t$ of the recent-weighting rate, and $\sqrt{1/t}$ is the ordinary visit-count decay. Setting $b_t$ at exactly this scale makes the accumulator $\beta_t:=2\sum_i\alpha_t^i b_i\in[2c\sqrt{H^3\iota/t},4c\sqrt{H^3\iota/t}]$ and, by induction down $h=H,\dots,1$, yields $0\le(Q_h^k-Q_h^\star)\le\alpha_t^0 H+\sum_i\alpha_t^i(V_{h+1}^{k_i}-V_{h+1}^\star)(x_{h+1}^{k_i})+\beta_t$ — optimism on the left, a recursion waiting to telescope on the right.

Assembling the regret: optimism gives $V_1^k\ge V_1^\star$, so $\mathrm{Regret}(K)\le\sum_k\delta_1^k$ with $\delta_h^k=(V_h^k-V_h^{\pi_k})(x_h^k)$. The per-step bound, splitting $Q_h^k-Q_h^{\pi_k}=(Q_h^k-Q_h^\star)+(Q_h^\star-Q_h^{\pi_k})$, using Bellman on the second piece and replacing the expectation $\mathbb P_h$ by its single realization at $x_{h+1}^k$ plus a mean-zero martingale term $\xi_{h+1}^k$, is $\delta_h^k\le\alpha_t^0 H+\sum_i\alpha_t^i\phi_{h+1}^{k_i}+\beta_t-\phi_{h+1}^k+\delta_{h+1}^k+\xi_{h+1}^k$ where $\phi_h^k=(V_h^k-V_h^\star)(x_h^k)\ge0$. Summing over $k$, the prior-leftover contributes at most $SAH$ (each pair pays it once), and regrouping $\sum_k\sum_i\alpha_{n_h^k}^i\phi_{h+1}^{k_i}$ by the tail-sum identity gives each $\phi_{h+1}^{k_i}$ a total weight $1+\frac1H$, so $\sum_k\delta_h^k\le SAH+(1+\frac1H)\sum_k\delta_{h+1}^k+\sum_k(\beta_{n_h^k}+\xi_{h+1}^k)$ after using $\phi\le\delta$. Recursing over the $H$ layers, the amplification compounds to $(1+\frac1H)^H\le e$, a constant, leaving $\sum_k\delta_1^k\le O(H^2SA+\sum_{h,k}(\beta+\xi))$. A pigeonhole on the bonus, $\sum_k\beta_{n_h^k}=O(\sqrt{H^3\iota})\sum_{x,a}\sum_{n=1}^{N}\frac1{\sqrt n}\le O(\sqrt{H^3SAK\iota})=O(\sqrt{H^2SAT\iota})$ per layer (the count split that maximizes $\sum\sqrt N$ is uniform, $N=K/SA$, and $K=T/H$), gives $O(\sqrt{H^4SAT\iota})$ across layers, while Azuma controls $|\sum_{h,k}\xi_{h+1}^k|\le cH\sqrt{T\iota}$. The additive $H^2SA$ folds into the root term, and with probability $1-p$,
$$\mathrm{Regret}(K)\le O\big(\sqrt{H^4SAT\,\iota}\big)=\tilde O\big(\sqrt{H^4SAT}\big).$$
That settles the question: a model-free algorithm — Q-learning plus a UCB bonus and the right rate, storing only the Q-table and one count per entry, $O(SAH)$ space and $O(T)$ time — achieves $\sqrt T$ regret without a simulator. The folklore is not a theorem.

The Hoeffding bonus pays $\sqrt{H^3}$ because it treats every next-state backup as swinging over the full range $H$, which is pessimistic. Along a fixed policy the increments $r_h+V_{h+1}^\pi-V_h^\pi$ are martingale differences whose sum is the return minus its mean, and the return lies in $[0,H]$, so by orthogonality $\mathbb E\sum_h\mathbb V_h V_{h+1}^\pi=\mathbb E\sum_h(r_h+V_{h+1}^\pi-V_h^\pi)^2\le H^2$ — the *total* variance per episode is $O(H^2)$, not the naive $O(H^3)$. Scaling the bonus by the online empirical variance $W_t$ of next-state values (a Bernstein bonus, computed from running sums $\sum V_{h+1}$ and $\sum V_{h+1}^2$, no extra storage) makes the relevant fluctuation $\sqrt{\frac Ht\mathbb V V^\star\iota}$ instead of $\sqrt{\frac{H^3}t\iota}$, and the $O(H^2)$ variance budget buys back one factor of $H$. With $\beta_t=\min\{c_1(\sqrt{\frac Ht(W_t+H)\iota}+\frac{\sqrt{H^7SA}\,\iota}{t}),c_2\sqrt{\frac{H^3\iota}{t}}\}$ and the bonus back-solved as $b_t=\frac{\beta_t-(1-\alpha_t)\beta_{t-1}}{2\alpha_t}$ so the accumulator still telescopes, this UCB-Bernstein variant attains $\mathrm{Regret}(K)\le O(\sqrt{H^3SAT\,\iota}+\sqrt{H^9S^3A^3}\,\iota^2)=\tilde O(\sqrt{H^3SAT})$. Against the lower bound — $H$ JAO two-state chains in series, each step seen only $T/H$ times, forcing $\Omega(\sqrt{H^2SAT})$ — UCB-Hoeffding is within a factor $H$ and UCB-Bernstein within $\sqrt H$ of optimal: model-free, nearly matching model-based, with nothing but a Q-table.

```python
import numpy as np

def q_learning_ucb_hoeffding(env, K, c=1.0, p=0.1):
    """Model-free, O(SAH) space. env exposes S, A, H, reset()->x, step(h,x,a)->(r,x')."""
    S, A, H = env.S, env.A, env.H
    iota = np.log(S * A * (K * H) / p)              # log(SAT/p)
    Q = np.full((H, S, A), float(H))                # optimistic initialization
    V = np.zeros((H + 1, S)); V[:H] = H             # V_{H+1} == 0
    N = np.zeros((H, S, A), dtype=int)
    for k in range(K):
        x = env.reset()
        for h in range(H):
            a = int(Q[h, x].argmax())               # greedy on optimistic Q
            r, x_next = env.step(h, x, a)
            N[h, x, a] += 1
            t = N[h, x, a]
            alpha = (H + 1) / (H + t)                # recent-weighting rate
            b = c * np.sqrt(H**3 * iota / t)         # UCB-Hoeffding bonus
            Q[h, x, a] = (1 - alpha) * Q[h, x, a] + alpha * (r + V[h + 1, x_next] + b)
            V[h, x] = min(H, Q[h, x].max())          # clip to [0,H]
            x = x_next
    return Q
```
