Let me start from what actually costs me when I generate an image. Sampling is solving a differential equation backwards from pure noise to clean data, and every single step of the solver calls the denoiser network — that network evaluation is the entire cost, everything else is arithmetic on tensors. So the only number I really care about is image quality at a small step budget N. If I could get away with five, ten, twenty network calls instead of a thousand, sampling would be two orders of magnitude cheaper. The question is what stops me from just using few steps, and the answer is that a numerical ODE solver is an approximation: at each step it drifts off the true trajectory by some local truncation error, those errors pile up over the N steps into a global error, and with few steps that error is large enough to wreck the picture. So the budget N is really set by how much error I can tolerate, and if I can shrink the per-step error I can shrink N. That reframes the whole thing: I'm not designing a generative model, I'm choosing where to put my few solver steps so that the integration is as accurate as possible.

The thing that decides where the steps land is the discretization — the sequence of noise levels sigma_0 = sigma_max > sigma_1 > ... down to the data, equivalently the times t_i = sigma^{-1}(sigma_i). That's a free choice. The sampler treats the trained network D(x; sigma) as a black box; it doesn't care how D was trained, it just queries it. And yet every schedule I've inherited comes straight from the model's *training-time* forward noising process. DDPM-style models hand me a beta schedule turned into cumulative alpha_bar; the variance-exploding line hands me a geometric sequence of sigmas because that's how its forward SDE was set up; improved DDPM hands me a cosine on alpha_bar. None of these was chosen to make the *reverse integration* accurate. They were chosen for the convenience of the forward process or for theoretical tidiness, and then reused for sampling because nobody pulled the two apart. So the first move is just to insist they're separable: the discretization {sigma_i}, the noise schedule sigma(t), the scaling s(t), and the trained D are independent dials. If that's true, I'm free to pick {sigma_i} purely to minimize integration error, and the same choice should transfer across models.

So what governs the error? From numerical analysis, Euler's method is first order: the local truncation error at a step of size h is tau_i = O(h_i^2). Halve the step, quarter the error. And if the right-hand side is Lipschitz — which it is for these networks — the global error after N steps is bounded by the worst single step, ||e_N|| <= E max_i ||tau_i||, with E depending on N, the endpoints, and the Lipschitz constant. That bound is the lever. To reduce the global error for a given N — and thereby be able to reduce N — I want to minimize max_i ||tau_i||, the largest single-step error. Two knobs do that: the solver (a higher-order method shrinks the exponent on h) and the schedule (which sets how the step sizes, hence the local errors, are distributed across the noise range). Let me handle the schedule first since that's the discretization I'm after.

The local error scales with the curvature of dx/dt. So before I optimize anything, the right schedule depends on where the trajectory is curved, and I should first make the trajectory as straight as I can — that's free error reduction. Here's a choice that does it. Take sigma(t) = t and s(t) = 1. Then the ODE collapses to dx/dt = (x - D(x; t)) / t. Look at what one Euler step straight to t = 0 gives: x + (0 - t)·(x - D)/t = x - (x - D) = D(x; t). The denoiser output itself. So the tangent of the solution trajectory, everywhere, points exactly at the current denoised estimate D(x; t). And that estimate changes only slowly with the noise level — at high sigma it's basically the dataset mean, at low sigma it's basically the input — so the tangent direction barely rotates over most of the range. A trajectory whose tangent barely rotates is nearly straight. Sketch it and the curvature is concentrated in a narrow middle band where the denoiser target is actually transitioning, with near-linear behavior at both large and small sigma. Straight trajectories mean small truncation error for free, so I'll lock in sigma(t) = t, s(t) = 1; this also makes sigma and t interchangeable, which simplifies everything downstream. Now the only thing left is how to space the sigmas.

Where does the error actually live along that trajectory? I can read this off a direct measurement that's been done on a pretrained variance-exploding model: fix a step from sigma_{i-1} to sigma_i, estimate the true x_i by taking two hundred tiny Euler sub-steps over the interval, and compare to the single big step. The single-step RMSE is huge at low noise — around 0.56 for sigma <= 0.5 when steps are an even 1.25 apart in sigma — and much smaller at high noise. And critically, the spread of that error across different random samples x_{i-1} is tiny: the error is essentially a function of the noise level, not of the particular image. That second fact is a gift — it means I don't need a per-sample schedule, a single deterministic {sigma_i} is enough, there'd be no benefit to adapting it image by image. The first fact tells me the obvious thing: the step size must decrease as sigma decreases, because the low-noise region is where a step of given length hurts most. Evenly spaced sigmas can't be right — equal steps everywhere dump a fixed step size into the low-sigma region that's screaming for small steps, while wasting fine steps in the high-sigma region where the field is gentle.

So I need a non-uniform spacing that shrinks the steps toward low sigma. Let me not jump to a formula; let me think about how to *generate* a controllable family of spacings. Here's a clean device: pick a monotonically increasing, unbounded warp function w, sample its argument on a uniform grid, and let sigma_{i<N} = w(A·i + B), with the constants A and B chosen so the endpoints come out right — sigma_0 = sigma_max and sigma_{N-1} = sigma_min — and sigma_N = 0 for the final hop to clean data. Any monotone w gives a valid decreasing schedule; the *shape* of w is what redistributes the steps. Linear w gives uniform spacing in sigma. A logarithm-flavored w packs steps toward small sigma. I want a single knob that sweeps between these and beyond, so I can dial how aggressively the steps concentrate at low noise.

The simplest one-parameter monotone warp is a power law, w(z) = z^rho. Let me see what it gives. Setting sigma = (A·i + B)^rho and demanding the endpoints, it's cleanest to work in the warped coordinate z = sigma^{1/rho}: there the schedule is linear, z_i = sigma_max^{1/rho} + (i/(N-1))(sigma_min^{1/rho} - sigma_max^{1/rho}), and then sigma_i = z_i^rho. Writing it out,

  sigma_{i<N} = ( sigma_max^{1/rho} + (i/(N-1)) (sigma_min^{1/rho} - sigma_max^{1/rho}) )^rho ,   sigma_N = 0.

Check the endpoints: i = 0 gives (sigma_max^{1/rho})^rho = sigma_max, and i = N-1 gives (sigma_min^{1/rho})^rho = sigma_min. Good, A and B fell out automatically by phrasing the interpolation in z. Now the knob. At rho = 1 the warp is the identity, so this is just the linear interpolation in sigma — uniform spacing, the baseline I already argued is wrong. As rho grows, the map z -> z^rho stretches the high end and compresses the low end, so equal steps in z should become long steps near sigma_max and short steps near sigma_min. Let me actually instantiate it and look, rather than trust the hand-wave. Take N = 6, sigma_min = 0.002, sigma_max = 80, and write down the step sizes sigma_i - sigma_{i+1}. At rho = 1 I get sigmas 80, 64, 48, 32, 16, 0.002 and steps all equal at 16 — uniform, as promised. At rho = 7 I get sigmas 80, 24.41, 5.84, 0.965, 0.085, 0.002, and the steps are 55.6, 18.6, 4.87, 0.880, 0.083 — they shrink by nearly three orders of magnitude from the top of the range to the bottom. That is exactly the "small steps where the error lives" behavior I wanted, and it falls straight out of the single parameter rho, with more concentration as rho increases.

Now let me check the other extreme to make sure I understand the family: as rho -> infinity, what happens? Take logs: log sigma_i = rho · log(z_i), and z_i = sigma_max^{1/rho}(1 + (i/(N-1))((sigma_min/sigma_max)^{1/rho} - 1)). For large rho, x^{1/rho} = exp((1/rho) log x) ≈ 1 + (1/rho) log x, so z_i ≈ 1 + (1/rho)[log sigma_max + (i/(N-1))(log sigma_min - log sigma_max)], and rho·log(z_i) ≈ rho·(z_i - 1) → log sigma_max + (i/(N-1))(log sigma_min - log sigma_max). That's uniform spacing in log sigma — the geometric sequence, precisely the variance-exploding schedule. The Taylor argument is suggestive but I've fooled myself with limits before, so I want to see the numbers converge. Comparing the rho-warped schedule to the exact geometric sigmas at N = 6, the maximum relative difference is 2.98 at rho = 10, 0.31 at rho = 50, 0.070 at rho = 200, 0.014 at rho = 1000, 0.0027 at rho = 5000 — falling steadily toward zero, so the limit really is the geometric schedule and not merely something close to it. Notice that rho = 10 is still far off (relative difference ~3): the convergence is slow, which is reassuring in a different way, because it tells me the useful values of rho live nowhere near the geometric limit and the family genuinely has room between its two endpoints. So the power-law family isn't a gadget conjured from nowhere; it's a parametric bridge with the uniform-in-sigma baseline at one end (rho = 1) and the geometric variance-exploding baseline at the other (rho -> infinity), with a continuous knob in between that I've now watched move. A generalization that recovers both schedules I'm trying to beat as limiting cases is at least the right shape of object to be searching over.

Now, what rho? This is where I have to be careful not to fool myself. The cleanest objective is the one I set out with: equalize the local truncation error across the noise levels, so that no single step is the bottleneck — that's what minimizes max_i ||tau_i||. I can probe this the same way the error was measured: for each candidate rho, build the schedule, and look at the measured single-step RMSE as a function of sigma. Increasing rho moves error around — it lowers the error at low sigma (because the steps there get shorter) and raises it at high sigma (because the steps there get longer). So there's a crossover rho where the curve flattens out, where low and high noise contribute about equally. From the measurements, with Euler that approximate balance is around rho = 2, but the RMSE there is still uncomfortably high, a few hundredths — the solver is drifting several units in the last place at every step, and I'd have to crank N up to fix it. That's the signal that Euler alone isn't enough; I need the solver knob too.

Let me bring in the higher-order solver, because the schedule and the solver order interact. Heun's method — improved Euler, the explicit trapezoidal rule — takes the Euler step as a predictor, evaluates the right-hand side again at the predicted endpoint, and averages the two slopes for the corrected step. It's a two-stage second-order Runge-Kutta scheme: one extra network evaluation per step, and the local truncation error drops from O(h^2) to O(h^3). Is one extra eval per step actually worth it? Per step I now pay 2 NFE instead of 1, but the error per step is O(h^3) instead of O(h^2). To make a like-for-like comparison I should fix the NFE budget, not the step count: a budget of M evals buys M Euler steps of size ~1/M, error ~M·(1/M)^2 = 1/M, versus M/2 Heun steps of size ~2/M, error ~(M/2)·(2/M)^3 = 4/M^2. So at matched cost Heun's accumulated error scales like 1/M^2 against Euler's 1/M — better for any M past the small constant, and dramatically better as the budget tightens, which is exactly the regime I care about. The trade is worth it. The one wrinkle: stepping to sigma = 0 would divide by zero in the corrector (the right-hand side has sigma in the denominator), so on that last hop to clean data I fall back to a plain Euler step. With Heun in place and the same measurement, the error curves separate more sharply by rho (cubic vs quadratic), and the rho that makes the measured single-step RMSE flattest across the whole range comes out near rho = 3, with RMSE sitting in a fairly tight band of a few thousandths. I'll call rho = 3 the truncation-error-balancing schedule, with the caveat that "flattest" here is read off one model's measurement and I'd want to see it hold across the others before leaning on the exact number. If I cared about raw numerical accuracy in RGB space — say I'm running the ODE forward to encode an image and then back to reconstruct it, where the global error literally tells me how much the image degrades — this is where I'd stop, at rho = 3.

But I have to stop and ask what I'm actually optimizing, because raw RMSE in pixel space is not the same as image *quality*. When I generate a novel image from scratch, I'm judged by FID, not by how faithfully I tracked one particular ODE trajectory. And different noise levels do not contribute equally to FID. So the truncation-error-balancing argument, clean as it is, optimizes the wrong proxy for generation. Let me think about which errors are perceptually cheap and which are expensive. At very high sigma — say sigma near sigma_max — the sample is essentially indistinguishable from pure Gaussian noise; the exact value of sigma_max is somewhat arbitrary, and I could move it around quite a bit without changing the distribution of images I ultimately produce. So error introduced way up there gets absorbed — it perturbs which noise sample I started from, not what the data looks like. Down at low sigma it's the opposite: that's where the fine structure of the image is resolved, where the denoiser is committing to actual content, and an error there shows up directly as a visible artifact in the output. So the perceptual value of accuracy is wildly unequal across sigma — cheap up high, decisive down low — and equalizing the *numerical* error across sigma is therefore leaving FID on the table. I should deliberately *unbalance* it: spend coarser steps up high where error is forgiven, to afford finer steps down low where it counts.

The direction is clear even if the magnitude isn't: push rho past the error-balancing value of 3, because larger rho concentrates resolution toward low sigma, which is exactly the reallocation I just argued for. How far past 3? This is the one place I can't settle from the desk — FID is a function of 50,000 generated images through an Inception network, not anything I can write down in closed form, so I have to lean on a sweep over rho across the models and step budgets and read off where FID is best. I expect the trend to be: rho = 3 already decent, FID improving as rho grows beyond it (trading tolerable high-noise error for reduced low-noise error), and then turning back up once rho is large enough that the high-sigma steps get so coarse the early trajectory falls apart — a single interior optimum rather than a monotone improvement, since I just verified the high-rho end runs off toward the geometric schedule, which is one of the baselines I'm beating. If that U-shape holds, what I want is a value comfortably inside the basin that doesn't need re-tuning per model. From such a sweep rho = 7 comes out as a robust choice sitting well inside that range, so I'll fix rho = 7 and stop treating it as a per-model knob — though I'd flag that 7 is an empirical pick from the sweep, not a derived constant, and the honest claim is only that the optimum lies well above the rho = 3 balance point, not that it is exactly 7. The conceptual split is the durable part: rho ≈ 3 balances the integration error, and the FID-best rho sits above it, and they differ exactly because high-noise accuracy is perceptually cheap.

Now the endpoints, sigma_min and sigma_max, because the formula needs them and they aren't arbitrary. At the bottom, I don't want to chase sigma all the way to zero with the power-law spacing — below some small noise level the denoiser's job is essentially trivial (the noise component is vanishingly small and discerning it is both hard and pointless), so I cap the lowest *resolved* level at a small sigma_min and then take the single final Euler step from there straight to sigma = 0 for the clean image. A value around sigma_min = 0.002 works: small enough that everything perceptually relevant is captured, large enough that the schedule isn't wasting steps splitting hairs in noise I can't see anyway. At the top, sigma_max needs to be large enough that p(x; sigma_max) is effectively pure Gaussian noise relative to the data scale. For images standardized to roughly unit-ish range — taking the data standard deviation around sigma_data = 0.5 — a sigma_max of 80 is enormous compared to the data, so the starting distribution really is indistinguishable from N(0, sigma_max^2 I). And notice this is the very same fact that made high-sigma error cheap: sigma_max being "somewhat arbitrary" is both why I can pick 80 freely and why I'm happy to let rho = 7 put coarse steps up there. The endpoints and the rho choice are telling the same story.

Let me also sanity-check the count and the boundaries of the formula. I want N solver steps, which means N+1 nodes t_0, ..., t_N. The power-law formula defines sigma_0 through sigma_{N-1} (that's N values, indices 0 to N-1, with i/(N-1) sweeping 0 to 1), and then sigma_N = 0 is appended as the terminal clean-data node — N+1 nodes total, N steps between them, the last of which is the Euler hop to zero. Strictly decreasing: z_i is linear and decreasing in i from sigma_max^{1/rho} to sigma_min^{1/rho}, and z -> z^rho is increasing for positive z, so sigma_i decreases monotonically; appending 0 keeps it decreasing. First node sigma_max, last resolved node sigma_min, terminal 0. All the contract conditions hold by construction.

One more thing I want to pin down before I write code: I picked Heun (a two-stage RK2), but RK2 is a one-parameter family — x_{i+1} = x_i + h[(1 - 1/(2a)) d_i + (1/(2a)) f(x_i + a·h·d_i; t_i + a·h)], where a controls where the second slope is sampled. a = 1 is Heun (second slope at the full endpoint), a = 1/2 is the midpoint method, a = 2/3 is Ralston. Why a = 1? Two reasons. Experimentally a = 1 is essentially optimal for this problem (a value slightly above 1 nudges it a hair better but overshoots the target and has no principled justification, so I won't make it a hyperparameter). And there's a structural reason that clinches it: with a = 1 the extra evaluation lands exactly at t_{i+1}, a node already in my schedule. That matters for models trained only at a discrete set of noise levels — I can guarantee every network query sits on a trained level, which the midpoint and Ralston variants can't promise because they probe at intermediate times. So a = 1, plain Heun, with the Euler fallback at the final zero-step.

Now I translate the derivation into the actual schedule contract. I use `ramp = linspace(0, 1, N)`, not `N+1`, because the formula is responsible for the `N` nonzero noise levels from `sigma_max` through `sigma_min`; the clean endpoint is a separate appended zero. I compute `sigma_min^{1/rho}` and `sigma_max^{1/rho}`, linearly interpolate between them on that ramp, raise the result to `rho`, and append `sigma_N = 0`. This detail is where an off-by-one error would quietly change the method: using `linspace(0, 1, N+1)` would make the terminal part of the warped interpolation instead of the explicit final clean-data node. Here the intended object is exactly `N` scheduled nonzero levels plus the appended zero, giving `N+1` nodes and `N` solver intervals.

I also check the implementation against the sampler loop, because the sign has to survive contact with code. With `sigma(t) = t` and `s(t) = 1`, each derivative is `d = (x - D(x; sigma)) / sigma`, and the step size is `dt = sigma_next - sigma_cur`, which is negative because the noise levels decrease. The Euler predictor is therefore `x_pred = x + dt d`, moving along the reverse-time trajectory toward lower noise. If `sigma_next = 0`, I stop there, because the corrector would divide by zero. Otherwise I evaluate `d_next = (x_pred - D(x_pred; sigma_next)) / sigma_next` at the endpoint and replace the predictor by the trapezoidal update `x + dt (d + d_next) / 2`. That is Heun with `alpha = 1`, so the second network query lands exactly on the next scheduled node.

The code cross-checks line up with this interpretation. The common `get_sigmas_karras` helper constructs `ramp = torch.linspace(0, 1, n)`, applies the same `(max_inv_rho + ramp * (min_inv_rho - max_inv_rho)) ** rho` expression, and appends zero. The generator loop writes the same ramp as `step_indices / (num_steps - 1)`, optionally rounds the nonzero levels to values supported by the network, and then concatenates a zero. The scheduler implementation in a widely used library does the same thing in two phases: `set_timesteps` supplies `linspace(0, 1, num_inference_steps)`, `_compute_karras_sigmas` applies the formula, and the final sigma is appended as zero by default. These all agree on the three practical details that matter: the ramp runs from 0 to 1 over the nonzero levels, the formula starts at `sigma_max` and ends at `sigma_min`, and the clean endpoint is appended.

The defaults now have their place in the reasoning rather than being taste: `rho = 7` is the empirical FID-best value from the sweep, deliberately on the high-resolution-at-low-noise side of the `rho ≈ 3` numerical-error balance point (with the honest caveat that only the *direction* past 3 is derived, the exact 7 is measured); `sigma_min = 0.002` is the lowest resolved noise before the final Euler hop to zero; `sigma_max = 80` is effectively pure noise for data with scale `sigma_data ≈ 0.5`. I can put a number on that last claim: the per-coordinate signal-to-noise ratio of clean data against the starting noise is sigma_data / sigma_max = 0.5 / 80 = 0.00625, so the data contributes about 0.006 of a standard deviation on top of the noise at the top of the schedule — far below anything the sampler resolves, so the start really is indistinguishable from N(0, sigma_max^2 I). That same smallness is why errors at that end are comparatively cheap.

Let me trace the causal chain once more. Sampling is numerical ODE integration where each step costs a network call, so quality at low `N` is governed by per-step truncation error, and the global error is bounded by the worst single step. I first straighten the trajectory by setting `sigma(t) = t`, `s(t) = 1`, which makes a single Euler step point at the denoiser output and keeps curvature confined to a narrow middle band. The measured single-step error then shows that the bottleneck is at low noise and is nearly sample-independent, so I need one deterministic schedule whose steps shrink toward low `sigma`. I get a controllable family by warping a uniform grid through `sigma_{i<N} = (sigma_max^{1/rho} + (i/(N-1))(sigma_min^{1/rho} - sigma_max^{1/rho}))^rho`, which contains uniform-in-`sigma` at `rho = 1` and the geometric variance-exploding schedule as `rho -> infinity`. Balancing numerical error points to `rho ≈ 3` with Heun, but generated image quality is judged by FID, and accuracy at high `sigma` is perceptually cheap while accuracy at low `sigma` is decisive, so the FID-best `rho` lies above the balance point — measured at `rho = 7`, which I take as the default. Heun gives `O(h^3)` local error at one extra evaluation per step (a 1/M^2-vs-1/M win at matched evaluation budget), the final zero step falls back to Euler, and the whole artifact is the schedule plus that solver pairing.
