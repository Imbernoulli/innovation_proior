The Web is enormous, directed, and uncurated, and a keyword query routinely matches more pages than anyone could read. A text index already tells me *which* pages contain the query words; what I cannot do yet is *order* them so the genuinely important pages come first. So the real problem is not matching but importance: I want one number per page, computed once over the whole link graph, query-independent, that says how important a page is — and the score must be objective and mechanical (read off the link structure, not from editorial lists, so it scales to ≈10⁸ nodes), resistant to manipulation (the Web is full of parties spinning up pages to inflate their own rank), and faithful to common sense (one link from the Yahoo home page should outweigh a thousand links from pages nobody visits). The obvious baselines all fail on at least one of these. Raw backlink counting treats every link as an equal vote, so it both ignores *who* is linking and rewards the cheapest possible attack — an adversary mass-producing pages that link to their own. Bonacich's eigenvector centrality, $x = (1/\lambda)Ax$, captures the recursive idea exactly but is well-behaved only on connected, essentially undirected graphs; on a directed, non-strongly-connected Web the dominant eigenvector is neither unique nor everywhere positive, rank concentrates in or drains from one-way subgraphs, and columns of zeros from dead-end pages destroy the stochasticity the argument relies on. Katz's attenuated path-counting, $x = ((I-\alpha A)^{-1}-I)\,\mathbf{1}$, needs $\alpha$ bounded against the spectral radius, still uses the raw adjacency so a high-out-degree node showers full-weight endorsements everywhere, and is not obviously a probability distribution nor defined on a disconnected directed graph. Seeley's row-normalized status has the right anti-manipulation instinct — split each node's endorsement among its choices — but the stochastic fixed point it defines is only well-posed on an irreducible chain. Every ancestor encodes "importance begets importance" as a linear-algebra fixed point and every one of them is clean only under a connectivity assumption the Web violates.

I propose PageRank. The starting commitment is that importance is recursive — a page is important if important pages link to it — and that each page divides one fixed unit of endorsement evenly among the pages it links to, because a fixed quantity split across out-links means adding out-links only dilutes your influence rather than manufacturing it, which is exactly the manipulation-resistance the raw-count and adjacency baselines lack. Writing $B_u$ for the pages linking to $u$ and $N_v$ for the out-degree of $v$, the recursion is $r(u) = \sum_{v\in B_u} r(v)/N_v$, which in matrix form with $M_{uv} = 1/N_v$ when $v$ links to $u$ becomes $$r = M r.$$ So $r$ is the eigenvalue-1 eigenvector of $M$, and $M$ is **column-stochastic** — every column $v$ sums to $N_v\cdot(1/N_v)=1$ — which fell out of "split your endorsement evenly," not from any imposition. That buys existence for free: a column-stochastic $M$ satisfies $M^\top e = e$ for the all-ones vector $e$, so 1 is an eigenvalue of $M^\top$ and hence of $M$, and a solution exists. There is a second reading I like even more: $M^\top$ is row-stochastic, the transition matrix of a Markov chain that from page $v$ jumps to a uniformly random out-link, and $r$ is the stationary distribution of that walk — importance is literally how often an aimless random clicker lands on a page.

The bare object is broken three ways on the real Web, and naming each failure tells me what to fix. When the graph splits into clumps that point richly within themselves but not to each other, $M$ is block-diagonal-ish, the eigenvalue-1 eigenspace is multi-dimensional, and $r=Mr$ has a whole family of solutions — the ranking is ambiguous. A pair of pages that link only to each other but receive rank from outside forms a **rank sink**: with no escaping out-links they hand their score back and forth and hoard whatever drips in, so iteration piles all importance into a cul-de-sac. And **dangling pages** with no out-links at all — un-crawled URLs, PDFs, images, dead-end leaves, tens of millions of them — have an all-zero column, $N_v=0$, so $M$ becomes column-*sub*stochastic, $M^\top e = e$ fails, total rank $\sum_u r(u)$ drops every step by the mass sitting on dead ends, and the whole vector decays toward zero. The physical fix is read straight off the random-surfer picture: a real person does not follow links forever or get trapped in a two-page loop or stranded on a dead end — they get bored and jump to some page out of the blue. So with probability $\alpha$ the surfer clicks a random out-link and with probability $1-\alpha$ teleports to a uniformly random page. First I make $M$ a genuine column-stochastic operator by replacing each dangling page's zero column with the uniform column $(1/n)\mathbf{1}$ — the dead-ended surfer simply jumps uniformly — and then I mix in boredom to get the **Google matrix** $$G = \alpha M + (1-\alpha)\,\tfrac{1}{n}\,\mathbf{1}\mathbf{1}^\top.$$ Every column of $\alpha M$ sums to $\alpha$ and every column of the teleport term sums to $1-\alpha$, so $G$ is column-stochastic: no leak, total rank conserved, dangling hole plugged. Rank sinks can no longer hoard because $(1-\alpha)$ of whatever they hold teleports out every step. Stated cleanly, from any page you reach any page in one step with probability at least $(1-\alpha)/n>0$, so **every entry of $G$ is at least $(1-\alpha)/n$ — $G$ is strictly positive** — and that single fact is the key that the ancestors could not reach.

Strict positivity lets me invoke Perron–Frobenius: for a strictly positive matrix the dominant eigenvalue is real, simple, and strictly larger in modulus than every other, with a unique strictly positive eigenvector and no other nonnegative eigenvector except its scalar multiples. Since $G$ is column-stochastic, $e^\top G = e^\top$ gives $\rho(G)\le 1$ while eigenvalue 1 exists, so the Perron root is exactly 1. Therefore $r = Gr$ has a unique solution up to scale, it is strictly positive (so no page is exactly zero — and the positivity forces a single ranking across what used to be disconnected components, killing the non-uniqueness too), and normalized it is the unique stationary distribution of the now irreducible, aperiodic random-surfer chain. Existence, uniqueness, and positivity — everything the ancestors had to assume — are delivered *because* the teleport made the matrix positive. Unfolding $G$ scalar-wise, with $d=\sum_{v:N_v=0} r(v)$ the current dangling mass, gives $r(u) = \alpha\sum_{v\to u,\,N_v>0} r(v)/N_v + \alpha d/n + (1-\alpha)/n$: an $\alpha$-weighted link flow, the dead-end mass $\alpha d/n$ put back into circulation, and the constant teleport trickle $(1-\alpha)/n$ handed to every page just for existing.

Computing this needs the power method, because $n\approx 10^8$ makes a full $O(n^3)$ eigendecomposition unthinkable and I only need the single eigenvalue-1 eigenvector of a sparse operator. I must never materialize $G$ — it is dense, every entry $\ge (1-\alpha)/n$ — but I never have to: $\alpha M r$ is a sparse mat-vec over the link columns, and both the redistributed dangling mass and the teleport are scalar broadcasts to the uniform target, so one step costs $O(\#\text{links}+n)$. Convergence is governed by the second eigenvalue, and the teleport pins it down. Any eigenvector $x_2$ of $G$ for $\lambda_2\neq 1$ is orthogonal to the eigenvalue-1 left eigenvector $e$ (left/right eigenvectors for distinct eigenvalues are orthogonal), so $e^\top x_2 = 0$, meaning $x_2$ sums to zero. The rank-one teleport then annihilates it: $(\mathbf{1}\mathbf{1}^\top)x_2 = \mathbf{1}(e^\top x_2)=0$. Hence $\lambda_2 x_2 = G x_2 = \alpha M x_2$, so $M x_2 = (\lambda_2/\alpha)x_2$, and since $M$ is column-stochastic with $\rho(M)\le 1$, $$|\lambda_2| \le \alpha.$$ The power-method error contracts like $|\lambda_2|^k \le \alpha^k$, so reaching an $L_1$ tolerance $\varepsilon$ takes about $\log\varepsilon/\log\alpha$ iterations — independent of $n$. With $\alpha=0.85$ this worst-case bound is roughly 85 iterations for $\varepsilon=10^{-6}$ and 99 for $\varepsilon=10^{-7}$. This is why $\alpha=0.85$ is not an arbitrary knob: $\alpha<1$ is what makes $G$ strictly positive (uniqueness) and $|\lambda_2|\le\alpha<1$ (convergence); pushing $\alpha\to 1$ makes the ranking more link-faithful but lets $|\lambda_2|$ approach 1 so convergence collapses and the disconnection and sinks reassert themselves; pushing $\alpha\to 0$ washes the score toward uniform $1/n$ and ignores the links entirely. The damping factor is simultaneously the surfer's persistence, the guarantor of a unique positive ranking, and the convergence-rate control — one number doing three jobs.

```python
import numpy as np

def pagerank(A, alpha=0.85, tol=1.0e-6, max_iter=100):
    """PageRank = principal eigenvector / stationary distribution of
    G = alpha*M + (1-alpha)*(1/n)*1*1^T, via the power method.
    A: sparse adjacency, A[i, j] = 1 iff page j links to page i."""
    n = A.shape[0]
    if n == 0:
        return np.array([])

    # Sparse normalized link matrix for non-dangling columns; dangling columns
    # stay zero here and are patched by the dangle_mass broadcast below.
    out_deg = np.asarray(A.sum(axis=0)).ravel()
    dangling = (out_deg == 0)                  # dead-end pages (zero columns)
    inv = np.zeros(n)
    inv[~dangling] = 1.0 / out_deg[~dangling]
    M = A.multiply(inv)

    p = np.full(n, 1.0 / n)     # uniform teleport target (rank source)
    r = np.full(n, 1.0 / n)     # positive start, sums to 1

    for _ in range(max_iter):
        r_last = r
        dangle_mass = alpha * r_last[dangling].sum()       # patch zero columns
        r = alpha * (M @ r_last) + (dangle_mass + (1.0 - alpha)) * p
        if np.abs(r - r_last).sum() < n * tol:             # L1 convergence
            return r
    raise RuntimeError("power iteration did not converge in max_iter")
```
