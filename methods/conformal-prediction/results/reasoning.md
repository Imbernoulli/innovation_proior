I start with a prediction algorithm that is useful but mute about its own reliability. It may be an SVM, a nearest-neighbor classifier, a least-squares line, or a neural classifier. It gives me a label, a number, or a score, but if I ask for a finite-sample statement about the next label, I immediately face a wall. A probability from the model is only as good as the model. A regression standard error is only as good as the regression assumptions. A PAC bound can be valid but too loose to guide a single prediction. I need a way to make a prediction set whose error probability is controlled without pretending that the model's private uncertainty is correct.

My first thought is to use the old validation idea: hold out data, measure errors, and pick a high empirical error quantile. That gets close to the practical recipe, but it does not yet explain why the next example should be protected in finite samples. A validation quantile is just a statistic unless I can show that the future error is in the same symmetric position as the past errors. If I train and tune in ways that look at the same data, the future point may no longer be comparable to those errors. I need to identify exactly what symmetry survives.

The older transductive confidence work points me in a better direction. Instead of asking the SVM for a probability, I can try possible labels for the new object and ask whether the completed sample looks strange. In the support-vector version, the new point's role as a support vector gives an incertitude, and the resulting confidence has a finite-sample interpretation. That is a real clue, but it is also too specialized. I do not want confidence to depend on a particular SVM geometry. I want the support-vector count to be just one possible way of measuring how out of place an example looks.

So I abstract the only part I really need. For any completed labeled sample, I assign each example a score measuring its strangeness relative to the others. The score must be computed symmetrically: if I permute the examples, the scores permute with them. It does not have to be a probability, a likelihood ratio, or a true conditional error. It can be an absolute residual, a nearest-neighbor ratio, a classifier-derived score, or another heuristic. Larger means the example fits worse.

Now I can test a candidate label. I temporarily set the new label to `y`, compute scores `alpha_1,...,alpha_n` for all examples in the completed bag, and look at the candidate example's score `alpha_n`. If `alpha_n` is larger than almost all of the others, then the completed sample is suspicious under that candidate label. The natural p-value is the fraction of completed-sample scores at least as large as `alpha_n`: `p_y = #{i: alpha_i >= alpha_n}/n`. I keep `y` when `p_y > eps` and reject it when `p_y <= eps`.

The proof finally becomes simple enough to trust. Suppose the candidate label is the true label. Then the completed examples are exchangeable. Conditional on their unordered bag, no position is special, so the true new example's score is equally entitled to be any one of the scores, up to conservative handling of ties. The event `p_{Y_n} <= eps` is exactly the event that the true new example looks too extreme relative to the completed bag. By exchangeability this can happen with probability at most `eps`. I am no longer proving that the model knows its uncertainty. I am proving that the true example has no privileged extreme rank among exchangeable examples.

This also explains why the method accepts any nonconformity measure but does not make all measures equally good. If the score is bad, the prediction set can become wide, multiple, or otherwise useless. It may include too many labels because the heuristic cannot separate plausible completions from implausible ones. But the error guarantee is still protected by symmetry. If the score is good, it rejects false labels aggressively while keeping the true label from being rejected too often. Validity and efficiency are different jobs.

The full candidate-label construction is conceptually clean, but it can be computationally painful. For many models, testing every candidate label means recomputing scores or refitting models many times. The practical compromise is to split the data. I first train or choose the scoring rule without using the calibration labels for threshold selection. Then I compute calibration scores `S_i = s(X_i,Y_i)`. For a new `x`, I include every `y` whose score `s(x,y)` is no larger than a calibrated threshold. The threshold is not the naive `1-alpha` quantile. With `n` calibration examples and `0 <= alpha < 1`, I set `k = ceil((n+1)(1-alpha))` and take the `k`th smallest value of the multiset `{S_1,...,S_n,+infty}`. When `k = n+1`, the threshold is `+infty`; otherwise this is the conservative higher empirical order statistic.

The split proof is the same rank argument with less machinery. Once the scoring rule is fixed, the calibration scores and the true future score are exchangeable. The prediction set misses exactly when the true future score exceeds the calibrated threshold. Because `k` is at least `(n+1)(1-alpha)`, no more than an `alpha` fraction of the `n+1` exchangeable rank positions can lie strictly above that threshold. Ties and discrete scores can make the result conservative, and randomized tie-breaking can sharpen upper-error statements, but the lower coverage guarantee does not come from the model.

I have to resist overselling the result. It is a marginal coverage guarantee over the exchangeable draw, not a promise of exact coverage at every fixed `x`. It does not say that every subgroup is calibrated unless I add a partition or a stronger multigroup construction. It does not say that a 90 percent set contains exactly the right semantic uncertainty for one individual case. It says something narrower and stronger in the finite-sample sense: under exchangeability, the procedure cannot miss the true label more often than the chosen significance level, regardless of the unknown distribution and regardless of whether the score is a correct probabilistic model.

The final method is therefore a wrapper around prediction rather than a new predictive model. It takes whatever structure I have for judging candidate labels and makes one demand of it: produce a symmetric ordering of how strange labeled examples look. The rest of the guarantee is extracted from permutation symmetry. That is the central turn from confidence as a model assertion to confidence as an exchangeable rank statement.
