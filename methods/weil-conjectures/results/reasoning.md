The starting data is a sequence of counts: for a variety `X` over `F_q`, the number `N_r = |X(F_{q^r})|` of points over each extension, packaged as `Z(X,t) = exp(sum_{r >= 1} N_r t^r/r)`. What would it mean to explain this sequence? The curve case, known since the German school of the 1930s and proved in general by Weil in the 1940s, shows the shape of a real explanation: there `Z` is rational, `N_r = 1 + q^r - sum_j a_j^r` for `2g` algebraic numbers `a_j`, and the Riemann hypothesis `|a_j| = q^(1/2)` comes from a positivity statement — on the Tate module of the Jacobian, the degree of `m - n pi` is a nonnegative integer for all integers `m, n`, and since that degree is `m^2 + c mn + q n^2` with `c` the trace of Frobenius, the discriminant must be nonpositive, forcing `c^2 <= 4q`. Two lessons are already visible here. First, the counts become transparent only when they are realized as traces of a linear operator — the `a_j` are eigenvalues of Frobenius on a rank-`2g` module. Second, the size bound is a separate input, a positivity engine, and it is genuinely the hard part.

My way into the general problem is computational, the same way Weil entered it in 1949. Take a diagonal hypersurface `a_0 X_0^m + ... + a_r X_r^m = 0` over `F_q`. The number of solutions can be written explicitly with Gauss sums, and the Davenport-Hasse relation (1935), which relates a Gauss sum over a field to its lift over every extension, is exactly what is needed to assemble all extensions at once: the generating series closes up into

`sum_{n >= 1} N_n T^(n-1) = d/dT log( 1 / ((1-T)(1-qT)...(1-q^(r-1)T)) ) + (-1)^r d/dT log P(T)`,

where `P` is a polynomial whose degree `A` is the number of rational tuples `alpha_i` satisfying `n alpha_i = 1 mod 1`, `sum alpha_i = 0 mod 1`, `0 < alpha_i < 1`. So for these varieties the zeta function is visibly rational, visibly an alternating product, and the degrees of its factors are specific computable integers. Then comes the shock that generates the whole program: Dolbeault pointed out to Weil that the Betti numbers of the same hypersurface viewed as a complex manifold are the coefficients of `1 + T^2 + ... + T^(2r-2) + A T^r`. The degrees of the arithmetic factors are the topological Betti numbers. That cannot be coincidence; it says the rational factors should be characteristic polynomials of something acting on cohomology groups of the right dimensions. A second heuristic points the same way: for a finite self-map of degree `delta` of a complex variety, the Lefschetz fixed-point formalism forces a functional equation `Z(1/delta^r ... )` with sign and exponent built from the Euler characteristic; Frobenius on a `d`-dimensional variety has degree `q^d`, which predicts exactly the conjectured functional equation. So the conjectures are not four separate guesses — they are what the zeta function would look like if a cohomology theory existed with Frobenius acting on it. The problem is now sharply posed: build that theory, and prove the eigenvalue bound.

The first attempt fails on coefficients, and the failure is instructive. One might hope for a cohomology with rational coefficients — after all, the traces `N_r` are integers. This is impossible, and a supersingular elliptic curve shows why: its endomorphism algebra is a quaternion division algebra over `Q`, nonsplit exactly at `p` and at infinity (Hasse 1936), and this algebra must act on `H^1`, which has dimension 2. A quaternion division algebra over `Q` cannot act on a 2-dimensional vector space over `Q`, and it stays a division algebra over `R` and over `Q_p`, so those coefficient fields fail too. Serre explained this obstruction to Grothendieck in the 1950s. Over `Q_l` for `l != p` the division algebra splits into a matrix algebra and the action exists. So the coefficient field is forced, not chosen: `l`-adic with `l != p`. The p-adic side fails differently — Serre's candidate Betti numbers from Hodge numbers give only an upper bound (Igusa's example), and Witt vector cohomology sees only the `H^(0,r)` part; a usable p-adic Weil cohomology (crystalline) comes only later and does not carry the comparison formalism needed here.

The second attempt fails on topology. The Zariski topology has too few open sets: constant sheaves have no interesting higher cohomology, and locally trivial fiber spaces are too rare. Serre's 1958 seminar enlarged "locally trivial" to "locally isotrivial" — trivial after pullback to finite etale covers — and Grothendieck's reaction was that this would give the Weil cohomology in all dimensions. His key move was to notice that sheaf theory does not need open subsets at all; it needs a category with coverings satisfying three axioms (stable under base change, local character, isomorphisms are coverings). Taking etale maps as coverings gives the etale site, and sheaf cohomology on it. The test that this is the right theory is Artin's comparison theorem: for torsion coefficients over `C`, etale cohomology coincides with singular cohomology. Since etale cohomology naturally takes torsion coefficients, one defines `H^i(X_bar, Z_l) = lim H^i_et(X_bar, Z/l^n)` and tensors with `Q_l` — this is the SGA 4 construction of 1963-64, and it inherits everything the axioms demand: finite dimensionality, functoriality, Poincare duality, Kunneth, cycle classes, and comparison to Betti cohomology after good reduction.

With the theory in hand, the first three conjectures are not so much proved as read off. A point over `F_{q^r}` is a fixed point of `Frob^r` on `X_bar`, and these fixed points have multiplicity 1 because `1 - Frob^r` is etale, so the graph of `Frob^r` meets the diagonal transversally. The Lefschetz trace formula — Lefschetz's original 1924 argument transcribed onto the Weil cohomology axioms, the graph class expanded in a Kunneth basis and intersected with the diagonal — gives `N_r = sum_i (-1)^i Tr(Frob^r | H^i(X_bar, Q_l))`. Now substitute into `log Z`: the elementary identity `log det(1 - tA) = -sum_{n >= 1} Tr(A^n) t^n / n` applied degree by degree collapses the generating function into `Z(X,t) = product_i det(1 - t Frob | H^i)^((-1)^(i+1))`. Rationality is finite-dimensionality; nothing arithmetic remains in the statement. The functional equation is duality acting on the spectrum: Frobenius acts as `q^d` on the top cohomology, so duality pairs each eigenvalue `alpha` in degree `i` with `q^d/alpha` in degree `2d-i`, and the conjectured symmetry `t -> 1/(q^d t)` falls out with the Euler characteristic as exponent. The Betti-number statement is the comparison theorem. Grothendieck's 1964 Bourbaki trace formula does all this in one stroke, even for arbitrary varieties once cohomology with compact support is used. Note what this route explains that Dwork's 1960 p-adic analytic proof of rationality does not: Dwork wrote `Z` as a quotient of p-adically entire functions — Serre records that Weil himself had tried to push his own method p-adically and gotten nowhere — and got rationality with no functional equation, no Betti interpretation, and no eigenvalue bound. The cohomological factorization is the content; rationality alone is a shadow of it.

What remains is the Riemann hypothesis, and here the machinery stalls. In the curve case the bound came from positivity — Weil's trace inequality `sigma(D ∘ D') = def(D) >= 0`, a restatement of the Castelnuovo-Severi defect inequality, which in turn follows from the Hodge index theorem on `C x C`. The natural generalization is Grothendieck's standard conjectures, which would supply exactly this positivity in all dimensions; but they are intractable, and the more direct hope of reducing everything to curves by devissage fails — Serre produced varieties that are not even birational to a quotient of a product of curves. Deligne's way through came from outside geometry altogether, from automorphic forms. Rankin in 1939, attacking the Ramanujan conjecture `|tau(p)| <= 2 p^(11/2)`, studied the Dirichlet series `sum |c(n)|^2 n^(-s)`; its coefficients are nonnegative, so Landau's theorem says the abscissa of convergence is a singularity, and control of the poles gives a bound — off from the truth, but a bound. Langlands remarked in 1970 that Rankin's trick, applied to enough L-functions at once, would actually prove Ramanujan: the `2m`-th power bound `|lambda|^(2m) <= p` for every `m` forces `|lambda| <= 1` in the limit. Deligne's insight was that Grothendieck's sheaf-theoretic zeta functions supply exactly the needed family, with poles computable by monodromy. Embed `X` in a Lefschetz pencil over `P^1`; on the open `U` where the fibers are smooth, the middle cohomology of the fibers forms a local system `E`, and Grothendieck's trace formula gives `Z(U, E, T)` as an alternating product of `det(1 - pi T | H^i_c(U, E))`. Cup product on the middle cohomology of odd-dimensional fibers is a nondegenerate alternating pairing, so monodromy lands in a symplectic group, and the monodromy is big — Zariski dense in `Sp`. Then classical invariant theory of the symplectic group computes the coinvariants of `E^(tensor 2m)`: they are copies of `Q_l(-md)`, so the zeta function of `E^(tensor 2m)` has poles only at `q^(md+1)`. Now the positivity engine turns: `Tr(pi^n | E^(tensor 2m)) = Tr(pi^n | E)^(2m) >= 0`, because the local traces are rational integers, so Landau's theorem applies to `log Z` and yields `|alpha|^(2m) <= q^(md+1)` for every eigenvalue `alpha` of Frobenius on the fiber cohomology. Take the `2m`-th root and let `m` go to infinity: `|alpha| <= q^(d/2)`; Poincare duality, which makes `q^d/alpha` also an eigenvalue, gives equality. The remaining slack of `1/2` in the general case is removed by applying the same bound to the even-dimensional powers `X^m` and using Kunneth — `alpha^m` occurs in middle cohomology of `X^m`, so `|alpha|^m <= q^(md/2 + 1/2)` for all even `m`, and again the limit is sharp.

So the whole route is a change of representation along the bridge fixed points -> cohomological traces -> Frobenius eigenvalues, and each conjecture is discharged by a different component of the machine: rationality by finite-dimensionality, the functional equation by duality, the Betti numbers by comparison, and the Riemann hypothesis by a positivity theorem that had to be imported from analytic number theory when the geometric positivity of the standard conjectures proved unreachable. Direct counting sees only the left side `N_r`; the etale theory constructs the right side as traces of powers of one operator; and the eigenvalue bound says that operator is, in every degree, as close to a scaled unitary as integrality allows.
