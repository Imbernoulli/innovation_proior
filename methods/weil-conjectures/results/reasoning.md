Start from the data Weil put on the table: for every extension `F_{q^r}` of the base field, a variety `X/F_q` has a finite number `N_r = |X(F_{q^r})|` of rational points. The zeta function `Z(X,t) = exp(sum_{r >= 1} N_r t^r/r)` is not merely a convenient package. If it is rational with controlled zeros and poles, then infinitely many arithmetic counts are being generated by finitely many hidden quantities.

The first insight is to stop treating `N_r` as a raw number of solutions and instead see it as a fixed-point count. A point of `X` over `F_{q^r}` is exactly a geometric point fixed by `Frob^r`. That makes the problem resemble the classical Lefschetz fixed-point formula: count fixed points of a map by taking alternating traces of the induced map on cohomology. If an appropriate cohomology theory exists over finite fields, then arithmetic counting should become a trace computation.

This is why the solution route had to be geometric rather than only analytic or combinatorial. Ordinary singular cohomology does not apply directly in characteristic `p`, and de Rham cohomology alone does not have the right finite-field Frobenius trace formalism in the required generality. Grothendieck's `l`-adic etale cohomology supplies finite-dimensional vector spaces `H^i(X_bar,Q_l)` for `l != p`, functoriality, duality, and an action of Frobenius. The operator whose spectrum matters is not invented after the counts are known; it is the same Frobenius map whose fixed points are the finite-field points.

Once the trace formula is available, the mechanism becomes linear algebra. With a fixed Frobenius convention, the point count has the form `N_r = sum_i (-1)^i Tr(Frob^r | H^i(X_bar,Q_l))`. The logarithm of the determinant identity `det(1 - tA) = exp(-sum_{r >= 1} Tr(A^r)t^r/r)` then transforms the point-count zeta function into `product_i det(1 - t Frob | H^i)^((-1)^(i+1))`. Rationality is no longer mysterious: it follows from finite-dimensionality of the cohomology groups.

The functional equation also becomes geometric. Poincare duality pairs degree `i` and degree `2d-i` cohomology, and Frobenius respects that pairing up to the expected power of `q`. The symmetry of zeros and poles is therefore the shadow of duality, not a coincidence in the sequence of counts. Likewise, the predicted Betti numbers arise because the dimensions of the `l`-adic cohomology groups match topological Betti numbers when the variety comes by good reduction from characteristic zero.

The remaining hard part is the analogue of the Riemann hypothesis. After the trace formula, it is enough to prove that every Frobenius eigenvalue on `H^i` has absolute value `q^(i/2)` under any complex embedding. This is the spectral reformulation of cancellation in the point counts. Deligne's contribution was to prove the needed weight and purity statements, so that cohomological degree controls eigenvalue size. In that form, the estimate is not a bound on solutions one equation at a time; it is a theorem about the possible weights of Frobenius acting on cohomology.

This explains the uniqueness of the route. Direct counting sees only the left side, `N_r`. The cohomological framework constructs the right side, traces of powers of Frobenius. Deligne's weights then constrain the spectrum that produces those traces. The Weil conjectures are solved by passing through the bridge `fixed points -> cohomological traces -> Frobenius eigenvalues`: arithmetic becomes geometry, and geometry becomes a finite-dimensional spectral problem.
