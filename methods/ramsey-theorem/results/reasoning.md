I am trying to decide consistency of a first-order formula whose quantifiers are all universal — for all x, for all y, …, some Boolean combination of predicates holds. To decide whether such a thing can be satisfied over an infinite domain I keep colliding with the same sub-problem, and I should settle that sub-problem cleanly before I touch the logic at all. Here it is. There are finitely many predicate symbols of bounded arity, so any fixed tuple of, say, r individuals realizes one of finitely many truth-patterns — which atomic relations hold among those r individuals. A universal formula is satisfied exactly according to how these patterns sit across all the tuples. The clean thing to ask: if I assign to every r-element subset of an infinite domain one of finitely many "patterns," can I always retreat to an infinite sub-domain on which a single pattern recurs on *every* r-subset? On such a homogeneous sub-domain the universal formula collapses to one condition and I can read off whether it holds. So the real question, with all the logic peeled away, is purely about colourings.

Let me state it to myself bluntly. Take a set, finite or infinite. Look at all of its r-element subsets. Sort them, however I like, into μ bins. Must there be a large — for the infinite domain, infinite — sub-set all of whose r-element subsets land in one and the same bin? Call such a sub-set homogeneous. I want: yes, always.

Do I have any handle on this at all? The only regularity principle I really trust is the pigeonhole principle: drop k objects into μ boxes, and if k is bigger than μ·(h−1), some box holds at least h of them. Stare at that. What is it in my new language? It is exactly my question with r = 1. The "subsets" are single points; sorting points into μ bins; a bin with ≥ h points is a homogeneous h-set, because for r = 1 a "set all of whose 1-subsets are one colour" is just a set of points all coloured alike. So r = 1 is solved already, and trivially: μ(h−1)+1 points force a monochromatic h-set, and infinitely many points in μ bins force an infinite monochromatic set, since if every bin were finite the whole set would be finite. Good. That is the base of whatever I build — the homogeneity question is the pigeonhole principle, only for points.

So why is r = 2 — colouring pairs — not just as easy? Suppose I two-colour all the pairs of a huge set. Pigeonhole on the pairs themselves says lots of pairs are, say, red. But "lots of red pairs" is not what I want. I want a set of *points* every pair of which is red. A huge pile of red pairs can be spread thin — a red pair here, a red pair there — touching many points without any three of them forming a red triangle. Applying pigeonhole once, globally, to all the pairs at once tells me a count of pairs and says nothing about whether the points knit into a homogeneous block. That is the wall. Pigeonhole controls one global tally; homogeneity is a nested, internal-structure demand. I need to use pigeonhole, but locally and repeatedly, not once globally.

Let me try the infinite case first, because in the infinite I never run out of room, and I suspect the pure idea will show itself without any counting. Two colours, pairs, r = 2. I have an infinite set Γ. Pick any point a₁. It sits in infinitely many pairs {a₁, x}, each red or blue. Two colours, infinitely many pairs — by pigeonhole one colour, say red, is taken by infinitely many of them. So there is an infinite set B₁ ⊆ Γ \ {a₁} with every pair {a₁, x}, x ∈ B₁, red. I have thrown away the points that were blue to a₁ and kept an infinite reservoir all of which is red to a₁. Recurse inside B₁: pick a₂ ∈ B₁; it sits in infinitely many pairs within B₁; one colour c₂ is hit infinitely often; get infinite B₂ ⊆ B₁ \ {a₂} with every {a₂, x}, x ∈ B₂, of colour c₂. Continue forever — and here I need to be able to keep choosing, infinitely often, each time passing to an infinite sub-reservoir; that is a use of the axiom of selections, and I should mark it, because in the finite version I will make only finitely many choices and won't need it.

What do I have after this runs forever? A sequence a₁, a₂, a₃, … with each aₙ chosen inside Bₙ₋₁, and a colour cₙ such that {aₙ, aⱼ} is colour cₙ for every j > n — because all later a's live in Bₙ ⊆ … and Bₙ is all-cₙ to aₙ. So the colour of a pair {aᵢ, aⱼ} (i < j) is determined by the smaller index: it equals cᵢ. Now I have an infinite sequence of colours c₁, c₂, c₃, …, each red or blue — and pigeonhole strikes again, at the meta level: infinitely many of the cₙ are equal, say all cₙ for n in some infinite index set S are red. Then {aₙ : n ∈ S} is an infinite set every pair of which is red, since for any two aᵢ, aⱼ with i < j and i, j ∈ S the colour is cᵢ = red. There is my infinite homogeneous set.

That is the whole idea, and what makes it work is using pigeonhole three times in three roles: once to fix the local colour at each vertex, implicitly across the construction to reduce each pair's colour to its lower endpoint, and once at the end to homogenize the sequence of vertex-colours. The move is "fix a point, pass to the same-colour infinite neighbourhood, iterate." Now I want to lift this to general r and general μ, because the logic needs r-tuples and any finite number of patterns.

For general r and two colours, induction on r is the natural axis, with r = 1 (plain pigeonhole) as base. Suppose I can solve every colouring of (p−1)-subsets — given an infinite set and a 2-colouring of its (p−1)-subsets, I can find an infinite homogeneous sub-set. Now I am handed a 2-colouring of the p-subsets of Γ, colours C₁, C₂. A point a₁ now sits not in pairs but in many p-subsets, and "{a₁} together with p−1 others" is governed by the colour of that p-subset. The structure at a₁ is itself a colouring of the (p−1)-subsets of Γ \ {a₁}: colour a (p−1)-subset F by whichever Cᵢ the p-subset {a₁} ∪ F lands in. That is exactly the object my induction hypothesis on r eats. But I have to be careful which infinite set I extract and what I record, so let me try the direct greedy version first and watch whether it closes.

Greedy attempt: pick a₁ and an infinite Γ₁ ⊆ Γ \ {a₁} such that every p-subset {a₁} ∪ (p−1 of Γ₁) is C₁ — a₁'s "neighbourhood" monochromatically C₁. Then a₂ ∈ Γ₁ with infinite Γ₂ ⊆ Γ₁ \ {a₂}, all {a₂} ∪ (p−1 of Γ₂) in C₁; and so on. If this never fails I get a₁, a₂, … with aₙ ∈ Γₙ₋₁, Γₙ ⊆ Γₙ₋₁ \ {aₙ}, and every p-subset through aₙ drawn from later a's in C₁. The aₙ are distinct: for r > s, aᵣ ∈ Γᵣ₋₁ ⊆ … ⊆ Γₛ, and Γₛ excludes aₛ, so aᵣ ≠ aₛ. Take Δ = {a₁, a₂, …}. Any p-subset of Δ: let aₛ be its least-index element; the other p−1 elements lie in Γₛ; so the p-subset is {aₛ} ∪ (p−1 of Γₛ), which is C₁. So Δ is infinite and homogeneous in C₁.

But the greedy step might fail. The construction presupposes that at each stage I can find a point whose neighbourhood is purely C₁. What if at some stage no such point-plus-infinite-reservoir exists — Γₙ₋₁ has no member x with an infinite sub-reservoir all-C₁ through x? I cannot just stop; I must extract the answer from the failure. So failure is where the other colour lives. What does it say? Pick any point y₁ ∈ Γₙ₋₁ (or Γ itself if n = 1). Look at the (p−1)-subsets of Γₙ₋₁ \ {y₁}; colour each one C′₁ or C′₂ according as adjoining y₁ gives a p-subset in C₁ or C₂. This is a 2-colouring of (p−1)-subsets, so by the induction on r there is an infinite Δ₁ ⊆ Γₙ₋₁ \ {y₁} homogeneous for it: all (p−1)-subsets of Δ₁ are one C′, meaning all p-subsets {y₁} ∪ (p−1 of Δ₁) are one actual colour. Which colour? It cannot be C₁ — for if it were, then y₁ together with the infinite Δ₁ would be exactly the "point with infinite all-C₁ neighbourhood" whose nonexistence defined the failure at stage n. So all p-subsets {y₁} ∪ (p−1 of Δ₁) are C₂. Now I have a point y₁ whose neighbourhood within Δ₁ is all-C₂. Recurse the same argument inside Δ₁: pick y₂ ∈ Δ₁, 2-colour the (p−1)-subsets of Δ₁ \ {y₂} by whether y₂ adjoined lands in C₁ or C₂, the induction gives infinite Δ₂ ⊆ Δ₁ \ {y₂} on which all {y₂} ∪ (p−1 of Δ₂) are one colour — and again it can't be C₁, because y₂ ∈ Δ₁ ⊆ Γₙ₋₁ and an all-C₁ reservoir through y₂ would have served at the failed stage; so it is C₂. Iterate: y₁, y₂, … with Δ₁ ⊇ Δ₂ ⊇ …, every yₙ's neighbourhood within Δₙ all C₂. As before, the p-subset with least index yₛ has its rest in Δₛ, hence is C₂. So Δ = {y₁, y₂, …} is infinite and homogeneous in C₂. Either way I land on an infinite homogeneous set, so r = p is done for two colours, and the induction on r runs from the pigeonhole base all the way up.

Now μ colours instead of two. Induct on μ; μ = 1 is vacuous, μ = 2 done. Suppose I can handle ν−1 colours; given ν colours C₁, …, C_ν, merge the last two: define a (ν−1)-colouring by C′₁ = C₁, …, C′_{ν−2} = C_{ν−2}, and C′_{ν−1} = C_{ν−1} ∪ C_ν. By the (ν−1)-colour case, an infinite Δ is homogeneous in some C′ᵢ. If i ≤ ν−2 that is a genuine single colour and I am done. If i = ν−1, then on Δ all r-subsets lie in C_{ν−1} ∪ C_ν — a two-colouring of Δ's r-subsets — so apply the μ = 2 result inside Δ to split off an infinite homogeneous sub-set in C_{ν−1} or in C_ν. Done. So for any finite μ, any r, any infinite Γ, an infinite homogeneous set exists. That settles the infinite version, and it is exactly the regularity I needed for the universal-formula consistency question over an infinite domain.

So far so clean. But for the decision procedure I really want the *finite* statement — and not just "some finite size works," I want to know *how large* the starting set must be, because a bound is the thing a procedure can compute with. My instinct is to get the finite version cheaply from the infinite one. Here is the cheap route. Suppose the finite statement were false for some r, μ, target m: that is, for every n there is a colouring cₙ of the r-subsets of {1, …, n} with no homogeneous m-set. Glue these into a single bad colouring of all of ℕ's r-subsets, which would contradict the infinite theorem. There are only finitely many ways to colour the r-subsets of the fixed initial block {1, …, r} (namely μ raised to the count of such subsets), so infinitely many of the cₙ agree on that block; pass to that infinite family. Among those, only finitely many ways to colour the r-subsets of {1, …, r+1}, so infinitely many agree there too; nest down. Continuing, I get colourings dₙ of {1, …, n}, each extending the previous, each being some cₖ restricted (so each has no homogeneous m-set), and mutually consistent. Define c on all of ℕ by c(F) = dₙ(F) for any n ≥ max F; well-defined by consistency, and it has no homogeneous m-set because each dₙ does. That contradicts the infinite theorem. So the finite version holds.

Wait — what did this actually buy me? It is a compactness argument; essentially the statement that the space of colourings of ℕ's r-subsets is compact, the König-lemma packaging of "an infinite finitely-branching tree of finite counterexamples has an infinite branch." And it is purely existential. It proves a sufficient finite size exists but produces *no value* for it — nothing a procedure could compute. For the decision problem this is useless. I cannot bound the search for a satisfying interpretation by "there is some m₀." I need m₀ as an actual function of r, μ, n. So I throw out the elegant compactness deduction for the purposes of the bound and prove the finite statement directly, by a recursion that carries the size along — paying the bookkeeping cost the infinite proof let me skip.

Let me set up the finite statement I want, with a bound. For finite classes I write Γ_m for an m-element class, Δ_n for an n-element sub-class, and m₀ for the threshold. Target: given r, n, μ, find m₀ such that any μ-colouring of the r-subsets of any Γ_m with m ≥ m₀ has a homogeneous Δ_n.

Try the same "fix a vertex" recursion, but now finitely, two colours first. Fix a vertex x of Γ_m. Its incident r-subsets {x} ∪ (r−1 of the rest) induce a 2-colouring of the (r−1)-subsets of Γ_m \ {x}. If I knew the finite theorem for r−1, I could extract a homogeneous (r−1)-set in that induced colouring, i.e. a set whose (r−1)-subsets all give the same colour when x is adjoined. But there is a problem the infinite proof hid from me. After I extract that set and append x, I have spent the freedom to grow it further. In the infinite case I always had an infinite reservoir left to keep appending vertices; in the finite case, each append shrinks what remains, and I must guarantee enough is left to continue. A bare "there exists a homogeneous (r−1)-set" is not self-propagating: it gives me one good set but no leftover stock for the next vertex.

The way out is to strengthen the statement so the induction feeds itself. I should not ask merely for a homogeneous Δ_n; I should ask for a homogeneous Δ_n *together with a reservoir* Λ_k of extra vertices such that every r-subset straddling Δ_n and Λ_k — taking at least one vertex from Δ_n — is also the same colour. Then the reservoir is exactly the stock I draw the next vertex from. Make that the statement to prove — for r, n, k with n+k ≥ r: there is an m₀ such that any 2-colouring of the r-subsets of Γ_m, m ≥ m₀, yields disjoint sub-classes Δ_n and Λ_k such that all r-subsets of Δ_n ∪ Λ_k including at least one member of Δ_n lie in a single colour Cᵢ. This is stronger than "homogeneous n-set" but weaker than "homogeneous (n+k)-set" — a homogeneous (n+k)-set would have *all* its r-subsets one colour, including those wholly inside Λ_k, which this does not demand. So the asymmetric (n, k) form sits exactly between asking for an n-set and an (n+k)-set, and that slack is precisely what lets the induction propagate. (This is the trick I will keep needing: prove a stronger, self-feeding claim so the induction step has something to append to.)

Now prove the (n, k) statement by recursion. The honest way to record it is to *define a function* — call it f(r, n, k) — that I will show serves as m₀, by writing recursion formulae and proving they work. Two parameters shrink as the proof runs: appending a vertex turns r-subsets through it into (r−1)-subsets of the rest, dropping r; and building the homogeneous set one vertex at a time drops n. So I expect a recursion on r on the outside and on n inside fixed r, with an auxiliary function to manage the reservoir growth. Let me derive the formulae rather than guess them.

Base r = 1. A 1-subset is a point; a 2-colouring of points; I want Δ_n all one colour and a reservoir Λ_k whose 1-subsets meeting Δ_n — but a 1-subset is a single point, "meeting Δ_n" means it is in Δ_n — so the condition is just: Δ_n monochromatic. By pigeonhole, m points in 2 colours force a monochromatic n-set as soon as m ≥ 2n−1 (if both colours had ≤ n−1 we would have ≤ 2n−2 points). I also need to physically fit Δ_n and Λ_k, so m must be ≥ n+k. So f(1, n, k) = max(2n−1, n+k) works.

Inductive step on r, with r−1 assumed for all valid n, k. Inside r, induct on n.

Base n = 1 (within r). I want a single-point Δ₁ = {x} and a reservoir Λ_k such that every r-subset that is {x} together with r−1 members of Λ_k is one colour. Pick any x ∈ Γ_m. Throw it out; on the remaining points the (r−1)-subsets get a 2-colouring C′₁/C′₂ by whether adjoining x lands in C₁ or C₂. Apply the r−1 theorem — in its full (n, k) reservoir form — to these remaining points: I want from it a set whose (r−1)-subsets are monochromatic of total size k, so that every r-subset {x} ∪ (that (r−1)-subset) is one actual colour. To get an (r−1)-homogeneous set of size k out of the asymmetric form for r−1, I take it with parameters n = k−r+2 and k = r−2 — because then n + k = (k−r+2) + (r−2) = k, the core-plus-reservoir size is k, and an (r−1)-subset of the union meeting the core uses at least one of the k−r+2 core points and the rest from the r−2 reservoir, which is exactly "an (r−1)-subset of a k-set with at least one core point"; since the reservoir has only r−2 < r−1 members, too few to fill an (r−1)-subset alone, *every* (r−1)-subset of this k-set must use a core point and so is monochromatic. So I need the remaining points after removing x to number at least f(r−1, k−r+2, r−2), giving

f(r, 1, k) = f(r−1, k−r+2, r−2) + 1   (valid when k+1 > r so the parameters make sense).

The "+1" is the vertex x I peeled off. The asymmetric form is *designed* so that pushing the reservoir size into the "n" slot of the lower uniformity forces full homogeneity there — which is why I bothered to strengthen the statement.

Inductive step on n (within r), assuming the result for n−1 and all k. I want Δ_n and Λ_k. Idea: first secure a Δ_{n−1} with a *big* reservoir; then convert one vertex of that big reservoir into the n-th core point while keeping a reservoir of size k for the result. I need an auxiliary function to size "big." Define g(r, n, k) to be the reservoir size I must demand at the (n−1)-stage so that, after promoting a vertex via the n = 1 machinery, I still end with a reservoir of k. Concretely, to promote one reservoir vertex into a core point while preserving the cross-colour condition I apply the n = 1 result inside the current reservoir, which costs one f(r, 1, ·) of overhead. So set

g(r, 0, k) = max(r−1, k),
g(r, n, k) = f( r, 1, g(r, n−1, k) )   (n ≥ 1),
f(r, n, k) = f( r, n−1, g(r, n, k) )   (n > 1).

Let me verify these *do* prove the step, not just typecheck. Take m ≥ f(r, n, k) = f(r, n−1, g(r, n, k)). By the n−1 result with reservoir parameter g(r, n, k), Γ_m contains a Δ_{n−1} and a reservoir Λ of size g(r, n, k) such that every r-subset of Δ_{n−1} ∪ Λ meeting Δ_{n−1} is one colour, say C₁. Now turn this into Δ_n by mining inside Λ. Because g(r, n, k) = f(r, 1, g(r, n−1, k)), the reservoir Λ is large enough to run the n = 1 result: there is a vertex x₁ ∈ Λ and a sub-reservoir Λ′ ⊆ Λ of size g(r, n−1, k) such that every r-subset {x₁} ∪ (r−1 of Λ′) is one colour. If that colour is C₁, set Δ_n := Δ_{n−1} ∪ {x₁}: every r-subset of Δ_n ∪ Λ′ meeting Δ_n either meets Δ_{n−1} (handled, C₁) or is {x₁} together with r−1 from Λ′ (also C₁), and Λ′ has g(r, n−1, k) ≥ k members, so I keep a reservoir of size k. Done.

But x₁'s neighbourhood might come out the *other* colour. If every {x₁} ∪ (r−1 of Λ′) is C₂, then x₁ has an all-C₂ neighbourhood inside Λ′ — and this is exactly the place where, in the infinite proof, "the colour cannot be C₁" was the whole point, except now I cannot just discard x₁, I have to keep the C₁-progress on Δ_{n−1}. So I peel x₁ aside as the seed of a *C₂*-set and recurse inside Λ′: since |Λ′| = g(r, n−1, k) = f(r, 1, g(r, n−2, k)), apply the n = 1 result again to get x₂ ∈ Λ′ and Λ″ ⊆ Λ′ of size g(r, n−2, k) with all {x₂} ∪ (r−1 of Λ″) one colour. Again it is C₁ (giving me Δ_n = Δ_{n−1} ∪ {x₂} and reservoir Λ″, done) or C₂. The recursion g(r, n, k) = f(r, 1, g(r, n−1, k)) is exactly "I may have to spend one promotion that lands in the wrong colour, so demand enough reservoir to afford another." After at most n such peels the reservoir target g(r, 0, k) = max(r−1, k) ≥ k is still met, and I have collected vertices x₁, x₂, … each with all-C₂ neighbourhoods inside the surviving reservoir. If any one of them came out C₁ I was already done; if all of them came out C₂, then x₁, …, x_n together with the final reservoir of size ≥ k form a *C₂* core-plus-reservoir of the kind I wanted — every r-subset of {x₁, …, x_n} ∪ (final reservoir) meeting the x's is C₂, by the same least-index argument as in the infinite proof, since each xᵢ's r-subsets into the later reservoir are C₂. Either way Δ_n with a size-k reservoir exists, so f(r, n, k) = f(r, n−1, g(r, n, k)) is a valid threshold. The recursion closes.

That proves the asymmetric (n, k) statement for two colours with an explicit f. The plain finite homogeneity for two colours, target n, is the special case where I do not need a leftover reservoir tuned for further appends — I just want a homogeneous n-set, which I get from the (n, k) form by taking the core to absorb everything: m₀ = f(r, n−r+1, r−1) serves, because an r-subset of an (n−r+1)+(r−1) = n point set must meet the core (the reservoir has r−1 members, too few to fill an r-subset alone), so the whole n-set is homogeneous. Write h(r, n, 2) = f(r, n−r+1, r−1).

Now lift from two colours to μ colours, finitely, carrying the bound. Same merge trick as the infinite case, but I must compose thresholds. Set

h(r, n, 2) = f(r, n−r+1, r−1),
h(r, n, μ) = h( r, h(r, n, μ−1), 2 )   (μ > 2).

The reasoning: with μ colours, merge all but the first into one super-colour C′₂ = C₂ ∪ … ∪ C_μ, keep C′₁ = C₁. Apply the 2-colour threshold h(r, ·, 2): if m ≥ h(r, h(r, n, μ−1), 2) I get a homogeneous set of size h(r, n, μ−1) in either C′₁ = C₁ (done, and even bigger than n) or in C′₂, i.e. a set of size h(r, n, μ−1) all of whose r-subsets avoid C₁ and use only the other μ−1 colours. On that set apply the (μ−1)-colour theorem to extract a genuinely monochromatic n-set. So h(r, n, μ) = h(r, h(r, n, μ−1), 2) is a valid threshold, peeling one colour at a time. That completes the finite theorem with an explicit m₀ for all r, n, μ.

Let me sanity-check the magnitude in the smallest nontrivial uniform case, r = μ = 2, to see whether the bound is reasonable or wildly loose. Tracing the general recursion for r = 2 gives an m₀ that comes out as 2^{n(n−1)/2} — exponential in n², which is grotesquely large. Against the existence floor, where a typical 2-colouring on fewer than about 2^{n/2} points avoids a monochromatic n-set, the *true* threshold should be only single-exponential in n; my recursion is exponentially worse than that. So for *pairs* specifically I should be able to do far better with a direct local pigeonhole — and the gain matters because pairs, i.e. graphs, are the most-used case.

Direct pairs argument. I claim for r = 2 and k colours I can take m₀ = k·(n+1)! to force a homogeneous n-set. Induct on n. For n = 1 trivial. Suppose k·n! points force a homogeneous (n−1)-set with the cross-colour reservoir condition. Take m ≥ k·(n+1)! = k·(n+1)·n! points. By the induction, Γ_m contains a Δ_{n−1} and a reservoir Λ_{k(n+1)} such that all pairs from Δ_{n−1} ∪ Λ_{k(n+1)} meeting Δ_{n−1} are one colour, say C₁. Now look inside Λ_{k(n+1)}, which has k(n+1) members. Either some x in it has k other members of Λ_{k(n+1)} forming pairs with x all of one colour C₁ — then Δ_{n−1} ∪ {x} is the C₁-core and those k members are the new reservoir, done — or no member has k same-as-C₁ partners. In the latter case peel: take x₁ ∈ Λ_{k(n+1)}; it has at most k−1 partners giving C₁, so Λ_{k(n+1)} \ {x₁} contains a Λ_{kn} of members all giving C₂ with x₁ (k(n+1)−1−(k−1) = kn remain). Take x₂ ∈ Λ_{kn}; at most k−1 of its partners there are C₁, so a Λ_{k(n−1)} remains all giving C₂ with x₂. Continue: after n peels I have x₁, …, x_n each pairing in C₂ with all later ones, and a leftover reservoir of ≥ k. So {x₁, …, x_n} is a homogeneous C₂ n-set (with reservoir), and m₀ = k·(n+1)! suffices. This factorial bound is enormously smaller than the 2^{n(n−1)/2} the generic recursion produced for pairs — that quantity is "altogether excessive" for graphs — which is exactly why the pairs case deserves its own argument. (It is still loose: when a colour class has even degree everywhere one can shave the reservoir, e.g. start from a reservoir one smaller, since if k is even no odd-sized class can have every member with exactly k−1 same-colour partners — that would make twice the number of such pairs odd. The bound is far from the truth but it is the right order.)

For two colours specifically the sharp packaging is cleaner in graph language, and it exposes the iterated pigeonhole in its purest finite form. Define R(s, t) as the least number of vertices forcing, in any red/blue colouring of all pairs, a red clique of s vertices or a blue clique of t vertices — equivalently the least N so every graph on N vertices has a clique of size s or an independent set of size t. By swapping the two colours R(s, t) = R(t, s); and R(2, t) = t, because t vertices either contain one red pair (a red K₂) or have all pairs blue (a blue K_t), while t−1 all-blue vertices have neither. Now the key recursion, and it is the finite shadow of "fix a vertex, pass to the majority neighbourhood":

R(s, t) ≤ R(s−1, t) + R(s, t−1).

Proof. Put n = R(s−1, t) + R(s, t−1) vertices and fix a vertex v. The other n−1 split into v's red-neighbours and v's blue-neighbours. Since (R(s−1, t) − 1) + (R(s, t−1) − 1) + 1 = n − 1, by pigeonhole either v has at least R(s−1, t) red-neighbours or at least R(s, t−1) blue-neighbours — it cannot fall short of both, since falling short of both caps the total at n−2. Case red: among v's ≥ R(s−1, t) red-neighbours there is, by definition, a red K_{s−1} or a blue K_t; a blue K_t finishes; a red K_{s−1} together with v (all red to it) is a red K_s. Case blue: symmetric — among ≥ R(s, t−1) blue-neighbours there is a red K_s (finish) or a blue K_{t−1}, which with v is a blue K_t. Either way the colouring is forced, so n vertices suffice and R(s, t) ≤ n. That the numbers R(s, t) are finite at all follows by induction from this and the base cases.

And now the recursion plus pigeonhole on the indices gives a closed bound. Claim R(s, t) ≤ C(s+t−2, s−1). Base s = 1 or t = 1: R = 1 = C(t−1, 0) = C(s−1, 0). Inductive step:
R(s, t) ≤ R(s−1, t) + R(s, t−1) ≤ C(s+t−3, s−2) + C(s+t−3, s−1) = C(s+t−2, s−1),
the last equality being Pascal's identity. So a closed form, far better than the generic recursion's constant. The diagonal: R(s, s) ≤ C(2s−2, s−1) ≤ 4^{s}/√s, so in particular R(s, s) < 4^{s}; equivalently any 2-colouring of the pairs of an N-point set contains a monochromatic set of size at least roughly (1/2)·log₂ N. Exponential, matching the existence floor's shape, and the cleanest statement of the whole circle of ideas.

Let me also record that the general r-uniform finite theorem runs as the same iterated-pigeonhole recursion in graph-flavoured language, which is what the function f really is. For r-subsets and μ colours, fixing a vertex v and restricting attention to the (r−1)-subsets-completed-by-v induces an (r−1)-uniform μ-colouring on the rest; the (r−1)-uniform theorem hands back a large set on which that induced colouring is constant, and the colour of v's "type" against that set is one of μ values, so over enough vertices pigeonhole forces many vertices to share their type and their pairwise (r-wise) colour — exactly the Δ_n I built. The bound is a tower whose height is r, as the f-recursion shows; for r = 2 it collapses to the single exponential above.

Let me close the loop on where this started. I wanted, for deciding consistency of a universal first-order formula over an infinite domain, to retreat to an infinite sub-domain on which every r-tuple realizes a single truth-pattern. The infinite theorem — fix a point, pass to its monochromatic infinite neighbourhood, iterate, then pigeonhole the sequence of vertex-colours — gives exactly that sub-domain, with the axiom of selections justifying the infinitely many choices. For an actual decision procedure I needed it finitely *and* with a size bound; compactness gave the finite statement but no number, so I proved it directly by a recursion f(r, n, k) built on an asymmetric core-plus-reservoir strengthening that feeds its own induction, lifted to μ colours by merging colours pairwise, with threshold h(r, n, μ). The generic bound is a tower in r and grows like 2^{n(n−1)/2} for pairs; for the graph case the local pigeonhole gives a factorial and, sharpest of all, R(s, t) ≤ R(s−1, t) + R(s, t−1) ≤ C(s+t−2, s−1), a single exponential matching the existence floor. The single engine throughout is the pigeonhole principle applied not once globally but locally and repeatedly — the homogeneity theorem is the pigeonhole principle lifted from points to r-subsets.

Here is the complete result, stated cleanly.

Theorem (infinite). Let Γ be infinite, r and μ positive integers, and let all r-element subsets of Γ be partitioned into μ classes C₁, …, C_μ. Then Γ has an infinite sub-class Δ all of whose r-element subsets lie in a single C_i (using the axiom of choice).

Proof. Induct on r, base r = 1 being pigeonhole (infinitely many points in μ classes ⇒ one class infinite). For r > 1 do μ = 2 first by the construction above (fix x, pass to an infinite same-colour neighbourhood via the r−1 case, iterate; the failure case yields the other colour), then μ > 2 by merging two colours and applying the μ = 2 case inside the homogeneous set. ∎

Theorem (finite, with bound). For all r, n, μ there is m₀ = h(r, n, μ) such that any μ-colouring of the r-subsets of any m-set with m ≥ m₀ has a homogeneous n-set, where
  f(1, n, k) = max(2n−1, n+k),
  f(r, 1, k) = f(r−1, k−r+2, r−2) + 1,
  g(r, 0, k) = max(r−1, k),  g(r, n, k) = f(r, 1, g(r, n−1, k)),
  f(r, n, k) = f(r, n−1, g(r, n, k))  (n > 1),
  h(r, n, 2) = f(r, n−r+1, r−1),  h(r, n, μ) = h(r, h(r, n, μ−1), 2)  (μ > 2).

For the graph case r = 2 the threshold R(s, t) — forcing a monochromatic K_s of one colour or K_t of the other — satisfies R(2, t) = t, R(s, t) = R(t, s),
  R(s, t) ≤ R(s−1, t) + R(s, t−1) ≤ C(s+t−2, s−1),  R(s, s) ≤ 4^{s}/√s,
proved by fixing a vertex, passing to its majority-colour neighbourhood, recursing, and applying Pascal's identity. ∎
