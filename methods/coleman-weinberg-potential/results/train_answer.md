Take the electrodynamics of a massless charged scalar — a complex field $\phi$ with two real components $\phi_1,\phi_2$, minimally coupled to the photon, with a quartic self-coupling and no mass term anywhere. The Lagrangian carries two dimensionless couplings $e$ and $\lambda$ and not a single dimensionful constant, so classically it is exactly scale-invariant. The classical potential is $V_{\rm cl} = (\lambda/4!)(\phi_1^2+\phi_2^2)^2$, and for $\lambda>0$ its one and only stationary point is the origin, a minimum. The semiclassical verdict is therefore flat: symmetric vacuum, massless scalar, massless photon, nothing happens. This theory has had a bad name for years, with paradoxes coming out of every attempt to make sense of it, and I want to know whether that verdict is actually right.

What nags me is that the classical analysis answers "where is the vacuum?" by minimizing the non-derivative part of the Lagrangian with all quantum fluctuations switched off. But the fields fluctuate, those fluctuations carry zero-point energy, and that energy depends on where in field space I sit, because the masses of the fluctuations depend on the background. A background $\phi_c$ gives the scalar fluctuations an effective mass-squared of order $\lambda\phi_c^2/2$ and the photon a mass-squared $e^2\phi_c^2$ — minimal coupling does this automatically. So the vacuum's zero-point energy is a function of $\phi_c$, and the classical potential ignores all of it. Whether the true vacuum sits at the origin is not a classical question at all; it is a question about the *total* energy, fluctuations included, as a function of the background. The standard mechanisms do not help, because Goldstone and Higgs breaking are both *driven by a negative mass-squared $-\mu^2|\phi|^2$ written into the Lagrangian by hand*: the asymmetry is an input. The effective potential of Jona-Lasinio is the right tool, but it had only ever been pointed at theories whose tree potential already chooses an asymmetric vacuum, to dress an already-tipped Mexican hat. Nobody had asked whether the closed-loop graphs *alone*, with no asymmetric input whatsoever, can create the breaking. That is the question.

What I propose is to compute the **Coleman-Weinberg effective potential** of this theory at one loop and show that radiative corrections, by themselves, drive spontaneous symmetry breaking and generate a mass scale out of a scale-free Lagrangian. The right object is the effective potential $V(\phi_c)$, the non-derivative part of the effective action $\Gamma[\phi_c] = W[J] - \int J\,\phi_c$, the Legendre transform of Schwinger's connected generating functional defined by $e^{iW[J]} = \langle 0^+|0^-\rangle_J$ with $\phi_c = \delta W/\delta J$. Then $\delta\Gamma/\delta\phi_c = -J$, so at vanishing source a translation-invariant vacuum obeys $dV/d\phi_c = 0$, and spontaneous breaking means the minimum sits at $\phi_c = \langle\phi\rangle \neq 0$. This object has exactly the virtue I need: its $n$-th derivative is the sum of all one-particle-irreducible graphs with $n$ zero-momentum external legs, so at tree level $V = V_{\rm cl}$ and every quantum effect lives in the closed loops; and it surveys *all* candidate vacua at once, so I never have to commit in advance to expanding about a point that might be unstable.

The truncation has to be chosen with care, and the obvious choice — expand order by order in the coupling — is wrong here, because shifting the field $\phi \to \phi - \langle\phi\rangle$ changes the split of the Lagrangian into free and interacting parts, so a coupling expansion is sensitive to exactly the thing I want to vary impartially. The cure is the **loop expansion**. Put a bookkeeping parameter $a$ in front of the whole Lagrangian, $L \to a^{-1}L$, playing the role of $\hbar$. Each internal line carries one power of $a$ (the propagator inverts the quadratic operator), each vertex carries $a^{-1}$, so a graph with $I$ internal lines and $V$ vertices carries $a^{I-V}$; and since the loop number is $L = I - V + 1$, the graph carries $a^{L-1}$. The expansion in $a$ is the loop expansion, and loop order is purely topological — blind to field shifts and to how I split the Lagrangian. That is the property I needed, and the fact that $a$ is really $1$ does not matter, because I am using it for shift-invariance, not as a smallness parameter.

Do the self-interacting scalar first as the template: $L = \tfrac12(\partial\phi)^2 - (\lambda/4!)\phi^4$ plus counterterms $\tfrac12 A(\partial\phi)^2 - \tfrac12 B\phi^2 - (1/4!)C\phi^4$. Even though I call this "massless," I must keep a mass counterterm $B$, because nothing forbids a bare mass for a single real scalar; "massless" is a *condition I impose* — that the renormalized mass vanish — not a structural gift. At one loop the $n$-th derivative of $V$ is the sum of polygon graphs, a single closed loop with $n$ vertices, each vertex attaching two zero-momentum external legs (the vertex factor is $-i\lambda/2$, the $\tfrac12$ being a Bose factor) and an overall cyclic-plus-reflection combinatoric $1/2n$. The one-loop term, with tree and counterterms, is

$$V = \frac{\lambda}{4!}\phi_c^4 + \tfrac12 B\phi_c^2 - \frac{1}{4!}C\phi_c^4 + i\!\int\!\frac{d^4k}{(2\pi)^4}\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{2n}\!\left[\frac{\tfrac12\lambda\phi_c^2}{k^2+i\epsilon}\right]^{\!n}.$$

Term by term this is an infrared catastrophe — at $k\to 0$ the $n$-th term diverges like $k^{-2n}$, worse as $n$ grows — which is exactly why one cannot compute these corrections by a power series at zero field. But I do not compute term by term: with $x = \tfrac12\lambda\phi_c^2/(k^2+i\epsilon)$, the sum is $\sum x^n/2n = -\tfrac12\ln(1-x)$, and after a Wick rotation to Euclidean momentum the whole one-loop piece collapses to $\tfrac12\int (d^4k_E/(2\pi)^4)\,\ln(1+\lambda\phi_c^2/2k_E^2)$. The resummation cures the infrared: each *term* diverged at $k=0$, but the *sum* behaves only like a logarithm there, and as a function of $\phi_c$ it is a harmless integrable log singularity at $\phi_c=0$. With a cutoff $k^2=\Lambda^2$ the integral yields a quadratic divergence and a quartic-with-log piece,

$$V = \frac{\lambda}{4!}\phi_c^4 + \tfrac12 B\phi_c^2 - \frac{1}{4!}C\phi_c^4 + \frac{\lambda\Lambda^2\phi_c^2}{64\pi^2} + \frac{\lambda^2\phi_c^4}{256\pi^2}\!\left[\ln\frac{\lambda\phi_c^2}{2\Lambda^2} - \tfrac12\right].$$

Now the renormalization conditions. Demanding the renormalized mass vanish, $d^2V/d\phi_c^2|_0 = 0$, fixes $B$ and absorbs the $\Lambda^2$. The natural condition on the coupling, $d^4V/d\phi_c^4|_0 = \lambda$, *fails* — the $\phi_c^4\ln\phi_c^2$ term has a fourth derivative that runs to $-\infty$ at the origin — so I do the field-space analogue of off-shell subtraction in momentum space: introduce an arbitrary mass-dimensioned scale $M$ and impose $d^4V/d\phi_c^4|_{\phi_c=M} = \lambda$, with $Z(M)=1$. This fixes $C$, the cutoff drops out entirely as renormalizability requires, and

$$V = \frac{\lambda}{4!}\phi_c^4 + \frac{\lambda^2\phi_c^4}{256\pi^2}\!\left[\ln\frac{\phi_c^2}{M^2} - \frac{25}{6}\right].$$

Changing the scale $M\to M'$ is compensated by $\lambda' = \lambda + (3\lambda^2/32\pi^2)\ln(M'^2/M^2)$: a reparametrization, not a change of physics. But this single-coupling theory cannot break its symmetry. To get a stationary point off the origin I must balance the tree term, order $\lambda$, against the loop term, order $\lambda^2\ln(\phi_c/M)$, which requires $\ln(\phi_c/M)\sim 1/\lambda$, i.e. $\phi_c/M\sim e^{1/\lambda}$ — astronomically far out, exactly where the one-loop approximation collapses, since there $\lambda^2\ln$ is the same size as $\lambda$ and the two-loop $\lambda^3\ln^2$ is the same again. The would-be minimum sits outside the region I am allowed to trust; it is an artifact. The obstruction is sharp: the only thing the loop has to balance against the tree term is *itself one order higher in the same coupling*, and bridging one power of $\lambda$ costs one power of $1/\lambda$ in the log.

A *second, independent* coupling removes the obstruction, because two different small couplings can be comparable in magnitude with no large log between them. That is why scalar electrodynamics is the right theory. In Landau gauge the photon propagator is transverse, so when the internal photon momentum (equal to the internal scalar momentum at zero external momentum) hits the longitudinal seagull vertex, the $k_\mu k_\nu/k^2$ piece kills whole classes of graphs, and only three polygon families survive — $\phi_1$, $\phi_2$, and the photon in the loop, the photon carrying an extra factor of three for its polarizations (the trace of the transverse numerator in four dimensions). Along the background the radial fluctuation has mass-squared $\lambda\phi_c^2/2$ and the would-be Goldstone (angular) mode has $\lambda\phi_c^2/6$, while the photon has $e^2\phi_c^2$. The same geometric sum and the same renormalization at $M$ give

$$V = \frac{\lambda}{4!}\phi_c^4 + \left(\frac{5\lambda^2}{1152\pi^2} + \frac{3e^4}{64\pi^2}\right)\phi_c^4\!\left[\ln\frac{\phi_c^2}{M^2} - \frac{25}{6}\right],$$

where the scalar coefficient is $(1/64\pi^2)[(\lambda/2)^2+(\lambda/6)^2] = 5\lambda^2/1152\pi^2$ and the photon coefficient is $(3/64\pi^2)(e^2)^2$. Renormalizability forces $\lambda$ to be at least of order $e^4$ — the quartic is needed to cancel the divergence in scalar Coulomb scattering, an order-$e^4$ process — so $\lambda^2\sim e^8$, and the $5\lambda^2/1152\pi^2$ term is order $e^8$, negligible against the $e^4$ photon term and in fact obligatory to drop, since the two-loop electromagnetic corrections I have not computed are themselves $O(e^6,e^8)$. Dropping it,

$$V = \frac{\lambda}{4!}\phi_c^4 + \frac{3e^4}{64\pi^2}\phi_c^4\!\left[\ln\frac{\phi_c^2}{M^2} - \frac{25}{6}\right].$$

Now find the minimum. Setting $dV/d\phi_c=0$ away from the origin and using the freedom in $M$ — choose $M = \langle\phi\rangle$, the location of the minimum itself, so $\ln(\langle\phi\rangle^2/M^2)=0$ — the condition collapses to a pure algebraic relation,

$$\frac{\lambda}{6} - \frac{11e^4}{16\pi^2} = 0 \;\Longrightarrow\; \lambda = \frac{33\,e^4}{8\pi^2}.$$

It is self-consistent: with $M=\langle\phi\rangle$ the troublesome logarithm vanishes right at the vacuum, so I evaluate the one-loop result exactly where it is most reliable. The wall of the single-coupling theory is gone because I balanced two *different* couplings of comparable size, $\lambda\sim e^4$, rather than one coupling against its own square — no large log is forced on me. And something I did not ask for falls out: the minimum condition has *determined* $\lambda$ in terms of $e$. I started with two free dimensionless parameters and am left with one dimensionless parameter $e$ and one *dimensionful* one, $\langle\phi\rangle$, the vacuum value that now sets the scale of the theory. A dimensionless coupling has been traded for a mass scale, even though the original Lagrangian had no dimensionful constant at all. This is dimensional transmutation, the inevitable face of spontaneous breaking in a theory with no scale to begin with. Writing the final potential with $\lambda$ and $M=\langle\phi\rangle$ absorbed,

$$V = \frac{3e^4}{64\pi^2}\,\phi_c^4\!\left[\ln\frac{\phi_c^2}{\langle\phi\rangle^2} - \tfrac12\right].$$

The spectrum follows. The scalar mass is the curvature at the vacuum, $m^2(S) = V''(\langle\phi\rangle) = 3e^4\langle\phi\rangle^2/8\pi^2$; the would-be Goldstone boson is eaten by the photon, which gets the conventional Higgs mass $m^2(V) = e^2\langle\phi\rangle^2$; and the ratio is

$$\frac{m^2(S)}{m^2(V)} = \frac{3e^2}{8\pi^2} = \frac{3}{2\pi}\cdot\frac{e^2}{4\pi},$$

a small mass *ratio* — not a small mass difference — emerging naturally from a small coupling. The new vacuum lies strictly below the symmetric one, $V(\langle\phi\rangle) = -3e^4\langle\phi\rangle^4/128\pi^2 < 0$: the loops created the breaking, with nothing put in by hand and the Lagrangian massless and symmetric throughout.

Because I was burned once by a loop-induced minimum, I check which features I believe. Near $\langle\phi\rangle$ the log is small and one loop is reliable; near the origin the log is large and negative and one loop is not, so I improve with the renormalization group, which is exactly the statement that physics is independent of $M$. With $t=\ln(\phi_c/M)$ and the one-loop beta function $\bar\beta = 3\lambda^2/16\pi^2$ (and $\bar\gamma=0$ at this order), the running coupling $\lambda'(t) = \lambda/(1 - 3\lambda t/16\pi^2)$ stays small for all $t<0$, so the improved $V^{(4)} = \lambda'(t)$ is trustworthy down to the origin and confirms it is a genuine maximum, no hidden minimum; $V(0)=0$ is fixed exactly, since at zero field there is no scale a correction could be made of. For scalar electrodynamics the same analysis with $\bar\gamma = 3e^2/16\pi^2$ and $e'^2 = e^2/(1 - e^2 t/24\pi^2)$ shows the restriction $\lambda\sim O(e^4)$ was no restriction at all — breaking occurs for arbitrary small $e$ and $\lambda$.

That the one-loop surprises are structural, not accidents, follows from two all-orders arguments. Classifying graphs by vertex type gives $L = \tfrac12 V_3 + V_4 + 1$, so for fixed $L$ only finitely many prototype graphs (no type-2 vertices) exist; every other graph comes from inserting type-2 vertices, and summing those insertions is a geometric series that shifts every internal scalar propagator $i/(k^2+i\epsilon) \to i/(k^2 - \tfrac12\lambda\phi_c^2 + i\epsilon)$ (and $e^2\phi_c^2$ for photon lines), so the infrared divergence becomes a singularity only at $\phi_c=0$ to all orders. Rescaling loop momenta $k = \lambda^{1/2}k'$ then shows the $L$-loop contribution is $\propto \phi_c^4\,f(\phi_c/M)\,\lambda^{L+1}$ — a pure power with no $\ln\lambda$ to any order, legitimate precisely because the coupling was subtracted at a field-space point and not a fixed momentum; for scalar QED it is $\propto (e^2)^{L+1}$. Finally, taking a mass $\mu\to 0$ reproduces the massless $V$ smoothly, while for *negative* $\mu^2$ the potential develops an imaginary part $\mathrm{Im}\,V = -(1/64\pi^2)[(\mu^2+\tfrac12\lambda\phi_c^2)^2\,\theta(-\mu^2-\tfrac12\lambda\phi_c^2) - \mu^4]$, uncancellable by the real counterterms — the field-theory analogue of the Euler-Heisenberg/Schwinger imaginary part for a constant electric field, signalling that the symmetric configuration is kinematically unstable; it vanishes at the real asymmetric vacuum, and the renormalization condition there becomes $\mathrm{Re}\,V''|_0 = \mu^2$.

The whole construction generalizes, because the one-loop polygon sum is structurally the trace-log of the field-dependent inverse propagator — the functional determinant of the operator governing fluctuations about $\phi_c$. For any massless renormalizable gauge theory, in Landau gauge,

$$V_1(\phi_c) = \frac{1}{64\pi^2}\left[\,\mathrm{Tr}\,W^2(\phi_c)\ln W(\phi_c) \;+\; 3\,\mathrm{Tr}\,M^4(\phi_c)\ln M^2(\phi_c) \;-\; \mathrm{Tr}\,(mm^\dagger)^2(\phi_c)\ln(mm^\dagger)(\phi_c)\,\right],$$

with $W = \partial^2 V_0/\partial\phi_a\partial\phi_b$ the scalar mass-squared matrix, $M^2_{ab} = g_a g_b (T_a\phi, T_b\phi)$ the vector mass-squared matrix from the gauge generators, and $mm^\dagger$ the fermion mass-squared from the Yukawa matrix. Each field contributes $\pm(2J+1)/64\pi^2$ times $(\text{mass}^2)^2\ln(\text{mass}^2)$, plus for bosons and minus for fermions, the $3$ being the Landau-gauge vector polarization count; massless scalar QED is the special case $W=\{\lambda\phi_c^2/2,\,\lambda\phi_c^2/6\}$, $M^2 = e^2\phi_c^2$. Applied to an $SU(2)$ gauge triplet with a scalar isovector it again breaks radiatively, leaving an unbroken $U(1)$ photon, with $m^2(S)/m^2(V) = (3/\pi)(e^2/4\pi)$ and $\lambda = 33e^4/4\pi^2$; applied to the $SU(2)\times U(1)$ lepton model it predicts the scalar mass from the vector masses, $m^2(S) = (3/32\pi^2)[2g^2 m^2(W) + (g^2+g'^2)m^2(Z)]$. A massless, scale-free Lagrangian breaks its own symmetry, by quantum mechanics alone.

```python
import sympy as sp
phi, M, lam, e, vev = sp.symbols('phi M lambda e v', positive=True)
pi = sp.pi

# Massless scalar electrodynamics, one loop (scalar-loop term dropped at order e^8):
V = lam/sp.factorial(4)*phi**4 + 3*e**4/(64*pi**2)*phi**4*(sp.log(phi**2/M**2) - sp.Rational(25,6))

# Minimum with M = <phi>: the log vanishes, giving lambda(e):
lam_min = sp.solve(sp.Eq(lam/6 - 11*e**4/(16*pi**2), 0), lam)[0]
assert lam_min == 33*e**4/(8*pi**2)

# Final potential parametrized by e and <phi>:
V_final = sp.simplify(V.subs({lam: lam_min, M: vev}))
assert sp.simplify(V_final - 3*e**4/(64*pi**2)*phi**4*(sp.log(phi**2/vev**2) - sp.Rational(1,2))) == 0

# Spectrum at the broken vacuum:
mS2   = sp.simplify(sp.diff(V_final, phi, 2).subs(phi, vev))   # 3 e^4 v^2 / (8 pi^2)
mV2   = e**2*vev**2                                            # e^2 v^2
ratio = sp.simplify(mS2/mV2)                                   # 3 e^2 / (8 pi^2)
depth = sp.simplify(V_final.subs(phi, vev))                   # -3 e^4 v^4 / (128 pi^2)
assert ratio == 3*e**2/(8*pi**2)               # = (3/2pi)(e^2/4pi)
assert depth == -3*e**4*vev**4/(128*pi**2)

# Cross-check coefficients against the master formula (1/64pi^2) Sigma (mass^2)^2:
assert sp.simplify(sp.Rational(1,64)/pi**2*((lam*phi**2/2)**2 + (lam*phi**2/6)**2)
                   - 5*lam**2/(1152*pi**2)*phi**4) == 0     # scalar loops -> 5 lam^2/1152pi^2
assert sp.simplify(3*sp.Rational(1,64)/pi**2*(e**2*phi**2)**2
                   - 3*e**4/(64*pi**2)*phi**4) == 0          # photon loop -> 3 e^4/64pi^2
```
