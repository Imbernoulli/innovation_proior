I start from the fact that the reward is the wrong thing to trust during exploration. If I optimize one reward while collecting data, I learn exactly enough about the transition system to support policies that matter for that reward. That is fine until the reward changes. Then the places I ignored can suddenly become the only places that matter, and the old interaction budget no longer buys me correctness.

So I do not ask first what reward to optimize. I ask what a dataset must certify before any reward appears. The value-difference lemma gives me the shape of the certificate. If I plan in an empirical model `Phat` but evaluate in the true model `P`, then for a fixed policy the error telescopes into a sum of terms like `(Phat_h - P_h) Vhat_{h+1}` weighted by where that policy goes. A bad transition estimate is harmless if no later policy uses it, but a reward chosen after seeing my data can make a policy route through precisely the weak spot. So whatever the dataset is, it has to control transition error along the occupancy of every policy that might later be optimal for some reward.

This is already different from setting the reward to zero. Zero reward gives me no adversary and no value function that exposes what the planner will later need. Optimism with an artificial unknown-state payoff can force visits to unknown regions, but it is still a coarse known-set objective. The later reward does not care whether I have crossed a knownness threshold; it cares whether the transition error, multiplied by the future value function and the later policy occupancy, stays small. So the quantity I have to make small is a coverage-weighted transition error, and I should design the data distribution against that quantity directly rather than against any reward proxy.

Now I hit something that looks like an impossibility, and I want to see whether it actually is one. The natural first goal is uniform coverage: visit each `(s,a)` enough times that its transition estimate is accurate. But in an MDP I cannot do that. Some states sit behind branches with probability `10^-6`; some are unreachable. If I demand a fixed absolute number of samples for each state-action pair, the goal is infeasible for reasons that have nothing to do with planning. Before I abandon the whole "cover everything" framing, I should check how much a barely-reachable state can actually hurt me, because if it cannot hurt me much then I am allowed to ignore it.

So let me quantify it. Suppose a state `(s,h)` has maximum reachability below some threshold: `max_pi P_h^pi(s) < delta`. The value at step `h` lives in `[0,H]`. Whatever later reward and later policy I face, that state's contribution to the value is (probability of being there) times (value there), which is at most `delta * H`. There are `S` states at each step, so one step contributes at most `S delta H`, and over `H` steps the whole insignificant tail contributes at most `H * S delta H = H^2 S delta`. That is a finite, controllable number, not an obstruction. The "impossibility" was an artifact of asking for uniform absolute coverage; once I weight by what a state can do, the unreachable tail collapses into the error budget.

That tells me the right notion is coverage relative to reachability. I should demand strong coverage only for states whose maximum reachability is at least `delta`, and pick `delta` so the ignored tail `H^2 S delta` fits inside the target accuracy. If I want the tail to cost at most `epsilon/2`, I set `delta = epsilon/(2 S H^2)` and check: `H^2 S delta = H^2 S * epsilon/(2 S H^2) = epsilon/2`. Exactly half the budget, as intended; the `S` and `H^2` cancel the way I chose them to. For the states above the threshold, the data should visit `(s,h)` in proportion to its best reaching probability `M = max_pi P_h^pi(s)`, not with a fixed absolute count: a state with `M=1` deserves many samples, a state with `M=delta` deserves just enough that its possible value contribution is safe.

So now I need to actually produce a distribution with that reachability-proportional property, and I have to do it without knowing the reward. Here the trick is that "reach a fixed cell" is itself a clean RL problem if I invent the right measuring reward. Fix a target `(s,h)` and set a synthetic reward equal to one at state `s` at step `h` and zero everywhere else. Under this reward, the value of any policy is its probability of being at `s` at step `h`, i.e. `P_h^pi(s)`, and the optimal value is `max_pi P_h^pi(s) = M`. This reward is not a downstream task; it is an instrument for measuring and reaching one cell of the transition geometry. If I solve this RL problem well, I get policies that are good at reaching that cell.

Do this for every `(s,h)`, collect the resulting policies into per-target multisets, mix them all, and sample trajectories from the mixture. One adjustment is needed at the target action. Reaching `(s,h)` is determined by everything before the action at step `h`, so the distribution of actions taken *at* `s` at step `h` is free: I can overwrite it with `Uniform(A)` without lowering the reaching probability at all. That converts state coverage into state-action coverage.

A plain regret oracle would be wasteful here, because some important targets are only barely above the threshold, and if I pay the full worst-case exploration cost for every target the construction becomes too expensive. What saves it is that the synthetic reward has a tiny total: along any trajectory it is at most one, and the optimal value is exactly `M`. A problem-dependent (variance-aware) regret bound like EULER's then makes faint targets cheap. For this reward EULER gives, for the learned multiset `Phi^(s,h)` of size `N_0`,

`M - (1/N_0) sum_{pi in Phi^(s,h)} P_h^pi(s) <= c sqrt(S A H iota_0 M / N_0) + S^2 A H^4 iota_0^3 / N_0`.

I want the average reaching probability to be at least `M/2`, i.e. the right side at most `M/2`. The worst case is the smallest admissible `M`, which is `M = delta`. Plug `N_0 = O(S^2 A H^4 iota_0^3 / delta)` and `M = delta` into each term. The second term is `S^2 A H^4 iota_0^3 / N_0 = delta`, so with the constant in `N_0` taken large enough it sits at `M/4`. The first term is `sqrt(S A H iota_0 * delta / N_0)`; substituting `N_0` makes the argument `S A H iota_0 delta^2 / (S^2 A H^4 iota_0^3) = delta^2 / (S H^3 iota_0^2)`, whose square root is `delta / (sqrt(S) H^{3/2} iota_0)`, which is far below `delta/4`. So both terms together stay under `M/2` for every target above the threshold, and larger `M` only makes the inequality easier. Good — the cheap-faint-target property is what makes the per-target cost scale with `delta`, not with a fixed worst case.

Now I want the coverage ratio I actually get out of this mixture, and I will track the factors rather than guess them. Fix a significant `(s,h)`. The multiset `Phi^(s,h)` reaches `s` at step `h` with average probability at least `M/2`, so within its own block the data puts at least `M/2` mass on `(s,h)`. The uniform-action override spreads that over `A` actions, giving at least `(M/2)/A` on each `(s,a,h)` within the block. I mix the `S H` target blocks uniformly, so the global data distribution has `mu_h(s,a) >= (1/(S H)) * (M/2) / A`. The numerator I am dividing into is `max_{pi,a} P_h^pi(s,a)`; reaching `s` and then taking any action has probability at most the reaching probability, which is at most `M`. So the ratio is at most

`M / [ (1/(S H)) (M/2) (1/A) ] = M * 2 S H A / M = 2 S A H`.

The `M` cancels — that is the whole point, and it is worth pausing on: because both the numerator and the data mass scale with the same reaching probability, the ratio is bounded by a reward-free, `M`-free quantity. The remaining factors are exactly `2` (from reaching only `M/2`), `A` (from spreading the target action uniformly), and `S H` (from mixing all target blocks). So

`max_{pi,a} P_h^pi(s,a) / mu_h(s,a) <= 2 S A H`

for every target above the reachability threshold.

I have a coverage ratio; I have not yet shown it is enough for *all* later rewards. A reward arrives, I form `Phat` from the logged trajectories, and I solve the empirical MDP. To transfer the empirical solution back to the true MDP I need a uniform policy-evaluation guarantee: for every policy and every reward, `Vhat^pi` and `V^pi` are close from the initial distribution. So I go back to the value-difference lemma and split its per-step occupancy sum into insignificant states (max reachability below `delta`) and significant states.

The insignificant part I already bounded: `H^2 S delta`, and with `delta = epsilon/(2 S H^2)` that is `epsilon/2`. For the significant states I apply Cauchy-Schwarz to the value-difference term and then reduce the current randomized action to a deterministic selector. That reduction is legitimate because `Vhat_{h+1}^pi` depends on the future policy, not on which action is randomly taken at step `h`: for a significant state and any fixed action `a`, I can splice a policy that reaches the same state and then deterministically plays `a`, so the coverage ratio `2 S A H` replaces the target occupancy by `mu`. The statistical object that survives is

`E_mu |(Phat_h - P_h) G(s,a)|^2 1[a=nu(s)]`

required to be small uniformly over value functions `G in [0,H]^S` and deterministic selectors `nu : S -> A`. The uniformity is forced on me because the reward, and hence both `G` and `nu`, are chosen after the data.

For the concentration I use a self-bounding comparison. For each sample, compare the empirical squared prediction error to the true one. The empirical transition estimate is, per cell, the minimizer of the empirical squared loss, so the empirical sum of the comparison variables is nonpositive. The expectation of the comparison variable is exactly the squared transition-estimation quantity I want, and a short calculation bounds its variance by `4 H^2` times its mean. Bernstein with a nonpositive empirical sum then yields a fast self-bounded rate after a covering argument. The covering is where the deterministic-selector restriction pays off: I do not net every `Q(s,a)` table; for a fixed selector only one action per state is active, so the cover costs `A^S (H/eps)^{2S}`, with `log` of order `S log(A H N / p)`. That gives

`E_mu |(Phat_h - P_h) G(s,a)|^2 1[a=nu(s)] <= O(H^2 S log(A H N / p) / N)`.

Now I combine the two pieces and check the exponents come out where they should. The per-step value-difference contribution is, up to constants, `sqrt(coverage ratio) * sqrt(self-bounded rate)`:

`sqrt(2 S A H) * sqrt(H^2 S iota / N) = sqrt(2 H^3 S^2 A iota / N)`,

i.e. order `sqrt(H^3 S^2 A iota / N)` per step. Summing over `H` steps multiplies by `H`:

`H * sqrt(H^3 S^2 A iota / N) = sqrt(H^5 S^2 A iota / N)`.

Setting this to the remaining budget `epsilon/2` and solving for `N` gives `sqrt(H^5 S^2 A iota / N) = epsilon/2`, hence `N = 4 H^5 S^2 A iota / epsilon^2`, i.e.

`N = O(H^5 S^2 A iota / epsilon^2)`.

The covered part is then at most `epsilon/2`, the insignificant tail is at most `epsilon/2`, so the full evaluation error is at most `epsilon`. The `H^5 S^2` in the leading term is not an accident of bookkeeping: one `S` is the usual fixed-reward state factor inside the self-bounded rate, the second `S` comes from the `S H` block-mixing in the coverage ratio, and the extra powers of `H` come from the horizon-length telescoping plus the per-step value range.

I should also sanity-check the total episode budget, since the exploration phase runs one EULER instance per target. The planning-phase data costs `N` episodes. The exploration phase runs `S H` targets at `N_0` each, costing `S H N_0 = S H * S^2 A H^4 iota_0^3 / delta`. With `delta = epsilon/(2 S H^2)` that is `S H * S^2 A H^4 iota_0^3 * 2 S H^2 / epsilon = 2 S^4 A H^7 iota_0^3 / epsilon`. So

`K = N + S H N_0 = O( H^5 S^2 A iota / epsilon^2 + S^4 A H^7 iota^3 / epsilon )`,

whose leading term in `1/epsilon` is `Otilde(H^5 S^2 A / epsilon^2)`. The two pieces I derived independently land exactly on the two terms of `K`, which is the consistency check I wanted.

Once uniform evaluation holds, the planning theorem is mechanical. Let `pi*` be optimal in the true MDP and `pihat*` optimal in the empirical MDP. The true suboptimality of the returned empirical policy decomposes into: evaluation error for `pi*` (at most `epsilon`), the empirical optimality gap `Vhat^{pihat*} - Vhat^{pi*} >= 0` (nonpositive contribution), the solver's own empirical optimization error (at most `epsilon`), and evaluation error for the returned policy (at most `epsilon`). Summing, the returned policy is `3 epsilon`-suboptimal in the true MDP. Because the evaluation event is uniform over policies and rewards, the same dataset handles any number of adaptively chosen later rewards.

The last thing I genuinely do not know yet is whether the extra `S` factor over fixed-reward RL is real or an artifact of my analysis. So I look for a matching lower bound. Take a one-state instance where every action has an unknown, near-uniform distribution over `2n` absorbing leaves. If I must be near-optimal for *every* leaf reward vector, the returned action has to identify which transition distribution has the best inner product with each reward direction — the rewards act as separating hyperplanes. A packing of nearly uncorrelated `+1/-1` perturbations plus Fano's inequality forces `Omega(n A / epsilon^2)` samples to decode the transition index per action. That is only one factor of `S` (here `n`). To get the second, embed `n` such instances at the leaves of a binary tree: a reward can first drive the policy to a chosen internal copy and then use that copy's terminal rewards to demand the correct action inside it. Since the data-collection phase has to support *whichever* copy a later reward selects, the learner pays the one-state cost `n` times, and persistent terminal reward contributes the `H^2` scaling. The result is `Omega(S^2 A H^2 / epsilon^2)`. So the extra `S` is a real price of reward-agnostic coverage, not slack in the upper bound.

Stepping back, what the derivation actually produced is a clean separation: the first phase never optimizes an absent task, it estimates the transition geometry precisely where any later task could exploit it, using synthetic indicator reward as a reachability instrument rather than a commitment. The confidence comes from two computed facts that fit together — a coverage ratio `2 S A H` that is independent of the reward, and a uniform self-bounded transition-evaluation bound — and the matching lower bound says the resulting state dependence cannot be improved.
