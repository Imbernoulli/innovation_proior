Let me start from what I can actually see when I look at a scatter of spatial points and immediately, effortlessly, pick out the groups. I am not measuring anything yet — I am just staring at a plot of an earth-observation database, a few patches of points plus some scattered junk, and asking myself: what is my eye doing that an algorithm isn't? The answer is uncomfortably simple. The patches are *dense* — inside a group the points crowd together — and between the patches, and out where the stray outliers sit, it's *sparse*. The density inside any group is conspicuously higher than the density just outside it, and the density out in the noise is lower than the density anywhere inside a group. That's the entire signal. My eye is doing density thresholding. So whatever I build should formalize exactly that and nothing fancier: a cluster is a connected region where the points are packed tightly, and the boundary of a cluster is where the packing thins out.

And I should be clear about the demands this has to satisfy at once, because that's where every method I know trips. I'm exploring a database I've never seen, so I do *not* know how many groups there are — I can't be made to type in `k`. The groups in spatial data are genuinely arbitrary shapes — rivers run in lines, settlements sprawl, geology draws sinuous bands — so a method that only returns round blobs is wrong on the data, not just imprecise. The database is large, far more than a few thousand points, so anything quadratic in the number of points is already too slow. And real spatial data has noise — outliers that belong to nothing — so I need to be able to *leave points out*, not force every point into some group. Four things together. Let me walk the tools I have and see precisely where each one breaks against this list, because the breakages are going to tell me what to build.

The obvious workhorse is partitioning: k-means, or k-medoid. Pick `k` representatives, send every point to its nearest one, and minimize the within-group cost `E = Σ_j Σ_{p∈C_j} dist(p, rep_j)`. In k-means the representative is the group's gravity center; in k-medoid (PAM, and Kaufman & Rousseeuw's machinery) it's an actual data object, a medoid. Iterate: assign to nearest rep, recompute reps, repeat. Now look hard at what that assignment rule *does to geometry*. "Each point goes to its single nearest representative" is the definition of a Voronoi diagram — the representatives carve space into Voronoi cells and each cluster lives inside one cell. Voronoi cells are convex. So *every cluster a partitioning method can produce is convex*, full stop. A curved band of points, or one group wrapped around another, simply cannot be a Voronoi cell; the method will slice it across several cells and call the pieces different clusters. That kills demand (2) outright. And it fails (1) — I had to know `k` — and it fails the noise requirement — every point is forced into a cluster, and a single far outlier yanks its representative toward itself, distorting the whole group. Three of my four demands broken by one assignment rule. The convexity is the deep one: it's baked into "nearest representative," and no amount of cleverness in *choosing* the representatives escapes it.

CLARANS, Ng & Han's method, is the state of the art for pushing k-medoid to bigger databases, so let me check whether it rescues anything. Its trick is purely in the *search*: think of a clustering as a node in a graph whose nodes are sets of `k` medoids, with an edge between two node-sets that differ in exactly one medoid; then do randomized hill-climbing — from the current set of medoids, sample a bounded number of single-medoid swaps, jump to one that lowers `E`, and restart a few times to dodge bad local optima. Cleverer than PAM grinding through every swap, and genuinely faster. They even address "what's the natural `k`": run CLARANS for each `k` from 2 to `n` and keep whichever clustering scores highest on the silhouette coefficient. But none of this touches my problem. It's still partitioning — still "nearest medoid," still convex Voronoi cells — so arbitrary shape is still impossible. The silhouette sweep over `k` means running an already roughly-quadratic clustering once for *every* candidate `k`, which on a large database is hopeless on efficiency. It assumes the whole database sits in main memory. And there's still no noise — every object is glued to its closest medoid. So CLARANS sharpens the wrong axis: it optimizes the search inside a model that can't represent what I need.

What about giving up on `k` entirely — hierarchical methods? Agglomerative clustering merges the two closest groups over and over, divisive splits; either way you get a dendrogram and you don't have to name `k` in advance. Good, that clears demand (1). But now I have to supply a *termination condition* — when do I stop merging? Say a threshold `D_min` on inter-cluster distance below which two groups count as one. And this is genuinely hard to set: it has to be small enough to keep two truly-separate groups apart, yet large enough not to chop a single loose group into fragments — and if my groups differ in tightness, no single `D_min` does both. There's a sharper variant, Ejcluster (García et al.), that replaces the termination threshold with a connectivity idea: two points are in the same group if you can *walk* from one to the other in sufficiently small steps. I like that a lot — "walk in small steps" is a shape-agnostic notion, it handles non-convex groups, and it derives its own stopping point. But the cost is `O(n^2)`, because deciding who can walk to whom inspects pairs of points; fine for character recognition on small sets, dead on a large spatial database. So the connectivity idea is *right in spirit* and *wrong in cost*.

And grid-histogram methods, Jain's line: chop the space into cells, build a multidimensional histogram of cell occupancy, call high-count cells the cluster cores and put boundaries in the histogram "valleys." This actually reads density off the data and can find arbitrary shapes — it's the closest in spirit to what my eye does. But storing and searching a multidimensional histogram blows up in space and time with dimension, and the whole thing hinges on the cell size: too coarse and groups merge, too fine and they fragment, and that size is just another parameter I have to guess.

So the landscape is: partitioning is efficient and parameter-light but convex-only and noise-blind; the connectivity/hierarchical idea handles shape but costs `O(n^2)` or needs an unsettable threshold; grid-histograms handle shape but are heavy and cell-size-sensitive. The thing I keep circling is that *density* is the real signal — my eye proved that in the first paragraph — and the Ejcluster "walk in small steps" connectivity is the right way to get arbitrary shape. I want both, made cheap by leaning on the spatial index the database already maintains, the R\*-tree, which answers "give me every object inside this small region" by descending only a few of its bounding-rectangle subtrees. If I can phrase all my work as a stream of small region queries, the index does the heavy lifting and I never pay `O(n^2)`.

OK. Density. Let me try to write it down. The crudest measure of "how dense is it around point `p`" is: how many other points sit within some radius. So fix a radius `Eps` and count. Define the `Eps`-neighborhood of `p` as every database point within `Eps` of it:

  N_Eps(p) = { q ∈ D | dist(p, q) ≤ Eps }.

Notice this is metric-agnostic — with Euclidean `dist` the neighborhood is a ball, with Manhattan it's a diamond — so by writing it through `dist` I inherit whatever metric the domain hands me, which is exactly the generality I wanted, and exactly what a region query returns. The local density at `p` is then just `|N_Eps(p)|`, the count.

Now the first instinct: declare that a cluster is a region where every point has high density — every point `p` in a cluster has `|N_Eps(p)| ≥ MinPts` for some threshold `MinPts`. Let me test that against a picture and watch it break. Take any solid blob. The points deep in the interior have lots of neighbors within `Eps`, sure. But the points right on the *rim* of the blob — they're genuinely part of the cluster, my eye says so — have a neighborhood that's half-empty, because half of their `Eps`-ball pokes out into the void. So a border point of a real cluster has *significantly fewer* `Eps`-neighbors than an interior point. If I set `MinPts` high enough to be a meaningful "this is dense," I exclude all the border points and my clusters come out gnawed-down to their cores. If I lower `MinPts` until the border points qualify, the threshold is now so low that it also admits noise. There is no single count threshold that includes the borders and excludes the noise, because *border density is not characteristic of the cluster* — it's an artifact of being at the edge. Wall.

The escape is to stop demanding high density of *every* cluster point and instead demand it of every cluster point's *vicinity*. A border point is allowed to be sparse itself, as long as it sits in the neighborhood of some genuinely-dense point that vouches for it. So I split the cluster's points into two kinds. Call a point *core* if it clears the bar itself, `|N_Eps(p)| ≥ MinPts` — these are the interior, the dense backbone. A point that doesn't clear the bar can still belong, provided it lies within `Eps` of a core point — these are the *border* points, riding on a core neighbor's density. And I'll make this precise as a one-directional relation, because the directionality is going to matter: `p` is *directly density-reachable* from `q` if

  (1) p ∈ N_Eps(q),   and   (2) |N_Eps(q)| ≥ MinPts   (q is core).

Read it as "`q` is dense and `p` is one of `q`'s neighbors, so `q` pulls `p` in." Stare at the symmetry. If both `p` and `q` are core, the relation goes both ways — `p ∈ N_Eps(q)` iff `q ∈ N_Eps(p)` since `dist` is symmetric, and both satisfy the core condition. But if `q` is core and `p` is a border point, then `p` is directly density-reachable from `q` (q is dense, p is its neighbor) while `q` is *not* directly density-reachable from `p` (p fails the core condition, so it can't vouch for anyone). So this relation is asymmetric precisely at the core/border interface, and that asymmetry is not a bug — it's the formal statement that density flows outward from the dense interior to the sparse rim, not the other way.

One step of "directly density-reachable" only reaches one ring out from a core point. To get a whole arbitrary-shaped region I need to chain it — exactly the Ejcluster "walk in small steps" intuition, now made concrete as a walk through core points. So `p` is *density-reachable* from `q` if there's a chain `p_1 = q, p_2, …, p_n = p` with `n ≥ 2` and each `p_{i+1}` directly density-reachable from `p_i`. This is the transitive extension of the one-step relation; a core point reaches itself because it directly reaches itself through the zero-distance self-neighbor, while a non-core point cannot even make that first step. Walk core-to-core-to-core through the dense backbone of the region, and you can thread your way along a river or around a curve — no convexity assumed anywhere, because at no point did I ask the region to be a ball or a Voronoi cell; I only ever asked the next point to be a dense neighbor of the current one. Arbitrary shape, for free, out of "follow the density."

Is density-reachability transitive? Yes, by construction — concatenate two chains and you get a chain. Is it symmetric? No, and I should see exactly why and where, because it's the same border-point issue one level up. Along the chain every point with an outgoing step, including the start and all intermediates, must be core; only the final endpoint may be a border point. So if `q` is core and `p` is a border point on the rim, `p` is density-reachable from `q` (chain of cores ending by pulling in the border point `p`) but `q` is generally not density-reachable from `p` (a border `p` can't start a chain — it can't directly-density-reach anyone). For two *core* points the relation is symmetric: every link in the chain goes between core points, so each directly-density-reachable step can be reversed and the whole chain can be read backward. Same asymmetry as before, inherited.

Now I hit the next snag, and it's subtle. Take two border points, `p` and `r`, sitting on opposite rims of the *same* cluster — say one on the north edge of a blob, one on the south. Neither is core, so neither can be density-reachable from the other (a border point can't start a chain). Yet they're plainly in the same cluster. If I define a cluster purely as "everything density-reachable from a seed," `p` and `r` are both reachable from a core point in the middle, fine — but I want a *relation between cluster members* that certifies they belong together, and density-reachability won't relate `p` to `r` directly because it's not symmetric and neither is core. I need a symmetric "same-cluster" relation that two border points can satisfy. The fix: route through a common dense witness. Say `p` is *density-connected* to `r` if there exists a point `o` such that *both* `p` and `r` are density-reachable from `o`; because reaching outward requires a core start, this witness is the core point in the interior whenever the relation is nontrivial. North-rim and south-rim border points are both reachable from that interior core point, so they're density-connected. And this relation *is* symmetric — if such an `o` exists for the pair, it exists for the pair in either order — and it's reflexive on any point that is itself density-reachable from some core point. Density-connectivity is the membership glue; density-reachability is the growth mechanism.

With those two relations I can finally define the object. A *cluster* `C` (with respect to `Eps`, `MinPts`) is a non-empty subset of the database satisfying two conditions:

  (1) Maximality: for all `p, q`, if `p ∈ C` and `q` is density-reachable from `p`, then `q ∈ C`.
  (2) Connectivity: for all `p, q ∈ C`, `p` is density-connected to `q`.

Maximality says the cluster swallows everything its members can reach — so it doesn't stop short of the true boundary; connectivity says it's all one piece — so it doesn't accidentally fuse two genuinely separate dense regions just because both got dragged into the set. And then *noise* is the cheap leftover: the set of points belonging to no cluster at all. That's demand four, satisfied by construction — noise isn't a special case I have to detect, it's simply what's not dense-connected to anything.

One thing should follow from these two conditions: does a cluster necessarily contain at least `MinPts` points? Suppose `C` is a cluster. It's non-empty, so it has some point `p`. By connectivity, `p` is density-connected to itself, which means there's a core point `o` with `p` density-reachable from `o`. But `o` being core means `|N_Eps(o)| ≥ MinPts` — `o` alone has at least `MinPts` neighbors, all of which (being directly density-reachable from the core `o`) are in `C` by maximality. So `|C| ≥ MinPts` — a cluster can't be a tiny sliver; the core condition floors its size.

Now the definitions are pretty, but they describe a cluster; they don't tell me how to *find* one without already knowing where it is. I need the bridge from "here is the predicate a cluster satisfies" to "here is a procedure that produces clusters." The intuition I'll lean on: a cluster is determined by any one of its core points, because from a core point I can reach the whole thing. Let me try to prove that, since the whole algorithm is going to hang on it.

Claim (call it Lemma 1): pick any point `p` in the database with `|N_Eps(p)| ≥ MinPts` (any core point). Then the set `O = { o | o is density-reachable from p }` is a cluster with respect to `Eps, MinPts`. I need to verify the two cluster conditions for `O`. First, is `O` non-empty? `p` is core, so `p ∈ N_Eps(p)` and `|N_Eps(p)| ≥ MinPts`, which makes `p` directly density-reachable from itself — so `p` is density-reachable from `p`, hence `p ∈ O`. Non-empty. Second, maximality: take `o ∈ O` and suppose `s` is density-reachable from `o`. By definition `o` is density-reachable from `p`, so I concatenate the chain `p → … → o` with the chain `o → … → s`; transitivity gives `s` density-reachable from `p`, so `s ∈ O`. Maximal. Third, connectivity: take any `o_1, o_2 ∈ O`. Both are density-reachable from `p`, and `p` is core — that's exactly the witness density-connectivity asks for, so `o_1` and `o_2` are density-connected through `o = p`. Connected. All three hold, so `O` is a cluster. Done.

That's already most of what I want: a core point *generates* a cluster as its density-reachable set. But to turn it into an algorithm I want the converse direction too — that if I'm handed a cluster and any core point in it, regrowing from that core point gives me back the *whole* cluster, nothing missing, nothing extra. Lemma 2: let `C` be a cluster and let `p` be any point of `C` with `|N_Eps(p)| ≥ MinPts`. Then `C` equals exactly `{ o | o is density-reachable from p }`. The `⊇` direction: any `o` density-reachable from `p` is in `C` by `C`'s maximality (since `p ∈ C`). The `⊆` direction is the one that needs the connectivity condition. Take any `q ∈ C`; I must show `q` is density-reachable from `p`. By connectivity, `p` and `q` are density-connected, so there is a point `o` with both `p` and `q` density-reachable from `o`. Since `p` is reached from `o` and `p` is core, the chain from `o` to `p` has core at both ends: if `o = p` this is immediate, and otherwise `o` must be core because it starts the first direct-reachability step. Every interior source in that chain is core too, so each direct step can be reversed; therefore `o` is density-reachable from `p`. Chain `p → … → o` with `o → … → q`, and by transitivity `q` is density-reachable from `p`. So every point of `C` is density-reachable from `p`. Both inclusions hold; `C` is precisely the density-reachable set of any of its core points.

Lemma 2 is the algorithm in disguise, so let me read the procedure straight off it. To find a cluster: grab an arbitrary unprocessed point `p` and ask whether it's core, by issuing one region query `N_Eps(p)` and checking `|N_Eps(p)| ≥ MinPts`. If it *is* core, Lemma 2 says the cluster containing `p` is exactly everything density-reachable from `p` — so I retrieve that set by a breadth-first expansion: start a seed list with `p`'s neighbors, and repeatedly take a seed, query *its* neighborhood, and if that seed is also core, add its as-yet-unassigned neighbors to the seed list; keep going until the seed list drains. That flood-fill along the dense backbone yields the whole cluster. If instead `p` is *not* core — it's either a border point or genuine noise — then nothing is density-reachable from `p` (it can't start a chain), so I can't grow a cluster from it; I tentatively mark it noise and move to the next point. The "tentatively" matters: a point I called noise might later turn out to be a border point of some cluster, reached when *another*, core, point's expansion sweeps over it — at which moment I reassign it. So the marking is provisional for non-core points and final only once a core point claims them.

Let me write the expansion as actual pseudocode, because the bookkeeping is where it lives or dies. I keep a single integer label per point — start everything UNCLASSIFIED — and walk every point once:

  for each point p in the database, if p is UNCLASSIFIED:
      if ExpandCluster(p, next_label) succeeds, advance next_label.

  ExpandCluster(p, ClId):
      seeds ← regionQuery(p, Eps)            # = N_Eps(p)
      if |seeds| < MinPts:                    # p is not core
          label(p) ← NOISE
          return False                        # not a cluster seed (yet — may be reclaimed)
      else:                                    # p is core; by Lemma 2 the cluster is its
          label every point in seeds ← ClId   #   density-reachable set, grown here
          remove p from seeds
          while seeds not empty:
              currentP ← seeds.first()
              result ← regionQuery(currentP, Eps)
              if |result| ≥ MinPts:           # currentP is also core: keep flooding
                  for each q in result:
                      if label(q) ∈ {UNCLASSIFIED, NOISE}:
                          if label(q) = UNCLASSIFIED:
                              seeds.append(q)  # only push unprocessed points (cores expand)
                          label(q) ← ClId      # claim it (a NOISE point becomes a border point)
              seeds.remove(currentP)
          return True

Two bookkeeping details matter here, plus one consequence for the outer scan. When `currentP` turns out to be a border point (`|result| < MinPts`), that one query has served only to prove it cannot propagate; I do *not* append its neighbors to the seeds, because border points have no density to flow outward. When a point currently labeled NOISE shows up inside a core point's `result`, I relabel it to the cluster — it was a border point all along — but I do *not* push it onto the seed list, because a point already labeled NOISE has already been region-queried and found sub-`MinPts`, so it has nothing to expand. Only UNCLASSIFIED points get pushed, since those still need to be checked for core-hood. And the order of the outer scan: by Lemma 2 the *set* of points a cluster contains is independent of which core point I seed from, so the clustering is independent of visiting order — except for the genuinely ambiguous case of a border point that lies within `Eps` of core points of two *different* clusters. Such a border point gets assigned to whichever cluster's expansion reaches it first. That can only happen when two clusters come within `Eps` of each other, and which of two clusters a single rim point lands in is of little practical interest; it's the one order-dependence, and I'll expect it to be negligible.

This bookkeeping is exactly where an off-by-one or a wrong push could ruin everything, so let me stop arguing and run the procedure by hand on a deliberately small case that contains every kind of point. Take `Eps = 1`, `MinPts = 3`, and six points laid out in the plane: a chain `A=(0,0), B=(1,0), C=(2,0), D=(3,0)` spaced one apart, a point `E=(0,1)` hanging off `A`, and `F=(10,0)` flung far away. First I compute each `N_Eps`, counting the self-point, and read off core-hood (`count ≥ 3`):

```
A : {A,B,E}  count 3  core
B : {A,B,C}  count 3  core
C : {B,C,D}  count 3  core
D : {C,D}    count 2  border
E : {A,E}    count 2  border
F : {F}      count 1  noise
```

So `A,B,C` are the dense backbone; `D` (only `C` and itself within 1) and `E` (only `A` and itself) are sub-threshold; `F` is alone. Now I walk the algorithm. The outer scan reaches `A` first — core and UNCLASSIFIED — so I label `A`→cluster 0 and push it. Pop `A` (core): its neighborhood is `{A,B,E}`; `B` is UNCLASSIFIED so it gets labeled 0 and pushed, `E` is UNCLASSIFIED so it gets labeled 0 but — since `E` is *not* core — it is **not** pushed. Pop `B` (core): neighborhood `{A,B,C}`, only `C` is still unlabeled, so `C`→0, pushed (core). Pop `C` (core): neighborhood `{B,C,D}`, only `D` unlabeled, so `D`→0, and `D` is *not* core so it is not pushed. Stack empty. Cluster 0 = `{A,B,C,D,E}`. The scan continues to `D,E` (already labeled, skipped), then `F`: `F` is not core, so it never seeds, and nothing ever reached it, so it keeps the noise label. Final labels: `A,B,C,D,E → 0`, `F → -1`, one cluster, only `A,B,C` ever popped and expanded — the whole dense chain plus both rim points (`D` at the far end, `E` dangling off the side) form one cluster, and the lone far point is set aside as noise, exactly what my eye wants. The trace exercised the two branches I was unsure of: a border point (`E`) claimed but not pushed during expansion, and an isolated point (`F`) left as noise without ever being grown from. Both hold.

Now I should count the work, because efficiency was demand three and the whole reason I leaned on the index. The key structural fact, visible in the pseudocode: `regionQuery` is called *exactly once per point in the database*. Each point is region-queried once — either as the seed `p` in some `ExpandCluster`, or as a `currentP` pulled off a seed list — and never again, because once a point has a definite cluster label it's skipped by the outer scan, and a point only enters a seed list while UNCLASSIFIED and is queried once when popped. So total run time is `n` region queries plus the bookkeeping, i.e. `O(n · Q)` where `Q` is the cost of one region query. With no index, a region query scans the whole database, `Q = O(n)`, and the algorithm is `O(n^2)` — already as good as the connectivity methods I admired, and with arbitrary shape and noise that they couldn't all give me. With the R\*-tree, a small region query descends only a few subtrees: in the regime that matters — `Eps`-neighborhoods small relative to the whole data space, so each query returns few points and touches few paths — a single query runs in roughly `O(log n)` on average, and the whole algorithm in roughly `O(n log n)` on average. I should be careful to state that as an *average*-case observation about small queries riding the index, not a worst-case guarantee — there's no theorem that a region query is `O(log n)` in the worst case, and if the queries aren't small the index doesn't save me. But the practical point stands: one query per point, cheap queries on an index, and the quadratic floor only in the degenerate case. Either way I've decoupled the cost from the all-pairs blowup that sank Ejcluster.

I've been carrying `Eps` and `MinPts` as if they were given, but the whole selling point was *minimal* domain knowledge, so I owe an account of how to choose them — and ideally collapse two parameters down to something a user can set. Let me think about `MinPts` first. It's the density threshold, the count that says "dense enough to be core," and its job is to *smooth* the density estimate — too small and a couple of stray noise points clumping together look like a cluster; large enough and the estimate is stable. The question is how touchy the output is to it: if a small change in `MinPts` re-draws the clusters, I can't just pin it, and I'm back to two real knobs. Let me actually look. I make two Gaussian blobs of 150 points each (centers at `(0,0)` and `(4,0)`, spread 0.4) plus 30 uniform-scattered noise points, hold `Eps = 0.5` fixed, and run the algorithm at `MinPts ∈ {3,4,5,6,8,10}`:

```
MinPts= 3: n_clusters=2, noise=20
MinPts= 4: n_clusters=2, noise=21
MinPts= 5: n_clusters=2, noise=21
MinPts= 6: n_clusters=2, noise=21
MinPts= 8: n_clusters=2, noise=21
MinPts=10: n_clusters=2, noise=23
```

The cluster count never budges off 2, and the noise count drifts by three points across a factor-of-three change in the threshold. So the output really is nearly flat in `MinPts` over a wide band — which means I don't need to agonize over it, I can fix it and move on. (The mechanism is clear in hindsight: inside a dense blob a point has *many* more than `MinPts` neighbors at any of these values, so raising the bar from 3 to 10 doesn't unseat interior cores; it only nibbles a couple of marginal rim points into noise.) For two-dimensional data, fix `MinPts = 4`. More generally, the right scale rides with dimension — a sensible rule of thumb is on the order of twice the dimensionality, `MinPts ≈ 2·dim`, raised somewhat for very noisy data or data with many duplicates, where I want the smoothing to be stronger. With `MinPts` pinned, I'm down to essentially *one* free parameter, `Eps`, which is the parameter-minimality I was after.

So how do I pick `Eps`? Here's a way to read it off the data. For a chosen `k`, define for every point its distance to its `k`-th nearest neighbor — call it `k-dist(p)` — and sort all the points in *descending* order of this value. Think about what that sorted curve, the "sorted `k`-dist graph," looks like. A point sitting deep inside a dense cluster has its `k`-th neighbor very close, so a small `k-dist`. A point out in the sparse noise has its `k`-th neighbor far away, so a large `k-dist`. So as I walk the descending-sorted curve, I start high (the sparse noise points, far-flung `k`-th neighbors) and drop down to low values (the dense cluster points). The drop isn't gradual — there's a "valley" or knee where the curve falls off a cliff, the transition from "noise-sparse" to "cluster-dense." Pick the `k-dist` value at that knee as `Eps`. Then a point whose `k-dist` is *larger* than this threshold — left of the knee, sparser than the threshold — has fewer than the required neighbors within `Eps` and falls to noise; a point with smaller `k-dist` — right of the knee, denser — sits in some cluster. In effect I'm setting `Eps` to the density of the *thinnest* cluster I'm still willing to call a cluster: everything denser than the knee is captured, everything sparser is noise. The knee is hard to detect automatically — it's much easier for a person to point at the valley in a plot, or to specify "I expect about this fraction noise" and read the threshold off — so in practice this is a semi-interactive step, but it's one decision a non-expert can make by looking, which is the bar I set.

There's an off-by-one I have to get exactly right or the whole thing is mis-tuned, and it's the kind of thing that's invisible until it bites. When I compute `k-dist(p)`, the `k`-th *nearest neighbor* conventionally does *not* count `p` itself — the nearest neighbors of `p` are other points. And I should keep a couple of cheap sanity checks in mind for whether `Eps` came out reasonable, because the knee can be ambiguous. A good clustering of real spatial data should leave *some* points as noise but not too many — somewhere in the range of a few percent up to perhaps a third is healthy; near-zero noise usually means `Eps` is too large (everything got swallowed) and very high noise means it's too small. And no single cluster should engulf most of the data: if the largest connected component runs past, say, a fifth to a half of the clustered points, that's a signal `Eps` is too large and dense sub-structure is being merged — pull `Eps` down. These aren't part of the algorithm, just red flags that tell me when the one free knob is set badly. They also expose the one real cost of using *global* `Eps, MinPts` for every cluster: two clusters of genuinely different density that happen to lie within `Eps` of each other can merge, because the global threshold can't be tight for the dense one and loose for the sparse one simultaneously. The remedy, if I need it, is to re-run on the merged blob with a higher `MinPts` (a denser threshold) to split it — but that's a rare, easily-spotted situation, and the simplicity of one global pair of parameters is worth it for the common case.

Let me step back and try to name what this construction *is*, because if it lines up with some classical object that's a sign I haven't just stapled heuristics together. The count `|N_Eps(p)|` is, up to a constant, a density estimate at `p`: it's a kernel density estimate using the simplest possible kernel — a uniform (flat) kernel of bandwidth `Eps` — evaluated at `p`, namely the number of sample points falling in the radius-`Eps` ball. (Concretely: a uniform-kernel KDE is `f̂(p) = (1/n) Σ_q (1/V) · 1[dist(p,q) ≤ Eps]` where `V` is the ball's volume, so `f̂(p) = |N_Eps(p)| / (nV)` — the count and the density estimate differ only by the fixed factor `nV`.) The core condition `|N_Eps(p)| ≥ MinPts` is then a *level-set* test: `p` is core iff `f̂(p) ≥ MinPts/(nV)`, i.e. the density estimate exceeds a fixed level. The core backbone of a cluster is a connected component of the super-level set `{ density ≥ threshold }`, and the border points are the sample points just below the level that touch such a component. So the construction reads as minimum-density-level-set clustering with a discrete boundary rule — thresholding a density estimate and taking connected dense pieces, with `Eps` the bandwidth and the level set by `MinPts`. That's reassuring: the formal object I built bottom-up out of "core, border, reachable" turns out to be a thing density estimation already knows about, which is more evidence the pieces fit together than if it had stayed a bespoke recipe.
