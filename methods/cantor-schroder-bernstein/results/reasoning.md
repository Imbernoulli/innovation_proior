I have two injections and I want a bijection. That's the whole thing. $f : A \to B$ embeds $A$ into $B$, $g : B \to A$ embeds $B$ into $A$, and I want to produce a single $h : A \to B$ that is one-to-one and onto. First, why isn't this trivial. If $f$ were already onto, I'd be done: an injection that's also surjective is a bijection. But $f$ need not be onto. Take the cleanest witness, $A = B = \mathbb N$, $f(n) = n+1$. It's injective, and its image is $\{1,2,3,\dots\}$ — it misses $0$. Neither one-sided map is a bijection, and there's no a priori bound on how big those leftovers are.

What about inverting? $g$ is injective, so on its image $g[B]$ it has a genuine inverse $g^{-1} : g[B] \to B$, and that *is* a bijection — onto $B$, even. The trouble is it's only a partial function on $A$: it's undefined on $A \setminus g[B]$, the very elements that have no $g$-preimage. So $f$ is total but not onto; $g^{-1}$ is onto but not total. Each fixes the other's defect exactly where the other works.

So maybe I can use $f$ on part of $A$ and $g^{-1}$ on the rest, letting each cover what it covers well. Concretely: split $A = C \sqcup C'$, set $h = f$ on $C$ and $h = g^{-1}$ on $C'$. For this to be a bijection the two images $f[C]$ and $g^{-1}[C']$ would have to tile $B$ with no overlap and no gap. Whether that's achievable I don't yet know — it depends entirely on *which* part is $C$. So the whole problem, if this shape is even viable, reduces to one question: where do I cut?

Let me feel out the constraints on the cut before I guess it. Two things can go wrong. $$ B = f[C] \;\sqcup\; \{\, b \in B : g(b) \in C' \,\}. $$
Equivalently, writing $C' = A \setminus C$, the elements of $B$ *not* equal to any $f(x)$ with $x \in C$ must be exactly those whose $g$-image lies outside $C$. $$ C = A \setminus g[\,B \setminus f[C]\,]. $$

Let me stare at $m(X) = A \setminus g[\,B \setminus f[X]\,]$ and ask whether it's monotone, because if it is, I have a fixed point for free. Suppose $X \subseteq Y$. I'd half-expected the two complements to make $m$ order-*reversing*, which would have killed the fixed-point route; counting the flips is what saved it. a **doubly-infinite path** — backward trace never stops and never repeats;
a **cycle** — backward trace loops around;
a path with a **first vertex in $A$** — backward trace stops at some $a_0 \in A \setminus g[B]$;
a path with a **first vertex in $B$** — backward trace stops at some $b_0 \in B \setminus f[A]$.

Along any of these, vertices alternate $A,B,A,B,\dots$. So *within a single component*, I just need to pair its $A$-vertices with its $B$-vertices, and then glue the components. And the finite witness already exhibited this — there $C=\emptyset$ (least) gave $h=[3,0,1,2]$ and $C=\{0,1,2,3\}$ (greatest) gave $h=[1,2,3,0]$, two *different* bijections, with the in-between fixed points $\{0,2\},\{1,3\}$ giving yet others. So the freedom is real and computed, not just conceded. Least fixed point = "$C$ as small as possible" = the orbit rule that puts cycles and doubly-infinite paths on the $g^{-1}$ side; greatest = the convention that puts them on the $f$ side. They're the two canonical bijections, differing only on the components where *both* maps work. (That's the same freedom I noticed in the picture — on cycles and doubly-infinite paths either choice is a bijection.) Good: the construction isn't just *a* canonical map, it's one of a tidy pair, and the asymmetry between them lives entirely on the "don't-care" components.

Let me make sure I can hit this with the *gluing lemma* too, since that's the route the picture suggested and it's worth having both. The claim there was: if $\{X_i\}$ are pairwise disjoint, $\{Y_i\}$ pairwise disjoint, and $X_i \approx Y_i$ for each $i$, then $\bigcup X_i \approx \bigcup Y_i$. Proof: pick the bijections $h_i : X_i \to Y_i$ and define $h = \bigcup h_i$ on $\bigcup X_i$, i.e. $h(x) = h_i(x)$ for the unique $i$ with $x \in X_i$ (unique by disjointness, so $h$ is well defined). It's injective: if $h(x)=h(x')$ with $x\in X_i, x'\in X_j$, then $h_i(x)=h_j(x') \in Y_i\cap Y_j$, so $Y_i\cap Y_j\ne\emptyset$, so $i=j$ (disjointness of the $Y$'s), and then $h_i(x)=h_i(x')$ gives $x=x'$. It's onto $\bigcup Y_i$ since each $h_i$ is onto $Y_i$. So the union of bijections on disjoint pieces is a bijection — exactly the gluing I need, and it's why I had to take $A,B$ disjoint and split into genuinely disjoint components: a naive "$A_1\approx B_1$ and $A_2\approx B_2$ so $A_1\cup A_2 \approx B_1\cup B_2$" is false without disjointness (the two bijections could clash on an overlap), and the component decomposition is precisely what guarantees disjointness on both sides. With it, the per-component bijections (each component's $A$-part $\approx$ its $B$-part, via the forced map) glue to a bijection $A\to B$. Same $h$, assembled bottom-up.

There's a third packaging worth deriving, because it removes the chain machinery entirely and is the most hands-on. Instead of seeding at the bottom, *approximate from above* by alternately pushing the whole sets through. Set $A_0 = A$, $B_0 = B$, and
$$ A_{n} = g[B_{n-1}], \qquad B_n = f[A_{n-1}] \quad (n\ge 1). $$
Injectivity gives the alternating equivalences $A_0 \approx B_1 \approx A_2 \approx \cdots$ and $B_0 \approx A_1 \approx B_2 \approx \cdots$ (each step is $f$ or $g$ restricted to the relevant piece, hence a bijection onto its image). And the inclusions descend: $A_0 \supseteq A_1 \supseteq A_2 \supseteq \cdots$ — because $B_0 \supseteq B_1$ gives $g[B_0]\supseteq g[B_1]$, i.e. $A_1 \supseteq A_2$, and so on down. If ever $A_N = A_{N+1}$ the chain stabilizes and the alternation already pairs everything; the interesting case is all inclusions strict. Now the inclusions overlap, so I can't glue them directly — I must carve disjoint pieces, the "annuli" $A_n^* = A_n \setminus A_{n+1}$ and $B_n^* = B_n\setminus B_{n+1}$, which *are* pairwise disjoint. Injectivity transports them: $f$ maps $A_n^* = A_n\setminus A_{n+1}$ bijectively to $f[A_n]\setminus f[A_{n+1}] = B_{n+1}\setminus B_{n+2} = B_{n+1}^*$ (using $f[S\setminus T]=f[S]\setminus f[T]$ for injective $f$), and $g$ maps $B_n^*$ to $A_{n+1}^*$. So $A_n^* \approx B_{n+1}^*$ and $B_n^* \approx A_{n+1}^*$. Pair them off: on the $A$-side annuli, send $A_{2n}^* \xrightarrow{f} B_{2n+1}^*$ and $A_{2n+1}^* \xrightarrow{g^{-1}} B_{2n}^*$ — every $A_n^*$ matched to some $B_m^*$, every $B_m^*$ used once, and the gluing lemma (disjoint pieces!) unions them into a bijection $\bigcup_n A_n^* \approx \bigcup_n B_n^*$. What's left over? The cores $\bar A = \bigcap_n A_n$ and $\bar B = \bigcap_n B_n$, on which $f[\bar A] = \bar B$ exactly (since $f[\bigcap A_n] = \bigcap f[A_n] = \bigcap B_{n+1} = \bar B$, using injectivity to commute $f$ with the intersection), so $f$ restricts to a bijection $\bar A \approx \bar B$. Glue the annular bijection with $f|_{\bar A}$ — disjoint again — and that's a bijection $A \approx B$. This nested-intersection packaging approximates the cut *from above*, by descending shells, rather than Bernstein's dual route that unions the *forward* iterates of the seed from below (which is exactly my chain $C$); both reach the same $h$, one peeling off shells and one growing from the seed. (And it does quietly use $\mathbb N$ to index the shells, which is exactly the dependence Dedekind's chain formulation was built to avoid — both are fine, they just pay for the iteration differently.)

So three constructions, one map: the fixed-point cut $C$ with $h = f$ on $C$ and $g^{-1}$ off it; the orbit/graph partition that *explains* the cut by where each component originates; and the shell-by-shell approximation. Each is grounded in nothing but $f$, $g$, images, the partial inverse $g^{-1}$ on $g[B]$, Dedekind's chain-of-a-seed (or the naturals, for the shells), and the disjoint-gluing lemma. None well-orders anything, none invokes Choice; only excluded middle, to classify each element's orbit. Let me also close the loop with the *fundamental lemma* form, because it shows the theorem is "really" a statement about embedding a set into a sandwiched subset, which is conceptually the cleanest. The lemma: if $h_0 : a \to e$ is a bijection and $e \subseteq d \subseteq a$, then $a \approx d$. The theorem reduces to it: given injections $f,g$, the composite $g\circ f : A \to A$ is a bijection onto $g[f[A]]$, and $g[f[A]] \subseteq g[B] \subseteq A$ — so with $a = A$, $e = g[f[A]]$, $d = g[B]$, the lemma gives $A \approx g[B]$; compose with $g^{-1} : g[B] \approx B$ and get $A \approx B$. And the lemma itself is the same chain argument in miniature: let $c$ be the chain of $a\setminus d$ under $h_0$ (smallest $h_0$-closed set containing $a\setminus d$), define $h^*(x) = h_0(x)$ on $c$ and $h^*(x) = x$ on $a\setminus c$. By the structural identity $c = (a\setminus d)\cup h_0[c]$, this $h^*$ is a bijection $a \to d$: on $c$ it bijects $c$ onto $h_0[c] = c\setminus(a\setminus d)$, the identity fixes $a\setminus c$, and the pieces $\{c, a\setminus c\}$ partition $a$ while $\{h_0[c], a\setminus c\}$ partition $d$ — exactly the same partition-and-glue, with "$\mathrm{id}$" playing the role $g^{-1}$ played before. Seeding the chain at $a\setminus d$ instead of $d\setminus e$ is Dedekind's least-fixed-point variant; seeding at $d\setminus e$ is Zermelo's greatest — the same two canonical maps showing up yet again, from the same single idea.

So here is the theorem, stated the way I'd stand behind it — the chain/fixed-point version, since it's the shortest airtight one and carries the orbit intuition inside it:

> **Theorem (Cantor–Schröder–Bernstein).** Let $A, B$ be sets. If there exist injections $f : A \to B$ and $g : B \to A$, then there exists a bijection $h : A \to B$.

**Proof.** With $m(X) = A\setminus g[B\setminus f[X]]$ monotone, $C = \bigcap\{X : m(X)\subseteq X\}$ its least fixed point, and $C = (A\setminus g[B])\cup g[f[C]]$ the structural identity derived above, set $h = f$ on $C$ and $h = g^{-1}$ on $A\setminus C$. Totality, injectivity, and surjectivity are exactly the three checks just run: $A\setminus g[B]\subseteq C$ makes $h$ well defined on $A\setminus C$; the cross-case collision forces the colliding point back into $C$, contradiction; and tracing $g(b)$ into or out of $C$ hits every $b\in B$ exactly once. $\qquad\blacksquare$
