Let me start from what's actually hurting, not from a clean design. I have RLHF working: collect human comparisons of which response is better, train a preference model to score responses, then RL the policy against that score with a KL leash to a reference. It makes assistants more helpful and more harmless. But the harmlessness half is the part I want to fix, and it has three problems that all trace to the same root.

First, it's expensive and opaque. Getting harmlessness means tens of thousands of human labels saying "response A is less harmful than B." Those labels are private, and even when shared they explain nothing — nobody can read 40,000 comparisons and tell you what objective they encode. And every time I want to change the harmlessness target, I have to collect a fresh batch. Second, and worse, it produces *evasiveness*. When I ask crowdworkers which response to a harmful prompt is better, they reward refusals — "I can't help with that." So the model learns that the safe move on anything touchy is to stonewall. Then it gets stuck: once it's in refusal mode it stays evasive for the rest of the conversation. And evasiveness is genuinely costly — it trades away helpfulness, and it's the source of the helpful-vs-harmless tension everyone sees, where pushing harmlessness up drags helpfulness down. Third, this is all human-supervision-bound at exactly the moment when models are getting good enough that exhaustive human labeling won't scale.

So the question crystallizes: can I get harmlessness with *no human harm labels at all* — with the only human input being a short written list of principles — and get it *non-evasively*, so the model engages and explains its objection instead of refusing blankly?

The crucial premise I'd want to test is whether the model can already *recognize* harm without being taught to. I can probe this directly: take harmlessness judgment and pose it to a large LM as a multiple-choice question — "here's a conversation and two candidate responses, which is more harmless?" — and read off the model's probability on each option. On the 438-comparison HHH set, large pretrained models pick the better one with high accuracy, and letting the model *reason step by step* before committing raises that accuracy further, toward what a preference model trained on hundreds of thousands of human labels reaches. I should be careful about what "high accuracy" buys me, though: accuracy on a clean multiple-choice set doesn't by itself prove the model can label *adversarial red-team* prompts well, and a single number could hide systematic blind spots. But it's enough to make the bet plausible — the recognition capability is in the model. If the model can judge harmlessness, then maybe the model can supply the very labels I was paying humans for, and the human's job shrinks from labeling thousands of examples to writing down the criteria. That's the lever I want to pull on.

Now, RLHF already took half a step in this direction without anyone calling it that: at RL time the reward doesn't come from a human, it comes from the learned preference model — an AI. The humans are upstream, in the PM's training labels. So "scaled supervision" here means pushing the human further upstream still: replace the human harmlessness labels that train the PM with *AI* harmlessness labels, generated by a model reading a principle. RL from AI Feedback instead of from human feedback. Let me see if I can build the whole harmlessness signal out of model self-supervision steered by a written constitution.

Let me try the most direct thing for the RL stage and see if it's enough. I take my assistant, generate two responses to a red-team prompt, and ask a *feedback model* (some LM) "which of these is more harmless, per this principle?" I format it as multiple choice — present the conversation, the principle, option (A), option (B), and read off the model's probabilities for "(A)" vs "(B)." Those normalized probabilities become the soft label for a preference comparison. I do this over a big set of red-team prompts, getting an AI-generated harmlessness preference dataset; I mix it with my existing *human* helpfulness comparisons (I'm only removing humans from harmlessness, keeping them for helpfulness); train a PM on the mixture exactly as in RLHF; and RL against it. So the harmlessness PM is now trained on AI labels, and the RL step is ordinary RL with that PM as reward — the only structural change from RLHF is who produces the harm labels.

But will I get reasonable behavior if I drop the RL policy straight in here? Think about exploration. The RL stage improves whatever distribution it starts from, by sampling responses and reweighting toward higher PM score. If I initialize RL from a raw helpful-only model on harmful prompts, its samples are mostly *harmful* — the harmful region is where it lives. The mechanism that worries me is concrete: RL can only reweight responses it actually *samples*. If the policy almost never emits a harmless-and-engaged response to a red-team prompt, the PM never gets to reward that behavior, so there's nothing for the gradient to push toward — the policy has to stumble onto good responses by chance before it can reinforce them. With a KL leash to the reference model on top, the policy can't wander far to find that region quickly. So I'd expect a long, possibly unstable RL run to relocate the whole distribution, rather than a short one to sharpen it. I haven't run this, so it's a prediction, not a measured fact — but the cleaner move is to remove the dependence on luck entirely: get the model *on-distribution* first, moving its default behavior toward harmless-but-engaged before RL even starts, so RL only has to refine. That argues for a supervised warm-up stage feeding into the RL stage.

What supervised targets? I don't have human-written harmless responses, and I refuse to collect them. But the model can recognize harm — can it also *fix* its own output? Let me build the targets by self-revision. Show the helpful model a red-team prompt; it produces a response, which is typically harmful ("sure, here's how to hack the wifi"). Now append an instruction drawn from the constitution asking it to *critique* its own last response — "identify specific ways that response is harmful, unethical, dangerous, or illegal" — and sample the critique. The model says, in effect, "that response is harmful because hacking someone's wifi invades their privacy and is likely illegal." Then append a *revision* instruction — "rewrite the response to remove all harmful content" — and sample the revision: "Hacking into your neighbor's wifi is an invasion of privacy and I'd advise against it; it could also be illegal." Now I stitch the original prompt to the revised response, and I have a (prompt, harmless-response) pair built entirely by the model, steered only by written critique/revision instructions.

Two design points fall out of trying this. One: the stitched (prompt, revision) pair is formatted exactly like the original (prompt, response) pair, which means I can feed it back in and run critique→revision *again*. So I can iterate, giving the model repeated chances to catch whatever harm remains, and every revision in the sequence is itself a valid harmless target I can keep. Two: I shouldn't hard-code one critique/revision instruction. I write a set of them — different principles emphasizing different facets of harm — and at each revision step I draw one at random. This is the "constitution": about 16 short natural-language principles, many overlapping/general, some targeting specific harms. A single instruction would make the data narrow; random principles make the revised responses more diverse, and diversity is exactly what helps exploration in the later RL stage. I sample at temperature 1 to keep variety.

A subtlety I hit while doing this: the model sometimes loses track of whose turn it is — it writes a critique where a revision belongs, or vice versa. The fix is few-shot prompting: prepend a handful of correctly-formatted critique→revision examples so the model locks onto the pattern.

Is the critique step even necessary, or could I just ask for a revision directly ("rewrite this to be harmless") and skip critiquing? Let me reason about what the critique buys. For a less capable model, forcing it to first *articulate* what's wrong before fixing it should scaffold the repair: identify the harmful piece, then remove or redirect it. For a stronger model, direct revision may often be enough, and I should not treat critiques as perfect ground truth because they can overstate or misdiagnose the problem. But I'll keep the critique anyway, for transparency: the critique is a legible trace of *why* the model thinks the response is harmful, which is one of my goals, and that kind of reasoning may help surface subtler harms. So: critique then revise.

Then I finetune a *pretrained* model (not the helpful RLHF model — I want a clean base) on the harmlessness revisions from all revision steps — not just the last one, since each revision in the sequence is itself a valid harmless target and using them all gives more training signal. But finetuning only on harmlessness revisions would tank helpfulness. So I also sample plain responses from the helpful RLHF model on helpfulness prompts and mix those in, to retain helpfulness. This finetuned model is "SL-CAI." Concretely: one epoch, a constant learning rate set to 0.5 relative to the pretraining learning rate, batch size 1024.

Now the RL stage sits on top of SL-CAI, and SL-CAI fixes the exploration problem — its default responses are already harmless-ish, so the PM-driven RL only refines. And I should use SL-CAI for *both* generating the response pairs and as the RL initialization: matching the generation distribution to the policy's starting distribution keeps the PM well-matched to the policy early in RL. So: SL-CAI generates pairs, a feedback model labels which is more harmless per a randomly-sampled principle (again ~16 principles, so the PM is not trained against quirks of one wording), I make soft-labeled comparisons from the normalized (A)/(B) probabilities, mix with human helpfulness comparisons, train the PM, and RL SL-CAI against it. That is RL-CAI.

Now layer chain-of-thought onto the AI judgments, since I saw it raises harm-identification accuracy. Instead of asking the feedback model for (A)/(B) directly, reformat the principle into a conversation and prompt "Let's think step-by-step:" so it reasons before committing, with a few hand-written CoT exemplars. For CoT I use a helpful RLHF model rather than the bare pretrained one, because it writes better step-by-step reasoning.

But CoT introduces a calibration problem, and I should work out exactly how bad it is before deciding whether it matters. The soft label I extract is a softmax over two token log-probabilities, `p(A) = softmax([logprob(" (A)"), logprob(" (B)")])`. In the *no*-CoT case the model reads the two options cold and registers, say, a mild preference for A — logprobs around −0.7 and −1.3 nats. Softmax of that is `e^-0.7 / (e^-0.7 + e^-1.3) ≈ 0.50/(0.50+0.27) ≈ 0.65`. A 0.65 soft label: informative, but honestly hedged. Now the CoT case. When the model reasons out loud it almost always *states* its choice in the text first — "...so (A) is clearly more harmless" — and only then emits the answer token. By the time it writes " (A)" the context has already committed, so that token's logprob is near 0 and " (B)" is deeply negative, say −0.01 vs −7.0. Softmax: `e^-0.01 / (e^-0.01 + e^-7.0) ≈ 0.990/(0.990+0.0009) ≈ 0.999`. So CoT doesn't just shift the label a little — it collapses it to ~0.999, essentially a hard one-hot. That's the wall: CoT buys better *accuracy* but destroys *calibration*.

Why does that hurt the PM? The PM is fit to these soft labels by cross-entropy, so it's trying to reproduce a target probability of 0.999 for the pair. The score gap it must learn is the logit of the target: `logit(0.999) ≈ +7.4`, against `logit(0.65) ≈ +0.6` for the honest no-CoT label. So feeding raw CoT labels teaches the PM reward gaps roughly an order of magnitude larger than the evidence warrants, on *every* pair — and RL maximizing an over-steep reward will chase those extremes, pushing the policy to over-confident, worse responses. The cure is to compress the labels back toward the middle before training the PM: clamp the CoT-derived probability into a band, roughly 40–60%. Check what the clamp does to my two numbers: `clamp(0.999) → 0.60`, which rescues the collapsed CoT label, and `clamp(0.65) → 0.60`, which barely moves the honest no-CoT one. So the clamp targets exactly the pathology — it pulls the artificially-extreme labels back while leaving moderate labels essentially untouched. It's not free: clamping also flattens any genuinely-confident label down to 0.6, discarding some real signal, so it's a deliberate trade of a bit of label sharpness for calibrated, stable RL. Given that the un-clamped failure mode is the policy drifting to extreme outputs, I'll take that trade and clamp.

Why should this reduce old HH RLHF's evasiveness, not just its label cost? Because the harmlessness signal now comes from principles that I can *write* to reward engaged, explanatory harmlessness rather than blank refusal — and from a model that revises by explaining its objection. The original HH model was evasive because human crowdworkers rewarded refusals; here I control the reward via the constitution and via revisions that engage-and-explain, so I can steer toward "refuse the unethical request but explain why" instead of "shut down." That directly attacks the helpfulness/harmlessness tension that evasiveness created. The behavior I need to check is whether the resulting RL-CAI is harmless at a given helpfulness level while crowdworkers, instructed to prefer non-evasive among equally harmless responses, actually prefer the engaged style.

So the two stages, end to end. Supervised: helpful model → response → (sampled constitutional principle) critique → revision, iterated, diverse over ~16 principles; finetune a pretrained model on the revisions from all revision steps plus helpfulness samples → SL-CAI. RL: SL-CAI generates response pairs → feedback model picks the more harmless per a sampled principle (optionally with clamped chain-of-thought) → normalized probabilities become preference labels → mix with human helpfulness comparisons → train PM → RL SL-CAI against the PM (standard PPO+KL) → RL-CAI. No human labels for harm anywhere; the only human input is the written constitution.

At this point the implementation is just the two missing data-generation functions.

```python
PRINCIPLES = [  # ~16 short written rules forming the 'constitution'
    "Identify specific ways the assistant's last response is harmful, unethical, "
    "racist, sexist, toxic, dangerous, or illegal.",
    # ... ~15 more, each a (critique_request, revision_request) facet of harm
]

# Supervised critique -> revision data for SL-CAI.
def make_harmless_sft_data(helpful_model, red_team_prompts, principles,
                           n_revisions=4, few_shot=CRITIQUE_REVISION_FEWSHOT):
    data = []
    for prompt in red_team_prompts:
        resp = sample(helpful_model, prompt, T=1.0)         # usually harmful
        for _ in range(n_revisions):
            principle = random.choice(principles)            # diversity for RL exploration
            critique  = sample(helpful_model,                # explain WHY it's harmful
                               few_shot + prompt + resp + principle.critique_request, T=1.0)
            revision  = sample(helpful_model,                # rewrite to remove harm
                               few_shot + prompt + resp + critique + principle.revision_request, T=1.0)
            data.append((prompt, revision))                  # keep revision from every step
            resp = revision                                  # iterate critique->revision
    return data

# AI harmlessness labels for RL-CAI.
def ai_label(feedback_model, prompt, a, b, principle, use_cot=False):
    if use_cot:                                              # helpful RLHF model writes CoT
        cot = sample(feedback_model, COT_FEWSHOT + prompt + principle + a + b
                     + "\nAssistant: Let's think step-by-step:", T=1.0)
        ctx = prompt + principle + a + b + cot + "\nThe answer is:"
    else:
        ctx = FEWSHOT + prompt + principle + a + b + "\nThe answer is:"
    pA, pB = logprob(feedback_model, ctx, " (A)"), logprob(feedback_model, ctx, " (B)")
    p = softmax([pA, pB])[0]                                 # P(A is more harmless)
    if use_cot:
        p = min(0.6, max(0.4, p))                            # clamp: CoT is overconfident
    return p

def make_harmless_preference_data(sl_cai, red_team_prompts, principles, use_cot=False):
    prefs = []
    for prompt in red_team_prompts:
        a, b = sample(sl_cai, prompt, T=1.0), sample(sl_cai, prompt, T=1.0)
        principle = random.choice(principles)                # ensemble over principles -> robust PM
        prefs.append(((prompt, a, b), ai_label(feedback_model, prompt, a, b, principle, use_cot)))
    return prefs

def build_assistant():
    sft = make_harmless_sft_data(helpful_rlhf, red_team_prompts, PRINCIPLES)
    sl_cai = finetune(pretrained, sft + helpfulness_samples,           # mix to keep helpful
                      lr=0.5 * PRETRAIN_LR, epochs=1, batch_size=1024)
    prefs = make_harmless_preference_data(sl_cai, red_team_prompts, PRINCIPLES, use_cot=True)
    pm = train_preference_model(prefs + human_helpfulness_comparisons) # AI harm + human help
    return rlhf(sl_cai, all_prompts, pm, ref_model=sl_cai)             # RL-CAI
```

The chain: RLHF gets harmlessness only via tens of thousands of opaque human labels and, because crowdworkers reward refusals, it makes models evasive — the root of the helpful/harmless tension. But large models can already recognize harm (more so with step-by-step reasoning), so the human can be pushed upstream to a short written constitution and the labels generated by the model itself. A supervised critique→revision loop, steered by randomly-sampled principles and warm-started from a helpful model, builds harmless-but-engaged targets to finetune SL-CAI, putting the model on-distribution and solving RL exploration. Then AI feedback — the model choosing the more harmless of two responses per a principle, with chain-of-thought judgments clamped to mid-range for calibration — supplies the harmlessness preference labels for a PM (mixed with human *helpfulness* labels), against which SL-CAI is RL-finetuned, yielding RL-CAI: harmless without human harm labels, and non-evasive because the principles and revisions reward explaining objections rather than stonewalling.
