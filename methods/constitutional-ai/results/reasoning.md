Let me start from what's actually hurting, not from a clean design. I have RLHF working: collect human comparisons of which response is better, train a preference model to score responses, then RL the policy against that score with a KL leash to a reference. It makes assistants more helpful and more harmless. But the harmlessness half is the part I want to fix, and it has three problems that all trace to the same root.

First, it's expensive and opaque. Getting harmlessness means tens of thousands of human labels saying "response A is less harmful than B." Those labels are private, and even when shared they explain nothing — nobody can read 40,000 comparisons and tell you what objective they encode. And every time I want to change the harmlessness target, I have to collect a fresh batch. Second, and worse, it produces *evasiveness*. When I ask crowdworkers which response to a harmful prompt is better, they reward refusals — "I can't help with that." So the model learns that the safe move on anything touchy is to stonewall. Then it gets stuck: once it's in refusal mode it stays evasive for the rest of the conversation. And evasiveness is genuinely costly — it trades away helpfulness, and it's the source of the helpful-vs-harmless tension everyone sees, where pushing harmlessness up drags helpfulness down. Third, this is all human-supervision-bound at exactly the moment when models are getting good enough that exhaustive human labeling won't scale.

So the question crystallizes: can I get harmlessness with *no human harm labels at all* — with the only human input being a short written list of principles — and get it *non-evasively*, so the model engages and explains its objection instead of refusing blankly?

Here's the observation that makes me think this is even possible. Take harmlessness judgment and pose it to a large LM as a multiple-choice question: "here's a conversation and two candidate responses, which is more harmless?" Large pretrained models already pick the better one with high accuracy — and if I let the model *reason step by step* before answering, the accuracy goes up, trending toward what a preference model trained on hundreds of thousands of human labels achieves. So the capability to *recognize* harm is already in the model. If the model can judge harmlessness, then maybe the model can supply the very labels I was paying humans for. The human's job shrinks from labeling thousands of examples to writing down the criteria.

Now, RLHF already took half a step in this direction without anyone calling it that: at RL time the reward doesn't come from a human, it comes from the learned preference model — an AI. The humans are upstream, in the PM's training labels. So "scaled supervision" here means pushing the human further upstream still: replace the human harmlessness labels that train the PM with *AI* harmlessness labels, generated by a model reading a principle. RL from AI Feedback instead of from human feedback. Let me see if I can build the whole harmlessness signal out of model self-supervision steered by a written constitution.

Let me try the most direct thing for the RL stage and see if it's enough. I take my assistant, generate two responses to a red-team prompt, and ask a *feedback model* (some LM) "which of these is more harmless, per this principle?" I format it as multiple choice — present the conversation, the principle, option (A), option (B), and read off the model's probabilities for "(A)" vs "(B)." Those normalized probabilities become the soft label for a preference comparison. I do this over a big set of red-team prompts, getting an AI-generated harmlessness preference dataset; I mix it with my existing *human* helpfulness comparisons (I'm only removing humans from harmlessness, keeping them for helpfulness); train a PM on the mixture exactly as in RLHF; and RL against it. That's RLAIF, and the rest of the pipeline — PM training, PPO with KL penalty — is untouched.

But will I get reasonable behavior if I drop the RL policy straight in here? Think about exploration. The RL stage improves whatever distribution it starts from, by sampling responses and reweighting toward higher PM score. If I initialize RL from a raw helpful-only model on harmful prompts, its samples are mostly *harmful* — the harmful region is where it lives — so the preference signal has to push a long way, exploration is slow, and RL runs long and unstable. The policy is "off-distribution" relative to where I want it. I should get the model *on-distribution* first — move its default behavior toward harmless-but-engaged before RL even starts — so RL only has to refine, not relocate. So I want a supervised warm-up stage before the RL stage.

What supervised targets? I don't have human-written harmless responses, and I refuse to collect them. But the model can recognize harm — can it also *fix* its own output? Let me build the targets by self-revision. Show the helpful model a red-team prompt; it produces a response, which is typically harmful ("sure, here's how to hack the wifi"). Now append an instruction drawn from the constitution asking it to *critique* its own last response — "identify specific ways that response is harmful, unethical, dangerous, or illegal" — and sample the critique. The model says, in effect, "that response is harmful because hacking someone's wifi invades their privacy and is likely illegal." Then append a *revision* instruction — "rewrite the response to remove all harmful content" — and sample the revision: "Hacking into your neighbor's wifi is an invasion of privacy and I'd advise against it; it could also be illegal." Now I stitch the original prompt to the revised response, and I have a (prompt, harmless-response) pair built entirely by the model, steered only by written critique/revision instructions.

Two design points fall out of trying this. One: the stitched (prompt, revision) pair is formatted exactly like the original (prompt, response) pair, which means I can feed it back in and run critique→revision *again*. So I can iterate, getting a sequence of revisions, each (in principle) more harmless. Empirically the first revision removes most of the harm and later ones help less, but iterating is free signal and improves harmlessness scores monotonically. Two: I shouldn't hard-code one critique/revision instruction. I write a set of them — different principles emphasizing different facets of harm — and at each revision step I draw one at random. This is the "constitution": about 16 short natural-language principles, many overlapping/general, some targeting specific harms. Sampling a random principle per step doesn't obviously raise the average harmlessness score (one good principle is about as harmless as many), but it makes the revisions *diverse*, and diversity is exactly what helps exploration in the later RL stage. I sample at temperature 1 to keep variety.

A subtlety I hit while doing this: the model sometimes loses track of whose turn it is — it writes a critique where a revision belongs, or vice versa. The fix is few-shot prompting: prepend a handful of correctly-formatted critique→revision examples so the model locks onto the pattern.

Is the critique step even necessary, or could I just ask for a revision directly ("rewrite this to be harmless") and skip critiquing? Let me reason about what the critique buys. For a smaller model, forcing it to first *articulate* what's wrong before fixing it gives a better revision — the explicit reasoning step scaffolds the fix; critiqued revisions score more harmless than direct ones. For a large model the gap mostly closes (it can fix in one shot), and honestly its critiques are sometimes inaccurate or overstated. But I'll keep the critique anyway, for transparency: the critique is a legible trace of *why* the model thinks the response is harmful, which is one of my goals, and that kind of reasoning may help surface subtler harms. So: critique then revise.

Then I finetune a *pretrained* model (not the helpful RLHF model — I want a clean base) on all the revisions, from every revision step. But finetuning only on harmlessness revisions would tank helpfulness. So I also sample plain responses from the helpful RLHF model on helpfulness prompts and mix those in, to retain helpfulness. This finetuned model is "SL-CAI." Concretely: one epoch, a constant learning rate set to 0.5 relative to the pretraining learning rate, batch size 1024.

Now the RL stage sits on top of SL-CAI, and SL-CAI fixes the exploration problem — its default responses are already harmless-ish, so the PM-driven RL only refines. And I should use SL-CAI for *both* generating the response pairs and as the RL initialization: matching the generation distribution to the policy's starting distribution keeps the PM well-matched to the policy early in RL. So: SL-CAI generates pairs, a feedback model labels which is more harmless per a randomly-sampled principle (again ~16 principles — ensembling over principles makes the PM notably more robust than reusing one principle for every label), I make soft-labeled comparisons from the normalized (A)/(B) probabilities, mix with human helpfulness comparisons, train the PM, and RL SL-CAI against it. RLAIF.

Now layer chain-of-thought onto the AI judgments, since I saw it raises harm-identification accuracy. Instead of asking the feedback model for (A)/(B) directly, reformat the principle into a conversation and prompt "Let's think step by step:" so it reasons before committing, with a few hand-written CoT exemplars. For CoT I use a helpful RLHF model rather than the bare pretrained one, because it writes better step-by-step reasoning.

But CoT introduces a calibration wall. When the model reasons out loud, it almost always *states* its chosen option explicitly in the reasoning ("...so (B) is clearly better"), which makes the readout probability essentially 0 or 1 — overconfident, not calibrated. If I feed near-0/near-1 soft labels into the PM, the PM learns to assign extreme scores, and RL against an over-extreme reward produces over-extreme, worse responses. The fix is to *clamp* the CoT-derived probabilities into a middle band — keep them within roughly 40–60% — before using them as PM targets. That re-injects appropriate uncertainty and gives more robust RL behavior; without the clamp, RL-CAI drifts to extreme outputs.

Why does this whole thing beat the old HH RLHF on evasiveness, not just on label cost? Because the harmlessness signal now comes from principles that I can *write* to reward engaged, explanatory harmlessness rather than blank refusal — and from a model that revises by explaining its objection. The original HH model was evasive because human crowdworkers rewarded refusals; here I control the reward via the constitution and via revisions that engage-and-explain, so I can steer toward "refuse the unethical request but explain why" instead of "shut down." That directly attacks the helpfulness/harmlessness tension that evasiveness created. The thing I'd want to validate is that the resulting RL-CAI lands at lower harmfulness for a given helpfulness than the human-feedback HH model, and that crowdworkers (told to prefer non-evasive among equally harmless responses) actually prefer it.

So the two stages, end to end. Supervised: helpful model → response → (sampled principle) critique → revision, iterated, diverse over ~16 principles; finetune a pretrained model on all revisions plus helpfulness samples → SL-CAI. RL: SL-CAI generates response pairs → feedback model picks the more harmless per a sampled principle (optionally with clamped chain-of-thought) → normalized probabilities become preference labels → mix with human helpfulness comparisons → train PM → RL SL-CAI against the PM (standard PPO+KL) → RL-CAI. No human labels for harm anywhere; the only human input is the written constitution.

Now the code, filling the two open slots.

```python
PRINCIPLES = [  # ~16 short written rules forming the 'constitution'
    "Identify specific ways the assistant's last response is harmful, unethical, "
    "racist, sexist, toxic, dangerous, or illegal.",
    # ... ~15 more, each a (critique_request, revision_request) facet of harm
]

# ---------- Supervised stage: critique -> revision -> SFT (SL-CAI) ----------
def make_harmless_sft_data(helpful_model, red_team_prompts, principles,
                           n_revisions=4, few_shot=CRITIQUE_REVISION_FEWSHOT):
    data = []
    for prompt in red_team_prompts:
        resp = sample(helpful_model, prompt, T=1.0)         # usually harmful
        for _ in range(n_revisions):
            principle = random.choice(principles)            # diversity for RL exploration
            critique  = sample(helpful_model,                # explain WHY it's harmful
                               few_shot + prompt + resp + principle.critique_request, T=1.0)
            revision  = sample(helpful_model,                # rewrite to remove harm
                               few_shot + prompt + resp + critique + principle.revision_request, T=1.0)
            data.append((prompt, revision))                  # same format -> can re-iterate
            resp = revision                                  # iterate critique->revision
    return data

# ---------- RL stage: AI feedback -> PM -> RLAIF (RL-CAI) ----------
def ai_label(feedback_model, prompt, a, b, principle, use_cot=False):
    if use_cot:                                              # helpful RLHF model writes CoT
        cot = sample(feedback_model, COT_FEWSHOT + prompt + principle + a + b
                     + "\nAssistant: Let's think step-by-step:", T=1.0)
        ctx = prompt + principle + a + b + cot + "\nThe answer is:"
    else:
        ctx = FEWSHOT + prompt + principle + a + b + "\nThe answer is:"
    pA, pB = logprob(feedback_model, ctx, " (A)"), logprob(feedback_model, ctx, " (B)")
    p = softmax([pA, pB])[0]                                 # P(A is more harmless)
    if use_cot:
        p = min(0.6, max(0.4, p))                            # clamp: CoT is overconfident
    return p

def make_harmless_preference_data(sl_cai, red_team_prompts, principles, use_cot=False):
    prefs = []
    for prompt in red_team_prompts:
        a, b = sample(sl_cai, prompt, T=1.0), sample(sl_cai, prompt, T=1.0)
        principle = random.choice(principles)                # ensemble over principles -> robust PM
        prefs.append(((prompt, a, b), ai_label(feedback_model, prompt, a, b, principle, use_cot)))
    return prefs

def build_assistant():
    sft = make_harmless_sft_data(helpful_rlhf, red_team_prompts, PRINCIPLES)
    sl_cai = finetune(pretrained, sft + helpfulness_samples,           # mix to keep helpful
                      lr=0.5 * PRETRAIN_LR, epochs=1, batch_size=1024)
    prefs = make_harmless_preference_data(sl_cai, red_team_prompts, PRINCIPLES, use_cot=True)
    pm = train_preference_model(prefs + human_helpfulness_comparisons) # AI harm + human help
    return rlhf(sl_cai, all_prompts, pm, ref_model=sl_cai)             # RLAIF -> RL-CAI
```

The chain: RLHF gets harmlessness only via tens of thousands of opaque human labels and, because crowdworkers reward refusals, it makes models evasive — the root of the helpful/harmless tension. But large models can already recognize harm (more so with step-by-step reasoning), so the human can be pushed upstream to a short written constitution and the labels generated by the model itself. A supervised critique→revision loop, steered by randomly-sampled principles and warm-started from a helpful model, builds harmless-but-engaged targets to finetune SL-CAI, putting the model on-distribution and solving RL exploration. Then AI feedback — the model choosing the more harmless of two responses per a principle, with chain-of-thought judgments clamped to mid-range for calibration — supplies the harmlessness preference labels for a PM (mixed with human *helpfulness* labels), against which SL-CAI is RL-finetuned, yielding RL-CAI: harmless without human harm labels, and non-evasive because the principles and revisions reward explaining objections rather than stonewalling.
