Let me start from what I actually want, which is to push Hilbert's program forward, not to break it. The whole edifice is supposed to rest on consistency proofs: geometry reduces to analysis, analysis to arithmetic, and if I can prove the consistency of arithmetic by finitary means, the keystone is in place. The piece I've taken on is the reduction one rung up — show that the consistency of analysis follows from the consistency of arithmetic. So I sit down to do it concretely. Analysis is about real numbers, and a real number is, up to coding, a set of naturals or a predicate of naturals; so I represent the reals by arithmetical predicates and try to verify the axioms of analysis, one by one, as statements of number theory.

And almost immediately I hit something. To *verify* the axioms of analysis — to say "this axiom holds" — I need to say that certain arithmetical sentences are *true*. Not provable: true. The verification is semantic. So what I really need is a predicate `True(x)` of number theory, holding of exactly the codes of the true arithmetical sentences, so that "axiom A holds" becomes the arithmetical statement `True(⌜A⌝)`. If I had that, the whole reduction would go through internally.

So the question narrows to a sharp one: can I define arithmetical truth inside arithmetic? Can there be a formula `True(x)`, in the language of number theory, that is satisfied by the code of a sentence exactly when that sentence is true?

Let me just try to build it and see what happens. To even pose the question I need to talk about sentences as objects. A sentence is a finite string of symbols; the symbols come from a fixed finite alphabet plus a supply of variables; so a sentence is a finite combinatorial object, and there are only countably many of them, and I can list them mechanically. In particular I can list the formulas with exactly one free number-variable in a definite order — by the sum of their symbol-codes, alphabetical within a tie, say — and call the `n`-th one `R(n)`. Substituting a numeral into the free variable is a mechanical operation on the string. All of this is bookkeeping; none of it needs anything but finite manipulation of finite strings.

Now suppose `True(x)` exists. I have a free-variable formula `True(x)`; I have an enumeration `R(n)`; I have substitution. So consider the property of a number `n`: the `n`-th one-variable formula, with `n` itself substituted into it, is *not* true. That's a perfectly good arithmetical condition on `n` — it's built from `True`, the enumeration `R`, and substitution, all of which I've just argued are arithmetically expressible if `True` is. So there's a one-variable formula expressing it; it sits somewhere in my enumeration, say it's `R(q)`. Now feed `q` to it. `R(q)` with `q` substituted says: "the `q`-th formula with `q` substituted is not true" — but the `q`-th formula with `q` substituted *is this very sentence*. So this sentence is true exactly when it is not true.

That's the Liar, reconstructed inside arithmetic. "This sentence is false." It can't be consistently true or false; if my system proves the equivalences that pin down `True`, it's outright inconsistent. So the attempt collapses: **there is no arithmetical truth predicate.** Truth in arithmetic is not arithmetically definable. The diagonal construction — enumerate, substitute a thing into its own index, negate — is doing all the work, and aimed at *truth* it is poison, exactly as Richard's antinomy already warned: Richard enumerates the definable reals and diagonalizes out of the list to "define" an undefinable real, and the contradiction is that he just defined it. Same shape, same explosion.

So I've wrecked my reduction. The natural reflex is to retreat — wall off the self-reference the way *Principia*'s type hierarchy does, forbid a formula from speaking about its own semantic status, and give up on the internal verification. But let me sit with the wreckage a moment before I retreat, because something about *which* notion exploded is bothering me.

The construction didn't blow up because self-reference is intrinsically illegal. It blew up because the predicate I plugged into the diagonal was *truth*. Stare at why the Liar is a contradiction and not merely a curiosity: it's that truth obeys `True(⌜S⌝) ↔ S` — the sentence and the claim that it's true are interchangeable, they must have the same truth value, no slack. Diagonalize against that rigid equivalence and "true iff not true" is a flat contradiction. The rigidity is the whole problem.

Now — what if I diagonalize against a predicate that is *not* truth, one without that rigid two-way grip? Look at what's actually around. The thing Hilbert's program cares about, the thing I can survey finitarily, is not truth at all; it's **provability**. And provability is a completely different animal from truth. Truth is a semantic notion about a model; I had to *assume* a predicate for it and the assumption was fatal. But "is provable" — unfold it. A proof is a finite series of formulas, each one an axiom or an immediate consequence of two earlier ones by the fixed rules, ending in the sentence in question. To check whether a given finite series *is* a proof of a given formula is a purely mechanical, finite, combinatorial test — no semantics, no model, just string-matching against the rules. "x is a proof of y" is decidable by inspection. And "y is provable" is "there exists a proof of y" — one existential quantifier over a *decidable* relation.

That's the asymmetry I need. Provability is an explicit combinatorial property of sentences, specifiable by elementary means. Truth is not. So provability *can* be mirrored inside arithmetic, where truth cannot. Let me carry the diagonal construction over to it and see whether it explodes or whether it does something else.

If I can build a sentence `G` that, run through the mirror, says "`G` is not provable" — the analogue of the Liar but with "provable" in place of "true" — what happens? I must not smuggle in the semantic assumption that every theorem is true. The usable fact has to be syntactic: if a particular number codes a proof of `G`, the represented proof relation must make the system prove the corresponding instance saying that this number is not a proof; and if the system proves the universal statement `G`, it must also prove that same instance. Then a single proof number would give a formula and its negation. That is not the Liar's "true iff not true." It is a finite contradiction extracted from a hypothetical proof. So instead of an immediate semantic explosion I expect a gap: consistency should block `G` itself, and a stronger condition may be needed to block `¬G`.

The swap has nothing to do with the philosophy of self-reference and everything to do with replacing the undefinable semantic predicate by the arithmetizable syntactic one. I have to actually build the mirror: code all syntax into numbers, prove that the syntactic relations I care about — above all "x is a proof of y" — are effective and *expressible inside the system*, and then use substitution to make the diagonal sentence.

Take the coding first. I want a one-to-one map from symbols and from finite strings of symbols to natural numbers, and I want it to be *mechanically decodable* — given the number I can recover the string by an effective operation, and conversely operations on strings become operations on numbers that stay within my effective class. Assign odd numbers to the constants: `0`↦1, `f`↦3, `¬`↦5, `∨`↦7, `∀`↦9, `(`↦11, `)`↦13. Variables of type `n` get the numbers `p^n` for primes `p > 13`, so I can read off both that it's a variable and what type it is. Now a string of symbols is a finite sequence of these codes `n₁, n₂, …, n_k`, and I need to fold a *sequence* into a single number, invertibly. The clean way is prime-power coding: send the sequence to

  2^{n₁} · 3^{n₂} · 5^{n₃} · … · p_k^{n_k},

with `p_k` the `k`-th prime. Unique factorization is exactly the guarantee I want: the number determines the exponents, the exponents are the terms, in order. Decoding is "read the exponent of the `i`-th prime," which is an effective operation. Why primes and not, say, just writing the digits in some base? Because I need the *length* to be recoverable and I need the individual terms extractable by effective functions, and unique factorization hands me both for free. A proof is one level up — a finite series of formulas, i.e. a finite series of *numbers* — so I code it the same way again, a number whose `i`-th exponent is the code of the `i`-th formula. Sequences of sequences, prime-coded twice. Now every formula is a number and every proof is a number.

With every piece of syntax now a number, every metamathematical notion becomes a numerical relation, and I have to check that the ones I need are *effective* — primitive recursive — because that's what will let me express them in the system. Let me build them up, each from earlier ones, staying inside the closure rules (composition, the recursion scheme, bounded quantifiers and bounded search — all of which preserve primitive recursiveness). I'll just walk the ladder.

Divisibility `x/y` is `∃z ≤ x (x = y·z)` — bounded existential over a primitive recursive matrix, so primitive recursive. `Prim(x)`, x is prime, is "x>1 and no `z` with `1<z<x` divides it" — again bounded. `n Pr x`, the `n`-th prime factor of `x`, by bounded least-search; `Pr(n)`, the `n`-th prime outright, by recursion with the bound `Pr(n)! + 1` (there's always a prime in that gap). `n Gl x`, the `n`-th term of the sequence coded by `x`, is the exponent of the `n`-th prime in `x` — got by bounded search for the largest power dividing. `l(x)`, the length, is the index of the largest prime that divides `x`. Concatenation `x * y` of two coded sequences, by the bounded specification that its first `l(x)` terms match `x` and the next `l(y)` match `y`. From these I get the syntactic *builders*: `R(x) = 2^x`, the one-term sequence; `E(x)`, bracketing, as `R(11) * x * R(13)`; `Neg(x) = R(5) * E(x)`; `x Dis y = E(x) * R(7) * E(y)`; `x Gen y = R(x) * R(9) * E(y)`, the generalization `∀x(y)`. And the numeral function — this one I have to be careful about — `Z(n)` is the *string* `f f … f 0` with `n` copies of `f`, defined by recursion: `Z(0) = R(1)` (the symbol `0`), `Z(n+1) = R(3) * Z(n)`. I want to flag why `Z` matters so much: I will need to feed a *number* into a formula, but I can't literally drop a number into a string — I have to drop in the *name* of the number, the numeral `Z(n)`, which is a genuine string. Keeping the number `n` and its numeral `Z(n)` distinct is going to be the hinge of the diagonal trick.

Climb further. `Var`, `Typ_n`, `Elf` (elementary formula `a(b)`), and then `Form(x)`, "x is a formula": x is a formula iff there's a construction-sequence — a series of strings, each elementary or got from earlier ones by `Neg`, `Dis`, `Gen` — ending in x. That "there exists a construction sequence" looks unbounded, but it isn't: the sequence need be no longer than, and its entries no bigger than, what `x` itself bounds, so the existential is bounded and `Form` stays primitive recursive. The free/bound machinery (`v Geb n,x`, `v Fr n,x`, the count of free occurrences) is bounded bookkeeping. And then **substitution** `Sb(x; v/y)` — replace the free occurrences of variable `v` in formula `x` by the string `y` — defined by recursion over the occurrence-places, each replacement a concatenation surgery; primitive recursive. Substitution is the operation the diagonal will live on.

Now the axioms and the proof relation. `Ax(x)`: x is one of the axioms — the Peano axioms have fixed codes; the propositional, quantifier, reducibility, and extensionality schemata are recognized by bounded existential checks ("x results from schema II-1 by substituting some formula", etc.). `Fl(x,y,z)`: x is an immediate consequence of y and z — either `y = z Imp x` (modus ponens) or x is `v Gen y` (generalization). `Bw(x)`: x codes a proof-schema — a series of formulas each of which is an axiom or an immediate consequence of two earlier ones — again a bounded condition, primitive recursive. And finally

  `x B y`  ≡  `Bw(x) & [l(x)] Gl x = y`,

"x is a proof of y": x is a proof-schema whose last formula is y. **This relation is primitive recursive** — decidable by inspecting the finite object x. That is the whole payoff of the coding: the relation "x proves y," which began life as an external metamathematical notion, is now a decidable arithmetical relation.

Then, and only then, provability:

  `Bew(y)`  ≡  `∃x (x B y)`.

One existential quantifier over the decidable proof-relation. And here is the asymmetry made concrete: of every relation I've built, `Bew` is the *only* one I can't claim is primitive recursive — it has that unbounded `∃x` with no bound on the size of a proof. Provability is merely semi-decidable, the existential closure of a decidable relation. That is exactly right, and exactly the contrast with truth: truth I couldn't define at all; provability I can define, but it sits one quantifier up, as `∃ proof`. The gap between "decidable proof-relation" and "the existential over it" is going to be where incompleteness lives.

Now the bridge I still owe: I've shown these relations are *effective*, but I need them *expressible inside the system* — I need that a true numerical fact about them turns into a *provable formula* of the system. This is the load-bearing lemma. The claim is: for every primitive recursive relation `R(x₁,…,x_n)` there is a formula `r` of the system, with free variables `u₁,…,u_n`, that *numeralwise represents* it:

  if `R(x⃗)` holds, then `⊢ Sb(r; u⃗/Z(x⃗))`,
  if `R(x⃗)` fails, then `⊢ Neg Sb(r; u⃗/Z(x⃗))`.

In words: substitute the *numerals* of the actual numbers into `r`, and the system proves the resulting closed formula when `R` holds and proves its negation when `R` fails. Why must this hold? Because primitive recursive means decidable *step by step from the axioms*: the value of a primitive recursive function on given numerals can be computed, and each computation step — a composition, a successor, an instance of the recursion equations — is mirrored by an instance of the system's arithmetical axioms, which the system proves. So I prove it by induction on the build-up (the "degree") of the defining function. For the base — constants and successor — the representing formula is immediate. For the inductive step, a function got by composition or by the recursion scheme from lower-degree functions: the representing formulas for the parts exist by hypothesis, and the operations of composition and of primitive recursion can themselves be carried out as *formal* derivations in the system (the recursion equations are provable, induction lets me chain them), so I assemble a representing formula for the whole from those of the parts and prove the two implications. Every recursive relation, in particular `x B y` and everything built from it, is representable. That's the bridge: arithmetic *about proofs* can now be carried on *inside* the system, with truths about the proof-relation becoming theorems.

Now the diagonal sentence. I want a closed formula that, read through the mirror, says "I am unprovable." The truth version exploded; the provability version, I argued, should merely fall into a gap. Let me build it honestly, with the substitution-on-its-own-code trick that the Liar construction was secretly using.

Fix the system's provability machinery; write `Bew_c`, `x B_c y` for provability and the proof-relation relative to a recursively given class `c` of formulas (the bare axioms are the case where `c` is empty — I keep `c` general so the result covers any system got by adding a recursive set of axioms). Consider the relation

  `Q(x, y)`  ≡  `¬ ( x B_c Sb(y; 19/Z(y)) )`.

Read it: "x is not a proof of the formula you get by taking the formula coded `y` and substituting into its free variable (variable number 19) the *numeral of y itself*." The inner `Sb(y; 19/Z(y))` is the diagonal operation — take a formula's own code and feed it back into the formula. Because `c` is recursive, both `x B_c (·)` and `Sb(y; 19/Z(y))` are primitive recursive, so `Q` is, and the representability lemma gives a formula `q`, with free variables 17 and 19. Since every theorem of the base system is also a theorem after adding `c`, the two represented alternatives are:

  `¬(x B_c Sb(y; 19/Z(y)))` ⟹ `⊢_c Sb(q; 17/Z(x), 19/Z(y))`,    (call it 9)
  ` (x B_c Sb(y; 19/Z(y)))` ⟹ `⊢_c Neg Sb(q; 17/Z(x), 19/Z(y))`.  (call it 10)

Now the self-application. Generalize `q` on the variable 17 and call the result

  `p = 17 Gen q`,

a formula with the single free variable 19. Its content is "for all x, ¬(x is a proof of `Sb(y; 19/Z(y))`)," i.e. "`Sb(y; 19/Z(y))` is unprovable," still with `y` free as variable 19. Then substitute `p`'s own numeral into `p`'s free variable. Define

  `r = Sb(q; 19/Z(p))`

and look at `17 Gen r`. Watch the codes line up. Substituting the numeral of `p` into `p = 17 Gen q`, and using that substitution and generalization on *different* variables commute,

  `Sb(p; 19/Z(p)) = Sb(17 Gen q; 19/Z(p)) = 17 Gen Sb(q; 19/Z(p)) = 17 Gen r`.    (call it 13)

So `17 Gen r` is *exactly* the formula obtained by feeding `p` its own numeral. And `p` said "`Sb(y; 19/Z(y))` is unprovable"; feeding `y := p` makes it say "`Sb(p; 19/Z(p))` is unprovable" — which by (13) is "`17 Gen r` is unprovable." The formula `17 Gen r` asserts its own unprovability. There's nothing circular in how it was built — it started life as an assertion about the unprovability of a perfectly determinate formula, namely `Sb(y;19/Z(y))` for a fixed `y`, and only after I computed the codes did it turn out that the determinate formula in question is the assertion itself. The self-reference is an arithmetical accident of the coding, achieved entirely by mechanical substitution, never by the formula "pointing at itself."

Also record the substitution identity I'll lean on: substituting `Z(p)` for variable 19 inside the two implications and using `r = Sb(q;19/Z(p))`,

  `Sb(q; 17/Z(x), 19/Z(p)) = Sb(r; 17/Z(x))`,    (call it 14)

so plugging `y := p` into (9) and (10) and rewriting by (13),(14):

  `¬( x B_c (17 Gen r) )` ⟹ `⊢_c Sb(r; 17/Z(x))`,    (15)
  ` ( x B_c (17 Gen r) )` ⟹ `⊢_c Neg Sb(r; 17/Z(x))`.   (16)

These two are the engine. (15) says: if `x` is *not* a proof of `17 Gen r`, the system with the added axioms `c` proves the `x`-th instance `Sb(r; 17/Z(x))`. (16) says: if `x` *is* a proof of `17 Gen r`, that same system proves the *negation* of that instance.

Now I read off whether `17 Gen r` is decidable. First direction — **is `17 Gen r` provable?** Suppose it is. Then some number `n` codes a `c`-proof of it, `n B_c (17 Gen r)`. By (16), `⊢_c Neg Sb(r; 17/Z(n))`. But `17 Gen r` is `∀(17) r`, and if the system proves a universally quantified statement it proves each numeral instance, so `⊢_c Sb(r; 17/Z(n))` as well. The system proves a formula and its negation — it is inconsistent. So **if the system is consistent, `17 Gen r` is not `c`-provable.** This direction used nothing but plain consistency. Read in the standard numbers, the sentence is then true exactly because no proof number exists, but the formal argument did not assume soundness.

Second direction — **is the negation `Neg(17 Gen r)` provable?** Here I have to be more careful, and this is where plain consistency stops being enough. Suppose `Neg(17 Gen r)` is `c`-provable. Since ω-consistency implies ordinary consistency, the first direction gives that `17 Gen r` is not `c`-provable, so *no* number codes a `c`-proof of it: for every `n`, `¬(n B_c (17 Gen r))`. Apply (15) to each `n`: for every `n`, `⊢_c Sb(r; 17/Z(n))`. So the system proves the instance `Sb(r;17/Z(n))` for *every individual numeral* `n`. And simultaneously, by assumption, it proves `Neg(17 Gen r)`, i.e. it proves the negation of `∀(17) r`. Now — is that a contradiction? Not by plain consistency. Proving each instance `φ(0̄), φ(1̄), φ(2̄), …` and *also* proving `¬∀x φ(x)` is not a formal contradiction, because no single derivation combines infinitely many instances into the universal. A system could consistently prove every numeral-instance of `φ` and yet prove `¬∀x φ(x)` — it would be asserting a counterexample that is not any standard numeral. Plain consistency does not forbid that. What forbids it is **ω-consistency** — there is no formula `a` such that the system proves `Sb(a; v/Z(n))` for every `n` *and also* proves `Neg(v Gen a)`. ω-consistency is exactly "you can't prove every instance and also deny the universal." Under it, the configuration I derived — every instance `Sb(r;17/Z(n))` provable, and `Neg(17 Gen r)` provable — is precisely forbidden, taking `a := r`, `v := 17`. So **if the system is ω-consistent, `Neg(17 Gen r)` is not `c`-provable.**

Both directions together: for an ω-consistent system in which the axioms are recursively specified and every recursive relation is representable, the formula `17 Gen r` is *undecidable* — neither it nor its negation is provable. That is the first incompleteness theorem. And the sentence is exhibited effectively, from the system's own description; the proof is constructive — every existence claim in it traces back to the representability lemma, which is itself constructive.

I want to make sure I understand *why* the asymmetry between the two directions, because it's telling me something about the system, not just about my proof. The first direction is about `17 Gen r` itself, a universal statement; refuting its provability only needs consistency because the argument runs through a single hypothetical proof and its single witness `n`. The second direction is about the *negation*, an existential `∃x ¬r(x)`; to refute *its* provability I have to rule out the system asserting a phantom witness, and plain consistency can't, because a witness is an unbounded object the finite proof never has to display. That's why I needed ω-consistency, and that it really is needed and not an artifact: take a consistent system and *add* `Neg(17 Gen r)` to it as a new axiom. The result is still consistent — if it weren't, `17 Gen r` would be provable in the original, which it isn't. But it is *not* ω-consistent, because in it (by 15) every instance `Sb(r;17/Z(n))` is still provable while now `Neg(17 Gen r)` is an axiom. So consistent-but-not-ω-consistent systems exist, and in them my second direction genuinely fails — `Neg(17 Gen r)` becomes provable even though `17 Gen r` is not. The hypothesis is doing real work; I can't drop it for free.

If I keep *only* plain consistency, I don't get an undecidable *sentence*, but I get something almost as striking: a primitive recursive property `r` such that every instance `Sb(r;17/Z(n))` is provable, and, assuming consistency, none of those instances is refutable, yet `∀(17) r` is not provable. The system verifies a property of every individual numeral but cannot prove it holds of all numbers.

Let me make sure the undecidable sentence is as concrete and arithmetical as the strongest form of the claim demands, because if it lives only up in the type hierarchy it's less damning. The sentence is `17 Gen r` with `r` representing the primitive recursive relation `Q`; I want it to be a statement purely about whole numbers, built from `+`, `·`, `=`, `¬`, `∨`, and quantifiers over naturals. So I show **every primitive recursive relation is arithmetical** in that narrow sense. Induction on degree again. Composition is handled by an existential over the intermediate values. The only real obstacle is the recursion scheme: `Φ(k+1,…) = μ(k, Φ(k,…),…)` refers to the whole prior course of values `Φ(0),…,Φ(k)`, and "there is a finite sequence with these values" naively needs a quantifier over sequences, which is *not* first-order arithmetical. I get around it by coding a finite sequence with `+` and `·` alone. Given any finite sequence `f₀,…,f_{k-1}`, I can find a pair of numbers `(n, d)` such that the residues `n mod (1+(i+1)d)` reproduce `f_i` for `i = 0,…,k-1`: take `d = l!` where `l` exceeds `k` and all the `f_i`, and solve the congruences. The moduli `1+(i+1)d` are pairwise coprime: any prime divisor of one of them is coprime to `d`, since the modulus is `1` modulo every prime dividing `d`; if a prime divided two moduli, it would divide their difference `(i-j)d`, hence, being coprime to `d`, would divide `i-j`; but the prime must be larger than `l`, while `|i-j| < l`. So the Chinese remainder theorem delivers `n`. Now "there exists a sequence with the recursion property" becomes "there exist numbers `n, d` such that the residues `n mod (1+(i+1)d)` satisfy the recursion step for each `i < k`," and the residue relation `x = n mod m` is just `∃z(n = z·m + x) & x < m` — pure `+`, `·`, `<`. So the recursion is expressed with a single pair of number-quantifiers and the arithmetical operations. Every primitive recursive relation is arithmetical; the undecidable sentence is a genuine assertion `∀x F(x)` about ordinary natural numbers with `F` decidable — the simplest interesting logical form. Under the same ω-consistency hypothesis, even the arithmetic of `+` and `·` is incomplete, and so is any system that contains it: set theory, the *Principia* system, Peano arithmetic. The conclusion isn't about exotic objects; it's about the integers.

Now the consequence for Hilbert's keystone — consistency. This is where I go back and look hard at the first direction of the incompleteness proof, because it used *only* consistency. What it established is, schematically,

  `Wid(c)` ⟹ `¬ Bew_c(17 Gen r)`,    (23)

"if `c` is consistent then `17 Gen r` is not provable." Unfold the right side via the proof-relation: `Wid(c) ⟹ (∀x)¬(x B_c (17 Gen r))`, and using (13) and the definition of `Q`,

  `Wid(c)` ⟹ `(∀x) Q(x, p)`.    (24)

Everything I did from the definition of "recursive" up through the first direction of the incompleteness theorem — the coding, the closure lemmas, the representability lemma, the case analysis — used nothing but ordinary, finitary, classical reasoning of the kind the system itself formalizes. So this whole argument can be *carried out inside the system*. In particular: `c` is definable in the system; let `w` be the formula that expresses the system's own consistency. The natural way to express consistency is "some formula is unprovable" — `Wid(c) ≡ ∃x(Form(x) & ¬Bew_c(x))` — since a system is inconsistent exactly when it proves everything. The relation `Q(x,y)` is expressed by `q`, so `Q(x,p)` is expressed by `r` (because `r = Sb(q;19/Z(p))`), and the universal `(∀x)Q(x,p)` is expressed by `17 Gen r`. The implication (24), being a theorem of the metatheory and the metatheory being formalizable, becomes a *formal* theorem:

  `⊢_P w Imp (17 Gen r)`,

and therefore this implication is also available in the extension by `c`. If `c` could prove `w` — its own consistency statement — then by this implication it would prove `17 Gen r`. But by the first direction, if `c` is consistent it does *not* prove `17 Gen r`. So a consistent `c` cannot prove `w`: **the consistency of the system is not provable within the system.** Taking `c` empty, the bare axioms cannot prove their own consistency; the same word-for-word argument applies to set theory and to the system of classical mathematics.

I should be exact about what this does and does not say against Hilbert, because it's tempting to overstate it. It does not produce a contradiction in his program; it says the consistency formula `w` is unprovable *in the system itself*. Hilbert wanted a consistency proof by *finitary* means, and it is conceivable that there are finitary arguments not formalizable in this particular system — so the result refutes the program only to the extent that the program insisted the consistency proof be formalizable internally. What it does kill, cleanly, is the hope that a single system rich enough for arithmetic could certify its own consistency, and with the first theorem, the hope that an ω-consistent such system could be complete. The keystone cannot be set from inside the arch.

Let me lay the causal chain end to end, because the path I took was nothing like the order a clean statement of the result would follow. I wanted to verify the analysis axioms inside arithmetic, which needed an arithmetical truth predicate; the diagonal construction showed no such predicate can exist, because it reproduces the Liar and forces inconsistency — truth is undefinable. Staring at *why* it exploded, the culprit was the rigid `True(⌜S⌝)↔S` equivalence, not self-reference as such. Provability is unlike truth in exactly the right way: a proof is a finite checkable object, so "x proves y" is decidable and "y is provable" is one existential over it — arithmetizable where truth was not. So I mirrored the syntax into arithmetic by numbering symbols and strings via prime powers, showed every syntactic relation I needed (formula, axiom, immediate consequence, "x is a proof of y") is primitive recursive, and proved that every primitive recursive relation is *representable* — true facts about the proof-relation become provable formulas. Then I ran the diagonal against *provability* instead of truth, building `17 Gen r` that asserts its own unprovability by substituting a formula's own numeral into itself. Consistency alone makes it unprovable (a true sentence the system misses, not a contradiction); ω-consistency makes its negation unprovable too — so it is undecidable, and it is a statement about plain integers. Finally, because the consistency-implies-unprovable-`G` argument is itself formalizable, the system that could prove its own consistency could prove `G`, which it can't — so it cannot prove its own consistency. One swap, truth for provability, turned a paradox that destroyed my reduction into two limits: an ω-consistent system strong enough for arithmetic has an undecidable arithmetical sentence, and a consistent one cannot certify its own consistency from within.
