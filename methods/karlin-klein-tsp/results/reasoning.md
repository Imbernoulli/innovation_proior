OK, let me think this through from scratch. The thing I keep circling back to is why Christofides' algorithm has refused to budge for four decades, because if I understand *exactly* where the $\tfrac32$ comes from, I'll know exactly what would have to change to beat it. The algorithm is almost insultingly simple: take a minimum spanning tree, then put a minimum-cost perfect matching on its odd-degree vertices, then shortcut. The tree costs at most $\mathrm{OPT}$ — delete any edge from the optimal tour and you have a spanning path, a tree, so the cheapest tree is no more expensive. And the matching costs at most $\tfrac12\mathrm{OPT}$: the optimal tour shortcut onto the odd set $O$ is a cycle on $O$, that cycle splits into two perfect matchings, the cheaper one is at most half the cycle which is at most half the tour. Add them: $\mathrm{OPT}+\tfrac12\mathrm{OPT}=\tfrac32\mathrm{OPT}$.

So where is the looseness? I want there to be looseness, somewhere I can squeeze. Let me look at the tree bound — no, that's basically tight and there's nothing clever to do, the tree just costs what it costs. The matching bound then. Is $c(M)\le\tfrac12\mathrm{OPT}$ tight? Take the unit-weight gadget: a long ladder-like graph where the minimum spanning tree is forced (make its edges a hair cheaper) and it comes out with *every single vertex* of odd degree. Then $O$ is everything, the cheapest matching on everything costs about half the tour, and the total marches up to $\tfrac32\mathrm{OPT}-o(1)$. So it's a genuine equality. The $\tfrac32$ is not a sloppy analysis I can tighten by being careful; it's a real floor for "pick one tree, charge its matching against one optimal tour."

Stare at *why* the gadget is bad. It's bad because the one tree we picked has all its vertices odd. The adversary got to see which tree we'd take and built the instance so that *that* tree is terrible. And here's the thing that nags at me: it's easy to design a graph where the minimum spanning tree has an expensive matching. But could the adversary design a graph where *many different* spanning trees *simultaneously* have expensive matchings? That feels much harder. The badness in the gadget is a property of one specific tree. If I didn't commit to a single tree — if I sampled a tree from some broad distribution — then maybe the adversary can't make *all* of them bad at once, and on average the matching would be cheaper. That's the crack. Not "be cleverer about the one tree," but "don't pick one tree."

So: randomize. But not for its own sake — I need a distribution where two things hold. First, the tree side stays free: I need $\mathbb{E}[c(T)]\le\mathrm{OPT}$. Second, the matching side has to actually drop below $\tfrac12\mathrm{OPT}$ in expectation. The first one tells me almost everything about which distribution to use. If I want $\mathbb{E}[c(T)]=\sum_e c_e\Pr[e\in T]$ to be controlled by the LP, I should make the *marginals* of my distribution equal to a good fractional solution. So let me solve the natural relaxation — the subtour LP, $x(\delta(v))=2$, $x(\delta(S))\ge 2$, $x\ge0$ — get its optimum $x$, whose cost is at most $\mathrm{OPT}$, and demand a distribution over spanning trees with $\Pr[e\in T]=x_e$ for every edge. Then $\mathbb{E}[c(T)]=c(x)\le\mathrm{OPT}$, automatically. (One technical normalization: split a vertex into $u_0,v_0$ across a weight-1, cost-0 edge $e_0$; then $x$ restricted to the rest lies in the spanning-tree polytope, so it really is a convex combination of trees and such a distribution exists.)

Now, marginals $x_e$ don't pin down the distribution — there are infinitely many distributions with those marginals. Which one? I want the matching to be cheap, and "cheap matching" is going to come from the odd set $O$ being *typically nice* rather than *worst-case bad*. So I want a distribution that spreads its mass as widely as possible over trees, so the odd set varies a lot and no single bad pattern dominates. The maximally-spread distribution subject to fixed marginals is the maximum-entropy one. And there's a beautiful fact: the max-entropy distribution with given spanning-tree marginals has the product form $\Pr[T]\propto\prod_{e\in T}\lambda_e$ for some edge weights $\lambda_e\ge0$ — a $\lambda$-uniform distribution — and $\lambda$ achieving marginals within $(1\pm2^{-n})$ can be found in polynomial time. Good, it's computable.

But "spread out" is a vibe, not a proof. I need leverage. Here's where I get lucky, or rather where the structure pays off: $\lambda$-uniform spanning-tree distributions are *strongly Rayleigh* — their generating polynomial $\sum_T \Pr[T]\prod_{e\in T}z_e$ is real stable, never zero when all the $z_e$ have positive imaginary part. That single analytic fact, due to Borcea–Brändén–Liggett, drags in an entire toolbox: negative association (an upward event on one set of edges can only push down marginals on a disjoint set), stochastic dominance, log-concave rank sequences, and closure under conditioning, projection, truncation, products. The one I expect to lean on hardest: if I condition on a vertex set $S$ being internally spanned by a tree, the sample splits into two *independent* trees, one inside $S$ and one on the contraction $G/S$, each with the inherited marginals. That independence-across-a-tight-cut is the kind of handle that lets me actually compute parities of cut crossings.

Does randomization actually buy anything, or is "spread the mass" just a vibe? Each vertex $v$ has $x(\delta(v))=2$, so the number of tree edges at $v$ is distributed as a sum of independent Bernoullis with mean $2$ (the rank sequence of an SR distribution behaves like independent Bernoullis). The Bernoulli-sum parity bound gives $\Pr[\deg_T(v)\text{ even}]\ge\tfrac12(1-e^{-2})\approx0.43$. So a typical vertex is even with constant probability, meaning a constant fraction of vertices are *not* in $O$. In the all-odd gadget that wrecked Christofides, the deterministic MST had every vertex odd; the random tree does not. That's encouraging — but I have to be honest that it's only encouragement. Even if every vertex were even with probability $1-o(1)$ independently, that wouldn't beat $\tfrac32$ on the cycle graph: two odd vertices at distance $\tfrac12\mathrm{OPT}$ already give a $\tfrac32$ matching. So "many even vertices" is hope, not a theorem. The real work is elsewhere.

Where? Let me reframe the matching cost the way the O-join polytope wants it. The minimum matching on $O$ equals the minimum of $c(y)$ over $y\ge0$ with $y(\delta(S))\ge1$ for every $S$ whose intersection with $O$ is odd — equivalently every $S$ crossed an odd number of times by the tree. The classical bound just sets $y_e=x_e/2$: since $x(\delta(S))\ge2$ on every cut, $y(\delta(S))\ge1$ always, cost $c(x)/2\le\tfrac12\mathrm{OPT}$. Now here's the lever I've been hunting for: a cut's constraint *only binds when the tree crosses it an odd number of times*. If $\delta(S)_T$ is even, I owe that cut nothing — I can let $y(\delta(S))$ be as small as I like. So every cut that happens to be even in my sampled tree is a place where I overpaid by setting $y_e=x_e/2$. The savings I want is exactly the cost I can claw back on edges that sit on cuts that turned out even.

This is more or less what was done for graphic TSP: find edges that lie *exclusively* on even near-min cuts and push their $y_e$ below $x_e/2$. But that stalls for general metrics, and I can see why immediately: a single expensive edge can cross *many* cuts, and the chance that *all* of them are simultaneously even can be exponentially small. So I can't demand "all cuts on $e$ are even." I need a local, amortized scheme.

Let me set it up as a perturbation. Start from $y_e=x_e/2$ and add a random *slack vector* $s$ (random because it depends on the sampled tree): the final O-join is $y_e=x_e/2+s_e$. The plan: when a few special cuts that $e$ is on come out even, I *reduce* $s_e$ to $-\beta x_e$ for a small constant $\beta$, banking the savings; and when some cut $e$ is on comes out odd and edges crossing it got reduced, I *increase* $s_e$ on other edges of that cut to repair the constraint. I win if I can guarantee $\mathbb{E}[s_e]<-\varepsilon\beta x_e$ for every edge — strictly negative on average — because then $\mathbb{E}[c(y)]=c(x)/2+\mathbb{E}[c(s)]<\tfrac12\mathrm{OPT}$.

First a cheap but vital observation that fences off most cuts. If I keep $\beta$ small compared to how far a cut is from minimum, the reduction can't possibly break that cut. Concretely, call $S$ an $\eta$-near-min cut if $x(\delta(S))\le 2+\eta$. For a cut with $x(\delta(S))>2(1+\eta)$, even if *every* edge crossing it is reduced by its full $\beta x_e$ and none is increased, the value drops by at most $\beta\,x(\delta(S))$, and if I choose $\beta\le\eta/4.1$ the cut still clears $y(\delta(S))\ge1$. So cuts that are more than a constant-$\eta$ distance from minimum take care of themselves. The entire game is confined to the near-minimum regime — the cuts barely above the connectivity threshold. That's a huge reduction: those are exactly the cuts the cactus/polygon machinery understands.

Now let me actually try to prove $\mathbb{E}[s_e]<0$ in the cleanest possible setting and see whether the numbers close, because if they don't even close in the easy case there's no point going further. Take a tight cut $S$ with $x(\delta(S))=2$, and inside it two children $u,v$ with an edge bundle $\mathbf e=(u,v)$ between them. Reduce $\mathbf e$ — set $s_{\mathbf e}=-\eta x_{\mathbf e}$ — exactly when both $\delta(u)_T$ and $\delta(v)_T$ are even; call that the reduction event, and say it happens with probability $p$. When it doesn't, say because $\delta(u)_T$ is odd, the cut $\delta(u)$ might be left unsatisfied if some edges going *higher* out of $u$ (edges in $\delta(u)\cap\delta(S)$) were themselves reduced. So $\mathbf e$ has to be ready to compensate: I'll make it responsible for an $m_{\mathbf e,u}$ fraction of the reductions on each higher edge $g$ out of $u$, increasing $s_{\mathbf e}$ to cover them when $\delta(u)_T$ is odd. Writing it out,
$$\mathbb{E}[s_{\mathbf e}]=-p\,\eta\,x_{\mathbf e}\;+\;m_{\mathbf e,u}\!\!\sum_{g\in\delta^\uparrow(u)}\!\!\Pr[\delta(u)_T\text{ odd}\mid g\text{ reduced}]\,\Pr[g\text{ reduced}]\,\eta\,\tfrac{x_g}{x(\delta^\uparrow(u))}\;+\;(\text{same for }v).$$
The reduction term is clean. Everything hinges on that conditional probability $\Pr[\delta(u)_T\text{ odd}\mid g\text{ reduced}]$. If it were $1$, the increase would exactly cancel the reduction and I'd gain nothing. I need it bounded *below* $1$.

Here's where the tight cut earns its keep. Because $x(\delta(S))=2$, conditioning on $S$ being a tree (which is essentially always, by near-min-cut concentration) makes the tree inside $S$ independent of the tree outside. The higher edges $g$ live in $\delta(S)$; the parity of $\delta(u)_T$ splits into the higher part $\delta^\uparrow(u)_T=\delta(u)\cap\delta(S)$ and the sideways part $\delta^\rightarrow(u)_T=\delta(u)\setminus\delta(S)$, and the sideways part is sampled *inside* $S$, independently of whatever happened to $g$ up in $\delta(S)$. So
$$\Pr[\delta(u)_T\text{ even}\mid g\text{ reduced}]\ge \min\big(\Pr[\delta^\rightarrow(u)_T\text{ even}],\,\Pr[\delta^\rightarrow(u)_T\text{ odd}]\big),$$
because whatever parity the higher edges land in, the sideways part has an independent shot at matching it. Now $\delta^\rightarrow(u)_T$ is a sum of independent Bernoullis; if $x_u:=x(\delta^\uparrow(u))\in[\varepsilon,1-\varepsilon]$ then since $x(\delta(u))=2$ the sideways mean sits in $[1+\varepsilon,2-\varepsilon]$, and a Bernoulli sum with mean bounded away from the integers is both even and odd with probability $\Omega(\varepsilon)$. So $\Pr[\delta(u)_T\text{ odd}\mid g\text{ reduced}]\le 1-\Omega(\varepsilon)$ — bounded away from $1$, which is exactly what the argument needs.

Feed that back, arrange the construction so every edge is reduced with the *same* probability $p$, and pick the matching fractions so that $m_{\mathbf e,u}+m_{\mathbf e,v}=x_{\mathbf e}$. Then
$$\mathbb{E}[s_{\mathbf e}]\le -p\eta x_{\mathbf e}+(1-\varepsilon)p\eta\,(m_{\mathbf e,u}+m_{\mathbf e,v})=-p\eta x_{\mathbf e}+(1-\varepsilon)p\eta x_{\mathbf e}=-\varepsilon p\eta x_{\mathbf e}<0.$$
It closes. The expected reduction beats the expected increase by exactly the slack $\varepsilon$ that negative dependence handed me. If I could prove this inequality for *every* edge, the matching's expected cost would be $(\tfrac12-\varepsilon)\mathrm{OPT}$ and I'd be done.

Of course I can't, yet — that derivation quietly assumed four things, and each one breaks somewhere. Let me hunt them down.

First crack: I assumed every edge is *good*, meaning its two top cuts $\delta(u),\delta(v)$ are even *simultaneously* with constant probability, so that reduction event has $p>0$. Is that always true? No. Picture an LP solution where two half-edges $(a,b),(c,d)$ are forced to be completely negatively correlated — exactly one of them is in any tree — and they're independent of everything else. Then their top cuts are locked into opposite parities and never even together; that edge is *bad*. Stare at when this can happen: it forces the edge to have fractional value near $\tfrac12$. A bad edge must be a *half-edge*, $x_{\mathbf e}\in\tfrac12\pm\varepsilon_{1/2}$. And bad half-edges are rare in a strong sense — of any two half-edge bundles sharing an endpoint in the hierarchy, at least one is good, and in any cut at least $\tfrac34$ of the fractional mass is good. (It may be no accident that half-edges are the troublemakers: half-integral LP solutions are conjectured to be the very hardest TSP instances to round.) So my fix is blunt: *never reduce a bad edge.* But that costs me twice — I have to make sure bad edges don't carry most of the LP cost (the $\tfrac34$-good-mass guarantee saves me), and since I won't reduce them I also can't increase them, so the burden of repairing odd cuts falls on fewer edges; I push for *non-positive* slack on bad edges and strictly negative on good ones, and the matching that distributes compensation only routes through good edges.

Second crack, and this one is sharper: I only looked at *top* edges — edges whose lowest common cut is a plain degree cut. What about edges deep inside the cut structure? To even talk about those I need to understand how the near-min cuts are organized, because they cross each other and I can't handle a tangle of crossing cuts by the local argument above. So let me build the organizing structure.

I don't want to wrestle with arbitrary near-min cuts of $x$; they can cross in complicated ways. Trick: average $x$ with the optimal tour itself. Set $z=(x+\mathrm{OPT})/2$. In $\mathrm{OPT}$, which is a Hamiltonian cycle, every minimum cut has value exactly $2$ and *every* non-min cut has value at least $4$. So averaging forces any $\eta$-near-min cut of $z$ to be a *contiguous interval of vertices along the $\mathrm{OPT}$ cycle* — anything that isn't an interval picks up extra crossings from $\mathrm{OPT}$ and is no longer near-minimum. (The cost: I'm now allowed to add slack on $\mathrm{OPT}$ edges too, a vector $s^*\ge0$, which is why this proves only an algorithmic bound and not a bound on the LP's integrality gap.) Arrange the cycle around a circle; identify each cut with the arc not containing $e_0$. Some cuts are crossed on both sides (left *and* right). For those, take the two crossing cuts of minimum overlap; each of the two "one edge between near-min cuts" events fails with probability only $O(\eta)$, so I charge those two failures to the two $\mathrm{OPT}$ edges in $\delta(S)$, paying $O(\eta^2)$ per $\mathrm{OPT}$ edge in expectation. That dispatches all both-sides-crossed cuts and leaves only $O(n)$ cuts to genuinely satisfy.

The cuts crossed on at most one side split into connected components of crossing cuts; each component is a *polygon*. And the polygons look like cycles: the atoms $a_0,a_1,\dots,a_{m-1}$ satisfy $x(E(a_i,a_{i+1}))\approx1$ and $x(\delta(a_i))\approx2$, with every cut a union of consecutive atoms. The reason is that the one-side-crossed cuts partition into two laminar families — those crossed on the left and those crossed on the right — neither of which self-crosses, so the whole interaction is just left-versus-right and forces the cycle structure. Once a polygon looks like a cycle, almost every internal cut is even with probability $1$ (if $x(E(a_i,a_{i+1}))=1$ and the neighboring degrees are exactly $2$, then exactly one tree edge crosses between consecutive atoms). The only cuts whose parity is in doubt are the ones touching the two ends $a_1$ and $a_{m-1}$. So I can *pretend every polygon is a triangle*: collapse it to a single triangle cut $a_1\cup\dots\cup a_{m-1}$ with two "sides" $A=E(a_0,a_1)$ and $B=E(a_0,a_{m-1})$, and call the triangle *happy* when $A_T$ and $B_T$ are both odd — because then all three of $\delta(a_1),\delta(a_{m-1}),\delta(\text{whole})$ are even, exactly the clean condition I want. The full structure I'll work with is a *hierarchy* $\mathcal H$: a laminar family of degree cuts (not crossed at all) and triangle cuts (one per polygon). Build a good slack vector on $\mathcal H$ and the leftover cuts are mopped up by $s^*$ on $\mathrm{OPT}$ edges at $O(\eta^2)$ each.

Now the third crack appears, inside the triangles. Consider a *bottom* edge $\mathbf f=(a_1,a_2)$ living inside a triangle, with $x_{\mathbf f}\approx1$. I want to reduce $s_{\mathbf f}$ when the triangle is happy. But when it's *not* happy, $\mathbf f$ may have to repair both $\delta(a_1)$ and $\delta(a_2)$ against reductions on the sides $A,B$ going higher — and since $x_{\mathbf f}=x(A)=x(B)=1$, it could be forced to compensate at *twice* its reduction rate. That would make $\mathbb{E}[s_{\mathbf f}]>0$, the exact opposite of the goal. The naive escape is to reduce bottom edges by more than top edges — bottom by $\beta$, top by $\tau$ with $\tau<\beta$. But if I push $\tau$ down to $\beta/2$ to protect the bottom edges, then when a *top* edge must repair a cut against reduced *bottom* edges, the top edge's increase outruns its reduction and I'm back to square one. A single ratio can't satisfy both directions.

The second idea closes it, and it's the subtle one. When I reduce a bottom edge, I don't merely condition on $A_T=B_T=1$; I condition on $A_T=B_T=1$ *together with the marginals inside $A$ and $B$ being approximately preserved*. There's a max-flow argument (an extension of the Gurvits-type lower bound for strongly Rayleigh distributions) giving an event of probability $\Omega(\alpha^2)$ on which $A_T=B_T=1$ holds *and* every marginal in $A\cup B$ shifts by total variation at most $\alpha$. Preserving marginals while conditioning is what makes the reduction $\infty$-ideal: conditioned on the bottom edge being reduced, the probability that any lower cut it sits on is odd stays well below $1$, all the way down. In the bottom-bottom case, where a triangle's parent is also a triangle, with $x(A^\uparrow)=\alpha$ marginal preservation gives $\Pr[\text{inner triangle happy}\mid \text{outer bottom bundle reduced}]=\alpha^2+(1-\alpha)^2$, and since the simultaneously-reduced bundle lets one increase fix both odd cuts at once,
$$\mathbb{E}[s_{\mathbf f}]\le -p\beta+p\beta\max_{\alpha\in[1/2,1]}\alpha\,[1-\alpha^2-(1-\alpha)^2]+p\tau.$$
That maximum is $8/27$. Take $\tau=0.571\beta$ and it comes out to $p\beta(-1+\tfrac{8}{27}+0.571)=-0.13\,p\beta<0$. The marginal-preserving conditioning is precisely what bought the $8/27$ instead of a $1$, and $0.571$ is the ratio that simultaneously keeps top edges negative when they repair against bottom reductions.

The fourth crack is the boundary regimes of the simple-case argument, $x_u$ near $1$ or near $0$, where the "$\Omega(\varepsilon)$ both parities" bound degenerates. When $x_u\approx1$, the higher edges out of $u$ either spread across many levels of the hierarchy — handle each level separately using independence between the trees at different levels — or pile up at one level, in which case that parent cut acts like a *pseudo-triangle* (atoms $u$ and the rest) and I apply the same 2-1-1 happiness rule, reducing only when one designated side has exactly one tree edge and the other configured parts behave, which forces a known parity and keeps the conditional odd-probability around $0.57$, below $1$. The 2-1-1 rule introduces its own finer notion of bad edge — good but not 2-1-1-good — and I show non-half-edges are always 2-1-1-good, and among two half-edges on the same side at least one is, with a 2-2-2 fallback rule when one is on each side. When $x_u\approx0$, the sideways mass dwarfs the higher mass, so I re-weight the matching (the $Z_u$ doubling) to halve each sideways edge's repair burden, removing the need for any nontrivial parity bound on $u$. Each boundary regime gets patched without disturbing the others; the patches are local because the hierarchy is laminar and the trees at different levels are independent.

Stack it all up. The hierarchy reduces everything to degree cuts and triangles; on a hierarchy I get a slack vector $s$ with $s_e\ge-\beta x_e$ always, $s(\delta(S))\ge0$ whenever a cut $S\in\mathcal H$ is not happy, and $\mathbb{E}[s_e]\le-\tfrac13\beta\varepsilon_P x_e$ for every LP edge, with $\varepsilon_P=3.12\cdot10^{-16}$ the constant that survives all the case analysis. The leftover cuts are satisfied by $s^*\ge0$ on $\mathrm{OPT}$ edges with $\mathbb{E}[s^*_{e^*}]\le 218\,\eta\,\beta$. Then the O-join $y_e=x_e/4+s_e$ on LP edges and $y^*_{e^*}=1/4+s^*_{e^*}$ on $\mathrm{OPT}$ edges (built on $z=(x+\mathrm{OPT})/2$, so the baseline is $z(\delta(S))/2$) is feasible: near-min odd cuts are covered by happiness or by $s^*$; far cuts are covered because $\beta\le\eta/4.1$. Its expected cost is
$$\mathbb{E}[c(y)+c(y^*)]\le \tfrac{\mathrm{OPT}}4+\tfrac{c(x)}4+218\eta\beta\,\mathrm{OPT}-\tfrac13\varepsilon_P\beta\,c(x).$$
Balance the two error terms by setting $218\eta=\tfrac16\varepsilon_P$, use $c(x)\le\mathrm{OPT}$, and the bound becomes $(\tfrac12-\tfrac16\varepsilon_P\beta)\mathrm{OPT}$. With $\beta=\eta/4.1=\varepsilon_P/5362.8$ and $\varepsilon_P=3.12\cdot10^{-16}$, the matching is $(\tfrac12-3\cdot10^{-36})\mathrm{OPT}$ in expectation, the tree is at most $\mathrm{OPT}$, and the tour is $\tfrac32-3\cdot10^{-36}$ times optimal. The $\varepsilon$ is microscopic because balancing demands $218\eta\ll\varepsilon_P$, forcing $\eta$ — hence $\beta$ — to be tiny; the polygon approximations are only good to $O(\eta)$, so everything has to be scaled down to where the slack survives. It is a sliver, but it is a sliver below $\tfrac32$, and the sliver came entirely from refusing to commit to one tree.

So the algorithm itself is almost exactly Christofides, with the first line changed: solve the subtour LP, find $\lambda$ giving those marginals, sample one tree from the $\lambda$-uniform max-entropy distribution, then run the ordinary minimum matching on its odd vertices and shortcut — in the existing pipeline, the open `choose_tree` slot is filled by a single `sample_spanning_tree(graph, lam)` call, and nothing else moves. The whole proof is the certificate that this one-line change makes the matching cheap; the code is short because all the depth is in the analysis.
