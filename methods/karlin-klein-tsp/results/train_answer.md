We want the best possible polynomial-time approximation ratio for metric TSP: given $n$ cities with symmetric distances obeying the triangle inequality, find a tour of cost as close to optimal as we can guarantee in the worst case. The problem is NP-hard, and NP-hard to approximate within $123/122$, so there is room above the hardness floor — yet for more than four decades one algorithm has held the ceiling. Christofides–Serdyukov takes a minimum spanning tree $T$, pairs up its odd-degree vertices $O$ with a minimum-cost perfect matching $M$, and shortcuts the Eulerian multigraph $T\cup M$ to a tour. The analysis is airtight: $c(T)\le\mathrm{OPT}$ because deleting an edge from the optimal tour leaves a spanning path; and $c(M)\le\tfrac12\mathrm{OPT}$ because the optimal tour shortcut onto $O$ is a cycle that splits into two perfect matchings, the cheaper costing at most half the tour. So the tour costs at most $\tfrac32\mathrm{OPT}$. The trouble is that this is genuinely tight, not a loose accounting. There is a unit-weight gadget in which the forced minimum spanning tree comes out with *every* vertex odd, so $O$ is everything and its cheapest matching really does cost $(\tfrac12-o(1))\mathrm{OPT}$. The $\tfrac32$ is a real wall for any method that picks one tree and charges its matching against a single optimal tour. The LP relaxations and the half-of-optimal matching bound ($y_e=x_e/2$ is a feasible O-join against any odd set, since $x(\delta(S))\ge2$ on every cut) recover exactly the same $\tfrac32$ polyhedrally, charging every cut at $x_e/2$ with no use of which cuts are actually odd. Prior randomized refinements — max-entropy rounding for graphic TSP, the half-integral case — broke below $\tfrac32$ only in special regimes: the graphic argument needs cheap edges and many cuts simultaneously even, which fails when one expensive edge crosses many cuts; the half-integral argument is wedded to the degree-cut structure of $x_e\in\{0,\tfrac12,1\}$ and does not survive general fractional solutions.

The crack to exploit is that the gadget is bad because of *one specific tree*. It is easy to build an instance whose minimum spanning tree has an expensive matching; it is far harder to build one where *many different* spanning trees are simultaneously bad. So I propose the max-entropy algorithm: instead of fixing the minimum spanning tree, sample a random tree from the maximum-entropy distribution whose edge marginals equal an optimum $x$ of the subtour LP, then run Christofides' matching step unchanged. Two requirements pin down the distribution. First, the tree must stay free in expectation, $\mathbb{E}[c(T)]=\sum_e c_e\Pr[e\in T]\le\mathrm{OPT}$; matching the marginals to a subtour-LP optimum gives this for nothing, since $\mathbb{E}[c(T)]=c(x)\le\mathrm{OPT}$ (after the normalization that splits one vertex across a weight-$1$, cost-$0$ edge $e_0$, so $x$ restricted to the remaining edges lies in the spanning-tree polytope and such a distribution exists). Second, among the infinitely many distributions with marginals $x$, I want the odd set $O$ to vary as widely as possible so no single bad pattern dominates — which is exactly the maximum-entropy distribution. That distribution has the product form $\Pr[T]\propto\prod_{e\in T}\lambda_e$ for some edge weights $\lambda_e\ge0$, a $\lambda$-uniform distribution, with $\lambda$ achieving marginals $x_e(1\pm2^{-n})$ computable in polynomial time.

What makes this more than a vibe is that $\lambda$-uniform spanning-tree distributions are strongly Rayleigh: their generating polynomial is real stable, never vanishing when every variable has positive imaginary part. That single analytic fact drags in the whole toolbox — negative dependence, log-concave rank sequences, and the load-bearing one, that conditioning on a vertex set $S$ being internally spanned by a tree splits the sample into two *independent* trees, one inside $S$ and one on the contraction. The degree at a vertex $v$ is a sum of independent Bernoullis with mean $x(\delta(v))=2$, so $\Pr[\deg_T(v)\text{ even}]\ge\tfrac12(1-e^{-2})\approx0.43$: a constant fraction of vertices are not in $O$, unlike the all-odd gadget. But that alone cannot beat $\tfrac32$ — two odd vertices at distance $\tfrac12\mathrm{OPT}$ already force a $\tfrac32$ matching — so the real work is in the matching cost itself, framed through the O-join polytope. The minimum matching on $O$ equals $\min c(y)$ over $y\ge0$ with $y(\delta(S))\ge1$ for every $S$ crossed an *odd* number of times by $T$. A cut's constraint only binds when it is odd; so every cut that comes out even is one where setting $y_e=x_e/2$ overpaid. The savings I want is exactly that reclaimed overpayment.

The mechanism is a random slack vector $s$ added on top of the baseline: the final O-join is $y_e=x_e/2+s_e$. When a few special cuts that an edge $e$ sits on come out even, reduce $s_e$ to $-\beta x_e$ for a small constant $\beta$, banking the cost; when some cut comes out odd and its crossing edges were reduced, increase $s_e$ on other edges of that cut to repair the constraint. I win if $\mathbb{E}[s_e]<-\varepsilon\beta x_e$ strictly for every edge, because then $\mathbb{E}[c(y)]=c(x)/2+\mathbb{E}[c(s)]<\tfrac12\mathrm{OPT}$. A cheap observation fences off most cuts: if $x(\delta(S))>2(1+\eta)$ then even reducing every crossing edge by its full $\beta x_e$ drops the value by at most $\beta\,x(\delta(S))$, and choosing $\beta\le\eta/4.1$ keeps $y(\delta(S))\ge1$. So the entire game lives in the near-minimum cut regime, where $x(\delta(S))\le2+\eta$ — precisely the cuts the cactus/polygon machinery understands. In the cleanest case, a tight cut $S$ with $x(\delta(S))=2$ holding two children $u,v$ joined by a bundle $\mathbf e$, reduce $\mathbf e$ exactly when both $\delta(u)_T$ and $\delta(v)_T$ are even (probability $p$), and make $\mathbf e$ responsible for fraction $m_{\mathbf e,u}$ of the reductions on each higher edge $g$ out of $u$, so

$$\mathbb{E}[s_{\mathbf e}]=-p\,\eta\,x_{\mathbf e}\;+\;m_{\mathbf e,u}\!\!\sum_{g\in\delta^\uparrow(u)}\!\!\Pr[\delta(u)_T\text{ odd}\mid g\text{ reduced}]\,\Pr[g\text{ reduced}]\,\eta\,\tfrac{x_g}{x(\delta^\uparrow(u))}\;+\;(\text{same for }v).$$

Everything hinges on $\Pr[\delta(u)_T\text{ odd}\mid g\text{ reduced}]$ being bounded *below* $1$; if it were $1$ the increase would exactly cancel the reduction. Here the tight cut earns its keep: because $x(\delta(S))=2$, conditioning on $S$ being a tree makes the inside independent of the outside, and the sideways part $\delta^\rightarrow(u)_T=\delta(u)\setminus\delta(S)$ is sampled inside $S$ independently of whatever the higher edge $g$ did. So $\Pr[\delta(u)_T\text{ even}\mid g\text{ reduced}]\ge\min(\Pr[\delta^\rightarrow(u)_T\text{ even}],\Pr[\delta^\rightarrow(u)_T\text{ odd}])$, and since $\delta^\rightarrow(u)_T$ is a Bernoulli sum with mean in $[1+\varepsilon,2-\varepsilon]$ when $x(\delta^\uparrow(u))\in[\varepsilon,1-\varepsilon]$, both parities have probability $\Omega(\varepsilon)$. Thus $\Pr[\delta(u)_T\text{ odd}\mid g\text{ reduced}]\le1-\Omega(\varepsilon)$. Arranging every edge to be reduced with the same probability $p$ and setting $m_{\mathbf e,u}+m_{\mathbf e,v}=x_{\mathbf e}$ gives $\mathbb{E}[s_{\mathbf e}]\le-p\eta x_{\mathbf e}+(1-\varepsilon)p\eta x_{\mathbf e}=-\varepsilon p\eta x_{\mathbf e}<0$. It closes — the expected reduction beats the expected increase by exactly the slack $\varepsilon$ that negative dependence handed over.

That clean derivation quietly assumed four things, each of which breaks somewhere, and the rest of the construction is patching them locally because the hierarchy is laminar and trees at different levels are independent. First, some edges are *bad*: their two top cuts are never simultaneously even because the two half-edges are forced into opposite parities. A bad edge must be a half-edge, $x_{\mathbf e}\approx\tfrac12$, and these are rare — at least $\tfrac34$ of the fractional mass in any cut is good, and of two half-edges sharing an endpoint at least one is good — so the fix is simply to never reduce a bad edge, routing compensation only through good edges and asking for non-positive slack on bad ones and strictly negative on good ones. Second, near-min cuts cross in tangles, so I average with the optimal tour: set $z=(x+\mathrm{OPT})/2$. In the Hamiltonian cycle $\mathrm{OPT}$ every non-min cut has value at least $4$, so any $\eta$-near-min cut of $z$ must be a contiguous interval along the $\mathrm{OPT}$ cycle. Cuts crossed on both sides are odd with probability only $O(\eta)$ and are charged to the two $\mathrm{OPT}$ edges in $\delta(S)$ at $O(\eta^2)$ each (a slack vector $s^*\ge0$ on $\mathrm{OPT}$ edges — which is why this bounds the algorithm, not the LP integrality gap). The remaining one-side-crossed cuts split into two laminar families, left and right, forming *polygons* that look like cycles ($x(E(a_i,a_{i+1}))\approx1$, $x(\delta(a_i))\approx2$), each collapsible to a *triangle* with sides $A=E(a_0,a_1)$, $B=E(a_0,a_{m-1})$ that is *happy* — all its cuts even — when $A_T,B_T$ are both odd. The structure is a laminar hierarchy $\mathcal H$ of degree cuts and triangle cuts. Third, a bottom edge $\mathbf f$ inside a triangle with $x_{\mathbf f}\approx1$ might have to repair both $\delta(a_1)$ and $\delta(a_2)$ at twice its reduction rate; a single reduction ratio cannot protect both bottom and top edges, so I reduce bottom edges by $\beta$ and top edges by $\tau=0.571\beta$, and reduce a bottom edge only when $A_T=B_T=1$ *with marginals approximately preserved* — a max-flow event of probability $\Omega(\alpha^2)$ extending the Gurvits-type strongly-Rayleigh lower bound. Marginal preservation keeps the reduction $\infty$-ideal so lower cuts stay odd with probability well below $1$; the bottom-bottom case gives $\mathbb{E}[s_{\mathbf f}]\le-p\beta+p\beta\max_{\alpha\in[1/2,1]}\alpha[1-\alpha^2-(1-\alpha)^2]+p\tau$, where the maximum is $8/27$, so $\mathbb{E}[s_{\mathbf f}]=p\beta(-1+\tfrac8{27}+0.571)=-0.13\,p\beta<0$. Fourth, the boundary regimes $x_u\approx1$ (handled per hierarchy level, or via a 2-1-1 pseudo-triangle happiness rule keeping the conditional odd-probability near $0.57$) and $x_u\approx0$ (re-weight the matching, the $Z_u$ doubling, to halve each sideways edge's repair burden) are patched without disturbing the others.

Stacking it up, the hierarchy yields a slack vector with $s_e\ge-\beta x_e$ always, $s(\delta(S))\ge0$ on every unhappy cut $S\in\mathcal H$, and $\mathbb{E}[s_e]\le-\tfrac13\beta\varepsilon_P x_e$ with $\varepsilon_P=3.12\cdot10^{-16}$ the constant surviving all the case analysis; leftover cuts are covered by $s^*\ge0$ on $\mathrm{OPT}$ edges with $\mathbb{E}[s^*_{e^*}]\le218\,\eta\,\beta$. Building the O-join on $z$ as $y_e=x_e/4+s_e$ and $y^*_{e^*}=1/4+s^*_{e^*}$, feasibility holds because near-min odd cuts are covered by happiness or by $s^*$ and far cuts by $\beta\le\eta/4.1$, and the expected cost is

$$\mathbb{E}[c(y)+c(y^*)]\le \tfrac{\mathrm{OPT}}4+\tfrac{c(x)}4+218\eta\beta\,\mathrm{OPT}-\tfrac13\varepsilon_P\beta\,c(x).$$

Balancing the two error terms with $218\eta=\tfrac16\varepsilon_P$, using $c(x)\le\mathrm{OPT}$ and $\beta=\eta/4.1=\varepsilon_P/5362.8$, the matching's expected cost drops to $(\tfrac12-\tfrac16\varepsilon_P\beta)\mathrm{OPT}=(\tfrac12-3\cdot10^{-36})\mathrm{OPT}$, the tree stays at most $\mathrm{OPT}$, and the tour comes out at $(\tfrac32-3\cdot10^{-36})\mathrm{OPT}$. The $\varepsilon$ is microscopic because balancing forces $\eta$, hence $\beta$, to be tiny — the polygon approximations are only good to $O(\eta)$ — but it is genuinely below $\tfrac32$, and the sliver came entirely from refusing to commit to one tree. The algorithm itself is almost exactly Christofides with the first line changed; all the depth is in the certificate that this one change makes the matching cheap.

def tsp_tour(graph, cost):
