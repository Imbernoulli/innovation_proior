Direct policy search is attractive for large or partially observed control problems: I adjust the parameters $\theta$ of a stochastic policy $\pi(a\mid s,\theta)$ rather than building a value function and acting greedily from it, so a small parameter change makes a small change in behavior, stochastic policies stay available, and I can phrase convergence arguments about the policy parameters themselves. The weak point is the update *direction*. The conventional rule, $\Delta\theta = \alpha\,\nabla\eta(\theta)$, follows the raw derivative of long-run reward with respect to the chosen coordinates, and in doing so it silently treats $\theta$ as a flat Euclidean object. But $\theta$ is only a coordinate chart for the policy. Under a smooth reparameterization of the same policy family the raw derivative transforms as a covector (by the inverse-transpose Jacobian) while the displacement I actually apply transforms as a tangent vector, so two coordinate descriptions of the same policy produce different physical moves in policy space. The step I take is therefore a property of my coordinates, not of the policy I am changing. The obvious alternatives do not fill the gap either. Greedy policy iteration is powerful with exact values but non-monotone and brittle under approximate ones. Second-order optimization is tempting, but the reward Hessian in a control problem is not a clean property of the parameterization — it is coupled to values and to how values shift as the policy moves, so it can be indefinite far from a maximum, which is exactly where a notion of local length should still be valid and positive. What is missing is a direction that is coordinate-respecting like information geometry, sample-estimable like the policy gradient, and still aimed at the improvement behavior that makes policy iteration appealing.

I propose the Natural Policy Gradient. The starting realization is that the first question is not how to estimate the gradient more accurately but what a "small move" even means. The ordinary gradient is steepest only after I secretly fix the squared length of a displacement to be $\sum_i d\theta_i^2$, the identity metric $G=I$ in the current chart. If instead I maximize the linearized reward change $\nabla\eta\cdot d\theta$ subject to a fixed length $d\theta^\top G\,d\theta$, the Lagrange condition $\nabla\eta = \lambda\,G\,d\theta$ gives the direction $G^{-1}\nabla\eta$; the raw gradient is just the arbitrary choice $G=I$. So the real task is choosing the metric, and a policy is valuable not because its coordinate vector moved a short Euclidean distance but because the action distributions it induces changed in a controlled way — a one-unit shift in the mean of a narrow Gaussian almost replaces the distribution, while the same shift in a broad Gaussian barely touches it. The right primitive is Amari's information geometry: when a parameter indexes a probability distribution, the Fisher information is the invariant local metric on that family, and it is the second-order local form of KL divergence, so $d\theta^\top F\,d\theta$ measures local *distributional* change. Capping a Fisher length caps policy change, not coordinate motion, and $F^{-1}\nabla\eta$ is genuinely the steepest direction on the distribution manifold rather than a preconditioning trick.

The complication is that a policy is not one distribution. At every state $s$ the parameters define a conditional action distribution $\pi(\cdot\mid s,\theta)$, so each state carries its own statistical manifold, while the objective averages consequences under the stationary distribution. I therefore build the metric in two stages. For a single state I use the Fisher metric of that conditional, with score $\psi(s,a)=\nabla\log\pi(a\mid s,\theta)$,
$$F_s(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{a\sim\pi(\cdot\mid s,\theta)}\!\left[\psi(s,a)\,\psi(s,a)^\top\right],$$
and then I weight the per-state metrics by exactly the distribution the objective uses, the current stationary distribution $\rho^\pi$:
$$F(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{s\sim\rho^\pi}\!\left[F_s(\theta)\right] = \sum_{s,a}\rho^\pi(s)\,\pi(a\mid s,\theta)\,\psi(s,a)\,\psi(s,a)^\top.$$
The dynamics enter only through $\rho^\pi$; the per-state Fisher depends only on how the action distribution reacts to $\theta$. The natural policy-gradient direction is then the metric steepest ascent $v = F(\theta)^{-1} g(\theta)$, where $g(\theta)=\nabla\eta(\theta)$, solved with a pseudoinverse or a small ridge when the score covariance is rank-deficient. Crucially this preserves the policy gradient's central advantage. The average-reward gradient from the policy-gradient theorem,
$$g(\theta) = \nabla\eta(\theta) = \sum_{s,a}\rho^\pi(s)\,\nabla\pi(a\mid s,\theta)\,Q^\pi(s,a),$$
contains *no* explicit derivative of $\rho^\pi$ — the hard state-distribution derivative cancels — and the metric $F(\theta)$ is an expectation under the same on-policy state-action distribution, just with score outer products instead of score-times-value. Both objects are estimable from rollouts, so I have changed the geometry without ever differentiating the environment.

What makes this more than information geometry pasted onto policy search is that the inverse-Fisher solve is already hiding inside actor-critic machinery. By Sutton's compatibility condition, if I approximate $Q^\pi$ with a linear critic whose features are precisely the score, $f(s,a;w)=w^\top\psi(s,a)$, then substituting it into the gradient formula introduces no bias once the approximation error is orthogonal to those features. Fitting $w$ by least squares weighted by $\rho^\pi(s)\,\pi(a\mid s,\theta)$ gives the normal equations
$$\Big[\sum_{s,a}\rho^\pi(s)\,\pi(a\mid s,\theta)\,\psi\psi^\top\Big]w = \sum_{s,a}\rho^\pi(s)\,\pi(a\mid s,\theta)\,\psi\,Q^\pi,$$
whose left bracket is exactly $F(\theta)$ and whose right side is exactly $g(\theta)$, since $\pi\psi=\nabla\pi$. Hence $F(\theta)\,w = g(\theta)$ and $w = v = F(\theta)^{-1}g(\theta)$: the compatible critic weights *are* the natural gradient. The same score features that make the critic unbiased are the ones whose covariance is the Fisher metric, and least squares with them performs the inverse-metric operation for free.

This also explains why the direction aims at good actions rather than merely making a tidy geometric statement, and why it is emphatically *not* Newton's method. For an exponential-family policy $\pi(a\mid s,\theta)\propto\exp(\theta^\top\phi_{sa})$ the score is the centered feature $\psi(s,a)=\phi_{sa}-\mathbb{E}_\pi[\phi_{s\cdot}]$, so $\arg\max_a w^\top\psi(s,a)=\arg\max_a w^\top\phi_{sa}$; stepping $\theta'=\theta+\alpha v$ puts $\theta^\top\phi_{sa}+\alpha v^\top\phi_{sa}$ in the logit, and as $\alpha\to\infty$ probability concentrates exactly on the actions maximizing the compatible critic — the greedy move of approximate policy iteration, which a raw gradient step does not deliver. For a general smooth policy I read the same fact locally: since $\nabla\pi=\pi\psi$,
$$\pi(a\mid s,\theta+\alpha v) = \pi(a\mid s,\theta)\big(1+\alpha\,f(s,a;w)\big)+O(\alpha^2),$$
so the update scales each action's probability by the critic's advantage-like value. By contrast the reward Hessian
$$\nabla^2\eta = \sum_{s,a}\rho^\pi(s)\big(\nabla^2\pi\,Q^\pi + \nabla\pi\,\nabla Q^{\pi\top} + \nabla Q^\pi\,\nabla\pi^\top\big)$$
carries $\nabla Q^\pi$ couplings and value weighting; $F(\theta)$ has neither — it is built from first derivatives of the policy distribution alone. That is why it is chosen for being a reparameterization-invariant measure of policy displacement, not for approximating curvature, and why it stays positive on the identifiable score subspace and yields an ascent direction even far from an optimum where a Hessian would turn indefinite.

The method is then forced. Roll out the current policy; estimate $g=\nabla\eta$ from score times return or differential value; estimate $F$ from score outer products (equivalently, fit the compatible critic by least squares); solve $F v = g$ with a pseudoinverse or ridge if the scores are rank-deficient; and step $\theta\leftarrow\theta+\alpha v$. To keep each step a controlled change in the policy distributions I size $\alpha$ by a local KL budget $\epsilon$: imposing $\tfrac{1}{2}\alpha^2\,g^\top F^{-1} g = \epsilon$ gives $\alpha=\sqrt{2\epsilon/(g^\top v)}$.

```python
def natural_policy_gradient_step(policy, mdp, q_value, epsilon=1e-2, ridge=1e-8):
    grad = 0.0
    fisher = 0.0
    for s in mdp.states:
        rho = mdp.stationary_prob(s, policy)
        for a in mdp.actions:
            p = policy.prob(s, a)
            psi = policy.score(s, a)
            grad += rho * p * psi * q_value(s, a)
            fisher += rho * p * outer(psi, psi)

    direction = solve(fisher + ridge * I, grad)
    step = sqrt(2 * epsilon / dot(grad, direction))
    policy.theta = policy.theta + step * direction
    return policy
```
