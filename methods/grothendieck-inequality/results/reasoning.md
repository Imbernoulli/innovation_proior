I start with the simplest optimization picture because it makes the question concrete. A matrix `A=(a_ij)` defines a bilinear form, and the sign problem asks for the largest value of `sum a_ij epsilon_i delta_j` with each variable in `{-1,1}`. Replacing each sign by a unit vector and each product by an inner product enlarges the feasible set, and since signs are one-dimensional unit vectors, the vector optimum `OPT_vec(A)` is always at least the sign optimum `OPT_sign(A)`. The question is how much larger it can be.

First I check the cheap routes, because if any of them worked there would be nothing to discover. Relaxing signs to scalars in `[-1,1]` gains nothing: a bilinear form is linear in each variable separately, so its extrema over the cube are attained at corners, and the relaxed problem collapses back to exactly `OPT_sign(A)`. Exact enumeration over signs solves the problem but is exponential and gives no certificate. Every coordinate-wise comparison I try between the vector problem and the sign problem picks up a factor that grows with the matrix size, and my honest prior is that this is unavoidable: a positive semidefinite Gram matrix has far more degrees of freedom than two sign vectors, and for most relaxations of this shape the integrality gap does grow with dimension. So the first thing to explain is why anyone conjectured a universal constant at all. Grothendieck did, in the 1953 Résumé on the metric theory of tensor products, proving the constant exists and trapping it between `pi/2` and `sinh(pi/2) ≈ 2.301`, in a tensor-product language that sat mostly unread until Lindenstrauss and Pełczyński recast it in 1968 as a statement about bounded bilinear forms. I want to see the phenomenon directly, in the finite matrix setting, and understand why the dimension drops out.

The natural attempt is to round. Take optimal unit vectors `x_i, y_j` for the vector problem and convert them back into signs, then compare values. Deterministic rounding goes nowhere, so round randomly: draw a standard Gaussian vector `g` and set `epsilon_i = sign(<g,x_i>)`, `delta_j = sign(<g,y_j>)`. The point of using a Gaussian is that everything is computable. The numbers `<g,x_i>` and `<g,y_j>` are jointly Gaussian with correlation `<x_i,y_j>`, and the sign-correlation of a bivariate Gaussian is a classical closed form, Grothendieck's identity:

`E[ sign(<g,x>) sign(<g,y>) ] = (2/pi) arcsin(<x,y>)`.

Since the maximum over sign assignments is at least the average over `g`, I get

`OPT_sign(A) >= (2/pi) sum_ij a_ij arcsin(<x_i,y_j>)`.

And here I hit the wall. The identity produced `arcsin` where I need the identity function. If all the coefficients were nonnegative I could use `arcsin(t) >= t` on `[0,1]` and be done, but `a_ij` have mixed signs, and `arcsin(t) - t` itself changes sign with `t`, so no termwise comparison between `sum a_ij arcsin(<x_i,y_j>)` and `sum a_ij <x_i,y_j> = OPT_vec(A)` exists. The arcsin sits between the rounding and the objective, and I cannot move it. This is the real obstruction, and it explains why the theorem is not a trivial rounding argument: the naive bridge from vectors to signs distorts every inner product by a nonlinear function that the coefficients refuse to commute with.

The way out is to stop fighting the arcsin and invert it instead. Suppose that, before rounding, I could preprocess: build new unit vectors `u_i, v_j` in some larger Hilbert space whose inner products are `sin(c <x_i,y_j>)` for a fixed constant `c <= pi/2`. Then `arcsin(<u_i,v_j>) = c <x_i,y_j>`, because `sin` maps `[-c,c]` into the principal branch, and applying the same Gaussian rounding bound to the new vectors gives

`OPT_sign(A) >= (2/pi) sum_ij a_ij arcsin(<u_i,v_j>) = (2c/pi) sum_ij a_ij <x_i,y_j> = (2c/pi) OPT_vec(A)`,

which is exactly the inequality I want, with constant `pi/(2c)`. The entire problem has collapsed to one question: can `sin(c t)` be realized as an inner product of unit vectors built out of the `x`'s and `y`'s?

Tensor powers do precisely this, and this is the load-bearing trick. Inner products multiply under tensoring: `<x^{otimes k}, y^{otimes k}> = <x,y>^k`. The Taylor series of `sin` is an alternating sum of odd powers, so if I take the direct sum over all odd tensor powers, weight the `k`-th piece by `sqrt(c^{2k+1}/(2k+1)!)`, and put the alternating signs on one side,

`u_i = bigoplus_{k>=0} (-1)^k sqrt(c^{2k+1}/(2k+1)!) x_i^{otimes(2k+1)}`,   `v_j = bigoplus_{k>=0} sqrt(c^{2k+1}/(2k+1)!) y_j^{otimes(2k+1)}`,

then

`<u_i,v_j> = sum_k (-1)^k c^{2k+1}/(2k+1)! <x_i,y_j>^{2k+1} = sin(c <x_i,y_j>)`,

while each norm computes itself against the full majorant: `<u_i,u_i> = sum_k c^{2k+1}/(2k+1)! = sinh(c)`, using `<x_i,x_i> = 1`. So the vectors are unit exactly when `sinh(c) = 1`, which forces the single choice `c = arcsinh(1) = ln(1+sqrt(2)) ≈ 0.881`. This is comfortably below `pi/2`, so the arcsin inversion is legitimate, and the proof closes:

`OPT_vec(A) <= (pi / (2 ln(1+sqrt(2)))) OPT_sign(A) ≈ 1.7822 OPT_sign(A)`.

This is Krivine's 1979 sharpening of Grothendieck's original `sinh(pi/2)` bound; Krivine conjectured it was tight, and Braverman, Makarychev, Makarychev, and Naor refuted that in 2011, while Davie's lower bound of about 1.677 still stands on the other side. The exact constant remains unknown, but the theorem was never really about the number.

Stepping back, I can see why the argument exists at all, and why it is specific to bilinear forms. Two accidents carried the proof: the sign-correlation of Gaussians is exactly `arcsin`, a function whose inverse has an odd entire Taylor series, and the majorant of that inverse is `sinh`, which can be normalized to `1` by a single scalar choice. For trilinear or higher-order forms there is no analogue of the sign-correlation identity, and indeed dimension-free constants fail there; the universality is a genuinely bilinear phenomenon. I also see now why Grothendieck's own framing was about tensor products: the statement says that testing a bilinear form on the extreme points of cubes — the `ell_infty` geometry — controls, up to a fixed factor, testing it on arbitrary spherical configurations — the Hilbert geometry. A priori the cube and the sphere have nothing to do with each other; the inequality says bilinear forms cannot tell them apart by more than `K_G`.

The last step is noticing what the vector problem actually is computationally. A family of unit vectors is encoded by its Gram matrix: inner products become entries, the unit constraints become diagonal ones, and positive semidefiniteness captures exactly the feasibility of such a configuration. So `OPT_vec(A)` is a semidefinite program, efficiently approximable, while `OPT_sign(A)` is NP-hard. Read this way, Grothendieck's inequality is an integrality-gap theorem: the natural SDP relaxation of bilinear sign optimization is loose by at most a universal constant, and the Gaussian sign rounding above is exactly the constructive procedure that turns near-optimal vectors back into near-optimal signs. That is the mechanism behind constant-factor approximations for quantities like the cut norm, where Alon and Naor made the bridge fully algorithmic.

So the clean final message is this: passing from signs to vectors enlarges a bilinear optimum by at most a fixed factor, independent of everything. The reason it is true is not a soft compactness argument but an explicit computation — Gaussian sign rounding costs an arcsin, the arcsin can be inverted at the level of vectors by a tensor-power preprocessing, and the price of the inversion is the single number `pi/(2 ln(1+sqrt(2)))`. The inequality converts a hard combinatorial object into a convex geometric one while losing only that constant, and it is at the same time a structural statement about how tightly the cube and the sphere are coupled through bilinear forms.
