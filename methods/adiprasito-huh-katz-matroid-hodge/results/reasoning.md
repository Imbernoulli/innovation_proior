Let me start from the thing that has been nagging me, which is not any matroid in particular but a stubborn pattern. I have a graph, I write down its chromatic polynomial, I strip the signs off the coefficients, and the sequence of absolute values goes up and then comes down, and more than that, each interior term is at least the geometric mean of its neighbors. Log-concavity. And the same thing happens for the characteristic polynomial of a matroid, and for the count of independent sets of each size. Three different sequences, three different decades-old conjectures, all saying the same shape: log-concave, hence unimodal. I keep asking myself why this should be true, and every time I push on it the answer slides away.

Here is the precise reason it slides away. The chromatic polynomial obeys deletion–contraction, χ_M = χ_{M\e} − χ_{M/e}. The independent-set counts obey f_k(A) = f_k(A\v) + f_{k−1}(A/v). These are additive recursions. So if I want to prove log-concavity by induction, I would dearly like log-concavity to be stable under the operations in the recursion. But it is not. Add two log-concave sequences and the sum is generically not log-concave; subtract and it is worse. So the property I am chasing survives a recursion under which it has every reason to be destroyed. That is not a nuisance. That is a clue. A quantity that is built additively but is nonetheless log-concave is behaving as if it secretly carried a *multiplicative* or *quadratic* structure that the additive presentation hides. Something is enforcing the inequality from underneath.

Where have I seen log-concavity enforced from underneath before? In geometry. Mixed volumes of convex bodies are log-concave — that is the Alexandrov–Fenchel inequality. Intersection numbers of divisors on a projective surface satisfy the Hodge index inequality, deg(D_1²) deg(D_2²) ≤ deg(D_1 D_2)², and more generally the Khovanskii–Teissier inequalities on a higher-dimensional variety. In every one of those cases the inequality is not proved by manipulating the numbers; it is proved by exhibiting the numbers as values of a symmetric bilinear form and reading off the inequality from the *signature* of that form. A 2×2 symmetric form with one positive and one negative eigenvalue has nonpositive determinant, and that determinant *is* the log-concavity inequality. So the shape I want is: realize my combinatorial numbers as deg(a · b) for elements a, b of some graded ring with a perfect top pairing, and then prove that the relevant bilinear form has the indefinite signature that forces the 2×2 determinant to be ≤ 0.

So the real target is not "prove an inequality." The target is "build a ring." A graded commutative algebra A* = ⊕_q A^q, finite-dimensional, with A^0 and the top piece both one-dimensional, with a perfect Poincaré pairing A^q × A^{r−q} → A^r ≅ ℝ, and with an element ℓ in degree 1 whose multiplication is so well-behaved that the associated forms have the eigenvalue pattern of a Kähler manifold. If I can put my characteristic-polynomial coefficients into that ring as intersection numbers, the log-concavity falls out of the signature, exactly as the Hodge index theorem falls out of the signature of the intersection form on the middle cohomology of a surface.

Now, for *some* matroids I already know how to do this, and that is worth dwelling on because it tells me what the ring should look like. If the matroid is realized by an actual hyperplane arrangement over a field, I can form the wonderful compactification of the complement — de Concini and Procesi built these — and its cohomology / Chow ring is a genuine smooth projective variety's, so it comes with Poincaré duality, hard Lefschetz, and the Hodge–Riemann relations for free. And in that realizable world the μ^k really are intersection numbers: the closure of the graph of the Cremona transformation, restricted to the arrangement complement, has class Σ μ^k [ℙ^{r−k}×ℙ^k], and Khovanskii–Teissier on that irreducible subvariety gives the log-concavity. That is exactly the realizable proof. But it is bolted to the existence of a variety. The moment the matroid is not realizable over any field — and most matroids are not — there is no arrangement, no compactification, no variety, and the entire scaffolding evaporates.

And yet — and this is the thing I cannot let go of — there is a perfectly good candidate ring even when there is no variety. Feichtner and Yuzvinsky noticed that the Chow ring of the wonderful model has a presentation by pure generators and relations that never mentions the field. Take one variable x_F for each nonempty proper flat F of M; form the polynomial ring; quotient by the quadratic monomials x_{F_1} x_{F_2} for incomparable flats (you cannot multiply classes of two divisors that do not meet), and by the linear forms Σ_{i_1∈F} x_F − Σ_{i_2∈F} x_F for distinct ground-set elements i_1, i_2 (these encode that every coordinate hyperplane pulls back to the same class). Call the quotient A*(M). For realizable M it is the Chow ring of the variety; for arbitrary M it is just... a ring. It is the Chow ring of a smooth toric variety attached to the Bergman fan Σ_M of the matroid, but that toric variety is noncompact and non-projective when M is not realizable, so there is no obvious projective geometry to borrow Hodge theory from. It was unclear how to even *state* the Hodge–Riemann relations here, never mind prove them. This is the chasm. There is no a priori reason a working Hodge theory should exist beyond the realizable case.

But the ring is *right there*, fully defined, for every matroid. So the question sharpens to a single audacious bet: can I prove Poincaré duality, hard Lefschetz, and the Hodge–Riemann relations for the abstract ring A*(M) directly, by algebra and combinatorics, with no variety underneath? If yes, I get the log-concavity for every matroid in one stroke. I should stop hoping to find a geometry to borrow from and instead reconstruct the *consequences* of geometry — the Kähler package — intrinsically.

The encouraging precedent is McMullen. For a simple polytope there is also a graded ring (the polytope algebra, equivalently the Chow ring of the complete simplicial normal fan), and McMullen proved hard Lefschetz and the Hodge–Riemann–Minkowski inequalities for it *combinatorially*, by induction. His engine is a deformation: start from a fan where the package is trivial, and modify the fan one elementary step at a time, all the way to the fan you care about, proving at each step that the package is preserved. That is exactly the philosophy I need. If I want a property to hold for a hard object, find an easy object where it holds, build a path of small modifications from the easy one to the hard one, and show each modification preserves the property. The whole Kähler package travels along the path. So I should not try to prove hard Lefschetz for A*(M) in one go. I should find the easy end of a path, the atomic step, and the preservation lemma.

What is the easy end? I want a "trivial matroid Chow ring" that is obviously ℙ^r. Look at the Bergman fan again. Its cones σ_{I<F} are indexed by a subset I and a flag of flats F that are compatible. If I throw away *all* the flat-variables and keep only the simplex structure, I should land on projective space. Let me make that precise by stratifying. Instead of using all flats at once, fix an **order filter** P of the poset of flats — a set of flats closed upward — and build an intermediate fan Σ_{M,P} and an intermediate ring A*(M,P) using only the flats in P plus the ground-set elements. When P is empty, no flat-variables survive; the linear relations force all the element-variables x_i equal, and the ring collapses to ℝ[x]/(x^{r+1}). That is the cohomology of ℙ^r. The package is trivial there: hard Lefschetz for ℙ^r is the statement that x^{r−2q} : (degree q) → (degree r−q) is an isomorphism, which it visibly is, and Hodge–Riemann for ℙ^r is one positive number per degree. Good. When P is the full poset of flats, A*(M,P) is the full A*(M) I want. So the path is: empty filter ⇝ add flats one at a time ⇝ full filter. Each "add one flat Z" is the atomic step. I will call it a matroidal flip, with center Z. Geometrically it is one stellar subdivision of the fan, the shadow of a single blow-up.

Let me set up the algebra of the intermediate ring carefully because the whole induction will run on it. I will use variables x_i for elements i of E and x_F for flats F in the order filter P. The ring A*(M,P) is the polynomial ring on these modulo four ideals: 𝓘_1, the incomparability relations x_{F_1}x_{F_2} for incomparable F_1,F_2 in P; 𝓘_2, the complement relations x_i x_F for F in P and i not in F; 𝓘_3, the closure relations Π_{i∈I} x_i for independent sets I whose closure lies in P∪{E}; and 𝓘_4, the linear relations (x_i + Σ_{i∈F∈P} x_F) − (x_j + Σ_{j∈F∈P} x_F) for distinct i,j. Let me check the two endpoints to be sure I have the relations right. With P empty, 𝓘_3 has the closure relation for any independent set of size r+1 (its closure is E), and 𝓘_4 says all x_i are equal to a single x; combined with 𝓘_3 that gives x^{r+1}=0, so A*(M,∅) ≅ ℝ[x]/(x^{r+1}). Yes, ℙ^r. With P the full poset, 𝓘_3 forces every x_i to be zero (a single element's closure, a rank-1 flat, is in P), and what remains is exactly the Feichtner–Yuzvinsky presentation of A*(M). The intermediate rings really do interpolate, and the element-variables x_i are the bookkeeping that fades out as flats accumulate.

I need three structural maps for a single flip from P_− to P_+ = P_− ∪ {Z}, where Z is maximal in the complement of P_−, hence minimal in P_+. First, a **pullback** Φ_Z : A*(M,P_−) → A*(M,P_+). What should it do? A blow-up pulls O(1) back to O(1) minus the exceptional divisor, so the natural guess is x_F ↦ x_F for old flats, and for an element i, x_i ↦ x_i + x_Z when i ∈ Z, x_i ↦ x_i when i ∉ Z. I have to check this is a ring map, i.e. it carries the old relations into the new ideal. The incomparability and linear relations go over directly. For a complement relation x_i x_F with i ∉ F: if i ∈ Z then Φ_Z(x_i x_F) = x_i x_F + x_Z x_F, and since Z is minimal in P_+, either Z and F are incomparable (so x_Z x_F ∈ 𝓘_1) or F ⊋ Z, but i ∉ F and i ∈ Z ⊆ F is a contradiction, so the leftover term lands in the ideal. For a closure relation Π_{i∈I} x_i with cl(I) ∈ P_−∪{E}: Φ_Z sends it to Π_{i∈I∖Z} x_i · Π_{i∈I∩Z}(x_i + x_Z); the all-x_i term is the old relation in 𝓘_3, and because Z is minimal in P_+ there is at least one element of I outside Z, so every cross term carries an x_Z next to an x_i with i ∉ Z, i.e. lands in 𝓘_2. So Φ_Z is a well-defined ring homomorphism. (When Z has rank 1, the flip does nothing geometrically: any two elements of Z are parallel, the linear relations make their x_i equal, and Φ_Z is an isomorphism with inverse x_Z ↦ x_j, x_i ↦ 0 for i ∈ Z. Worth noting so I can later replace M by its parallel-free combinatorial geometry without loss.)

Second, **Gysin** maps Ψ^{p,q}_Z : A^{q−p}(M_Z) → A^q(M,P_+) pushing forward from the exceptional divisor, x_𝓕 ↦ x_Z^p x_𝓕 (here M_Z is the contraction, whose flats are the flats containing Z). Check it respects relations: incomparability is clear; for the linear relation among i_1,i_2 ∉ Z, multiplying by x_Z^p and using that Z is minimal gives a combination of 𝓘_1, 𝓘_2, 𝓘_4. Good. There is a mirror Gysin Γ^{p,q}_Z : A^{q−p}(M^Z) → A^q(M) using the restriction M^Z, same proof with i_1,i_2 ∈ Z. Third, the **restriction-to-the-star** pullback p_Z : A*(M,P_−) → A*(M_Z), the geometric "restrict to the exceptional locus," coming from the star of the cone σ_{Z<∅}, which is exactly the Bergman fan of M_Z.

Now I want to understand the image of A^q(M,P_+) under these maps. The claim I will work toward — call it the Decomposition theorem — is that A^q(M,P_+) splits as the pullback image ⊕ the Gysin images:
A^q(M,P_+) = im Φ^q_Z ⊕ ⊕_{p=1}^{rk(Z)−1} im Ψ^{p,q}_Z.
This is the combinatorial blow-up formula: the Chow ring of a blow-up is the Chow ring of the base plus the Gysin pushforwards of the exceptional Chow ring, in the degrees 1 through rk(Z)−1 (the codimension range of the exceptional projective bundle). Before I can prove it is a direct sum I need surjectivity, and surjectivity is mostly combinatorics with the relations.

Let me get surjectivity. First, the ideal generated by x_Z. Since Z is minimal in P_+, any nonzero monomial divisible by x_Z, after using 𝓘_1 and 𝓘_2 to kill incomparable and complementary products, has the form x_Z^k Π_F x_F^{k_F} Π_{i∈I} x_i^{k_i} with I ⊆ Z and the flats above Z. I need a relation that lets me trade x_i x_Z for things downstairs. Take the linear relation for i ∈ Z and j ∉ Z and multiply by x_Z: since Z is minimal, this collapses to
x_Z x_i + x_Z² + x_Z β_{M_Z} = 0,
where β_{M_Z} := Σ_{Z ⊊ F, F ⊉ stuff} x_F is the "β" element of the contraction (this is just Σ_{Z⊊F⊊F∪{j}} x_F, the image of β under the degree-1 Gysin). So whenever x_Z appears with positive exponent I can rewrite, and every x_Z-divisible monomial lands in the sum of the Ψ-images. Hence the ideal (x_Z) equals Σ_{p,q} im Ψ^{p,q}_Z. And modulo (x_Z), any monomial not divisible by x_Z is, by the same x_i ↦ x_i+x_Z bookkeeping, the Φ_Z-image of a monomial downstairs. So Φ_Z and the Ψ's together hit everything: surjectivity holds.

I have to be careful about the Gysin range, because the formula only uses p up to rk(Z)−1, not all p. The contracted matroid M_Z has rank crk(Z), so A^{q−p}(M_Z) vanishes once q−p exceeds crk(Z)−1, but more to the point I need the high-p Gysin images to be redundant. Here is the reduction: pick a basis I of Z (size rk(Z)) and expand Π_{i∈I}(x_i + x_Z), which lies in im Φ_Z. The all-x_i term is a closure relation, hence zero, so using x_Z x_i = −x_Z² − x_Z β_{M_Z} repeatedly,
Π_{i∈I}(x_i + x_Z) = (−β_{M_Z})^k − (−x_Z − β_{M_Z})^k ∈ im Φ_Z, where k = rk(Z).
Expanding the right side, the top power x_Z^k appears, so x_Z^k ∈ im Φ_Z + Σ_{p<k} im Ψ^{p,k}_Z. Since im Ψ^{k,k}_Z is generated by x_Z^k, the rank-rk(Z) Gysin image is absorbed by the lower ones plus Φ. Multiplying up by powers of x_Z and by flat-monomials extends this to all q, so indeed only p = 1,…,rk(Z)−1 are needed. Surjectivity of Φ^q_Z ⊕ ⊕_{p=1}^{rk(Z)−1} Ψ^{p,q}_Z is established.

In top degree this already gives me something clean. When q = r, the Gysin maps Ψ^{p,r}_Z with p < rk(Z) land in A^{r}(M_Z)-shifts that vanish (the contraction has too small rank), so Φ^r_Z is surjective in top degree; combined with both top groups being ≅ ℤ (the empty-filter end is ℤ[x]/(x^{r+1}) so its top is ℤ, and I can show A^r(M) ≅ ℤ), Φ^r_Z is an isomorphism between top degrees. Let me nail A^r(M) ≅ ℤ now, because the degree map depends on it. Define α_M := Σ_{i∈F} x_F (independent of i, by the linear relations) — the matroid pullback of O(1). I claim A^r(Σ_M) is generated by α_M^r and that any maximal flag monomial x_𝓕 equals α_M^r. The computation is a descending/ascending induction: for any i ∉ F, x_F α_M = x_F Σ_{G ⊋ F, i∈G} x_G, so multiplying flags by α_M either kills them (if the flag's ranks are wrong) or advances them along a maximal flag; chasing this shows x_{F_1}⋯x_{F_k} α_M^{r−k} = α_M^r whenever rk(F_m)=m for all m, and = 0 otherwise. So every top monomial is α_M^r, the top group is generated by one element, and balancing of Minkowski weights (the dual side: a top weight on Σ_M is forced constant, because the flats covering a fixed flat partition the complement, so the local balancing at each codimension-1 cone forces constancy, and the Bergman fan is connected in codimension 1 by an induction on rank) pins A^r(M) ≅ ℤ. Define deg: A^r(M) → ℤ by sending every maximal flag monomial to 1; equivalently deg(α_M^r)=1.

Now the direct-sum-ness and Poincaré duality. I will prove them together, by lexicographic induction on (rank of M, size of the order filter). Poincaré duality I need even to *state* hard Lefschetz, so it must come first. The base of the induction is A*(M,∅) ≅ ℝ[x]/(x^{r+1}), which is the cohomology of ℙ^r and visibly a Poincaré duality algebra. The inductive step: assume Poincaré duality for A*(M_Z) (smaller rank) and for A*(M,P_−) (smaller filter); deduce the Decomposition for the flip and Poincaré duality for A*(M,P_+).

Two ingredients drive the step. First, the multiplication interacts with the maps in a block-upper-triangular way: im Ψ^{p,q_1}_Z · im Φ^{q_2}_Z ⊆ im Ψ^{p,q_1+q_2}_Z, and im Ψ^{p_1,q_1}_Z · im Ψ^{p_2,q_2}_Z ⊆ im Ψ^{p_1+p_2,q_1+q_2}_Z. In particular, in complementary top degree q_1+q_2 = r, a Gysin-times-pullback product with p < rk(Z) is forced into a Gysin image of *top* type that vanishes, so im Ψ^{p,q_1}_Z · im Φ^{r−q_1}_Z = 0, and Gysin-times-Gysin with p_1+p_2 < rk(Z) is 0 too. Second, the Gysin map between top degrees is itself an isomorphism: Ψ^{rk(Z),r}_Z : A^{crk(Z)−1}(M_Z) ≅ A^r(M,P_+). I verify it sends a generator to a generator by computing, for flags 𝓩_1 below Z and 𝓩_2 above Z, x_{𝓩_1} x_Z x_{𝓩_2} = (−1)^{rk(Z)−1} x_Z^{rk(Z)} x_{𝓩_2}, using the α-relation x_Z x_{𝓩}(x_Z + α_{M^Z})=0 from the mirror Gysin. That (−1)^{rk(Z)−1} sign is going to matter later; I file it away.

With these, the injectivity and the trivial-intersection follow from Poincaré duality of the smaller pieces. If ξ ≠ 0 in the domain of Φ^q_Z, then by Poincaré duality of (M,P_−) there is something it pairs nontrivially with downstairs, and since Φ_Z is a top isomorphism, Φ^q_Z(ξ)·im Φ^{r−q}_Z ≠ 0, so Φ^q_Z is injective; and the Gysin images, paired against im Φ^{r−q}_Z, give 0 by the upper-triangularity, so im Φ^q_Z ∩ Σ im Ψ^{p,q}_Z = 0. Likewise each Ψ^{p,q}_Z is injective by Poincaré duality of M_Z (pair Ψ^{p,q}_Z(ξ) against Ψ^{rk(Z)−p,r−q}_Z), and distinct Gysin images meet trivially by upper-triangularity. So the sum is direct: the Decomposition theorem holds. And then Poincaré duality for A*(M,P_+) is a determinant computation: in the chosen bases respecting the decomposition, the pairing matrix 𝓜_+ is block upper triangular with diagonal blocks the pairing 𝓜_− of (M,P_−) and the pairings 𝓜_p of M_Z, so det 𝓜_+ = ± det 𝓜_− · Π_p det 𝓜_p = ±1 by the inductive Poincaré dualities. Perfect pairing, hence Poincaré duality for A*(M,P_+). The lexicographic induction closes. So A*(M,P) is a Poincaré duality algebra of dimension r for every order filter P. The skeleton is built.

Now the hard part: the *positivity*, hard Lefschetz and Hodge–Riemann. Let me first set up the abstract algebra so I know exactly what I am proving. For a Poincaré duality algebra R* of dimension r with a degree map and an ℓ ∈ R^1, the Lefschetz operator L^q_ℓ : R^q → R^{r−q} is a ↦ ℓ^{r−2q} a, the Hodge–Riemann form is Q^q_ℓ(a_1,a_2) = (−1)^q deg(a_1 ℓ^{r−2q} a_2), and the primitive space is P^q_ℓ = ker(ℓ·L^q_ℓ) = ker(L^{q−1}_ℓ restricted up). HL(ℓ) says every L^q_ℓ is an isomorphism for q ≤ r/2; HR(ℓ) says Q^q_ℓ is positive definite on P^q_ℓ for q ≤ r/2. Two abstract facts I will lean on. First, HL(ℓ) is equivalent to nondegeneracy of every Q^q_ℓ — because Q^q_ℓ is the composite R^q → R^{r−q} → Hom(R^q, R^r) of L^q_ℓ with the Poincaré pairing, and the second arrow is an iso, so Q^q_ℓ nondegenerate ⇔ L^q_ℓ iso. Second, under HL the Lefschetz decomposition R^q = ⊕_{p≤q} ℓ^p P^{q−p}_ℓ is orthogonal for Q^q_ℓ, and there is an isometry (P^p_ℓ, Q^p_ℓ) ≅ (ℓ^{q−p} P^p_ℓ, (−1)^{q−p} Q^q_ℓ). Consequently HR(ℓ) is equivalent to nondegeneracy of every Q^q_ℓ *together with* the specific signature Σ_{p≤q} (−1)^{q−p}(dim R^p − dim R^{p−1}). This is the crucial reformulation: HR is a *signature* statement, and signatures of nondegenerate forms are locally constant in continuous families. That is the lever for the deformation argument. And note HR(ℓ) ⇒ HL(ℓ): if L^q_ℓ(a)=0 then a ∈ P^q_ℓ and Q^q_ℓ(a,a)=0, so positive-definiteness on P^q_ℓ forces a=0.

I also need that HL and HR are stable under tensor product, because the local structure I am about to exploit will be a product. Let R_1*, R_2* be Poincaré duality algebras with HR(ℓ_1), HR(ℓ_2); I want HR(ℓ_1⊗1 + 1⊗ℓ_2) on R_1*⊗R_2*. The model case is R_i = ℝ[x_i]/(x_i^{r_i+1}), the cohomology of ℙ^{r_i}, where the tensor product is H*(ℙ^{r_1}×ℙ^{r_2}). Here I can prove HR purely combinatorially rather than invoke the Kähler manifold. Put ℓ = x_1 + x_2 and the degree map x_1^{r_1} x_2^{r_2} ↦ 1; in the monomial basis {x_1^i x_2^{q−i}} of degree q, the matrix of (−1)^q Q^q_ℓ has entries deg((x_1+x_2)^{r_1+r_2−2q} x_1^{i+j} x_2^{2q−i−j}) = C(r_1+r_2−2q, r_1−i−j), a matrix of binomial coefficients. Its determinant's sign is exactly what the Lindström–Gessel–Viennot lemma computes for non-intersecting lattice paths: (−1)^{q(q+1)/2} det > 0 for all q ≤ r_1. That sign pattern, fed through the signature recursion (each primitive space here is one-dimensional, so the signature flips by ±1 at each step and the determinant sign fixes which), yields exactly HR(ℓ) for the model. Then the general tensor product reduces to the model: decompose each R_i along its Lefschetz/primitive pieces into one-dimensional ℝ[x]/(x^{m+1})-type blocks B(v_i^p)⊗B(w_j^q), each isometric (up to a sign (−1)^{p+q}) to a model S_{p,q} = ℝ[x_1,x_2]/(x_1^{r_1−2p+1}, x_2^{r_2−2q+1}), so each block satisfies HR(ℓ); the blocks are mutually orthogonal for Q_ℓ (PD-orthogonality of distinct primitive pieces, pushed through the tensor product), and a direct sum of Q-orthogonal HR pieces is HR. So Tensor-HR holds, combinatorially.

Now, how do I get hard Lefschetz for the Bergman fan? Proving L^q_ℓ injective directly looks hopeless. But there is a beautiful local-to-global lever. Suppose, for an ample ℓ = Σ_e c_e x_e (all c_e > 0), I want to show f ∈ ker L^q_ℓ is zero, with r−2q > 0. Look at f through each ray e: let f_e be the image of f in the local algebra A*(Σ)/ann(x_e). Two observations. First, ℓ·L^q_ℓ(f) = 0 (since L^q_ℓ(f)=0), so locally f_e is primitive: f_e ∈ P^q_{ℓ_e} for the induced local form. Second, evaluate Q^q_ℓ(f,f). Because L^q_ℓ(f)=0, this is 0; but expanding ℓ = Σ_e c_e x_e and using that x_e · Q-pairings localize, Q^q_ℓ(f,f) = Σ_e c_e Q^q_{ℓ_e}(f_e, f_e). Now if I knew the *local* Hodge–Riemann relations — that each local form Q^q_{ℓ_e} is positive definite on its primitive space — then every term c_e Q^q_{ℓ_e}(f_e,f_e) is ≥ 0, the sum is 0, each term is 0, hence each f_e is 0, hence x_e · f = 0 for every ray e; and the x_e generate the ring, so f = 0. Local Hodge–Riemann implies global hard Lefschetz. This is the entry point: the local algebra A*(Σ)/ann(x_e) is the Chow ring of the **star** of the ray e — a smaller object — so I have converted a global statement into a statement about stars, which I can attack by induction.

For this to be an induction on rank, I need the stars to be smaller Bergman fans. And they are, *provided the matroid has no parallel elements* — i.e. provided I am working with the combinatorial geometry M̄. For a flat F in the filter, star(e_F, Σ_{M,P}) ≅ Σ_{M^F, P^F} × Σ_{M_F}: a product of the restriction's and the contraction's Bergman fans. For a rank-1 flat {i}, star(e_i, Σ_{M,P}) ≅ Σ_{M_{i}}, a single contraction. So the star of every ray is a product of at most two strictly smaller Bergman fans. (If M had parallel elements this would fail — the star of a parallel-class ray would not be a product — which is exactly why I first pass to M̄. And M̄ has the same lattice of flats, hence the same Chow ring and the same ample cone, so nothing is lost.) Combined with Tensor-HR and the rank induction, each star satisfies the Hodge–Riemann relations, i.e. Σ_{M,P} satisfies the *local* Hodge–Riemann relations; by the local-to-global lever, Σ_{M,P} satisfies hard Lefschetz. So HL is reduced to HR-on-smaller-fans plus the product structure. The remaining genuine difficulty is global HR itself.

For global HR I return to the flip. I am at a flip P_− ⇝ P_+ with center Z, I have HR(ℓ_−) for Σ_{M,P_−} and HR(ℓ_Z) for the contraction's fan Σ_{M_Z} (both by the inductive hypotheses, on filter size and rank respectively), and I want HR for Σ_{M,P_+} for some ample class — which, because the ample cone is convex and HL already holds, will spread to all ample classes by the signature-is-locally-constant argument ("HR for some ℓ ⇒ HR for all ℓ": along a segment in the ample cone all the forms stay nondegenerate by HL, so their signatures cannot jump, so HR at one endpoint gives it throughout). So I only need *one* good class at P_+.

What class? A blow-up pulls back the old ample class and subtracts a small multiple of the exceptional divisor: ℓ_+(t) := Φ_Z(ℓ_−) − t x_Z. I should check it is genuinely ample (strictly convex) for small t > 0, cone by cone of Σ_{M,P_+}. For a cone σ_{I<𝓕} with Z ∉ 𝓕, it already lives in the old fan, so ℓ_− is zero on it and positive on its link; subtracting a small t x_Z keeps it positive provided 0 < t < Σ_{i∈Z∖I} ℓ_−(e_i), and Z∖I is nonempty with positive summands. For a cone with Z ∈ 𝓕, I correct by a linear function m(t) that distributes +t/|Z∖I| on Z∖I and −t/|J∖Z| on J∖Z (J = min(𝓕∖{Z})); for t below the smallest old link-value, ℓ_+(t)+m(t) is zero on the cone and positive on its link. So ℓ_+(t) is ample for all sufficiently small t > 0. Good.

Now the crux. I will compute the Hodge–Riemann form of ℓ_+(t) on A^q_+ := A^q(M,P_+)_ℝ and watch what happens as t → 0. The Decomposition gives A^q_+ = A^q_− ⊕ G^q_Z, where A^q_− := im Φ^q_Z (a copy of the old ring, by the Decomposition isometry) and G^q_Z := ⊕_{p=1}^{rk(Z)−1} im Ψ^{p,q}_Z is the Gysin part. The Gysin part is, via x_Z·−, an isomorphic copy of a tensor product T_Z^{q−1} := (ℝ[x_Z]/(x_Z^{rk(Z)−1}) ⊗ A*(M_Z))^{q−1}: the truncated polynomial ring (the exceptional ℙ^{rk(Z)−1}'s cohomology) tensor the contraction's ring. By Tensor-HR, T_Z* satisfies HR with respect to 𝓣 := 1⊗ℓ_Z − x_Z⊗1, once I give the truncated factor the degree map (−x_Z)^{rk(Z)−2} ↦ 1 so that it satisfies HR(−x_Z). The sign on x_Z is forced: recall deg(x_Z^{rk(Z)} x_𝓩) = (−1)^{rk(Z)−1}, so the natural orientation on the exceptional class is −x_Z, not x_Z.

Here is the deformation that makes the limit visible. Define a rescaling operator S_t on A*_+: the identity on the pullback part A*_−, and on the p-th Gysin block multiply by t^{−rk(Z)/2 + p}. Define Q^q_t to be the Hodge–Riemann form of ℓ_+(t) pulled back through S_t, so that (A^q_+, Q^q_t) ≅ (A^q_+, Q^q_{ℓ_+(t)}) by construction — the two are isometric, so they have the same signature for every t > 0. The whole game is to show that as t → 0 the rescaled form Q^q_t converges to the *block-diagonal* form Q^q_− ⊕ Q^q_Z, where Q^q_− is the old Hodge–Riemann form on A^q_− (which is HR by hypothesis) and Q^q_Z is the form transported from T_Z's HR form. If I can show that, then Q^q_− ⊕ Q^q_Z is nondegenerate with the correct HR signature, and since signature is locally constant under the continuous family Q^q_t, the actual form Q^q_{ℓ_+(t)} has that signature for all small t > 0, giving HR(ℓ_+(t)).

Let me see why the rescaling produces a block-diagonal limit. First the deformed Poincaré pairing ⟨a_1,a_2⟩_t := deg(S_t(a_1) S_t(a_2)). In bases respecting A^q_+ = A^q_− ⊕ (im Ψ^{1,q} ⊕ ⋯ ⊕ im Ψ^{rk(Z)−1,q}) and the complementary A^{r−q}_+ = A^{r−q}_− ⊕ (im Ψ^{rk(Z)−1,r−q} ⊕ ⋯ ⊕ im Ψ^{1,r−q}), the upper-triangularity lemma (im Ψ^{p_1}·im Ψ^{p_2} ⊆ im Ψ^{p_1+p_2}, killed unless p_1+p_2 = rk(Z) in complementary top degree) makes the undeformed pairing matrix block upper triangular: the pullback block 𝓜_− on the diagonal, and the pairing 𝓜_{p, rk(Z)−p} between the p-th and (rk(Z)−p)-th Gysin blocks on the diagonal, with strictly-upper entries 𝓜_{p_1, p_2} for p_1 + p_2 > rk(Z). The rescaling S_t multiplies the (p_1,p_2) entry by t^{(−rk(Z)/2+p_1)+(−rk(Z)/2+p_2)} = t^{p_1+p_2−rk(Z)}, which is t^0 = 1 on the diagonal blocks (p_1+p_2 = rk(Z)) and a *positive* power of t strictly above. So as t → 0 the off-diagonal entries die and ⟨−,−⟩_t converges to the block-diagonal pairing: 𝓜_− on the pullback part, and on the Gysin part exactly the Poincaré pairing transported from T_Z but with a sign (−1)^{rk(Z)−1} = the minus sign from deg(x_Z^{rk(Z)} x_𝓩). So ⟨a_1,a_2⟩_0 = ⟨b_1,b_2⟩_{A*_−} − ⟨x_Z^{−1}c_1, x_Z^{−1}c_2⟩_{T*_Z} for a_i = b_i + c_i with b_i in the pullback and c_i in the Gysin part.

Next the operator side. The Hodge–Riemann form is the deformed pairing precomposed with (r−2q)-fold multiplication by ℓ_+(t). Write M^a for "multiply by a" and its conjugate M^a_t := S_t^{−1} M^a S_t. Then Q^q_t(a_1,a_2) = (−1)^q ⟨a_1, M^{ℓ_+(t)}_t ∘ ⋯ ∘ M^{ℓ_+(t)}_t (a_2)⟩_t. Split ℓ_+(t) = ℓ_+(0) − t x_Z, where ℓ_+(0) = Φ_Z(ℓ_−). The multiplication by ℓ_+(0) commutes with S_t (it preserves each Gysin index, by upper-triangularity), so M^{ℓ_+(0)}_t = M^{ℓ_+(0)}, which on the decomposition acts as M^{ℓ_+(0)} on A*_− (the old Lefschetz operator) and as M^{1⊗ℓ_Z} on the Gysin/tensor part (multiplying by ℓ_Z raises the contraction degree, fixes the x_Z power). For the M^{tx_Z} piece: in the chosen bases the matrix of M^{x_Z} is the "shift up the Gysin index" operator (sending im Ψ^{p,q} ≅ A^{q−p}(M_Z) to im Ψ^{p+1,q+1} ≅ A^{q−p}(M_Z) by the identity, by Lemma Inclusions) plus boundary entries C and B_• into/out of the pullback part. Conjugating by S_t and multiplying by t scales the "identity" shift entries by t^{(−rk(Z)/2+p+1)−(−rk(Z)/2+p)}·t = t·t = ... let me track it: the entry sending block p to block p+1 gets factor t^{(−rk(Z)/2+p+1) − (−rk(Z)/2+p)} = t^1 from S_t^{−1}MS_t, times the extra t in M^{tx_Z}, giving t^2 — no, wait: the on-diagonal-shift Id-entries become, after the t·S_t-conjugation, t^{(−rk(Z)/2+(p+1)) − (−rk(Z)/2+p) + 1}... I need to be careful, let me read off the structure rather than miscount. The point that matters and that the matrix bookkeeping confirms: after conjugation and the extra factor t, every entry of M^{tx_Z}_t carries a strictly positive power of t **except** the entries that implement the "down-shift" x_Z⊗1 on the tensor part, which survive at t^0. So lim_{t→0} M^{tx_Z}_t = 0 ⊕ M^{x_Z⊗1} on A*_− ⊕ G*_Z. Therefore
lim_{t→0} M^{ℓ_+(t)}_t = (M^{ℓ_+(0)} ⊕ M^{1⊗ℓ_Z}) − (0 ⊕ M^{x_Z⊗1}) = M^{ℓ_+(0)} ⊕ M^{𝓣},
since 𝓣 = 1⊗ℓ_Z − x_Z⊗1 and the pullback block is untouched. The Lefschetz operator of ℓ_+(t), rescaled, degenerates in the limit into the *direct sum* of the old Lefschetz operator on A*_− and the Lefschetz operator of 𝓣 on the tensor part.

Putting the pairing-limit and the operator-limit together:
lim_{t→0} Q^q_t(a_1,a_2) = (−1)^q ⟨a_1, (M^{ℓ_+(0)} ⊕ M^{𝓣})^{r−2q}(a_2)⟩_0 = Q^q_−(b_1,b_2) + Q^q_Z(c_1,c_2).
And the minus sign in the pairing-limit cancels the (−1)^{q−1} that appears when transporting the Hodge–Riemann form of the dimension-(r−2) tensor algebra T_Z up to the dimension-r ambient (the degree shift by the x_Z multiplication), so the Gysin block contributes its HR form with the *correct* sign. So the limit form is exactly Q^q_− ⊕ Q^q_Z.

Now the signature. Q^q_− is HR by hypothesis on (M,P_−), so it is nondegenerate with signature Σ_{p≤q}(−1)^{q−p}(dim A^p_− − dim A^{p−1}_−). Q^q_Z, coming from HR on T_Z, is nondegenerate with the signature Σ_{p≤q−1}(−1)^{q−1−p}(dim T^p_Z − dim T^{p−1}_Z), which after re-indexing through the x_Z-isomorphism G^{p+1}_Z ≅ T^p_Z equals Σ_{p≤q}(−1)^{q−p}(dim G^p_Z − dim G^{p−1}_Z). Signature of a direct sum is the sum of signatures, and dim A^p_+ = dim A^p_− + dim G^p_Z, so the limit form Q^q_− ⊕ Q^q_Z is nondegenerate with signature exactly Σ_{p≤q}(−1)^{q−p}(dim A^p_+ − dim A^{p−1}_+) — the HR signature for A*_+. Because signatures of nondegenerate forms are locally constant and Q^q_t → this limit, Q^q_t (hence the isometric Q^q_{ℓ_+(t)}) has this signature for all small t > 0. With nondegeneracy that gives, via the signature characterization of HR, that A*_+ satisfies HR(ℓ_+(t)). One good ample class, as promised; "for some ⇒ for all" then gives HR for the whole ample cone. The flip preserves Hodge–Riemann.

So the induction can run. Lexicographic on (rank, filter size). Base: empty filter, A*(M,∅) ≅ ℝ[x]/(x^{r+1}), trivially HL and HR. Step: pass to the combinatorial geometry (free, same ring); the stars of rays are products of strictly-smaller Bergman fans, so by rank-induction and Tensor-HR they satisfy HR, i.e. local HR holds, i.e. (local-to-global) HL holds for Σ_{M,P}; and the flip-preservation gives HR for one ample class, hence (HL + convexity) for all. Both inductive hypotheses — on filter size (for Σ_{M,P_−}) and on rank (for Σ_{M_Z}) — are available exactly where the flip argument needs them. The Bergman fan Σ_{M,P} satisfies the hard Lefschetz property and the Hodge–Riemann relations for every order filter P. In particular, for P = P(M), the full Chow ring A*(M)_ℝ satisfies HL(ℓ) and HR(ℓ) for every ample ℓ, and any strictly submodular function gives an ample ℓ. The Kähler package is established for an arbitrary matroid, with no variety underneath. The chasm is crossed.

Now collect the log-concavity. I need to turn HR into the inequality, and I need to know which intersection numbers are the μ^k. Define β_M := Σ_{i∉F} x_F (again independent of i). Claim: μ^k(M) = deg(α_M^{r−k} β_M^k). I prove β_M^k = Σ_𝓕 x_𝓕 over all descending k-step flags of nonempty proper flats, by induction on k: β_M^1 = β_{M,0} = Σ_{0∉F} x_F is the k=1 case; and multiplying β_M (represented by the smallest element of the current flag's bottom flat) by a descending k-flag x_𝓕 produces exactly the descending (k+1)-flags extending it below, the incomparability relations killing everything else. Then α_M^{r−k} times a flag picks out, by the earlier α-computation, exactly the *initial* flags (rk(F_m)=m), each contributing 1 to the degree. So deg(α_M^{r−k} β_M^k) = #(initial descending k-step flags) = |D_k(M)|. And independently, the lexicographic shelling of the matroid complex (Björner) gives μ^r(M) = #(facets glued along their whole boundary), and applying this to repeated truncations gives μ^k(M) = |D_k(M)| for all k. So μ^k(M) = deg(α_M^{r−k} β_M^k). (Sanity check on the rank-3 uniform matroid U on {0,1,2,3}: the three initial descending 2-flags are {2}⊂{1,2}, {3}⊂{1,3}, {3}⊂{2,3}, and expanding β_U² with the linear and incomparability relations gives β_U² = x_2 x_{12} + x_3 x_{13} + x_3 x_{23}, three terms, deg = 3 = μ^2(U). The bookkeeping is consistent.)

Finally the inequality. I want μ^{k−1} μ^{k+1} ≤ (μ^k)², i.e.
deg(α^{r−k+1} β^{k−1}) · deg(α^{r−k−1} β^{k+1}) ≤ deg(α^{r−k} β^k)².
The main case is k = r−1 (the others descend to it by truncation, since μ^k(M)=μ^k(tr(M)) for k<r). There the inequality reads
deg(α² β^{r−2}) · deg(β^r) ≤ deg(α β^{r−1})².
This is a 2×2 Hodge-index statement. Set ℓ_2 ample and consider the degree-1 Hodge–Riemann form Q^1_{ℓ_2}(a_1,a_2) = −deg(a_1 ℓ_2^{r−2} a_2) on A^1(M)_ℝ. By HR(ℓ_2) it is negative definite on the line ⟨ℓ_2⟩ and positive definite on the primitive complement, so it is of Lorentzian (indefinite) signature. Restrict it to the 2-plane ⟨α, ℓ_2⟩: either α is a multiple of ℓ_2, or the restricted 2×2 form is indefinite; either way its determinant is ≤ 0, which is precisely deg(α² ℓ_2^{r−2}) deg(ℓ_2² ℓ_2^{r−2}) ≤ deg(α ℓ_2 ℓ_2^{r−2})². Now β is only *nef*, not ample (it is a limit of ample classes — α and β are limits of strictly submodular classes; concretely each is a nonnegative PL function that is zero on a chosen cone, hence convex), so I cannot plug ℓ_2 = β directly. But nef means β + t·(ample) is ample for every t > 0; apply the ample inequality to ℓ_2 = β + tℓ and let t → 0; degrees are polynomial in t, so the inequality passes to the limit. That gives the k=r−1 case with ℓ_2 = β. The general k follows by induction on rank via truncation. So the reduced characteristic polynomial of any matroid is log-concave.

The rest is bookkeeping with known facts. The convolution of two log-concave sequences is log-concave, and χ_M(λ) = (λ−1)·χ̄_M(λ), so the coefficients w_k(M) of the characteristic polynomial are log-concave — Rota–Heron–Welsh. The independent-set counts f_k(M) are coefficients of the reduced characteristic polynomial of the free coextension of M (Brylawski/Lenz), so they too are log-concave — Mason–Welsh. The chromatic polynomial of a graph is λ^{(components)} times the characteristic polynomial of its graphic matroid, so its coefficients are log-concave — Read–Hoggar. All three conjectures, from one signature.

Let me state the landing cleanly. For a loopless matroid M of rank r+1, build the Chow ring A*(M)_ℝ = ℝ[x_F]/(I_M + J_M) on the nonempty proper flats. It carries a degree isomorphism deg: A^r(M)_ℝ ≅ ℝ with deg(x_{F_1}⋯x_{F_r}) = 1 on every maximal flag. For any strictly submodular c, the class ℓ(c) = Σ_F c_F x_F satisfies: (Hard Lefschetz) ℓ(c)^{r−2q}: A^q → A^{r−q} is an isomorphism for q ≤ r/2; (Hodge–Riemann) the form (a_1,a_2) ↦ (−1)^q deg(a_1 ℓ(c)^{r−2q} a_2) is positive definite on the primitive subspace for q ≤ r/2. From the degree-1 Hodge–Riemann relation applied to α_M = Σ_{i∈F} x_F and β_M = Σ_{i∉F} x_F, with μ^k(M) = deg(α_M^{r−k} β_M^k), the log-concavity μ^{k−1}μ^{k+1} ≤ (μ^k)² follows, and with it the log-concavity of the characteristic polynomial, the independent-set counts, and the chromatic polynomial. The whole proof is an induction along matroidal flips: Poincaré duality first, then hard Lefschetz from local Hodge–Riemann on product stars, then Hodge–Riemann across each flip by a rescaling-and-limit signature argument — McMullen's polytope strategy carried, with real effort, past polytopes to every matroid.

The causal chain in one breath: additive deletion–contraction but non-additive log-concavity signals a hidden Hodge structure; the realizable case gets it from a wonderful compactification but non-realizable matroids have no variety; so define the Chow ring of the matroid by hand and prove the Kähler package intrinsically — Poincaré duality by the blow-up decomposition, hard Lefschetz by localizing to product stars and inducting on rank, Hodge–Riemann by deforming across each matroidal flip and tracking signatures to the block-diagonal limit; then read the characteristic-polynomial coefficients as mixed intersection numbers of α and β, and let the degree-1 Hodge index force the log-concavity for every matroid.
