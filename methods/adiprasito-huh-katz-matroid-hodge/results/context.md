# Context: Log-concavity of matroid invariants

## Research question

A matroid M on a finite set E abstracts the notion of linear independence common to graphs and finite vector configurations: it is given by a closure operator on subsets of E satisfying the Steinitz–MacLane exchange property, and its closed sets are called flats, organized into a graded lattice of flats whose top rank is r+1. Two numerical sequences attached to M carry positivity conjectures dating to the 1970s.

The first is the characteristic polynomial
χ_M(λ) = Σ_{I⊆E} (−1)^{|I|} λ^{crk(I)} = Σ_F μ(∅,F) λ^{crk(F)},
where crk is corank and μ is the Möbius function of the lattice of flats. Writing w_k(M) for the absolute value of the coefficient of λ^{r−k+1}, the **Rota–Heron–Welsh conjecture** asserts that the sequence w_k(M) is log-concave:
w_{k−1}(M) w_{k+1}(M) ≤ w_k(M)² for all 1 ≤ k ≤ r.
For graphs this specializes (via the graphic matroid) to the **Read–Hoggar conjecture** on the coefficients of the chromatic polynomial. The second is the sequence f_k(M) of numbers of independent k-element subsets; the **Mason–Welsh conjecture** asserts f_{k−1}(M) f_{k+1}(M) ≤ f_k(M)² for all 1 ≤ k ≤ r.

Log-concavity implies unimodality for a positive sequence. The goal is to prove these inequalities for a matroid M on the same footing as a graph or a vector configuration, including matroids for which there is no vector configuration over any field that produces them.

Both sequences obey an **additive** recursion. The characteristic polynomial satisfies deletion–contraction, χ_M = χ_{M\e} − χ_{M/e}; the independent-set counts satisfy f_k(A) = f_k(A\v) + f_{k−1}(A/v). The sum (or difference) of two log-concave sequences is, in general, not log-concave, so these recursions do not directly transmit the log-concavity property.

## Background

**Matroids and the lattice of flats.** A loopless matroid M of rank r+1 on E has a rank function rk, corank crk, and closure cl. Its nonempty proper flats form a graded poset P(M). For a flat F there is a restriction M^F (a matroid on F) and a contraction M_F (a matroid on E∖F); the flats of M_F are identified with flats of M containing F, and the flats of M^F with flats of M contained in F. Two reductions are standard and lattice-preserving: passing to the **combinatorial geometry** M̄ (the simple matroid with the same lattice of flats, obtained by deleting parallel elements), and **truncation** tr(M) (cap the rank at r, i.e. delete the top-rank flats). The combinatorial numbers above are computed from the lattice of flats alone, hence are invariants of M̄, and truncation relates them across ranks.

**The characteristic polynomial and shellability.** The reduced characteristic polynomial χ̄_M(λ) := χ_M(λ)/(λ−1) has coefficients (−1)^k μ^k(M). Björner's lexicographic shelling of the matroid (broken-circuit) complex shows that μ^r(M) counts the facets glued along their whole boundary, and applying this to repeated truncations identifies μ^k(M) with the number of "initial descending" k-step flags of flats; in particular all μ^k(M) are positive. These are pre-existing combinatorial facts about the numbers themselves.

**The realizable case.** When M is realized by vectors over a field, the combinatorial data acquire a geometric meaning. De Concini–Procesi built "wonderful" compactifications of hyperplane-arrangement complements; for such M the relevant numbers become genuine intersection numbers on a smooth projective variety, where classical positivity inequalities for intersection numbers (of Khovanskii–Teissier / Hodge-index type, parallel to Alexandrov–Fenchel for mixed volumes) are available. This produced proofs for realizable matroids (see Baselines).

**The combinatorial cohomology ring.** Independently of realizability, Feichtner and Yuzvinsky associated to ANY matroid a graded commutative ℝ-algebra
A*(M)_ℝ := S_M / (I_M + J_M), S_M = ℝ[x_F : F a nonempty proper flat of M],
where I_M is generated by the quadratic monomials x_{F_1} x_{F_2} for incomparable flats F_1,F_2, and J_M by the linear forms Σ_{i_1∈F} x_F − Σ_{i_2∈F} x_F for distinct elements i_1,i_2 of E. When M is realizable this ring is the Chow ring of the wonderful compactification; it is defined verbatim for non-realizable M as well. Geometrically it is the Chow ring of a smooth toric variety X(Σ_M) attached to the **Bergman fan** Σ_M (the fine subdivision of the tropical linear space of M), a unimodular fan whose cones σ_{I<F} are indexed by compatible pairs of a subset I and a flag F of flats. For non-realizable M the variety X(Σ_M) is noncompact and non-projective.

**The toolkit for fans.** On a unimodular fan Σ there is, with no reference to any variety: the group PL(Σ) of integral piecewise linear functions (a basis of Courant functions x_e, one per ray e); the quotient A^1(Σ) = PL(Σ)/M (linear functions); the notion of a function being **convex** / **strictly convex** around a cone, hence the closed **nef cone** and the open **ample cone** K_Σ ⊂ A^1(Σ)_ℝ of (classes of) strictly convex PL functions; the graded Chow ring A*(Σ) = ℤ[x_e]/(I_Σ+J_Σ); pullback ring maps to a subfan, to the **star** of a cone, and across a product of fans. Dual to A^k(Σ) are the **Minkowski weights** MW_k(Σ): integer functions on k-cones satisfying a balancing condition at every (k−1)-cone (Fulton–Sturmfels, McMullen, Lee). For a complete fan the top weights are constant (≅ ℤ), giving a degree map. Strictly convex PL functions on the permutohedral fan are exactly the **strictly submodular** functions c (c_{I_1}+c_{I_2} > c_{I_1∩I_2}+c_{I_1∪I_2}); their restrictions give ample classes on Σ_M, and I ↦ |I||E∖I| is one explicit example.

**The Kähler package, abstractly.** For a graded Artinian ℝ-algebra R* with R^0≅R^r≅ℝ and a degree map deg: R^r→ℝ, three properties can be stated purely algebraically for an element ℓ∈R^1: Poincaré duality (the pairing R^q × R^{r−q} → R^r is perfect); the hard Lefschetz property HL(ℓ) (multiplication L^q_ℓ: a ↦ ℓ^{r−2q} a is an isomorphism R^q→R^{r−q} for q ≤ r/2); and the Hodge–Riemann relations HR(ℓ) (the form Q^q_ℓ(a_1,a_2)=(−1)^q deg(a_1 ℓ^{r−2q} a_2) is positive definite on the primitive subspace P^q_ℓ=ker(ℓ·L^q_ℓ)). HR(ℓ) implies HL(ℓ) implies Poincaré-pairing nondegeneracy. These hold for the cohomology of smooth projective varieties and of compact Kähler manifolds, and (combinatorially) for simple polytopes; for the latter, McMullen gave a purely combinatorial, **inductive** proof — Hard Lefschetz and the Hodge–Riemann–Minkowski inequalities established by deforming the fan through a sequence of elementary modifications, propagating the properties one step at a time.

## Baselines

**Huh 2012 (Milnor numbers).** For a matroid realizable over a field of characteristic 0, realize it by a hyperplane arrangement and relate w_k(M) to the Milnor numbers of the singularity at the origin; these are mixed multiplicities, log-concave by Khovanskii–Teissier. This proved Read's conjecture for graphs. The argument uses an honest complex singularity, defined for characteristic-0 realizable matroids.

**Huh–Katz 2012 (intersection numbers on a toric variety).** For all realizable matroids (any field), identify the coefficients μ^k of the reduced characteristic polynomial as intersection numbers: the closure V of the graph of the Cremona transformation restricted to the arrangement complement has class [V] = Σ μ^k [ℙ^{r−k}×ℙ^k] in A_*(ℙ^n×ℙ^n), and log-concavity of the μ^k follows from Khovanskii–Teissier applied to the irreducible V. The identification uses Fulton–Sturmfels' combinatorial intersection theory and the Bergman-fan description (Ardila–Klivans). The inequality is imported from the projective variety V realizing M.

**Feichtner–Yuzvinsky 2004 (the ring without a variety).** Define A*(M) for arbitrary M by generators-and-relations as above. For realizable M it equals the Chow ring of the wonderful model; A^r(M) ≅ ℤ and the ring behaves like the cohomology of a smooth r-dimensional variety. For arbitrary M the ring is defined by the same generators and relations.

**McMullen 1993 (combinatorial Kähler package for polytopes).** For a simple polytope (equivalently its complete simplicial normal fan), prove Poincaré duality, Hard Lefschetz, and the Hodge–Riemann–Minkowski inequalities by a purely combinatorial induction, deforming the fan along elementary modifications and showing each property is preserved. This established the necessity half of the g-theorem on face numbers. The argument lives on a complete fan (the permutohedron / a simple polytope).

**Fulton–Sturmfels / Lee (weights and the dual side).** Minkowski weights MW_k(Σ) give a combinatorial homology dual to A^k(Σ), with a balancing condition and (for complete fans) a canonical top class. This provides duals and a degree map.

**Adiprasito–Sanyal (concentration of measure).** Log-concavity holds for a class of non-realizable matroids (Goresky–MacPherson c-arrangements), proved by measure-concentration estimates.

## Evaluation settings

The yardstick is not an experiment but a precise mathematical target. A solution must prove, for an **arbitrary** loopless matroid M (no realizability assumed) of rank r+1:
- log-concavity of (w_k(M)), the absolute coefficients of the characteristic polynomial (Rota–Heron–Welsh), and its corollary the unimodality;
- log-concavity of (f_k(M)), the independent-set counts (Mason–Welsh);
- as a special case, log-concavity of the chromatic-polynomial coefficients of any graph (Read–Hoggar).

The natural intermediate quantities are the coefficients μ^k of the reduced characteristic polynomial χ̄_M(λ)=χ_M(λ)/(λ−1), known to be positive and combinatorially interpretable (counts of initial descending flags / shelling facets); a proof of μ^{k−1}μ^{k+1} ≤ (μ^k)² for 0<k<r implies the rest, since the convolution of log-concave sequences is log-concave and the independent-set counts are coefficients of a reduced characteristic polynomial of a related matroid (Brylawski/Lenz). Test instances range from small explicit matroids (uniform matroids, the rank-3 matroids realizable over fields of prescribed characteristic, or over no field at all) to the general case; correctness is a complete proof, with the small cases serving as sanity checks on the constructions (e.g. computing the degree map on a uniform matroid).
