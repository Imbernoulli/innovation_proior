The thing I need to expose is not whether the network can fit the data. It can. The random-label evidence already tells me that a sufficiently large neural network can store arbitrary finite labels, and the small transformer I plan to use will have far more degrees of freedom than a small symbolic dataset needs. The hidden variable is which solution the optimizer is moving toward while the training set is already fit: a table-lookup solution that only stores observed facts, or a compact rule-like solution that also answers the unobserved cases.

So I need a setting where those two solutions make different predictions and where I can measure that difference exactly. Natural data is too soft for this. If I train on images or text, I do not know the exact rule, and I can always argue about distribution shift or semantic similarity. I want a finite world where every possible query has a known answer. A mathematical operation gives me that immediately: for each pair of elements, there is one result. If I reveal some entries and hide the rest, train accuracy measures whether the model remembers the revealed entries, while validation accuracy measures whether it has inferred the missing entries of the same object.

Now I have to be careful not to smuggle the rule into the input representation. If I write numbers as decimal digits, the model can use digit algorithms. If I write permutations in line notation, the notation itself exposes structure. That contaminates the experiment because a held-out answer might come from parsing the representation rather than learning the operation from examples. I therefore make every element an opaque token. The model sees one symbol for each residue or permutation, plus operation and equals tokens. It receives no internal decomposition of the element. The only path to a held-out cell is through the pattern of interactions among symbols across the equations it has seen.

The dataset becomes a full collection of token sequences of the form `<a> <op> <b> <=> <c>`. I take a random fraction of all sequences as training examples and keep the rest as validation. This split is important: train and validation are not different kinds of data, they are disjoint cells of the same table. I score only the right-hand side after the equals sign, because the prefix is the query and the answer side is what the model has to infer. For the binary-operation rows this means the answer symbol, and in a shifted sequence implementation it can also include the trailing end token. A small decoder-only transformer is enough; I am not trying to win an architecture contest. I want a standard overparameterized learner that I can train cheaply for many more steps than ordinary practice would tolerate.

I expect the first phase to be memorization. The model can see a finite set of short sequences, and nothing prevents it from fitting them directly. In the modular division example with half the table revealed, that is exactly what happens: train accuracy becomes close to perfect before about 10^3 optimization steps. At that point validation accuracy is still at chance. If I stop there, I call the run overfit and conclude that the model has not learned the operation.

But I do not stop, because the whole point is to test whether interpolation is the end of learning. I keep optimizing orders of magnitude longer. For a long time the held-out curve still looks dead. Then, around 10^5 steps in the dramatic modular-division run, validation accuracy begins to rise, and by about 10^6 steps it reaches the same near-perfect level as training accuracy. The training set is already fit, yet the function represented by the weights is still changing in a way that matters for unseen cells. That is the phenomenon: delayed generalization after memorization.

The loss curves make the distinction sharper. Training loss falls early. Validation loss first rises, because the model is becoming confidently wrong on held-out entries, and then it descends later. This resembles double descent in loss, but I should not collapse the two ideas. The double-descent ancestors vary model capacity or effective capacity around interpolation thresholds, and even epoch-wise double descent is usually about a peak in test error near the point of fitting. Here the model and task are fixed, the held-out accuracy is monotone rather than peaked, and the second descent occurs far after the first small training loss, sometimes tens of thousands of epochs later. I am not merely rediscovering double descent; I am isolating a training-time transition in a tiny exact rule-learning problem.

I next need to know whether the delay is a curiosity of one split or a controllable dimension. Data fraction is the cleanest knob. If I reduce the number of revealed entries, ordinary intuition says final generalization should get worse. What I see instead, over a range, is that final validation accuracy can still reach 100 percent, but the time to get there grows very rapidly. On S_5 product, near 25 to 30 percent of the table, removing 1 percent of the training data raises the median time to 99 percent validation accuracy by about 40 to 50 percent. Meanwhile the time to 99 percent train accuracy stays around 10^3 to 10^4 updates and often trends downward with less data. So the small dataset is not simply preventing generalization. It is charging optimization time for it. The effect becomes most visible near the smallest training fraction that still generalizes within the budget.

This also tells me why the experiment has to be long. A short run would systematically mislabel the model as a memorizer. The optimizer can spend a huge interval on a solution that fits training examples but does not yet implement the rule. Only later does the held-out behavior reveal that a different internal solution has taken over. Training budget is therefore not a nuisance hyperparameter; it is part of the scientific object.

I want internal checks that the task really behaves as an abstract-symbol problem. Modular arithmetic gives me one. For prime p, every nonzero residue mod p is a power of a primitive root, so multiplication mod p is isomorphic to addition mod p - 1 under a relabeling, and division matches subtraction under the same relabeling. With opaque symbols, the model should not distinguish those structures except through the operation table. The similar data requirements for `x - y` and `x / y` support that the setup is measuring symbol interactions rather than notation tricks. Symmetric operations are easier than related non-symmetric ones, which also makes sense because a transformer can cheaply ignore operand position when the rule is symmetric. Some operations still look effectively random to the model within budget, so this is not a guarantee that every finite rule becomes learnable.

Now I ask what pushes the optimizer from the memorizing solution toward the generalizing one. I sweep interventions on S_5. Weight decay stands out: it more than halves the data needed compared with most alternatives. Decay toward the initialization helps too, but decay toward the origin helps more, so "stay near where you started" is not the whole explanation. A prior for low-norm or near-zero-weight solutions explains part of the effect, but the geometry of the trajectory matters as well.

Noise points in the same direction. Minibatch noise and injected Gaussian weight or update noise help generalization. Prior sharpness work suggests that noisy small-batch training can bias optimization away from sharp minima and toward flatter regions. If the memorizing table-lookup solution is a sharp or high-norm way to fit the revealed entries, and the rule-like solution is flatter or lower-norm, then long optimization with decay and noise can gradually shift the model from one to the other. The preliminary sharpness check supports this: across S_5 runs stopped at a fixed time, validation accuracy and the sharpness proxy have a Spearman correlation around -0.79548, with p below 0.000014. That is not a proof, but it is exactly the kind of evidence I would expect if generalization appears only after the weights reach flatter regions.

I also need to rule out the easy capacity story. Maybe the model generalizes because it cannot memorize enough exceptions. The outlier experiment breaks that explanation. I corrupt some training answers and the model still reaches 100 percent train accuracy, fitting the false labels too. The point where it fits the training set is not much affected by the number of outliers. Small numbers of outliers do not much hurt generalization, while large numbers reduce the range of train fractions that generalize. So the model has enough capacity to memorize both the rule-consistent examples and explicit lies. Generalization is not forced by incapacity; it is a separate solution that optimization eventually favors.

Finally I look for evidence that the held-out accuracy corresponds to a real learned structure. The output-layer embeddings provide it. In modular addition, the symbols arrange into a circle, and adding a fixed residue walks around that circle. In S_5, embeddings cluster into cosets of a subgroup or its conjugates. These are not arbitrary high-dimensional accidents; they are recognizable traces of the algebra. Weight decay makes the structure cleaner, matching the ablation result.

The result is now more than "train longer." The experiment creates a microscope for a hidden transition. A network first finds a fast memorizing solution, then, under the right data fraction and optimizer pressures, moves toward a structured representation that implements the operation. The held-out curve turns that internal transition into an observable event. By using abstract-symbol operation tables and training far past interpolation, I can separate memorization time from rule-discovery time and show that generalization may emerge long after overfitting appears complete.
