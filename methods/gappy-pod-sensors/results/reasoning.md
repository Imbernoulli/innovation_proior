Let me start from what actually hurts. I have a high-dimensional state — a vorticity field on a fine grid, a pressure field over a surface, a $1024$-pixel image — call it $\mathbf{x}\in\mathbb{R}^n$ with $n$ in the tens of thousands or more. I cannot afford to instrument every component. I get to drop down $p$ point sensors, each of which reads exactly one entry $x_{\gamma_i}$ of the state, and from those $p$ numbers I want to reconstruct the *entire* field. So the question is brutally concrete: which $p$ locations $\gamma_1,\dots,\gamma_p$ should I pick?

The naive answer is "try them all and keep the best subset," and that dies immediately. There are $\binom{n}{p}=\frac{n!}{(n-p)!\,p!}$ choices of $p$ locations out of $n$. For $n$ in the thousands and $p$ in the dozens that's an astronomically large search; no amount of Moore's law saves me, because the count grows combinatorially, not polynomially. Whatever I do has to be a tractable surrogate for that search, something I can run with standard linear algebra and that scales to large $n$.

So why should sparse sensing even be possible? Because these states are not generic vectors in $\mathbb{R}^n$ — the physics lives on a low-dimensional attractor. A vortex-shedding wake, a face under different lighting, a global temperature map: each is built from a small number of dominant coherent structures. If I can write down those structures, then $\mathbf{x}$ is essentially determined by a handful of coordinates, and a handful of coordinates is exactly what a handful of sensors can pin down. The compressibility is the whole reason there's hope.

How do I get those structures from data? I collect a training set of snapshots, stack them as columns of $\mathbf{X}=[\mathbf{x}_1\,\cdots\,\mathbf{x}_m]\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times m}$, and take the singular value decomposition $\mathbf{X}=\boldsymbol{\Psi}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{V}^\top$. The left singular vectors $\boldsymbol{\Psi}$ are the POD modes — the same object that goes by Karhunen–Loève expansion, principal component analysis, empirical orthogonal functions. I keep the leading $r$ of them, $\boldsymbol{\Psi}_r\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times r}$. Why the leading $r$ and why this particular basis rather than, say, a generic Fourier or wavelet basis? Because of Eckart–Young: among all rank-$r$ matrices, $\boldsymbol{\Psi}_r\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_r\mathbf{V}_r^\top$ minimizes $\|\mathbf{X}-\tilde{\mathbf{X}}\|_F$. The truncated SVD is the *optimal* least-squares low-rank fit to my data, so for a fixed budget of $r$ modes no basis represents my specific signal class more compactly. That's exactly the leverage I want: a basis *tailored* to the data, not a universal one. Any state then approximately satisfies
$$\mathbf{x}\approx\boldsymbol{\Psi}_r\mathbf{a},\qquad \mathbf{a}=\boldsymbol{\Psi}_r^\top\mathbf{x}\in\mathbb{R}^r,$$
where I used $\boldsymbol{\Psi}_r^\top\boldsymbol{\Psi}_r=\mathbf{I}_r$ (the modes are orthonormal) to read off the coordinates by projection. So instead of recovering $n$ numbers I only need to recover $r\ll n$ numbers, the modal mixture $\mathbf{a}$.

Now connect this to the measurements. My $p$ point sensors form a matrix $\mathbf{C}\in\mathbb{R}^{p\times n}$ whose rows are canonical basis vectors $\mathbf{e}_{\gamma_i}^\top$ — row $i$ is all zeros except a $1$ in position $\gamma_i$ — so that $\mathbf{y}=\mathbf{C}\mathbf{x}=[x_{\gamma_1}\,\cdots\,x_{\gamma_p}]^\top$ literally reads off the chosen entries. Substituting the low-rank model,
$$\mathbf{y}=\mathbf{C}\mathbf{x}\approx\mathbf{C}\boldsymbol{\Psi}_r\,\mathbf{a}=\boldsymbol{\Theta}\,\mathbf{a},\qquad \boldsymbol{\Theta}:=\mathbf{C}\boldsymbol{\Psi}_r\in\mathbb{R}^{p\times r}.$$
And here $\mathbf{C}\boldsymbol{\Psi}_r$ has a clean meaning: it's just the $p$ rows of $\boldsymbol{\Psi}_r$ indexed by my sensor locations. In MATLAB-ese, $\boldsymbol{\Theta}=\boldsymbol{\Psi}_r(\gamma,:)$. So I have a small $p\times r$ linear system relating measurements to modal coordinates.

If I take at least as many sensors as modes, $p\ge r$, this system is (over)determined and I can solve it for $\mathbf{a}$ by least squares:
$$\hat{\mathbf{a}}=\boldsymbol{\Theta}^\dagger\mathbf{y}=(\mathbf{C}\boldsymbol{\Psi}_r)^\dagger\mathbf{y},\qquad \hat{\mathbf{x}}=\boldsymbol{\Psi}_r\hat{\mathbf{a}},$$
with $\boldsymbol{\Theta}^\dagger=\boldsymbol{\Theta}^{-1}$ collapsing to the plain inverse when $p=r$ makes $\boldsymbol{\Theta}$ square. I didn't invent this — it's exactly gappy POD, the trick Everson and Sirovich used to reconstruct images with missing pixels. Their framing is a mask vector $\mathbf{n}$ with $n_i=1$ where data is present and $0$ where it's missing, a "gappy" inner product $(\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v})_\mathbf{n}=\sum_i n_i u_i v_i$ that only counts observed entries, and then coefficients $\mathbf{b}$ chosen to minimize the repair error $E=\|\mathbf{g}-\sum_i b_i\boldsymbol{\phi}_i\|_\mathbf{n}^2$. Setting the gradient to zero gives a small normal-equations system $\mathbf{M}\mathbf{b}=\mathbf{f}$ with $M_{ij}=(\boldsymbol{\phi}_i,\boldsymbol{\phi}_j)_\mathbf{n}$ — and for *point sensors* the mask just selects the measured components, so $\mathbf{M}=\boldsymbol{\Theta}^\top\boldsymbol{\Theta}$, $\mathbf{f}=\boldsymbol{\Theta}^\top\mathbf{y}$, and $\hat{\mathbf{a}}=(\boldsymbol{\Theta}^\top\boldsymbol{\Theta})^{-1}\boldsymbol{\Theta}^\top\mathbf{y}=\boldsymbol{\Theta}^\dagger\mathbf{y}$. The least-squares reconstruction is gappy POD with the gaps being everywhere I didn't put a sensor. Everson and Sirovich even did the right physical demo — reconstructing a full airfoil pressure field from a few surface taps. But here's the thing they left wide open: *which* entries to keep. Their procedure took the mask as given, or chose it randomly. Nobody told me where to put the sensors so that $\mathbf{M}=\boldsymbol{\Theta}^\top\boldsymbol{\Theta}$ is actually invertible in a stable way. That's the gap I have to close.

So let me think about what goes wrong if I place sensors carelessly. The reconstruction hinges on inverting $\boldsymbol{\Theta}$ (or $\boldsymbol{\Theta}^\top\boldsymbol{\Theta}$). My measurements are never clean — there's sensor noise, $\mathbf{y}=\boldsymbol{\Theta}\mathbf{a}+\boldsymbol{\xi}$. Suppose $\boldsymbol{\Theta}$ is square and invertible for a moment, and write its SVD with singular values $\sigma_{\min}\le\cdots\le\sigma_{\max}$. Take the worst case where the true signal $\mathbf{a}$ aligns with the right singular vector of the *smallest* singular value and the noise aligns with that of the *largest*. Then passing through $\boldsymbol{\Theta}$ scales the signal by $\sigma_{\min}$ and the noise by $\sigma_{\max}$, so
$$\mathrm{SNR}_{\text{out}}=\frac{\sigma_{\min}}{\sigma_{\max}}\,\mathrm{SNR}_{\text{in}}=\frac{\mathrm{SNR}_{\text{in}}}{\kappa(\boldsymbol{\Theta})},\qquad \kappa(\boldsymbol{\Theta})=\frac{\sigma_{\max}(\boldsymbol{\Theta})}{\sigma_{\min}(\boldsymbol{\Theta})}.$$
And the inverse direction is just as bad: a random error in $\mathbf{y}$ gets amplified by $\sigma_{\max}(\boldsymbol{\Theta}^{-1})=1/\sigma_{\min}(\boldsymbol{\Theta})$ when I apply $\boldsymbol{\Theta}^{-1}$. So the condition number $\kappa(\boldsymbol{\Theta})$ is the multiplier on my reconstruction error. If I happen to place all my sensors where the POD modes barely differ — where the rows of $\boldsymbol{\Psi}_r$ are nearly parallel or tiny — then $\sigma_{\min}(\boldsymbol{\Theta})\to 0$, $\kappa$ blows up, and one noisy reading gets magnified into garbage across the whole reconstructed field. The placement problem *is* the conditioning problem. I want to choose the rows of $\mathbf{C}$ — the sensor locations — so that $\boldsymbol{\Theta}=\mathbf{C}\boldsymbol{\Psi}_r$ is as well-conditioned as I can make it.

Let me make "well-conditioned" precise by asking what the noise actually does to the estimate. With $\boldsymbol{\xi}\sim\mathcal{N}(0,\eta^2\mathbf{I})$, the least-squares estimator $\hat{\mathbf{a}}=\boldsymbol{\Theta}^\dagger\mathbf{y}$ has error covariance
$$\mathrm{Var}(\mathbf{a}-\hat{\mathbf{a}})=\eta^2\,(\boldsymbol{\Theta}^\top\boldsymbol{\Theta})^{-1}.$$
Choosing sensors to make reconstruction robust is *identical* to the classical statistician's problem of choosing experiments to estimate parameters with minimum variance. Each candidate sensor location $\gamma_i$ contributes a row $\boldsymbol{\theta}_i=\boldsymbol{\Psi}_r(\gamma_i,:)$, i.e. one "experiment" probing the $r$-dimensional parameter space, and the information accumulates as $\boldsymbol{\Theta}^\top\boldsymbol{\Theta}=\sum_{i=1}^p\boldsymbol{\theta}_i^\top\boldsymbol{\theta}_i$. To shrink the error I want this information matrix $\mathbf{M}_\gamma:=\boldsymbol{\Theta}^\top\boldsymbol{\Theta}$ as "large" as possible, where large means small inverse. Optimal experimental design gives me a menu of scalarizations of $\mathbf{M}_\gamma$ to maximize:
$$\text{E-optimal:}\ \max_\gamma\sigma_{\min}(\mathbf{M}_\gamma)=\min_\gamma\|\mathbf{M}_\gamma^{-1}\|_2,\quad \text{A-optimal:}\ \max_\gamma\mathrm{tr}(\mathbf{M}_\gamma)=\max_\gamma\sum_i\lambda_i,$$
$$\text{D-optimal:}\ \max_\gamma|\det\mathbf{M}_\gamma|=\max_\gamma\prod_i\lambda_i(\mathbf{M}_\gamma)=\max_\gamma\prod_i\sigma_i^2(\boldsymbol{\Theta}).$$
E-optimal fixes the worst direction, A-optimal the average squared error, D-optimal the *volume* of the error-covariance ellipsoid $\eta^2\mathbf{M}_\gamma^{-1}$ — since its volume is proportional to $\det\mathbf{M}_\gamma^{-1}=1/\det\mathbf{M}_\gamma$, maximizing $\det\mathbf{M}_\gamma$ shrinks the ellipsoid's volume. Each of these, taken literally, is still a search over $\binom{n}{p}$ subsets. So I've sharpened the objective but I haven't dodged the combinatorics yet.

The standard escape route is convex relaxation, and I should see why it doesn't satisfy me. Joshi and Boyd relax the hard binary choice — sensor $i$ in or out — to a continuous weight $\beta_i\in[0,1]$ and solve
$$\boldsymbol{\beta}_\star=\arg\max_{\boldsymbol{\beta}\in\mathbb{R}^n}\log\det\sum_{i=1}^n\beta_i\boldsymbol{\theta}_i^\top\boldsymbol{\theta}_i\quad\text{s.t.}\quad \sum_i\beta_i=p,\ 0\le\beta_i\le 1,$$
then read off the largest weights as the chosen sensors. The $\log\det$ makes it concave, so this is a genuine convex program with a performance certificate. But operationally it's heavy: each Newton/SDP iteration factors an $n\times n$ matrix, $O(n^3)$ per iteration with $O(n^2)$ storage, and I have to rerun the entire optimization for every new value of $p$. For a fine spatial grid with $n$ in the tens of thousands that's painful, and the rounding from fractional $\boldsymbol{\beta}$ back to a hard subset throws away the optimality guarantee anyway. I want something cheaper and structurally simpler — ideally one matrix factorization, total, that hands me a *ranked* list of sensors I can truncate at any $p$.

Let me lean on the D-optimal objective, because there's something special about a determinant: it factors. If I had a greedy way to build up a submatrix one row at a time, always grabbing the row that most increases the volume, I'd be doing greedy determinant maximization — and determinant maximization is exactly what one matrix factorization already does for free. Stare at column-pivoted QR. Businger and Golub designed it in the 1960s for least squares: factor $\mathbf{A}\mathbf{C}^\top=\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{R}$ where at each step $k$ you scan the not-yet-selected columns, pick the one with the largest residual Euclidean norm, swap it into position, and then apply a Householder reflector that zeros that column below the diagonal and — crucially — subtracts that column's contribution from every remaining column. After the reflector, the diagonal entry $r_{kk}$ holds the residual norm of the chosen column orthogonal to all previously chosen ones. Now recall that the volume (the absolute determinant) of a matrix equals the product of its singular values, and for the triangular factor that's the product of the diagonal magnitudes:
$$|\det\mathbf{A}|=\prod_i\sigma_i=\prod_i|r_{ii}|.$$
So at every step QR pivoting is choosing the column that maximizes the next $|r_{kk}|$, i.e. greedily maximizing the running product of diagonal entries, i.e. greedily maximizing the volume of the submatrix it has selected so far. That is *exactly* the greedy version of D-optimal subset selection. The pivoting even comes with a structural guarantee on how dominant those diagonals are: the triangular factor satisfies a diagonal-dominance inequality
$$|r_{ii}|^2\ \ge\ \sum_{j=i}^{k}|r_{jk}|^2,\qquad 1\le i\le k\le m,$$
which says each pivot's "captured energy" dominates what's left in the trailing columns — the picture of a well-conditioned, large-volume submatrix.

So suppose $p=r$ exactly, the square case, so I want $r$ sensor rows of $\boldsymbol{\Psi}_r$ that maximize $|\det\boldsymbol{\Theta}|=|\det(\mathbf{C}\boldsymbol{\Psi}_r)|$. I run column-pivoted QR on $\boldsymbol{\Psi}_r^\top$ (an $r\times n$ matrix whose *columns* are the candidate locations),
$$\boldsymbol{\Psi}_r^\top\mathbf{C}^\top=\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{R},$$
and take the first $r$ pivots as my sensor indices $\gamma$. Greedy volume maximization of the column-selected submatrix of $\boldsymbol{\Psi}_r^\top$ is greedy volume maximization of the row-selected submatrix $\mathbf{C}\boldsymbol{\Psi}_r=\boldsymbol{\Theta}$ — the pivots are precisely the rows that best condition $\boldsymbol{\Theta}$. This is the same selection that Q-DEIM arrives at in the reduced-order-modeling world, where Drmač and Gugercin showed that doing the point selection by pivoted QR of the mode matrix, instead of DEIM's older mode-by-mode residual greedy, gives a dramatically sharper bound on $\|(\mathbf{C}\boldsymbol{\Psi}_r)^{-1}\|_2$ — and that bound is the rigorous backbone of "QR picks good rows," so let me reconstruct it. Because $\boldsymbol{\Psi}_r$ has orthonormal columns, the leading $r\times r$ triangular block $\mathbf{T}$ of the QR factor satisfies $\mathbf{R}\mathbf{R}^\top=\mathbf{I}_r$, and combining that with the diagonal dominance,
$$1=\|\mathbf{R}(r,:)\|^2=|T_{rr}|^2+\sum_{j>r}|R_{rj}|^2\le (n-r+1)\,|T_{rr}|^2\ \Longrightarrow\ \min_i|T_{ii}|=|T_{rr}|\ge\frac{1}{\sqrt{n-r+1}},$$
so the smallest pivot can't collapse, and $\|(\mathbf{C}\boldsymbol{\Psi}_r)^{-1}\|_2=1/\sigma_{\min}(\boldsymbol{\Theta})$ stays controlled — only polynomially in $n$, versus DEIM's $(1+\sqrt{2n})^{\,r-1}$, which is exponential in the number of modes. That gap is the difference between a bound that means something and one that doesn't.

Let me also be clear *why* I'm reaching for DEIM's successor and not DEIM itself. DEIM (and its continuous parent EIM) build the selection mode by mode: pick $p_1=\arg\max_i|u_1(i)|$, then for each new mode $\mathbf{u}_j$ solve $\mathbf{S}_{j-1}^\top\mathbf{U}_{j-1}\mathbf{z}=\mathbf{S}_{j-1}^\top\mathbf{u}_j$, form the residual $\mathbf{r}_j=\mathbf{u}_j-\mathbf{U}_{j-1}\mathbf{z}$ (the part of mode $j$ not yet explained by the chosen points), and take $p_j=\arg\max_i|r_j(i)|$. It's a sensible greedy, but it recomputes a residual and a small solve at every iteration, its answer depends on the *ordering* of the modes (and is ill-defined when singular values are clustered, exactly the vortex-shedding-pair situation), and structurally it produces a *square* $p=r$ selection — one point per mode. Pivoted QR folds the whole thing into one factorization, is invariant to unitary reshuffling of the basis, reuses the column norms across steps so it's fast, and — this is what I need next — it generalizes past $p=r$.

Because $p=r$ is often not enough. When the singular values decay slowly, or when the sensors are noisy, $r$ measurements give me a $\boldsymbol{\Theta}$ that's square but uncomfortably close to singular, and I'd love to throw in extra sensors $p>r$ to lift $\sigma_{\min}$ and average down the noise — $\mathrm{Var}(\mathbf{a}-\hat{\mathbf{a}})=\eta^2(\boldsymbol{\Theta}^\top\boldsymbol{\Theta})^{-1}$ shrinks as I add informative rows. But now $\boldsymbol{\Theta}$ is tall, $p\times r$, and "maximize $|\det\boldsymbol{\Theta}|$" is meaningless for a non-square matrix. The right object is the D-optimal one I already wrote: maximize $\det(\boldsymbol{\Theta}^\top\boldsymbol{\Theta})$, the volume of the $r\times r$ information matrix. How do I greedily select $p>r$ rows to maximize that?

Here's the move. $\det(\boldsymbol{\Theta}^\top\boldsymbol{\Theta})$ is the product of the squared singular values of $\boldsymbol{\Theta}$, and the nonzero singular values of $\boldsymbol{\Theta}$ and of $\boldsymbol{\Theta}\boldsymbol{\Theta}^\top$ coincide, so
$$\det(\boldsymbol{\Theta}^\top\boldsymbol{\Theta})=\prod_{i=1}^r\sigma_i^2(\boldsymbol{\Theta})=\prod_{i=1}^r\sigma_i(\boldsymbol{\Theta}\boldsymbol{\Theta}^\top),$$
and $\boldsymbol{\Theta}\boldsymbol{\Theta}^\top=\mathbf{C}(\boldsymbol{\Psi}_r\boldsymbol{\Psi}_r^\top)\mathbf{C}^\top$ is just the $p\times p$ principal submatrix of the $n\times n$ Gram matrix $\boldsymbol{\Psi}_r\boldsymbol{\Psi}_r^\top$ indexed by my chosen points. The $(i,j)$ entry of $\boldsymbol{\Psi}_r\boldsymbol{\Psi}_r^\top$ is $\boldsymbol{\psi}_i\cdot\boldsymbol{\psi}_j$, the inner product of the mode-coordinate rows at points $i$ and $j$ — it's the Gram matrix *of the candidate locations*. So if I run column-pivoted QR on $\boldsymbol{\Psi}_r\boldsymbol{\Psi}_r^\top$,
$$(\boldsymbol{\Psi}_r\boldsymbol{\Psi}_r^\top)\,\mathbf{C}^\top=\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{R},$$
and take the first $p$ pivots, I'm greedily selecting $p$ points whose row-block of the Gram matrix has maximal volume in the $r$-dimensional row space. Picking the next point with the largest residual norm (most orthogonal to those already chosen) increases the leading $r$ singular values of $\boldsymbol{\Theta}\boldsymbol{\Theta}^\top$, hence increases $\det(\boldsymbol{\Theta}^\top\boldsymbol{\Theta})$ — exactly the D-optimal objective, now for $p>r$. The same greedy-volume logic, just applied to the Gram matrix so it can rank all $n$ candidate points rather than only filling an $r$-dimensional square.

So the algorithm falls out:
$$\gamma=\begin{cases}\mathrm{qrPivot}(\boldsymbol{\Psi}_r,\ r), & p=r,\\[2pt]\mathrm{qrPivot}(\boldsymbol{\Psi}_r\boldsymbol{\Psi}_r^\top,\ p), & p>r,\end{cases}\qquad \mathbf{C}=[\mathbf{e}_{\gamma_1}\ \cdots\ \mathbf{e}_{\gamma_p}]^\top.$$
I should weigh the cost. The $p=r$ branch factors an $n\times r$ matrix, $O(nr^2)$ — cheap, and for the cylinder wake with $r=42$ and $n\approx 90000$ that's the difference between trivial and hopeless. The $p>r$ branch factors the $n\times n$ Gram matrix, $O(n^3)$, with $O(n^2)$ storage — that's the price of oversampling, and for very large $n$ I'd downsample the candidate grid (say to a few thousand points) to keep it in memory. But even the expensive branch beats the convex relaxation on two counts: it's *one* factorization rather than one per Newton iteration, and the pivots come out *hierarchically ranked*, so a single run gives me the best $r$ sensors, the best $r+1$, the best $r+2$, … all at once. Need more sensors later? Just take more pivots; no re-solve. The convex program would have to start over for each $p$.

One more thing I want to settle: how many modes $r$? If I crank $r$ up past the point where the singular values carry real signal, the extra modes are noise, QR dutifully places sensors to resolve that noise, and reconstruction *overfits* — accuracy plateaus and then degrades as sensors get spent chasing meaningless modes. So $r$ should sit at the knee of the singular-value spectrum, where the modes stop containing coherent structure; a principled choice is the Gavish–Donoho optimal hard threshold for the spectrum under additive Gaussian noise. Set $r$ there, take $p\ge r$ pivots, and I get accurate reconstruction with the fewest sensors — pushing the elbow of the error-vs-sensors curve down and to the left.

Let me also note the boundary of what this buys me, by contrast with the random-measurement / compressed-sensing route. If I *didn't* have training data and didn't know the signal class, I'd be forced to use random measurements in a universal basis and recover by $\ell_1$ minimization, needing $\mathcal{O}(K\log(n/K))$ samples for a $K$-sparse signal and paying for a convex solve at recovery time. That's more general — it handles unknown signals — but it needs many more sensors, the placement is random rather than informative, and the recovery is expensive. The whole bet here is the opposite: I *do* have a tailored basis from data, the intrinsic rank $r$ is often far below the sparsity $K$ in a universal basis, so optimized point sensors in $\boldsymbol{\Psi}_r$ recover the field from far fewer measurements with nothing more than a least-squares solve. (As an aside, I shouldn't confuse the two procedures: running pivoted QR on a *universal* basis to "find a sparse solution" doesn't find the sparsest vector — the pivots depend only on the basis, not on the signal's frequency content, so it returns $p$ nonzeros unrelated to the true $K$. QR pivoting is a placement-and-conditioning tool, not a sparse-recovery tool.)

Now let me write it as code I can actually run, grounded in the two primitives that already exist — a truncated SVD and a pivoted QR.

```python
import numpy as np
from scipy.linalg import qr, lstsq, solve
from sklearn.decomposition import TruncatedSVD


def pod_basis(X, r):
    """Psi_r: leading r left singular vectors (POD modes) of snapshots X (m, n).
    Eckart-Young => the optimal rank-r least-squares basis for this data."""
    svd = TruncatedSVD(n_components=r).fit(X)        # rows of X are snapshots
    return svd.components_.T                          # (n, r), orthonormal columns


def select_sensors(Psi_r, p):
    """Greedy D-optimal sensor rows via column-pivoted QR (Businger-Golub).
       p == r : pivot Psi_r^T          -> maximize |det(C Psi_r)|        (Q-DEIM)
       p  > r : pivot Psi_r Psi_r^T    -> maximize det((C Psi_r)^T C Psi_r) (oversampled)"""
    n, r = Psi_r.shape
    if p == r:
        _, _, piv = qr(Psi_r.conj().T, pivoting=True)        # columns = candidate points
    else:                                                    # p > r
        _, _, piv = qr(Psi_r @ Psi_r.conj().T, pivoting=True)  # Gram of the points
    return piv[:p]                                            # ranked sensor locations gamma


def reconstruct(Psi_r, sensors, y):
    """Gappy-POD least squares: fit modal coords a to the measured entries, then lift.
       a_hat = Theta^dagger y,  x_hat = Psi_r a_hat,  Theta = Psi_r[sensors, :]."""
    Theta = Psi_r[sensors, :]                                 # C Psi_r, the measured rows
    if Theta.shape[0] == Theta.shape[1]:                      # p == r, square
        a_hat = solve(Theta, y)
    else:                                                     # p > r, least squares
        a_hat, *_ = lstsq(Theta, y)
    return Psi_r @ a_hat                                      # full-state estimate


# end to end
```

To recap the causal chain: the state is high-dimensional but low-rank, so a data-tailored POD basis $\boldsymbol{\Psi}_r$ (optimal by Eckart–Young) reduces recovery to estimating $r$ coordinates $\mathbf{a}$; a few point sensors give $\mathbf{y}=\boldsymbol{\Theta}\mathbf{a}$ with $\boldsymbol{\Theta}=\mathbf{C}\boldsymbol{\Psi}_r$ the selected rows, and least squares $\hat{\mathbf{a}}=\boldsymbol{\Theta}^\dagger\mathbf{y}$ reconstructs the field (gappy POD); noise gets amplified by $\kappa(\boldsymbol{\Theta})$, with error covariance $\eta^2(\boldsymbol{\Theta}^\top\boldsymbol{\Theta})^{-1}$, so good placement = well-conditioned $\boldsymbol{\Theta}$ = large $\det(\boldsymbol{\Theta}^\top\boldsymbol{\Theta})$, the D-optimal criterion; exact maximization is combinatorial, but column-pivoted QR greedily maximizes the diagonal product $\prod|r_{ii}|$ = the volume, so QR pivoting of $\boldsymbol{\Psi}_r^\top$ ($p=r$) or of $\boldsymbol{\Psi}_r\boldsymbol{\Psi}_r^\top$ ($p>r$) yields a single-factorization, hierarchically-ranked, near-D-optimal set of sensor locations.
