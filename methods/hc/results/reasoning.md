Let me start from what I actually have and what I actually want. I have a database `D` of `m` complete cases over `n` discrete variables `x_1, ..., x_n`, each case an integer-coded joint assignment, and I believe — this is my modeling premise — that the data was generated by some Bayesian network I can't see: a directed acyclic graph `G` together with conditional probability tables, where the graph asserts that each variable depends only on its parents, so the joint factors as `p(x_1, ..., x_n) = ∏_i p(x_i | Π_i)`. What I want back is that graph, or as much of it as the data can possibly tell me. The phrase "as much as it can possibly tell me" is already nagging at me, but let me set it aside and first nail down the more basic question: given two candidate structures, how do I say which one the data prefers? Until I can score a single structure I have nothing to search.

So fix a structure `B_S` and ask: how probable is it, given the data? That's `p(B_S | D)`. By Bayes, `p(B_S | D) = p(B_S) · p(D | B_S) / p(D)`. The denominator `p(D) = Σ_{B_S} p(B_S) p(D | B_S)` is a sum over all structures — and there are astronomically many, so I cannot compute it. But I don't have to. `p(D)` is the *same constant* for every structure, so when I compare two structures it cancels. All I ever need is the relative size of the numerator `p(B_S) p(D | B_S)`, which is the joint `p(B_S, D)`. Good — comparison only needs a quantity I can hope to compute, not the normalizer I can't.

Now the load-bearing piece is `p(D | B_S)`, the probability of the data given the *structure* — note, given the structure, not given a particular set of CPT numbers. The structure fixes the graph but not the table entries `Θ`, and I'm uncertain about those. The honest thing is to integrate them out against a prior: `p(D | B_S) = ∫ p(D | B_S, Θ) p(Θ | B_S) dΘ`. This is not the maximized likelihood of one best-fit `Θ`; it's an *average* over all parameter settings weighted by the prior. And that distinction is going to do real work for me, because an average over parameters automatically guards against overfitting — a structure with many parents has a high-dimensional `Θ`, its prior mass is spread thin, and the average likelihood gets discounted relative to a leaner structure that happens to fit nearly as well. So this marginal likelihood has a built-in complexity penalty, for free, with no explicit regularizer. That's exactly the kind of criterion I want: a clean probabilistic object, not an ad-hoc fit-minus-penalty.

But an integral over all the table entries of a graph — that sounds intractable. Let me see if I can grind it into closed form, because if I can't compute it cheaply from the data the whole approach is dead on arrival. Let me lay out the assumptions that would make the integral collapse, and check each is reasonable. First, the data are an i.i.d. multinomial sample from a fixed network — fixed meaning the generating process doesn't drift over the cases. Second, I'll need the parameters to be independent across the pieces of the network: the conditional distribution of `x_i` given one parent configuration is, a priori, independent of the conditional distribution of any other variable, and of `x_i` under any *other* parent configuration. Within a single conditional distribution the entries are of course coupled — they sum to one — but across distributions, independent. Third, the data is complete, no missing values; that's true here, the cases are fully observed. With those, `p(D | B_S, Θ)` factors over cases and within a case over variables, and the prior `p(Θ | B_S)` factors over the local pieces, so the whole integral factors into a product of *separate* one-dimensional-family integrals, one per (variable, parent-configuration) pair. That's the moment it stops being one monstrous integral and becomes many tiny ones.

Each tiny integral is: I have a multinomial over the `r_i` states of `x_i` in parent-configuration `j`, with parameters `Θ_ij = (θ_ij1, ..., θ_ijr_i)` summing to one, and I've observed counts `N_ijk` = number of cases where `x_i = k` and `Π_i = j`. I need `∫ ∏_k θ_ijk^{N_ijk} · p(Θ_ij) dΘ_ij`. The likelihood here is a product of powers of the `θ`s; what prior would make this integrable in closed form? The product-of-powers form is itself the kernel of a Dirichlet distribution. So if I take the prior on `Θ_ij` to be Dirichlet with exponents `N'_ijk` — `p(Θ_ij) ∝ ∏_k θ_ijk^{N'_ijk − 1}` — then prior times likelihood is again a Dirichlet kernel, `∏_k θ_ijk^{N'_ijk + N_ijk − 1}`, and the integral is just the Dirichlet normalizing constant, which I know. The Dirichlet is the conjugate prior for the multinomial; choosing it is not arbitrary, it is the family that keeps the posterior in the same form and the integral elementary. The `N'_ijk` are prior pseudocounts — "imaginary" prior observations — strictly positive.

The Dirichlet normalizer is a ratio of Gamma functions. The integral of `∏_k θ_k^{a_k − 1}` over the simplex is `∏_k Γ(a_k) / Γ(Σ_k a_k)`. So the prior normalizer (exponents `N'_ijk`) is `∏_k Γ(N'_ijk) / Γ(N'_ij)` with `N'_ij = Σ_k N'_ijk`, and the prior-times-likelihood normalizer (exponents `N'_ijk + N_ijk`) is `∏_k Γ(N'_ijk + N_ijk) / Γ(N'_ij + N_ij)` with `N_ij = Σ_k N_ijk`. The integral I want is the second divided by the first:

```
∫ ∏_k θ_ijk^{N_ijk} p(Θ_ij) dΘ_ij = [ Γ(N'_ij) / Γ(N'_ij + N_ij) ] · ∏_k [ Γ(N'_ijk + N_ijk) / Γ(N'_ijk) ].
```

Multiply these over all parent-configurations `j` of `x_i`, over all variables `i`, and tack on the structure prior `p(B_S)`, and the marginal likelihood is in closed form:

```
p(D, B_S) = p(B_S) · ∏_i ∏_{j=1}^{q_i} {
    [ Γ(N'_ij) / Γ(N'_ij + N_ij) ]
    · ∏_{k=1}^{r_i} [ Γ(N'_ijk + N_ijk) / Γ(N'_ijk) ]
}.
```

where `r_i` is the number of states of `x_i` and `q_i = ∏_{p ∈ Π_i} r_p` is the number of joint configurations its parents can take. Let me sanity-check it degenerates correctly. If I set every pseudocount `N'_ijk = 1`, the prior is uniform on each simplex, and using `Γ(x+1) = x Γ(x)` the term `Γ(N'_ij) = Γ(r_i) = (r_i − 1)!`, `Γ(N'_ijk) = Γ(1) = 1`, `Γ(N'_ijk + N_ijk) = Γ(1 + N_ijk) = N_ijk!`, so the per-family factor becomes `(r_i − 1)! / (N_ij + r_i − 1)! · ∏_k N_ijk!`. Let me put a number to it to be sure I haven't slipped a factorial: a single config with counts `(4, 2, 3)`, so `r = 3`, `N_ij = 9`. The factorial form gives `2! / 11! · 4! 2! 3! = 2 / 39916800 · 24·2·6 = 5760 / 79833600 ≈ 1.4430e-5`. Evaluating the Gamma-ratio expression directly — `Γ(3)/Γ(12) · Γ(5)Γ(3)Γ(4) / (Γ(1)Γ(1)Γ(1))` — lands on the same `1.4430e-5`. The two agree, so the reduction is right: this is the K2 `α = 1` score, a clean factorial computable from counts alone (though, as I'll find, not generally score-equivalent). The closed form is real and the old special case falls out of it.

Now the structure of this score is something I want to stare at, because it's going to decide how I search. The total score is a *product over nodes* — every factor depends only on `x_i` and the counts conditioned on its parents `Π_i`. Take the logarithm and it's a *sum* over nodes, `log p(D, B_S) = Σ_i s(x_i | Π_i)`, with each `s` a local family score. This is decomposability, and it's the single most important property I have. Why? Because if I change the structure by touching only the parents of one node `x_j` — say I add an arc into `x_j`, or remove one — then every term in that sum stays fixed except `s(x_j | Π_j)`. To compare the new structure to the old, I don't rescore the whole graph; I recompute one local term. The cost of evaluating a structural change is local, not global. That's the lever that makes searching this gigantic space even thinkable.

OK, so I can score a structure, and I can score a *small change* to a structure cheaply. Now the question I shelved: which structure do I pick? The naive answer is enumerate them all and take the max. Let me check whether that's remotely feasible. Robinson worked out how many DAGs there are on `n` nodes: 3 for `n = 2`, 25 for `n = 3`, about 29,000 for `n = 5`, and around `4.2 × 10^18` for `n = 10`. Super-exponential. For Win95pts at 76 nodes the count is a number with hundreds of digits. Enumeration is out, completely. Wall. I need heuristic search, and the decomposability lever is what's going to pay for each step of it.

Let me look at the cleverest exact thing first, because if a tractable optimum exists in some restricted regime I should understand it before settling for a heuristic. Restrict every node to at most *one* parent. Then by decomposability the best structure maximizes `Σ_i s(x_i | Π_i)` with each `Π_i` either empty or a single node. Define an edge weight `w(x_i, x_j) = s(x_i | x_j) − s(x_i | ∅)` — the local gain from giving `x_i` the single parent `x_j` versus none. Maximizing the total score becomes choosing, acyclically, the best incoming arc for each node, which is a maximum-branching problem on a weighted digraph, solvable in polynomial time. And if the score is symmetric under edge reversal — `w(x_i, x_j) = w(x_j, x_i)`, which I'll come back to — it reduces to an undirected maximum-weight spanning forest, a Chow–Liu tree. So there *is* an exact, polynomial optimum, but only inside this restriction. And the at-most-one-parent restriction is a straitjacket: in any real domain, a symptom has several causes, a node has several parents. The moment I allow two or more parents per node, finding the top-scoring structure is NP-hard — this is established even for a score-equivalent metric. So the exact route exists only in a regime too poor to be useful, and the general problem is genuinely hard. Heuristic it is.

What's the standard heuristic, and where does it fall short, so I know what I'm fixing? The greedy parent-selection procedure: exploit decomposability to its limit by optimizing each node's parent set *independently*. Pick a topological ordering of the variables. For each node `x_i`, start with no parents; then repeatedly look at the nodes that come *before* `x_i` in the ordering and add the single one whose addition most increases the local score `s(x_i | Π_i)`, stopping when no available addition improves the score (or a parent cap is hit). The ordering is doing a lot of quiet work: because you only ever add parents from among predecessors, the result is automatically acyclic — no two arcs can ever close a cycle. And it's fast, low-order polynomial with the Gammas precomputed; it reconstructed the 37-node ALARM network from 10,000 sampled cases up to a single missing and a single extra arc, and from only 3,000 cases got essentially the same graph. So the score-plus-greedy idea clearly works.

But there's the rub, and it's fatal for my setting. The whole thing is hostage to the ordering. The ordering is exactly the topological order of the true DAG — which is to say, it presupposes you already know the parent/child direction of every dependency before you start. Where would I get that for purely observational data on Win95pts or Hailfinder? If the ordering is wrong, the procedure happily learns the wrong arcs: a child placed before its true parent in the ordering can never receive that parent, so the dependency gets reversed or shoved onto some other node. Garbage ordering in, garbage structure out. And I have no ordering. Wall.

So I have to drop the ordering assumption — and the instant I do, acyclicity stops being free. Without an ordering to lean on, when I consider adding an arc `i → j` I have to *actively check* that it doesn't create a directed cycle. That's the price of freedom, and it's a price I can pay: a reachability check, is there already a directed path from `j` back to `i`? If yes, the arc `i → j` would close a cycle and I must reject it. So the move set has to operate on the whole DAG, with acyclicity enforced explicitly at every step, instead of being guaranteed by an ordering I don't have.

Let me think about what moves I even have, working directly in the space of DAGs. Take the current graph. For any ordered pair of nodes `(i, j)`: if there's no arc between them, I could *add* `i → j` (or `j → i`). If there's an arc `i → j`, I could *delete* it, or I could *reverse* it to `j → i`. Add, delete, reverse — those are the elementary single-arc edits, and any DAG is reachable from any other by a sequence of them, so the move set is rich enough to wander the whole space. Each move, of course, subject to "the result must remain acyclic." Why include reverse at all, when a reverse is just a delete followed by an add? Because the intermediate — delete `i → j`, then later add `j → i` — passes through a graph with *neither* arc, and a greedy step-by-step search might never take the first step if removing `i → j` alone lowers the score, even though the reversed arc would raise it. Reverse lets the search cross that valley in one atomic move that's scored as a unit. It's the move that lets me fix an arc I oriented backwards without first throwing it away.

Now, how cheap is each move, thanks to decomposability? Adding or deleting an arc `i → j` changes the parent set of *only* `x_j`, so the change in total score is just `Δ = s(x_j | Π_j^new) − s(x_j | Π_j^old)` — one local recompute. Reversing `i → j` to `j → i` changes the parents of `x_j` (loses `i`) *and* the parents of `x_i` (gains `j`), so `Δ = [s(x_j | Π_j^new) − s(x_j | Π_j^old)] + [s(x_i | Π_i^new) − s(x_i | Π_i^old)]` — two local recomputes. Everything else in the score is untouched. And since I'll be evaluating the same local family scores over and over as I sweep candidate moves, I should *cache* them: key a local score by the node and its sorted parent set, compute once, reuse. Decomposability plus caching is what turns "rescore a huge graph for every one of `O(n^2)` candidate edits each sweep" into "recompute one or two small local terms per edit, mostly from cache."

So the loop writes itself. Start from the empty graph — no arcs, every node parentless. Repeat: scan every eligible move (add `i → j` for non-adjacent pairs that stay acyclic; delete `i → j` for existing arcs; reverse `i → j` for existing arcs that stay acyclic after flipping), compute its score delta, and remember the single best improving move. If the best move improves the score, apply it, update the cached local score(s) of the touched node(s), and loop. If no move improves the score, stop — I'm at a local optimum where no single-arc edit helps. That's the hill-climb: at each step take the steepest improving step in the neighbourhood of single-arc edits.

Two things I have to be careful about. First, the acyclicity guard for reverse is subtle. To test whether reversing `i → j` to `j → i` stays acyclic, I want to know if there's already a directed path from `i` to `j` *not going through the arc `i → j` itself*. The clean way: temporarily remove `i → j`, then ask "is `j` reachable from `i`?" If `j` is still reachable from `i` without that arc, then adding `j → i` would close a cycle (a path `i → ... → j → i`), so the reverse is illegal. If not, it's safe; restore the arc and record the reversed move. Let me convince myself this isn't off by one alternate path. Take three nodes with `0 → 1` and `1 → 2`, and ask to reverse `0 → 1`. Remove `0 → 1`: now `0` has no out-edges at all, so `1` is not reachable from `0` — the guard says legal, and indeed flipping to `1 → 0` gives `1 → 0`, `1 → 2`, no cycle. Correct. Now perturb: arcs `0 → 1`, `0 → 2`, `2 → 1`, again reverse `0 → 1`. Remove `0 → 1`: is `1` reachable from `0`? Yes — `0 → 2 → 1` survives. The guard says illegal, and rightly: adding `1 → 0` would close `0 → 2 → 1 → 0`. So the "remove-then-test-reachability" guard distinguishes exactly the two cases — it's the alternate path through other nodes, not the arc itself, that I have to catch, and removing the arc first is what isolates it. For a plain add `i → j`, the test is simply: is `i` reachable from `j`? (i.e., is there already a path `j → ... → i`?) — if so, `i → j` closes a cycle. Reachability is a depth-first search over the adjacency, cheap enough at these sizes.

Second — and this is the "as much as the data can tell me" issue I parked at the very start. I have to face that some structures are statistically indistinguishable. Consider `x → y → z`, `x ← y → z`, and `x ← y ← z`: all three assert exactly the same independence, `x ⫫ z | y`, and nothing else. From observational data alone, no criterion based on `p(D | B_S)` can prefer one over another — they generate the same family of joint distributions. In fact a good score *should* assign them equal value; this is score equivalence, the property that two structures asserting the same conditional independences (isomorphic, Markov-equivalent structures) get the same score. The set of all structures equivalent to a given one is its Markov equivalence class, and it's characterized by two invariants: the undirected skeleton (which pairs are adjacent) and the v-structures (the colliders `a → c ← b` with `a`, `b` non-adjacent), both of which *are* identifiable from independences. Everything else — the orientation of the "covered" edges — is free within the class. So whatever DAG my hill-climb lands on, the arc directions on those covered edges are arbitrary; two runs could land on different DAGs in the same class with the same score. The object I can actually claim to have recovered is the *class*, not the particular DAG. The standard summary of a class is the CPDAG: keep an edge directed if every member of the class orients it the same way (a compelled edge), and leave it undirected if its orientation varies within the class. So after hill-climbing to a DAG, I must collapse it to its CPDAG before returning anything — otherwise I'm overclaiming orientations the data never determined. That collapse is a known operation (read off the skeleton and v-structures, then apply the orientation rules), and I'll use the library routine `dag2cpdag` for it.

That score-equivalence requirement loops back and constrains the pseudocounts `N'_ijk`, and I should stop and actually test whether the natural choices satisfy it rather than just hoping. The smallest case where score equivalence has teeth is two binary variables `x`, `y`: the structures `x → y` and `y → x` assert the *same* (empty) set of independences, so a score-equivalent metric must give them an equal value. Let me grind both scores out by hand on a concrete dataset and compare. Take 20 cases with joint counts `N(x,y)` = `[[7, 3], [2, 8]]` (rows indexed by `x`, columns by `y`), deliberately asymmetric so any orientation-dependence would show.

For `x → y` the log-score is `s(x | ∅) + s(y | x)`. The parentless `x` has one configuration, with child counts `(N_{x=0}, N_{x=1}) = (10, 10)` (row sums); `y` has parent `x`, two configurations, with child counts `(7, 3)` and `(2, 8)`. For `y → x` it's `s(y | ∅) + s(x | y)`: parentless `y` has child counts `(9, 11)` (column sums), and `x | y` has configurations `(7, 2)` and `(3, 8)`. These are genuinely different families of counts — different parent configs, different cell tallies — so there is no a-priori reason the two sums should match; if they do, it's the pseudocounts doing the work.

Plugging the unit-pseudocount (K2) form `N'_ijk = 1` into both — using the factorial reduction I just derived — I get `x → y = −28.5613` and `y → x = −28.5530`. They differ, by about `0.0083`. So K2 is *not* score-equivalent: it would let the climb prefer one orientation of an undirected edge over the other purely as an artifact, chasing structure the data can't support. That's the defect I have to design out.

Now try tying the pseudocounts to a prior. Specifying a separate pseudocount for every (node, parent-config) of every structure by hand is hopeless, so tie them to two inputs: a prior joint distribution over the domain (prior belief about the next case) and a single scalar `N'`, the equivalent sample size — how many prior "imaginary" cases the prior is worth — via `N'_ijk = N' · p(x_i = k, Π_i = j | prior)`. The interpretation is clean: the pseudocount for a cell is `N'` times the prior probability of landing in that cell, so the total prior mass over all cells is `N'` — the prior is worth `N'` cases, distributed according to the prior joint. Specialize the prior to the most ignorant choice, a *uniform* joint over the whole domain: then `p(x_i = k, Π_i = j) = 1 / (r_i · q_i)`, and the pseudocounts become

```
α_ijk = N' / (r_i · q_i),     α_ij = Σ_k α_ijk = N' / q_i,
```

a single uniform pseudocount per child-state-per-parent-config, scaled by the equivalent sample size and split evenly across the `r_i · q_i` cells of the family. It needs no elicited prior knowledge — just the one scalar `N'`. But "this construction is score-equivalent" is exactly the kind of claim I was just burned by with K2, so I should re-run the same two-node test with these pseudocounts before I trust it. Same counts `[[7, 3], [2, 8]]`, now with `α_ijk = N'/(r_i q_i)`. For `x → y`: `x` has `r=2`, `q=1`, so `α = N'/2` per cell; `y | x` has `r=2`, `q=2`, so `α = N'/4` per cell. For `y → x` the cardinalities are identical by symmetry of the binary case (`r=2` everywhere) — and crucially the *parent-config count* `q` also matches between the two orientations (both have one parentless node with `q=1` and one single-parent node with `q=2`), so the pseudocount magnitudes line up. With `N' = 1` I get `x → y = −30.34166054` and `y → x = −30.34166054`; the two agree to the last printed digit (their difference is `0`). Bumping to `N' = 10` they again coincide (both `−27.5524338`, difference `≈ 3.5e-15`, pure floating-point noise). So the uniform-equivalent pseudocounts really do equalize the two orientations, while the unit pseudocounts did not — the equality is not something I assumed, it survived a direct numeric check on an asymmetric dataset at two different `N'`. That's the property I needed: the search can't be tricked into preferring an arc direction that the data doesn't distinguish. The scalar `N'` remains a gain/smoothing control: small `N'` lets the data dominate the prior joint quickly; large `N'` keeps the marginal likelihood anchored to it. The local score routine I am using defaults to `N' = 1`.

Let me write the local family score in the log domain, because the products of Gammas underflow badly for any real database and addition of `lgamma` is the numerically sane way. For node `i` with parent set `Π_i`, the mathematical sum ranges over all `q_i` parent configurations; if a configuration is unobserved then `N_ij = 0` and all `N_ijk = 0`, so its Gamma terms cancel to zero in log space, which is why the implementation only has to loop over the observed configurations:

```
s(x_i | Π_i) =
    |Π_i| log(ρ / (n - 1)) + (n - 1 - |Π_i|) log(1 - ρ / (n - 1))
    + Σ_j [ lgamma(α_ij) − lgamma(N_ij + α_ij)
            + Σ_k ( lgamma(N_ijk + α_ijk) − lgamma(α_ijk) ) ].
```

Here `ρ` is the structure-prior hyperparameter used by the implementation; with no parameters supplied it is `1`, so each possible parent gets local prior probability `1 / (n - 1)`. That first line is still decomposable because it depends only on this node's parent count. The Gamma-ratio line is the log of the per-node marginal-likelihood factor with the uniform-equivalent pseudocounts, and the total log-score is `Σ_i s(x_i | Π_i)`. One small but important convention to get right: I'll have the local score routine return the *negative* of this quantity, so that "better structure" means "smaller score" — then the hill-climb is looking for the move with the most-negative delta, and "improving" reads as `Δ < 0`. I have to keep that sign convention consistent between the score and the comparison. Concretely the comparison becomes: a candidate move is recorded only if its delta beats the current best by a small margin, `Δ < best_delta − ε` with `ε` a tiny tolerance like `1e-6` to avoid thrashing on numerically-zero improvements.

Now let me write the procedure as the code I'd actually ship, filling the one empty slot — the search — in the score-based structure-search harness. The harness already gives me the decomposable, cached local score (`score_func.score(i, parents)`, lower better), the adjacency matrix with a reachability test, and the graph datatypes. I keep a vector of current per-node local scores so a delta is `new_local − current_local`; I cache-update only the touched node(s) after each accepted move; and I add the final collapse from the learned DAG to its CPDAG.

```python
def run_causal_discovery(X: np.ndarray) -> GeneralGraph:
    """
    Input:  X of shape (n_samples, n_variables), integer-encoded discrete data
    Output: estimated CPDAG as causallearn.graph.GeneralGraph.GeneralGraph
    """
    from causallearn.score.LocalScoreFunctionClass import LocalScoreClass
    from causallearn.score.LocalScoreFunction import local_score_BDeu
    from causallearn.utils.DAG2CPDAG import dag2cpdag

    N = X.shape[1]
    # Decomposable local score: lower is better. parameters=None auto-computes
    # observed cardinalities and uses N' = 1, structure_prior = 1.
    score_func = LocalScoreClass(
        data=X, local_score_fun=local_score_BDeu, parameters=None
    )

    nodes = [GraphNode(f"X{i + 1}") for i in range(N)]
    adj = np.zeros((N, N), dtype=int)            # adj[i, j] == 1 means i -> j

    # Current per-node local scores; total score is their sum (decomposability),
    # so any single-arc move's effect is a sum of one or two local recomputes.
    local_scores = np.zeros(N)
    for j in range(N):
        local_scores[j] = score_func.score(j, [])   # start: every node parentless

    def _has_path(src, tgt):
        """DFS: is there a directed path src -> ... -> tgt in adj? (acyclicity guard)"""
        visited, stack = set(), [src]
        while stack:
            node = stack.pop()
            if node == tgt:
                return True
            if node in visited:
                continue
            visited.add(node)
            for c in np.where(adj[node] == 1)[0]:
                if int(c) not in visited:
                    stack.append(int(c))
        return False

    # Hill climb: each sweep, pick the single best improving add/delete/reverse.
    improved = True
    while improved:
        improved = False
        best_delta = 0.0
        best_op = None

        for i in range(N):
            for j in range(N):
                if i == j:
                    continue

                if adj[i, j] == 0 and adj[j, i] == 0:
                    # ADD i -> j, only if it doesn't close a cycle (j !-> i already)
                    if not _has_path(j, i):
                        pj_new = sorted(np.where(adj[:, j] == 1)[0].tolist() + [i])
                        delta = score_func.score(j, pj_new) - local_scores[j]
                        if delta < best_delta - 1e-6:
                            best_delta, best_op = delta, ("add", i, j)

                elif adj[i, j] == 1:
                    # DELETE i -> j  (only Pi_j changes)
                    pj_new = [p for p in np.where(adj[:, j] == 1)[0] if p != i]
                    delta = score_func.score(j, sorted(pj_new)) - local_scores[j]
                    if delta < best_delta - 1e-6:
                        best_delta, best_op = delta, ("delete", i, j)

                    # REVERSE i -> j  to  j -> i  (both Pi_j and Pi_i change).
                    # Test acyclicity with i -> j removed: if i still reaches j,
                    # adding j -> i would close a cycle.
                    adj[i, j] = 0
                    if not _has_path(i, j):
                        pj_del = sorted(np.where(adj[:, j] == 1)[0].tolist())
                        pi_new = sorted(np.where(adj[:, i] == 1)[0].tolist() + [j])
                        delta = ((score_func.score(j, pj_del) - local_scores[j])
                                 + (score_func.score(i, pi_new) - local_scores[i]))
                        if delta < best_delta - 1e-6:
                            best_delta, best_op = delta, ("reverse", i, j)
                    adj[i, j] = 1                       # restore before continuing

        if best_op is not None:
            op, i, j = best_op
            if op == "add":
                adj[i, j] = 1
            elif op == "delete":
                adj[i, j] = 0
            elif op == "reverse":
                adj[i, j] = 0
                adj[j, i] = 1
            # Refresh only the cached local scores the move touched.
            local_scores[j] = score_func.score(j, sorted(np.where(adj[:, j] == 1)[0].tolist()))
            if op == "reverse":
                local_scores[i] = score_func.score(i, sorted(np.where(adj[:, i] == 1)[0].tolist()))
            improved = True

    # The hill-climb lands on a DAG, but observational data only identifies the
    # Markov equivalence class, so collapse it to the CPDAG before returning.
    G = GeneralGraph(nodes)
    for i in range(N):
        for j in range(N):
            if adj[i, j] == 1:
                G.add_directed_edge(nodes[i], nodes[j])
    return dag2cpdag(G)
```

I should be honest with myself about the failure mode I'm accepting. This is a greedy local search, and the space of DAGs is riddled with local optima — there's no guarantee the structure I stop at is the global best, only that no single-arc edit improves on it. With enough data I can hope the score landscape is informative, but the empty-graph start plus best single-arc improvements can certainly get stuck. The honest patches, if I wanted them, are well known: keep climbing past the first local optimum by allowing the best *non-worsening* move while forbidding recently visited graphs (a tabu list), and/or perturb the structure with a batch of random edits and re-climb several times (random restarts), keeping the best graph seen. Those add robustness at the cost of more compute and a couple of knobs; the core procedure is the plain best-improvement climb above, and the extensions bolt straight onto the same `local_scores` / delta machinery.

So the causal chain, start to finish. I wanted the structure the data most supports, and comparison only needs the relative joint `p(B_S, D)`, so I needed the marginal likelihood `p(D | B_S)`. Integrating the table parameters out — which builds in an automatic complexity penalty — looked intractable until parameter independence plus complete data factored it into one tiny family integral per (node, parent-config), and Dirichlet conjugacy turned each into a ratio of Gammas, giving a closed-form score computable from counts. That score is decomposable — a sum of local family terms — so a single-arc change costs one or two local recomputes, which is the lever for search. Enumeration is impossible (super-exponentially many DAGs) and the exact optimum is only reachable in the useless one-parent regime, NP-hard otherwise. The standard greedy parent-selection works but needs a variable ordering I don't have, and dropping the ordering forces me to search the full DAG space with explicit acyclicity checks. The natural single-arc moves are add, delete, reverse — reverse earns its place by crossing a score valley that delete-then-add can't, in one atomic step — and best-improvement hill-climbing on the cached lower-is-better score deltas climbs to a local optimum. Because observational data can't tell isomorphic structures apart, the score must give them equal value — and the two-node check showed unit pseudocounts fail that (the orientations split by `0.0083`) while the uniform-equivalent pseudocounts `α_ijk = N'/(r_i q_i)` make `x → y` and `y → x` coincide to machine precision at both `N' = 1` and `N' = 10`, so those are what I use (needing only the scalar `N' = 1`). Finally I collapse the learned DAG to its CPDAG so I claim only the orientations the data actually determines.
