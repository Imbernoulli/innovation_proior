I start from the simplest thing that already works: a countable list of classifiers f_1, f_2, ..., each carrying a prior weight P(f_i) fixed before I see any data, and a bounded loss. Chernoff concentration says that for one fixed f_i the empirical loss can't drift far from the true loss; if I spend a P(f_i)-sized slice of the failure probability on each f_i and union-bound over i, I get, with probability at least 1 - delta over the sample, simultaneously for every i,

L(f_i) <= hat L(f_i,S) + sqrt((ln(1/P(f_i)) + ln(1/delta)) / (2m)).

This already does something non-trivial: because the guarantee is simultaneous over all i, I am free to look at the data, pick whichever f_i I like, and the bound still holds for that choice. ln(1/P(f_i)) reads exactly like a Bayesian or MDL description length, so this looks like structural risk minimization: trade fit against a prior-encoded complexity. That is attractive precisely because of Kearns, Mansour, Ng and Ron's 1995 experiments, which are the cautionary tale I am trying to route around: ordinary Bayesian and MDL model selection can overfit badly the moment the assumed generative story is even slightly wrong, because their guarantees are conditional on the prior being true. What I have needs no such condition; P and the true data distribution D can be completely unrelated and the bound still holds for every sample.

But this only ever certifies a single selected f_i, and model selection is known to lose to model averaging in exactly the settings I care about. Smoothing a trigram model with a bigram model with a unigram model in language modeling is a mixture, not a selection, and outperforms picking a single n-gram order by MDL; averaging over many prunings of an overgrown decision tree beats committing to one pruned tree. So the object I actually want to certify is a distribution, or at least a collection, of classifiers, not a point.

My first attempt is to keep the union-bound machinery but move it up one level: instead of certifying single concepts f_i, certify arbitrary measurable subsets U of the concept space, where "using U" means drawing a concept from U with probability proportional to the prior restricted to U. If I can union-bound over sets the way I union-bounded over points, weighting each U by P(U) instead of P(f_i), I should get something like

L(U) <= (ln(1/P(U)) + ln(1/delta) + 2 ln m + 1) / m,

valid simultaneously for every measurable U consistent with the sample. Getting there needs a genuinely different device, because I can no longer just union-bound over an uncountable family of sets: I first show that a bound of the form "for all c, with probability 1 - delta over the sample, some property holds" can have its two quantifiers reversed — using a lemma that pushes an anti-monotone function through Markov's inequality twice, once on each side — so that it becomes "with probability 1 - delta over the sample, for all c, the same property holds at a rescaled confidence level." Once the quantifiers are reversed I can instantiate the free confidence parameter at P(U)/m for whichever U I want, and out comes the bound above. Point sets recover the old bound exactly; the guarantee degrades gracefully as U grows. This is real progress — a genuine PAC-Bayesian statement over sets, not points, uniform over data-dependent choice of U.

But it is still not what I want. A set with a restricted-and-renormalized prior is a very crude kind of posterior: every element of U is favored identically relative to P, and every element outside U is excluded entirely. A real posterior should be able to smoothly up-weight some concepts and down-weight others without an all-or-nothing membership test. If I try to generalize U to an arbitrary real-valued reweighting Q(c), the whole quantifier-reversal argument breaks, because it is built around a crisp event ("c is in U" or not) at every step — there is no way to run a union bound, reversed or otherwise, over a continuum of real-valued weightings the way I ran it over a continuum of sets. I am stuck wanting a genuinely Bayesian posterior Q, with no idea whether the guarantee survives that generalization. This is exactly the gap McAllester's own 1998 paper ends on: a MAP-like procedure over sets, with the explicit remark that it would be more satisfying to have a real PAC-Bayesian posterior distribution over concepts, and no proof that one exists.

The move that gets me unstuck is to stop trying to run any kind of union bound at all, crisp or reversed, and instead directly control an average under the PRIOR of an exponential moment of a per-classifier deviation statistic, and only afterward transport that statement to whatever posterior Q I like. Concretely, let Delta(c) = |L(c) - hat L(c,S)|. Chernoff already tells me that for one fixed c, P(Delta(c) >= x) <= 2 e^{-2mx^2}. I want to know how large E_S[e^{(2m-1) Delta(c)^2}] can possibly be given only that tail bound, because if I can control that exponential moment uniformly in c, I can average it over the prior without ever naming a specific hypothesis. The worst case is realized by the continuous density that saturates the tail inequality with equality: differentiating the tail integral_x^infinity f(Delta) dDelta = 2 e^{-2mx^2} gives f(Delta) = 8m Delta e^{-2m Delta^2} on [0, infinity). So

E_S[e^{(2m-1) Delta(c)^2}] <= integral_0^infinity 8m Delta e^{(2m-1) Delta^2} e^{-2m Delta^2} dDelta = 8m integral_0^infinity Delta e^{-Delta^2} dDelta = 4m,

independent of c. Average this over c ~ P — since P does not depend on the sample, Fubini lets me swap the order of the sample expectation and the prior expectation — and apply Markov's inequality: with probability at least 1 - delta over S,

E_{c~P}[e^{(2m-1) Delta(c)^2}] <= 4m/delta.

This is the whole trick compressed into one line: a single high-probability statement, computed entirely on the prior side, before I have named any Q. It plays the role the union-bound budget used to play, except it is paid in exponential-moment currency rather than per-hypothesis failure probability, and there is no obstruction left to transporting it to a continuum.

Now for the transport itself. I need: given that sum_i P_i e^{beta y_i} <= K, what is the best possible bound on sum_i Q_i y_i for an arbitrary probability vector Q? Phrase it as a constrained optimization: maximize sum_i Q_i y_i over y subject to sum_i P_i e^{beta y_i} <= K, with Q, P, beta, K fixed. At the maximum the Kuhn-Tucker conditions say the gradient of the objective is proportional to the gradient of the active constraint: Q_i = lambda P_i beta e^{beta y_i} for some multiplier lambda, so y_i = ln(Q_i / (lambda beta P_i)) / beta. The constraint must be tight at the optimum, so sum_i P_i e^{beta y_i} = sum_i Q_i / (lambda beta) = 1/(lambda beta) = K, giving lambda = 1/(beta K), and

sum_i Q_i y_i = sum_i Q_i * (ln(Q_i/P_i) + ln K) / beta = (KL(Q||P) + ln K) / beta.

That number is the actual worst case, not merely an upper bound I happened to guess, because it is realized at an honest maximum of the constrained problem. Apply it with y_i = Delta(c_i)^2, beta = 2m - 1, K = 4m/delta:

E_{c~Q}[Delta(c)^2] <= (KL(Q||P) + ln(4m/delta)) / (2m - 1),

for every Q simultaneously, at the same 1 - delta confidence as before, because the randomness was already spent proving the prior-side statement and this last step is a deterministic inequality with no further sample dependence. This is the moment the whole architecture pays off: I never had to touch a union bound over Q, because Q never had to be drawn from a pre-listed family.

Two elementary steps close the loop: L(Q) - hat L(Q,S) = E_{c~Q}[L(c) - hat L(c,S)] <= E_{c~Q}[Delta(c)], just bounding a difference of expectations by the expectation of its absolute value, and Jensen's inequality on the convex map x -> x^2 gives (E_{c~Q}[Delta(c)])^2 <= E_{c~Q}[Delta(c)^2]. Combining,

L(Q) <= hat L(Q,S) + sqrt((KL(Q||P) + ln(4m/delta)) / (2m - 1)) = hat L(Q,S) + sqrt((KL(Q||P) + ln(1/delta) + ln m + 2) / (2m - 1)),

since ln(4m) = ln m + ln 4 <= ln m + 2. This is McAllester's square-root PAC-Bayes bound, holding simultaneously for every posterior Q on any measurable concept space, countable or continuous.

Looking back, I did not have to reach this exact bound in one pass. The earliest version of this argument ran the Kuhn-Tucker change-of-measure lemma directly on the linear quantity y_i = Delta(c_i), bounding sum_i Q_i Delta_i first, and then needed a separate application of Jensen's inequality afterward to turn that linear bound into the quadratic shape the theorem actually needs. That route works, but it is a wasted round trip: the Chernoff step already hands me control of the squared deviation from the very start (the exponent in E_S[e^{(2m-1)Delta(c)^2}] is Delta^2, not Delta), so there is no reason not to run the change-of-measure optimization on y_i = Delta(c_i)^2 directly and land on the theorem in a single pass, which is the simplification Robert Schapire pointed out.

Once Q collapses to a point mass on a single discrete h, KL(Q||P) = E_Q[ln(Q/P)] reduces exactly to ln(1/P(h)), so the new theorem contains the old Occam bound as the special case where the posterior refuses to spread out at all. But nothing in the derivation used countability of the class or positivity of P at a point — Fubini and Markov hold over an arbitrary measurable space, and the Kuhn-Tucker argument is really an optimization over probability measures, not probability vectors. So a continuous class, where every individual parameter value has prior mass exactly zero, is no longer a degenerate edge case: KL(Q||P) stays finite whenever Q is absolutely continuous with a well-behaved density relative to P, even though ln(1/P(h)) for any single h in that class is infinite. This is exactly why the earlier detour through crisp sets U was the wrong shape of generalization: a set-membership indicator is only the coarsest possible reweighting of P, uniform on U and zero outside it. The fix was not a richer combinatorial object; it was to let the answer to "which classifier to certify" be an arbitrary likelihood ratio dQ/dP, and to prove the bound as a statement about that ratio via a change-of-measure inequality instead of about set membership via a union bound.

The same architecture — prior-side exponential moment, Markov, then change of measure — produces a sharper form when I swap in Bernoulli relative entropy as the deviation statistic instead of a squared Hoeffding-style deviation. m * hat L(c,S) is Binomial(m, L(c)) under sampling, so the moment E_S[e^{m * kl(hat L, L)}] is controlled by the size of the binomial coefficient: Seeger bounds it by m + 1 using C(m,k) <= e^{m H(k/m)}, and Maurer sharpens the same convexity and Stirling-style argument down to 2 sqrt(m) for m >= 8, which is a real gain, since it roughly halves the additive log term in the final bound. Averaging over the prior, Markov, then the identical change-of-measure step (now transporting the Bernoulli-kl statistic instead of Delta^2), then Jensen on the jointly convex kl function, gives

kl(hat L(Q,S), L(Q)) <= (KL(Q||P) + ln((m+1)/delta)) / m,

tightened by Maurer to ln(2 sqrt(m) / delta) in place of ln((m+1)/delta). Because kl(p,q) >= 2(p-q)^2 (Pinsker), this kl-form bound implies the square-root bound and is strictly tighter whenever the empirical loss is small: the square-root form throws away exactly the information that a loss near 0 is harder to fluctuate away from than a loss near 1/2, because Hoeffding's e^{-2mx^2} tail does not distinguish the two regimes.

The theorem is also an objective, not just a certificate, and the same optimization machinery that solved the change-of-measure step solves this one too. Fix beta = 2m - 1 and K = ln(1/delta) + ln m + 2, and minimize B(Q) = E_{c~Q}[hat L(c,S)] + sqrt((KL(Q||P) + K) / (2m-1)) over probability distributions Q. Introducing a Lagrange multiplier lambda for the simplex constraint sum_i Q_i = 1 and differentiating, using d/dQ_i[Q_i ln(Q_i/P_i)] = ln(Q_i/P_i) + 1,

L_hat_i + (1 / (2m-1)) * ((KL(Q||P)+K)/(2m-1))^{-1/2} * (ln(Q_i/P_i) + 1) = lambda for every i,

and any i with Q_i = 0 while P_i > 0 would make this derivative equal to minus infinity, meaning mass can always be moved there to shrink B, so the true minimum has Q and P sharing support. Solving for Q_i, the two constants collapse into a single inverse temperature and

Q_i is proportional to P_i * e^{-beta * hat L(c_i,S)}, where beta = 2 * sqrt((2m-1) * (KL(Q_beta||P) + ln(1/delta) + ln m + 2)).

This is a Gibbs distribution, but a self-consistent one: beta is not a free knob I can set independently of Q, it is pinned down by an equation in which the very KL(Q_beta||P) it produces appears on the right-hand side. That is a genuinely surprising feature of the optimum — minimizing the bound does not just tilt the prior toward low empirical loss at some fixed temperature; it forces the temperature itself into equilibrium with how far the resulting posterior has wandered from the prior.

What this derivation gives me, and what it withholds, is worth being precise about. It certifies the stochastic classifier that draws a fresh c ~ Q at prediction time and reports E_{c~Q}[loss]; it does not by itself certify the deterministic majority-vote or expectation classifier usually meant by model averaging, and whether a comparable guarantee holds for that deterministic aggregate is a question the derivation leaves open — exactly where averaging seemed to promise more than stochastic selection in the first place. It never assumes P matches the true data-generating distribution D; keeping that assumption out was the entire point, unlike ordinary Bayesian model averaging, whose guarantees really do depend on the prior being right, which is the failure mode Kearns, Mansour, Ng and Ron documented empirically. And it does not make the bound automatically small: insist on a Q far from P, or pick a class with no good classifiers, and KL(Q||P) or hat L(Q,S) will be large, and the certificate will be honest about that. What the derivation does buy is exactly the substitution I was chasing from the start: the description length ln(1/P(h)) that used to price a single selected hypothesis is replaced, with no loss of rigor and no new assumption, by the relative entropy KL(Q||P) that prices an entire learned distribution — finite exactly where the old quantity was not, and degrading exactly to the old quantity the moment the posterior refuses to be anything but a point.
