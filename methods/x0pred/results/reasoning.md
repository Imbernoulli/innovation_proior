Let me start from what the reverse process actually has to do, step by step, and not from the loss anyone happens to write down first. I have a fixed forward process that turns a clean image into noise: draw `x_0` from the data, draw `epsilon` standard Gaussian, mix them by the schedule, `x_t = sqrt(alpha_t) x_0 + sqrt(1 - alpha_t) epsilon`, where `alpha_t` slides from near 1 (barely noised) down to near 0 (almost pure noise). To generate, I run this backward: start from `x_T ~ N(0, I)` and repeatedly take a small denoising step `x_t -> x_{t-1}`. So the whole question is, what does one denoising step need? Each reverse step is approximately Gaussian — that's the payoff of making each forward step add only a little noise — so I only have to produce its mean, and the variance I can pin to a schedule constant. What is that mean? If I condition on the true clean image, the forward process reverses in closed form: `q(x_{t-1} | x_t, x_0)` is Gaussian with mean

  `tilde_mu_t(x_t, x_0) = ( sqrt(alpha_bar_{t-1}) beta_t / (1 - alpha_bar_t) ) x_0 + ( sqrt(1 - beta_t) (1 - alpha_bar_{t-1}) / (1 - alpha_bar_t) ) x_t.`

Stare at that for a second. It's linear in `x_0` and linear in `x_t`. The only thing in it I don't have at sampling time is `x_0` — `x_t` is the input I'm holding, `x_t`'s coefficient is fixed arithmetic, `x_0`'s coefficient is fixed arithmetic. So the entire learnable content of one reverse step is: *given the noisy `x_t`, what is the clean image `x_0`?* Everything else is substitution. That reframes the model's job completely. I'm not fundamentally learning "a reverse transition kernel" or "a noise vector" — I'm learning a denoiser, a function that looks at a corrupted image and tells me the clean one, and then I plug its answer into a fixed formula. A deterministic sampler makes this even more naked: given `x_t`, estimate `x_0`, then step toward the next noise level using that estimate in place of the unknown truth. The thing the sampler reaches for, at every single step, is an estimate of `x_0`.

So if the sampler wants `x_0`, the most direct thing imaginable is to have the network just *output* `x_0`. Let me hold that thought and check it against the alternative everyone reaches for first, because I want to know why predicting `x_0` isn't simply the obvious universal default — clearly there's a reason the field reached for something else, and I should understand the tension before I commit.

The alternative is to predict the noise `epsilon`. Where does that come from? There's a clean identity behind it. The forward kernel is Gaussian, `N(x_t; sqrt(alpha_t) x_0, (1 - alpha_t) I)`, and its score is `grad_{x_t} log q(x_t | x_0) = -(x_t - sqrt(alpha_t) x_0)/(1 - alpha_t)`. But `x_t - sqrt(alpha_t) x_0 = sqrt(1 - alpha_t) epsilon` by the forward equation, so the score is `-epsilon / sqrt(1 - alpha_t)`. Up to a fixed positive scale, the added noise *is* the negative score. That's lovely: regress the network onto `epsilon`, and you've simultaneously learned a score estimate that a Langevin-flavored sampler can walk on, and the reverse mean rewrites neatly into "subtract a learned multiple of the predicted noise from `x_t`." That's a real reason `epsilon` became the default, and I shouldn't pretend it isn't.

But now I have to ask the question that actually decides this, which is: are these even different models? At a fixed `t`, write `a = sqrt(alpha_t)`, `b = sqrt(1 - alpha_t)`, so `a^2 + b^2 = 1` and `x_t = a x_0 + b epsilon`. Then `x_0 = (x_t - b epsilon)/a` and `epsilon = (x_t - a x_0)/b`. Each is an exact, invertible linear function of the other given `x_t`. So whether the network emits `x_0` or `epsilon`, I can always convert to whichever the sampler wants — and in the limit of infinite capacity and infinite data, the two parameterizations describe the *identical* function and the identical optimal model. If they were truly equivalent I could stop here and flip a coin. They aren't, and the reason they aren't is the whole point: I train with a finite network, a finite number of steps, a fixed optimizer, a fixed sampler. Under a finite budget, *where I put the regression target* changes the gradients, changes how error at one noise level trades against error at another, and so changes the final samples. So I can't reason about the limit; I have to reason about the loss the finite optimizer actually sees.

Let me make that precise, because this is where the choice lives. Suppose I train a flat mean-squared error directly on whatever the network emits. If it emits `epsilon`, the loss is `|| epsilon - epsilon_hat ||^2`. If it emits `x_0`, the loss is `|| x_0 - x0_hat ||^2`. These look symmetric but they are not the same objective, because the conversion between the two carries a `t`-dependent factor. From `epsilon = (x_t - a x_0)/b`, the error in the two readings is linked: `epsilon - epsilon_hat = (x_t - a x_0)/b - (x_t - a x0_hat)/b = (a/b)(x0_hat - x_0)`. Square it:

  `|| epsilon - epsilon_hat ||^2 = (a^2 / b^2) || x_0 - x0_hat ||^2 = SNR_t || x_0 - x0_hat ||^2,`

with `SNR_t = a^2/b^2 = alpha_t / (1 - alpha_t)`, the signal-to-noise ratio at that noise level. So flat MSE on `epsilon` is exactly an *`SNR_t`-weighted* MSE on the clean image. The two are the same loss only after multiplying the clean-image error by `SNR_t` at each `t`. That single factor is the entire difference between the parameterizations under flat training, and now I can read off what each one does to the budget. At low noise (`t` small) `alpha_t -> 1`, `SNR_t` is large, so noise-MSE pours weight onto the low-noise steps. At high noise (`t` large) `alpha_t -> 0`, `SNR_t -> 0`, so noise-MSE almost ignores the high-noise steps. Predicting `epsilon` therefore quietly concentrates the finite training budget on the low-noise end — which is the perceptually important end, the steps that paint in fine detail. Predicting `x_0` with a flat MSE, by contrast, weights *every* noise level equally. That's the trade. Neither is obviously right; they're different allocations of the same capacity, and which one yields lower FID under this exact pipeline is an empirical question — but at least now I know what I'm actually choosing between, and it's a weighting across noise levels, not a different model.

Now I want to find the failure modes, because the choice should be made on where each one breaks, not on taste. Take the `epsilon` reading first and push it to high noise, since that's the regime a 50-step sampler spends its first, biggest moves in. The sampler needs `x_0`, and from `epsilon` it gets it by inverting the forward equation: `x0_hat = (x_t - b epsilon_hat)/a = (x_t - sqrt(1 - alpha_t) epsilon_hat)/sqrt(alpha_t)`. There's a `1/a` out front, and `a = sqrt(alpha_t) -> 0` as the noise grows. So a small error `delta` in the emitted `epsilon` becomes an error of size `(b/a) delta = sqrt(SNR_t^{-1}) delta`... let me be careful, `b/a = sqrt((1-alpha_t)/alpha_t) = 1/sqrt(SNR_t)`, which *blows up* as `SNR_t -> 0`. The implied clean-image estimate is hypersensitive to the network's output exactly where the noise is largest. And at the very top, `alpha_t = 0`, the map `x0_hat = (x_t - b epsilon_hat)/a` divides by zero — the emitted `epsilon` implies *no* clean-image estimate at all. The link between what the network says and what the sampler needs degenerates precisely at the high-noise steps. With a thousand sampling steps you can survive this, because the iterates get clipped back into range and later steps repair early missteps; but the fewer steps you take, the more each early, high-noise step matters and the less room there is to recover. So `epsilon`'s clean weighting of the perceptual detail comes paired with a clean-image instability at high noise that gets worse as the sampler gets cheaper.

Does the `x_0` reading have the mirror-image problem? It can't have *that* one, because there's no inversion to do — the network's output already is the clean-image estimate the sampler wants. `predict_x0` is the identity. Whatever numerical sensitivity `1/a` injects into the `epsilon` route simply isn't present: the implied clean-image prediction is the network output, stable across every noise level by construction, no `sqrt(alpha_t)` in any denominator. That's the structural advantage, and it's exactly the thing a few-step deterministic sampler should want — a clean-image estimate that doesn't detonate at the noisy end where it takes its first leaps.

So why isn't this just strictly better — why was predicting `x_0` actually observed to give *worse* samples in the early going? I have to find `x_0`-prediction's own wall, and it's on the other end of the weighting argument I just did. Flat MSE on `x_0` weights all noise levels equally, including the high-noise ones. But think about what "predict `x_0` from `x_t`" even means when `t` is large. `x_t = a x_0 + b epsilon` with `a -> 0` is almost pure noise; a single noisy `x_t` is consistent with a huge set of clean images, all of which could have produced it. The best any regressor can do under squared error is the conditional mean `E[x_0 | x_t]`, and as `a -> 0` that conditional mean collapses toward the overall data mean — a gray, structureless, blurry image. There's essentially no recoverable signal there: the target is nearly the same blob for every input. Yet a flat clean-image MSE keeps insisting the network spend gradient on nailing that near-unlearnable, near-constant target, with the same weight it gives the low-noise steps where real detail lives. Compare that to what `epsilon`-MSE does at the same place: it multiplies the clean-image error by `SNR_t -> 0`, so it correctly *stops caring* about the high-noise steps where there's nothing to learn, and pours its attention onto the low-noise steps. That's the wall: under a flat clean-image loss, the budget bleeds into the high-noise regime that has no learnable detail, starving the low-noise regime that the perceptual metric actually rewards. The very weighting that makes `x_0`-prediction stable for the sampler makes its *training loss* mis-allocated relative to `epsilon` under a finite budget. That is exactly consistent with "predicting `x_0` directly led to worse sample quality early on" — not because the parameterization is wrong, but because flat MSE on it is the wrong weighting, and that hadn't been disentangled yet from the parameterization itself.

Now I can see the shape of the resolution, and I want to derive it rather than assert it. The two facts I've established — `epsilon`-prediction is numerically unstable for the sampler at high noise, `x_0`-prediction is mis-weighted under flat MSE at high noise — are about *different things*: one is about the sampler's sensitivity to output error, the other is about how the training loss spreads attention. They're separable. The sampler instability is intrinsic to the `epsilon` *reading* (the `1/a` in the inversion), and there's no inversion in the `x_0` reading, so the `x_0` reading simply doesn't have it. The training mis-weighting is intrinsic to the *flat MSE choice on `x_0`*, and that's a loss-weighting knob, not a property of the parameterization — I could in principle reweight. So the clean baseline to lay down is: take the parameterization whose output is exactly what the sampler consumes — predict `x_0` directly — accept the flat MSE as the simplest, no-knobs objective, and have it on the table as the stable, sampler-aligned reference that the weighting question can then be argued against. The contribution I'm building is precisely that target choice: emit the clean image, recover it trivially, keep the rest of the fixed pipeline untouched.

Let me also confirm `x_0`-prediction sits coherently between the other two readings, so I know it's a principled corner of the design space and not an oddball. There's a third reading, the velocity `v = a epsilon - b x_0`, which is a rotation of the `(x_0, epsilon)` pair: in the angle `phi` with `a = cos phi`, `b = sin phi`, the noisy point is `z = cos(phi) x_0 + sin(phi) epsilon` and `v = cos(phi) epsilon - sin(phi) x_0` is literally its derivative as `phi` advances — the velocity of the point on the circle. Its clean-image loss weight works out to `(1 + SNR_t)`. So I have three weightings of the same clean-image error: `epsilon` gives weight `SNR_t` (kills high noise), `v` gives `1 + SNR_t` (a balanced compromise that never drops to zero), and predicting `x_0` directly gives flat weight `1` (equal across noise). Predicting `x_0` is the corner where the regression target lives *entirely in the space the sampler ultimately consumes* and the implied clean prediction is maximally stable; `epsilon` is the opposite corner, maximally emphasizing perceptual detail but unstable at high noise; `v` interpolates. Laying down `x_0`-prediction as the baseline is choosing the sampler-aligned, no-inversion corner.

Time to write it, into the one editable region — the two coupled functions, with the hard constraint that the recovery must exactly invert the target so the sampler is fed a correct clean-image estimate. The target is the cleanest possible: the clean image itself. And the recovery is the identity, because the network's output already *is* the clean-image estimate — there is nothing to invert, which is the whole structural point I arrived at. No schedule tensors are even needed in either function; that absence is the signal that this reading carries no `1/a` amplification.

```python
def compute_training_target(x_0, noise, timesteps, schedule):
    # The reverse step is fundamentally a denoiser: the only unknown in the
    # closed-form posterior mean is the clean image x_0, and the DDIM sampler
    # consumes an estimate of x_0 at every step. So train the network to emit
    # x_0 directly -- the regression target is the clean image itself.
    return x_0


def predict_x0(model_output, x_t, timesteps, schedule):
    # The output already IS the clean-image estimate the sampler wants, so the
    # recovery is the identity -- it exactly inverts the (identity) target, and
    # there is no 1/sqrt(alpha_t) inversion to amplify error at high noise.
    return model_output
```

That's the whole method, and it drops into the fixed harness with nothing else changed: the training step still does `x_t = sqrt_alpha * x_0 + sqrt_one_minus_alpha * noise`, calls the UNet, and takes `MSE(output, target)` — only now `target` is `x_0`; the DDIM step still calls `predict_x0` to get its clean-image estimate, forms the implied noise direction `eps_hat = (x_t - sqrt_alpha * x0_hat)/sqrt_one_minus_alpha`, and steps `sqrt_alpha_prev * x0_hat + sqrt_one_minus_alpha_prev * eps_hat` to the next noise level.

Walking the chain back: the reverse step's only learnable content is the clean image `x_0`, since the closed-form posterior mean is linear in `x_0` with everything else fixed, and a deterministic sampler reaches for an `x_0` estimate at every step. The output can be read as `x_0`, as `epsilon`, or as their rotation `v`, all linearly interconvertible at fixed `t`, so under infinite budget they coincide — but under a finite budget the reading sets a per-noise-level weighting, since flat noise-MSE equals `SNR_t`-weighted clean-image-MSE. Reading the output as `epsilon` weights perceptual low-noise detail but makes the implied clean-image estimate blow up like `1/sqrt(alpha_t)` at high noise, hurting a cheap sampler; reading it as `x_0` with flat MSE keeps the implied clean prediction perfectly stable (no inversion) but spreads the budget evenly, including onto high-noise steps where `x_0` is barely recoverable and the target collapses toward a blur — which is why predicting `x_0` under flat MSE was seen to lag `epsilon` on sample quality even though it is the corner most aligned with what the sampler actually needs. The baseline that falls out is exactly that corner: train the network to emit the clean image, recover it with the identity, leave the schedule, optimizer, and DDIM sampler untouched.
