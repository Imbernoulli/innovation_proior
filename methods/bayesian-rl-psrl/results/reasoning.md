I want an exploration rule that does not mistake ignorance for knowledge. If I plug in maximum-likelihood transition and reward estimates, I may get a crisp policy too early, and the policy may avoid exactly the state-action pairs that would correct it. I can add random moves, but random moves do not necessarily carry me through a chain of states toward an informative consequence. In a controlled Markov process, information can be delayed, so exploration has to be temporally coherent.

The fully Bayesian answer tells me to plan in the belief state, but that is too large. I would have to integrate over models, observations, and future policies. That gives the right decision problem and the wrong computational object. I need something that uses the posterior without solving the Bayes-adaptive MDP.

Probability matching suggests a tempting shortcut. In a bandit, I sample a parameter from the posterior and take the action that is best for that sample. If an action is still plausibly optimal, it keeps being tried; if evidence rules it out, its sampling probability fades. But I cannot simply resample at every step in an MDP. If the possible world I sample says "go right for ten steps and the reward is at the end," then changing the sampled world after one step can destroy the experiment before it reaches the evidence. So the sampling cannot happen per-step; the natural granularity is a whole model held fixed across one episode. Draw one MDP from the posterior at the start of the episode, solve it, and follow its optimal policy until the episode ends. That is cheap — one planning solve per episode — and it keeps a deep exploratory plan coherent.

The trouble is the analysis. Regret compares me with the optimal policy for the true MDP `M^*`, and I never observe that policy. Worse, that policy may go through state-action pairs my sampled policy never visits, so a confidence-width argument over my own data has nothing to attach to. I need to remove the unknown comparator from the regret expression before I can bound anything.

Optimism is the standard escape: if the algorithm itself chooses the most favorable plausible MDP, then its value dominates the true optimal value whenever the confidence set contains the truth, and the comparator is handled by construction. But I do not want to build that into the algorithm. If I do, I am back to designing confidence sets, making them tractable, and living with conservative rectangular bounds over rewards and transitions; the simple sampled-model rule disappears into another optimistic algorithm. I want to keep the algorithm as it is and find the comparator-removal somewhere else — in the analysis.

Here is the only special structure the sampled rule gives me. At the start of an episode, conditioned on the history `H` I have collected, the true MDP `M^*` and the model `M_k` I just sampled are both distributed according to the same posterior — `M^*` because that is what conditioning on data means, `M_k` because that is literally how I drew it. So for any functional `g` of a model that I am allowed to choose using only `H`,

`E[g(M^*) | H] = E[g(M_k) | H]`.

I should not trust this just because it sounds right; it is the load-bearing step, so let me check it on a case I can compute. Take a one-armed problem with reward parameter `p`, and suppose the history has driven the posterior to `Beta(5,3)`. Both `M^*｜H` and `M_k` are then `Beta(5,3)`. Pick an arbitrary functional, say `g(p) = p^2 - 0.3 p`, standing in for some value-like quantity. Analytically, with `E[p] = 5/8 = 0.625` and `E[p^2] = (5·6)/((8)(9)) = 0.41667`, I get `E[g] = 0.41667 - 0.3·0.625 = 0.22917`. Drawing two million independent samples for each of `M^*` and `M_k` and averaging `g` gives `0.22893` and `0.22927` — both sitting on `0.22917` to Monte-Carlo error. The identity holds: it does not matter that one model is "the truth" and the other is "my guess," because at this instant they are the same random object.

Now apply it with `g` equal to the optimal-value functional, `g(M) = sum_s rho(s) V^M_{mu^M,1}(s)`, the value of acting optimally in `M` from the start distribution. By the identity, `E` of the true optimal value equals `E` of the sampled-MDP optimal value, conditioned on the history. So in expectation I may replace the value of the unknown true optimal policy in `M^*` with the value of my sampled policy in `M_k` — a quantity built only from things I chose and executed. The episode regret, in expectation, becomes

`tilde Delta_k = sum_s rho(s) ( V^{M_k}_{mu_k,1}(s) - V^{M^*}_{mu_k,1}(s) )`.

The unknown comparator is gone. What remains is the gap between how good my policy `mu_k` looks in the world I imagined and how good it actually is in the real world — the same policy on both sides.

That gap is a simulation-lemma object: two MDPs, one fixed policy, compare its value. I want it to break into one-step model errors along the trajectory `mu_k` actually generates. Let me confirm the decomposition is exact rather than assume it. Write `D_h = V^{M_k}_{mu_k,h} - V^{M^*}_{mu_k,h}`. Expanding each side with its own Bellman backup,

`D_h(s) = (r^{M_k} - r^{M^*})(s,a) + (P^{M_k} - P^{M^*})(.|s,a) · V^{M_k}_{h+1} + P^{M^*}(.|s,a) · D_{h+1}`,

with `a = mu_k(s,h)`. I coded a random two-state, horizon-3 instance with independent random `M_k` and `M^*`, computed `D_h` directly by backward induction, and computed the right-hand side; the maximum discrepancy across all `h` and states was `2.2e-16`, i.e. exact up to floating point. The structure I needed is real: the difference is a reward gap plus a transition gap dotted into `V^{M_k}` of the next step, plus the same kind of difference one step deeper. Unrolling the last term down the horizon under `M^*`'s dynamics telescopes `D` into a sum of these one-step gaps evaluated at the states and actions `mu_k` visits. The pure transition-noise piece — the deviation of the realized next state from its `M^*`-mean — is a martingale difference and has zero conditional expectation, so it drops out of `E[tilde Delta_k]`. What is left is exactly the per-step model error `(r^{M_k}-r^{M^*}) + (P^{M_k}-P^{M^*})·V^{M_k}` at visited pairs.

So now I need those reward and transition gaps to be small at the pairs I actually visit. This is where confidence sets come back — but only as a measuring tool, never as a controller. Define UCRL-style empirical radii: a reward radius and a transition radius around the running estimates at each state-action pair, shrinking like the inverse square root of the visit count. With high probability the truth `M^*` lies in the confidence set. And here the posterior symmetry pays a second time: `M_k` is drawn from the same posterior as `M^*｜H`, so the probability that `M_k` escapes the confidence set is the same as for `M^*`. The "bad" episodes where some confidence statement fails are controlled twice over, once for the truth and once for the sample, and their total contribution stays low-order. On the good episodes, each one-step gap is bounded by the confidence width at the visited pair.

That leaves a counting question: summing those widths over the run. At a single state-action pair, the width on its `n`-th visit scales like `1/sqrt(n)`, so its total contribution over `N` visits is `sum_{n=1}^{N} 1/sqrt(n)`. I want to know this really grows like `sqrt(N)` and not faster. Summing directly: `N=100` gives `18.59`, and `2 sqrt(100) = 20`; `N=10^4` gives `198.54` against `200`. The partial sum tracks `2 sqrt(N)` from above and the ratio to `sqrt(N)` climbs toward `2`, exactly the integral bound `sum 1/sqrt(n) <= 2 sqrt(N)`. Spreading the budget `T` across `SA` pairs and bounding by Cauchy-Schwarz turns the per-pair `sqrt(visits)` into an aggregate `sqrt(SA·T)`, and carrying the horizon and state count through the Bellman backups multiplies by `tau` and `S`. The expected regret comes out as order `tau S sqrt(A T)`, up to logarithmic factors from the high-probability confidence statements.

The shape of the result is then settled by the path I took, not assumed at the start. The algorithmic content is to hold one sampled model for a whole episode so deep exploration stays coherent; that part is cheap and concrete. The analytic content is that posterior symmetry lets me trade the unobservable true-optimal comparator for the value of my own executed policy, after which a simulation-lemma telescoping and a `sqrt`-visit count finish the bound. The confidence sets explain why learning happens, but they never appear in the algorithm — they sit entirely inside the proof. That is the difference between this and "just run Thompson sampling in RL": the same single-sample, episode-committed rule that I wanted for cheapness turns out to carry an implicit optimism in expectation, and that is what makes its regret bound go through.
