I want an exploration rule that does not mistake ignorance for knowledge. If I plug in maximum-likelihood transition and reward estimates, I may get a crisp policy too early, and the policy may avoid exactly the state-action pairs that would correct it. I can add random moves, but random moves do not necessarily carry me through a chain of states toward an informative consequence. In a controlled Markov process, information can be delayed, so exploration has to be temporally coherent.

The fully Bayesian answer tells me to plan in the belief state, but that is too large. I would have to integrate over models, observations, and future policies. That gives the right decision problem and the wrong computational object. I need something that uses the posterior without solving the Bayes-adaptive MDP.

Probability matching suggests a tempting shortcut. In a bandit, I sample a parameter from the posterior and take the action that is best for that sample. If an action is still plausibly optimal, it keeps being tried; if evidence rules it out, its sampling probability fades. But I cannot simply resample at every step in an MDP. If the possible world I sample says "go right for ten steps and the reward is at the end," then changing the sampled world after one step can destroy the experiment before it reaches the evidence. I need to sample a whole model and commit to its optimal policy for an episode.

Now I have a clean algorithmic picture: draw one MDP from the posterior at the start of the episode, solve it, and follow its optimal policy for the episode. That gives coherent deep exploration, and it is much cheaper than optimizing over a family of plausible MDPs. But the proof is still stuck. Regret compares me with the optimal policy for the true MDP, and I do not observe that policy. Worse, that policy may go through state-action pairs my sampled policy never visits, so a direct confidence-width argument has nothing to attach to.

Optimism solves this by construction. If I choose the most favorable plausible MDP, then its value dominates the true optimal value as long as the confidence set contains the truth. But I do not want to build that optimism into the algorithm. If I do, I am back to designing confidence sets, making them tractable, and living with conservative rectangular bounds over rewards and transitions. The simple sampled-model rule would disappear into another optimistic algorithm.

The way out is to stop asking for pathwise optimism. At the beginning of an episode, conditioned on everything I have seen, the true MDP and my sampled MDP have the same posterior distribution. Therefore any model function I choose using only that history has the same conditional expectation under the truth and under the sample. I can apply this to the optimal-value functional. The value of the true optimal policy in the true MDP and the value of the sampled optimal policy in the sampled MDP are not equal path by path, but their conditional expectations line up in the exact place the regret proof needs.

So I rewrite the episode regret. Instead of carrying the unknown true optimal policy forward, I replace expected true regret by the expected difference between my sampled policy's value in the sampled MDP and the same policy's value in the true MDP. This surrogate contains the policy I actually execute. That is the decisive step: the counterfactual comparator vanishes, and the remaining error is model-estimation error along my own trajectory.

Once I have that surrogate, dynamic programming does the rest of the translation. The value difference telescopes into one-step Bellman errors, evaluated at the states and actions I actually visit during the episode, while the remaining transition-noise term has zero conditional expectation. If the sampled transition and reward laws are close to the true laws at those visited pairs, the error is small. As visits accumulate, the empirical confidence radii shrink.

I can now borrow confidence sets from optimistic RL without making the algorithm optimistic. I define UCRL-style reward and transition radii only in the proof. If the true MDP is inside the confidence set with high probability, then the sampled MDP is inside with the same conditional probability because it is drawn from the same posterior as the truth. The bad episodes are controlled twice, once for the truth and once for the sample. On good episodes, the Bellman error is bounded by the confidence width at the visited state-action pair, and the usual counting argument sums those widths to order tau times S times the square root of A T up to logarithms.

This is why the result is not just "use Thompson sampling in RL." The algorithmic idea is to keep a sampled model long enough to make a deep exploratory plan coherent. The proof idea is to use posterior symmetry to turn regret against an unknown optimal policy into Bellman error of the executed policy, then use confidence machinery only as analysis. The confidence set explains why learning happens, but it never directs the learner.
