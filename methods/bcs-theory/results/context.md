## Low-Temperature Facts

The phenomenon to be explained is not only vanishing resistance. A material below its critical temperature also expels magnetic field from its bulk, has a sharp thermodynamic transition, and shows low-temperature electronic behavior consistent with a gap in the excitation spectrum.

## Normal Metal Baseline

The ordinary quantum theory of a metal starts from Bloch states filled up to a Fermi surface. That picture works well for many normal-state properties, but it treats low-energy excitations as individual electrons and holes that can be made arbitrarily close to the Fermi energy.

## Interaction Accounting

Most Coulomb and lattice-correlation energy is present in both phases, so it cannot by itself explain a small condensation energy. The useful microscopic question is narrower: which part of the electron interaction changes the state near the Fermi surface without double-counting the normal-metal background?

## Near-Fermi Attraction

Virtual exchange of lattice vibrations can make the effective interaction between two near-Fermi electrons attractive over an energy shell set by a phonon scale. Screened Coulomb repulsion still competes with it, so the relevant condition is not attraction alone but a net attractive channel in the narrow region where many low-energy states are available.

## Macroscopic Constraints

Any microscopic account must also recover the older electromagnetic clues. The magnetic response suggests rigidity over a finite coherence distance, not just high mobility of individual carriers. The theory therefore has to connect a near-Fermi microscopic rearrangement to a current response that excludes magnetic field.
