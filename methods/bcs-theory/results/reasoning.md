I start by trying to keep the normal-metal picture and merely improve the scattering story, but that cannot be enough. A metal with independent Bloch quasiparticles still has states arbitrarily close to the Fermi surface, while the experiments ask for a thermodynamic transition, a magnetic-field expulsion, and a gap-like suppression of low-energy electronic excitations. Cleaner single-particle transport changes lifetimes; it does not open a gap or remove the continuum of low-lying excitations. The object that changes cannot be a single electron moving more cleanly.

The first useful crack is Cooper's two-electron problem. If I place two electrons above an inert filled Fermi sea, Pauli exclusion removes all the states below the Fermi surface and leaves a near-degenerate shell of available states. In that setting even a weak net attraction creates a bound eigenstate below the scattering continuum. That tells me the Fermi surface is unstable, but it does not yet tell me the state of a real metal. The pair is enormous in real space, so many other pairs overlap inside its volume. A dilute molecular picture is the wrong next step.

I need the many-electron wavefunction to use the attractive matrix elements without violating Fermi statistics. If I just take random occupation configurations and let the attraction scatter pairs between them, the off-diagonal matrix elements come with the fermion signs from anticommuting the operators, and across an unstructured set of configurations those signs are as often plus as minus, so the coherent energy gain washes out. The way to keep a common sign is to restrict the important configurations so that states occur in time-reversed pairs: if `k up` is occupied, `-k down` is occupied. Then every pair-scattering term `c^dagger_{k up} c^dagger_{-k down} c_{-k' down} c_{k' up}` moves the system between two configurations of the chosen family, and the signs line up, so the energy can be lowered coherently rather than canceling.

I still cannot make each pair state definitely occupied or empty. If I fix every occupation, the pair has nowhere to scatter and I lose the interaction energy I am trying to gain. So I let every near-Fermi pair state be in a superposition of empty and occupied amplitudes, `u_k + v_k c^dagger_{k up} c^dagger_{-k down}`, and write the ground state as the product of these factors over one representative of each time-reversed pair. Particle number is then sharp only in the macroscopic limit or after projection, which I am willing to accept for a variational estimate.

Now I should actually minimize rather than assume the result. Keeping only the pair-scattering channel, the reduced Hamiltonian is `H_red = sum eps_k c^dagger c - sum V_{k k'} c^dagger_{k up} c^dagger_{-k down} c_{-k' down} c_{k' up}`. Taking its expectation in the product state, each pair contributes `2 eps_k v_k^2` from the kinetic part and the interaction couples pairs through the field `Delta = V sum_k u_k v_k`, so the per-pair energy I have to minimize is `2 eps_k v_k^2 - 2 Delta u_k v_k`. Writing `u_k = cos th_k`, `v_k = sin th_k` and differentiating, the stationarity condition is `2 eps_k sin 2th_k - 2 Delta cos 2th_k = 0`, i.e. `tan 2th_k = Delta / eps_k`. That fixes `cos 2th_k = eps_k / E_k` and `sin 2th_k = Delta / E_k` with `E_k = sqrt(eps_k^2 + Delta^2)`, which gives `u_k^2 = (1 + eps_k/E_k)/2` and `v_k^2 = (1 - eps_k/E_k)/2`. Substituting back, the minimized per-pair energy is `eps_k - E_k`: I checked this reduces correctly, `2 eps_k v_k^2 - 2 Delta u_k v_k = eps_k(1 - eps_k/E_k) - Delta^2/E_k = eps_k - E_k`. Since `E_k > |eps_k|`, this sits below both the empty-pair energy `0` and the filled-pair energy `2 eps_k`, so the paired superposition really does lower the energy near the Fermi surface; the pair instability has become a collective ground state, not a two-body bound state. The gap also follows from this: the cheapest excitation flips one pair to a quasiparticle of energy `E_k`, whose minimum over `k` is `Delta`, and a probe that breaks a pair must remove both partners, so it sees a threshold `2 Delta`.

Demanding `Delta` be self-consistent gives `Delta_k = sum_{k'} V_{k k'} u_{k'} v_{k'}(1 - 2 f_{k'})`, and for a constant `V` in a shell `|eps| < hbar omega` with single-spin density of states `N(0)`, this is `1 = N(0) V int_0^{hbar omega} tanh(E/2 k_B T)/E \, d eps`. Before trusting it I want to see whether it actually reproduces the observed scales, so I solve it numerically with `hbar omega = 1` and a representative `N(0)V = 0.3`. At `T=0` (replace `tanh` by 1) I get `Delta(0) = 0.0714`, and the weak-coupling closed form `2 hbar omega exp[-1/(N(0)V)] = 0.0713` lands on top of it, so `Delta(0) approx 2 hbar omega e^{-1/N(0)V}`. Setting `Delta -> 0` and solving for the temperature where a nonzero solution first appears gives `k_B T_c = 0.0404`, and the analytic weak-coupling form `(2 e^gamma/pi) hbar omega e^{-1/N(0)V) = 1.134 hbar omega e^{-1/N(0)V}` gives `0.0405` -- the prefactor I extract numerically is `1.1339`, so `k_B T_c approx 1.14 hbar omega exp[-1/N(0)V]`. The exponential is the same in both, so the dimensionless ratio is parameter-free: numerically `2 Delta(0)/k_B T_c = 3.53`, matching the analytic `2 pi/e^gamma = 3.527`. This is the strong claim of the construction -- a universal number independent of the coupling -- and it comes out of the numerics rather than being put in, which is the check I most wanted.

I then ask whether this microscopic state can also carry the old electromagnetic facts, since a gap alone is not the same as a Meissner effect. Computing the current response in this state, the paramagnetic part is suppressed because exciting across the gap costs energy, so it no longer cancels the diamagnetic term in the long-wavelength limit; the leftover diamagnetic response is exactly London rigidity at zero temperature, and at finite coherence length it takes the nonlocal form Pippard had inferred phenomenologically. I have not carried that integral out here with the care I gave the gap equation, so I hold it as the expected behavior I would want to verify on the kernel rather than as a closed check. Taken together, the effective attraction explains why the Fermi sea wants paired correlations, the variational state lets overlapping pairs use that attraction coherently, the minimization produces gapped quasiparticles with the right universal ratio, and the rigid current response accounts for why transport is not ordinary single-particle motion.
