I start by trying to keep the normal-metal picture and merely improve the scattering story, but that cannot be enough. A metal with independent Bloch quasiparticles still has states arbitrarily close to the Fermi surface, while the experiments ask for a thermodynamic transition, a magnetic-field expulsion, and a gap-like suppression of low-energy electronic excitations. The object that changes cannot be a single electron moving more cleanly.

The first useful crack is Cooper's two-electron problem. If I place two electrons above an inert filled Fermi sea, Pauli exclusion removes all the states below the Fermi surface and leaves a near-degenerate shell of available states. In that setting even a weak net attraction creates a bound eigenstate below the scattering continuum. That tells me the Fermi surface is unstable, but it does not yet tell me the state of a real metal. The pair is enormous in real space, so many other pairs overlap inside its volume. A dilute molecular picture is the wrong next step.

I need the many-electron wavefunction to use the attractive matrix elements without violating Fermi statistics. Random occupation configurations make the off-diagonal signs cancel. The way out is to restrict the important configurations so that states occur in time-reversed pairs: if `k up` is occupied, `-k down` is occupied. Then pair-scattering terms connect the chosen configurations with a common sign, and the energy can be lowered coherently.

I still cannot make each pair state definitely occupied or empty. If I do that, the pair has nowhere to scatter and I lose the interaction energy I am trying to gain. So I let every near-Fermi pair state be in a superposition of empty and occupied amplitudes, `u_k + v_k c^dagger_{k up} c^dagger_{-k down}`. The ground state is the product of these factors over one representative of each time-reversed pair, with the particle number sharp only in the macroscopic limit or after projection. This is the point where the pair instability becomes a collective state rather than a two-body bound-state story.

Minimizing the reduced Hamiltonian gives a quasiparticle spectrum `E_k = sqrt(epsilon_k^2 + Delta^2)`. Now the reason for the gap is visible: one quasiparticle costs at least `Delta`, and pair-breaking or single-particle probes have the threshold `2 Delta` because blocking a pair state removes scattering possibilities for all the other pair states. The same variational parameter that mixes pair occupancy becomes the order parameter, and the normal Fermi surface is replaced by paired coherence across a shell around it.

I then check whether this microscopic state can carry the old electromagnetic facts. The current response calculation shows that the paramagnetic current no longer cancels the diamagnetic term in the long-wavelength limit. At zero temperature the response reduces to London rigidity, while at finite coherence distance it has the nonlocal form Pippard inferred phenomenologically. That closes the loop: the effective attraction explains why the Fermi sea wants paired correlations, the variational state lets overlapping pairs use that attraction coherently, and the resulting gapped, rigid pair state explains why the current response is not ordinary single-particle transport.
