Let me get the question pinned down before I reach for anything. I have a finite set of points in the plane, and I'm told they don't all sit on one line. I want to prove that some line through the set passes through *exactly* two of the points — what I'll call an ordinary line. The opposite of what I want would be a configuration where every line that passes through two of my points is forced to pass through a third (or more): every connecting line is "rich," none is ordinary. So the claim is really that a finite, non-collinear point set cannot be all-rich. That's the thing to kill.

First instinct: is the claim even plausible, or am I about to chase something false? Let me try to *build* a counterexample — a finite set where every two-point line hits a third — and watch where it strains. Start with a triangle, three points. The three connecting lines each have exactly two points: that's three ordinary lines already, so a triangle is hopeless as a counterexample; I need to add points to "fix" each ordinary line. Add a point D on side AB to make line AB rich. That does fix AB, but AC and BC are still ordinary, and D together with C creates a fresh line CD that also has only two of my points. Every point I throw in to extinguish one ordinary line seems to light up another two-point line that demands its own third point. It feels like a fire I can't put out with finitely many buckets. I can't make it close up. That's not a proof, but it suggests the obstruction is some kind of "you always generate a fresh ordinary line faster than you can kill it." I'd like to turn that feeling into something rigorous.

The statement is about the real Euclidean plane, not just an abstract table of which points lie on which lines. That matters. The plane gives me extra structure: distance from a point to a line, perpendiculars, and the order of points along a line. If a bare incidence count does not expose an ordinary line, I should not be shy about spending those real geometric features.

Let me try the incidence-counting attack anyway, just to feel it fail, so I know why I'm abandoning it. Suppose every connecting line has at least three points. Count incidences between points and connecting lines. With n points and, say, t connecting lines, each line eats up at least three points... I can write down inequalities relating n, t, and the number of lines through each point, and I can imagine a heavier route if I dualize the picture, turn points into lines, and try to force a crossing where exactly two lines meet by using Euler's formula on the arrangement. But that is a lot of machinery for such a bare statement: projective duality, an embedded arrangement, face and edge counts. More to the point, the counting by itself doesn't hand me a *specific* line and say "this one is ordinary." It tries to prove existence by a global tally. I keep wanting a *local* reason: some particular feature of the configuration that *forces* an ordinary line right there. Let me set the counting aside and hunt for a local handle.

I want existence of an ordinary line, and I want it *located* by a local feature rather than tallied up globally. Existence proofs that feel local usually come from an *extremal principle*: define some quantity over the configuration, take the configuration element that minimizes or maximizes it, and show that *that* element must have the property you want — because if it didn't, you could find another element with a strictly more extreme value, contradiction. The minimizer can't be improved, so it's forced to be special. The art is choosing the right quantity, the one whose minimizer is provably ordinary.

So: what quantity, attached to a connecting line, would force minimality to imply ordinariness? I want a number that *goes down* when a line is rich — when it has a spare third point I can exploit. Let me brainstorm. The number of points on the line? Minimizing that just gives me a two-point line directly — too good to be true, and indeed it's circular, because "a connecting line with the fewest points" being ordinary is exactly the statement I'm trying to prove; minimizing a count over lines doesn't give me a mechanism, it just renames the goal. I need a quantity whose extremum I can *contradict* by an explicit geometric construction, not one that restates the conclusion.

What geometric quantity can I *decrease* using a spare point on a rich line? A spare third point on a line ℓ is a point sitting on ℓ that I'd like to "peel off." Peeling a point off a line... distance. The distance from a point to a line. That's a real number, it's positive when the point is off the line, and crucially it's the kind of thing a third collinear point lets me shrink. Let me chase this.

Define the quantity on *pairs*: take a point P of my set and a connecting line ℓ that does **not** pass through P, and look at the perpendicular distance d(P, ℓ). I want pairs where the distance is strictly positive, so P ∉ ℓ. Do such pairs exist? Yes: my points aren't all collinear, so there's a connecting line — take any two points, that's a connecting line — and there's at least one point off it, because if every point lay on it they'd all be collinear. So at least one admissible pair (P, ℓ) with d(P, ℓ) > 0 exists. And there are only finitely many points, hence finitely many connecting lines, hence finitely many such pairs. A positive-valued function on a finite nonempty set attains its minimum. So choose a pair (P, ℓ) with d(P, ℓ) minimal among all point-to-connecting-line pairs with the point off the line. This minimum is some honest positive number h.

Now the bet: I claim *this* ℓ — the closest connecting line to any point not on it — is ordinary. Let me try to prove it by contradiction and see if the construction materializes. Suppose ℓ is *not* ordinary, so ℓ carries at least three of my points. I want to use that spare third point to build a *strictly closer* pair, contradicting that (P, ℓ) was the closest.

Drop the perpendicular from P to ℓ; call the foot Q. So PQ ⊥ ℓ and the length PQ = h is my minimal distance. Now Q is a point on the line ℓ, and ℓ has at least three of my points on it. Picture them strung along ℓ. The foot Q splits ℓ into two rays. I have three (or more) of my points sitting on ℓ; by pigeonhole at least two of them lie on the *same* side of Q — the same closed ray out of Q. (I'll let a point that happens to land exactly at Q count as belonging to either ray, so the pigeonhole is clean: three points, two closed rays, two of them share a ray.) Call those two points B and C, with B the one closer to Q — so along ℓ the order is Q, then B, then C, with B possibly sitting right on Q. The key structural fact I've bought: B lies on the segment from Q to C, strictly before C, with B ≠ C.

What do I do with B and C? C is a point of my set, P is a point of my set, P ≠ C, so the line through P and C is a connecting line — call it m. I measure the distance from B to m. If that distance is strictly less than h, then (B, m) is an admissible pair beating (P, ℓ), and the minimal choice of (P, ℓ) collapses.

Let me make sure (B, m) is even admissible, i.e. B is genuinely *off* m, so the distance is positive and it's a legal competitor. Suppose instead B were on m. Then B, C, P would be collinear (B and C both on m, P on m). But B and C are two distinct points of ℓ, so the only line through both of them is ℓ itself; if P were also on that line, P would be on ℓ — contradicting P ∉ ℓ. So B ∉ m, and d(B, m) > 0. Good, it's a legal pair.

Drop the perpendicular from B to m, with foot B′, so d(B, m) = BB′. Before I use the angle at C, I have to check that B′ is on the segment CP, not on the opposite ray of the same line. Put coordinates on this picture with Q = (0,0), the line ℓ as the x-axis toward C, and P = (0,h). Then C = (c,0) for some c > 0, and B = (b,0) with 0 ≤ b < c. A point on the segment from P to C has the form P + t(C - P) = (tc, h(1 - t)). The orthogonal projection of B onto the line PC has parameter

  t = (h^2 + bc)/(h^2 + c^2).

Since h > 0 and 0 ≤ b < c, this gives 0 < t < 1. So B′ really lies between P and C, and ray CB′ is the same as ray CP. Now the two right triangles are in the right position. Triangle PQC has its right angle at Q (since PQ ⊥ ℓ and C is on ℓ). Triangle BB′C has its right angle at B′ (since BB′ ⊥ m and C is on m). They share the angle at C: B lies on the segment QC, so ray CB is the same as ray CQ, and B′ lies on the segment CP, so ray CB′ is the same as ray CP. Two right triangles sharing an acute angle are similar by AA: triangle CQP ~ triangle CB′B.

From the similarity, corresponding sides are proportional. The side opposite the shared angle... let me just line them up by vertices: C ↔ C, Q ↔ B′, P ↔ B. So QP corresponds to B′B, and CP corresponds to CB. Therefore

  BB′ / PQ = CB / CP.

So BB′ = PQ · (CB / CP). For BB′ < PQ I need exactly CB / CP < 1, i.e. CB < CP. Is that true? B sits on segment QC, so CB ≤ CQ. And in the right triangle PQC, the side CP is the hypotenuse (right angle at Q) while CQ is a leg, and the hypotenuse is strictly longer than either leg as long as the triangle is non-degenerate — which it is, because PQ = h > 0 means P is genuinely off ℓ, so the right angle at Q is a real right angle and CP > CQ strictly. Chaining: CB ≤ CQ < CP. So CB < CP, the ratio is strictly less than 1, and

  BB′ = PQ · (CB / CP) < PQ = h.

Let me double-check the one edge case I was nervous about, B = Q. If B coincides with the foot Q, then CB = CQ, and I still have CQ < CP strictly, so the ratio CB/CP = CQ/CP < 1 and BB′ < h still holds. The argument doesn't degrade. The pigeonhole guaranteed two points on one side of Q precisely so that I'd have a B with CB ≤ CQ; that "same side of the foot" is exactly what makes B sit between Q and C and keeps CB no larger than the leg CQ. If the two points had straddled Q, I couldn't have placed both between the foot and a common far point, and CB ≤ CQ would fail. So the side-of-the-foot pigeonhole isn't decoration — it's the thing that delivers CB ≤ CQ.

I have produced an admissible pair (B, m) with d(B, m) = BB′ < h, strictly smaller than the supposed minimum. That contradicts the choice of (P, ℓ) as the closest point-to-connecting-line pair. The contradiction came purely from assuming ℓ had a third point. Therefore ℓ has exactly two points of my set: ℓ is ordinary. The minimizer was forced to be special after all.

The mechanism is now clear in my hands. A rich line ℓ gives me, on one side of P's foot, two of its own points B and C; swinging the connecting line over to m = PC and measuring from the nearer point B, the similar-triangle ratio CB/CP < 1 scales the distance below h. Richness is self-defeating: the extra point is the lever that beats the minimum. The real features I needed are exactly the ones the proof spends — order along ℓ to put B between Q and C, and Euclidean perpendicular distance to turn that order into a strictly smaller admissible distance.
