Let me start from what actually hurts. I want to train a humanoid — whole-body locomotion, dexterous hands, a continuous action vector in [-1,1] with dozens of dimensions — and the thing killing me is the clock. On HumanoidBench even strong algorithms fail to solve many tasks after 48 hours, and the way robotics actually works is that I shape a reward, train, watch the gait, decide the arms flail too much, add a penalty, and train again. Every retrain that costs a day means I get a handful of iterations a week. So I don't just need a *capable* algorithm; I need one that is capable *and* finishes in hours on a single GPU, and stays stable while doing it, and — the part nobody writes down but matters most — is simple enough that I and everyone else can actually run and tune it. A fast method that only one lab can reproduce doesn't move the field.

What do people reach for today? PPO. It's the default for a reason: with a massively parallel simulator throwing thousands of environments at the GPU at once, PPO drowns in fresh on-policy data and learns behaviors fast in wall-clock terms. But it's on-policy by construction — it uses the current policy's rollouts for one update and discards them. That means it is fundamentally not sample-efficient: it can't reuse experience, so I can't easily fine-tune it from a few real-world interactions or warm-start it from demonstrations. For the iterate-on-reward loop I care about, throwing away every transition feels wasteful. I keep wanting the off-policy property: a replay buffer, reuse each transition many times.

So why isn't off-policy the obvious answer? Because the recent off-policy work that *is* sample-efficient — the line that cranks the update-to-data ratio way up and squeezes strong returns out of few environment steps — pays for it in complexity and wall-clock time. High-UTD off-policy learning is unstable, and the way that line keeps it alive is by bolting on stabilizers: LayerNorm everywhere, residual blocks, hyperspherical normalization, sometimes whole auxiliary objectives. Each one is another moving part, another thing to tune, another reason training is slow and hard to reproduce. The instability they're all fighting is the same old beast — the deadly triad: bootstrap your target off your own network (bootstrapping), do it with a neural net (function approximation), and train on data from an older policy (off-policy), and value learning can diverge. That's the wall the whole sample-efficiency camp keeps running into, and their answer is "add architecture."

I don't want to add architecture if I can avoid it. Let me hold that thought and look at the off-policy backbone for continuous actions first, because that's what I'd actually build on.

The deterministic line. The deterministic policy gradient says: if my actor is a deterministic map μ_φ(s)→a and my critic is Q_θ(s,a), I can push the actor uphill on the critic directly by the chain rule, ∇_φ J = E_s[ ∇_a Q_θ(s,a)|_{a=μ_φ(s)} · ∇_φ μ_φ(s) ]. No score-function trick, no integral over actions — just differentiate the critic with respect to the action and backprop into the actor. DDPG made this run with neural nets: replay buffer for reuse, slow target networks θ'←τθ+(1-τ)θ' so the bootstrapped target doesn't chase itself, additive Gaussian noise on the action for exploration, and input normalization because the observation coordinates of a robot live on wildly different physical scales (a joint angle in radians next to an angular velocity next to a contact force) and a network learns badly across that. The known disease of this setup is overestimation: the critic, bootstrapped through a target that effectively maximizes over actions, systematically inflates Q, and then the actor — whose whole job is to maximize Q — eagerly chases the inflated value. Garbage in, garbage chased.

TD3 is the clean fix for that, and it's where I'll plant my flag because it's *deterministic* (good exploitation of the value function) and it's *off-policy* (reuse). Three moves. First, clipped double Q-learning: keep two critics Q_{θ1}, Q_{θ2}, and build the shared bootstrap target with the minimum, y = r + γ·min_{i=1,2} Q_{θ'_i}(s', a'). Why the min? Because the overestimation bias makes each critic an upper-biased estimate of the truth, and taking the smaller of two independent-ish upper-biased estimates clamps the worse one — it can't introduce *more* overestimation than a single critic would, so at worst it underestimates, and an underestimate doesn't get explicitly propagated and amplified by the policy the way an overestimate does. There's a second, quieter benefit: a noisy high-variance estimate is more likely to be penalized by the minimum, so the target tends to prefer value estimates that are reliable enough not to win by a brittle spike. Second, target policy smoothing: when I compute the target action, perturb it, a' = π_{φ'}(s') + ε with ε ~ clip(N(0,σ̃), -c, c). This averages the critic over a small neighborhood of the action so the policy can't exploit a needle-thin spurious peak in the approximate Q — it's a SARSA-flavored regularizer that says "nearby actions should have similar value." Third, delayed policy updates: only update the actor and the targets every d critic steps, a two-timescale thing so the critic settles before each policy move and the policy isn't built on a critic that's still thrashing.

TD3 is solid. But out of the box on a single environment with a modest batch and a scalar critic, it is exactly the slow, sample-hungry thing I am trying to avoid — and it has a real weakness on top: a *deterministic* actor explores terribly. One actor plus a little additive noise gives me one thin trajectory of behavior; the data distribution is narrow, and TD3's strength (greedily exploiting the value function) turns against me when the value function is learned from data that never sees the interesting states. So if I just run vanilla TD3 I get the worst of both: slow and stuck.

Here's the thing I keep circling back to. Off-policy *can* be fast and sample-efficient — there's a precedent. Scaling through a massively parallel GPU simulator, with large batches and a distributional critic, makes value-based off-policy RL both quick in wall-clock and efficient in samples. But the precedent's *core* contribution was a complicated piece of machinery: three asynchronous processes — one collecting data, one learning the policy, one learning the value — running concurrently with hand-set ratios controlling how often each fires relative to the others, all glued together with a distributed-computing library. That asynchrony is what cuts the wall-clock time, and it's also exactly the implementation complexity that kept people from adopting it. So let me ask the question that actually matters: how much of that speed and efficiency comes from the *async parallelism*, and how much comes from the *other ingredients* — the parallel simulation, the big batch, the distributional critic? Because if the heavy lifting is the ingredients and not the async scheduling, then I can throw the complicated part away and keep a simple, synchronous training loop that anyone can run.

Let me reason ingredient by ingredient, starting with parallel environments, because this is where TD3's exploration weakness gets quietly solved without my touching the algorithm. Suppose instead of one environment I step N of them in parallel — say 128, or thousands on a GPU-native sim — each with the same deterministic actor but its own independent exploration noise and its own random initial state. What's the data distribution now? It's the actor's behavior smeared over N independent noise realizations and N starting points simultaneously. The narrow, thin trajectory I was worried about becomes a broad band. And notice this is *precisely* the cure TD3 needs: its weakness was exploration, its strength was exploitation, and parallel simulation injects diversity into the data on the collection side without forcing me to make the policy stochastic or add an entropy bonus or any of the things that complicate the algorithm. The deterministic actor stays deterministic — I get to keep the clean DPG and the strong exploitation — and the fleet of parallel noisy copies manufactures the exploration. That's a much better trade than making the policy itself stochastic, because a stochastic policy in a dozens-of-dimensions humanoid action space is genuinely hard to optimize (this is where maximum-entropy methods start to wobble: maximizing entropy over a 50-dimensional action is a different and nastier problem than over a 6-dimensional one). So: keep the deterministic actor, get exploration from parallelism. And a side effect I'll want later — N environments per step means the replay buffer fills N times faster, so I have a lot of fresh, diverse transitions sitting in the buffer almost immediately.

Now the batch size, and this one only makes sense *because* of the parallel envs, so let me chain it. I've got a big buffer being fed by 128+ environments every single step. If I now sample a normal-sized minibatch — 256, say — from that buffer to do a gradient update, what fraction of the buffer does each update actually see? A sliver. Most of the diverse data I am collecting never touches a gradient before it ages out or before the next batch is drawn. The mismatch is between a high *data-production* rate and a low *data-consumption* rate per update. Two ways to close it: do many more updates per step (high UTD — but that's the unstable, stabilizer-needing regime I'm trying to avoid), or make each update *bigger*. If I take a genuinely large batch — 32,768 — then each gradient step averages the critic loss over a huge, diverse cross-section of the buffer. What does that buy the critic? A low-variance, stable learning signal. The critic's target is a bootstrapped, noisy thing; the deadly triad's instability is partly a variance problem; averaging the TD update over tens of thousands of diverse transitions damps that variance hard and gives a smooth, representative gradient at each step. Yes, a 32k batch costs more wall-clock per update — but I do *fewer* total updates and each one is so much more informative that overall training time goes down, not up, and stability goes up. The large batch is, in effect, a stabilizer that costs no extra architecture — it's just arithmetic over the data the parallel sim is already producing.

This is the moment the "no architecture" intuition pays off. The sample-efficiency camp needs LayerNorm and residuals because high-UTD off-policy updates are violently off-policy — they hammer the same stale data over and over, far from the current policy, and the triad bites. But with a fleet of parallel environments and a huge diverse batch, the *effective* off-policyness of each update is low: the data is fresh (fast-filling buffer) and broad (huge batch), so each update is much closer to "on the current distribution" than a high-UTD update on a thin buffer would be. If the triad is being tamed by data diversity, then the architectural stabilizers are solving a problem I no longer have. Let me actually test that thought against the alternative: if I drop in LayerNorm and residual connections à la the sample-efficiency methods, I'd expect them to add per-update compute (slower) for little gain (the instability they fix isn't present). So the prediction is they slow training without helping — which means a plain descending-width MLP, no normalization, no residual, is the right call here, *and* it's the simple call. Two birds. I'll size the MLPs by the usual logic — bigger nets have more capacity for these high-dimensional value functions, and shrinking them too far should hurt both how fast and how well it learns — landing on a descending stack, 1024→512→256 for the critic and 512→256→128 for the actor, wide at the input where the raw high-dimensional state comes in and tapering toward the output.

Next ingredient: the distributional critic. Why not just regress a scalar Q? Think about what the return distribution looks like in a humanoid task. Standing balance: most of the time you stay up and accumulate reward, but occasionally you tip and the return collapses — that's a bimodal return, a "survive" mode and a "fall" mode. A scalar critic fits the *mean* of that, which is a value no actual outcome ever takes, and worse, the mean smears the two modes together and loses the structure. If instead I learn the whole distribution Z(s,a) of the return, I keep "survive" and "fall" as separate masses, and the critic has a far richer target to fit — which empirically gives a more stable and more capable critic, the same thing the parallel-sim precedent found. The distributional Bellman recursion is sound: Z(s,a) =^D R(s,a) + γ Z(S',A') is a γ-contraction in a maximal Wasserstein metric, so iterating it converges. The trouble is representing a distribution. I'll use the categorical recipe: fix a support of N atoms z_i = v_min + i·Δz, i = 0..N-1, with Δz = (v_max - v_min)/(N-1), and have the critic emit N logits whose softmax gives the probability mass p_i(s,a) on each atom. For a humanoid with returns that can run into the hundreds I'll set the support wide, v_min = -250, v_max = +250, with N = 101 atoms — fine enough to represent structure, coarse enough to be cheap. The price is two new hyperparameters, v_min and v_max, but they're not hard to set; you just need the support to cover the range of returns the task can produce.

The subtlety — and the one piece of real math in the recipe — is the projection. When I apply the Bellman update to the target distribution, each atom z_j gets mapped to Tz_j = r + bootstrap·discount·z_j, and that shifted, scaled location almost never lands exactly on my fixed grid {z_i}. The target distribution and my parametrized distribution usually sit on different support points, so I can't just match them atom-for-atom. I have to *project* the shifted mass back onto the fixed support. Let me derive it carefully because getting a sign or a neighbor wrong here silently corrupts the critic. Take a single target atom at value Tz_j (clamped into [v_min, v_max] so it can't fall off the grid) carrying probability p_j. Its position in grid-index units is b = (Tz_j - v_min)/Δz, a real number between 0 and N-1. Its two integer neighbors are l = floor(b) and u = ceil(b). The natural projection is linear interpolation: split p_j between the two neighbors by how close b is to each, giving the *farther* neighbor the *smaller* share. So atom l receives p_j·(u - b) and atom u receives p_j·(b - l). Check: if b is between adjacent integers, the weights sum to (u - b) + (b - l) = 1, so probability is conserved. If b lands exactly on an integer, the raw floor/ceil pair has l == u and both weights are zero; I must handle that edge case before scattering. The implementation chooses an adjacent pair that still contains the original atom: if b = 0, use l = 0 and u = 1, so the lower atom gets weight 1 and the upper atom gets weight 0; if b = k > 0, use l = k - 1 and u = k, so the lower atom gets weight 0 and atom k gets weight 1. The whole p_j lands on the intended grid atom and total mass stays p_j. Sum this over all target atoms j and I get the projected target distribution m, where m_i = Σ_j p_j · [the interpolation weight of atom j onto grid point i]. In one closed form, the i-th component is (Φ T̂Z)_i = Σ_j [ 1 - |clip(Tz_j) - z_i|/Δz ]_0^1 · p_j(s',a'), the triangular ("tent") kernel of width Δz centered on each shifted atom, clipped to [0,1] — same thing as the floor/ceil split, written as a hat function. Now both the target m and my prediction p(s,a) live on the same support, so I can compare them, and the right loss is cross-entropy: I want my predicted distribution to match the projected Bellman target, which is minimizing the KL D_KL(m ‖ p(s,a)), i.e. loss = -Σ_i m_i · log p_i(s,a). Concretely if the network emits logits, that's -Σ_i m_i · log_softmax(logits)_i, averaged over the batch. That's the categorical-critic loss.

Now I have to fuse the distributional critic with TD3's clipped double Q, and I should stop and think because "min of two Q's" doesn't obviously make sense when each critic is a *distribution*, not a number. I can't take a min of two distributions directly. But I don't need to — the min in TD3 is about picking which value estimate to trust for bootstrapping, and a distribution still has a scalar value, its mean Q = E[Z] = Σ_i p_i·z_i. So: compute both target critics' projected distributions, read off their scalar means via Σ_i (proj)_i·z_i, compare the two *means*, and keep the entire *distribution* belonging to whichever critic had the smaller mean. The min selects a distribution by its expectation; I then use that selected distribution as the cross-entropy target for both critics. That preserves the clipped-double-Q logic (pessimistic value bootstrap) while staying fully distributional. In the no-normalization setting I am choosing, the empirical ablation says this minimum generally beats averaging the two critics, so the min stays in and remains a knob worth tuning per task.

The actor objective follows the same pattern. The DPG says push the actor up on the critic's value; with distributional critics the "value" is the mean of the predicted distribution, Q = Σ_i softmax(logits)_i · z_i for each critic, and I take the clipped-double-Q min of the two means and ascend it — so actor_loss = -min(Q1, Q2).mean(). The backward pass flows through the critic to the action and then into the actor, but only the actor optimizer steps on this update. Delayed updates carry over directly: only do this actor step every policy_frequency critic steps.

Exploration. I am already making parallelism do the heavy lifting, but I want a noise scheme that doesn't make me tune one scale per task. Borrow the mixed-noise idea: give each of the N parallel environments its own Gaussian exploration scale, sampled once from a range [σ_min, σ_max], and resample an environment's scale when its episode ends. With a fleet of environments this is free diversity in exploration aggressiveness — some envs explore timidly, some boldly, and I never have to find the single right σ for a task because the fleet spans a spread. Empirically the *mixed* part doesn't buy much over a fixed scale, but it costs only a few lines and keeps me from per-task tuning, and a *large* σ_max helps — so I keep mixed noise with a generous ceiling. Separately, TD3's target-policy-smoothing noise (the small clipped ε added to the target action) is its own thing and stays.

Update-to-data ratio. The sample-efficiency camp pushes UTD high and pays with instability and stabilizers. I'll deliberately stay *low* — a couple of gradient updates per 128+ environment steps. Why does low UTD work for me when it's "inefficient" elsewhere? Because high UTD's danger is early overfitting: hammering a small amount of early data with many updates burns the critic into a bad value function before the buffer has seen anything interesting. Operating at a low UTD over a fast-filling, diverse buffer sidesteps that — each update sees fresh broad data, and I'm not over-grinding any of it. Sample-efficiency improves if I raise the UTD, but at wall-clock cost, so a low UTD is the right point on the speed/efficiency trade for "fast on a single GPU." And crucially it scales *without* needing the stabilizers — the same no-architecture story.

A couple of remaining knobs that follow from the setup. The replay buffer: instead of a single global capacity, size it as N per environment, i.e. buffer = N × num_envs, and store the whole thing on the GPU (these are low-dimensional states, not images, so it fits and avoids the CPU↔GPU transfer that would otherwise dominate). Why per-env rather than global? Because with a global cap, increasing the number of environments silently shrinks how much of each environment's trajectory survives — at a fixed global size, doubling the envs halves the per-env history retained, truncating trajectories. Sizing per-env decouples the buffer from the env count so each environment can keep a full trajectory regardless of how many I run. The bootstrap mask: when a transition ends an episode I must not bootstrap through a true terminal (there is no future), but I *must* bootstrap through a time-limit truncation (the episode was artificially cut, the future exists) — so the bootstrap multiplier is (truncated OR not-done), which is 1 except on genuine terminations. Optimizer: AdamW with a bit of weight decay to regularize the large nets, and a cosine learning-rate schedule annealing over training. And for raw speed, mixed-precision (bfloat16 autocast) and torch.compile — these are pure wall-clock wins with no effect on the math, and bf16 is numerically safe here.

Step back and look at what I am building. I am not adding a new architecture, a new loss family, or a new training paradigm. I take TD3 — deterministic actor, twin critics, clipped double Q, target smoothing, delayed updates — and I (1) swap the scalar critics for categorical distributional critics with the projection-and-cross-entropy machinery, (2) drive exploration with a fleet of parallel environments plus per-env mixed noise instead of fattening the policy, (3) make each gradient update a huge, diverse, low-variance batch so the critic learns stably, (4) keep the UTD low and the network a plain descending MLP with no normalization, because the data diversity from (2) and (3) already tames the deadly triad that would otherwise demand stabilizers, and (5) size the buffer per-env on the GPU and turn on AMP/compile for speed. Every choice points the same way: get the stability and exploration from *data* (parallelism + big batch), not from *architecture* (LayerNorm + residual + high-UTD machinery), and you get something fast, capable, *and* simple.

Now I map that design onto the actual harness. I make `DistributionalQNetwork` the single categorical critic: concatenate observation and action, pass them through the plain descending MLP, emit atom logits, and keep `v_min`, `v_max`, and `num_atoms` so the projection can compute `delta_z`. Its `projection` method applies `target_z = rewards + bootstrap * discount * q_support`, clamps to the support, computes `b`, `l`, and `u`, and, crucially, computes `l_mask = is_int & (l > 0)` and `u_mask = is_int & (l == 0)` before changing either index, so the integer-atom edge case cannot duplicate probability mass. Then it softmaxes the target critic logits, scatters each atom's probability to the two chosen support indices with `index_add_`, and returns the projected distribution.

I make `Critic` just two of those networks plus a shared `q_support` buffer. Its `projection` returns the two projected target distributions, and its `get_value` is only the expectation `Σ_i p_i z_i`; that expectation is the scalar bridge I need for clipped double Q and the actor objective. I make `Actor` the deterministic descending MLP with a tanh action head and a small final-layer initialization, and I store one exploration scale per environment. In `explore`, I resample only the scales for environments whose episodes have ended, then add Gaussian noise with those per-env scales; evaluation calls `forward` directly and gets the deterministic action.

I fit this into `update_main`, not a separate abstract critic update. The data branch first chooses the critic observations: privileged critic observations when the environment provides asymmetric observations, otherwise the normalized actor observations. It sets `bootstrap = (truncations | ~dones).float()` unless the explicit disable flag is on, in which case it uses `(~dones).float()`. It builds the target-smoothed next action from the current actor plus clipped noise, uses `discount = gamma ** effective_n_steps`, and asks the target critic for both projected distributions. If clipped double Q is on, it computes each projected distribution's mean and keeps the whole distribution from the smaller-mean critic; both current critics train against that same selected target. If clipped double Q is off, each critic trains against its own projected target. The loss is exactly the positive cross-entropy, `-Σ target_i log_softmax(logits)_i`, summed over both critics, then the optimizer step follows the training code's scaler, unscale, optional gradient clipping, and logging path.

I fit the actor into `update_pol`. I pass the actor's action from the normalized actor observation into the critic observation branch, convert both critic logits to probabilities, read off their support-weighted means, and use `torch.minimum(qf1_value, qf2_value)` when clipped double Q is enabled. The actor loss is the negative mean of that value. Backpropagation passes through the critic's action gradient into the actor, but only the actor optimizer steps, and the policy update is still delayed by the outer loop's `policy_frequency`.

The causal chain, start to finish. I need a humanoid-control algorithm that is fast, capable, stable, and simple; PPO is fast but on-policy and sample-inefficient, while the sample-efficient off-policy line is complex and slow because high-UTD off-policy learning trips the deadly triad and has to be propped up with architecture. TD3 gives me a clean off-policy deterministic backbone — twin critics, clipped double Q to kill overestimation, target smoothing, delayed updates — but vanilla it is slow and explores poorly. The precedent that off-policy can be fast-and-efficient hangs its speed on complicated asynchronous parallel processes, so I ask whether the *ingredients* rather than the *async scheduling* do the work, and the design points to yes: parallel environments supply diverse exploration data, which is exactly the cure for TD3's deterministic-exploration weakness and lets me keep the deterministic actor; a fast-filling diverse buffer plus an unusually large 32,768 batch gives the bootstrapped critic a low-variance, stable learning signal; together they drop the effective off-policyness of each update so much that the deadly triad is tamed by data diversity, which means a plain descending MLP with no LayerNorm and no residual is both sufficient and faster, and a low UTD scales without stabilizers. I swap TD3's scalar critics for categorical distributional ones — fixed-support atoms, the floor/ceil interpolation projection that conserves probability, cross-entropy to the projected Bellman target — because the full return distribution keeps separated value modes (survive vs. fall) distinct and yields a more stable, more capable critic, and I make clipped double Q work distributionally by selecting the whole distribution whose mean is smaller. Per-env mixed exploration noise with a large σ_max removes per-task tuning for free, the per-env GPU replay buffer decouples capacity from env count and avoids transfer overhead, the bootstrap mask handles time-limit truncations correctly, and AMP plus torch.compile are pure wall-clock wins. Nothing new is invented; every choice is selected so the stability and exploration come from data, not architecture — which is exactly what makes it fast, capable, and simple at once.
