The thing I actually need is a number, and the central limit theorem will not give it to me. An insurance company is the sum of its claims: over a year its net position is a sum $S_n = Z_1 + \dots + Z_n$ of many independent individual outcomes, and what sizes its reserves is not the typical total but the probability of a *ruinously large* one — a total far out in the right tail. So the question is the asymptotics of $\mathrm{Prob}(Z_1 + \dots + Z_n \text{ large})$, where "large" means large on the scale of $n$ itself, not on the $\sqrt{n}$ scale, and I want not just that this is small but its actual order of magnitude. Set it up cleanly: the $Z_k$ are i.i.d. with common CDF $V$, centred and scaled so $E(Z)=0$, $E(Z^2)=\sigma^2>0$, and write $F_n(x) = \mathrm{Prob}\big((Z_1+\dots+Z_n)/(\sigma\sqrt n) \le x\big)$. The Laplace–Lyapunov theorem, in Lindeberg's and Lévy's sharpened form, gives $F_n(x) \to \Phi(x)$ for each fixed $x$, even with the Lyapunov rate $|F_n(x)-\Phi(x)| \le k\,(\log n)/\sqrt n$ uniformly, $k=(3/\sigma^3)\int |y|^3\,dV(y)$. That looks like it should settle everything, and it settles nothing here. Let $x$ grow with $n$, which is exactly what "large total" forces: as $x\to+\infty$, both $F_n(x)\to 1$ and $\Phi(x)\to 1$, so the limit relation degenerates to the empty $1=1$. The content has migrated into the tail, and the natural object is the *ratio* $(1-F_n(x))/(1-\Phi(x))$ (and on the left $F_n(-x)/\Phi(-x)$). Feeding the Lyapunov bound into that ratio — with $1-\Phi(x)\approx e^{-x^2/2}/(x\sqrt{2\pi})$ — keeps it near $1$ only while the error $O\big((\log n/\sqrt n)\,x\sqrt{2\pi}\,e^{x^2/2}\big)$ stays small, i.e. only for $|x|\lesssim\sqrt{\log n}$, a microscopic window. Past that, and certainly anywhere near $x\sim\sqrt n$ where the sum reaches order $n$, the central limit theorem is silent.

The tempting refinements do not rescue this. Edgeworth/Charlier expansions correct the centre by expanding the *difference* $F_n-\Phi$ in inverse powers of $\sqrt n$ with cumulant coefficients; but deep in the tail the relevant correction to the ratio is not a small additive perturbation, it is an exponentially large *multiplicative* factor that must undo the entire Gaussian tail $e^{-x^2/2}$ and substitute the true one — something no additive expansion of a difference can produce. The prior tail fragments (Khinchin 1929, Smirnov 1933, Lévy 1937) are tied to the single binomial model and confined to small growth of $x$ (Smirnov needs $x=o(n^{1/(4s+6)})$); none is a general mechanism for an arbitrary light-tailed $V$ reaching the $\sqrt n$ scale. And there is a structural ceiling: if $V$ is supported on a bounded interval, $1-F_n(x)$ is identically zero past $x>\mu\sqrt n$, so no universal asymptotic can hold for arbitrarily large $x$ — the honest target is precisely $x\sim\sqrt n$, the sum at order $n$, which is the risk scale. For the right tail to decay at a clean exponential rate one also needs summands lighter than any polynomial, so I impose the basic condition that $R(h)=\int e^{hy}\,dV(y)$ converges for $|h|<A$; then $\log R(h)=\sum_{\nu\ge 2}(\gamma_\nu/\nu!)h^\nu$ generates the cumulants $\gamma_\nu$ of $V$, with $\gamma_1=0$ and $\gamma_2=\sigma^2$.

Here is the real difficulty stated bluntly: the only sharp tool I own, the central limit theorem (and behind it the law of large numbers), is sharp only at the *centre* of a distribution, and my event sits far out in the tail. How do I bring a centre-tool to bear on the tail? I turn the sentence around — if I cannot move my event to the centre, I move the centre to my event. I do not get to change $V$ (it is the data), but I can study a *different* law that agrees with $V$ in shape yet is shifted so that the rare region of $V$ becomes the typical region of the new law. The device for this is sitting in the actuarial toolbox: Esscher's exponential re-weighting. I propose to make it the engine of a general tail theory, and the resulting theorem is **Cramér's large-deviation theorem**: the probability that the sum of i.i.d. light-tailed variables reaches order $n$ decays exponentially, at a rate equal to the **Legendre transform of the cumulant generating function** of the summand, with an explicit $1/\sqrt n$ prefactor in its sharp form.

The mechanism. Pick a real $h$ in the convergence strip and tilt the law, $dV̄ = e^{hy}\,dV/R$ with $R=R(h)$ — again a genuine CDF, with the factor $e^{hy}$ ($h>0$) transferring mass toward $+\infty$, pushing the centre right. The tilted variable $Z̄$ has mean and variance that are just derivatives of the cumulant generating function, $m̄ = (d/dh)\log R = \sum_{\nu\ge 2}\gamma_\nu h^{\nu-1}/(\nu-1)!$ and $\sigmā^2 = dm̄/dh = (d^2/dh^2)\log R$. Since $\sigmā^2>0$ is a variance, $m̄$ is continuous and strictly increasing in $h$ with $m̄(0)=0$: this gives a *dial*, a unique tilt $h$ for each target tilted mean, and that monotonicity is what the whole construction leans on. The exact bookkeeping connecting what I want to what the CLT can handle goes through characteristic functions: with $\nu(z)=\int e^{izy}dV(y)$ holomorphic in $|\Im z|<A$, one has $R=\nu(-ih)$ and $\bar\nu(z)=\nu(z-ih)/R$, so by independence $\bar w_n(z)=\nu(z-ih)^n/R^n = w_n(z-ih)/R^n$; shifting $z\to z+ih$ and using $\bar w_n(ih)=1/R^n$ gives $w_n(z)=R^n\bar w_n(z+ih)$, which is equivalent to the CDF identity $W_n(x)=R^n\int_{-\infty}^x e^{-hy}\,dW̄_n(y)$. This un-tilts the $n$-fold sum, and the price of un-tilting is the explicit factor $R^n e^{-hy}$ — the same exponential weight raised to the power matching $n$ summands — which is exactly where the rare-event cost hides. In normed variables this reads

$$1 - F_n(x) = R^n\, e^{-h m̄ n} \int_{(\sigma x - m̄\sqrt n)/\sigmā}^{\infty} e^{-h\sigmā\sqrt n\, y}\, dF̄_n(y),$$

with $F̄_n$ the normed tilted sum. The CLT applies to $F̄_n$ because it is the normed sum of i.i.d. tilted variables. The clever part is the lower limit: choosing $x$ so that $\sigma x = m̄\sqrt n$, i.e. $x=m̄\sqrt n/\sigma$, sets the lower limit to $0$ — the centre of $F̄_n$. The rare event of $F_n$ has been dialed into the centre of $F̄_n$, where the CLT is sharp. Writing $F̄_n=\Phi+Q_n$ with $|Q_n|<K(\log n)/\sqrt n$, the $d\Phi$ piece is a Gaussian-against-exponential integral and the $dQ_n$ piece is $O(\log n/\sqrt n)$ after integration by parts; completing the square in $(1/\sqrt{2\pi})\int_0^\infty e^{-(m̄\sqrt n/\sigma)y - y^2/2}\,dy = e^{n m̄^2/(2\sigma^2)}[1-\Phi(m̄\sqrt n/\sigma)]$ collects the whole correction into the exponential of $n\big(m̄^2/(2\sigma^2) - h m̄ + \log R\big)$. Setting $z=m̄/\sigma$ (so $x=z\sqrt n$) and inverting the cumulant series for $h$ in terms of $z$, the order-$z^2$ terms cancel and what survives starts at $z^3$: $m̄^2/(2\sigma^2)-h m̄ +\log R = z^3\lambda(z)$ with $\lambda(z)=c_0+c_1 z+\dots$, $c_0=\gamma_3/(6\sigma^3)$, $c_1=(\sigma^2\gamma_4-3\gamma_3^2)/(24\sigma^6)$. This is the fundamental theorem in the moderate range: for $1<x=o(\sqrt n/\log n)$,

$$\frac{1-F_n(x)}{1-\Phi(x)} = \exp\!\Big[\frac{x^3}{\sqrt n}\,\lambda\!\Big(\frac{x}{\sqrt n}\Big)\Big]\cdot\big[1+O(x\log n/\sqrt n)\big],$$

with the symmetric left-tail statement proved identically. The lead coefficient $c_0=\gamma_3/6\sigma^3$ is the skewness — a right-skewed $V$ makes large positive sums more likely than Gaussian — and the consistency check is built in: since the exponent is $(x^3/\sqrt n)\lambda$, for $x=o(n^{1/6})$ it $\to 0$ and the ratio $\to 1$, recovering the CLT regime. (One checks $h\sqrt n\sim x/\sigma$ is bounded below for $x>1$, so the tilt genuinely sends the event to the centre.) This already absorbs the binomial fragments of Khinchin, Smirnov and Lévy as the two-point special case of one mechanism.

To reach the real prize, $x=c\sqrt n$ (sum of order $c\sigma n$), the obstruction is the $\log n$ in the Lyapunov error, which forces $x\log n/\sqrt n\to 0$. That $\log n$ is the worst-case CLT error for a general CDF that can oscillate near a lattice; if $V$ has even a little smoothness it disappears. So I add a mild condition: in $V=\beta U_1+(1-\beta)U_2$ (absolutely continuous part $U_1$, singular part $U_2$) require $\beta>0$, a nontrivial smooth component, inherited by $V̄$. Then $|Q_n|<K/\sqrt n$ with no $\log n$, the regime opens to $x=o(\sqrt n)$, and I can take $x$ to order $\sqrt n$. Set $x=c\sqrt n$ with $0<c<C_1$, where $\sigma C_1=\lim_{h\to A_1}m̄$ is the largest reachable tilted mean. Because $m̄$ is continuous and strictly increasing, $m̄=\sigma c$ has a unique root $h$ in the strip — this is the punchline of the tilt: choose $h$ precisely so that $\sigma c$ becomes the tilted mean, making the deviation typical under the tilted law, so the CLT/LLN error is genuinely $o(1)$. Redoing the integral with the sharper error gives the sharp asymptotic: there is $C_1>0$ such that for $0<c<C_1$,

$$1-F_n(c\sqrt n) = \frac{1}{\sqrt n}\, e^{-\alpha n}\Big[b_0 + \frac{b_1}{n} + \dots + \frac{b_{k-1}}{n^{k-1}} + O(1/n^k)\Big],\qquad b_0=\frac{1}{h\sigmā\sqrt{2\pi}}>0,$$

with rate $\alpha = h m̄ - \log R$ at the root of $m̄=\sigma c$. Now recognize $\alpha$: since $m̄=(d/dh)\log R$ and $h$ solves $(d/dh)\log R=\sigma c$, the expression $h(\sigma c)-\log R(h)$ is exactly the value of the Legendre transform $\Lambda^*(a)=\sup_h[h a-\log R(h)]$ at $a=\sigma c$. So $\alpha=\Lambda^*(\sigma c)$: the exponential rate of a rare sum is the Legendre transform of the cumulant generating function of the summand, and by Jensen ($\log R(h)\ge h E(Z)=0$) it is $\ge 0$ with equality only at the mean, hence strictly positive for $c\neq 0$.

There is a leaner argument hiding inside this that pins the *rate* alone from above and below separately, and it is worth giving because it shows why the Legendre transform is forced rather than guessed. In bare i.i.d. form, with $\Lambda(t)=\log E[e^{tX_1}]$ and $a>E[X_1]$, the claim is $\lim_n (1/n)\log\mathrm{Prob}(S_n/n\ge a)=-\Lambda^*(a)$, $\Lambda^*(a)=\sup_t[ta-\Lambda(t)]$. The upper bound is almost free: for any $t\ge 0$, $\{S_n\ge na\}=\{e^{tS_n}\ge e^{tna}\}$, so Markov gives $\mathrm{Prob}(S_n\ge na)\le e^{-tna}(E[e^{tX_1}])^n = e^{-n(ta-\Lambda(t))}$; this holds for every $t\ge 0$, so optimizing the free tilt yields $e^{-n\sup_{t\ge 0}(ta-\Lambda(t))}=e^{-n\Lambda^*(a)}$ (the constraint $t\ge 0$ is non-binding for $a>E[X_1]$). The Legendre transform *emerges* from optimizing the tilt in the Chernoff bound — it was not assumed. The matching lower bound is where the tilt earns its keep probabilistically. Let $t^*$ be the unique root of $\Lambda'(t^*)=a$ and define the tilted law $d\mu_{t^*}(x)=e^{t^*x-\Lambda(t^*)}d\mu(x)$, a probability measure whose mean is $\Lambda'(t^*)=a$ — under it the rare value is typical. Restricting to the slab $\{S_n\in[na,na+n\delta)\}$ and rewriting the original probability as an expectation under the tilt, then bounding $e^{-t^*\sum x_i}\ge e^{-t^*n(a+\delta)}$ on the slab, gives $\mathrm{Prob}(S_n\in[na,na+n\delta))\ge e^{n\Lambda(t^*)}e^{-t^*n(a+\delta)}\,\mu_{t^*}^{\otimes n}(S_n/n\in[a,a+\delta))$; since $a$ is now the mean and the tilted variance $\Lambda''(t^*)$ is finite, that last probability is bounded away from $0$ (in fact $\to 1/2$ by the CLT). Taking $(1/n)\log$ and letting $\delta\to 0$ gives $\liminf\ge -\Lambda^*(a)$. The two bounds meet because the value of the sup in the upper bound is attained at exactly the $t^*$ where $\Lambda'(t^*)=a$ that the lower bound re-centres around — the optimization and the re-centering are two faces of one convex-duality fact, which is *why* the rate is exactly the Legendre transform and not merely bounded by it. (For unbounded support or $\Lambda(t)=\infty$, truncate $X$ to $[-A,A]$, run the argument, and let $A\to\infty$, using $\Lambda_A\uparrow\Lambda$.) The rate function $\Lambda^*$ is convex, nonnegative, lower-semicontinuous, vanishes only at the mean, and grows on both sides; the tail of a sum decays like $e^{-n\Lambda^*(a)}$, the magnitude a risk reserve must be sized against. As a concrete check, for Bernoulli $X\in\{0,1\}$ with $P(X=1)=p$ one has $\Lambda(t)=\log(1-p+pe^t)$ and $\Lambda^*(a)=a\log(a/p)+(1-a)\log((1-a)/(1-p))$, the relative entropy of $\mathrm{Bernoulli}(a)$ from $\mathrm{Bernoulli}(p)$ — recovering the classical Chernoff bound for coin tossing.

The rate function is the Legendre transform of the cumulant generating function, directly computable from a sample of the summand:

```python
import numpy as np
from scipy.optimize import minimize_scalar

def mgf(t, sample):
    return np.mean(np.exp(t * sample))                      # R(t) = E[e^{tZ}]

def rate_function(a, sample):
    """Λ*(a) = sup_t ( t a − log R(t) ): the exponential rate of P(S_n/n ≈ a)."""
    Lambda = lambda t: np.log(mgf(t, sample))               # Λ(t) = log E[e^{tZ}]
    obj = lambda t: -(t * a - Lambda(t))                    # negate to maximize
    res = minimize_scalar(obj)
    return -res.fun                                          # = sup_t (t a − Λ(t))
```
