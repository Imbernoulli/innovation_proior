I start with a practical nuisance rather than a new block. I have a convolutional network that works at one budget, and when someone gives me more compute I know the usual move: make it deeper, or wider, or feed larger images. All three moves work for a while. The problem is that they are not rules. They are habits, and each habit gets expensive before it gets reliable.

Let me name the degrees of freedom cleanly. A convolutional network is a sequence of stages. Stage i has an operator F_i repeated L_i times, and it acts on tensors with spatial size H_i by W_i and channel count C_i. If I keep the operators fixed, the scalable quantities are just L_i, C_i, H_i, and W_i. In words, those are depth, width, and resolution.

If I push only depth, I get richer composition and larger effective receptive fields. But I also remember the residual-network lesson: skip connections and batch normalization make very deep networks trainable, yet the accuracy gain still flattens. A thousand-layer residual network is not a simple free upgrade over a hundred-layer one. So depth alone is not the answer.

If I push only width, the network gets more channels for fine-grained features and it is often easier to optimize than a very deep thin network. Wide residual networks make that case clearly. But if the network is too shallow, those wide feature maps do not automatically become high-level abstractions. Width also saturates.

If I push only input resolution, I give the model more pixels per object and more fine detail. That has helped the field move beyond 224 by 224 inputs. But it also saturates, and the cost is severe because spatial area grows quadratically with side length.

The common failure mode is the clue. Each single dimension improves accuracy, then flattens. That makes me suspect the knobs are coupled. A higher-resolution image is not useful by itself: if the image is larger, the late features need enough depth to cover comparable object extent, and they need enough channels to represent the extra detail. So the right question is not which knob wins. It is how to move depth, width, and resolution together.

The unconstrained version is too large. I could choose a separate repeat count, channel count, and spatial size for every stage under memory and FLOP constraints, but then every new target budget asks for another search. That defeats the purpose. I want a small rule that keeps the coupling while avoiding a new architecture search at every scale.

Let me try the strongest simplification that still respects all three dimensions. Tie everything to one budget coefficient φ and three fixed constants: depth d = α^φ, width w = β^φ, resolution r = γ^φ, with α, β, γ at least one because this is a scale-up rule. The whole family is then controlled by one knob once the three constants are chosen.

But three constants and one φ are still four numbers, and I do not yet have a reason that pins them down. The missing constraint should come from compute, since the entire motivation is spending a budget. So let me write the cost law. A regular k by k convolution with C_in input channels, C_out output channels, and H by W output positions costs proportional to k^2 C_in C_out H W. If I add d times as many layers, the cost scales linearly in d. If I multiply all channels by w, both C_in and C_out scale, so the cost scales as w^2. If I multiply each spatial side by r, area scales as r^2. So the dominant convolutional cost scales like d · w^2 · r^2.

Substituting the one-knob rule gives α^φ · β^(2φ) · γ^(2φ) = (α β^2 γ^2)^φ. Now I have the lever I was missing. If I fix the base α β^2 γ^2 to a chosen value, then a single number controls how compute grows with φ, and I can make φ mean something clean. The natural choice is to make it a doubling: pick α β^2 γ^2 ≈ 2, so that each unit of φ roughly doubles FLOPs.

I want to be careful before I trust the word "doubling," because it is easy to assert and easy to get slightly wrong. Suppose I had a triple whose product is exactly 2. Then the compute multiplier is 2^φ: φ = 1 doubles, φ = 2 quadruples, and so on. Clean. But the triples I will actually be able to use are not exact — channels and repeats round to integers, image sizes have to be usable — so the product will land near 2, not on it. Let me carry a concrete near-2 triple and see how much the rounding of the *base* away from 2 matters as φ grows.

Take α = 1.2, β = 1.1, γ = 1.15, a triple of the right shape that I will justify shortly. The product is 1.2 · 1.1^2 · 1.15^2. Computing it: 1.1^2 = 1.21, 1.15^2 = 1.3225, and 1.2 · 1.21 · 1.3225 = 1.92027. So the base is 1.9203, not 2. Now the compute multiplier is 1.9203^φ, and I should check what that does at larger φ rather than pretend it is 2^φ:

- φ = 1: d = 1.20, w = 1.10, r = 1.15, and d w^2 r^2 = 1.9203, against a nominal 2. Off by 4%.
- φ = 2: d = 1.44, w = 1.21, r = 1.3225, and d w^2 r^2 = 3.687, against a nominal 4. Off by 8%.
- φ = 3: d = 1.728, w = 1.331, r = 1.521, and d w^2 r^2 = 7.08, against a nominal 8. Off by 11%.

So the gap compounds: φ is an *approximate* compute-doubling knob, and at the high end of the family (φ near 7) the accumulated drift is large enough that I should never describe the FLOPs as exactly 2^φ. The honest statement is that constraining α β^2 γ^2 ≈ 2 makes one unit of φ cost about one doubling, with the understanding that the actual factor is the base raised to φ. That is good enough for the purpose — the rule is for distributing a budget, not for hitting a FLOP count to three digits — but I would rather state it as the controlled approximation it is than overclaim a clean power of two.

This same constraint also tells me something about the *balance* I should expect, before any accuracy data. Depth appears linearly in d w^2 r^2, while width and resolution appear quadratically. For a fixed compute budget, then, a unit of α buys more shape than a unit of β or γ at the same FLOP cost, so I would expect the fitted depth coefficient to come out larger than the width and resolution coefficients unless the accuracy response strongly says otherwise. That is not a theorem about accuracy; it is a cost prior imposed by convolution arithmetic. Worth holding onto as a sanity check on whatever the search returns.

I still need actual α, β, and γ. Searching them around a large model is exactly what I am trying to avoid, so I search them once around the small baseline with φ fixed to 1. The grid only has to cover triples satisfying α β^2 γ^2 ≈ 2, which is a one-dimensional surface, not a free three-dimensional sweep — cheap. For this baseline the grid returns α = 1.2, β = 1.1, γ = 1.15, the triple I already used above; its product 1.9203 sits just under the intended 2 after rounding to one decimal each. And the ordering matches the cost prior: depth's 1.2 exceeds width's 1.1 and resolution's 1.15, as the linear-versus-quadratic asymmetry led me to expect. Then I freeze the constants and reuse them for larger members of the family.

There is an assumption here, and I should not hide it. I am assuming the best balance between depth, width, and resolution is stable enough across scales that the small-model grid search remains useful later. That may not be globally optimal — the true optimum could drift as the network grows. But it turns an otherwise repeated expensive search into a one-time calibration, and the coupling argument is structural enough to make the reuse plausible. I would want to confirm it by checking, on at least one larger member, that a fresh local search does not beat the frozen ratios by much; short of that, I am trading some optimality for not re-searching at every budget.

Now I have to build a baseline worth scaling. Scaling preserves the operator family. If the starting block is weak, a clean scaling rule only gives me a larger weak network. So the baseline needs an efficient modern block before the scaling rule matters.

A standard convolution entangles spatial filtering and channel mixing in one expensive operation. Depthwise separable convolution splits those jobs: a depthwise k by k filter handles spatial filtering per input channel, and a pointwise 1 by 1 convolution mixes channels. Let me confirm the saving is real and not marginal. Standard cost on a D_F by D_F map from M to N channels is k^2 M N D_F^2. Separated, it is k^2 M D_F^2 (depthwise) plus M N D_F^2 (pointwise), so the ratio of separated to standard is (k^2 M D_F^2 + M N D_F^2) / (k^2 M N D_F^2) = 1/N + 1/k^2. For k = 3 and any sizeable N, 1/k^2 = 1/9 dominates and 1/N is small, so the block costs roughly an eighth to a ninth of the dense one. That is the order-of-magnitude saving that makes mobile-size networks possible; it is large enough to build on.

The block should not be a classic residual bottleneck. With depthwise convolution the spatial filtering is cheap enough to run in an expanded channel space, while the endpoints should stay thin for memory and cheap residual connections. So I use an inverted residual: expand with 1 by 1 convolution, filter with depthwise convolution, project back with 1 by 1 convolution, and connect the residual across the thin endpoints when stride and channel count match.

The projection back to the thin endpoint should stay linear. A ReLU in a narrow bottleneck can zero out coordinates and erase information that, once collapsed in a low-dimensional space, cannot be recovered. In the expanded space the nonlinearity has room to fold without destroying rank; in the compressed endpoint it is too destructive. So I leave the final projection without an activation. This is the linear-bottleneck choice from the inverted-residual line of work.

I add channel recalibration because it is cheap and directly useful. After the depthwise phase, I squeeze each channel by global average pooling to one number, pass that descriptor through a small two-layer bottleneck ending in a sigmoid, and multiply the resulting per-channel weights back into the feature map. One detail to get right is how small the bottleneck is. If I tie the reduction to the *expanded* filter count, the squeeze path grows with the 6x expansion and stops being negligible; tying it to the block's *input* filters keeps it small. Concretely, with se_ratio 0.25 and an input of 24 channels, the reduced descriptor is int(24 · 0.25) = 6 channels regardless of the expanded width — a tiny two-layer head. So I compute the reduction from the input filters, as in the reference implementation.

For the baseline stage table, I do not want to hand-place every operator. I reuse the same multi-objective architecture search as the mobile NAS predecessor, but with FLOPs rather than measured latency in the reward, because I am not targeting one device. The objective is accuracy times a soft cost term, ACC(m) · (FLOPS(m) / T)^w, with w around −0.07 and a target T near 400M FLOPs. The search returns a compact baseline built entirely from the MBConv block above: a stride-2 3 by 3 stem to 32 channels, then seven MBConv stages with output channels 16, 24, 40, 80, 112, 192, 320 and repeats 1, 2, 2, 3, 3, 4, 1, with kernels 3, 3, 5, 3, 5, 5, 3. The first stage uses expansion 1, the rest expansion 6, and every stage uses squeeze-excitation ratio 0.25. That is 1+2+2+3+3+4+1 = 16 MBConv layers in the baseline body. Call this member of the family the φ = 0 point, B0.

When I turn the scaling rule into deployable models, I cannot keep real-valued channels or repeats. Width is multiplied by the coefficient and snapped to the nearest multiple of 8, with a guard so that snapping down never drops more than 10 percent of the intended channels. Let me trace this on the baseline channels to make sure it does something sensible and not, say, collapse distinct stages together. Take the width coefficient 1.1 (the B2 width):

- 16 → 16 · 1.1 = 17.6 → nearest multiple of 8 is 16.
- 40 → 44.0 → snaps *up* to 48.
- 80 → 88.0 → already a multiple of 8, stays 88.
- 112 → 123.2 → snaps *down* to 120.

So the rounding genuinely goes both ways, 44 up to 48 and 123.2 down to 120, which is what "nearest multiple of 8" should do, and the stages stay distinct. I also checked when the 10 percent guard actually fires: across the real channel counts and every width coefficient in the family it never triggers — it only matters for tiny counts like 5 channels, where rounding to 8 would otherwise lose more than 10 percent. So the guard is a safety net that does not bind on this network; good to know it is inert rather than silently reshaping the table.

Depth repeats are multiplied by the depth coefficient and rounded *up* with a ceiling, so a scaled stage never has fewer layers than the baseline. Tracing the deepest member, depth coefficient 3.1, on the baseline repeats 1, 2, 2, 3, 3, 4, 1: ceil of 3.1·[1,2,2,3,3,4,1] = [4, 7, 7, 10, 10, 13, 4], summing to 55 layers, up from 16 in B0. For B4 at depth 1.8 the same rule gives [2, 4, 4, 6, 6, 8, 2] = 32 layers. Both are monotone increasing in the coefficient and never drop below the baseline, as the ceiling guarantees.

The named family then stores rounded practical coefficients per model — B0 at width 1.0, depth 1.0, resolution 224, up to B7 at width 2.0, depth 3.1, resolution 600 — which are the snapped points along the balanced (α, β, γ) direction rather than exact raw powers printed with all their decimals. The resolutions climb roughly as γ^φ as well: 224, 240, 260, 300, 380, 456, 528, 600.

The regularization must scale too, because the larger members have more capacity and will overfit at the small-model dropout. So dropout rises from 0.2 at the small end to 0.5 at the large end. Stochastic depth is implemented as drop-connect on residual branches with survival probability 0.8 at the deepest end, scaled linearly by block index so early blocks are almost always kept and later blocks are dropped more often. During training the kept branch is divided by its survival probability, so the expected residual contribution is unchanged; with survival 0.8 the surviving branch is scaled by 1/0.8 = 1.25, and 0.8 · 1.25 = 1, confirming the expectation is preserved.

Stepping back, the pieces fit together as a chain rather than a coincidence. The empirical obstacle is that single-dimension scaling saturates; the structural reason is that higher resolution is only useful with enough receptive field and channels to match it; the cost law d w^2 r^2 then turns "scale all three together" into the single constraint α β^2 γ^2 ≈ 2, which I checked actually controls compute (as a drifting approximation, 1.9203^φ, not a clean 2^φ). The constants come from one small grid search whose ordering matches the linear-versus-quadratic cost prior, and the deployable family is that balanced direction snapped to integer channels and ceiling'd repeats — 16 layers at B0, 55 at B7 — built from inverted-residual, linear-bottleneck, depthwise-separable, squeeze-excitation blocks, with reference-faithful rounding, SAME padding, and per-block drop-connect.
