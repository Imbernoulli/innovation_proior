I start with a practical nuisance rather than a new block. I have a convolutional network that works at one budget, and when someone gives me more compute I know the usual move: make it deeper, or wider, or feed larger images. All three moves work for a while. The problem is that they are not rules. They are habits, and each habit gets expensive before it gets reliable.

Let me name the degrees of freedom cleanly. A convolutional network is a sequence of stages. Stage i has an operator F_i repeated L_i times, and it acts on tensors with spatial size H_i by W_i and channel count C_i. If I keep the operators fixed, the scalable quantities are just L_i, C_i, H_i, and W_i. In words, those are depth, width, and resolution.

If I push only depth, I get richer composition and larger effective receptive fields. But I also remember the residual-network lesson: skip connections and batch normalization make very deep networks trainable, yet the accuracy gain still flattens. A thousand-layer residual network is not a simple free upgrade over a hundred-layer one. So depth alone is not the answer.

If I push only width, the network gets more channels for fine-grained features and it is often easier to optimize than a very deep thin network. Wide residual networks make that case clearly. But if the network is too shallow, those wide feature maps do not automatically become high-level abstractions. Width also saturates.

If I push only input resolution, I give the model more pixels per object and more fine detail. That has helped the field move beyond 224 by 224 inputs. But it also saturates, and the cost is severe because spatial area grows quadratically with side length.

The common failure mode is the clue. Each single dimension improves accuracy, then flattens. That makes me suspect the knobs are coupled. A higher-resolution image is not useful by itself: if the image is larger, the late features need enough depth to cover comparable object extent, and they need enough channels to represent the extra detail. So the right question is not which knob wins. It is how to move depth, width, and resolution together.

The unconstrained version is too large. I could choose a separate repeat count, channel count, and spatial size for every stage under memory and FLOP constraints, but then every new target budget asks for another search. That defeats the purpose. I need a small rule that keeps the coupling while avoiding a new architecture search at every scale.

I try the strongest simplification that still respects all three dimensions: one budget coefficient φ and three fixed constants. Depth becomes d = α^φ, width becomes w = β^φ, and resolution becomes r = γ^φ. The constants α, β, and γ are at least one, because this is a scale-up rule. Now the whole family is controlled by one knob once I have chosen the three constants.

The FLOP constraint tells me how to choose the form of the constraint. A regular k by k convolution with C_in input channels, C_out output channels, and H by W output positions costs proportional to k^2 C_in C_out H W. If I add d times as many layers, the cost scales linearly in d. If I multiply all channels by w, both C_in and C_out scale, so the cost scales as w^2. If I multiply each spatial side by r, area scales as r^2. So the dominant convolutional cost scales like d w^2 r^2.

Substituting the one-knob rule gives α^φ β^(2φ) γ^(2φ), or (α β^2 γ^2)^φ. This is the important arithmetic. If I constrain α β^2 γ^2 to be about 2, then φ means a power-of-two compute dial: φ = 1 is about 2x FLOPs, φ = 2 is about 4x, and so on. The approximation is honest, because channels and repeats must be rounded to integers and image sizes must be usable.

This constraint also warns me about the balance. Depth is linear in the cost law, while width and resolution are quadratic. I should expect the fitted depth coefficient to be larger than the width and resolution coefficients unless the accuracy response says otherwise. That is not a theorem about accuracy; it is a cost prior imposed by convolution arithmetic.

I still need α, β, and γ. Searching them around a large model is exactly what I am trying to avoid, so I search them once around the small baseline with φ fixed to 1. The grid only has to cover triples satisfying α β^2 γ^2 ≈ 2. For this baseline, the selected constants are α = 1.2, β = 1.1, and γ = 1.15. The product is 1.2 * 1.1^2 * 1.15^2 = 1.9203, close enough to the intended doubling after rounding. Then I freeze the constants and reuse them for larger members of the family.

There is an assumption here, and I should not hide it. I am assuming that the best balance between depth, width, and resolution is stable enough across scales that the small-model grid search remains useful later. That may not be globally optimal. But it turns an otherwise repeated expensive search into a one-time calibration, and the coupling argument is structural enough to make the reuse plausible.

Now I have to build a baseline worth scaling. Scaling preserves the operator family. If the starting block is weak, a beautiful scaling rule only gives me a larger weak network. So the baseline needs an efficient modern block before the scaling rule matters.

A standard convolution entangles spatial filtering and channel mixing in one expensive operation. Depthwise separable convolution splits those jobs: a depthwise k by k filter handles spatial filtering per input channel, and a pointwise 1 by 1 convolution mixes channels. The cost becomes k^2 M D_F^2 + M N D_F^2 instead of k^2 M N D_F^2. For 3 by 3 kernels and ordinary channel counts, that is the large savings that make mobile networks possible.

The block should not be a classic residual bottleneck. With depthwise convolution, the spatial filtering is cheap enough to run in an expanded channel space, while the endpoints should stay thin for memory and cheap residual connections. So I use an inverted residual: expand with 1 by 1 convolution, filter with depthwise convolution, project back with 1 by 1 convolution, and connect the residual across the thin endpoints when stride and channel count match.

The projection back to the thin endpoint must stay linear. A ReLU in a narrow bottleneck can erase information that cannot be recovered. In the expanded space the nonlinearity has room; in the compressed endpoint it is too destructive. This is the linear-bottleneck lesson from MobileNetV2.

I add channel recalibration because it is cheap and directly useful. After the depthwise phase, I squeeze each channel by global average pooling, pass that descriptor through a small bottleneck and a sigmoid, and multiply the resulting channel weights back into the feature map. The reduction is tied to the block input filters, not the expanded filters, so the squeeze-excitation path stays small as in the reference implementation.

For the baseline stage table, I use the same kind of multi-objective architecture search as the mobile NAS predecessor, but with FLOPs rather than measured latency because I am not targeting one device. The reward is accuracy times a soft cost term, ACC(m) * (FLOPS(m) / T)^w, with w around -0.07 and a target near 400M FLOPs. The search chooses the stage operators and yields a compact baseline: a stride-2 3 by 3 stem to 32 channels, then seven MBConv stages with output channels 16, 24, 40, 80, 112, 192, and 320, and repeats 1, 2, 2, 3, 3, 4, and 1. The kernels are 3, 3, 5, 3, 5, 5, and 3. The first block has expansion 1; the rest use expansion 6; every block uses squeeze-excitation ratio 0.25.

When I turn the scaling rule into deployable models, I cannot keep real-valued channels or repeats. Width is multiplied and rounded to a multiple of 8, with a guard so rounding down does not lose more than 10 percent of the intended channels. Depth repeats are multiplied and rounded up with a ceiling. The official family stores practical coefficients for each named model, for example B0 at width 1.0, depth 1.0, resolution 224 and B7 at width 2.0, depth 3.1, resolution 600. These are the snapped family points along the balanced direction, not exact raw powers printed with all decimals.

The regularization must scale too. Larger members have more capacity, so dropout rises from 0.2 at the small end to 0.5 at the large end. Stochastic depth is implemented as drop-connect on residual branches with survival probability 0.8 at the deepest end, linearly adjusted by block index so earlier blocks are nearly always kept and later blocks receive more dropping. During training the kept branch is divided by its survival probability, so the expected residual contribution is unchanged.

At this point the story closes. The empirical obstacle is that single-dimension scaling saturates. The structural explanation is that higher resolution needs both more receptive field and more channels. The mathematical constraint is that convolution FLOPs scale as d w^2 r^2, which makes α β^2 γ^2 ≈ 2 the clean way to make φ a compute-doubling knob. The implementation consequence is a strong small NAS baseline built from inverted residual, linear-bottleneck, depthwise-separable, squeeze-excitation blocks, with reference-faithful rounding, SAME padding, and per-block drop-connect.
