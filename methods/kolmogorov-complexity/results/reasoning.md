I want a notion of information that attaches to a single object in front of me — this one binary string — not to the process that might have produced it. Shannon's theory does not give me that. Entropy is a property of a source: it measures the average code length over an ensemble of possible outcomes, and the same string is typical under one distribution and wildly atypical under another. Everything probabilistic I say about the string is really a statement about a model I chose from outside. So the question I keep circling is: what is the intrinsic information content of an individual string, taken in isolation?

There is already one serious attempt at individual randomness on the table, and it is worth walking into it because it fails in an instructive way. Von Mises proposed that an infinite sequence is random — a collective — if the limiting frequency of 1s exists and no admissible place-selection rule can extract a subsequence with a different limit. This is his impossibility of a gambling system: a random sequence is one no gambler can systematically exploit. The obvious defect is that "admissible" is doing all the work. Allow all selection rules and nothing is random, since the rule "select exactly the positions where x has a 1" destroys any sequence. Wald showed that restricting to any countable family of rules restores existence: almost every sequence is then a collective. Church (1940) made the natural move and restricted the rules to the computable ones. But Ville (1939) had already shown this whole style of definition is too weak: he built a sequence that passes countably many selection rules yet whose running frequency of 1s approaches the limit only from below, never oscillating around it — it violates the law of the iterated logarithm, a genuine law of randomness. So betting rules miss real structure. And I cannot patch the hole by demanding that the sequence pass every probability-one law, because "x is not equal to this particular y" is a probability-one law for every y, and that leaves no random sequences at all. The test-based approach forces me to decide in advance which laws count, and any enumerated list can be outflanked.

So I change the direction of attack. Instead of asking which tests the object passes, I ask how much it costs to specify it completely. A string with structure can be reconstructed from less than itself; a string with no structure must essentially be quoted in full. Randomness, on this view, is not passing tests but resisting compression.

The first formulation that comes to mind is naive, and it collapses immediately. Say the complexity of a number is the length of its shortest description. Then consider "the smallest positive integer not definable in fewer than twenty English words." If any integer is not so definable, that phrase defines one in nineteen words — a contradiction. This is the Berry paradox, and it tells me the word "description" is the poison: natural-language descriptions are not well-defined objects, so quantifying over them is incoherent. But the fix is sitting inside the paradox. If I restrict descriptions to programs on a fixed computing machine, a description becomes a finite binary input with a definite output, and description length becomes a precise function. The paradox does not disappear; it converts into a theorem. I will come back to that.

So I define, provisionally: fix a machine φ, and let the complexity of x relative to φ be the length of the shortest program p with φ(p) = x. Immediately there is a new obstruction. Which machine? If the answer depends on φ in an uncontrolled way, I have traded dependence on a probability model for dependence on a hardware manual — no better than where I started. The rescue is the existence of universal machines. A universal U can simulate any other machine φ if I prefix the program with an interpreter for φ, and that interpreter is one fixed string whose length depends only on φ, not on x. So for every x, the complexity under U is at most the complexity under φ plus a constant c_φ, and by symmetry any two universal machines disagree by at most an additive constant on every string. The additive constant is the price of universality, and it is cheap: for long strings it is noise. The definition is machine-relative in its small values but absolute in its behavior, which is all I need. (Solomonoff, in 1960 and 1964, had already hit this same invariance fact from the direction of inductive inference, where the shortest program plays the role of the best hypothesis.)

Now the crucial check: does the definition deliver random objects at all, or have I defined a property nothing has? A one-line counting argument settles it. There are 2^n strings of length n, but only 1 + 2 + ... + 2^(n-1) = 2^n − 1 programs shorter than n. Programs are in fixed supply, and each program produces at most one output, so at least one string of every length is incompressible — no shorter program prints it. The same count gives more: fewer than 2^(n-c+1) programs have length at most n − c, so all but a fraction of about 2^(−c+1) of the length-n strings compress by fewer than c bits. Incompressible strings are not exotic; they are the overwhelming majority. That is exactly what the intuition of randomness demands — most strings should be random — and here it falls out of arithmetic.

This also retroactively explains what went wrong for von Mises. His gambler had to commit to one style of exploitation at a time: selection rules. But a pattern is just any effective regularity, and every effective regularity is a program. If x fails any effective statistical law — if its ones cluster, if its frequency approaches from below as in Ville's construction, if it has any computable peculiarity at all — then that peculiarity is a short description of x within the class of strings sharing it, and x is compressible. Contrapositively, an incompressible string passes every effective law of randomness at once, not one rule at a time. I no longer have to enumerate the laws in advance; the shortest program absorbs all of them simultaneously. The quantification over "all effective descriptions" is doing precisely the work that the quantification over "all admissible tests" could not.

Let me record the examples that fix the intuition. A string of a million alternating bits is huge as data and tiny as a program: a loop and a count. The first million digits of π look statistically unremarkable — they pass the usual frequency tests — but they are far from random in my sense, because a compact digit algorithm plus the bound one million regenerates them exactly; their raw length wildly exaggerates their information content. And a genuinely incompressible million-bit string has no such recipe; its shortest program is essentially "print" followed by itself. Pattern is compression; randomness is the absence of any shorter generator. Finding a law in data and shortening the program that reproduces the data are the same act.

The caveats are real and I should be honest about what they cost. First, the additive constant means K(x) is meaningless as a small number; the theory lives in asymptotics and comparisons, not in certifying that some particular 30-bit string is random. Second — and here the Berry paradox returns as promised — K is not computable. Suppose I had a program that, given any string, outputs its complexity. Then I could write a short program that enumerates strings until it finds one certified to have complexity greater than, say, ten billion bits, and prints it. But that whole procedure — the enumerator plus the complexity subroutine — is itself a program far shorter than ten billion bits that produces the string, contradicting the certificate. The assumption is false: no such complexity subroutine exists. This is exactly the Berry argument, made legitimate by the restriction to programs. What was a paradox for descriptions in English is a rigorous proof about descriptions in code, and it is also why K sits at the level of the halting problem. So practical compressors only ever give upper bounds: gzip can expose structure and thereby prove a string is not random, but no run of any fixed compressor can certify randomness, because the ideal notion quantifies over all effective descriptions, not over the tools currently on my desk.

None of this dims the shift. The probabilistic viewpoint starts with a distribution and asks how surprising the object is under it. I have moved the measurement inside the object: K(x) is the length of the shortest complete causal recipe for x itself, machine-fixed up to a constant. Randomness of an individual string stops being a statement about our ignorance of the source and becomes irreducibility — the string is random when the shortest way back to it is to write it down. That is the definition I was after: probability-free, intrinsic to the object, and stable exactly where it needs to be.
