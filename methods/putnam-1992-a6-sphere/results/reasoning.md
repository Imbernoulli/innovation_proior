Four points scattered uniformly and independently on a sphere, and I want the chance that the tetrahedron they span swallows the center. Let me get the setup as clean as I can before I reach for anything. Put the center at the origin $O$; the four points $P_1,P_2,P_3,P_4$ are independent and uniform on the unit sphere. "The center lies inside the tetrahedron" means $O$ is an interior point of the convex hull of the four points. So I'm really asking: what's the probability that the origin sits strictly inside the convex hull of four independent uniform points on the sphere?

My first instinct is to fix three of the points and ask where the fourth must land. Fix $P_1,P_2,P_3$; then the set of positions for $P_4$ that make the tetrahedron contain $O$ is some region of the sphere, and I'd integrate its area, normalized by $4\pi$, over all placements of the first three. But the region's shape depends intricately on $P_1,P_2,P_3$, and then I have to average that area over the joint distribution of three points on a sphere — a surface integral over a configuration space that I can already feel turning into a mess. There has to be more structure here than brute integration is exposing. Let me not push the integral yet.

The honest move when a problem in dimension three is opaque is to shrink it until I can see the whole thing, and run the same question one dimension down. So: three points uniform on a circle, what's the probability the triangle they form contains the center? Same shape of question — points on a sphere $S^{d-1}$, do they enclose the origin — just with $d=2$ instead of $d=3$. If I can really *understand* the circle case, not just get its number, maybe the understanding ports upward.

Circle, center $O$ at the origin, three uniform points $P_1,P_2,P_3$. Fix $P_1$ and $P_2$ for a moment and let only $P_3$ wander. When does triangle $P_1P_2P_3$ contain $O$? Draw the diameter through $P_1$ — the line through $O$ and $P_1$ — and the diameter through $P_2$. These two lines through the center cut the circle into four arcs. Now where can $P_3$ go to capture the center? The center $O$ is inside the triangle exactly when $O$ is on the interior side of all three edges. Stare at the two diameters: they pin down the directions of $P_1$ and $P_2$ as seen from $O$. The triangle contains $O$ precisely when $P_3$ lies on the arc *opposite* to both $P_1$ and $P_2$ — the arc cut off by the two diameters that faces away from $P_1$ and $P_2$. Geometrically that's the arc bounded by the antipode of $P_1$ and the antipode of $P_2$. If $P_3$ falls anywhere else, all three points sit in a halfplane through $O$ and the triangle slumps off to one side, missing the center.

Let me make that quantitative. Say the smaller central angle from $P_1$ to $P_2$ is $\alpha\in[0,\pi]$. The opposite arc, bounded by the two antipodes $-P_1$ and $-P_2$, has the same angular length $\alpha$ by central symmetry. So conditional on $P_1,P_2$, the probability that $P_3$ lands in the good arc is $\alpha/(2\pi)$. Now I average over the placement of $P_1,P_2$. Fix $P_1$ anywhere by rotational symmetry; then $P_2$ is uniform, and this smaller central separation $\alpha$ is uniform on $[0,\pi]$, so the proportion $\alpha/(2\pi)$ is uniform on $[0,\tfrac12]$. Its average is $\tfrac14$. So the probability in the circle case is $\tfrac14$.

That number stops me. $\tfrac14$. Not $\tfrac1\pi$, not some arctangent — a clean $\tfrac14 = \left(\tfrac12\right)^2$. When an integral over angles collapses to a bare power of two, the integral was secretly counting something, and I was doing analysis where I should have been doing combinatorics. Where would a $2^2$ come from? I drew two diameters. Each diameter has two endpoints. Two diameters, two choices each, $2\times 2 = 4$. That has to be the four. I want the whole computation to fall out as "one good case out of four equally likely ones," with no integral at all.

Choosing a uniform random point on the circle is the same as choosing a uniform random *diameter* — a line through the center — and then flipping a fair coin to pick which of its two endpoints I keep. The two endpoints are antipodal, and the uniform distribution is symmetric under the antipodal map, so the coin is genuinely fair and independent of which line I drew. This looks like a pointless reparametrization of "pick a random point," but it splits each point into a continuous part (the line) and a binary part (the coin), and the binary parts are exactly where the powers of two were hiding.

So redo the experiment in that language. To get $P_1$ and $P_2$: pick two random diameters $\ell_1,\ell_2$, and pick the third point $P_3$ as just an ordinary random point — and crucially, imagine $P_3$ is chosen *first*, before I flip any coins. Now the two coins decide which endpoint of $\ell_1$ is $P_1$ and which endpoint of $\ell_2$ is $P_2$. The two lines and the point $P_3$ are now frozen; the only remaining randomness is two fair coin flips, giving four equally likely outcomes for the pair $(P_1,P_2)$. And I already worked out the containment criterion: the triangle contains $O$ iff $P_3$ sits on the arc opposite both chosen endpoints. With $\ell_1,\ell_2,P_3$ fixed, $P_3$ lies in exactly one of the four arcs carved out by the two lines. For the triangle to swallow $O$, I need $P_1$ to be the endpoint of $\ell_1$ *away* from $P_3$'s side and $P_2$ likewise the endpoint of $\ell_2$ away from $P_3$ — so that $P_1,P_2$ flank $O$ opposite $P_3$. That pins each coin to one specific outcome. Exactly one of the four coin combinations works. And this is true *no matter* what $\ell_1,\ell_2,P_3$ turned out to be — the count is always one-out-of-four. So I don't average anything: the conditional probability is $\tfrac14$ for every configuration, hence the answer is $\tfrac14$. The integral was a disguised way of counting four equally likely sign choices and finding one good one.

Now I see why this matters: the argument never used that the circle is a circle, only that points come in antipodal pairs and the uniform law is antipodal-symmetric. That ports straight up to the sphere. Let me lift it verbatim and see the three-dimensional answer drop out.

Sphere, center $O$ at the origin, four uniform points. Reparametrize three of the points the same way: pick three random diameters $\ell_1,\ell_2,\ell_3$ — lines through $O$ — and pick the fourth point $P_4$ as an ordinary random point, chosen first. Then flip three fair coins to choose which endpoint of each $\ell_i$ becomes $P_i$, for $i=1,2,3$. Three coins, $2^3=8$ equally likely outcomes, eight candidate tetrahedra over the same three diameters and the same $P_4$. By the antipodal symmetry of the uniform law, these eight are equally likely. If for every fixed choice of the three diameters and the point $P_4$, *exactly one* of the eight coin outcomes produces a tetrahedron containing $O$, then the conditional probability is $\tfrac18$ for every configuration, and the answer is $\tfrac18$ with no integration at all.

So the whole problem has collapsed onto one combinatorial claim: among the eight sign choices, exactly one encloses the center. In the circle case I can see "exactly one of four" by eye, watching which arc $P_3$ falls into. In three dimensions I don't trust my eye on spherical triangles. I need a criterion for $O$ being inside a tetrahedron that responds cleanly when I flip a vertex to its antipode.

Take four points $v_1,v_2,v_3,v_4$ in space (these will be $P_4$ and the three chosen endpoints). The origin is inside their convex hull iff $O$ can be written as a convex combination with strictly positive weights: $O=\sum_i \lambda_i v_i$ with all $\lambda_i>0$ and $\sum_i\lambda_i=1$. That's the definition of the interior of the hull — points reachable with strictly positive barycentric weights. Generic four points on a sphere are affinely independent (the cases where they aren't, or where $O$ lands exactly on a face, are a measure-zero event I'll set aside, since they contribute probability zero), so the weights, if they exist, are unique.

I want to say "flip $v_i\mapsto -v_i$ and the weight $\lambda_i$ just flips sign." If I flip $v_4$ to $-v_4$, then to reproduce $O=\lambda_1 v_1+\lambda_2 v_2+\lambda_3 v_3+\lambda_4 v_4$ I'd write $\lambda_1 v_1+\lambda_2 v_2+\lambda_3 v_3+(-\lambda_4)(-v_4)$, so the weight on $-v_4$ is $-\lambda_4$ — the sign does flip. But the constraint $\sum_i\lambda_i=1$ does *not* survive that flip: $\lambda_1+\lambda_2+\lambda_3-\lambda_4$ is generally not $1$. So I can't just carry the normalized barycentric weights through the antipodal map; the normalization breaks. I need a formulation of "origin inside the hull" that is blind to that normalization, so that a sign flip acts cleanly.

Drop the normalization and work with the raw linear dependence instead. Four vectors in three-dimensional space are linearly dependent: there's a nonzero $(c_1,c_2,c_3,c_4)$ with $c_1v_1+c_2v_2+c_3v_3+c_4v_4=0$, and generically (the tetrahedron is nondegenerate and any three of the vectors are independent) this dependence is unique up to an overall scale. This coefficient vector is the normalization-free object I want. The question is how its signs relate to $O$ being inside the hull.

So when is the origin inside the convex hull of $v_1,\dots,v_4$, phrased through this dependence? Go back to the barycentric definition: $O$ is strictly interior iff $O=\sum_i\lambda_i v_i$ with all $\lambda_i>0$ and $\sum_i\lambda_i=1$. The vector equation $\sum_i\lambda_i v_i=0$ says $(\lambda_1,\dots,\lambda_4)$ is a linear dependence among the $v_i$; but the dependence space of four generic vectors in three-dimensional space is exactly one-dimensional, spanned by my $(c_1,\dots,c_4)$. So *every* solution of $\sum_i\lambda_i v_i=0$ is a scalar multiple $\lambda_i=t\,c_i$. The extra normalization $\sum_i\lambda_i=1$ then forces $t\sum_j c_j=1$; this has a solution iff $\sum_j c_j\ne0$, namely $t=1/\sum_j c_j$, giving the unique candidate weights
$$\lambda_i=\frac{c_i}{\sum_j c_j}.$$
These are the only possible barycentric weights of $O$, and $O$ is strictly interior iff they are all positive. But $\lambda_i=c_i/\sum_j c_j$ is positive for every $i$ precisely when all the $c_i$ share one sign (then $\sum_j c_j$ has that same sign and each ratio is positive); if the $c_i$ have mixed signs, either $\sum_j c_j=0$ and there are no normalized barycentric weights for $O$, or $\sum_j c_j\ne0$ and the ratios inherit mixed signs. In neither case is $O$ interior. So the origin is strictly inside the hull of $v_1,\dots,v_4$ iff the coefficients of their unique-up-to-scale linear dependence $\sum_i c_i v_i=0$ are all nonzero and all of the same sign. This is the normalization-blind criterion I wanted: it reads off $O$'s position purely from the signs of the dependence coefficients, and the awkward $\sum\lambda_i=1$ has been absorbed into the single rescaling by $\sum_j c_j$.

That is precisely the object that flips cleanly. Fix the three diameters; the four points of a candidate tetrahedron are $v_1=P_4$ (held fixed — I never flip the free point), $v_2=\varepsilon_2 q$, $v_3=\varepsilon_3 r$, and $v_4=\varepsilon_4 s$, where $q,r,s$ are one chosen endpoint of each diameter and $\varepsilon_2,\varepsilon_3,\varepsilon_4\in\{+1,-1\}$ are the three coins selecting which endpoint. Suppose for the base choice $(q,r,s)$ the dependence is
$$c_1 P_4 + c_2 q + c_3 r + c_4 s = 0,$$
with all $c_i\ne 0$ generically. What's the dependence for the flipped tetrahedron with vertices $P_4,\varepsilon_2 q,\varepsilon_3 r,\varepsilon_4 s$? I want coefficients $d_i$ with $d_1 P_4+d_2(\varepsilon_2 q)+d_3(\varepsilon_3 r)+d_4(\varepsilon_4 s)=0$. Matching against the base dependence, $d_1=c_1$, and $d_i\varepsilon_i=c_i$, i.e. $d_i=\varepsilon_i c_i$ for $i=2,3,4$ (using $\varepsilon_i^2=1$). So flipping endpoint $i$ multiplies that one dependence coefficient by $\varepsilon_i$ and leaves the others alone — exactly the clean sign action I couldn't get from the normalized weights. The dependence vector for the coin pattern $(\varepsilon_2,\varepsilon_3,\varepsilon_4)$ is
$$(c_1,\ \varepsilon_2 c_2,\ \varepsilon_3 c_3,\ \varepsilon_4 c_4).$$

Now apply the criterion. The flipped tetrahedron contains $O$ iff these four numbers all share a sign. The first, $c_1$, is fixed — I never flip $P_4$ — so "all share a sign" means each of $\varepsilon_2 c_2,\varepsilon_3 c_3,\varepsilon_4 c_4$ must match the sign of $c_1$. For each $i\in\{2,3,4\}$ that's one linear condition on the coin: $\varepsilon_i c_i$ has the sign of $c_1$ iff $\varepsilon_i=\operatorname{sign}(c_1)/\operatorname{sign}(c_i)$, which forces $\varepsilon_i$ to one specific value. Three coins, each forced to exactly one value, so exactly one of the $2^3=8$ patterns makes all four coefficients share $c_1$'s sign — and that single pattern is the one and only tetrahedron, among the eight, that contains the center. Not zero, not two: precisely one, for every base configuration $(q,r,s,P_4)$, because the forcing of each coin is independent and unique.

And the eight tetrahedra are equally likely — each coin is a fair, independent flip from the antipodal symmetry of the uniform measure — so conditional on any choice of the three diameters and the free point $P_4$, the probability that the realized tetrahedron contains $O$ is exactly $\tfrac18$. This conditional probability does not depend on the diameters or on $P_4$ at all; it's the constant $\tfrac18$. Averaging a constant over the diameters and $P_4$ leaves it unchanged. So the probability is $\boxed{\tfrac18}$.

Let me look back down the dimensions to make sure the machine is consistent and I haven't fooled myself. In the circle case the same argument runs with two diameters: a free point $P_3$ and two coins, dependence coefficients $(c_1,\varepsilon_2 c_2,\varepsilon_3 c_3)$ for three vectors $v_1,v_2,v_3$ in the plane (three planar vectors always have a unique-up-to-scale dependence), origin inside the triangle iff all three coefficients share a sign, exactly one of $2^2=4$ coin patterns achieves it, probability $\tfrac14$ — matching the integral I did by hand. The pattern is transparent now: $d$ diameters and one free point, $2^d$ equally likely sign patterns, exactly one encloses the origin, probability $2^{-d}$; the plane gives $2^{-2}=\tfrac14$ and the sphere gives $2^{-3}=\tfrac18$. The clean power of two was never a coincidence of the integral — it's the count of antipodal coin flips, all but one of which throw the center out.
