The starting problem is a mismatch between the unconstrained optimality rule and what actually happens at a constrained boundary. If there are no constraints, a local minimizer has no first-order downhill direction, so the gradient must be zero. With constraints, that condition is too strong. A point can be optimal even while the objective still has a nonzero slope, because the directions that would improve the objective may leave the feasible set.

The older boundary-search instinct is to guess where the optimum sits: maybe on one constraint, maybe on the intersection of several constraints, maybe in the interior. That works for small examples but it does not scale as a method. It treats the active boundary as something discovered by enumeration, and it separates the geometry of feasibility from the calculus of the objective.

Let me make that complaint concrete on the smallest example I can think of, because I want to see exactly where the unconstrained rule breaks and what would have to replace it. Take $\min x^2+y^2$ subject to $x+y\ge 1$. The unconstrained minimizer is obviously $(0,0)$, but there $x+y=0<1$, so the origin is infeasible. The feasible optimum sits somewhere on the line $x+y=1$, and at that point $\nabla f=(2x,2y)$ is not zero. So $\nabla f=0$ genuinely fails, and I need a condition that tolerates a nonzero objective gradient as long as it points "into" the constraint.

The natural rewrite is to allow the constraint's gradient to absorb the leftover slope. Write the inequality as $g(x,y)=1-x-y\le 0$, so $\nabla g=(-1,-1)$. If at the optimum the objective gradient is exactly canceled by some multiple of $\nabla g$, I'd require $\nabla f+\lambda\nabla g=0$ for some scalar $\lambda$. That gives $2x-\lambda=0$ and $2y-\lambda=0$, hence $x=y=\lambda/2$. I now have to decide whether the constraint is actually active, and this is the real fork — the place where the old enumeration instinct hides.

Suppose I guess the constraint is inactive, i.e. it does not bind, so its multiplier is $\lambda=0$. Then stationarity collapses to $2x=2y=0$, giving $x=y=0$ again. But check feasibility: $g(0,0)=1-0-0=1>0$. The point is infeasible. So the inactive guess is not ruled out by intuition — it's ruled out by an actual sign check on $g$. That tells me the constraint must bind, $g(x^\star)=0$, i.e. $x+y=1$. Combined with $x=y=\lambda/2$, this gives $\lambda/2+\lambda/2=1$, so $\lambda=1$ and $x^\star=y^\star=1/2$. The objective there is $1/4+1/4=1/2$.

Let me verify this candidate really satisfies everything I'd want. Stationarity: $2x-\lambda=1-1=0$ in each coordinate. Primal feasibility: $g=1-\tfrac12-\tfrac12=0\le 0$. The multiplier came out $\lambda=1$, which is nonnegative — and that sign is not a coincidence I imposed, it fell out of the algebra. And the product $\lambda\, g(x^\star)=1\cdot 0=0$. So a candidate that genuinely beats the unconstrained rule satisfies four separate conditions at once: gradient cancellation, feasibility, $\lambda\ge 0$, and the product $\lambda g=0$. The first-order rule for the constrained problem is therefore not one equation but this bundle.

Each piece earns its place, and I can see why by asking what fails if I drop it. Drop stationarity and any feasible point passes. Drop feasibility and $(0,0)$ with $\lambda=0$ would pass even though it's outside the set. Drop the sign condition $\lambda\ge0$ and I'd accept a multiplier pointing the wrong way: a negative $\lambda$ would mean the objective gradient aligns *with* descent into infeasibility, i.e. the "constraint force" pushes the point out of the set rather than holding it in — that would certify a point where moving infeasible-ward lowers $f$, exactly backwards. Drop the product condition $\lambda g=0$ and I could leave a positive multiplier on a slack constraint that isn't even touched. So the inequality case forces $\lambda\ge 0$ together with $\lambda g=0$, while an equality constraint, which binds by definition, needs no sign restriction on its multiplier and no slackness product. Writing it out for the general problem:

$$
\nabla f(x^\star)+\sum_i\nu_i\nabla h_i(x^\star)+\sum_j\lambda_j\nabla g_j(x^\star)=0,
$$

with $h_i(x^\star)=0$, $g_j(x^\star)\le 0$, $\lambda_j\ge0$, and $\lambda_j g_j(x^\star)=0$. The stationarity equation says the objective gradient is not required to disappear by itself; it must be balanced by the normals of the constraints that can actually push back.

There's a mechanical reading of the same algebra. The objective applies a local force that wants to move the point downhill. Equality constraints are rails: they can push in both normal directions, so their multipliers have either sign. Inequality constraints are one-sided walls: they can push only when touched, and only in the direction that keeps the point feasible, so their multipliers are nonnegative. If a wall is not touched, it cannot push, and $\lambda_j g_j(x^\star)=0$ records exactly that. In the worked example $\nabla f=(1,1)$ at the optimum and $\lambda\nabla g=(−1,−1)$ — equal and opposite, so the picture is literally a balanced pair of forces, not just a metaphor.

That mechanical reading also predicts an economic one, and this is the part I most want to check rather than assert: the multiplier should measure how much the optimum improves if I relax the constraint. The optimal value as a function of the right-hand side $b$ in $x+y\ge b$ is, by the same solution, $V(b)=b^2/2$. So $V'(b)=b$, and at $b=1$ that derivative is $1$ — exactly the multiplier I found. To be sure I'm not fooling myself with the closed form, perturb numerically: $\big(V(1.01)-V(1)\big)/0.01=1.005$ and $\big(V(0.99)-V(1)\big)/(-0.01)=0.995$, bracketing $1$, and at step $10^{-4}$ the slope is $1.00005$. The multiplier really is the marginal value of the constraint. When a constraint is slack, the same logic forces its price to zero, as I saw with the $\lambda=0$ branch: relaxing an untouched wall buys nothing.

A caveat survives all of this. Everything above is first-order and local. In the example the problem is convex, so the single KKT point is the global minimum; here the certificate is sufficient as well as necessary, which is why I trusted it without a second-order check. For a nonconvex problem the same four conditions can be met at a local minimum, a saddle-type boundary point, or another stationary point, and distinguishing them needs second-order information. There's also a regularity caveat I should flag honestly rather than wave away: the necessity direction relies on the active constraint gradients being well-behaved (a constraint qualification). In my example $\nabla g=(-1,-1)$ is a single nonzero vector, so regularity is trivially fine; but if a feasible set had a degenerate cusp with linearly dependent active gradients, the multiplier $\lambda$ might fail to exist even at a true optimum, and I'd want to verify the qualification before trusting the balance description.

What the worked example settles is the shape of the rule. Constrained optimality is not "find where the boundary happens to stop descent" by case guessing — the inactive-versus-active choice that the old method makes by enumeration is instead decided inside the equations by a feasibility-and-sign check, the way $\lambda=0$ got rejected and $\lambda=1$ accepted. It is "find a feasible point where objective descent is locally neutralized by admissible constraint reactions, with inactive constraints carrying zero load," and that balance is algebraic, checkable on a concrete problem, and reusable.
