Let me start from the thing that keeps nagging me, which is that the same sentence keeps coming out of completely different models: "good events help each other." In an Ising ferromagnet, if I know one spin is up, its neighbours are more likely up — Griffiths proved a version of this, ⟨fg⟩ − ⟨f⟩⟨g⟩ ≥ 0 for a suitable class of spin observables. And in percolation, Harris showed that if I know one region is well connected, another region is more likely connected too — increasing events satisfy P(A∩B) ≥ P(A)P(B). These two results are proved by completely different machinery and they're filed under "spin systems" and "percolation" as if they had nothing to do with each other. But they're saying the same thing. Two quantities that both go *up* with the configuration are positively correlated. I want to know why. Not why-in-the-Ising-model and why-in-percolation separately — I want the one structural reason, the minimal hypotheses that force this, with the physics stripped out.

So let me ask what the two results actually depend on. Griffiths needs the couplings J(R) ≥ 0 — ferromagnetic — and it restricts the magnetic field, and the observables have to live in a special cone generated by the spin products σ_R. Harris needs the percolation edges to be *independent*. Those hypotheses look unrelated. But the conclusion is identical in form: a covariance of two increasing things is ≥ 0. The hypotheses must be two different sufficient conditions for one underlying inequality. If I can find that underlying inequality, both should fall out as special cases.

What's common to the *setting*? In both cases a configuration is a subset of some finite ground set — occupied sites, or open edges — i.e. a point of {0,1}^X. And the things I'm correlating are *increasing* in that configuration: adding occupied sites, or opening edges, only helps the event. So "increasing" needs an order, and the order is inclusion of subsets, or coordinatewise ≤. That order isn't total — {a} and {b} are incomparable — it's partial. So whatever I prove has to live on a *partially ordered set*, and the central word is "increasing function on a poset."

Let me first get the totally ordered case completely under control, because if I can't even see why it's true there, I have no hope partially ordered. Take Γ totally ordered, μ a positive measure, ⟨f⟩ = Z⁻¹ Σ_x μ(x) f(x), Z = Σ_x μ(x). I want ⟨fg⟩ − ⟨f⟩⟨g⟩ ≥ 0 for f, g increasing. The clean way to see a covariance is to double it and antisymmetrize. Write

  ⟨fg⟩ − ⟨f⟩⟨g⟩ = Z⁻¹ Σ_x μ(x) f(x) g(x) − Z⁻² (Σ_x μ(x) f(x))(Σ_y μ(y) g(y)).

Multiply through by Z² and symmetrize the x,y double sum:

  Z²(⟨fg⟩ − ⟨f⟩⟨g⟩) = Σ_x μ(x) f(x) g(x) · Σ_y μ(y) − Σ_x μ(x) f(x) · Σ_y μ(y) g(y)
                     = Σ_{x,y} μ(x) μ(y) (f(x) g(x) − f(x) g(y)).

Now swap x ↔ y in a copy of this and average the two:

  Z²(⟨fg⟩ − ⟨f⟩⟨g⟩) = ½ Σ_{x,y} μ(x) μ(y) (f(x) − f(y))(g(x) − g(y)).

So the covariance is (2Z²)⁻¹ Σ_{x,y} μ(x) μ(y) (f(x) − f(y))(g(x) − g(y)). Each weight μ(x)μ(y) ≥ 0. And on a *totally* ordered set, x and y are comparable, say x ≥ y; then f increasing gives f(x) ≥ f(y) and g increasing gives g(x) ≥ g(y), so the product (f(x) − f(y))(g(x) − g(y)) ≥ 0. Every summand is ≥ 0. Done. That's just the discrete Chebyshev sum inequality, the rearrangement fact that two sequences sorted the same way have a non-negative covariance. And notice: I used *nothing* about μ except positivity. On a chain, every positive measure works.

This is the whole engine. The product (f(x) − f(y))(g(x) − g(y)) ≥ 0 whenever x, y are comparable. The trouble in the partial case is exactly the incomparable pairs: if x = {a}, y = {b}, then f could go up while g goes down across this pair, and the summand can be negative. So the partial-order obstruction is concentrated entirely on incomparable pairs, and that's where the measure has to earn its keep.

So let me think about what structure I even need on the poset to make progress. I'm correlating two functions and I want to combine information about x and y. On a partial order the natural way to "combine" two configurations is to take their join x∨y (the smallest thing above both — union, coordinatewise max) and their meet x∧y (the largest thing below both — intersection, min). For those to exist for *every* pair I need a **lattice**. And the real examples — subsets under inclusion, products of chains — aren't just any lattice, they're **distributive**: x∧(y∨z) = (x∧y)∨(x∧z). Is that a real restriction or am I just describing my examples? Birkhoff's representation theorem settles it: every finite distributive lattice of length n is isomorphic to a sublattice of the subsets of an n-element set. So a finite distributive lattice is exactly a coordinatewise order structure that is closed under meet and join. That's the right arena. It's not an arbitrary abstraction; it's precisely the shape of a configuration space.

Now the measure. On a chain any positive μ worked. On a lattice it can't, because a perverse μ could conspire with the incomparable pairs and anti-correlate increasing functions — let me hold that thought and force the condition to reveal itself rather than guessing it. What's the boundary case I *know* must work? Independence. If the coordinates are independent — μ(x) = ∏_i μ_i(x_i), a product measure — then Harris already tells me increasing functions correlate. So whatever condition I write down, the product measure must satisfy it. Let me compute what a product measure does to the meet and join. Coordinatewise, (x∧y)_i = min(x_i, y_i) and (x∨y)_i = max(x_i, y_i), and for each i the pair {min, max} is just a reordering of {x_i, y_i}. So

  μ(x∧y) μ(x∨y) = ∏_i μ_i(min(x_i,y_i)) · ∏_i μ_i(max(x_i,y_i)) = ∏_i μ_i(x_i) · ∏_i μ_i(y_i) = μ(x) μ(y).

The product measure satisfies μ(x∧y) μ(x∨y) = μ(x) μ(y), with **equality**, for every pair. So independence sits exactly on the knife's edge. And the inequality I want — positive correlation — should be on the side where the measure does *better* than independence at putting large-x and large-y together. That picks the direction:

  (A)   μ(x∧y) μ(x∨y) ≥ μ(x) μ(y)   for all x, y.

Let me sanity-check the meaning by taking logs. Write λ = log μ. Then (A) is λ(x∧y) + λ(x∨y) ≥ λ(x) + λ(y) — supermodularity of λ on the lattice. On subsets this says: take R, and add a coordinate r ∉ R; the increment λ(R + r) − λ(R) is an *increasing* function of R. The more you already have, the more a new coordinate adds. That is precisely "the measure itself makes coordinates want to co-occur." It's the log-density analogue of a convex / increasing-differences condition. And the borderline λ additive (modular) — increments independent of R — is exactly the independent measure. So (A) reads: the measure does not anti-associate the coordinates. That's the one condition. Let me write down the claim and then earn it: on a finite distributive lattice, if μ satisfies (A), then for f, g both increasing, ⟨fg⟩ − ⟨f⟩⟨g⟩ ≥ 0.

First a cleanup so I don't trip over zeros. The average ⟨f⟩ only sees the support Γ₀ = {x : μ(x) > 0}. If x, y ∈ Γ₀ then by (A), μ(x∧y) μ(x∨y) ≥ μ(x) μ(y) > 0, so both μ(x∧y) > 0 and μ(x∨y) > 0 — meaning Γ₀ is closed under ∧ and ∨, i.e. Γ₀ is itself a sublattice, finite and distributive, and (A) holds on it. An increasing f on Γ restricts to an increasing f on Γ₀. So I lose nothing by assuming μ > 0 strictly on a finite distributive lattice Γ. Good, that's the working setting.

Now, how do I actually prove it? The chain case was a one-line termwise argument, but partially ordered I can't sign the incomparable terms. I need an *induction*. Induction on what? The natural size of a distributive lattice is its **length** l(Γ) — the longest chain. If l(Γ) = 0, Γ is a single point, ⟨fg⟩ − ⟨f⟩⟨g⟩ = 0 trivially, equality. So I want to peel one unit of length off Γ and reduce to smaller lattices where I can assume the result. How do you remove a unit of length from a distributive lattice? Pick an **atom** a — a minimal nonzero element, one step above the bottom. The atom should let me cut Γ into a piece "below/avoiding a" and a piece "above a," each shorter.

Let me set up the pieces carefully, because this is where it lives or dies. Given an atom a, define
  Γ'_a = {x ∈ Γ : x ≥ a}     — everything above the atom,
  Γ''_a = {x ∈ Γ : x ≱ a}    — everything *not* above the atom,
  Γ_a  = {x = x' ∨ a : x' ∈ Γ''_a}   — push the "not-above-a" part up by joining a.
I need three facts about these. Let me prove them, because the whole induction rests on them.

Are they sublattices? For Γ'_a: if x ≥ a and y ≥ a then x∧y ≥ a (a is below both) and x∨y ≥ x ≥ a, so both stay in Γ'_a — sublattice. For Γ''_a: I claim x ≱ a is equivalent to x∧a = O (the bottom), because a is an atom, so the only things ≤ a are O and a itself; x∧a ∈ {O, a}, and x∧a = a means x ≥ a. So x ∈ Γ''_a iff x∧a = O. Now if x∧a = O and y∧a = O, then (x∧y)∧a = x∧(y∧a) = x∧O = O, so x∧y ∈ Γ''_a; and (x∨y)∧a = (x∧a)∨(y∧a) = O∨O = O — here I used *distributivity*, this is exactly where it's needed — so x∨y ∈ Γ''_a. Sublattice. For Γ_a: take x = x'∨a, y = y'∨a with x', y' ∈ Γ''_a. Then x∧y = (x'∨a)∧(y'∨a) = (x'∧y')∨a (distributivity again) and x'∧y' ∈ Γ''_a, so x∧y ∈ Γ_a; and x∨y = (x'∨y')∨a with x'∨y' ∈ Γ''_a, so x∨y ∈ Γ_a. Sublattice. All three are finite distributive lattices.

Second, Γ''_a and Γ_a are isomorphic via x' ↦ x'∨a. It's onto Γ_a by definition. Is it one-to-one? Suppose x'∨a = x''∨a with x', x'' ∈ Γ''_a. Then x' = x' ∧ (x'∨a) = x' ∧ (x''∨a) = (x'∧x'')∨(x'∧a) = (x'∧x'')∨O = x'∧x'' (using x'∧a = O since x' ∈ Γ''_a, and distributivity), and symmetrically x'' = x'∧x'', so x' = x''. Injective. And it respects ∧ and ∨ by the computations I just did. So it's a lattice isomorphism. This matters for the induction length. Γ'_a is the interval [a, I], so a maximal chain in Γ'_a starts at a; adding O below a makes a chain in Γ one step longer, hence Γ'_a has length at most n − 1. Since Γ_a sits inside Γ'_a, it also has length at most n − 1, and the isomorphism gives the same bound for Γ''_a.

Third, Γ_a is a *semi-ideal* of Γ'_a — meaning if x ∈ Γ_a and y ∈ Γ'_a with y ≤ x then y ∈ Γ_a (it's downward closed inside Γ'_a). Take x = x'∨a ∈ Γ_a and y ∈ Γ'_a with y ≤ x. Then (y∧x')∨a = (y∨a)∧(x'∨a) = y∧x = y (since y ≤ x and y ≥ a means y∨a = y), so y = y'∨a with y' = y∧x' ≤ x', and y' ∈ Γ''_a because y' ≤ x' and Γ''_a is downward closed. So y ∈ Γ_a. Semi-ideal. I'll need this in a second — semi-ideals are exactly the supports of *decreasing* indicator functions, and that lets me feed a monotonicity step back into the inductive hypothesis.

Now the actual induction. Suppose the proposition holds for every lattice of length ≤ n − 1, and let Γ have length n ≥ 1, μ > 0, f, g increasing. Look at

  Ω = Z²(⟨fg⟩ − ⟨f⟩⟨g⟩) = Σ_{x,y ∈ Γ} μ(x) μ(y) (f(x) g(x) − f(x) g(y)).

Split each of x, y by whether it's in Γ'_a or Γ''_a — these two sets partition Γ (every x is either ≥ a or not). Denote Σ' for a sum over Γ'_a and Σ'' for a sum over Γ''_a. The double sum breaks into four blocks: (x,y) both in Γ'_a, both in Γ''_a, and the two cross blocks. The two same-block pieces,

  Σ'_x Σ'_y μ(x)μ(y)(f(x)g(x) − f(x)g(y))  and  Σ''_x Σ''_y μ(x)μ(y)(f(x)g(x) − f(x)g(y)),

are *exactly* the quantity Ω computed on the sublattices Γ'_a and Γ''_a respectively — μ satisfies (A) there, f, g are increasing there, and both have length ≤ n − 1, so by the inductive hypothesis both are ≥ 0. So I can drop them; they only help. What's left are the two cross blocks. Combine them and symmetrize x ↔ y across the blocks; the cross contribution becomes

  Σ'_x Σ''_y μ(x)μ(y)(f(x)g(x) − f(x)g(y) + f(y)g(y) − f(y)g(x))
   = Σ'_x Σ''_y μ(x)μ(y)(f(x) − f(y))(g(x) − g(y)).

Now I can't sign this termwise — x ∈ Γ'_a, y ∈ Γ''_a need not be comparable. Let C denote this cross contribution. Abbreviating Σ'μ = Σ'_x μ(x), Σ'μf = Σ'_x μ(x)f(x), and likewise Σ''μ, Σ''μf, I first get tempted to write

  Σ'μf · Σ''μg + Σ''μf · Σ'μg − Σ'μ · Σ''(μfg) − Σ''μ · Σ'(μfg)? 

No, the signs on the μfg terms go the other way. Expanding C cleanly gives
  Σ'μfg · Σ''μ − Σ'μf · Σ''μg − Σ'μg · Σ''μf + Σ'μ · Σ''μfg.
This still has the awkward μfg terms. To bound it I use the inductive hypothesis *again*, this time to replace Σ'μfg by something ≥ its product form on Γ'_a, and Σ''μfg likewise on Γ''_a: on Γ'_a, ⟨fg⟩ ≥ ⟨f⟩⟨g⟩ gives Σ'μfg · Σ'μ ≥ Σ'μf · Σ'μg, and on Γ''_a similarly. Substituting these lower bounds for the first and last terms and clearing denominators gives

  C ≥ (Σ'μ · Σ''μ)⁻¹ (Σ'μf · Σ''μ − Σ'μ · Σ''μf)(Σ'μg · Σ''μ − Σ'μ · Σ''μg).

The same-block pieces were already nonnegative, so the same lower bound applies to Ω. Look at what each factor is: after division by Σ'μ · Σ''μ, the first factor is exactly ⟨f; Γ'_a⟩ − ⟨f; Γ''_a⟩. So the factor is positive iff the average of f over the "above-a" part exceeds its average over the "not-above-a" part:

  ⟨f; μ, Γ''_a⟩ ≤ ⟨f; μ, Γ'_a⟩,   and the same for g.

If I can show that, both factors are ≥ 0, their product is ≥ 0, and Ω ≥ 0 — the proposition for length n. So the entire weight of the induction now rests on a single, intuitive claim: *an increasing function has a larger μ-average over the part of the lattice that lies above the atom a than over the part that doesn't.* Of course it should — being above a is "larger," and f is increasing. But "should" isn't a proof; the measure could in principle distort it. Let me prove it, and the natural bridge is the third set, Γ_a, sitting between the two:

  ⟨f; μ, Γ''_a⟩ ≤ ⟨f; μ, Γ_a⟩ ≤ ⟨f; μ, Γ'_a⟩.

Take the right inequality first, ⟨f; μ, Γ_a⟩ ≤ ⟨f; μ, Γ'_a⟩. Γ_a is a semi-ideal of Γ'_a, so its indicator χ (= 1 on Γ_a, 0 on Γ'_a \ Γ_a) is a *decreasing* function on Γ'_a — downward-closed support means χ drops as you go up. Now ⟨f; μ, Γ_a⟩ = ⟨fχ; μ, Γ'_a⟩ / ⟨χ; μ, Γ'_a⟩, because averaging fχ over Γ'_a and renormalizing by the mass of χ is exactly averaging f over the sub-region Γ_a. Apply the inductive hypothesis on Γ'_a (length ≤ n − 1) to the increasing f and the *decreasing* χ: f increasing, χ decreasing means −χ increasing, so ⟨f(−χ)⟩ ≥ ⟨f⟩⟨−χ⟩, i.e. ⟨fχ; μ, Γ'_a⟩ ≤ ⟨f; μ, Γ'_a⟩ ⟨χ; μ, Γ'_a⟩. Divide by ⟨χ; μ, Γ'_a⟩ > 0: ⟨f; μ, Γ_a⟩ ≤ ⟨f; μ, Γ'_a⟩. That's the right step, and it's lovely — the semi-ideal structure turned "average over a sub-region" into an instance of the very inequality I'm proving, one lattice shorter, with one decreasing function.

Now the left inequality, ⟨f; μ, Γ''_a⟩ ≤ ⟨f; μ, Γ_a⟩. Here I transport everything to Γ''_a through the isomorphism x' ↦ x'∨a. Define on Γ''_a the pushed measure μ̃(x') = μ(x'∨a) and the pushed function f_a(x') = f(x'∨a). The average of f over Γ_a (with measure μ) equals the average of f_a over Γ''_a with measure μ̃ — that's just relabeling by the isomorphism. So I need ⟨f; μ, Γ''_a⟩ ≤ ⟨f_a; μ̃, Γ''_a⟩. Two facts should make this go, and the second one is where (A) finally gets used.

First, f_a(x') = f(x'∨a) ≥ f(x') because x' ≤ x'∨a and f is increasing — so f ≤ f_a pointwise on Γ''_a, and f_a is itself increasing. That's the "values go up" half.

Second, what does (A) say about the density ratio μ̃/μ on Γ''_a? Take x, y ∈ Γ''_a with y ≤ x. Apply (A) to the pair x and y∨a: μ(x ∧ (y∨a)) μ(x ∨ (y∨a)) ≥ μ(x) μ(y∨a). Compute the meet: x ∧ (y∨a) = (x∧y) ∨ (x∧a) = (x∧y) ∨ O = x∧y = y (using distributivity, x∧a = O since x ∈ Γ''_a, and y ≤ x). And the join: x ∨ (y∨a) = x∨a. So μ(y) μ(x∨a) ≥ μ(x) μ(y∨a), i.e. μ(y) μ̃(x) ≥ μ(x) μ̃(y), i.e. μ̃(x)/μ(x) ≥ μ̃(y)/μ(y) whenever y ≤ x. So **μ̃/μ is increasing on Γ''_a.**

Now combine. The inductive hypothesis on Γ''_a (length ≤ n − 1), applied to the two *increasing* functions f and μ̃/μ, gives positive correlation: ⟨f · (μ̃/μ)⟩ ≥ ⟨f⟩⟨μ̃/μ⟩ under μ, which is exactly ⟨f; μ̃, Γ''_a⟩ ≥ ⟨f; μ, Γ''_a⟩ (re-weighting by an increasing density raises the average of an increasing function). Then f ≤ f_a lifts the μ̃-average the rest of the way:

  ⟨f; μ, Γ''_a⟩ ≤ ⟨f; μ̃, Γ''_a⟩ ≤ ⟨f_a; μ̃, Γ''_a⟩ = ⟨f; μ, Γ_a⟩.

Let me make sure I haven't flipped the density sign, because this is the one place the whole proof could silently invert. μ̃(x') = μ(x'∨a): the pushed measure looks at the configuration *with a added*. (A) made μ̃/μ increasing, meaning μ̃ up-weights the large x' relative to μ — which is the right direction, because Γ_a = (Γ''_a)∨a is genuinely Γ''_a *shifted up*, so transporting its average back to Γ''_a must favor the larger configurations. If I had instead found μ̃/μ decreasing, the μ̃-reweighting would have *lowered* the average and fought the f → f_a gain, and the step would not close. It's reassuring that (A) supplies exactly the sign that makes the two halves cooperate rather than fight — that is (A) doing its single job in this proof.

So, assembling: ⟨f; μ, Γ''_a⟩ ≤ ⟨f; μ, Γ_a⟩ ≤ ⟨f; μ, Γ'_a⟩, hence ⟨f; μ, Γ''_a⟩ ≤ ⟨f; μ, Γ'_a⟩, and the same for g, so both factors in Ω ≥ (Σ'μ Σ''μ)⁻¹(…)(…) are ≥ 0, so Ω ≥ 0, i.e. ⟨fg⟩ ≥ ⟨f⟩⟨g⟩ on Γ. Induction closes. The proposition is proved: on a finite distributive lattice with a measure satisfying μ(x∧y)μ(x∨y) ≥ μ(x)μ(y), any two increasing (or two decreasing) functions are positively correlated.

Let me step back and feel the architecture, because it's not an accident. The chain case was the engine — the termwise Chebyshev fact. The induction peels off one atom at a time, and at each peel the obstruction (the incomparable cross terms) is reduced to a single monotonicity statement — an increasing function averages higher above the atom than off it — and *that* statement is itself an instance of the same inequality on a shorter lattice, with one of the two functions being a decreasing indicator (the semi-ideal χ) or a monotone density ratio (μ̃/μ). The condition (A) enters in exactly one place: it's what forces μ̃/μ to be monotone, i.e. it's what guarantees the reweighting between "off the atom" and "above the atom" doesn't fight the order. Without (A) that ratio could go the wrong way and the cross terms would win. So (A) is not a technical convenience — it is precisely the hypothesis that keeps the atom-by-atom reweighting aligned with the order.

Now the special case I started from. Take a product measure, μ(x) = ∏_i μ_i(x_i). I checked it satisfies (A) with equality, so the proposition applies immediately: increasing functions of independent coordinates are positively correlated, ⟨fg⟩ ≥ ⟨f⟩⟨g⟩, and with f = 1_A, g = 1_B for increasing events, P(A∩B) ≥ P(A)P(B). That's Harris's percolation inequality — the independent-edge measure is a product measure, increasing connection events are increasing functions, done. It really was the same fact all along.

And in the product case there's an even more transparent proof that doesn't need the lattice machinery, just induction on the number of coordinates by conditioning — worth writing out because it shows the mechanism nakedly. Let x = (x_1, …, x_n) have independent coordinates, f, g increasing. For n = 1, x and an independent copy y live on a single chain, so they're comparable and (f(x) − f(y))(g(x) − g(y)) ≥ 0 always; taking expectations, 0 ≤ E[(f(x) − f(y))(g(x) − g(y))] = 2(E[fg] − E[f]E[g]), so E[fg] ≥ E[f]E[g] — the same Chebyshev base case. For n ≥ 2, condition on the first coordinate. Define f_1(y_1) = E[f | x_1 = y_1] = E_{x_2,…,x_n}[f(y_1, x_2, …, x_n)], and likewise g_1, and h_1(y_1) = E[fg | x_1 = y_1]. For each fixed y_1 the map (x_2, …, x_n) ↦ f(y_1, x_2, …, x_n) is increasing in n − 1 variables, same for g, so the inductive hypothesis on those n − 1 independent coordinates gives h_1(y_1) = E[fg | x_1 = y_1] ≥ f_1(y_1) g_1(y_1) for every y_1. Also f_1 and g_1 are increasing functions of the single variable x_1 — increasing because f, g are increasing in x_1 and expectation preserves it. Therefore

  E[fg] = E[h_1] ≥ E[f_1 g_1] ≥ E[f_1] E[g_1] = E[f] E[g],

where the first ≥ is the conditional inequality, the second is the n = 1 case applied to f_1, g_1 on the single coordinate x_1, and the ends are the tower property. So the product-measure inequality is just the scalar Chebyshev fact iterated one coordinate at a time through conditioning — the partial-order proof is the same idea carried out without the luxury of independence, where the atom-splitting replaces the conditioning and (A) replaces the factorization.

A couple of immediate corollaries fall out for free. If A, B are both *decreasing* events, their complements are increasing, and P(A∩B) ≥ P(A)P(B) follows the same way. If A is increasing and B decreasing, apply the inequality to A and the complement of B to get the *reverse*: P(A∩B) ≤ P(A)P(B) — an increasing and a decreasing event are negatively correlated. And the inequality iterates: if A_1, …, A_k are all increasing, then P(A_1 ∩ … ∩ A_k) ≥ ∏_i P(A_i), since an intersection of increasing events is increasing and I can peel them off one at a time. Good — "good events help each other" is now a single theorem with two hypotheses.

Finally let me connect it back to the spin systems that started this, to confirm the unification is genuine. The configuration space of a lattice gas / Ising system on a site set X is exactly P(X) (occupied sites) ≅ {0,1}^X, a distributive lattice. Write the measure as μ = exp(λ) with H = −λ the Hamiltonian. Condition (A) in log form is supermodularity of λ. If I expand λ as λ(R) = Σ_{P⊆R} φ(P), the exact condition is that every second discrete difference is nonnegative, equivalently Σ_{P⊆R} φ(P∪{r,s}) ≥ 0 for all r ≠ s and R disjoint from them. That imposes no restriction on the one-body term φ({r}), i.e. on the magnetic field; in the pair-interaction case it reduces to nonnegative pair couplings. So I get ⟨σ_r σ_s⟩ − ⟨σ_r⟩⟨σ_s⟩ ≥ 0 for any inhomogeneous field in the attractive cases this condition covers. And the random-cluster measure μ(R) = κ^{c(R)} ∏_{r∈R} p_r ∏_{s∉R} q_s, with c(R) counting connected components of the open subgraph on the fixed vertex set, satisfies (A) for κ ≥ 1 because c(R) is supermodular. Increasing connection events correlate there too — and at κ = 2, where this measure reproduces the Ising partition function, the second Griffiths–Kelly–Sherman inequality for pair interactions drops out as a special case. Percolation, Ising, random-cluster — one inequality, two hypotheses: a distributive lattice, and a log-supermodular measure.
