I want, for an arbitrary finitely generated algebra `A = k[x_1,...,x_n]/I`, a set of algebraically independent elements `y_1,...,y_d` inside `A` over which the whole algebra becomes a finite module. I do not get to assume the ground field is infinite, algebraically closed, or of characteristic zero: the setting I actually care about is invariants of a finite group in characteristic `p`, where the order of the group can be divisible by the characteristic, so I cannot lean on any argument that secretly needs infinitely many field elements to choose from. Whatever construction I find has to work over every field, down to the smallest finite ones.

Rename the images of `x_1,...,x_n` in `A` as `y_1',...,y_m'` (dropping any that vanish, so `m <= n`). If these `m` elements happen to already be algebraically independent, there is nothing left to do: `A` is itself the polynomial ring `k[y_1',...,y_m']`, and I take `y_i = y_i'`. So all the content of the theorem sits in the case where the `y_i'` satisfy at least one nonzero polynomial relation `f(y_1',...,y_m') = 0`. My plan is to trade one generator in for a monic integral relation over the rest, then induct on the number of generators until independence is reached.

The first thing to try is a linear change of coordinates. Fix the last variable `y_m'`, let `e = deg f`, and let `f_e` be its degree-`e` homogeneous part, nonzero by definition of the degree. Substitute `y_i' = z_i + c_i*y_m'` for `i = 1,...,m-1`, for scalars `c_i` still to be chosen, and leave `y_m'` alone. Because this substitution is linear and degree-preserving, the coefficient of the top possible power `y_m'^e` in the transformed relation works out to exactly `f_e(c_1,...,c_{m-1},1)`; every other term of `f` contributes only to lower powers of `y_m'`. If that number is nonzero, `f` becomes a degree-`e` polynomial in `y_m'` over `k[z_1,...,z_{m-1}]` with nonzero leading coefficient, and dividing by it gives a monic relation: `y_m'` integral over `k[z_1,...,z_{m-1}]`, which is exactly the elimination I want.

Write `f_e` sorted by power of `y_m'`, `f_e = sum_k y_m'^k * h_k(y_1',...,y_{m-1}')`, with the `h_k` homogeneous of pairwise distinct degrees `e-k`. Then `g(c) := f_e(c,1) = sum_k h_k(c)` cannot be the zero polynomial in `c_1,...,c_{m-1}`, because homogeneous pieces of different degree cannot cancel each other and `f_e` itself is nonzero. Over an infinite field, a nonzero polynomial is never the zero function, so some choice of scalars makes `g(c) != 0` and the elimination goes through. This is the classical picture: a sufficiently general linear projection turns any relation among the generators into an integral dependence.

But the whole argument leans on one fact — a nonzero polynomial over `k` is not the zero function — and that fact is simply false the moment `k` is finite. Over `F_q`, the one-variable polynomial `x^q - x` is nonzero yet vanishes at every element of `F_q`; the same collapse can afflict `f_e(c,1)` for a bad `f_e`. So `g` could vanish at literally every point of `k^{m-1}`, and I could try every one of the finitely many scalars available and never find a legal `(c_1,...,c_{m-1})`. The genericity the linear trick needs simply does not exist inside a finite field, and a finite (or otherwise badly behaved characteristic-`p`) field is exactly the case I cannot wave away.

So I stop hunting for a good point in `k^{m-1}` and look instead somewhere that is never small no matter how small `k` is: the exponents. Instead of shifting the other generators by a scalar multiple of `y_m'`, I shift them by an enormous power of it. Write `y~ := y_m'` and set `z_i = y_i' - y~^(r^(i-1))` for `i = 1,...,m-1`, i.e. `y_i' = z_i + y~^(r^(i-1))`, for an integer `r` I get to choose. Substitute this into one monomial `c*y_1'^{a_1}*...*y_{m-1}'^{a_{m-1}}*y~^{a_m}` of `f`. Expanding each factor `(z_i + y~^(r^(i-1)))^{a_i}` by the binomial theorem, its term of highest `y~`-degree is the one with no `z_i` in it at all, `y~^(a_i*r^(i-1))`; every other term in that expansion carries a positive power of `z_i` and a strictly smaller power of `y~`. So the single monomial's largest possible contribution to the `y~`-degree, as a polynomial over `k[z_1,...,z_{m-1}]`, is exactly the integer `a_1 + a_2*r + ... + a_{m-1}*r^{m-2} + a_m`, and the coefficient sitting at that degree is `c`, untouched by any `z`.

Now choose `r` larger than every exponent appearing anywhere in `f` — `r = 1 + deg f` is more than enough, since every `a_i <= deg f`. Then every exponent tuple `(a_1,...,a_m)` of `f` is a legal string of base-`r` digits, and reading such a tuple as the digits of a base-`r` integer is injective: distinct monomials of `f` are sent to distinct integers `N`. Among the finitely many monomials of `f`, there is a unique one with the largest such `N`, and its coefficient survives untouched as the leading coefficient of the transformed `f`, now viewed as a polynomial of degree `N` in `y~` over `k[z_1,...,z_{m-1}]` — nothing else, from any other monomial or from a lower-order binomial term of the same monomial, can reach degree `N`. Dividing by that coefficient gives a monic relation for `y~` over `k[z_1,...,z_{m-1}]`: integral dependence, with no hypothesis on `k` anywhere in the argument. I moved the genericity I needed out of the field, where it might not exist, and into the integers, where there are always more of them than there are terms in a fixed polynomial.

Peeling off one generator this way and inducting on the rest — eliminate `y~`, look at whatever relations remain among `z_1,...,z_{m-1}`, repeat — terminates after at most `m` steps at algebraically independent elements `y_1,...,y_d`, with `A` finite as a module over `k[y_1,...,y_d]` because module-finiteness composes along a chain of eliminations: finite over finite is finite. On `X = Spec A` this is exactly the statement that the inclusion `k[y_1,...,y_d] -> A` gives a finite morphism `X -> A^d_k`: the `y_i` sweep out the base, and every remaining coordinate is pinned down above each base point by the monic relation the elimination produced — a finite fiber, never a hidden positive-dimensional one lurking underneath.

The smallest case makes the mechanism vivid, and it also shows the naive failure and the fix living inside the very same formula. Take `A = k[x,y]/(xy-1)`, so `f = xy - 1`, `e = 2`, `f_e = xy`. Eliminating `y` via `x = z + c*y` needs `f_e(c,1) = c*1 = c` to be nonzero. At `c = 0` this is the unshifted, naive projection onto `x` alone, and the formula itself explains the failure: `f_e(0,1) = 0`, so no monic relation for `y` can be extracted, matching the geometric fact that `y = 1/x` satisfies no monic polynomial over `k[x]` at all — as `x` tends to `0` the point runs off to infinity, the fiber over `x = 0` is empty, and the map to `A^1_k` given by `x` is not finite. Any nonzero `c` repairs this: taking `c = -1` gives `z = x + y =: t`, and the relation becomes `(t - y)*y - 1 = 0`, i.e. `y^2 - t*y + 1 = 0`, monic in `y` over `k[t]`. By the symmetry of `xy - 1` in `x` and `y`, `x` satisfies the same relation, so `x` and `y` are the two roots of `z^2 - t*z + 1 = 0`, and `{1, x}` spans `A` as a free rank-two module over `k[t]`. Here I can see the working shift by inspection; the exponent trick is what guarantees a shift with the same effect always exists, over any field, once the number of generators and relations grows past what inspection can handle.
