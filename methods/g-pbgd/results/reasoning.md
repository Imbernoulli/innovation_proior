Let me start from what actually hurts. I have a bilevel problem: minimize an upper objective f(x,y) over (x,y), but y is not free — it has to solve a lower problem, y ∈ S(x) := argmin_{y∈U(x)} g(x,y), and S(x) drifts as x moves. In my applications x is something like a vector of per-example importance weights or a shared representation, and y is the parameters of a model — possibly a deep net with millions of entries — trained for that x. So three things are forced on me before I write down any algorithm. I can only afford first-order information about g; forming, let alone inverting, the Hessian ∇_yy g is out. I can't store anything that grows with the number of inner optimization steps. And I cannot assume g(x,·) is strongly convex — over-parameterized nets and policy-return objectives are flat-bottomed, S(x) can be a whole valley, not a point. I want something that handles all of that and still comes with a finite-accuracy guarantee, and also respects an upper-level constraint x ∈ C.

What does the favorable case even give me, so I can see exactly where it dies? If g(x,·) is strongly convex, S(x) = {y*(x)} is a single point and the whole thing collapses to minimizing the implicit function f(x, y*(x)). I can get its gradient by leaning on the lower-level stationarity identity ∇_y g(x, y*(x)) = 0 and differentiating it in x: ∇_xy g + ∇_yy g · (dy*/dx) = 0, so dy*/dx = −(∇_yy g)^{-1} ∇_xy g^T, and by the chain rule the hypergradient is df/dx = ∇_x f − ∇_xy g (∇_yy g)^{-1} ∇_y f, all evaluated at (x, y*(x)). That's the implicit-gradient method, and Ghadimi and Wang got a finite-time rate for it. But look at what it needs: ∇_yy g invertible *everywhere*. The moment g(x,·) loses strong convexity, ∇_yy g is singular, the inverse doesn't exist, S(x) is no longer a singleton, and there is simply no implicit gradient to compute. The object I'd descend doesn't exist in my regime. And even where it does exist, an inverse-Hessian–vector product on a million-parameter y is a tax I'd rather not pay. So the implicit-gradient route is out twice over — once on existence, once on cost.

The escape route people take is to stop solving the lower problem exactly and instead unroll it: run T steps of inner gradient descent ω_{t+1} = ω_t − β ∇_y g(x, ω_t), treat the final ω_{T+1} as a differentiable surrogate for y*(x), and backpropagate through the entire trajectory to get a hypergradient. Reverse-mode does this; truncated reverse-mode backprops through only the last K inner steps to save memory. This genuinely tolerates a non-strongly-convex g — no Hessian inverse needed. But the costs are exactly the ones I swore off: to reverse through T inner steps I have to keep (or recompute) the T inner iterates, so memory and compute grow with T; the finite-time guarantee only holds under restrictive conditions on the iterate map; and if the inner problem is constrained, the projection inside the loop is painful to differentiate. So unrolling buys me non-convexity at the price of the memory budget I can't spend.

Both failures share a shape worth naming: they try to chase y*(x) — either by inverting the Hessian at it, or by simulating the iteration that reaches it — and y*(x) is the troublesome object, the one that may not be unique and may be expensive to pin down. So let me drop the idea of tracking the lower solution as a function of x at all, and instead fold the lower-level optimality requirement directly into a single objective over (x,y) jointly. The constraint is "y solves the lower problem." If I had a scalar penalty p(x,y) that is zero exactly when y is lower-optimal and positive otherwise, I could just minimize F_γ(x,y) = f(x,y) + γ p(x,y) over (x,y) with a big constant γ and run plain gradient descent on both blocks. No inverse, no unroll, no second variable, one loop. This is the penalty-method idea applied to the optimality constraint.

What do I use for p? Two metrics are sitting right there. The squared lower-level gradient norm, p(x,y) = ||∇_y g(x,y)||², is zero exactly at stationary points of g(x,·). The function-value gap, p(x,y) = g(x,y) − v(x) with v(x) := min_y g(x,y), is zero exactly on the solution set S(x). The gradient-norm one looks cheap, and I should be precise about why before I trust the impression. Differentiate ||∇_y g||² = ∇_y g · ∇_y g. In y: ∇_y(||∇_y g||²) = 2 ∇_yy g · ∇_y g. In x: ∇_x(||∇_y g||²) = 2 ∇_xy g · ∇_y g. Both are Hessian–vector products — the Hessian (or mixed Hessian) of g times the already-computed vector ∇_y g. I never materialize ∇_yy g; I compute ∇_y g once keeping the autodiff graph, then take one more backward of (∇_y g · ∇_y g) and the framework hands me 2 ∇_yy g · ∇_y g. Compare to the value-gap penalty: its gradient needs ∇v(x), which requires knowing a lower-level solution y*(x) — exactly the object I was trying to avoid. So on computational grounds the gradient-norm penalty has a clear edge: no inner loop, no value tracking, just one extra backward. Let me carry p(x,y) = ||∇_y g||² forward as the candidate and minimize F_γ = f + γ||∇_y g||² by joint gradient descent, but hold the commitment loosely until I've checked it actually solves the bilevel problem.

That check is where I should be most suspicious, because the penalty method's whole reputation rests on "γ large enough ⟹ penalized solution ≈ constrained solution," and the standard proofs of that assume convexity I don't have. Let me stress-test it on the simplest non-convex instance I can write down where I know the true answer. Take min f(x,y) = sin²(y − 2π/3) subject to y ∈ argmin g(x,y) = y² + 2 sin² y. First, what is the true bilevel solution? ∇_y g = 2y + 2 sin 2y. I want its zeros. For y > 0, 2y > 0 and 2 sin 2y ∈ [−2, 2], but the linear term 2y outpaces the bounded oscillation once 2y > 2, i.e. y > 1, and below that I should just check numerically rather than wave hands. Scanning ∇_y g over y ∈ [−10, 10], the sign changes only at y ≈ 0; there is no other zero. So the only lower solution is y* = 0, the only bilevel solution is y = 0, and there f = sin²(−2π/3) = 3/4 > 0 — fine, that's the constrained optimum. Now (∇_y g)² is zero exactly on argmin g, so it is a legitimate optimality metric, and the penalty is F_γ = f + γ(y + sin 2y)² after absorbing the constant 4 into γ.

Now I want to know whether minimizing F_γ actually finds y = 0. Rather than trust the asymptotic theory, let me hunt for stationary points of F_γ directly and then, separately, run the descent and watch where it goes. Stationarity: F_γ'(y) = f'(y) + γ · 2(y + sin 2y)(1 + 2 cos 2y) = 0. The point y = 2π/3 jumps out: there 1 + 2 cos(4π/3) = 1 + 2·(−1/2) = 0, so the entire penalty-derivative factor vanishes regardless of γ; and f'(2π/3) = sin(2·0) = 0 because 2π/3 is a peak of sin²(y − 2π/3). I should not eyeball trig at special angles, so I evaluate: at y = 2π/3, g'(y) = 2.4567 (nonzero, so this is *not* a lower solution), g''(y) = 2 + 4 cos(4π/3) = −1.8×10⁻¹⁵ ≈ 0, f'(y) = 0, and the penalty derivative = −2.2×10⁻¹⁵ ≈ 0. So y = 2π/3 is a stationary point of F_γ for *every* γ > 0, and it is not lower-feasible.

A spurious stationary point existing is not yet a disaster — gradient descent need not be attracted to it. So let me actually run the descent. With the exact penalty gradient ∇p = 2 g''(y) g'(y), I iterate y ← y − α(f'(y) + γ · ∇p(y)) at γ = 10, α = 10⁻³, for 2×10⁵ steps, from several starts:

```
start 1.8 -> y_final = 2.094395    start 2.0 -> 2.094395
start 2.2 -> 2.094395              start 2.5 -> 2.094395   (2pi/3 = 2.094395)
start -0.5 -> -0.001204            start 0.3 -> -0.001204    start 0.8 -> -0.001204
```

So this is not a near-miss: from a whole basin of starts the penalized gradient descent converges to y = 2π/3 ≈ 2.0944, a point that is *not* on argmin g, while a strictly better admissible point (y = 0, where f = 3/4 and the penalty is genuinely zero) provably exists and is found only from starts already near it. I just watched the penalty method I was about to commit to settle on a non-solution. The cause is now pinpointed: at y = 2π/3, ∇_yy g = 0 while ∇_y g ≠ 0, so the penalty gradient 2∇_yy g·∇_y g vanishes even though ∇_y g is nonzero — a spurious critical point of ||∇_y g||² created exactly where the lower-level Hessian degenerates, with nothing to do with S(x). The worry was not hypothetical, and it is specific to the gradient-norm penalty at points where ∇_yy g goes singular.

This reframes the whole project. The question is not "implement a penalty," it's "for which penalty terms p, which constants γ, and which problem geometries is a solution of min f + γp actually a solution of the bilevel problem?" Until I can answer that, the penalty is a trap. Let me try to build the relation from the ground up rather than hoping a large γ saves me.

The lower-level constraint is "y ∈ S(x)," and since S(x) is closed that's the same as d_{S(x)}(y) = 0, where d_{S(x)}(y) = min_{y'∈S(x)} ||y − y'|| is the distance to the solution set. So the bilevel problem is exactly min f(x,y) s.t. x ∈ C, d²_{S(x)}(y) = 0. If I could penalize d²_{S(x)}(y) directly I'd be done, but that squared distance is non-differentiable and uncomputable — I don't know S(x). So I want a *smooth surrogate* p that upper-bounds it. What is the minimal property such a surrogate must have for the penalty relation to go through? Let me posit it and find out: call p a ρ-squared-distance bound (a ρ-SDB) if p(x,y) ≥ 0, if ρ p(x,y) ≥ d²_{S(x)}(y) (it dominates the squared distance up to a constant), and if p(x,y) = 0 exactly when d_{S(x)}(y) = 0 (it vanishes precisely on the solution set, no spurious zeros). That last clause is the part the naive analysis forgets — and notice it only constrains where p hits *zero*, not where p has local minima; I'll have to come back to that, since the y = 2π/3 trap was a spurious *local minimum*, not a spurious zero.

First, does an SDB-penalized problem even approximate the bilevel one? Define the ε-approximate bilevel problem BP_ε: min f(x,y) s.t. x ∈ C, p(x,y) ≤ ε; at ε = 0 this is exactly BP. The only assumption I'll lean on about f is that f(x,·) is L-Lipschitz continuous in y — completely standard. Let me see what |f* − f*_ε| comes out to. Let (x_ε, y_ε) solve BP_ε. Its lower-level infeasibility is bounded: d²_{S(x_ε)}(y_ε) ≤ ρ p(x_ε, y_ε) ≤ ρε. Pick the nearest true solution ȳ_ε ∈ S(x_ε) with ||ȳ_ε − y_ε|| = d_{S(x_ε)}(y_ε) ≤ √(ρε). Then f(x_ε, ȳ_ε) − f(x_ε, y_ε) ≤ L ||y_ε − ȳ_ε|| ≤ L√(ρε). Now (x_ε, ȳ_ε) is genuinely feasible for BP, so f(x_ε, ȳ_ε) ≥ f*; and the true optimum (x*, y*) is feasible for the relaxed BP_ε (its p is zero ≤ ε), so f*_ε = f(x_ε, y_ε) ≤ f*. Chain those: f* ≤ f(x_ε, ȳ_ε) ≤ f(x_ε, y_ε) + L√(ρε) = f*_ε + L√(ρε), and f*_ε ≤ f*, so |f* − f*_ε| ≤ L√(ρε). So a small p-budget ε buys an O(√ε) objective error: the squared-distance bound converts a bound on p into a bound on the geometric infeasibility d, and the L-Lipschitz f converts that into an objective gap. The SDB property is the right abstraction; the open question is now whether minimizing F_γ = f + γp keeps p small.

Take the global solutions first. I want a threshold γ* above which globals of BP_γp are near-feasible for BP_ε. Set γ* := L²ρ/(4ε₁) for a target ε₁ and see whether that choice does the work. For any feasible (x,y), with y_x the projection of y onto S(x) so d := d_{S(x)}(y) = ||y_x − y||,
  f(x,y) + γ* p(x,y) − f(x, y_x) ≥ −L d + γ* p(x,y) ≥ −L d + (γ*/ρ) d²,
using f(x,y) − f(x,y_x) ≥ −L d (Lipschitz) and γ* p ≥ (γ*/ρ) d² (the SDB). The right side is a scalar quadratic in d ≥ 0; minimize φ(z) = −L z + (γ*/ρ) z² over z ≥ 0. Vertex at z = Lρ/(2γ*), value −L²ρ/(4γ*). Let me actually plug numbers to be sure the constant is what I think: with L = 1.3, ρ = 0.7, ε₁ = 0.05, γ* = L²ρ/(4ε₁) = 5.915, vertex z = 0.0769, and φ(vertex) = −0.0500 — exactly −ε₁, and a bounded numerical minimization over z ≥ 0 agrees (−0.0500 at z = 0.0769). So φ ≥ −ε₁ with this γ*, hence f(x,y) + γ* p(x,y) − f* ≥ −ε₁ everywhere (using f(x, y_x) ≥ f*). Read off both directions: a global solution of BP, where p = 0, achieves ε₁-global-minimum of BP_γp; and conversely, if (x_γ, y_γ) is an ε₂-global-min of BP_γp with γ > γ*, plugging the true optimum (x*, y*) (with p = 0) into its optimality and using the inequality gives (γ − γ*) p(x_γ, y_γ) ≤ ε₁ + ε₂, i.e. p(x_γ, y_γ) ≤ (ε₁ + ε₂)/(γ − γ*). So for global solutions the penalty residual shrinks like O(1/γ) and feeds straight into the ε-approximation lemma. The penalty is principled for global solutions.

But the y = 2π/3 trap was not a global one — it was a *local* minimum, and gradient descent finds local minima, not global ones. The descent run above is the proof: it converged to a non-solution, so the global theorem, reassuring as it is, does not cover what my algorithm actually does. I need the local relation, and it is genuinely more delicate, because in general a local minimum of f + γp can sit anywhere — there's no reason its p has to be small. Let me see what extra structure forces a *local* solution of BP_γp to be lower-level-near-feasible.

Let (x_γ, y_γ) be a local minimum of BP_γp on a ball N of radius r, with f(x_γ,·) L-Lipschitz on that ball. I want to bound p(x_γ, y_γ), and the only handle I have is local optimality against nearby competitors — so let me build a competitor with smaller p. Suppose the feasible set U(x_γ) is convex and p(x_γ,·) is convex along the segment toward the solution set — more precisely, restricted-α-sublinear at y_γ, meaning p(x_γ, (1−α)y_γ + α y_x) ≤ (1−α)p(x_γ, y_γ) + α p(x_γ, y_x) for the projection y_x of y_γ onto S(x_γ) and a small α. Take ȳ = (1−α) y_γ + α y_x with α ∈ (0,1] small enough that ȳ stays in the ball N. Sublinearity plus p(x_γ, y_x) = 0 (it's a solution) gives p(x_γ, ȳ) ≤ (1−α) p(x_γ, y_γ). Local optimality says f(x_γ, y_γ) + γ p(x_γ, y_γ) ≤ f(x_γ, ȳ) + γ p(x_γ, ȳ). Substitute the bound on p(x_γ, ȳ) and rearrange:
  γ α p(x_γ, y_γ) ≤ f(x_γ, ȳ) − f(x_γ, y_γ) ≤ L ||ȳ − y_γ|| = L α d_{S(x_γ)}(y_γ),
the last step because ȳ − y_γ = α(y_x − y_γ) so ||ȳ − y_γ|| = α d. Cancel α and use d ≤ √(ρ p) from the SDB: γ p(x_γ, y_γ) ≤ L √(ρ p(x_γ, y_γ)), hence p(x_γ, y_γ) ≤ L²ρ/γ². So for *local* solutions the penalty residual is O(1/γ²) — even better than the global O(1/γ) — but **only under the extra convexity-along-the-segment condition on p**. And that condition is exactly what fails at y = 2π/3: the penalty (y + sin 2y)² is not convex toward the solution set there (it has a local minimum *away* from S(x)), so the interpolation argument can't run, and the spurious local minimum the descent found survives. So the theorem and the counterexample are not in tension — they delineate each other: when p(x_γ,·) is well enough behaved toward S(x), local penalty minima are near-feasible; when it isn't, they need not be, and I have a concrete example of the "need not be."

Now I have a clean target: I need a penalty p that (a) is a ρ-SDB and (b) satisfies the local condition for non-convex lower problems. This is where the PL inequality earns its keep. Recall g(x,·) is (1/μ)-PL if ||∇_y g(x,y)||² ≥ (1/μ)(g(x,y) − v(x)). PL is much weaker than strong convexity — it allows non-unique, non-convex solution sets — yet a Lipschitz-smooth PL function obeys the error bound / quadratic growth property g(x,y) − v(x) ≥ (1/μ) d²_{S(x)}(y) (Karimi–Nutini–Schmidt). Let me check both candidate penalties against the SDB definition under PL.

Value-gap penalty p = g − v: it's ≥ 0 by definition of v, zero exactly on S(x), and by quadratic growth g − v ≥ (1/μ) d², so ρ = μ works — it's a μ-SDB whenever g(x,·) is (1/μ)-PL. One chain of PL gets me there.

Gradient-norm penalty p = ||∇_y g||²: ≥ 0 trivially, and ||∇_y g||² = 0 ⟺ g(x,y) = v(x) ⟺ y ∈ S(x) under PL (PL forces stationary points to be global minima). For the SDB bound I have to chain twice: first PL gives ||∇_y g||² ≥ (1/μ̃)(g − v) for whatever PL constant 1/μ̃ I assume, then quadratic growth gives g − v ≥ (1/μ̃) d², so ||∇_y g||² ≥ (1/μ̃²) d². If I want the same target ρ = μ as the value-gap case, I need (1/μ̃²) = 1/μ, i.e. μ̃ = √μ — so I have to assume the slightly stronger (1/√μ)-PL inequality. Let me sanity-check the arithmetic on g = y²: there ||∇_y g||² = 4y², g − v = y², d² = y². PL gives 4y² ≥ (1/μ̃) y² (so μ̃ can be as small as 1/4), QG gives y² ≥ (1/μ̃) y² (so μ̃ ≥ 1), and chaining the squaring step, with target μ = 4 I'd need μ̃ = √4 = 2 and then 1/μ̃² = 1/4 = 1/μ — consistent. So the asymmetry isn't bookkeeping; it says the gradient-norm penalty is *one PL-chain weaker* a surrogate than the value gap, precisely because squaring the gradient takes me an extra step away from the distance. Under (1/√μ)-PL, p = ||∇_y g||² is a μ-SDB. Both penalties land in the framework.

So why would I ever pick the gradient-norm penalty over the value gap, given it needs the stronger PL assumption and — as the descent run showed — can stall at degenerate-Hessian points? The answer is the computational one I flagged at the start, and now I can state the trade exactly. The value-gap penalty's gradient needs ∇v(x), which (when computable) equals ∇_x g(x, y*(x)) at a lower solution — so to use it I'd have to find y*(x), e.g. by an inner GD loop. That's the fully-first-order route, robust but requiring an inner solve. The gradient-norm penalty's gradient is exact and inner-loop-free: ∇p = (2 ∇_xy g · ∇_y g, 2 ∇_yy g · ∇_y g), pure Hessian–vector products. So the gradient-norm variant is the *simplest* member of the family — plain projected gradient descent on F_γ, with the penalty gradient available exactly — at the cost of needing second-order information of g (the HVPs, so it is not first-order in the strict derivative sense), the (1/√μ)-PL assumption, and the nondegeneracy condition I'm about to nail down. I'll build that variant, with eyes open about its fragility.

Let me make the local condition concrete for p = ||∇_y g||² and see exactly when the gradient-norm penalty's local minima are good, recovering the counterexample as the boundary case. Take a local solution (x_γ, y_γ) of BP_γp and read the first-order stationarity in y: ∇_y f(x_γ, y_γ) + γ · 2 ∇_yy g(x_γ, y_γ) ∇_y g(x_γ, y_γ) = 0. Rearranging and using ||∇_y f|| ≤ L (Lipschitz f), ||∇_yy g · ∇_y g|| ≤ L/(2γ). Now suppose the singular values of ∇_yy g are bounded below by σ > 0 on the set of non-solutions. Then ||∇_yy g · ∇_y g|| ≥ σ ||∇_y g||, so ||∇_y g|| ≤ L/(2γσ), giving p(x_γ, y_γ) = ||∇_y g||² ≤ L²/(4γ²σ²) = O(1/γ²). Let me check this bound is real and tight on a non-degenerate example: g = y², so ∇_yy g = 2 everywhere (σ = 2), f = y (L = 1). Stationarity 1 + γ·2·2·(2y) = 0 gives y = −1/(8γ); then p = (2y)² = 4y² = 1/(16γ²), and the bound L²/(4γ²σ²) = 1/(16γ²) — equal, so the σ-bound is attained, not merely valid. At y = 2π/3 in the counterexample, ∇_yy g = 0 — σ = 0 — and the bound vacates (it would assert p ≤ ∞), which is exactly the regime where the descent parked on a non-solution. The value-gap penalty, by contrast, certifies that same instance through the convexity/quadratic-growth route without any σ requirement, which is why it is the more robust default and the gradient-norm penalty the simpler-but-more-fragile one. The theorem now accounts for the descent's behavior instead of contradicting it.

How large does γ have to be? From p_local ≤ L²ρ/γ² (the convex/σ case), to push the residual below a target δ I need L²ρ/γ² ≤ δ, i.e. γ ≥ L√(ρ/δ) = Ω(δ^{-0.5}). Is that scaling actually tight or just sufficient? Take the trivial instance min f = y s.t. y ∈ argmin g = y², where both penalties coincide to p = y² (since ∇_y g = 2y, ||∇_y g||²/4 = y² and g − v = y²). The penalized problem min y + γ y² has its minimizer at y = −1/(2γ). Let me tabulate the residual: γ = 5 → y* = −0.1, p = y² = 0.01; γ = 50 → y* = −0.01, p = 1.0×10⁻⁴; γ = 500 → p = 2.5×10⁻⁷ — i.e. p = 1/(4γ²) exactly, so to hit p = δ I must take γ = 1/(2√δ) = Θ(δ^{-0.5}). Reading it the other way: δ = 10⁻⁴ forces γ = 50, δ = 10⁻⁶ forces γ = 500. So γ = Θ(δ^{-0.5}) is the right scaling, sufficient and tight — and crucially a *finite* γ for any finite target accuracy. I do not need γ → ∞, which is the impractical regime the classical asymptotic penalty analyses demanded; a bounded γ tied to the accuracy I want suffices, and the SDB framework is what licenses stopping at a finite value.

There's one more thing I want, and I should be careful not to overclaim it. Take the gradient-constrained reformulation min f(x,y) s.t. ∇_y g(x,y) = 0; its ε-stationarity (the KKT conditions) asks for a multiplier w with ||∇_x f + ∇_xy g · w|| ≤ ε, ||∇_y f + ∇_yy g · w|| ≤ ε, and feasibility ||∇_y g|| ≤ ε. Now look at what a δ-stationary point of my penalized problem BP_γp with p = ||∇_y g||² actually satisfies under the (γ/2)||∇_y g||² convention: ||∇_x f + γ ∇_xy g · ∇_y g|| ≤ δ and ||∇_y f + γ ∇_yy g · ∇_y g|| ≤ δ. Stare at those two lines next to the KKT stationarity conditions. If I simply *define the multiplier* as w = γ ∇_y g, the first two KKT stationarity conditions are exactly my two penalty-stationarity conditions, met with the same δ — no constraint qualification, the multiplier is handed to me by the penalty gradient itself. But that only matches the *stationarity* blocks. The *feasibility* block ||∇_y g|| ≤ ε is not free: I cannot read it off from ||∇_yy g · ∇_y g|| alone — and the counterexample is precisely a point where the stationarity residual is tiny (the penalty gradient ≈ 0) yet ∇_y g = 2.4567 ≠ 0, so feasibility plainly does not follow from stationarity. I do get it, but only with a large enough γ: from ||∇_y f|| ≤ L and ||∇_yy g|| ≤ L_g (smoothness), the y-stationarity bound propagates to ||∇_y g|| = O((L + δ)/γ), and with γ = Ω(δ^{-0.5}) this gives ||∇_y g|| = O(δ^{0.5}) ≤ O(δ) for δ ≤ 1. So the honest statement is conditional: when the residual is controlled (γ = Ω(δ^{-0.5})), a δ-stationary point of the penalized problem is an O(δ)-stationary point of the bilevel problem, the multiplier read directly off γ ∇_y g and no constraint qualification needed for the stationarity blocks; without that residual control, gradient-norm stationarity alone does not certify bilevel feasibility — the counterexample is the witness. Still, the absence of any CRCQ/LICQ on the stationarity side is the structural advantage over the KKT-based first-order baselines.

Now I'm confident the gradient-norm penalty is principled, and I know its limits (needs (1/√μ)-PL, needs the σ-nondegeneracy to exclude spurious local minima, needs the HVP second-order info, and needs γ = Ω(δ^{-0.5}) for the feasibility side). Let me turn it into the actual update. The penalized objective is F_γ(x,y) = f(x,y) + γ ||∇_y g(x,y)||², and since ∇p is exact, the meta-algorithm is just projected gradient descent on F_γ jointly over (x,y):
  (x_{k+1}, y_{k+1}) = Proj_{C×U}( (x_k, y_k) − α ( ∇f(x_k,y_k) + γ ∇p(x_k,y_k) ) ),
with ∇p(x,y) = (2 ∇_xy g · ∇_y g, 2 ∇_yy g · ∇_y g). In an autodiff framework I don't hand-code those HVPs: I compute ∇_y g(x,y) keeping the graph (create_graph=True), then form the scalar f + (γ/2) ||∇_y g||² and call backward once; the framework differentiates through the retained ∇_y g and the (γ/2) makes the penalty contribution come out as exactly γ ∇_yy g · ∇_y g and γ ∇_xy g · ∇_y g, no stray factor of two to track. So the 1/2 in (γ/2)||∇_y g||² is a deliberate convenience: it cancels the 2 from differentiating the square, and only rescales the penalty constant.

A few practical knobs follow from the analysis, not from taste. First, γ should not be slammed to its target value from step one. F_γ is L_γ-smooth with L_γ growing linearly in γ (roughly L_f + γ(2L_g + L_g²μ)), and a stable gradient step needs α ≲ 1/L_γ; a huge γ at initialization means a stiff landscape that forces a tiny step before y has gotten anywhere near the lower-level valley. So I ramp γ from 0 up to its (finite) cap over a fixed number of steps — start as essentially pure validation-loss descent, then phase in the lower-level stationarity penalty as the iterate settles. Second, the penalty contribution to the gradient is γ ∇p, which scales with γ; once γ exceeds 1, that term dominates and a fixed step size would overshoot. The fix is to rescale the whole outer step by min(1/γ, 1) once γ > 1, so the effective penalty step is ∇p (order one) rather than γ ∇p (order γ) — this keeps the joint update inside the α ≤ 1/L_γ stable regime as γ climbs to its cap. (In the toy version the same effect is achieved by setting α = α₀/γ directly.) Third, x and y are different beasts — x might be per-example weights, y a network's parameters, with very different scales and curvature — so I give them separate block step sizes rather than one shared α. And the upper variable gets projected onto its feasible set C each step (a box, a norm ball), which is trivial because the projection acts only on x and never touches an inner unrolling that I don't have.

Let me write the data-hyper-cleaning instance concretely, because that's where all of this becomes code. The lower problem trains a classifier y on the training set reweighted by per-example importances ω^i(x) = sigmoid(x_i) ∈ (0,1): g(x,y) = mean_i sigmoid(x_i) · CE(net_y(d_tr^i), l_tr^i) (plus an optional ridge reg). The upper problem is the clean validation loss f(x,y) = CE(net_y(D_val), l_val), to be minimized over the weights x and the model y jointly subject to y solving the weighted training problem. So:

```python
import torch
import torch.nn.functional as F

# x : data-cleaner logits, weights are sigmoid(x) in (0,1);  y : net parameters.
# We minimize  F_gamma(x, y) = f(x, y) + (gamma/2) || d g / d y ||^2  by joint projected
# gradient descent, with the penalty gradient taken EXACTLY through a retained autodiff
# graph (no inner loop, no value function, no Hessian inverse).

def g_pbgd_step(x, net, x_opt, y_opt, tr, val, gam, reg):
    x_opt.zero_grad()
    y_opt.zero_grad()

    # upper objective f: clean validation loss
    fy = F.cross_entropy(net(val.data), val.clean_target)

    # lower objective g: importance-weighted (corrupted) training loss (+ optional ridge)
    ce_tr = F.cross_entropy(net(tr.data), tr.dirty_target, reduction='none')
    gxy = (torch.sigmoid(x) * ce_tr).mean() + reg * loss_F(net.parameters())

    # lower-level gradient, graph retained so a 2nd backward gives the HVP
    dgdy = torch.autograd.grad(gxy, net.parameters(), create_graph=True)

    # penalty p = ||d g / d y||^2 ; the 1/2 makes d(gam/2 ||dgdy||^2) = gam * Hessian-vec.
    # once gamma > 1, rescale the whole joint step by 1/gamma so the penalty term
    # (gam * grad p) contributes an O(1) step rather than an O(gamma) one (keeps alpha<=1/L_gamma).
    lr_decay = min(1.0 / (gam + 1e-8), 1.0)
    loss = lr_decay * (fy + gam / 2.0 * loss_F(dgdy))

    loss.backward()        # fills grads on x (via sigmoid(x)->g->dgdy) and on net params y
    x_opt.step()           # joint GD step on (x, y); separate block step sizes lrx, lry
    y_opt.step()


def loss_F(tensors):       # squared L2 norm summed over a parameter / gradient list
    return sum(torch.linalg.norm(w) ** 2 for w in tensors)


# outer loop: ramp gamma from 0 to a finite cap; x kept feasible via the sigmoid parametrization
def train(x, net, hparams, tr, val):
    y_opt = torch.optim.SGD(net.parameters(), lr=hparams['lry'])
    x_opt = torch.optim.SGD([x], lr=hparams['lrx'])
    gam = hparams['gamma_init']                                   # start at 0: pure f descent
    step_gam = (hparams['gamma_max'] - hparams['gamma_init']) / hparams['gamma_argmax_step']
    for k in range(hparams['outer_itr']):
        g_pbgd_step(x, net, x_opt, y_opt, tr, val, gam, hparams['reg'])
        gam = min(hparams['gamma_max'], gam + step_gam)           # finite cap, no gamma->inf
    return x, net
```

And the toy / numerical-verification instance is the same update with closed-form gradients and an explicit box projection of x onto [0,3], with the step folded as α = α₀/γ (the same min(1/γ,1) rescale in the limit γ ≥ 1):

```python
import numpy as np

def box(v, lo, hi):
    return min(max(v, lo), hi)

# F_gamma = f + gamma * p, joint projected GD step; for the gradient-norm penalty
# grad p is exact (dpenalty supplies grad_{x,y} ||grad_y g||^2 = 2 H_g * grad_y g).
def g_pbgd_toy_step(x, y, alpha, gam, df, dpenalty, xlim=(0.0, 3.0)):
    dF = df(x, y) + gam * dpenalty(x, y)          # d/d(x,y) [ f + gamma * ||grad_y g||^2 ]
    x_new = box(x - alpha * dF[0], *xlim)         # project upper variable onto C = [0,3]
    y_new = y - alpha * dF[1]                      # y is unconstrained
    return x_new, y_new

def solve_toy(x0, y0, gam, df, dpenalty, alpha0=0.1, eps=1e-5):
    alpha = alpha0 / gam                           # alpha = alpha0/gamma  (the 1/gamma rescale)
    x, y, k = x0, y0, 0
    while True:
        x_new, y_new = g_pbgd_toy_step(x, y, alpha, gam, df, dpenalty)
        pg = np.linalg.norm([(x - x_new) / alpha, (y - y_new) / alpha])  # projected-grad norm
        x, y, k = x_new, y_new, k + 1
        if pg <= eps:
            return x, y, k
```

Let me retrace the causal chain so I'm sure nothing is bolted on. I started stuck because the implicit-gradient route needs the lower-level Hessian invertible everywhere — strong convexity — which my non-strongly-convex, possibly-non-singleton lower problems don't have, and the unrolling route buys non-convexity only by paying memory that grows with the inner horizon. Both chase the troublesome object y*(x), so I dropped that and folded lower-level optimality into a single joint objective F_γ = f + γp, leaning toward the gradient-norm penalty p = ||∇_y g||² because its gradient is exactly computable as Hessian–vector products — no inner loop, no value function, no inverse. But before committing I ran plain penalized gradient descent on a non-convex toy and watched it converge, from a whole basin of starts, to y = 2π/3 — a point that is not lower-feasible, where ∇_yy g degenerates to 0 while ∇_y g ≠ 0. That forced the real question: precisely when is a penalized solution a bilevel solution? Abstracting the constraint y ∈ S(x) as d²_{S(x)}(y) = 0 and demanding only that p be a squared-distance bound (nonnegative, dominating d² up to ρ, vanishing exactly on S(x)), I proved the penalized problem ε-approximates the bilevel one with objective gap L√(ρε); that globals of F_γ have penalty residual O(1/γ) (constant checked numerically: γ* = L²ρ/(4ε₁) lands φ at exactly −ε₁); and that locals have residual O(1/γ²) under a convexity-toward-S(x) or, for the gradient-norm penalty specifically, a σ-nondegeneracy of ∇_yy g — the very condition the counterexample violates (σ = 0 at y = 2π/3), which is why the spurious local minimum survives there and the value-gap penalty is the more robust sibling. PL (with the (1/√μ) strengthening for the gradient-norm case, arithmetic checked on g = y²) certifies p = ||∇_y g||² as a μ-SDB without strong convexity. The accuracy/penalty scaling γ = Θ(δ^{-0.5}) is both sufficient and tight (witnessed on min y s.t. y ∈ argmin y², where p = 1/(4γ²) exactly), so a *finite* γ suffices — no γ → ∞. Reading the multiplier off as w = γ ∇_y g turns penalty-stationarity into bilevel KKT-stationarity with no constraint qualification on the stationarity blocks — though the feasibility block still needs that same γ = Ω(δ^{-0.5}) residual control, exactly because the counterexample is a point with small stationarity residual but ∇_y g ≠ 0, so the guarantee is conditional, not unconditional. The algorithm that drops out is the simplest thing possible: projected gradient descent on F_γ, the penalty gradient taken through a retained autodiff graph, γ ramped from 0 to a finite cap, the joint step rescaled by min(1/γ,1) to stay stable as γ grows, separate block step sizes for the data-weights x and the model y, and an explicit box projection on x where the upper level is constrained.
