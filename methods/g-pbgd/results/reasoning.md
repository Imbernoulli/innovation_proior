Let me start from what actually hurts. I have a bilevel problem: minimize an upper objective f(x,y) over (x,y), but y is not free — it has to solve a lower problem, y ∈ S(x) := argmin_{y∈U(x)} g(x,y), and S(x) drifts as x moves. In my applications x is something like a vector of per-example importance weights or a shared representation, and y is the parameters of a model — possibly a deep net with millions of entries — trained for that x. So three things are forced on me before I write down any algorithm. I can only afford first-order information about g; forming, let alone inverting, the Hessian ∇_yy g is out. I can't store anything that grows with the number of inner optimization steps. And I cannot assume g(x,·) is strongly convex — over-parameterized nets and policy-return objectives are flat-bottomed, S(x) can be a whole valley, not a point. I want something that handles all of that and still comes with a finite-accuracy guarantee, and also respects an upper-level constraint x ∈ C.

What does the favorable case even give me, so I can see exactly where it dies? If g(x,·) is strongly convex, S(x) = {y*(x)} is a single point and the whole thing collapses to minimizing the implicit function f(x, y*(x)). I can get its gradient by leaning on the lower-level stationarity identity ∇_y g(x, y*(x)) = 0 and differentiating it in x: ∇_xy g + ∇_yy g · (dy*/dx) = 0, so dy*/dx = −(∇_yy g)^{-1} ∇_xy g^T, and by the chain rule the hypergradient is df/dx = ∇_x f − ∇_xy g (∇_yy g)^{-1} ∇_y f, all evaluated at (x, y*(x)). That's the implicit-gradient method, and Ghadimi and Wang got a finite-time rate for it. But look at what it needs: ∇_yy g invertible *everywhere*. The moment g(x,·) loses strong convexity, ∇_yy g is singular, the inverse doesn't exist, S(x) is no longer a singleton, and there is simply no implicit gradient to compute. The object I'd descend doesn't exist in my regime. And even where it does exist, an inverse-Hessian–vector product on a million-parameter y is a tax I'd rather not pay. Dead end for my problem.

The escape route people take is to stop solving the lower problem exactly and instead unroll it: run T steps of inner gradient descent ω_{t+1} = ω_t − β ∇_y g(x, ω_t), treat the final ω_{T+1} as a differentiable surrogate for y*(x), and backpropagate through the entire trajectory to get a hypergradient. Reverse-mode does this; truncated reverse-mode backprops through only the last K inner steps to save memory. This genuinely tolerates a non-strongly-convex g — no Hessian inverse needed. But the costs are exactly the ones I swore off: to reverse through T inner steps I have to keep (or recompute) the T inner iterates, so memory and compute grow with T; the finite-time guarantee only holds under restrictive conditions on the iterate map; and if the inner problem is constrained, the projection inside the loop is painful to differentiate. So unrolling buys me non-convexity at the price of the memory budget I can't spend. Another wall.

Both failures share a shape: they try to chase y*(x) — either by inverting the Hessian at it, or by simulating the iteration that reaches it — and y*(x) is the troublesome object, the one that may not be unique and may be expensive to pin down. So let me drop the idea of tracking the lower solution as a function of x at all, and instead fold the lower-level optimality requirement directly into a single objective over (x,y) jointly. The constraint is "y solves the lower problem." If I had a scalar penalty p(x,y) that is zero exactly when y is lower-optimal and positive otherwise, I could just minimize F_γ(x,y) = f(x,y) + γ p(x,y) over (x,y) with a big constant γ and run plain gradient descent on both blocks. No inverse, no unroll, no second variable, one loop. That's the classical penalty method.

What do I use for p? Two metrics are sitting right there. The squared lower-level gradient norm, p(x,y) = ||∇_y g(x,y)||², is zero exactly at stationary points of g(x,·). The function-value gap, p(x,y) = g(x,y) − v(x) with v(x) := min_y g(x,y), is zero exactly on the solution set S(x). The gradient-norm one is irresistibly cheap, and I should be precise about why: its gradient is *exactly computable* with first-order tools. Differentiate ||∇_y g||² = ∇_y g · ∇_y g. In y: ∇_y(||∇_y g||²) = 2 ∇_yy g · ∇_y g. In x: ∇_x(||∇_y g||²) = 2 ∇_xy g · ∇_y g. Both are Hessian–vector products — the Hessian (or mixed Hessian) of g times the already-computed vector ∇_y g. I never materialize ∇_yy g; I compute ∇_y g once keeping the autodiff graph, then take one more backward of (∇_y g · ∇_y g) and the framework hands me 2 ∇_yy g · ∇_y g for free. Compare to the value-gap penalty: its gradient needs ∇v(x), which requires knowing a lower-level solution y*(x) — exactly the object I was trying to avoid. So for sheer computational simplicity the gradient-norm penalty wins outright: no inner loop, no value tracking, just one extra backward. Let me provisionally commit to p(x,y) = ||∇_y g||² and minimize F_γ = f + γ||∇_y g||² by joint gradient descent.

Now I have to check this actually solves the bilevel problem, because the penalty method's whole reputation rests on "γ large enough ⟹ penalized solution ≈ constrained solution," and I have a nagging worry that the lower-level non-convexity will break it. Let me stress-test it on the simplest non-convex instance I can write down where I know the true answer. Take min f(x,y) = sin²(y − 2π/3) subject to y ∈ argmin g(x,y) = y² + 2 sin² y. The lower objective y² + 2 sin² y is non-convex, but ∇_y g = 2y + 2 sin 2y has y = 0 as its only zero (the y term forces the derivative positive for y > 0 and negative for y < 0 once you account for |sin 2y| ≤ 1 against |2y|), so the only lower solution is y* = 0, and the only bilevel solution is y = 0, where f = sin²(−2π/3) > 0 — fine, that's the constrained optimum. Now (∇_y g)² = (2y + 2 sin 2y)² is indeed zero exactly on argmin g, so it's a legitimate optimality metric. Penalize: minimize f(x,y) + γ(y + sin 2y)² (absorbing the factor 4). Look for stationary points of the penalty piece (y + sin 2y)²: its derivative is 2(y + sin 2y)(1 + 2 cos 2y). At y = 2π/3, the second factor 1 + 2 cos(4π/3) = 1 + 2(−1/2) = 0. And f′(2π/3) = 0 too (sin² is flat at its peaks/troughs there). So y = 2π/3 is a stationary point of F_γ for *any* γ, and it isn't on the solution set. Gradient descent on F_γ can park there. I just watched the penalty method converge to a point that is not a solution of the bilevel problem — and a strictly better admissible point (y = 0) provably exists, the optimizer just can't be driven to it by this penalty. The decisive structural fact: at y = 2π/3, the second derivative ∇_yy g = 2 + 4 cos 2y = 2 + 4(−1/2) = 0, while ∇_y g there is nonzero. The Hessian of g degenerates, and that degeneracy lets the penalty gradient 2∇_yy g·∇_y g vanish even though ∇_y g ≠ 0 — a spurious critical point of ||∇_y g||² that has nothing to do with S(x). So the worry is real, and it's specifically a property of the gradient-norm penalty at points where ∇_yy g goes singular.

This reframes the whole project. The question is not "implement a penalty," it's "for which penalty terms p, which constants γ, and which problem geometries is a solution of min f + γp actually a solution of the bilevel problem?" Until I can answer that, the penalty is a trap. Let me try to build the relation from the ground up rather than hoping a large γ saves me.

The lower-level constraint is "y ∈ S(x)," and since S(x) is closed that's the same as d_{S(x)}(y) = 0, where d_{S(x)}(y) = min_{y'∈S(x)} ||y − y'|| is the distance to the solution set. So the bilevel problem is exactly min f(x,y) s.t. x ∈ C, d²_{S(x)}(y) = 0. If I could penalize d²_{S(x)}(y) directly I'd be done, but that squared distance is non-differentiable and uncomputable — I don't know S(x). So I want a *smooth surrogate* p that upper-bounds it. What is the minimal property such a surrogate must have for the penalty relation to go through? Let me posit it and find out: call p a ρ-squared-distance bound (a ρ-SDB) if p(x,y) ≥ 0, if ρ p(x,y) ≥ d²_{S(x)}(y) (it dominates the squared distance up to a constant), and if p(x,y) = 0 exactly when d_{S(x)}(y) = 0 (it vanishes precisely on the solution set, no spurious zeros). That last clause is the part the naive analysis forgets — and notice it only constrains where p hits *zero*, not where p has local minima; I'll have to come back to that, it's where my toy counterexample lived.

First, does an SDB-penalized problem even approximate the bilevel one? Define the ε-approximate bilevel problem BP_ε: min f(x,y) s.t. x ∈ C, p(x,y) ≤ ε; at ε = 0 this is exactly BP. The only assumption I'll lean on about f is that f(x,·) is L-Lipschitz continuous in y — completely standard. Claim: if p is a ρ-SDB then BP_ε is a genuine ε-approximation in the sense that |f* − f*_ε| ≤ L√(ρε). Proof: let (x_ε, y_ε) solve BP_ε. Its lower-level infeasibility is bounded: d²_{S(x_ε)}(y_ε) ≤ ρ p(x_ε, y_ε) ≤ ρε. Pick the nearest true solution ȳ_ε ∈ S(x_ε) with ||ȳ_ε − y_ε|| = d_{S(x_ε)}(y_ε) ≤ √(ρε). Then f(x_ε, ȳ_ε) − f(x_ε, y_ε) ≤ L ||y_ε − ȳ_ε|| ≤ L√(ρε). Now (x_ε, ȳ_ε) is genuinely feasible for BP, so f(x_ε, ȳ_ε) ≥ f*; and the true optimum (x*, y*) is feasible for the relaxed BP_ε (its p is zero ≤ ε), so f*_ε = f(x_ε, y_ε) ≤ f*. Chain those: f* ≤ f(x_ε, ȳ_ε) ≤ f(x_ε, y_ε) + L√(ρε) = f*_ε + L√(ρε), and f*_ε ≤ f*, so |f* − f*_ε| ≤ L√(ρε). Good — a small p-budget ε buys an O(√ε) objective error. The squared-distance bound is exactly the lever that converts a bound on p into a bound on the geometric infeasibility d, and the L-Lipschitz f converts that into an objective gap. So the SDB property is the right abstraction; the question is now whether minimizing F_γ = f + γp keeps p small.

Take the global solutions first. I'll show a threshold γ* above which globals of BP_γp are near-feasible for BP_ε. Set γ* := L²ρ/(4ε₁) for a target ε₁. The key inequality: for any feasible (x,y), with y_x the projection of y onto S(x) so d := d_{S(x)}(y) = ||y_x − y||,
  f(x,y) + γ* p(x,y) − f(x, y_x) ≥ −L d + γ* p(x,y) ≥ −L d + (γ*/ρ) d²,
using f(x,y) − f(x,y_x) ≥ −L d (Lipschitz) and γ* p ≥ (γ*/ρ) d² (the SDB). The right side is a scalar quadratic in d ≥ 0; minimize −L z + (γ*/ρ) z² over z ≥ 0: vertex at z = Lρ/(2γ*), value −L²ρ/(4γ*) = −ε₁ with my choice of γ*. And f(x, y_x) ≥ f* since (x, y_x) is feasible for BP. So f(x,y) + γ* p(x,y) − f* ≥ −ε₁ everywhere. Read that off both directions: a global solution of BP, where p = 0, achieves ε₁-global-minimum of BP_γp; and conversely, if (x_γ, y_γ) is an ε₂-global-min of BP_γp with γ > γ*, plugging the true optimum (x*, y*) (with p = 0) into its optimality and using the inequality above gives (γ − γ*) p(x_γ, y_γ) ≤ ε₁ + ε₂, i.e. p(x_γ, y_γ) ≤ (ε₁ + ε₂)/(γ − γ*). So for global solutions the penalty residual shrinks like O(1/γ) and feeds straight into the ε-approximation lemma. The penalty *is* principled — for global solutions.

But my toy counterexample wasn't a global trap, it was a *local* one: y = 2π/3 is a spurious local minimum of the penalty, and gradient descent finds local minima, not global ones. So the global theorem, while reassuring, doesn't actually cover what my algorithm does. I need the local relation, and it's genuinely more delicate, because in general a local minimum of f + γp can sit anywhere — there's no reason its p has to be small. Let me see what extra structure forces a *local* solution of BP_γp to be lower-level-near-feasible.

Let (x_γ, y_γ) be a local minimum of BP_γp on a ball N of radius r, with f(x_γ,·) L-Lipschitz on that ball. I want to bound p(x_γ, y_γ). The trick is to find a nearby competitor with smaller p and use local optimality against it. Suppose the feasible set U(x_γ) is convex and p(x_γ,·) is convex along the segment toward the solution set — more precisely, restricted-α-sublinear at y_γ, meaning p(x_γ, (1−α)y_γ + α y_x) ≤ (1−α)p(x_γ, y_γ) + α p(x_γ, y_x) for the projection y_x of y_γ onto S(x_γ) and a small α. Take ȳ = (1−α) y_γ + α y_x with α ∈ (0,1] small enough that ȳ stays in the ball N. Sublinearity plus p(x_γ, y_x) = 0 (it's a solution) gives p(x_γ, ȳ) ≤ (1−α) p(x_γ, y_γ). Local optimality says f(x_γ, y_γ) + γ p(x_γ, y_γ) ≤ f(x_γ, ȳ) + γ p(x_γ, ȳ). Substitute the bound on p(x_γ, ȳ) and rearrange:
  γ α p(x_γ, y_γ) ≤ f(x_γ, ȳ) − f(x_γ, y_γ) ≤ L ||ȳ − y_γ|| = L α d_{S(x_γ)}(y_γ),
the last step because ȳ − y_γ = α(y_x − y_γ) so ||ȳ − y_γ|| = α d. Cancel α and use d ≤ √(ρ p) from the SDB: γ p(x_γ, y_γ) ≤ L √(ρ p(x_γ, y_γ)), hence p(x_γ, y_γ) ≤ L²ρ/γ². So for *local* solutions the penalty residual is O(1/γ²) — even better than the global O(1/γ) — but **only under the extra convexity-along-the-segment condition on p**. That condition is exactly what my toy counterexample fails: at y = 2π/3 the penalty (y + sin 2y)² is not convex toward the solution set, so the interpolation argument can't run, and the spurious local minimum survives. The theorem and the counterexample fit together perfectly: when p(x_γ,·) is "well enough behaved toward S(x)," local penalty minima are near-feasible; when it isn't, they need not be.

Now I have a clean target: I need a penalty p that (a) is a ρ-SDB and (b) satisfies the local condition for non-convex lower problems. This is where the PL inequality earns its keep. Recall g(x,·) is (1/μ)-PL if ||∇_y g(x,y)||² ≥ (1/μ)(g(x,y) − v(x)). PL is much weaker than strong convexity — it allows non-unique, non-convex solution sets — yet a Lipschitz-smooth PL function obeys the error bound / quadratic growth property g(x,y) − v(x) ≥ (1/μ) d²_{S(x)}(y) (Karimi–Nutini–Schmidt). Let me check both candidate penalties against the SDB definition under PL.

Value-gap penalty p = g − v: it's ≥ 0 by definition of v, zero exactly on S(x), and by quadratic growth g − v ≥ (1/μ) d², so ρ = μ works — it's a μ-SDB whenever g(x,·) is (1/μ)-PL. One chain of PL gets me there.

Gradient-norm penalty p = ||∇_y g||²: ≥ 0 trivially, and ||∇_y g||² = 0 ⟺ g(x,y) = v(x) ⟺ y ∈ S(x) under PL (PL forces stationary points to be global minima). For the SDB bound I have to chain twice: first PL gives ||∇_y g||² ≥ (1/μ̃)(g − v) for whatever PL constant 1/μ̃ I assume, then quadratic growth gives g − v ≥ (1/μ̃) d², so ||∇_y g||² ≥ (1/μ̃²) d². If I want the same target ρ = μ as the value-gap case, I need (1/μ̃²) = 1/μ, i.e. μ̃ = √μ — so I have to assume the slightly stronger (1/√μ)-PL inequality. That asymmetry isn't bookkeeping; it's telling me the gradient-norm penalty is *one PL-chain weaker* a surrogate than the value gap, precisely because squaring the gradient takes me an extra step away from the distance. Under (1/√μ)-PL, though, p = ||∇_y g||² is a μ-SDB. Both penalties land in the framework. Good.

So why would I ever pick the gradient-norm penalty over the value gap, given it needs the stronger PL assumption and — as my counterexample showed — can stall at degenerate-Hessian points? The answer is the computational one I flagged at the start, and now I can be exact about the trade. The value-gap penalty's gradient needs ∇v(x), which (when computable) equals ∇_x g(x, y*(x)) at a lower solution — so to use it I'd have to find y*(x), e.g. by an inner GD loop. That's the fully-first-order route, robust but requiring an inner solve. The gradient-norm penalty's gradient is exact and inner-loop-free: ∇p = (2 ∇_xy g · ∇_y g, 2 ∇_yy g · ∇_y g), pure Hessian–vector products. So the gradient-norm variant is the *simplest possible* member of the family — plain projected gradient descent on F_γ, with the penalty gradient available exactly — at the cost of needing second-order information of g (the HVPs, so it is not first-order in the strict derivative sense), the (1/√μ)-PL assumption, and the nondegeneracy condition I'm about to nail down. I'll build that variant; it's the natural baseline.

Let me make the local condition concrete for p = ||∇_y g||² and see exactly when the gradient-norm penalty's local minima are good, recovering my counterexample as the boundary case. Take a local solution (x_γ, y_γ) of BP_γp and read the first-order stationarity in y: ∇_y f(x_γ, y_γ) + γ · 2 ∇_yy g(x_γ, y_γ) ∇_y g(x_γ, y_γ) = 0. Rearranging and using ||∇_y f|| ≤ L (Lipschitz f), ||∇_yy g · ∇_y g|| ≤ L/(2γ). Now suppose the singular values of ∇_yy g are bounded below by σ > 0 on the set of non-solutions (the regularity my toy violates exactly where ∇_yy g = 0). Then ||∇_yy g · ∇_y g|| ≥ σ ||∇_y g||, so ||∇_y g|| ≤ L/(2γσ), giving p(x_γ, y_γ) = ||∇_y g||² ≤ L²/(4γ²σ²) = O(1/γ²). So the gradient-norm penalty's local solutions are O(1/γ²)-near-feasible *provided* ∇_yy g doesn't go singular at non-solution points. At y = 2π/3 in my counterexample, ∇_yy g = 0 — σ = 0 — and the bound vacates: the spurious local minimum is exactly the failure of this σ-condition. The value-gap penalty, by contrast, certifies that same instance through the convexity/quadratic-growth route without any σ requirement, which is precisely why it's the more robust default and the gradient-norm penalty the simpler-but-more-fragile one. The theorem now *explains* the picture I observed rather than being contradicted by it.

How large does γ have to be? From p_local ≤ L²ρ/γ² (the convex/σ case), to push the residual below a target δ I need L²ρ/γ² ≤ δ, i.e. γ ≥ L√(ρ/δ) = Ω(δ^{-0.5}). And this is tight, not just sufficient — take the trivial instance min f = y s.t. y ∈ argmin g = y², where both penalties coincide to p = y² (since ∇_y g = 2y, ||∇_y g||²/4 = y² and g − v = y²). The penalized problem min y + γ y² has its minimizer at y = −1/(2γ), so p = 1/(4γ²) = δ forces γ = 1/(2√δ) = Θ(δ^{-0.5}). So γ = Θ(δ^{-0.5}) is exactly the right scaling — crucially a *finite* γ for any finite target accuracy. I do not need γ → ∞, which is the impractical regime the classical asymptotic penalty analyses demanded; a bounded γ tied to the accuracy I want suffices, and the SDB framework is what licenses stopping at a finite value.

There's one more thing I want, and I should be careful not to overclaim it. Take the gradient-constrained reformulation min f(x,y) s.t. ∇_y g(x,y) = 0; its ε-stationarity (the KKT conditions) asks for a multiplier w with ||∇_x f + ∇_xy g · w|| ≤ ε, ||∇_y f + ∇_yy g · w|| ≤ ε, and feasibility ||∇_y g|| ≤ ε. Now look at what a δ-stationary point of my penalized problem BP_γp with p = ||∇_y g||² actually satisfies under the (γ/2)||∇_y g||² convention: ||∇_x f + γ ∇_xy g · ∇_y g|| ≤ δ and ||∇_y f + γ ∇_yy g · ∇_y g|| ≤ δ. Stare at those two lines next to the KKT stationarity conditions. If I simply *define the multiplier* as w = γ ∇_y g, the first two KKT stationarity conditions are exactly my two penalty-stationarity conditions, met with the same δ — no constraint qualification, the multiplier is handed to me by the penalty gradient itself. But that only matches the *stationarity* blocks. The *feasibility* block ||∇_y g|| ≤ ε is not free: I cannot read it off from ||∇_yy g · ∇_y g|| alone (the toy example is exactly a point with small stationarity residual but ∇_y g ≠ 0). I do get it, but only with a large enough γ: from ||∇_y f|| ≤ L and ||∇_yy g|| ≤ L_g (smoothness), γ||∇_y g|| ≤ ||∇_y f + γ∇_yy g·∇_y g|| · (something) propagates so that ||∇_y g|| = O((L + δ)/γ), and with γ = Ω(δ^{-0.5}) this gives ||∇_y g|| = O(δ^{0.5}) ≤ O(δ) for δ ≤ 1. So the honest statement is conditional: when the residual is controlled (γ = Ω(δ^{-0.5})), a δ-stationary point of the penalized problem is an O(δ)-stationary point of the bilevel problem, the multiplier read directly off γ ∇_y g and no constraint qualification needed for the stationarity blocks; without that residual control, gradient-norm stationarity alone does not certify bilevel feasibility. Still, the absence of any CRCQ/LICQ on the stationarity side is the structural advantage over the KKT-based first-order baselines.

Now I'm confident the gradient-norm penalty is principled, and I know its limits (needs (1/√μ)-PL, needs the σ-nondegeneracy to exclude spurious local minima, needs the HVP second-order info, and needs γ = Ω(δ^{-0.5}) for the feasibility side). Let me turn it into the actual update. The penalized objective is F_γ(x,y) = f(x,y) + γ ||∇_y g(x,y)||², and since ∇p is exact, the meta-algorithm is just projected gradient descent on F_γ jointly over (x,y):
  (x_{k+1}, y_{k+1}) = Proj_{C×U}( (x_k, y_k) − α ( ∇f(x_k,y_k) + γ ∇p(x_k,y_k) ) ),
with ∇p(x,y) = (2 ∇_xy g · ∇_y g, 2 ∇_yy g · ∇_y g). In an autodiff framework I don't hand-code those HVPs: I compute ∇_y g(x,y) keeping the graph (create_graph=True), then form the scalar f + (γ/2) ||∇_y g||² and call backward once; the framework differentiates through the retained ∇_y g and the (γ/2) makes the penalty contribution come out as exactly γ ∇_yy g · ∇_y g and γ ∇_xy g · ∇_y g, no stray factor of two to track. So the 1/2 in (γ/2)||∇_y g||² is a deliberate convenience: it cancels the 2 from differentiating the square, and only rescales the penalty constant.

A few practical knobs follow from the analysis, not from taste. First, γ should not be slammed to its target value from step one. F_γ is L_γ-smooth with L_γ growing linearly in γ (roughly L_f + γ(2L_g + L_g²μ)), and a stable gradient step needs α ≲ 1/L_γ; a huge γ at initialization means a stiff landscape that forces a tiny step before y has gotten anywhere near the lower-level valley. So I ramp γ from 0 up to its (finite) cap over a fixed number of steps — start as essentially pure validation-loss descent, then phase in the lower-level stationarity penalty as the iterate settles. Second, the penalty contribution to the gradient is γ ∇p, which scales with γ; once γ exceeds 1, that term dominates and a fixed step size would overshoot. The fix is to rescale the whole outer step by min(1/γ, 1) once γ > 1, so the effective penalty step is ∇p (order one) rather than γ ∇p (order γ) — this keeps the joint update inside the α ≤ 1/L_γ stable regime as γ climbs to its cap. (In the toy version the same effect is achieved by setting α = α₀/γ directly.) Third, x and y are different beasts — x might be per-example weights, y a network's parameters, with very different scales and curvature — so I give them separate block step sizes rather than one shared α. And the upper variable gets projected onto its feasible set C each step (a box, a norm ball), which is trivial because the projection acts only on x and never touches an inner unrolling that I don't have.

Let me write the data-hyper-cleaning instance concretely, because that's where all of this becomes code. The lower problem trains a classifier y on the training set reweighted by per-example importances ω^i(x) = sigmoid(x_i) ∈ (0,1): g(x,y) = mean_i sigmoid(x_i) · CE(net_y(d_tr^i), l_tr^i) (plus an optional ridge reg). The upper problem is the clean validation loss f(x,y) = CE(net_y(D_val), l_val), to be minimized over the weights x and the model y jointly subject to y solving the weighted training problem. So:

```python
import torch
import torch.nn.functional as F

# x : data-cleaner logits, weights are sigmoid(x) in (0,1);  y : net parameters.
# We minimize  F_gamma(x, y) = f(x, y) + (gamma/2) || d g / d y ||^2  by joint projected
# gradient descent, with the penalty gradient taken EXACTLY through a retained autodiff
# graph (no inner loop, no value function, no Hessian inverse).

def g_pbgd_step(x, net, x_opt, y_opt, tr, val, gam, reg):
    x_opt.zero_grad()
    y_opt.zero_grad()

    # upper objective f: clean validation loss
    fy = F.cross_entropy(net(val.data), val.clean_target)

    # lower objective g: importance-weighted (corrupted) training loss (+ optional ridge)
    ce_tr = F.cross_entropy(net(tr.data), tr.dirty_target, reduction='none')
    gxy = (torch.sigmoid(x) * ce_tr).mean() + reg * loss_F(net.parameters())

    # lower-level gradient, graph retained so a 2nd backward gives the HVP
    dgdy = torch.autograd.grad(gxy, net.parameters(), create_graph=True)

    # penalty p = ||d g / d y||^2 ; the 1/2 makes d(gam/2 ||dgdy||^2) = gam * Hessian-vec.
    # once gamma > 1, rescale the whole joint step by 1/gamma so the penalty term
    # (gam * grad p) contributes an O(1) step rather than an O(gamma) one (keeps alpha<=1/L_gamma).
    lr_decay = min(1.0 / (gam + 1e-8), 1.0)
    loss = lr_decay * (fy + gam / 2.0 * loss_F(dgdy))

    loss.backward()        # fills grads on x (via sigmoid(x)->g->dgdy) and on net params y
    x_opt.step()           # joint GD step on (x, y); separate block step sizes lrx, lry
    y_opt.step()


def loss_F(tensors):       # squared L2 norm summed over a parameter / gradient list
    return sum(torch.linalg.norm(w) ** 2 for w in tensors)


# outer loop: ramp gamma from 0 to a finite cap; x kept feasible via the sigmoid parametrization
def train(x, net, hparams, tr, val):
    y_opt = torch.optim.SGD(net.parameters(), lr=hparams['lry'])
    x_opt = torch.optim.SGD([x], lr=hparams['lrx'])
    gam = hparams['gamma_init']                                   # start at 0: pure f descent
    step_gam = (hparams['gamma_max'] - hparams['gamma_init']) / hparams['gamma_argmax_step']
    for k in range(hparams['outer_itr']):
        g_pbgd_step(x, net, x_opt, y_opt, tr, val, gam, hparams['reg'])
        gam = min(hparams['gamma_max'], gam + step_gam)           # finite cap, no gamma->inf
    return x, net
```

And the toy / numerical-verification instance is the same update with closed-form gradients and an explicit box projection of x onto [0,3], with the step folded as α = α₀/γ (the same min(1/γ,1) rescale in the limit γ ≥ 1):

```python
import numpy as np

def box(v, lo, hi):
    return min(max(v, lo), hi)

# F_gamma = f + gamma * p, joint projected GD step; for the gradient-norm penalty
# grad p is exact (dpenalty supplies grad_{x,y} ||grad_y g||^2 = 2 H_g * grad_y g).
def g_pbgd_toy_step(x, y, alpha, gam, df, dpenalty, xlim=(0.0, 3.0)):
    dF = df(x, y) + gam * dpenalty(x, y)          # d/d(x,y) [ f + gamma * ||grad_y g||^2 ]
    x_new = box(x - alpha * dF[0], *xlim)         # project upper variable onto C = [0,3]
    y_new = y - alpha * dF[1]                      # y is unconstrained
    return x_new, y_new

def solve_toy(x0, y0, gam, df, dpenalty, alpha0=0.1, eps=1e-5):
    alpha = alpha0 / gam                           # alpha = alpha0/gamma  (the 1/gamma rescale)
    x, y, k = x0, y0, 0
    while True:
        x_new, y_new = g_pbgd_toy_step(x, y, alpha, gam, df, dpenalty)
        pg = np.linalg.norm([(x - x_new) / alpha, (y - y_new) / alpha])  # projected-grad norm
        x, y, k = x_new, y_new, k + 1
        if pg <= eps:
            return x, y, k
```

Let me retrace the causal chain so I'm sure nothing is bolted on. I started stuck because the implicit-gradient route needs the lower-level Hessian invertible everywhere — strong convexity — which my non-strongly-convex, possibly-non-singleton lower problems don't have, and the unrolling route buys non-convexity only by paying memory that grows with the inner horizon. Both chase the troublesome object y*(x), so I dropped that and folded lower-level optimality into a single joint objective F_γ = f + γp, choosing the gradient-norm penalty p = ||∇_y g||² because its gradient is exactly computable as Hessian–vector products — no inner loop, no value function, no inverse. But a one-line stress test on a non-convex toy showed plain penalization converges to a spurious point where ∇_yy g degenerates, so I had to ask precisely when a penalized solution is a bilevel solution. Abstracting the constraint y ∈ S(x) as d²_{S(x)}(y) = 0 and demanding only that p be a squared-distance bound (nonnegative, dominating d² up to ρ, vanishing exactly on S(x)), I proved the penalized problem ε-approximates the bilevel one with objective gap L√(ρε); that globals of F_γ have penalty residual O(1/γ); and that locals have residual O(1/γ²) under a convexity-toward-S(x) or, for the gradient-norm penalty specifically, a σ-nondegeneracy of ∇_yy g — the very condition my counterexample violates, which is why the spurious local minimum survives there and the value-gap penalty is the more robust sibling. PL (with the (1/√μ) strengthening for the gradient-norm case) certifies p = ||∇_y g||² as a μ-SDB without strong convexity. The accuracy/penalty scaling γ = Θ(δ^{-0.5}) is both sufficient and tight, so a *finite* γ suffices — no γ → ∞. Reading the multiplier off as w = γ ∇_y g turns penalty-stationarity into bilevel KKT-stationarity with no constraint qualification on the stationarity blocks — though the feasibility block still needs that same γ = Ω(δ^{-0.5}) residual control, so the guarantee is conditional, not unconditional. The algorithm that drops out is the simplest thing possible: projected gradient descent on F_γ, the penalty gradient taken through a retained autodiff graph, γ ramped from 0 to a finite cap, the joint step rescaled by min(1/γ,1) to stay stable as γ grows, separate block step sizes for the data-weights x and the model y, and an explicit box projection on x where the upper level is constrained.
