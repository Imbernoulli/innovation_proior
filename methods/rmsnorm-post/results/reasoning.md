Let me start from what is actually breaking. I am trying to train a very deep transformer — dozens of layers, billions of parameters — and to fit it in memory and run it fast I am doing the arithmetic in 16-bit. And it dies. Within the first thousand iterations the loss goes to NaN. Not slowly, not as a gradual divergence I could chase with a smaller learning rate, but a hard NaN, which in FP16 means something overflowed: some value climbed past the roughly 6.5×10^4 ceiling of the format, became infinity, and then any statistic computed from it — a sum, a variance — became NaN and poisoned everything downstream. So this is not a "tune the learning rate" problem in the usual sense. Something in the forward pass is manufacturing enormous numbers, and I need to find out where and why before I can stop it.

The block I am using is the standard pre-normalized one, because that is what everyone uses now and for a good reason. Let me recall exactly why pre-normalization won, because the reason is going to matter. The original transformer put the LayerNorm *after* the residual addition: x ← LN(x + Attn(x)), then x ← LN(x + MLP(x)). Post-LN. The trouble with that, made precise by the mean-field analysis of Xiong and colleagues, is the gradient at initialization. When the norm sits on the residual *sum*, the expected gradient norm of the parameters near the output layer comes out to O(d√(ln d)), and crucially that is *independent of depth* — it stays large no matter how many layers you stack. A large gradient on the output parameters from step one means you cannot just turn on a big learning rate; you have to creep up to it with a warmup schedule, and the whole thing is delicate. Moving the norm *inside* the residual branch fixes this: x ← x + Sublayer(LN(x)). Now the residual path is a clean identity highway, the sublayer always reads a freshly normalized copy of the stream, and the same analysis gives a last-layer gradient of O(d√(ln d / L)) — it actually *shrinks* with depth, it is well-behaved at initialization, and you can drop the warmup. That is why pre-normalization is the default. I do not want to give that up. Whatever I do has to keep the gradients healthy at init.

But here is the thing I had been treating as a pure win and now need to look at more carefully. The same analysis that gives me the gradient result also tells me what happens to the *forward* activations, and the two placements behave oppositely there. In Post-LN, the residual sum just before the layer-end normalization has constant expected squared norm — E[‖x_l^{post,5}‖²] = (3/2)d — and then the normalization projects the state itself back to scale √d. In Pre-LN it is not constant. The analysis gives bounds (1 + l/2)d ≤ E[‖x_l‖²] ≤ (1 + 3l/2)d — the squared norm of the residual stream *grows linearly with the layer index l*. And of course it does, when I think about the mechanism instead of the formula. The residual path in pre-normalization is never normalized — that was the whole point, that is what gives me the gradient benefit. So whatever each sublayer outputs gets added onto the stream raw, and it stays. Layer after layer, raw contributions pile up on an identity highway with nothing rescaling it. The norm only ever touches the *copy* that goes into a sublayer; it never touches the highway itself.

So I have a stream whose typical scale grows with depth, and I have FP16 with a hard ceiling, and I have a NaN. That is starting to feel like the same fact three times. But before I commit to that story I should put numbers on it, because linear growth in the *expected* norm, on its own, might be gentle. Take the upper bound (1 + 3l/2)d at l = 60 layers and d ≈ 2000: that is (1 + 90)·2000 ≈ 1.8×10^5 in squared norm. Spread over d coordinates that is a mean square of ~91 per coordinate, a per-coordinate scale of about √91 ≈ 9.5 — order ten. FP16's ceiling is 6.5×10^4. So the *average* coordinate, even at 60 layers, sits five thousand-fold below the ceiling. The expected-norm bound, on its own, does not overflow anything. It is an average over coordinates and over the random init, and it is not what kills me. What kills me has to be the *worst* coordinate, and the growth there must be doing something more violent than the additive accumulation the bound describes. Let me actually chase that, because "values explode in deep layers" is the observation and I need the why.

Write the recursion for the stream. x_{l+1} = x_l + b_l, where b_l is the output of the sublayer branch on this layer, b_l = Sublayer(LN(x_l)). Take the variance, coordinate-wise, treating it loosely: Var(x_{l+1}) = Var(x_l) + Var(b_l) + 2·Cov(x_l, b_l). If b_l were a fixed-scale, zero-mean perturbation uncorrelated with the stream, the covariance term would vanish and this would be Var(x_l) + const — the gentle linear growth, the additive case. But b_l is not independent of x_l: it is *computed from* x_l. Once training has moved the weights even a little, b_l can be positively correlated with x_l along whatever directions the layer has learned to amplify, so that covariance term is positive and the recursion behaves less like Var(x_l) + const and more like Var(x_l)·(1 + g) for some g > 0 — geometric, compounding.

Let me see whether that distinction is actually enough to reach the FP16 ceiling, because "compounding" is only scary if the numbers say so. Track the worst coordinate's *scale* s_l (its standard deviation) instead of the whole vector, since that is the thing that overflows. Give it a realistic starting point: a normalization layer emits coordinates at scale ~√d, and an outlier channel rides at scale 10^1–10^2, so set s_0 = 10. Run the two recursions out to a deep stack, L = 64. Additive: s_{l+1} = √(s_l² + c) with a per-layer added variance c; take c = 100 (a branch contributing at scale 10 in that coordinate). After 64 layers that gives s ≈ √(100 + 64·100) ≈ √6500 ≈ 80 — never within three orders of magnitude of 6.5×10^4. Geometric: s_{l+1} = s_l·(1 + g). With g = 0.10, s_0·(1.1)^64 = 10·456 ≈ 4.5×10^3 — large but survives. With g = 0.15, 10·(1.15)^64 ≈ 7.7×10^4, and stepping through the layers it crosses 6.5×10^4 at layer 63. Solving for the exact threshold, s_0·(1+g*)^64 = 6.5×10^4 gives (1+g*) = 6500^{1/64}, so g* ≈ 0.147 — a per-layer amplification of about 15% is all it takes to walk an outlier coordinate from scale 10 across the FP16 ceiling within a 64-layer stack, while the additive recursion under the same start ends near 80. So the additive picture genuinely cannot overflow and a compounding factor a few percent above 1 genuinely can: the gap between the expected-norm story and the NaN is precisely the covariance term. That is the mechanism I need to kill — not the additive accumulation, but the multiplicative one.

Now, *why* would a branch output be correlated with, and as large as, the stream in particular directions? Look at what the branch actually produces. The branch starts with LN(x_l). The output of a normalization layer, coordinate by coordinate, is the standardized input times a learned gain. Standardized means unit variance per coordinate, so before the gain the vector has L2 norm on the order of √d — for d in the thousands, a norm of order tens. So normalization does not produce small numbers; it produces numbers of scale √d. Then that vector goes through the sublayer's linear projections, the attention output projection or the MLP down-projection. Those projections preserve and reshape scale, and transformers are known to develop a few activation dimensions that are persistently much larger than the rest — outlier channels. The projection reproduces those large directions in its output, so b_l is large precisely in a handful of coordinates, at scale 10^1 to 10^2. Add b_l back onto the highway: the stream is now larger in exactly those coordinates. Next layer, the LN sees a stream that is larger in those directions; even though LN restandardizes the *copy*, the gain and the learned projection again reproduce and re-amplify those same directions, so b_{l+1} is larger there too, and gets added on top. The large directions feed themselves. It is a positive feedback loop running down the depth of the network, and the un-normalized residual add is the wire that closes the loop. After enough layers the worst coordinate crosses the FP16 ceiling, the next normalization's variance overflows, and I get my NaN.

Now I can see the shape of the problem cleanly. Pre-normalization gives me good gradients *because* the residual path is un-normalized, and it gives me forward explosion *because* the residual path is un-normalized. Same property, two consequences. So the naive fixes are both wrong. If I go back to Post-LN — normalize the residual sum — I do flatten the forward scale (that is exactly why the layer-end residual sum and the normalized state do not grow with depth), but I am right back to the O(d√(ln d)) output-layer gradients and the warmup-dependent, delicate optimization that drove everyone to pre-normalization. And if I just keep Pre-LN and lower the learning rate or shrink the init, I am fighting an exponential with a constant; I might delay the overflow by a few hundred iterations but I have not removed the feedback loop. I need something that breaks the loop without normalizing the identity highway.

Let me think about where in the loop I can intervene. The loop is: large directions in the stream → LN copies them out at scale √d → sublayer projects them to a large branch output b_l → b_l added raw to the stream → stream larger in those directions → repeat. There are really only a few places to cut. I cannot touch the stream itself (that is the identity path I am protecting). I cannot remove the pre-sublayer LN (that is what gives me the normalized input and the gradient health). The attention and MLP internals are off-limits — I am not allowed to change what they compute, and anyway the problem is not inside them, it is that their *output* is large and gets re-injected. So the one place left is the branch output b_l, in the gap between the sublayer and the residual add. That is the wire that closes the loop. What if I normalize *there*?

Put a normalization on the branch output, just before it rejoins the stream: x_{l+1} = x_l + Norm(Sublayer(LN(x_l))). Let me check what this does to the recursion, because that is the only thing that matters. The added term is now Norm(b_l) instead of b_l. A normalized vector, times a learned gain g initialized to 1, has a mean-square scale set by the gain alone — call the branch contribution β₀ — *regardless* of how large b_l was before normalization. That is the structural change: in the bare recursion the added variance Var(b_l) and the covariance Cov(x_l, b_l) both scaled *with* the branch's raw magnitude, which is how an outlier coordinate fed itself; here the branch magnitude is divided out before the add, so the contribution is β₀ no matter what the sublayer emitted. Feed that back into the same coordinate-scale recursion I just ran. The worst-coordinate update is no longer s_{l+1} = s_l·(1 + g) with g sourced from the branch's own size; it is s_{l+1} = √(s_l² + β₀), the additive form. With β₀ of order the gain (start it at 1) and L = 64, s ends near √(s_0² + 64) — order ten, not 10^4. The 15%-per-layer multiplicative walk that crossed the ceiling cannot happen, because the factor that drove it was the raw branch scale and that scale never reaches the stream now. The branch can no longer hand a raw 10^2 sublayer output to the highway; whatever the sublayer produces, large or small, is rescaled to β₀ before it is added. The growth that remains is the same benign additive accumulation the expected-norm bound suggested in the first place, now *enforced* on the branch contribution rather than hoped for, instead of the geometric one that was actually killing me.

But wait — did I just reinvent Post-LN? Let me be careful, because they look superficially similar and the difference is the entire point. Post-LN normalizes the residual *sum*: x ← LN(x + b). My move normalizes the *branch output* and then adds it to an un-normalized stream: x ← x + Norm(b). The identity highway in my version is still a clean, un-normalized skip from x_l straight to x_{l+1}; the norm sits inside the branch, not across the addition. That distinction is exactly the one Xiong's gradient analysis cares about. The reason Post-LN has the bad O(d√(ln d)) output gradient is that the norm is *on the residual path* — the gradient flowing back through the identity connection has to pass through a normalization at every layer. In my version the gradient flowing back along the identity highway passes through *nothing*; it is a pure skip, just like Pre-LN. The extra norm is only in the branch, off to the side. So I should keep the gradient health of Pre-LN, not inherit the gradient pathology of Post-LN. I get the forward-scale control of Post-LN and the gradient behavior of Pre-LN, because I normalized the branch rather than the sum.

I want to check the gradient story on the backward pass too, not just assert it, because forward control bought with a backward blowup would be no bargain. Take the simplest version of the branch-end map to keep the algebra honest: y = a / ‖a‖ (RMS up to the √n constant, gain held at 1). Differentiate one output coordinate by one input coordinate. ∂y_i/∂a_j = δ_ij/‖a‖ − a_i a_j / ‖a‖³. The first term carries the 1/‖a‖ I was hoping for; the second, written as −(a_i/‖a‖)(a_j/‖a‖)/‖a‖ = −ŷ_i ŷ_j/‖a‖, is the unit vector ŷ outer-producted with itself, also divided by ‖a‖. Both terms have a 1/‖a‖ out front. So the entire Jacobian scales as 1/‖a‖: the upstream gradient that reaches the branch's parameters is divided by exactly the magnitude the branch produced. When a branch starts to misbehave and emits a huge a, the gradient back into that branch's weights is *shrunk* by 1/‖a‖, which damps the very weights that were inflating it. Contrast plain Pre-LN, where there is no branch-end norm: the gradient through the output projection scales *with* the activation magnitude, so a huge forward activation means a huge gradient, the backward face of the same instability. So the branch-end norm does not just rescale the forward values; the same division that controls the forward scale appears in the backward Jacobian and reduces the gradient through a misbehaving branch instead of letting it grow with the activation. Stabilizing on both passes, and doing it inside the branch where I want it, not on the identity highway where it would cost me.

So now I have a norm in *two* places around each sublayer: one before it (the pre-norm I kept for the input/gradient health) and one after it on the branch output (the post-norm I just added to control the forward scale). A sublayer wrapped in normalization on both sides. The pre-norm earns the well-behaved-at-init gradient; the post-norm earns controlled branch contributions; and because the post-norm is on the branch and not the sum, adding it does not cost me the gradient property the pre-norm bought. Two norms per sublayer, the sublayer in the middle. The whole block, written out, is:

  x ← x + Norm_post1( Attn( Norm_pre1(x) ) )
  x ← x + Norm_post2( MLP ( Norm_pre2(x) ) )

Let me make sure I have not broken anything at initialization, because I added two new norms per block and I do not want them to perturb the carefully-set init forward map in a bad direction. The learned gain in each norm is initialized to 1 and there is no bias, so at init Norm_post maps the branch output to unit RMS scale. Does inserting that normalization on the branch output change the *expected* forward behavior at init relative to plain Pre-LN? At init the branch output is already a roughly zero-mean, moderate-scale vector — there is no explosion *yet*, the explosion is a training-time phenomenon that needs the weights to have learned to amplify directions. So at init Norm_post is acting on a well-behaved vector and reparameterizes its magnitude to a controlled scale. It does not introduce a depth-dependent factor and it does not touch the identity path. So convergence should be essentially unaffected: I am not changing what the network can represent, only controlling how large the branch contributions are allowed to get before they rejoin the stream, subject to the learned gain. And the effect I am after is precisely where the failure was — deep, large-scale, low-precision training that previously overflowed should now keep the maximum activation away from the 10^4-10^5 range instead of letting it climb freely. That is what I would want to confirm: that the controlled branch-scale recursion I derived actually keeps the training from NaN-ing, at negligible cost to the loss it reaches.

Now, separately from where the norms sit, there is the question of *which* normalization op to use, and this is an orthogonal choice I can make on its own merits. I have just committed to four norm calls per block instead of two — two pre, two post — so the per-call cost of normalization now matters twice as much as it did. That makes me look hard at LayerNorm and ask whether I am paying for something I do not need. LayerNorm does two things: it subtracts the mean (re-centering) and divides by the standard deviation (re-scaling). Each call is two reductions over the channel dimension — one for the mean, one for the variance — plus an elementwise subtraction. The standard story is that both invariances matter. But let me question the re-centering. What does subtracting the mean actually buy me in terms of *scale control*, which is the entire job I am asking these norms to do? The claim I want to test is that subtracting a constant from every coordinate shifts the vector but does not change its variance. That is checkable directly. Take a concrete channel vector a = [10.29, 6.20, 7.94, 11.72, 10.60, 2.07, 7.85, 4.55], mean μ ≈ 7.65. Its variance is Var(a) ≈ 9.413. Now subtract the mean and recompute: a − μ has the same pairwise differences, every coordinate moved by the same 7.65, and Var(a − μ) ≈ 9.413 — identical to six figures. Variance is defined as the mean squared deviation *from the mean*, and shifting every entry moves the mean with them, so the deviations are untouched. Re-centering provably leaves the spread alone, and the same argument applies to the gradients' variance. So the thing that controls the spread — that keeps activations and gradients from growing — is the *division by the spread*, the re-scaling, not the subtraction. Re-centering just slides the cloud around; re-scaling is what shrinks it to a fixed size. If the re-scaling is what does the stabilizing work, then re-centering is dispensable, and I can drop the mean entirely.

So normalize by the root mean square alone:

  ā_i = a_i / RMS(a) · g_i,   RMS(a) = sqrt( (1/n) Σ_{i=1}^n a_i² ),

with a learned per-channel gain g (init 1) and no bias — there is no mean being removed, so there is no shift to restore, so there is nothing for a bias to do here. Before I trust this as a drop-in I should confirm it actually reduces to LayerNorm where it should: when the input already has zero mean, RMS(a) = sqrt((1/n)Σ a_i²) is supposed to be exactly the standard deviation. Check it on the centered version of my vector above, b = a − μ (mean now zero): RMS(b) = √(mean(b²)) ≈ 3.068, and std(b) (population, ddof 0) ≈ 3.068 — equal. And dividing b by each gives the same normalized vector to machine precision, so RMSNorm(b) and LayerNorm(b) are literally the same map on a zero-mean input. So I am not changing behavior in the regime LayerNorm was already in; I am only dropping the part that handles a nonzero mean. And I keep the property I actually care about: for a positive scale α, RMS(αa) = √(mean(α²a²)) = |α|·RMS(a), and for α > 0 the |α| is just α, so dividing by it makes the output invariant to positive rescaling of the input or the whole weight matrix — the α cancels top and bottom. I checked the sign case too: at α = −3, RMS(αa) still equals 3·RMS(a) (the square kills the sign), but the numerator αa keeps the minus, so the normalized output flips sign rather than staying fixed. So the usual invariance claim is the positive scale case — exactly the activation- and weight-magnitude changes I care about — and the denominator's |α| is the honest statement. That is the re-scaling invariance, the part I argued is load-bearing, and it survives intact. What I give up is invariance to shifts and to per-weight-vector rescaling, which I just argued are not what controls scale. There is even a gradient bonus: the weight gradient under RMSNorm is invariant to input scaling and inversely correlated with weight scale for positive scaling — bigger weights get smaller gradients — which is an implicit per-layer learning-rate adaptor, exactly the kind of scale self-correction that fits a method whose whole purpose is keeping scales in line. Cost-wise, RMSNorm is one reduction (the sum of squares) and a reciprocal-square-root, versus LayerNorm's two reductions and a subtraction. With four of these per block, that saving is real and recurring, and I lose nothing on the stability I was buying them for.

A couple of numerical details I have to get right, because the entire motivation was FP16 overflow and I must not reintroduce it inside the norm itself. The reduction — the sum of squares — should be computed in fp32 even when the activations are fp16, because squaring fp16 values and summing thousands of them is exactly the kind of operation that loses significance or overflows in the narrow format; do the mean-of-squares in float, take the reciprocal square root, then cast the normalized activation back before applying the gain. And I need an eps inside the square root: rsqrt(mean(x²) + eps), so that a degenerate all-zero row does not divide by zero. A small constant, 1e-5, sized well below any healthy activation RMS so it floors the pathological case without perturbing the normal one. The gain g is initialized to ones so the normalized scale starts at unit gain.

Let me now write the two pieces as code, filling the two open slots — the normalization module and the block wiring. In the actual edit surface I keep the scaffold class name `LayerNorm` and the signature `(ndim, bias)`, even though the internals are now RMS normalization and the `bias` argument is ignored; that way the block stack and the final norm are both replaced without touching the surrounding GPT code. The attention and MLP modules are untouched — I only change what wraps them.

```python
import torch
import torch.nn as nn


class LayerNorm(nn.Module):
    """RMSNorm internals, kept under the scaffold's LayerNorm name."""

    def __init__(self, ndim, bias):
        super().__init__()
        self.weight = nn.Parameter(torch.ones(ndim))   # gain g, init 1
        self.eps = 1e-5                                  # floor for an all-zero row

    def forward(self, x):
        # reduce in fp32: squaring + summing fp16 over a wide channel dim loses/overflows
        rms = x.float().pow(2).mean(-1, keepdim=True).add(self.eps).rsqrt()  # 1 / sqrt(mean(x^2)+eps)
        return (x * rms).type_as(x) * self.weight       # cast normalized activation back, then apply gain


class CausalSelfAttention(nn.Module):
    """Existing multi-head causal self-attention sublayer. Unchanged."""
    def __init__(self, config):
        super().__init__()
        ...
    def forward(self, x):
        ...


class MLP(nn.Module):
    """Existing position-wise feed-forward sublayer. Unchanged."""
    def __init__(self, config):
        super().__init__()
        ...
    def forward(self, x):
        ...


class Block(nn.Module):
    """A sublayer is wrapped in a norm on BOTH sides:
    a pre-norm (keeps Pre-LN's normalized input and well-behaved-at-init gradients)
    and a post-norm on the branch OUTPUT before the residual add (controls the forward
    scale of what rejoins the un-normalized identity stream, weakening the
    layer-by-layer value aggravation). The identity residual path stays
    un-normalized, so the gradient skip is intact -- this is NOT Post-LN
    (which would normalize the residual sum and reintroduce the large output
    gradients)."""

    def __init__(self, config):
        super().__init__()
        self.ln_pre1  = LayerNorm(config.n_embd, bias=config.bias)   # before attention
        self.attn     = CausalSelfAttention(config)
        self.ln_post1 = LayerNorm(config.n_embd, bias=config.bias)   # on attention's branch output
        self.ln_pre2  = LayerNorm(config.n_embd, bias=config.bias)   # before MLP
        self.mlp      = MLP(config)
        self.ln_post2 = LayerNorm(config.n_embd, bias=config.bias)   # on the MLP's branch output

    def forward(self, x):
        # x + Norm_post( Attn( Norm_pre(x) ) ): branch normalized before rejoining x
        x = x + self.ln_post1(self.attn(self.ln_pre1(x)))
        x = x + self.ln_post2(self.mlp(self.ln_pre2(x)))
        return x
```

Let me retrace the whole chain to be sure it holds together. I started with a deep transformer NaN-ing in FP16 — a hard overflow, not a tunable divergence. The block was pre-normalized, which I keep, because putting the norm inside the residual branch is what gives the well-behaved-at-init gradients (O(d√(ln d / L)) at the output, shrinking with depth) that let me train deep without warmup; the alternative, Post-LN, normalizes the residual sum and pays a large depth-independent output gradient that needs warmup. But the same un-normalized identity path that earns the gradient benefit also lets every sublayer's raw output accumulate on the stream, so the forward scale grows with depth, and — because branch outputs are correlated with the directions the stream is already large in, and because normalization emits vectors at scale √d that the projections reproduce in a few persistent outlier directions — that growth compounds layer over layer into the 10^4-10^5 range and overflows. I could not normalize the highway (that is the gradient path) or remove the pre-norm or touch the sublayer internals, so I cut the feedback loop at the only remaining wire: the branch output, just before it rejoins the stream. Normalizing the branch output turns the compounding Var(x_l)·(1+...) recursion into the benign additive Var(x_l)+β₀, controlled linear growth rather than geometric growth, while leaving the identity skip un-normalized so the gradient stays a clean pass — the forward-scale control of Post-LN with the gradient health of Pre-LN, achieved by normalizing the branch rather than the sum, and the branch-end norm even damps the backward pass by 1/‖a‖ so a misbehaving branch has its gradient reduced. That leaves a sublayer wrapped in a norm on both sides — pre for input/gradient health, post for forward-scale control. Then, since I now run four norms per block, I made the orthogonal choice to drop re-centering: the mean-subtraction shifts but does not control spread, so RMSNorm — divide by the root mean square, learned gain, no bias, fp32 reduction, eps floor — gives the same scale control at one reduction instead of two, and equals LayerNorm whenever the input mean is already zero. The result drops into the existing decoder by replacing the norm op and the block wiring, with the embeddings, stack, final norm, head, optimizer, and schedule all untouched.
