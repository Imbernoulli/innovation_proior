Let me get the claim straight before I do anything clever with it. I have positive integers $a,b$ with $ab+1$ dividing $a^2+b^2$, so $q=(a^2+b^2)/(ab+1)$ is an honest positive integer, and I'm told to prove $q$ is always a perfect square. My first reaction is that this can't be forced — a divisibility between two free parameters normally lets the quotient be anything. So either I'm missing some rigidity hiding in the symmetry of $a^2+b^2$ against $ab+1$, or the claim is just false and I should hunt for a counterexample. Either way the honest move is to compute, not to theorize.

So let me tabulate. I want pairs where $ab+1$ divides $a^2+b^2$. The cheapest admissible pairs are the ones with a zero coordinate: $b=0$ gives $q=a^2/1=a^2$, automatically a square, so $(1,0)\to1$, $(2,0)\to4$, $(3,0)\to9$. Those are degenerate, but let me allow them; they might be telling me something. Now the genuinely two-sided ones. $(1,1)$: $a^2+b^2=2$, $ab+1=2$, $q=1$. $(2,8)$? $a^2+b^2=68$, $ab+1=17$, $68/17=4$. That's a strong rigidity. I want to understand where it comes from.

Stare at the data a little longer, because there's more structure than just "always square." Group the pairs by their $q$-value. The $q=4$ pairs, written with the smaller coordinate first: $(0,2),(2,8),(8,30),(30,112),(112,418),\dots$ — and look, the second coordinate of each pair is the first coordinate of the next. They're not separate solutions at all; they're *consecutive terms of a single sequence* $0,2,8,30,112,418,\dots$. Same for $q=9$: $0,3,27,240,\dots$. So for a fixed square value of $q$, the admissible pairs are adjacent terms of one fast-growing sequence. Let me find the recurrence. For $q=4$: $8=4\cdot2-0$, $30=4\cdot8-2$, $112=4\cdot30-8$. So $T_n=4T_{n-1}-T_{n-2}$. For $q=9$: $27=9\cdot3-0$, $240=9\cdot27-3$. So $T_n=9T_{n-1}-T_{n-2}$. In both cases the multiplier is exactly $q$ itself: $T_n=q\,T_{n-1}-T_{n-2}$.

That's the whole problem talking to me. If the admissible pairs for a given $q$ are governed by $T_n = q\,T_{n-1} - T_{n-2}$, then given any solution $(a,b)$ — say $b$ is the larger, $b=T_n$, $a=T_{n-1}$ — the recurrence both pushes me forward to a bigger solution $(b,\,qb-a)$ and pulls me *backward* to a smaller one. Rearranged, $T_{n-2}=q\,T_{n-1}-T_n$, so the solution before $(a,b)$ is $(\,qa-b,\;a\,)$ — wait, let me be careful with which coordinate is which. If consecutive terms are $(T_{n-1},T_n)=(a,b)$ with $a<b$, then the previous pair is $(T_{n-2},T_{n-1})=(qa-b,\;a)$. The new smaller coordinate is $qa-b$, paired with the old smaller coordinate $a$. And if I keep stepping backward, the terms shrink — they're positive and strictly decreasing — so the sequence bottoms out at $T_0=0$, paired with $T_1=$ some $r$. And at that bottom, the pair is $(0,r)$, whose quotient is $r^2/1=r^2$. A perfect square. The square isn't an accident I have to explain pair by pair; it's just the *value of $q$ at the bottom of the ladder*, and the ladder preserves $q$.

That's the picture. Now I have to turn "the data looks like a linear recurrence" into a proof, because right now I've only *observed* the recurrence on examples; I haven't shown that from a genuine algebraic solution I can always produce the predecessor and that it's a legitimate solution with the same $q$. Let me first try the route I'd normally try for a divisibility-of-sums-of-squares problem, just to be sure I'm not overcomplicating, and watch it fail.

The instinct is Gaussian integers: $a^2+b^2=(a+bi)(a-bi)$ in $\mathbb Z[i]$, and divisibility of sums of two squares is governed by which primes are $1\bmod4$ versus $3\bmod4$. So maybe I factor $ab+1$ into Gaussian primes and match them against $(a+bi)(a-bi)$. I start pushing this and it goes nowhere. The trouble is the divisor $ab+1$ — it's a *mixed* quadratic in both $a$ and $b$, it does not factor over $\mathbb Z[i]$ in any way that talks to the factorization of $a^2+b^2$, and the prime-by-prime bookkeeping just doesn't close. I'm tracking Gaussian primes of one expression against a number that has no compatible factorization, and I never get a statement about the quotient. Dead end. The factorization structure of $a^2+b^2$ is real but it's the wrong handle, because the constraint isn't really "primes of $a^2+b^2$" — it's a *relation between two integers on a curve*. So drop $\mathbb Z[i]$ entirely.

Back to the curve. Clear the denominator: $ab+1$ divides $a^2+b^2$ with quotient $q$ means

$$a^2+b^2=q(ab+1)=q\,ab+q,$$

i.e.

$$a^2-q\,ab+b^2-q=0.$$

This is a single polynomial equation in $a$ and $b$ with $q$ a constant. It's a conic — symmetric in $a,b$, a hyperbola for $q$ large — and the admissible pairs are its lattice points in the first quadrant. The recurrence I saw is surely a symmetry of this conic permuting its lattice points. So fix the value of $q$ for the rest of the argument; I'm now studying *one* conic at a time and asking about its integer points.

Here's the thing I want to exploit. The equation is quadratic, and it's quadratic *separately in each variable*. So fix $b$ (and $q$), and read the relation as a quadratic in the single variable $a$:

$$a^2-(qb)\,a+(b^2-q)=0.$$

My given $a$ is one root of this. But a quadratic has *two* roots. Call the other one $a'$. What is it? This is exactly the situation where the sum and product of roots hand you the second root for free. The sum of the two roots equals the coefficient $qb$, so

$$a+a'=qb\quad\Longrightarrow\quad a'=qb-a.$$

And the product of the roots equals the constant term $b^2-q$, so

$$a\,a'=b^2-q\quad\Longrightarrow\quad a'=\frac{b^2-q}{a}.$$

Now look at what these two expressions for the *same* number $a'$ are telling me. The first one, $a'=qb-a$, is an integer minus an integer times an integer — so $a'$ **is an integer**. The second one, $a'=(b^2-q)/a$, I'll use to control its sign and size. And here's the payoff I was groping toward with the recurrence: $(a',b)$ is *also a solution* of $a^2-q\,ab+b^2-q=0$ with the *same* $q$, because $a'$ is the other root of the very quadratic I built from that equation. I've produced a new admissible pair, with the same quotient, by replacing one coordinate with the partner root. This is the predecessor step the data was showing me — $a'=qb-a$ is exactly the backward recurrence — but now I have it as an algebraic fact, not an observed pattern. And it's symmetric: I could equally fix $a$ and flip $b\mapsto qa-b$. Each solution has two neighbors on the conic, gotten by flipping either coordinate to its partner root.

So I have a move that, given any solution, manufactures another solution with the same $q$. To make this a *descent* I need the move to make things smaller, and I need to control the new coordinate so it doesn't escape into negatives. Let me set it up as a clean descent and chase every detail, because the whole proof lives in whether $a'$ stays non-negative and strictly smaller.

Order the coordinates: suppose $a>b\ge1$ (if $a=b$ I'll check that case separately, and if a coordinate is already $0$ I'm at the bottom). I want to flip the *larger* coordinate, $a$, down to $a'=qb-a=(b^2-q)/a$, keeping $b$, and claim $(b,a')$ is a strictly smaller solution. Three things to verify: $a'$ is an integer, $a'$ is non-negative, and the flip genuinely decreases.

$a'$ integer: done, $a'=qb-a$.

$a'<b$ (so the larger coordinate really shrank below the other): from $a'=(b^2-q)/a$ and $q>0$,

$$a'=\frac{b^2-q}{a}<\frac{b^2}{a}<b<a,$$

using $q>0$ in the first strict inequality and $b<a$ in the second. In particular the weaker bookkeeping inequality $a'<b^2/a\le a$ also holds, but the useful fact is stronger: $0\le a'<b<a$ once the sign is settled. The new pair $(a',b)$ has smaller maximum coordinate and smaller sum, $a'+b<a+b$. That's the strict decrease I need for descent.

Now the dangerous one: could $a'$ be *negative*? $a'=(b^2-q)/a$, and if $q>b^2$ this is negative, so I can't just wave it away — I have to rule it out using that $(a',b)$ is a *solution*. Suppose, for contradiction, $a'<0$. The pair $(a',b)$ satisfies $q=(a'^2+b^2)/(a'b+1)$, i.e. $a'^2+b^2=q(a'b+1)$. The left side $a'^2+b^2$ is strictly positive (it's a sum of squares with $b\ge1$). The right side is $q\cdot(a'b+1)$ with $q>0$. For a positive number to equal $q$ times $(a'b+1)$, I need $a'b+1>0$, that is $a'b>-1$. But if $a'<0$ and $b\ge1$ then $a'\le-1$, so $a'b\le-b\le-1$, giving $a'b+1\le0$ — and then $q(a'b+1)\le0<a'^2+b^2$, a contradiction. So $a'<0$ is impossible. Hence $a'\ge0$. The sign is forced not by the formula $(b^2-q)/a$ — which looked like it could go negative — but by the fact that the *flipped pair is still a point on the conic with positive quotient*, and a sum of two squares can't be a positive multiple of a non-positive number. That's the subtle step, and it's the one that makes the whole thing work.

Let me now assemble it as a descent, two ways, because the data suggested a clean direct descent and I also want the airtight contradiction form.

Direct descent. Take any admissible pair; I'll show $q$ is a square by descending on, say, the product $ab$ (or equivalently the larger coordinate). If a coordinate is $0$, I'm done immediately: the quotient is the square of the surviving coordinate — that's the base case. Otherwise both coordinates are $\ge1$; WLOG $a>b$ (if $a=b$, then $a^2\mid 2a^2$ forces $a^2\mid$ the relation tightly — concretely $a=b$ gives $q=2a^2/(a^2+1)$, which is a positive integer only when $a^2+1\mid 2a^2=2(a^2+1)-2$, so $a^2+1\mid2$, so $a=b=1$ and $q=1$, a square; that diagonal case is finished). With $a>b\ge1$, flip the larger coordinate: replace $(a,b)$ by $(a',b)=(qb-a,\,b)$. By the three facts just proved, $a'$ is a non-negative integer, $a'<b<a$, so this is a *genuinely smaller* admissible pair with the *same* $q$. The strictly decreasing positive-integer measure can't decrease forever, so after finitely many flips I reach a pair with a $0$ coordinate, and there $q$ equals the square of the surviving coordinate. Since $q$ never changed along the descent, the original $q$ is that same square. Done — and this is precisely the ladder the data drew: each flip is the backward step of $T_n=qT_{n-1}-T_{n-2}$, walking the solution down to $(r,0)$ with $q=r^2$.

I can even read off the bookkeeping the data hinted at. Going *up* instead of down, if the base pair is $(0,r)$ and $q=r^2$, the forward recurrence is the same relation solved the other way:

$$T_n=qT_{n-1}-T_{n-2}.$$

It starts $0,r,qr=r^3,\dots$ and then continues by the recurrence, not by powers. For $q=4$ this gives $0,2,8,30,112,418,\dots$; for $q=9$ it gives $0,3,27,240,\dots$. Swapping coordinates only changes orientation. The solutions of one conic look like a family rooted at the base pair, with adjacent terms connected by this two-term recurrence — exactly the structure I expected from the Markov comparison.

Now the contradiction form, which is cleaner to state and doesn't even need me to identify the base case by name. Suppose, for contradiction, that some non-square positive integer $q$ *does* occur. (At least one such pair exists by assumption, and $a+b$ ranges over positive integers, so a minimum exists by well-ordering.) Since $q$ is non-square, $B$ cannot be $0$, because then $q=A^2$ immediately; hence $B\ge1$. Fix $B$ and flip $A$: the quadratic $x^2-(qB)x+(B^2-q)=0$ has $A$ as one root and the other root is $x_2=qB-A=(B^2-q)/A$. As before, $x_2=qB-A$ is an integer. $$x_2=\frac{B^2-q}{A}<\frac{B^2}{A}\le A,$$

so $x_2<A$, hence $x_2+B<A+B$. But $(x_2,B)$ is an admissible pair with the same $q$ and a strictly smaller coordinate sum — contradicting that $(A,B)$ minimized $a+b$. The descent is the same engine; the contradiction framing just packages it as "a minimal counterexample can always be made smaller, so there is no counterexample."

Both forms hinge on the identical move, so let me make sure I see the *geometry* of it too, because it's what the conic picture was promising. Fix $q$ (take $q>2$ so it's a real hyperbola; $q=1$ is trivially a square, and $q=2$ would need $a^2-2ab+b^2=(a-b)^2=2$, impossible in integers, so those edge values are out of the way). A lattice point $(x,y)$ with $x<y$ sits on one branch; fixing $x$ and flipping the quadratic in $y$ sends $y\mapsto y'=qx-y$, the partner root, which lands the point $(x,y')$ on the *other* branch. The same sign argument as above — from $H$, $1+xy'>0$, and $x>0$ forces $y'\ge0$ — keeps it in the first quadrant. Reflecting across $y=x$ turns $(x,y')$ into $(y',x)$, back on the original branch; and the product-of-roots relation $yy'=x^2-q$ gives $y'=(x^2-q)/y<x$ (since $x<y$), so the reflected point has strictly smaller coordinates. So geometrically the move is: flip across the conic to the other branch, then reflect across $y=x$, and you slide down the same branch to a lattice point nearer the origin. The coordinates are non-negative integers strictly decreasing, so the slide terminates at a point $(0,y)$ on the branch — and substituting $x=0$ into $H$ gives $y^2-q=0$, i.e. $q=y^2$. Same conclusion, now visibly a statement about lattice points marching down a hyperbola to the axis.

*Proof.* Suppose not: let $q$ be a non-square positive integer realized by some admissible pair, and among all such pairs choose $(A,B)$ with $A+B$ minimal, $A\ge B\ge1$. Then $A$ is a root of $x^2-(qB)x+(B^2-q)=0$; let $x_2=qB-A=(B^2-q)/A$ be the other root. From $x_2=qB-A$, $x_2\in\mathbb Z$. From $x_2=(B^2-q)/A$ and $q$ non-square, $x_2\ne0$. Since $(x_2,B)$ satisfies $x_2^2+B^2=q(x_2B+1)$ with the left side positive and $q>0$, we get $x_2B+1>0$, hence $x_2\ge0$; with $x_2\ne0$, $x_2\ge1$.
