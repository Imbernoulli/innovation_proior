Let me start from the thing that actually defeats me when I try to train a really deep convolutional rectifier net from scratch. I stack, say, twenty or thirty conv layers, fill the weights with i.i.d. Gaussian noise at the std everyone copies from the AlexNet recipe — 0.01 — and the run just sits there: the loss doesn't move, and when I look at the gradients in the early layers they're essentially zero. Shallower nets with the same recipe train fine. So the failure is something about depth, and it's there from the very first step, before any learning could have gone wrong — which means it's an *initialization* failure, not an optimization failure. The workarounds people use confirm that diagnosis: the VGG folks pre-train an eight-layer model and use it to seed the deeper one; others hang auxiliary classifiers off the middle of the network to inject gradient closer to the input. Both are admitting the same thing — that with a fixed-std Gaussian, signal or gradient can't survive the trip through that many layers. I don't want a crutch; I want to understand why the trip kills the signal and write down the std that fixes it.

Whatever a single layer does to the *scale* of the signal, it does it again at the next layer, and again, multiplicatively. If each layer multiplies the variance of the forward responses by some factor — call it β — then after L layers the variance is multiplied by β^L. The same is true going backward for the gradients. And β^L is a vicious function: if β is a little above 1, β^L explodes and the activations (or gradients) run off to infinity; if β is a little below 1, β^L collapses toward zero and nothing reaches the early layers. There's no gentle middle for large L — either you blow up or you stall. So the entire game is to make that per-layer factor equal to one. Not approximately, not on average over the run, but *at initialization*, as a property of the random numbers I put in. If I can force the per-layer variance factor to be exactly 1, the product is 1 for any depth, and depth stops being the enemy.

So I need to compute that per-layer factor and set it to one. The natural tool is the variance-propagation analysis — track Var of the responses as they go forward, track Var of the gradients as they go backward, and choose the weight variance to keep each fixed. Glorot and Bengio did exactly this. Let me redo their forward calculation in my own notation for a conv layer, because the details are where the rectifier is going to bite. A response at one output pixel is `y = W x + b`, where `x` is the vector of the co-located `k×k` pixels across the `c` input channels stacked up — so `x` has length `n = k²·c`, the number of incoming connections feeding one response — and `W` is `d×n`, one row per output filter, `d` filters total. Take the weights in a layer to be i.i.d., zero-mean, with a distribution symmetric about zero; take the entries of `x` to be i.i.d. and independent of `W`. Then for a single output element, summing `n` independent product terms, `Var[y] = n·Var[w·x]`. Since `w` is zero-mean and independent of `x`, the variance of the product is `Var[w·x] = E[w²]E[x²] − E[w]²E[x]² = E[w²]E[x²] = Var[w]·E[x²]`. So

  Var[y_l] = n_l · Var[w_l] · E[x_l²].

Now I have to be careful, and this is the exact spot where I can't just copy Glorot. They wrote `E[x²] = Var[x]`. That's only true if `x` has zero mean. But `x_l = f(y_{l−1})`, the activation of the previous layer's response, and my `f` is a rectifier, `x = max(0, y_{l−1})`, which is never negative. Its mean is strictly positive, so `E[x²] ≠ Var[x]`. The rectifier breaks the very identity their derivation leans on. I have to keep `E[x²]` honest and compute it for a ReLU.

What is `E[max(0, y)²]` when `y` is zero-mean and symmetric? Symmetry is the thing to lean on. If `w_{l−1}` is symmetric about zero and the bias `b_{l−1} = 0`, then `y_{l−1} = W_{l−1} x_{l−1}` is a sum of symmetric terms, so `y_{l−1}` itself is symmetric about zero, with as much mass on the negative side as the positive side, mirror images of each other. The rectifier keeps the positive half exactly and zeroes the negative half. So `E[max(0,y)²] = ∫_{0}^{∞} y² p(y) dy`, and because `p` is symmetric, `∫_{0}^{∞} y² p(y) dy = (1/2)∫_{−∞}^{∞} y² p(y) dy = (1/2)E[y²]`. And `E[y²] = Var[y]` because `y` is zero-mean. So

  E[x_l²] = (1/2) Var[y_{l−1}].

That factor of one-half is the whole story. It's not a fudge; it's the literal fraction of its input that the rectifier passes through to the second moment, because it discards exactly half the (symmetric) distribution. Substitute it back:

  Var[y_l] = (1/2) · n_l · Var[w_l] · Var[y_{l−1}].

So the per-layer forward factor is `(1/2) n_l Var[w_l]`, and chaining L of them,

  Var[y_L] = Var[y_1] · ∏_{l=2}^{L} (1/2) n_l Var[w_l].

To stop the exponential, I want every factor in that product to be one. A sufficient condition is

  (1/2) n_l Var[w_l] = 1   for every l,

which solves to `Var[w_l] = 2/n_l`, a zero-mean Gaussian with standard deviation `√(2/n_l)`. And `b = 0`, which I needed anyway for the symmetry argument to hold. There it is, and the factor of two stands out against Glorot, whose linear analysis gives `n Var[w] = 1`, i.e. `√(1/n)`. My std is `√2` times bigger *per layer*. The rectifier ate half the variance at every layer, so I have to put twice as much in to compensate — that's the entire correction, and it's exactly a factor of 2 inside the square root.

I should sanity-check the first layer, because there's no rectifier in front of the raw image. For `l = 1` the input `x_1` is the image, not `max(0, ·)` of anything, so the honest condition there is `n_1 Var[w_1] = 1`, without the half — i.e. there is no factor of 2 for the very first layer. But does that one layer matter? The product runs over L layers; getting one factor wrong by 2 changes the total product by 2, a single constant, not something that compounds with depth. The catastrophe was always β^L; one layer's β being off doesn't make β^L explode or vanish. So I'll just use the same `(1/2) n Var[w] = 1` rule on the first layer too, for uniformity, and accept the harmless single factor of 2.

Now I've only looked forward. The thing that was actually dying in my failed runs was the *gradient* at the early layers, so let me run the same analysis backward and make sure I'm not preserving forward variance while quietly strangling the gradient. The back-propagated gradient through a conv layer is `Δx_l = Ŵ_l Δy_l`, where I write `Δx` for `∂E/∂x` and `Δy` for `∂E/∂y`, and `Ŵ` is the same weights rearranged into the shape back-prop uses — a `c×n̂` matrix, with `n̂ = k²·d`. Notice the asymmetry: forward, a response gathered from `n = k²·c` connections; backward, a gradient at an input pixel gathers from `n̂ = k²·d` connections, because the number of filters `d` plays the role going backward that the number of input channels `c` played going forward. `n̂ ≠ n` in general. This is the fan-out versus fan-in distinction, and I have to respect it.

For the variance, take `w_l` and `Δy_l` independent. With `w` symmetric zero-mean, `Δx_l` comes out zero-mean for every layer. So `Var[Δx_l] = n̂_l Var[w_l] Var[Δy_l]`. But I need `Var[Δy_l]` in terms of the next layer's gradient, and that's where the rectifier reappears, through its *derivative* this time. In back-prop, `Δy_l = f'(y_l) · Δx_{l+1}`. For a ReLU, `f'(y)` is 1 when `y > 0` and 0 when `y < 0`, and by the same symmetry of `y` these happen with probability one-half each. Take `f'(y_l)` independent of `Δx_{l+1}`. Then `E[Δy_l] = E[f'(y_l)]·E[Δx_{l+1}] = (1/2)·0 = 0`, and the second moment is `E[(Δy_l)²] = E[f'(y_l)²]·E[Δx_{l+1}²]`. Since `f'` is 0 or 1, `f'² = f'`, so `E[f'²] = 1/2`. Hence `Var[Δy_l] = (1/2) Var[Δx_{l+1}]`. The same one-half drops out — but for a completely different reason than forward. Forward the half came from the rectifier keeping half the *output mass*; backward it comes from the rectifier's derivative being one on half the inputs and zero on the other half. Two different mechanisms, same `1/2`. Putting it together,

  Var[Δx_l] = (1/2) · n̂_l · Var[w_l] · Var[Δx_{l+1}],

and chaining,

  Var[Δx_2] = Var[Δx_{L+1}] · ∏_{l=2}^{L} (1/2) n̂_l Var[w_l].

So the backward sufficient condition is `(1/2) n̂_l Var[w_l] = 1`, giving std `√(2/n̂_l)`, identical in form to the forward rule but with `n̂_l = k²·d_l` (fan-out) where the forward rule had `n_l = k²·c_l` (fan-in).

Now I'm staring at two conditions that disagree: forward wants `(1/2) n_l Var[w_l] = 1`, backward wants `(1/2) n̂_l Var[w_l] = 1`, and `n_l ≠ n̂_l` unless the channel counts happen to match. I can't satisfy both with one variance per layer. Glorot's resolution was to split the difference, `Var[w] = 2/(n + n̂)` — keep neither variance exactly fixed, keep their average. That's reasonable, but let me ask whether I even need the compromise, because if one of the two conditions alone already keeps the *other* product from diverging, I can just pick one and be done. Suppose I impose the backward condition, `(1/2) n̂_l Var[w_l] = 1` for all l. Then by construction the backward product `∏ (1/2) n̂_l Var[w_l] = 1`. What does that do to the forward product? Substitute `Var[w_l] = 2/n̂_l` into the forward factors:

  ∏_{l=2}^{L} (1/2) n_l Var[w_l] = ∏_{l=2}^{L} (1/2) n_l · (2/n̂_l) = ∏_{l=2}^{L} n_l/n̂_l.

Now `n_l = k_l² c_l = k_l² d_{l−1}` and `n̂_l = k_l² d_l`, so `n_l/n̂_l = d_{l−1}/d_l`, and the product telescopes:

  ∏_{l=2}^{L} d_{l−1}/d_l = d_1/d_L = c_2/d_L.

That's it — the forward product isn't 1, but it's `c_2/d_L`, the ratio of the second layer's input channels to the last layer's filter count. In any normal architecture that's a fixed, modest number, not something that shrinks with depth. So if I scale the weights to preserve the backward signal, the forward signal is automatically off by only this single non-diminishing constant, which can't cause the β^L catastrophe. And by the symmetric argument, imposing the forward condition leaves the backward product equal to `d_L/c_2`, equally harmless. So I don't need Glorot's compromise at all: either condition alone is sufficient to make the network trainable. I'll use the backward form, `√(2/n̂_l)` with the fan-out, for the conv layers — that's the one that directly preserves what was actually dying in my runs, the gradient.

Let me make this concrete on a network I know stalls, to check the size of the effect and that my derivation actually explains the observed failure rather than just sounding good. Take the VGG-style "model B": ten `3×3` conv layers with filter counts 64, 128, 256, 512. My backward rule says the std should be `√(2/n̂) = √(2/(9·d))`, which is `√(2/(9·64)) = 0.059` for the 64-filter layers, then `0.042`, `0.029`, and `0.021` for 128, 256, 512. The fixed recipe uses `0.01` everywhere — several times smaller than what every one of these layers wants. How badly does that compound? The gradient passing back through a layer is scaled by its weight std, so the *std* of the back-propagated gradient picks up one factor of the layer's std per layer. If the matched std should be `s` and I use `0.01` instead, that layer scales the gradient std by `0.01/s` relative to the matched init, and these factors multiply across layers. In units of `0.01`, the matched stds are `5.9` for the 64-filter layers, `4.2` for 128, `2.9` for 256, `2.1` for 512; with model B carrying one 64-, two 128-, two 256-, and four 512-filter layers between conv10 and conv2, the gradient std reaching the early layers under the fixed `0.01` recipe is around `1/(5.9·4.2²·2.9²·2.1⁴) ≈ 1/(1.7×10⁴)` of what the variance-matched init would give. A gradient four orders of magnitude too small at the early layers *is* the "diminishing gradients" people report when deep nets stall. So the mechanism checks out: the fixed std isn't merely suboptimal, it's exponentially wrong, and my rule says by exactly how much.

And it tells me why Xavier — which does scale with the fan — still isn't quite right for these nets. Xavier's std is `√(1/n)`-flavored (linear analysis), mine is `√(2/n)`. Mine is bigger by `√2` per layer, so Xavier is smaller than what the rectifier needs by a factor `1/√2` per layer, i.e. `1/√2^L = 1/2^{L/2}` over L layers. For a 22-layer net that is already about `1/2^11 ≈ 1/2000`; for a 30-layer net it is about `1/2^15 ≈ 1/32768`. A missing constant in one layer becomes a large depth-dependent multiplier. So the `√2` is not cosmetic. It is the amount needed to cancel the rectifier's lost second moment before depth turns that loss into an exponential collapse.

I keep using ReLU, but there's a sibling I should fold in, because the analysis generalizes for almost free and it tells me the rule isn't really "about ReLU" but about the rectifier's *slope*. Consider the leaky/parametric rectifier `f(y) = max(0, y) + a·min(0, y)` — it passes the positive half unchanged and the negative half scaled by `a`, where `a = 0` recovers ReLU and `a = 1` recovers a plain linear unit. Redo `E[x²]` for this. With `y` zero-mean symmetric, the positive half contributes `(1/2)E[y²]` as before, and the negative half is no longer thrown away but scaled by `a`, contributing `a²·(1/2)E[y²]` (squaring brings in `a²`, and again it's half the mass). So `E[x²] = (1/2)(1 + a²)Var[y]`. The forward factor becomes `(1/2)(1 + a²) n_l Var[w_l]`, and setting it to one gives

  (1/2)(1 + a²) n_l Var[w_l] = 1,   i.e.   Var[w_l] = 2/((1 + a²) n_l),

with std `√(2/(1+a²))/√(n_l)`. Beautiful consistency check: `a = 0` gives back my `√(2/n)`; `a = 1` gives `√(1/n)`, which is precisely Glorot's linear result. The linear case is the `a = 1` corner of my own formula — Glorot wasn't wrong, they were solving the `a = 1` special case and applying it where `a = 0`. The backward case generalizes identically to `(1/2)(1 + a²) n̂_l Var[w_l] = 1`. So the right way to think about it: the weight std is `gain / √(fan)`, where the gain is a number that depends only on the nonlinearity's slope — `√2` for ReLU, `√(2/(1+a²))` for slope `a`, `1` for linear/symmetric units — and `fan` is `n` (fan-in) or `n̂` (fan-out) depending on which direction I choose to preserve.

There's a numerical wrinkle worth nailing down before I write code: the variance-preservation I'm imposing means the *input* signal's variance is roughly carried, unchanged, all the way to the last layer. If the input isn't normalized — say pixel values live in `[−128, 128]` — that's a large variance that propagates undimmed to the logits, and the softmax can overflow. Two outs: normalize the input (but that may shift other hyper-parameters), or quietly shrink the signal with a small per-layer factor like `(1/128)^{1/L}` spread across L layers, which only nudges β slightly below 1 and so deflates the propagated magnitude by `1/128` overall without reintroducing the exponential problem. In practice the cheap version is to make the fully-connected stack smaller than `√(2/n)` would say — e.g. std `0.01` on the first two fc layers and `0.001` on the last, all below `√(2/4096)` for 4096-wide layers — so the final magnitude is tamed. That's a practical patch on top of the principled rule for the classifier end, not a change to the variance-matching itself.

Now I want to land this in the actual harness, which hands me a fully built model and asks me to set every parameter in place from its shape alone — no data, no calibration passes. So I walk the modules and apply the rule per type. For a conv weight tensor shaped `[out, in, k, k]`, the fan-in is `in·k²` and the fan-out is `out·k²` — exactly my `n` and `n̂` — and the std is `gain/√fan` with `gain = √2` for ReLU. I want to draw from a zero-mean Gaussian with that std; equivalently, since a library `kaiming_normal_` computes `fan`, calls `calculate_gain`, and samples `normal_(0, std)` with `std = gain/√fan`, I call it with `nonlinearity='relu'` so the gain is exactly `√2`. I'll preserve the *backward* signal on the convs, so `mode='fan_out'`, matching the condition I chose above. For a linear layer, shaped `[out, in]`, fan-in is `in` and fan-out is `out`; there's no real asymmetry to agonize over, and the conventional choice is to preserve the forward variance, so `mode='fan_in'`. Biases come from the derivation: they must be zero, because the whole symmetry-of-`y` argument required `b = 0`. The one module type my variance analysis didn't cover is batch normalization, which sits between conv and the next layer; its job is to renormalize the activations, so at initialization I want its affine transform to be the identity — scale `weight = 1`, shift `bias = 0` — so it neither rescales nor offsets the variance I just carefully set, and lets the conv init speak for itself.

```python
import torch.nn as nn


def initialize_weights(model, config):
    """He/Kaiming initialization for a deep rectifier (ReLU) CNN.

    Data-independent: each tensor's std is set from its shape alone, so neither
    the forward responses nor the backward gradients drift exponentially with depth.
    """
    for m in model.modules():
        if isinstance(m, nn.Conv2d):
            # Var[w] = 2 / fan  =>  std = sqrt(2)/sqrt(fan), the ReLU gain sqrt(2)
            # putting back the factor of 2 the rectifier removed (E[x^2] = 1/2 Var[y]).
            # fan_out = out_channels * k^2 = n_hat: preserves the BACKWARD-pass variance,
            # the quantity that was dying in the early layers.
            nn.init.kaiming_normal_(m.weight, mode='fan_out', nonlinearity='relu')
            if m.bias is not None:
                nn.init.constant_(m.bias, 0)   # b = 0: needed for y to stay zero-mean symmetric
        elif isinstance(m, nn.BatchNorm2d):
            nn.init.constant_(m.weight, 1)     # affine starts as identity so it does not
            nn.init.constant_(m.bias, 0)       # perturb the variance the conv init just set
        elif isinstance(m, nn.Linear):
            # same gain sqrt(2); fan_in = in_features preserves the FORWARD-pass variance.
            nn.init.kaiming_normal_(m.weight, mode='fan_in', nonlinearity='relu')
            if m.bias is not None:
                nn.init.constant_(m.bias, 0)
```

To recap the chain that got me here. Deep rectifier nets refused to train from scratch with a fixed-std Gaussian, and the reason was multiplicative: any per-layer scaling factor β compounds to β^L, so the only stable choice is to force the per-layer variance factor to exactly one. Tracking the forward variance through a conv layer gave the factor `(1/2) n Var[w]`, where the one-half is the rectifier discarding the negative half of its symmetric input (`E[x²] = (1/2)Var[y]`) — the exact step Glorot's linear analysis skips, which is why their `√(1/n)` is short by `√2` per layer. Setting the factor to one gives `Var[w] = 2/n`, std `√(2/n)`. The backward pass produces the same one-half, this time from the rectifier's derivative being one on half its domain, and the same form with the fan-out `n̂ = k²d` in place of the fan-in `n = k²c`; and because imposing either condition leaves the other product equal to the non-diminishing constant `c_2/d_L`, one condition alone suffices, so I preserve the backward signal on convs. The leaky/parametric generalization gives `Var[w] = 2/((1+a²)n)`, with `a = 1` collapsing exactly to Glorot's linear formula — confirming the rule is "gain over root-fan" where the gain encodes the slope. Biases are zero because the symmetry argument demands it; batch-norm affine starts at identity so it doesn't disturb the matched variance; the last fc layers can be shrunk a touch to keep an unnormalized input from overflowing the softmax. The result is a one-shot, shape-only initializer that holds the signal variance near constant across arbitrary depth, so a very deep rectifier network can be trained directly from scratch.
