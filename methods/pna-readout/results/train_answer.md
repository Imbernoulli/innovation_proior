The thing that bothers me about message passing is that every layer reduces a node's neighbor multiset to one vector through a *single* aggregator — a mean, a sum, a max — chosen by intuition or by what worked last time. The expressivity story everyone trusts says a sum with a learned per-element map is injective on the neighbor multiset and makes the network as strong as the 1-Weisfeiler-Lehman test, the ceiling for the whole message-passing family, so "use sum and stop worrying" looks like settled advice. But that argument rests on a foundation it quietly assumes: the node features come from a *countable* universe — discrete labels — and the proof is essentially a place-value code that encodes integer multiplicities in a base large enough to avoid carries. The vectors an aggregator actually sees three layers deep are not discrete labels; they are continuous hidden activations in $\mathbb{R}^d$. So "sum is maximal" is a guess wearing a theorem's clothes: it is proved for a countable alphabet and never checked against the continuous multisets we really aggregate. The mean (GCN), the max-pool (GraphSAGE), the attention-weighted mean (GAT) are all in the same position — each is one continuous reduction picked by hand, each is degree-blind or degree-coupled in some fixed way, and the empirical folklore that different tasks favor different aggregators is a hint that no single one is enough.

So I make the question precise and adversarial: can *one* continuous, permutation-invariant map from a size-$n$ multiset of real vectors to one vector ever be injective — never collapse two genuinely different neighborhoods? Strip to scalar features, a multiset of $n$ reals, which is a continuous function on the quotient of $\mathbb{R}^n$ by the symmetric group. The obstruction is topological. Borsuk–Ulam says any continuous map from the $n$-sphere into $\mathbb{R}^n$ identifies some antipodal pair, and the shape generalizes: a continuous map into a space of too low a dimension relative to its domain is forced to glue points together. A single aggregator's scalar output lives in too few dimensions to separate all $n$-element multisets of reals — there are more independent ways an $n$-element continuous multiset can differ than one number can record. Pushing the dimension-counting through gives a sharp conclusion: to discriminate all multisets of size $n$ over $\mathbb{R}$, you need *at least $n$ aggregators*. One is provably insufficient, and the deficit grows with neighborhood size. That is far stronger than "sum is a bit lossy" — it says single-aggregator GNNs are *structurally* unable to tell apart neighborhoods a task may genuinely need separated, and no amount of training repairs a representational collapse. It also retro-explains the folklore: each single aggregator is a different *projection* of the multiset, each loses a different part, so which neighborhoods a model confuses depends on which aggregator it happened to pick.

I propose Principal Neighbourhood Aggregation (PNA). Since $n$ is unbounded in a real graph — I cannot instantiate one aggregator per possible degree — I do not chase exact injectivity. Instead I take a small, fixed set of aggregators whose information *losses are complementary*, so that together they retain far more of the multiset than any one, and let the per-node update MLP recombine them. The operator is built on two orthogonal axes. The first axis is *which statistic*: I enumerate what each candidate keeps and discards and pick a covering set. The mean keeps the *distribution* of neighbor features and throws away absolute count — $k$ copies of a neighborhood and one copy give the same mean, since $\tfrac{1}{kn}\,k\sum = \tfrac{1}{n}\sum$. The max keeps the upper envelope, the coordinatewise *support*, and discards counts and proportions. The min is its mirror image, the lower envelope; on signed continuous features it carries what the max cannot, because the smallest value is not recoverable from the largest. But those three are all *location* statistics — none of them sees how *spread out* a neighborhood is, and two neighborhoods can share a mean, a max, and a min yet differ in dispersion. The natural permutation-invariant statistic for spread is the standard deviation, and it is cheap: the variance is $\mathbb{E}[x^2]-\mathbb{E}[x]^2$, two means I am already most of the way to computing. So the covering set is $\{\mu, \max, \min, \sigma\}$ — center, upper envelope, lower envelope, spread. One numerical caution makes the std safe rather than fragile: $\mathbb{E}[x^2]-\mathbb{E}[x]^2$ is non-negative in exact arithmetic, but floating-point cancellation can drive it slightly negative for a near-constant neighborhood, and then the square root is a NaN that poisons the batch. I clamp the variance non-negative before the root,
$$\sigma = \sqrt{\operatorname{ReLU}\!\big(\mathbb{E}[x^2]-\mathbb{E}[x]^2\big)+\varepsilon},$$
with a small $\varepsilon$ so the gradient is finite at zero spread.

The second axis is not *which* statistic but how the aggregation behaves as a function of *degree*. A sum couples its magnitude to degree: a node with 50 neighbors produces a sum about $50\times$ a node with one, and stacking sum layers makes signal magnitudes grow roughly like a product of degrees down the depth, which explodes in dense regions and destabilizes training — the known reason people retreat to the mean. But the mean overcorrects: it divides degree out *entirely*, so it cannot *use* degree even when degree is structurally informative, and a hub and a leaf play different roles. What I want is a *controllable* dependence on degree — a knob that lets aggregation be amplified, left alone, or attenuated by degree, so the network learns how much degree should matter instead of having "fully" (sum) or "not at all" (mean) baked in. So I introduce a positive scaler, a tunable function of the node degree $d$,
$$S(d,\alpha) = \left(\frac{\log(d+1)}{\delta}\right)^{\alpha},$$
where $\log(d+1)$ is the slow degree dependence (the $+1$ keeps it finite and positive at $d=0$), $\delta$ is a fixed normalizer — the average of $\log(d+1)$ over the training graphs — so the base of the power hovers near $1$ and the scaler needs no per-dataset retuning, and $\alpha$ is the knob. Setting $\alpha=0$ gives the identity and recovers the bare aggregator; $\alpha=+1$ amplifies, up-weighting high-degree nodes; $\alpha=-1$ attenuates, up-weighting low-degree nodes. Three settings, $\{-1,0,+1\}$, spanning amplify / neutral / attenuate.

Why *logarithmic* and not linear is the load-bearing choice, and it is worth checking rather than asserting. Take the linear scaler $S(d)=d$. A degree-amplifying linear scaler applied to a *mean* reproduces a *sum*, because mean times degree is sum. So the linear-scaler family literally *contains* the sum as a special case, and a degree-linear injective scaler composed with a suitably constructed per-element map makes the mean injective on bounded countable multisets — that is the spirit of the scaler-injectivity result, the constructed element map plus the cardinality scaler doing the work, not a raw mean times any scaler. But the linear scaler is exactly the pathology I ruled out: scaling by $d$ compounds multiplicatively across layers and blows up exponentially with depth. The logarithmic scaler $S(d,+1)=\log(d+1)/\delta$ is its bounded-magnitude *replacement*. It does not reproduce the sum — $\log(d+1)\neq d$ — but it reinjects a controlled, monotone dependence on degree that recovers the count-sensitivity a mean throws away, without the unbounded growth a sum or a linear scaler risks across depth. Keeping degree as a usable, learnable signal while staying stable as the network deepens is the entire point of choosing log over linear.

Now combine the two axes. The aggregators disagree about *which statistic*; the scalers disagree about *how degree modulates*. The honest way to combine several of each is the tensor (outer) product: apply every scaler to every aggregator and concatenate all the resulting channels,
$$\oplus = \big[\,I,\; S(d,+1),\; S(d,-1)\,\big]^{\mathsf{T}} \otimes \big[\,\mu,\; \sigma,\; \max,\; \min\,\big],$$
twelve channels, each a different (statistic $\times$ degree-modulation) view of the same neighbor multiset. Every channel is individually lossy — the lower bound guarantees no single one can be otherwise — but the twelve together retain far more than any one, and they are cheap because they reuse the same neighbor messages; only the scaling and the reduction differ. I concatenate the twelve channels with the node's own current state and hand them to the per-node update MLP $U$, which learns whatever task-specific recombination it needs. The update is where the channels get mixed back down to the layer width; the aggregator's only job was to *not destroy* the information on the way in.

The operator does the right thing exactly at the extremes I designed it for. Two neighborhoods that share a mean but differ in spread: the $\mu$ channels collapse them, the $\sigma$ channels separate them. A multiset versus its $k$-fold inflation — same mean, max, min, std but different count: the identity-scaler aggregators, all degree-blind, can collapse them, but the amplification channel $S(d,+1)\cdot\mu$ scales by degree and distinguishes the inflated copy, caught precisely by the channel built to reinject count. Two that differ only in their upper envelope: the max splits them where mean and min do not. And because the scalers are logarithmic, stacking these layers does not compound magnitudes the way stacked sums do, so the operator *extrapolates* to graphs with larger degrees than training without the activations running away — the stringent test that most exposes degree-unstable aggregation, and the one I most want to pass. The honest cost is that twelve channels is $12\times$ the width into the update relative to a single aggregator, so $U$'s first linear layer is the dominant new parameter cost; if I ever need to trim, the place to cut is an aggregator whose channel is near-constant on a given feature distribution — for instance the min channel on strictly non-negative features, where the elementwise min clusters near zero — but the canonical operator keeps all four aggregators and three scalers, the full twelve.

In code: scatter the messages to their destination nodes once, compute the four aggregators by scatter-reduce, build the per-node degree scalers, take the outer product, and concatenate.

```python
import torch
import torch.nn as nn
import torch.nn.functional as F
from torch_geometric.utils import degree, scatter


class PNAAggregation(nn.Module):
    """4 aggregators {mean, max, min, std} x 3 log-degree scalers {I, +1, -1}."""

    def __init__(self, in_dim, avg_deg_log):
        super().__init__()
        # delta: average of log(d+1) over the training set (fixed normalizer).
        self.register_buffer("delta", torch.tensor(float(avg_deg_log)))
        self.eps = 1e-5
        self.out_dim = in_dim * 12

    def _aggregate(self, messages, index, num_nodes):
        mean = scatter(messages, index, dim=0, dim_size=num_nodes, reduce="mean")
        mx = scatter(messages, index, dim=0, dim_size=num_nodes, reduce="max")
        mn = scatter(messages, index, dim=0, dim_size=num_nodes, reduce="min")
        mean_sq = scatter(messages * messages, index, dim=0,
                          dim_size=num_nodes, reduce="mean")
        std = torch.sqrt(F.relu(mean_sq - mean * mean) + self.eps)
        return torch.cat([mean, mx, mn, std], dim=-1)            # [N, 4F]

    def forward(self, messages, index, node_degree, num_nodes):
        agg = self._aggregate(messages, index, num_nodes)        # [N, 4F]
        log_deg = torch.log(node_degree + 1.0)                   # [N]
        ratio = (log_deg / self.delta.clamp(min=self.eps)).clamp(min=self.eps)
        amp = ratio.unsqueeze(-1)                                # S(d, +1)
        att = ratio.pow(-1.0).unsqueeze(-1)                      # S(d, -1)
        return torch.cat([agg, agg * amp, agg * att], dim=-1)    # [N, 12F]
```
