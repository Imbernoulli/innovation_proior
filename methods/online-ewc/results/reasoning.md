Let me start from what actually breaks when I run a regularisation-based continual learner for a long time. I have a network with a fixed number of parameters theta, and contexts arrive one after another: task 1, then task 2, then task 3, and I want it to keep doing all of them. The reigning answer is elastic weight consolidation: after I finish a context t, I freeze a snapshot of where the weights ended up, theta*_t, and I measure how much each weight mattered to that context with the diagonal of its Fisher information F_t, and then while I learn later contexts I add a quadratic penalty that pulls each weight back toward theta*_t with a stiffness proportional to F_{t,i}. The total loss while learning some new context is L_new(theta) + Σ_t (lambda/2) Σ_i F_{t,i} (theta_i - theta*_{t,i})^2, the sum running over every past context t. Each term is a spring: stiff in the directions a past context cared about, slack everywhere else, so the optimiser can move the weights that don't hurt the old contexts and is resisted when it moves the ones that do. It works. But look at that sum. It has one term per past context, and each term carries its own anchor theta*_t (a full copy of the parameter vector) and its own Fisher F_t (another full copy). After a hundred contexts I am storing a hundred parameter-sized anchors and a hundred parameter-sized Fishers, and every single gradient step I have to sweep all hundred springs to form the penalty. The cost — memory and per-step compute both — grows linearly in the number of contexts I have ever seen. For a genuine lifelong agent that is the thing that kills it: the bookkeeping outgrows the model. So the question I actually have to answer is sharper than "how do I not forget." It is: can I keep the protection EWC gives me but pay a cost that is *constant* in the number of contexts?

The naive thing first, just to feel the wall. EWC itself notes that two quadratic penalties sum to a single quadratic penalty — a sum of springs is a spring. So can't I just merge them? For one coordinate, take two terms, (1/2) F_A (theta - theta*_A)^2 + (1/2) F_B (theta - theta*_B)^2. Expanding gives (1/2)(F_A+F_B)theta^2 - (F_A theta*_A + F_B theta*_B)theta + const. To write that as one square, the centre has to be m = (F_A theta*_A + F_B theta*_B)/(F_A+F_B), coordinate by coordinate wherever the summed stiffness is nonzero, so the merged penalty is (1/2)(F_A+F_B)(theta-m)^2 + c, where c = (1/2)(F_A theta*_A^2 + F_B theta*_B^2 - (F_A+F_B)m^2), equivalently (1/2)F_A F_B(theta*_A-theta*_B)^2/(F_A+F_B) when F_A+F_B > 0. The constant c does not affect gradients. So the *stiffness* merges into a running sum of Fishers, Σ_t F_t, which is one parameter-sized object regardless of how many contexts — good, that's constant. But the anchor m is a Fisher-weighted blend of all the past optima, and to update it incrementally when a new context arrives I'd carry both the running Σ_t F_t and the running Σ_t F_t theta*_t and divide. That's still constant memory, two parameter-sized accumulators. So mechanically I *can* fold EWC's penalties into a single quadratic with constant storage. Fine. But this is just algebra on a sum of springs I haven't questioned. Before I trust the merged anchor m I should ask whether that pile of springs was the right object in the first place — whether anchoring a fresh penalty at *every* past optimum is even what the Bayesian story I started from actually prescribes. If the springs are wrong, merging them faithfully just preserves the error in a cheaper container.

So back to the derivation, slowly, because EWC came out of a probabilistic argument and I want to redo that argument without assuming the per-context stack. The weights are random; what I know after the data of contexts 1..k is the posterior p(theta | T_{1:k}). The contexts' data are conditionally independent given theta, so the joint likelihood factors, and the posterior builds up recursively: p(theta | T_{1:k}) ∝ p(theta | T_{1:k-1}) p(T_k | theta). Stare at that for a second. It says the posterior after all k contexts is the posterior after the first k-1 — used as the prior — times the likelihood of just the k-th. It is purely sequential and it doesn't care about ordering. And it never asks me to keep the individual context likelihoods p(T_t | theta) lying around. The only object the recursion needs is the *running posterior*. That's the tell. The per-context stack of springs is not in this equation at all; what's in the equation is one evolving distribution. EWC's linear growth came not from the Bayesian math but from how EWC chose to *approximate* it.

How did the approximation go? The posterior over a deep net is intractable, so Laplace's method: near the mode theta*, approximate -log of a distribution by its second-order Taylor expansion. At a mode the gradient is zero, so the expansion is purely quadratic, -log p(theta | ·) ≈ (1/2)(theta - theta*)^T H (theta - theta*) + const, with H the Hessian of the negative log posterior at theta*. Near a well-fit optimum the data part of H is well-approximated by the Fisher F (times the sample count), and EWC keeps only its diagonal — one scalar stiffness per weight, computed from squared first-order gradients, positive semidefinite, the right local curvature. So far so good and identical for everyone. The branch point is *what* you Laplace-approximate. EWC applies the Gaussian approximation to each context's *likelihood* p(T_t | theta) separately — one Gaussian N(theta*_t, F_t^{-1}) per context — and then the product of those Gaussians is the product of springs, one anchored at each theta*_t. That's where the stack is born. But the recursion above suggests a different target: apply Laplace not to each likelihood but to the *whole running posterior* p(theta | T_{1:k-1}), which is the single object the recursion actually carries forward.

Let me run that consistent version and see what it gives, going context by context, because the difference only shows up from the third one. Two contexts, A then B. The log posterior is log p(theta | D_A, D_B) = log p(D_B | theta) + log p(theta | D_A) + const, where the constant is independent of theta. The first term is just the loss for B. The second, p(theta | D_A), is the posterior after A — intractable, so I Laplace it around its mode theta*_A: -log p(theta | D_A) ≈ (1/2) Σ_i (F_{A,i}) (theta_i - theta*_{A,i})^2 + const, with F_A the diagonal Fisher at theta*_A (folding the prior's curvature in if I keep one). So learning B I minimise L_B(theta) + (1/2) Σ_i F_{A,i} (theta_i - theta*_{A,i})^2 — one spring around theta*_A. Identical to EWC for two contexts. No disagreement yet.

Now the third context, C, after A and B. The exact log posterior is log p(theta | D_A, D_B, D_C) = log p(D_C | theta) + log p(theta | D_A, D_B) + const. I need to Laplace-approximate the running posterior p(theta | D_A, D_B). But here's the thing I have to be honest about: I already threw D_A and D_B away, so I do not have the *true* p(theta | D_A, D_B) to expand. What I have is the *approximation* I built while learning B, namely log p(theta | D_A, D_B) ≈ log p(D_B | theta) - (1/2) Σ_i F_{A,i} (theta_i - theta*_{A,i})^2 + const. So I Taylor-expand *that* around its mode — and its mode is the point I already found while learning B, which is theta*_B (it's a local optimum of exactly this approximated objective, so the first-order term vanishes there, good). The curvature of this approximated posterior at theta*_B has two pieces: the negative-log-likelihood part of B contributes its Fisher F_B (curvature of -log p(D_B|theta) ≈ F_B), and the spring around theta*_A contributes its own curvature, which is just its stiffness F_A — a quadratic's second derivative is its coefficient, constant everywhere. So the curvature of the running posterior at theta*_B is F_A + F_B. Therefore the consistent Laplace approximation of p(theta | D_A, D_B), expanded at theta*_B, is a *single* quadratic: (1/2) Σ_i (F_{A,i} + F_{B,i}) (theta_i - theta*_{B,i})^2. And the loss for learning C becomes

  L_C(theta) + (1/2) Σ_i (F_{A,i} + F_{B,i}) (theta_i - theta*_{B,i})^2.

Now compare. EWC at context C would carry *two* springs, one at theta*_A and one at theta*_B. The consistent recursion gives *one* spring, anchored at theta*_B — the most recent optimum — with a stiffness that is the *sum* of the past Fishers. The number of penalty terms did not grow. It stayed one, and that fell out of just being consistent about what to approximate.

And once I see it I can also see *why* EWC's extra spring is not merely redundant but actively wrong, which matters because I almost merged the springs faithfully a moment ago. theta*_B was not found in a vacuum — it was found while already being pulled toward theta*_A by the spring (1/2) F_A (theta - theta*_A)^2. So information about A is *already baked into* theta*_B; the spring I now place around theta*_B inherits A's pull through where theta*_B sits. If on top of that I keep a *separate* spring still anchored at theta*_A, I am imposing A's constraint a second time. I'm double-counting A's data. Over a long sequence this is a systematic bias toward the earliest contexts — they get re-asserted at every step through their own surviving anchors while later contexts are only represented once. The merged-anchor m I computed earlier, the Fisher-weighted blend of all past optima, preserves exactly this double-counted structure in a cheaper container; it was the wrong object made compact. The consistent thing is not to blend all the old anchors — it is to drop them entirely and anchor only at the *latest* optimum, because the latest optimum already encodes the cumulative pull of everything before it. So the running state I should keep is exactly two parameter-sized objects: the latest mean theta*_{i-1}, and a running *sum* of Fishers, F-summed = Σ_t F_t, updated each context by F-summed ← F-summed + F_i. One spring, re-centred forward each time, with a Fisher that accumulates. Constant memory, constant per-step cost. The penalty for context i is (1/2) Σ_r [Σ_{t<i} F_{t,r}] (theta_r - theta*_{i-1,r})^2.

I should name the price of this, because re-centring isn't free and I don't want to pretend it is. Fisher is a *local* quantity — F_t describes the loss surface only in a small neighbourhood of theta*_t. When I re-centre the accumulated penalty at the new optimum theta*_{i-1}, I am pretending the old contexts' curvature, measured way back at their own optima, is still the right curvature here at theta*_{i-1}. Strictly I'd have to recompute every old F_t at the new point, which needs the old data and defeats the entire purpose. So re-centring is an approximation on top of the diagonal approximation. Concretely it means the very first contexts get gradually un-anchored: there is no longer any spring sitting at theta*_A directly holding the weights near where A wanted them, only the inherited memory carried forward through the moving mean and the summed Fisher. So I should *expect* the earliest contexts to be remembered a little less faithfully than a method that keeps an explicit anchor at each one. That is a real, predictable trade I am making in exchange for constant cost: the recent past is favoured over the distant past. Whether that's a good trade depends on the regime — if every context trained to convergence and all the penalties can be satisfied at once, this single re-centred summary is exactly the consistent Bayesian object and should be the better choice; but if the network is undertrained and never really satisfies the constraints, an explicit heavy anchor on the first context will hold it better. Good to know which way the bias points; it's a property of the two weightings, not something I have to measure to believe.

So I have a constant-memory method already: keep one mean and one summed Fisher, F-summed ← F-summed + F_i, penalty around the latest mean. Is that the end? Two things make me keep going. First, the fixed-capacity problem I started with hasn't actually been solved — it's been *compressed*. With F-summed = Σ_t F_t the stiffnesses only ever add, never shrink. Over a long sequence the summed Fisher grows without bound in every direction any context ever cared about, and the network — which has a fixed number of parameters — becomes so rigid that new contexts can't be learned at all. It's documented that EWC "misbehaves when the model runs out of capacity," and my undamped running sum has exactly the same disease; I've made the bookkeeping cheap but I haven't made room for the future. Second, contexts can *recur* in a lifelong setting. If task A comes around again, the right thing is to *update* A's contribution to the running summary in light of the fresh data — not to add a brand-new, redundant term on top of the stale one I folded in last time. But I deliberately don't store A's term separately (that's what gives me constant memory), so how do I revise it? Both problems point at the same missing capability: I need to be able to *remove*, or at least *down-weight*, a context's earlier contribution to the shared summary without ever having stored it on its own.

That phrasing rings a bell from the approximate-inference side. Expectation propagation has exactly this move: it keeps one approximating factor per data factor, and to refine one it forms the "cavity" by *dividing that factor out* of the product, recomputes, and multiplies back. The trouble is EP keeps a factor *per* context — linear memory, the very thing I'm fleeing. But stochastic EP relaxes precisely this: instead of one factor per data factor, it keeps a *single shared* averaged factor that stands in for all of them, and it treats any one factor's contribution as a *fraction* of that shared factor. So when you want to update one factor, you don't divide out a stored copy of it — you down-weight the shared factor by the appropriate fraction (a partial removal), refine on the new data, and fold back in. That is exactly the capability I just said I needed, and it costs nothing extra: it's a scalar down-weighting of the one summary I'm already keeping.

Let me transcribe that into my Fisher accumulation. Right now I add: F-summed ← F-summed + F_i. The stochastic-EP move says: before folding in the new context, *partially remove* the previous shared contribution by scaling it down by a fraction gamma < 1, then add the new Fisher:

  F* ← gamma · F*_old + F_i.

That single change does both jobs at once. For recurrence: when a context is revisited, scaling the running summary by gamma is the fractional removal of its previous presentation's contribution before the fresh data is added — no per-context factor needed, and notice it requires no task *identity*, only that a boundary occurred, because every context is treated identically through the same gamma. For capacity: gamma < 1 turns the unbounded running sum into a *geometric* one. Unroll it — F*_i = Σ_{t≤i} gamma^{i-t} F_t — so a context that finished k boundaries ago contributes with weight gamma^k inside the stored summary, decaying geometrically. The stored summary F* no longer climbs forever; for roughly comparable Fishers it settles to an effective sum on the order of 1/(1-gamma) recent contexts, while the actual loss-side stiffness has one extra factor of gamma because the penalty uses gamma F*. That is exactly the room I need to keep learning new contexts on fixed capacity. And it's *graceful*: instead of catastrophically dropping an old context, gamma fades it smoothly. That's a feature in its own right — if an old context never converged and its constraint is half-wrong, I'd rather let it decay than have it permanently distort the summary. The endpoints check the indexing. gamma = 1 recovers the strict undecayed single-penalty sum F* = Σ_t F_t, and because the loss-side factor is also gamma, the next loss uses that full summary. As gamma approaches 0, the stored update after a context becomes just that context's Fisher, but the next loss uses gamma F*, so at gamma = 0 there is no protective penalty at all. A middle value such as 0.9 keeps roughly the last ten contexts strongly represented inside the stored summary and lets older ones fade. So gamma is simultaneously the EP partial-removal fraction and the explicit forgetting knob; the same scalar wears both hats, which is a sign it's the right scalar.

Now what exactly is the penalty I apply, in terms of this F*? The shared summary after i-1 contexts is the Gaussian with mean theta*_{i-1} and precision F*_{i-1}, and what I regularise the new context against is the *down-weighted* shared factor — the same gamma scaling, because the factor I'm holding the new context to is the cavity-style shared summary, not the raw sum. So the penalty is

  (1/2) ‖theta - theta*_{i-1}‖^2 over gamma · F*_{i-1} = (1/2) Σ_i gamma · F*_{i-1,i} (theta_i - theta*_{i-1,i})^2.

The gamma sits in the loss as well as in the accumulation. I want to be careful here, because there's a genuinely ambiguous reading: one could make the next update decayed but regularise the current context against the undamped F*_{i-1}. That would treat the stored summary as the prior directly. The cavity-style reading is different: before learning the new context I first partially remove the shared factor, and the object I regularise against is already the down-weighted shared factor. Under that convention the applied stiffness is gamma · F*_{i-1}, and the update after the context is gamma F*_{i-1} + F_i. I'll keep those two uses of gamma tied together.

One more refinement matters in policy settings, and it's about making each context count *equally*, which I claimed gamma helps with but which the raw Fisher quietly undermines. The penalty protects the network's output behaviour in expectation, and the Fisher is the curvature of that output's divergence under weight changes. For a context whose solution is very *deterministic* — a peaked output distribution — a small change in theta produces a large change in the predicted distribution, so the Fisher has a large overall norm; for a context with a flatter, more uncertain solution the Fisher norm is small. So if I accumulate raw Fishers, F* ← gamma F* + F_i, a deterministic context dumps a large-norm F_i into the sum and dominates it, while a soft context barely registers — purely because of the *scale* of its Fisher, not because it's more important to protect. If I want equal task scale, I normalise each context's Fisher before folding it in, dividing F_i by a task-level norm so what enters the running sum is the *relative* importance of weights within that context. For the minimal hook I need here, the invariant is the raw all-label diagonal-Fisher path: compute F_new, make the stored accumulator land on F* ← gamma F*_old + F_new, and use 0.5 · gamma · F* in the loss.

Now the importance estimator itself — what F_i concretely is and how I compute it from squared gradients, because that's the other empty slot. The diagonal Fisher at the finished context's optimum is F_{i,j} = E_x E_{y ~ p_theta(·|x)} [ (∂ log p_theta(y|x) / ∂theta_j)^2 ] — the expected squared score of the model's *own* predictive distribution. I estimate the outer expectation over x by passing examples from the just-finished context through the trained network, and the inner expectation over y by the model's predicted class probabilities. So per example I run a forward pass to get the output logits, take softmax to get the per-class weights p_theta(y=c|x), and for each class c I form the negative log-likelihood of class c, backprop it to get the gradient, square the gradient elementwise, and add it weighted by that class's softmax probability. Summing the squared gradients of every class weighted by its own predicted probability is exactly the Monte-Carlo estimate of the inner expectation over y — this is the "all labels" estimator, the true Fisher rather than the cheaper empirical Fisher that would use only the observed label. I do this in eval mode (so dropout and the like don't perturb the curvature estimate), over a capped number of single-example passes for speed, and I normalise the running sum by the number of examples I actually used. The reason this is affordable on a big net is property (b) of the Fisher: it's all first-order gradients, no Hessian, one diagonal scalar per weight.

The loop follows. After a context finishes: zero an accumulator the shape of the parameters; eval mode; for each of up to N examples, forward, softmax the logits to class weights, and for each class c backprop -log p(c|x) and add softmax_c · grad^2 into the accumulator; divide by the number of examples actually processed; then fold into the running summary with the decay, F* ← gamma F*_old + F_new. At every training step of the next context, the penalty is 0.5 · gamma · Σ over params of F* · (theta - theta*)^2, where theta* is the snapshot from the previous boundary. And the framework I'm dropping into accumulates whatever I return *additively* into its running importance buffer — so to make its additive accumulator end up holding gamma F*_old + F_new, I return the *increment* relative to what's already stored: I compute the full decayed-plus-new Fisher gamma·existing + F_new myself, then subtract the existing value so that "existing + increment" lands exactly on the decayed sum. That's the bookkeeping the loop's add-don't-replace convention forces.

The two hooks become:

```python
import torch
import torch.nn.functional as F
from torch.utils.data import DataLoader


def estimate_importance(model, dataset, prev_params, device):
    """Called once after a context finishes. Returns {param_name: increment}
    so the loop's additive accumulator ends holding  F* = gamma*F*_old + F_new."""
    # gamma < 1: the EP partial-removal fraction == the graceful-forgetting knob.
    # gamma = 1 gives the strict undecayed single-penalty sum.
    model.gamma = 0.9
    gamma = model.gamma

    # accumulator for the new context's diagonal Fisher
    est_fisher = {}
    for gen_params in model.param_list:
        for n, p in gen_params():
            if p.requires_grad:
                n = n.replace('.', '__')
                est_fisher[n] = p.detach().clone().zero_()

    mode = model.training
    model.eval()                                   # freeze dropout etc. for a clean curvature estimate

    data_loader = DataLoader(dataset, batch_size=1, shuffle=False)
    max_samples = min(len(data_loader), 200)       # cap the passes for speed
    n_samples = 0

    for idx, (x, y) in enumerate(data_loader):
        if idx >= max_samples:
            break
        x = x.to(device)
        output = model(x)                          # logits
        with torch.no_grad():
            label_weights = F.softmax(output, dim=1)   # p_theta(y=c|x): inner-expectation weights
        for c in range(output.shape[1]):
            label = torch.LongTensor([c]).to(device)
            negloglikelihood = F.cross_entropy(output, label)   # -log p_theta(c|x)
            model.zero_grad()
            negloglikelihood.backward(             # one backward per class; keep graph until the last
                retain_graph=True if (c + 1) < output.shape[1] else False)
            for gen_params in model.param_list:
                for n, p in gen_params():
                    if p.requires_grad:
                        n = n.replace('.', '__')
                        if p.grad is not None:
                            # weight class c's squared score by its predicted prob:
                            #   F_jj = E_x E_{y~p_theta} (d log p / d theta_j)^2
                            est_fisher[n] += label_weights[0][c] * (p.grad.detach() ** 2)
        n_samples += 1

    est_fisher = {n: v / max(n_samples, 1) for n, v in est_fisher.items()}   # average over examples

    # fold the new Fisher into the running summary with the decay:
    #   F* = gamma * F*_old + F_new
    existing = getattr(model, '_custom_importance', {})
    for n in est_fisher:
        if n in existing:
            est_fisher[n] = gamma * existing[n] + est_fisher[n]

    # the loop accumulates ADDITIVELY into its stored importance, so return the
    # increment (decayed-sum minus what's already stored) to land on F* exactly
    result = {}
    for n in est_fisher:
        result[n] = est_fisher[n] - existing[n] if n in existing else est_fisher[n]

    model.train(mode=mode)
    return result


def compute_regularization_loss(model, importance_dict, prev_params_dict):
    """Per step: 0.5 * gamma * sum( F* * (theta - theta*)^2 ), one re-centred spring."""
    gamma = getattr(model, 'gamma', 0.9)           # the same gamma as in the accumulation
    losses = []
    for gen_params in model.param_list:
        for n, p in gen_params():
            if p.requires_grad:
                n = n.replace('.', '__')
                if n in importance_dict and n in prev_params_dict:
                    fisher = importance_dict[n]            # F* (running, decayed sum of Fishers)
                    prev = prev_params_dict[n]             # theta*_{i-1}: latest-boundary snapshot
                    losses.append((fisher * (p - prev) ** 2).sum())
    if losses:
        return 0.5 * gamma * sum(losses)           # gamma scales the regularising shared factor
    return torch.tensor(0.0, device=next(model.parameters()).device)
```

Let me retrace the causal chain end to end. I started stuck: EWC stops catastrophic forgetting by keeping a quadratic spring per past context, but that means one anchor and one Fisher stored per context and one spring swept per step, so memory and compute grow linearly in the number of contexts — fatal for a lifelong agent, and on fixed capacity the pile of springs eventually over-constrains the net. Merging the springs algebraically gave constant memory but preserved a structure I hadn't questioned. Going back to the Bayesian recursion p(theta|T_{1:k}) ∝ p(theta|T_{1:k-1}) p(T_k|theta) showed the math only ever needs the running posterior, not a per-context stack; EWC's stack was an artefact of Laplace-approximating each likelihood separately. Applying Laplace consistently to the *whole running posterior* instead collapses the stack: from the third context on, the consistent approximation is a *single* spring re-centred at the latest optimum theta*_{i-1} with a stiffness that is the *sum* of past Fishers — and keeping the old anchors too would double-count the early contexts, biasing toward them, so dropping them is not just cheaper but correct, at the honest cost of remembering the distant past a little less. That already gives constant memory (one mean, one summed Fisher) but two problems remain: the undamped sum grows without bound and re-rigidifies a fixed-capacity net, and recurring contexts can't be revised without storing per-context factors. Stochastic EP's single-shared-factor-with-fractional-removal supplies the missing move: down-weight the shared summary by gamma < 1 before folding in each new Fisher, F* ← gamma F*_old + F_new, which bounds the sum to ~1/(1-gamma) contexts of effective memory (room to keep learning), gracefully fades old contexts, and handles recurrence with a scalar and no task labels — gamma = 1 recovers the strict consistent sum, while gamma = 0 leaves no old-task penalty under the loss-side gamma convention. The same gamma scales the penalty, since the new context is regularised against the partially-removed shared factor. Normalising each context's Fisher before accumulation is the policy-scale refinement when equal task weighting is required; the hook here keeps the standard all-label Fisher estimator. The importance estimator is the diagonal Fisher computed from softmax-weighted squared gradients over all labels — the model's own expected curvature, first-order and cheap — in eval mode over a capped sample. And the whole thing drops into the continual-learning harness as two slots: an estimator that returns the decayed-sum increment, and a per-step penalty of 0.5 · gamma · Σ F* (theta - theta*)^2 — constant memory and constant per-step cost in the number of contexts.
