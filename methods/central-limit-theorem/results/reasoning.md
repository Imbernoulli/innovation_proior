I start with the fact that direct convolution is the wrong place to look for the general pattern. In the binomial case I can count coefficients and watch the middle of the expansion become smooth, but that computation is too special. If every summand has its own law, the sum is still a convolution, yet the formula becomes less informative with each new term. I need a representation in which independence simplifies the algebra instead of making it worse.

I look for an object attached to a distribution that always exists and that turns addition of independent variables into multiplication. A bounded oscillatory exponential has exactly that feature. For a centered variable `X`, the quantity `E exp(itX)` is defined for every real `t`, and for independent variables the transform of the sum is the product of the transforms. This changes the problem from understanding a complicated distribution directly to understanding many factors near one.

The next question is what each factor looks like after normalization. If the variable is small on the final scale and has variance `sigma^2`, the local expansion must begin as `1 - t^2 sigma^2/2`; the linear term vanishes because the variable is centered. The normal law is already visible in that quadratic term, because multiplying many expressions of the form `1 - small quadratic` should produce an exponential of a negative quadratic. This is the same shape that appears in the classical binomial calculation, but now it is emerging from variance rather than from explicit coefficients.

I cannot simply assume every remainder is harmless. A summand may be usually small but occasionally large, and those occasional large values can carry variance. That is the place where the real condition must enter. I split the second moment of each summand into the part below a fixed threshold and the part above it. On the small part the Taylor remainder is uniformly controlled. On the large part I do not need fine structure; I need only know that the total variance living in those tails goes to zero. This identifies the right invisibility condition: after normalization, the row must have no visible variance in terms whose absolute size exceeds any fixed epsilon.

With that condition in place, the transform proof becomes mechanical. For each row I compare the true factor `E exp(itX_{n,j})` with the quadratic proxy `1 - t^2 sigma_{n,j}^2/2`. The accumulated error is bounded by the sum of all Taylor remainders, and the tail condition makes that sum vanish. The product of the true factors therefore has the same limit as the product of the quadratic proxies.

Now the total variance does the remaining work. Since the row variances sum to one, the logarithm of the quadratic proxy product tends to `-t^2/2`. Hence the characteristic function of the normalized sum tends pointwise to `exp(-t^2/2)`. That function is continuous at zero and is the transform of the standard normal law, so the continuity theorem converts the transform limit back into convergence of distributions.

This also explains why the identical finite-variance theorem is only a special case. If I take `X_j / sqrt(n)`, the chance that one scaled term exceeds a fixed epsilon is the chance that `|X_j|` exceeds `epsilon sqrt(n)`, and the finite second moment forces the corresponding tail variance to vanish. The same argument also explains the older Lyapunov condition: a moment of order above two is a convenient way to force the tail variance to disappear, but it is not the essence of the theorem. The essence is that no individual summand or tail of a summand survives the normalization with visible variance.
