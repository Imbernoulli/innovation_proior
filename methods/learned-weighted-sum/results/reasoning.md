Let me start from the thing I actually do a dozen times inside one of these pyramids and never think about: I have several feature maps that I need to turn into one. They came from different places — different pyramid levels, different resolutions, in the weather setting different physical variables that each got tokenized on its own — and at some point I resize them all to a common shape and I have to collapse the stack `(I_1, ..., I_V)` at every spatial location into a single map `O`. What do I write down? I write `O = I_1 + I_2 + ... + I_V`, or I divide by `V` and call it a mean. That's it. That's the line that's everywhere — FPN merges its lateral feature and its upsampled top-down feature by element-wise addition, `P_out_l = Conv(P_in_l + Resize(P_out_{l+1}))`; PANet fuses its pooled per-level grids by element-wise max or sum; NAS-FPN, whatever clever topology the search hands it, still combines the inputs arriving at a node by summing them. And in the variable-aggregation setting the parameter-free baseline is exactly the uniform mean over the `V` variables. Every one of these gives each source the same weight, one, and nobody checked whether that's right.

So let me actually question it, because it's a free decision I've been making for free. When I add `I_1 + ... + I_V`, I am asserting that each source contributes equally to the fused output. Is that true? The sources are heterogeneous by construction — they're at different resolutions, they carry different semantics, in the weather case they're literally different physical fields, temperature versus geopotential versus a land-sea mask. There's no reason on earth that all `V` of them deserve the same say at a given location. And I don't have to argue this in the abstract; the evidence is already sitting in front of me. When PANet went and instrumented *which* level each pooled feature actually came from, the punchline was that the usefulness of a source is only weakly tied to the level it nominally lives on — for small objects nominally assigned to the finest level, on the order of seventy percent of the helpful features came from *other* levels; for large objects assigned to the coarsest level, more than half came from lower levels. Read plainly: when you combine several sources, they contribute unequally, and which one carries the load shifts case to case. PANet surfaced that and then... still fused with max-or-sum. It showed me the phenomenon and gave me no handle to act on it. The equal-weight sum is leaving a degree of freedom on the table that the data is openly asking me to use.

Fine — so don't force the weights to be equal. The most direct thing imaginable: attach a weight `w_i` to each source and combine by a weighted sum,

  `O = sum_i w_i · I_i`,

and let `w_i` be a learnable parameter the network trains by ordinary backprop, like any other weight. The gradient flows straight through: `dO/dw_i = I_i`, so a source that consistently helps reduce the loss will see its weight pushed up, one that hurts pushed down. The network gets to discover the importance of each input instead of me legislating it. This is almost embarrassingly cheap, too, and I want to be deliberate about *how* cheap, because there's a knob here. The weight `w_i` could be a scalar — one number per source — or a vector of length `D`, one per channel, or even a full tensor, one per pixel. The per-channel and per-pixel versions are strictly more expressive: they let different channels or different locations prefer different sources. But the thing I'm trying to decide is, at its core, "how much does source `i` matter overall," and that's naturally *one number per source*. With `V` sources a scalar weighting is `V` parameters total — utterly negligible next to the convolutions and projections around it, basically free. So I'll start with the scalar-per-source form; it's the cheapest knob that expresses the thing I actually want, and if it turns out I need channel-level discrimination I can lift it to a per-channel weight later (a vector `w_i` of length `D`, broadcast over the channel axis). The default is the scalar.

Now let me try to actually train `O = sum_i w_i I_i` with `w_i` free real numbers and feel where it breaks. The map from `(w_1, ..., w_V)` to `O` is linear and *homogeneous of degree one* in the weights: scale every `w_i` by some `c` and `O` scales by `c` too. Let me just put numbers on that to be sure I'm not imagining the problem. Three sources with values `(1, 2, 3)` at a location: with `w = (1, 1, 1)` the fused output is `6`, and with `w = (10, 10, 10)` — the same *relative* split, every source still equal — it's `60`. The split didn't change at all; only the magnitude did, and it tracked `c` exactly. So nothing in this expression pins down the overall magnitude of `w`: there's an entire ray of weight settings, all the `c·w` for `c > 0`, that produce identical relative mixing and differ only by a global gain, and the optimizer can drift along that ray however it likes. That's bad in two concrete ways. First, the downstream network is built expecting features on a particular scale (its own normalization, its own learned scales tuned to whatever `O` usually looks like); if `sum_i w_i` wanders up to ten, I've silently multiplied the fused representation by ten — the `6`-vs-`60` above — and yanked the rest of the net off its operating point. Second, there's no ceiling at all: `w_i` is a free scalar, it can grow without bound, and an unbounded multiplicative gain in the middle of a deep network is exactly the kind of thing that makes training diverge. I've handed the network expressiveness and a way to blow itself up in the same line. Wall.

Let me look hard at what I actually wanted, because the failure is pointing at the cure. I never cared about the *absolute* size of the weights. I cared about the *relative* contribution: how much source `i` matters *compared to the others*. That's a ratio, not a magnitude. The absolute scale of `w` is a nuisance degree of freedom — it's precisely the thing that ran away on me. So I should quotient it out: fix the overall scale and let the network only move the relative split. The cleanest way to fix the scale is to demand the weights sum to one. And if on top of that I keep them nonnegative, then `sum_i w_i I_i` with `w_i >= 0`, `sum_i w_i = 1` is a *convex combination* of the inputs — a weighted average. That's exactly the well-behaved object I wanted: it lives in the convex hull of the `I_i`, so it's on the same scale as a single input, it can't introduce an arbitrary gain, and when all the `w_i` are equal it collapses back to the plain mean. The equal-weight average I started from is just the uniform point of this family, and now I'm letting the network pick any other point of it. Bounding the weights onto the simplex `{w >= 0, sum w = 1}` removes the runaway scale and keeps every bit of the relative-importance expressiveness. That's the constraint I need.

So now the real question is purely mechanical: how do I parameterize a point on the simplex with free, unconstrained parameters, smoothly and differentiably, so that plain SGD or Adam can train it with no projection step and no constrained optimizer? I do not want to carry `w_i >= 0, sum w_i = 1` as hard constraints during optimization — that means projecting onto the simplex after every step, an ugly special-case in an otherwise vanilla training loop. I want raw parameters `a_i` living anywhere in `R^V`, and a fixed function that maps them onto the simplex, with gradients flowing through it. Let me think about what function does that. I need nonnegativity and sum-to-one out of arbitrary reals. Nonnegativity from an arbitrary real — the obvious smooth positive map is the exponential, `e^{a_i} > 0` always. Sum-to-one — divide by the total. Put them together:

  `w_i = e^{a_i} / sum_j e^{a_j}`.

That's softmax. It clearly gives strictly positive entries (exp), and the denominator is the sum of the numerators so they sum to one by construction — algebraically it lands on the simplex. But I've talked myself into the wrong thing before, so before I commit let me actually pin down the three properties I'm relying on, with numbers, rather than nod at them.

First, the claim I most need: a convex combination of the inputs stays on the scale of the inputs. Let me draw a small `[V=4]` stack of source maps with values spread over a wide range and an arbitrary nonzero offset, push some raw weights `a = (0.5, -1.2, 2.0, 0.3)` through softmax, and form `sum_i w_i I_i`. With inputs ranging roughly `[-9.6, 12.0]`, the fused output came out in `[1.0, 5.5]` — strictly inside the input range. And checking it the right way, per location: at every `(b, l, d)` the fused entry sits between the min and max of the four source values at that same location. That's the convex-hull property made concrete — the output is a within-bounds blend, never an extrapolation, so it cannot manufacture the arbitrary gain that the unbounded version could. The downstream net keeps seeing features on the scale it was tuned for. Good — this is the property the whole simplex detour was *for*, and it holds.

Second, the initialization. If all `a_i` are equal — say I set them all to zero — then `w_i = e^0 / (V·e^0) = 1/V`. For `V = 4` that's `0.25` each; softmax of all-zeros gives exactly `(0.25, 0.25, 0.25, 0.25)`, summing to one. So at initialization the softmax-weighted sum *is* the plain mean over the `V` sources. That's a genuinely good place to start: I begin from the safe, sensible equal-contribution prior — the very baseline I'm trying to beat — and training perturbs the `a_i` off uniform only insofar as the data rewards it. There's no cold-start risk of landing in some wild corner of the simplex; it starts at the centroid and climbs. And the choice of constant doesn't matter: softmax is shift-invariant, `e^{a_i + c}/sum_j e^{a_j + c} = e^{a_i}/sum_j e^{a_j}`. I checked this isn't wishful — `softmax((0.5,-1.2,2.0,0.3))` and `softmax` of the same vector plus `3.7` agree to seven digits, `(0.154, 0.028, 0.691, 0.126)` both times — so all-zeros and all-ones give the identical uniform start.

Third, the gradient — because the reason I'm doing softmax instead of an external constraint is that I want plain backprop to train the raw `a_i`. I claimed the gradient couples the sources, since the denominator depends on all of them. Let me make sure the coupling has the *sign* I want. The analytic Jacobian of softmax is `dw_i/da_j = w_i(delta_ij - w_j)`: positive on the diagonal, negative off it. Confirming against autograd for `a = (0.5, -1.2, 2.0)`, the numeric Jacobian matches `w_i(delta_ij - w_j)` to within `3e-8`, and every off-diagonal entry is negative. So nudging `a_j` up to favor source `j` mechanically pushes the relative share of every other source down — they compete for a fixed unit budget, which is exactly the relative-importance behavior I wanted and not something I have to enforce by hand. The constraint isn't a penalty bolted on; it's baked into the functional form, and that's why it composes cleanly with the rest of the network — the optimizer just moves unconstrained reals and softmax keeps the result on the simplex every forward pass. Reading the weights as a probability/importance distribution over the `V` sources is then literally what they are.

Is there a cheaper way to land on the simplex, in case the exponential bothers me? Let me check, because I want to know what I'm choosing against. I could take `w_i = relu(a_i) / (eps + sum_j relu(a_j))` — clamp to nonnegative with a ReLU and normalize by the sum, with a small `eps` in the denominator so I never divide by zero if every weight gets clamped to zero. That also produces nonnegative weights summing to (nearly) one, no exponential, so it's a touch cheaper — there's no `exp`, and softmax's reduction over the source axis is the kind of op that tends to be slower on GPU than a plain ReLU-and-divide. Structurally it gives the same shape of object: the normalized weights still live in `[0,1]` and still sum to one up to `eps`. I'd *expect* it to learn a similar per-source split to softmax, since both are just learned points on (essentially) the same simplex — but that's an empirical claim about the trained optimum, not something I can settle from the algebra here; I'd want to confirm it by actually training both and comparing the learned weights. The cost I *can* see now is that it's a slightly less clean object: a ReLU can drive a weight to exactly zero, and there it's stuck (no gradient through a dead ReLU), and the `eps` makes the sum only *approximately* one. It also forces a different init — at all-zeros every `relu(a_i)` is zero and the weights are undefined-then-`eps`-rescued to nothing, so this sibling must start at a positive constant like ones, where softmax could start at zeros. Softmax never zeroes a weight out completely and is the textbook smooth map onto the simplex, so for this aggregator slot I'll take softmax as the clean default and keep ReLU-normalize in my back pocket as the latency-oriented sibling for when the softmax reduction's GPU cost is actually the binding constraint. Either way the structural idea is identical: nonnegative, normalized, learned per-source weights.

Let me now also place this against the heavyweight alternative so I'm sure I'm not under-building. The fully expressive way to combine sources is attention: project each source into a query/key/value, and let a query at each location decide, per location, how much to read from each source. That captures source-dependent *and* location-dependent mixing — strictly more than I'm doing, because my `w_i` is a single distribution shared across every location and every example, whereas attention recomputes the mixing at every token. But that expressiveness isn't free: attention drags in the query/key/value projection matrices, which are parameters scaling with the channel dimension `D`, plus an attention computation at every spatial location. On top of an already-large backbone that's a real tax in parameters and FLOPs, to do a job — "decide how much each source contributes" — that, by the diagnostic I started from, may well be answerable with a *global* per-source split rather than a per-location one. My `V` scalars are the minimal answer to that question: one learned mixing distribution for the whole tensor, no projections, no attention matrix. If the global split is most of the signal, I get most of the benefit at essentially none of the cost; that's the bet, and it's a sensible one to make as the simple, strong middle point between the parameter-free mean and full cross-attention.

So let me commit to the concrete form and write it as code, because the structure is now fixed and I want it to drop straight into the aggregator slot. The state is one learnable vector of length `V` — the raw per-source weights `a`. The forward pass: softmax `a` over the source axis to get the simplex weights `w`, broadcast them against the `[B, V, L, D]` stack so each source map is scaled by its scalar weight, and sum over the source axis to collapse `V -> 1`, leaving `[B, L, D]`.

```python
import torch
import torch.nn as nn
import torch.nn.functional as F


class VariableAggregator(nn.Module):
    """Aggregate V source feature maps by a learned, softmax-normalized weighted sum.

    One scalar weight is learned per source; softmax over the source axis turns the
    raw weights into a probability distribution (nonnegative, summing to one), so the
    fused output is a convex combination of the inputs -- same scale as a single input,
    reducing to a plain mean when the weights are uniform.
    """

    def __init__(self, embed_dim, num_heads, num_vars):
        super().__init__()
        self.embed_dim = embed_dim          # D (unused: the weights are per-source scalars)
        self.num_heads = num_heads          # unused: no attention here
        self.num_vars = num_vars            # V: number of sources to combine
        # One raw scalar weight per source. Init to zeros so softmax gives a uniform
        # 1/V start -- i.e. the plain mean -- which training then moves off of.
        self.var_weights = nn.Parameter(torch.zeros(num_vars), requires_grad=True)

    def forward(self, x):
        # x: [B, V, L, D]  -- V source feature maps per spatial location
        # Map raw per-source weights onto the simplex: nonnegative, sum to one.
        w = F.softmax(self.var_weights, dim=0)          # [V]
        w = w.view(1, self.num_vars, 1, 1)              # [1, V, 1, 1] to broadcast over B, L, D
        # Convex combination across sources: scale each source map by its weight, sum over V.
        out = (x * w).sum(dim=1)                        # [B, L, D]
        return out
```

That's the whole thing. Let me trace the causal chain once, to be sure it hangs together. I started where everyone starts — collapsing several heterogeneous feature sources into one by an equal-weight sum or mean — and noticed that "every source counts the same" is an unchecked assumption that the PANet diagnostic flatly contradicts: sources contribute unequally, and the equal-weight combine gives the network no way to act on that. Attaching a learnable scalar weight per source fixes the expressiveness, but leaving those weights unbounded leaves the overall scale of the combination free, which both knocks the downstream network off its operating point and risks unbounded-gain instability. What I actually wanted was relative contribution, not magnitude, so I quotiented out the scale by forcing the weights onto the simplex — nonnegative and summing to one — which makes the output a convex combination on the same scale as its inputs and reduces to the mean at the uniform point. Softmax is the smooth, unconstrained-parameter map onto that simplex: it gives a per-source probability/importance distribution, is differentiable everywhere so plain backprop trains it with no projection step, and at equal initialization recovers the mean exactly so training starts from the safe baseline and climbs. A scalar weight per source keeps it to `V` parameters — free — and the whole aggregator is one softmax over a length-`V` vector plus a broadcast-multiply-and-sum: more expressive than the mean it generalizes, and a tiny fraction of the cost of the cross-attention it sits below.
