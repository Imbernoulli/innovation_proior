Let me start from what actually goes wrong, because the standard fix feels like it's leaving something on the table and I want to know what. I have a classifier `sign(f(x))`, `f` a deep net, and an adversary who, given a clean labeled point `(x, y)`, gets to move it anywhere inside the `l_inf` ball `B(x, eps)` and wins if my prediction flips off `y`. The honest object I'd love to minimize is the robust error, the probability that *some* point in the ball is misclassified: `R_adv(f) = E 1{ exists x' in B(x, eps) s.t. f(x') y <= 0 }`. Compare that to the ordinary natural error `R_nat(f) = E 1{ f(x) y <= 0 }`. Obviously `R_adv >= R_nat`, with equality only at `eps = 0`, because the ball contains the center. Two problems hit immediately. The inner `exists` is a maximum over a continuum, and the loss is a 0-1 indicator, so the whole thing is NP-hard and non-differentiable — I can't backprop through it. That's the surface difficulty and there's a known way to relax it. The deeper difficulty is the one I keep circling: people report that the harder you push a model to be robust, the worse it gets on clean data. So whatever I build can't just be "drive `R_adv` down"; it has to let me say *how much* clean accuracy I'm willing to trade, and ideally tell me why the trade exists at all.

The going answer is Madry's. Write the defense as a saddle point, `min_theta E max_{||delta||_inf <= eps} L(f_theta(x + delta), y)`, approximate the inner max with a few steps of projected gradient ascent — `x <- Pi_{B(x,eps)}(x + alpha sign(grad_x L(f(x), y)))` from a random start — and train `theta` on those worst-case points with SGD. It works, it's the strongest thing around, and PGD behaves like a near-universal first-order adversary. But look at what the objective *is*: `E max_{x'} phi(f(x') y)`, the surrogate loss evaluated at the worst point against the *true label*. It is one number, an upper bound on `R_adv`, and I minimize it. Two things nag at me. First, is that upper bound even tight? It's `max over the ball of the loss-against-the-label`; in a complicated landscape there's no reason that's a faithful stand-in for "fraction of balls containing a misclassification." Second, and this is the real itch — there is exactly *one* term. There's no place in `E max_{x'} phi(f(x') y)` where I can dial the balance between "be right on clean `x`" and "be stable across the ball." Clean accuracy just falls out as a byproduct, and what falls out, the reports say, is often bad. I want a formulation where the trade-off is *written into the objective* as a knob, not left to chance. So let me not start from the min-max. Let me start from `R_adv` itself and ask where its mistakes actually come from.

Take a point `x` that is robustly misclassified — some `x'` in its ball flips it. There are only two ways that can happen, and they're mutually exclusive. Either I already get `x` itself wrong (the center is misclassified), or I get `x` right but it sits so close to my own decision surface that a permitted nudge tips it over. The first bucket is just the natural error. The second bucket is new: correctly-classified points that are *near the boundary*. Let me name the boundary `DB(f) = {x : f(x) = 0}` and its `eps`-neighborhood `B(DB(f), eps) = { x : exists x' in B(x, eps) s.t. f(x) f(x') <= 0 }` — the set of points from which some allowed step crosses where `f` changes sign. Then define the boundary error as the mass of *correctly* classified points sitting in that band: `R_bdy(f) = E 1{ x in B(DB(f), eps), f(x) y > 0 }`. And now the two buckets tile the robustly-wrong set exactly:

```
R_adv(f) = R_nat(f) + R_bdy(f).
```

Let me make sure that's an equality and not just a bound. A robustly-misclassified `x` either has `f(x) y <= 0` (counted once in `R_nat`, and such a point is by definition not in the `f(x) y > 0` set of `R_bdy`), or has `f(x) y > 0` but admits a flipping `x'`, which means `x` is within `eps` of the boundary and correctly classified, i.e. exactly the event in `R_bdy`. Disjoint and exhaustive. Equality. Good — this is more useful than the min-max bound because it *separates* the two things I care about into two additive terms. Clean accuracy lives entirely in `R_nat`; the extra fragility lives entirely in `R_bdy`. If I can get a differentiable handle on each, I can weight them however I like, and the weight *is* the trade-off knob I was missing.

But before I get excited, I should check whether the trade-off is even real or whether I'm inventing a tension that a big enough model dissolves. Let me build the meanest example I can. Put `X` uniform on `[0,1]` and make the label deterministic but oscillating with period `2 eps`: `Pr(Y=1 | X=x) = 0` on `[2k eps, (2k+1)eps)` and `= 1` on `((2k+1)eps, (2k+2)eps]`. The Bayes classifier tracks the oscillation, so its clean error is `0` — optimal natural error. But every single point is within `eps` of a label flip, so the boundary band is the entire space: `R_bdy = 1`, hence `R_adv = 1`. Now the dumb all-ones classifier: it's wrong half the time, `R_nat = 1/2`, but it has *no* decision boundary in `[0,1]`, so `R_bdy = 0` and `R_adv = 1/2`. So the all-ones classifier is *strictly more robust* than Bayes, while Bayes is strictly more accurate. The accuracy-optimal model and the robustness-optimal model are different models, and they're far apart. That settles it: the conflict is intrinsic, not a capacity artifact, and a single-objective method that just minimizes one number can't be right in general. I need the two-term decomposition and a weight between them. Watch what the weight does even here: lean all the way toward `R_nat` and I get Bayes; lean all the way toward `R_bdy` and I get the constant classifier; in between I trade. That's exactly the dial.

Now, both terms are 0-1 losses, so neither is trainable yet. Take the natural term first, because that's a solved problem and I should reuse the solution rather than reinvent it. Bartlett, Jordan and McAuliffe's calibration theory says: pick a surrogate margin loss `phi` (hinge, logistic, exponential, sigmoid) and minimize `R_phi(f) = E phi(f(X) Y)`. A surrogate is *classification-calibrated* if being forced to take the wrong sign strictly costs you — with `C_eta(alpha) = eta phi(alpha) + (1-eta) phi(-alpha)`, `H(eta) = inf_alpha C_eta(alpha)`, and `H^-(eta) = inf_{alpha (2eta-1) <= 0} C_eta(alpha)`, calibration means `H^-(eta) > H(eta)` for `eta != 1/2`. From that they construct the transform `psi = (psi~)^{**}`, the convex hull of `psi~(theta) = H^-((1+theta)/2) - H((1+theta)/2)`, and prove `psi` is non-decreasing, continuous, convex on `[0,1]`, `psi(0)=0`, and crucially that excess 0-1 risk is squeezed by excess surrogate risk: `R_nat(f) - R_nat^* <= psi^{-1}(R_phi(f) - R_phi^*)`. For hinge, `psi` is the identity, so the bound is almost embarrassingly direct. Great: the natural term `R_nat - R_nat^*` is bounded by `psi^{-1}` of something differentiable I already know how to minimize. One term down.

The boundary term `R_bdy = Pr[x in B(DB(f), eps), f(x) y > 0]` is the one with no off-the-shelf surrogate, and it's the whole novelty, so let me grind on it. First a trivial but useful slackening: drop the "correctly classified" qualifier and just bound by the probability of being near the boundary at all, `R_bdy <= Pr[x in B(DB(f), eps)]`. I lose a little (I now also count some already-misclassified points), but I gain a quantity that no longer references the label `y` — and that is going to matter, because a term that doesn't need labels is a term I can also estimate from unlabeled data, and more immediately it tells me the boundary term is about the *geometry of `f` near `x`*, not about correctness. Now, what does "`x` is within `eps` of the boundary" mean operationally? It means there's a point `x'` in the ball where `f` has the opposite sign, i.e. where the predicted label differs from the one at `x`. So

```
Pr[x in B(DB(f), eps)] = E max_{x' in B(x, eps)} 1{ f(x) f(x') <= 0 }.
```

That's still a 0-1 indicator, but now it's an indicator of *prediction disagreement between the center and a perturbed point*, which is a far more natural thing to relax than "near a boundary." Rewrite the disagreement as a sign condition: crossing the boundary means `f(x')` and `f(x)` have non-positive product, and scaling by any positive `lambda` doesn't change that sign relation, so I can use `1{ f(x) f(x') / lambda <= 0 }`. Why drag in a `lambda`? Because I'm about to upper-bound this indicator by a surrogate `phi`, and `phi` is a *margin* loss -- it cares about the magnitude of its argument, not just its sign -- so the scale at which I feed it `f(x) f(x')` is a genuine free parameter, a margin width. The pointwise step has to be exact: for the standard margin losses on the table, `phi` is nonnegative, non-increasing, and satisfies `phi(0) >= 1`. Then if `z > 0`, `1{z <= 0}=0 <= phi(z)`; if `z <= 0`, monotonicity gives `phi(z) >= phi(0) >= 1 = 1{z <= 0}`. So `phi(f(x)f(x')/lambda) >= 1{f(x)f(x')/lambda <= 0}` pointwise, and therefore

```
R_bdy <= E max_{x' in B(x, eps)} 1{ f(x) f(x') / lambda <= 0 }
      <= E max_{x' in B(x, eps)} phi( f(x) f(x') / lambda ).
```

There it is — a *differentiable* upper bound on the boundary error, and it's an expectation of a worst-case-over-the-ball surrogate evaluated on the product `f(x) f(x')`, the agreement between the clean score and the perturbed score. Not the label. The clean prediction `f(x)` plays the role of the target.

Now stack the two pieces. From the decomposition, `R_adv - R_nat^* = (R_nat - R_nat^*) + R_bdy`, and I have a differentiable upper bound for each:

```
R_adv(f) - R_nat^*  <=  psi^{-1}( R_phi(f) - R_phi^* )  +  E max_{x' in B(x, eps)} phi( f(x) f(x') / lambda ).
```

This is the object I actually want to push down, and it reads beautifully: the first term is "be accurate on clean data," the second is "don't let any nearby perturbation disagree with your clean prediction." The first is the calibrated natural surrogate; the second is a *robustness regularizer* that measures the difference between `f(x)` and `f(x')` rather than between `f(x')` and `y`. That difference from Madry is the entire point -- Madry's inner loss is `phi(f(x') y)`, perturbed-vs-label; mine is `phi(f(x) f(x') / lambda)`, perturbed-vs-clean-prediction. Mine pushes the boundary *away from the data* by making the score keep the same sign across the ball, without asking the perturbed point to match the label. The scale `lambda` controls how hard disagreement is punished: with small `lambda`, a negative product becomes a large negative margin and the loss becomes severe, while a confidently positive product can drive the loss down; with large `lambda`, the boundary term becomes a gentler pressure relative to the natural surrogate. Check it against the toy example: as `lambda -> +inf` the regularizer effectively stops shaping the solution and I'm left minimizing the natural surrogate, which gives Bayes; as `lambda -> 0` the regularizer forces a constant sign, giving the all-ones robust solution. The knob does exactly what the decomposition promised.

I should pause and ask whether this upper bound is any good, because "an upper bound" is cheap -- Madry has one too, and my whole complaint was that his might be loose. Can I show mine is *tight*, that no differentiable bound of this shape does uniformly better? Let me try to construct a worst case that saturates it. Fix a target excess robust error `theta in [0,1]`. Since `psi` is the convex envelope of `psi~`, for any small `xi` I can pick `gamma, alpha_1, alpha_2 in [0,1]` with `theta = gamma alpha_1 + (1-gamma) alpha_2` and `psi(theta) >= gamma psi~(alpha_1) + (1-gamma) psi~(alpha_2) - xi/3`; the two-point mixture can come within slack of the envelope value. Build a two-atom distribution on `{x_1, x_2}` with `Pr[X = x_1] = gamma`, `Pr[X = x_2] = 1-gamma`, and conditional label probabilities `eta(x_1) = (1+alpha_1)/2`, `eta(x_2) = (1+alpha_2)/2`. Choose `f` negative everywhere -- so it predicts a constant `-1` and has no boundary on the instance space -- but tuned so that `C_{eta(x_i)}(f(x_i)) <= H^-(eta(x_i)) + xi/3` at each atom, i.e. `f` is as good as it can be subject to disagreeing with the Bayes sign. Because `eta(x_i) >= 1/2`, the Bayes classifier is positive on both atoms. Then make `lambda` small enough that the boundary surrogate `E max phi(f(x) f(x')/lambda)` is below some tiny `eps'`: all products are positive, so division by a small `lambda` sends the margin to `+inf`, and the usual losses satisfy `phi(z) -> 0` as `z -> +inf`. Now compute the surrogate excess: `R_phi(f) - R_phi^* = gamma[C_{eta(x_1)}(f(x_1)) - H(eta(x_1))] + (1-gamma)[C_{eta(x_2)}(f(x_2)) - H(eta(x_2))] <= gamma psi~(alpha_1) + (1-gamma) psi~(alpha_2) + xi/3 <= psi(theta) + 2xi/3 <= psi(theta - boundary_term) + xi`, using continuity of `psi` to absorb the tiny boundary term. The lower inequality is already forced by the upper-bound theorem. On the risk side, the boundary is empty, and outside it the constant negative classifier disagrees with the positive Bayes classifier everywhere, so the risk equality gives `R_adv(f) - R_nat^* = E|2eta(X) - 1| = gamma alpha_1 + (1-gamma) alpha_2 = theta`. So for this instance the achieved excess robust error is exactly `theta`, and the surrogate excess is squeezed to `psi(theta - boundary_term)` up to `xi`. Inverting, my upper bound is met up to arbitrarily small slack. The bound is *tight* -- there is no uniformly sharper differentiable surrogate bound of this form. This is the thing Madry's formulation can't claim, and it's why I trust the regularizer is the *right* one and not just *a* one.

Now I have to turn the population bound into an algorithm, and three gaps stand between me and code. The theory is binary (`y in {-1,+1}`, scalar `f`), real problems are multi-class. The `psi^{-1}` is a nuisance — I don't want to compute the inverse `psi`-transform every step. And the inner `max_{x' in B(x,eps)}` is the same intractable maximization I started with.

The multi-class gap first. The clean statement of calibration generalizes: a multi-class surrogate is *calibrated* if minimizers of the surrogate risk also minimize the 0-1 risk, and cross-entropy is such a loss. So replace the binary `phi(f(x) y)` with multi-class cross-entropy `L(f(x), y)` for the accuracy term, where now `f(x)` is the logit vector (softmax on top). For the boundary term I had `phi(f(x) f(x') / lambda)`, a surrogate measuring *disagreement between the clean and perturbed predictions*. Its multi-class analogue is a discrepancy between the two output distributions, `L(f(x), f(x'))` -- cross-entropy with the clean prediction `f(x)` as the soft target. For the inner maximization over `x'`, cross-entropy with target `p(x)` and `KL(p(x) || p(x'))` have the same `x'` gradient because the entropy of `p(x)` is constant with respect to `x'`. The KL form also makes the regularizer exactly zero when `p(x') = p(x)`, which is the clean "no disagreement" baseline, so the implementation should maximize and then minimize `KL(p(x) || p(x'))`. The multi-class objective becomes

```
min_f  E { L(f(x), y)  +  (1 / lambda) * max_{x' in B(x, eps)} KL( p(x) || p(x') ) }.
```

The `psi^{-1}` gap: do I really need to apply the inverse transform around the natural term? In principle the bound contains `psi^{-1}(R_phi - R_phi^*)`, and a nonlinear transform inside a sum is not something I can simply erase. But in a deep-network training objective I am already making two approximations: replacing population risks by minibatches and replacing the exact inner maximizer by PGD. Computing an explicit `psi^{-1}` for the chosen loss would add machinery whose main practical effect is to rescale how strongly the accuracy term competes with the boundary term. I already need a scalar to set that competition, so I use the monotonicity of `psi^{-1}` as guidance, drop the explicit transform as a heuristic simplification, and let `lambda` carry the trade-off. The trainable objective is `L(f(x), y) + (1/lambda) * boundary_term`; write `beta = 1/lambda` for the implementation weight, so `beta` large means robustness-heavy.

The inner `max` gap is the operational heart. I need, per training point, the perturbation `x'` in `B(x, eps)` that *most* increases `KL(p(x) || p(x'))` — the worst disagreement with the clean prediction. This is a constrained maximization of a differentiable function over an `l_inf` box, exactly the shape PGD handles: ascend along the gradient, project back into the box. Under an `l_inf` budget the steepest ascent direction is the sign of the gradient (the same linearization that gives FGSM its sign), so the inner loop is `x' <- Pi_{B(x,eps)}( x' + eta_1 sign( grad_{x'} KL(p(x) || p(x')) ) )`, repeated `K` times, where `Pi` clips back into the `eps`-box and into the valid image range `[0,1]`.

There's a subtlety in *where to start* the inner ascent, and it bit me the moment I wrote `grad_{x'} KL(p(x) || p(x'))`. Consider the inner objective as a function of `x'`: `g(x') = KL(p(x) || p(x'))`. At `x' = x` it is exactly zero — that's its global *minimum*, since KL is non-negative and zero iff the distributions match. And at a minimum the gradient vanishes. So if I initialize the attack at `x' = x`, the very first gradient is zero and the ascent never leaves the start. I have to break that stationarity. Initialize instead at a tiny random nudge off the center, `x' = x + 0.001 * N(0, I)` — small enough to stay essentially at `x`, large enough that `grad g` is nonzero and the ascent can climb. (A uniform nudge inside the box would also work; what matters is breaking the symmetry at the global min.) Madry initializes randomly too, but for him it's about not getting stuck in a bad local max of the *label* loss; for me it's structurally forced, because my inner objective's minimum sits exactly at the point I'd otherwise start from.

One more direction choice I should justify rather than assume: `KL(p(x) || p(x'))`, with the clean distribution as the *first* argument. I want the adversarial output to be pulled toward the clean output, and I want the clean output to act as the anchor during the inner attack. Forward KL `KL(p(x) || p(x'))` treats `p(x)` as the reference measure; the inner attack maximizes the divergence of the perturbed prediction *from that anchor*, and the outer minimization then drives the perturbed prediction *back to* it. In PyTorch's KL convention, that is `KLDivLoss(log_softmax(model(x_adv)), softmax(model(x_natural)))`: the log-probability input is the adversarial branch, and the probability target is the natural branch. During the inner attack I only take gradients with respect to `x_adv`; during the outer update I recompute the two branches and let the loss follow the implementation exactly. It also has the right invariance: the regularizer is zero exactly when the perturbed prediction equals the clean one, which is the flatness I'm trying to induce.

Now alternate the two optimizations — that's heuristic, the objective is a min over `theta` of an `E` of a `max` over `x'`, and I'm solving the inner `max` approximately by `K` PGD steps and the outer `min` by one SGD step on the resulting point, then repeating. For each minibatch: freeze the network, run `K` sign-gradient ascent steps on the KL to get `x'_i` for each `x_i`; then unfreeze and take the gradient of `L(f(x_i), y_i) + beta * KL(p(x_i) || p(x'_i))` and step `theta`. Freezing during the inner attack matters in practice because batch-norm statistics shouldn't shift while I'm crafting perturbations, so the network runs in eval mode for the inner loop and train mode for the outer update.

Let me also note what this buys beyond the trade-off knob. Because the boundary regularizer never references the label, the whole second term can be estimated from *unlabeled* data — more unlabeled points just sharpen the expectation `E max KL(...)`. So the formulation drops straight into a semi-supervised setting, where labels feed only the cross-entropy term. I won't build that now, but it's a free consequence of having decoupled robustness from the label, and it confirms the decomposition was the right cut.

Let me write the training loss in the same function shape as the adversarial-training harness: generate the worst-disagreement perturbation, then return clean CE plus the weighted KL regularizer for the outer SGD step.

```python
import torch
import torch.nn as nn
import torch.nn.functional as F


def trades_loss(model, x_natural, y, optimizer,
                step_size=0.003, epsilon=0.031,
                perturb_steps=10, beta=6.0, distance='l_inf'):
    criterion_kl = nn.KLDivLoss(reduction='sum')
    model.eval()
    batch_size = len(x_natural)

    # Inner maximization: start just off x, then ascend the KL disagreement.
    x_adv = x_natural.detach() + 0.001 * torch.randn_like(x_natural).detach()
    if distance == 'l_inf':
        for _ in range(perturb_steps):
            x_adv.requires_grad_(True)
            with torch.enable_grad():
                loss_kl = criterion_kl(F.log_softmax(model(x_adv), dim=1),
                                       F.softmax(model(x_natural), dim=1))
            grad = torch.autograd.grad(loss_kl, [x_adv])[0]
            x_adv = x_adv.detach() + step_size * torch.sign(grad.detach())
            x_adv = torch.min(torch.max(x_adv, x_natural - epsilon),
                              x_natural + epsilon)
            x_adv = torch.clamp(x_adv, 0.0, 1.0)
    else:
        raise NotImplementedError("this derivation uses the l_inf branch")

    # Outer minimization: clean CE plus beta times KL(p_natural || p_adv).
    model.train()
    x_adv = torch.clamp(x_adv, 0.0, 1.0)
    optimizer.zero_grad()
    logits = model(x_natural)
    loss_natural = F.cross_entropy(logits, y)
    loss_robust = (1.0 / batch_size) * criterion_kl(
        F.log_softmax(model(x_adv), dim=1),
        F.softmax(model(x_natural), dim=1))
    loss = loss_natural + beta * loss_robust
    return loss
```

So the causal chain. I refused the single-term min-max because it has no dial for the robustness/accuracy trade-off and only a possibly-loose upper bound on the robust error. Decomposing the robust error exactly into natural error plus boundary error — correctly-classified points crowding the decision surface — split the problem into the thing accuracy cares about and the thing fragility cares about, and a toy oscillating-label example proved the two optima are genuinely different classifiers, so a weight between the terms is not optional. Calibration theory handled the natural term: excess 0-1 risk is bounded by `psi^{-1}` of excess surrogate risk. For the boundary term I bounded "near the boundary" by "some perturbation disagrees with the clean prediction," wrote disagreement as a sign condition, and upper-bounded the indicator by a margin surrogate `phi(f(x) f(x')/lambda)`, giving a differentiable regularizer that measures perturbed-vs-clean rather than perturbed-vs-label — the decisive structural difference from Madry. Stacking the two gave the trainable upper bound `psi^{-1}(R_phi - R_phi^*) + E max phi(f(x)f(x')/lambda)`, and a two-atom construction showed it is tight up to arbitrary slack, so the regularizer is the right one. Crossing to practice: cross-entropy for the multi-class calibrated loss, KL between clean and perturbed predictions for the boundary surrogate, a tuned `beta = 1/lambda` after dropping the explicit `psi^{-1}` from the optimized loss, and PGD sign-gradient ascent for the inner max — started at a tiny random nudge because the clean point is the regularizer's own global minimum with zero gradient. The result is clean CE plus a `beta`-weighted KL stability term, alternating inner attack and outer SGD step, with `beta` the knob that buys robustness with clean accuracy at a rate I now understand.
