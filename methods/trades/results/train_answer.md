We want a classifier $\text{sign}(f(x))$, with $f$ a deep network, that is accurate not just on a clean labeled point $(x,y)$ but on the worst input an adversary can pick inside the $l_\infty$ ball $B(x,\epsilon) = \{x' : \|x'-x\|_\infty \le \epsilon\}$. The honest object to minimize is the robust error, the probability that *some* point in the ball is misclassified, $R_{\text{adv}}(f) = \mathbb{E}\,\mathbf{1}\{\exists\, x' \in B(x,\epsilon)\ \text{s.t.}\ f(x')y \le 0\}$, against the ordinary natural error $R_{\text{nat}}(f) = \mathbb{E}\,\mathbf{1}\{f(x)y \le 0\}$. Two difficulties hit at once. The inner $\exists$ is a maximum over a continuum and the loss is a 0-1 indicator, so the objective is NP-hard and non-differentiable. And the deeper one: pushing a model harder toward robustness reliably degrades its clean accuracy, so any defense must let me say *how much* clean accuracy I am willing to spend, not leave it to chance.

The standard answer is Madry's saddle point, $\min_\theta \mathbb{E}\,\max_{\|\delta\|_\infty \le \epsilon} L(f_\theta(x+\delta), y)$, with the inner max solved by a few steps of projected gradient ascent and the parameters trained on those worst-case points. It is the strongest empirical defense of its time, but look at what its objective is: $\mathbb{E}\,\max_{x'} \phi(f(x')y)$, the surrogate loss evaluated at the worst point against the *true label*. It is a single number, an upper bound on $R_{\text{adv}}$, and that bound need not be tight in a complicated landscape. Worse, it carries exactly one term, so there is no place in it to dial the balance between being right on clean $x$ and being stable across the ball. Clean accuracy just falls out as a byproduct, and what falls out is often bad. The regularization defenses (Kurakin et al., Ross & Doshi-Velez, Zheng et al.'s stability training, Miyato et al.'s VAT) and Adversarial Logit Pairing all add some stability penalty, but none ties its regularizer to the robust error by a guarantee, and several compare the perturbed prediction to the *label* or to a *Gaussian-noised* copy rather than controlling worst-case prediction stability. Certified defenses optimize a provable bound but ignore clean performance and scale poorly. So the trade-off is left untreated everywhere.

The solution is TRADES — TRadeoff-inspired Adversarial DEfense via Surrogate-loss minimization. The starting move is to stop minimizing one number and instead decompose the robust error exactly. Take a robustly-misclassified $x$: either I already get the center wrong, or I get it right but it sits within $\epsilon$ of my decision surface and a permitted nudge tips it over. These two cases are disjoint and exhaustive. Naming the boundary $DB(f) = \{x : f(x)=0\}$ and its $\epsilon$-neighborhood $B(DB(f),\epsilon) = \{x : \exists\, x' \in B(x,\epsilon),\ f(x)f(x') \le 0\}$, and defining the boundary error as the mass of *correctly* classified points in that band, $R_{\text{bdy}}(f) = \mathbb{E}\,\mathbf{1}\{x \in B(DB(f),\epsilon),\ f(x)y > 0\}$, the two buckets tile the robustly-wrong set exactly:
$$R_{\text{adv}}(f) = R_{\text{nat}}(f) + R_{\text{bdy}}(f).$$
This is an equality, not a bound — a robust mistake with $f(x)y \le 0$ is counted once in $R_{\text{nat}}$ and is excluded from the $f(x)y>0$ event of $R_{\text{bdy}}$, while a robust mistake with $f(x)y>0$ admits a flipping $x'$, which is precisely a correctly-classified point near the boundary. The point of the split is that clean accuracy lives entirely in $R_{\text{nat}}$ and the extra fragility lives entirely in $R_{\text{bdy}}$, so a weight between them *is* the trade-off knob I was missing.

That the conflict is real, and not a capacity artifact dissolved by a bigger model, comes out of a clean example. Put $X$ uniform on $[0,1]$ with a deterministic label oscillating with period $2\epsilon$. The Bayes classifier tracks the oscillation and has clean error $0$, but every point sits within $\epsilon$ of a flip, so $R_{\text{bdy}}=1$ and $R_{\text{adv}}=1$. The constant all-ones classifier is wrong half the time, $R_{\text{nat}}=1/2$, but has *no* boundary in $[0,1]$, so $R_{\text{bdy}}=0$ and $R_{\text{adv}}=1/2$ — strictly more robust than Bayes, while Bayes is strictly more accurate. The accuracy-optimal and robustness-optimal models are genuinely different classifiers, and a single-objective method cannot be right in general. Leaning the weight toward $R_{\text{nat}}$ recovers Bayes; leaning it toward $R_{\text{bdy}}$ recovers the constant classifier; in between, it trades.

Both terms are 0-1 losses, so neither is trainable yet. The natural term is a solved problem: by the calibration theory of Bartlett, Jordan & McAuliffe (2006), pick a classification-calibrated margin surrogate $\phi$ and minimize $R_\phi(f) = \mathbb{E}\,\phi(f(X)Y)$; with the $\psi$-transform $\psi = (\tilde\psi)^{**}$ built from the conditional risk, excess 0-1 risk is squeezed by excess surrogate risk, $R_{\text{nat}}(f) - R_{\text{nat}}^* \le \psi^{-1}(R_\phi(f) - R_\phi^*)$ (for hinge, $\psi$ is the identity). The boundary term is the whole novelty and has no off-the-shelf surrogate. First slacken: drop the "correctly classified" qualifier, $R_{\text{bdy}} \le \Pr[x \in B(DB(f),\epsilon)]$, which buys a quantity that no longer references the label $y$ at all — it is purely about the geometry of $f$ near $x$. Being within $\epsilon$ of the boundary means some $x'$ in the ball has the opposite sign, so $\Pr[x \in B(DB(f),\epsilon)] = \mathbb{E}\,\max_{x' \in B(x,\epsilon)} \mathbf{1}\{f(x)f(x') \le 0\}$, an indicator of *prediction disagreement between the center and a perturbed point*. Since scaling by any $\lambda > 0$ preserves the sign relation, write it as $\mathbf{1}\{f(x)f(x')/\lambda \le 0\}$, where $\lambda$ is a genuine free margin width because the surrogate $\phi$ I am about to apply cares about magnitude, not just sign. For the standard margin losses, $\phi$ is nonnegative, non-increasing, and $\phi(0) \ge 1$: if $z>0$ then $\mathbf{1}\{z\le 0\}=0 \le \phi(z)$, and if $z \le 0$ then $\phi(z) \ge \phi(0) \ge 1 = \mathbf{1}\{z \le 0\}$, so pointwise $\phi(f(x)f(x')/\lambda) \ge \mathbf{1}\{f(x)f(x')/\lambda \le 0\}$ and therefore $R_{\text{bdy}} \le \mathbb{E}\,\max_{x'} \phi(f(x)f(x')/\lambda)$. Stacking the two pieces gives the object I actually push down:
$$R_{\text{adv}}(f) - R_{\text{nat}}^* \;\le\; \psi^{-1}\!\big(R_\phi(f) - R_\phi^*\big) \;+\; \mathbb{E}\,\max_{x' \in B(x,\epsilon)} \phi\!\big(f(x)f(x')/\lambda\big).$$
The first term is "be accurate on clean data"; the second is a robustness regularizer measuring the difference between $f(x)$ and $f(x')$ — perturbed-vs-clean-prediction — rather than between $f(x')$ and $y$. That is the decisive structural difference from Madry, whose inner loss is $\phi(f(x')y)$. Mine pushes the boundary away from the data by keeping the score's sign constant across the ball, without asking the perturbed point to match the label. The scale $\lambda$ is the dial: as $\lambda \to \infty$ the regularizer stops shaping the solution and I recover Bayes from the natural surrogate; as $\lambda \to 0$ it forces a constant sign and recovers the robust all-ones solution. And this bound is tight — a two-atom construction, mixing two points whose mixture comes within slack of the $\psi$-envelope and using a constant negative $f$ with $\lambda$ small enough to drive the boundary surrogate to zero, achieves excess robust error exactly $\theta$ while the surrogate excess is squeezed to $\psi(\theta)$ up to arbitrary slack. So there is no uniformly sharper differentiable surrogate bound of this form, which is what Madry's formulation cannot claim.

Crossing to a trainable multi-class algorithm closes three gaps. Cross-entropy is a calibrated multi-class loss, so it replaces $\phi(f(x)y)$ for the accuracy term with $f(x)$ now the logit vector. For the boundary term, the multi-class analogue of "disagreement between the clean and perturbed predictions" is a divergence between the two output distributions; I use the KL divergence $\text{KL}(p(x)\,\|\,p(x'))$ with $p = \text{softmax}(f)$ and the clean distribution as the anchor, because it is exactly zero when $p(x')=p(x)$ — the flatness I want to induce — and shares the same $x'$-gradient as cross-entropy with target $p(x)$ (the entropy of $p(x)$ is constant in $x'$). The $\psi^{-1}$ around the natural term is dropped as a training heuristic: its main practical effect is to rescale how strongly accuracy competes with the boundary term, and I already need a scalar for that competition, so I write $\beta = 1/\lambda$ and let it carry the trade-off, large $\beta$ meaning robustness-heavy. The objective becomes
$$\min_f\ \mathbb{E}\,\Big\{\,\text{CE}(f(x), y) \;+\; \beta\,\max_{x' \in B(x,\epsilon)} \text{KL}\big(p(x)\,\|\,p(x')\big)\,\Big\}.$$
The inner $\max$ is the operational heart: per training point I need the $x'$ in $B(x,\epsilon)$ that most increases $\text{KL}(p(x)\,\|\,p(x'))$, a constrained maximization over an $l_\infty$ box, which is exactly PGD's shape. Under an $l_\infty$ budget the steepest ascent direction is the sign of the gradient, so the inner loop is $x' \leftarrow \Pi_{B(x,\epsilon)}\big(x' + \eta_1\,\text{sign}(\nabla_{x'}\text{KL}(p(x)\,\|\,p(x')))\big)$ for $K$ steps, projecting back into the $\epsilon$-box and into $[0,1]$. There is a subtlety in where to start: at $x'=x$ the inner objective is exactly zero, its global *minimum* (KL is non-negative, zero iff the distributions match), so its gradient vanishes and the ascent never leaves the center. The fix is structurally forced — initialize at a tiny random nudge $x' = x + 0.001\,\mathcal{N}(0,I)$, small enough to stay essentially at $x$ but large enough to break the stationarity. This differs from Madry's random start in motivation: for him it avoids bad local maxima of the *label* loss; for me the clean point *is* the regularizer's own global minimum. Finally, alternate the two optimizations heuristically — freeze the network (eval mode, so batch-norm statistics do not shift while crafting perturbations), run $K$ sign-gradient ascent steps to get $x'$, then unfreeze (train mode) and take one SGD step on $\text{CE}(f(x),y) + \beta\,\text{KL}(p(x)\,\|\,p(x'))$. In PyTorch's KL convention this is `KLDivLoss(log_softmax(model(x_adv)), softmax(model(x_natural)))`, the adversarial branch as the log-probability input and the natural branch as the probability target. Because the boundary regularizer never references the label, the whole second term can be estimated from unlabeled data, so the formulation extends directly to semi-supervised training — a free consequence of having decoupled robustness from the label. Standard settings: $\beta = 6$ for the robustness-leaning regime ($\beta \in [1,10]$ useful), nudge scale $0.001$; CIFAR-10 with $\epsilon = 0.031$, $\eta_1 = 0.007$, $K=10$, WRN-34-10; MNIST with $\epsilon = 0.3$, $\eta_1 = 0.01$, $K=40$.

```python
import torch
import torch.nn as nn
import torch.nn.functional as F


def trades_loss(model, x_natural, y, optimizer,
                step_size=0.003, epsilon=0.031, perturb_steps=10, beta=6.0):
    """Clean CE + beta * KL(p(x) || p(x')) boundary regularizer (l_inf)."""
    criterion_kl = nn.KLDivLoss(reduction='sum')
    model.eval()
    batch_size = len(x_natural)

    # inner maximization: x' that most disagrees with the clean prediction.
    # start off-center: x is the global minimum of KL(p(x) || p(x')).
    x_adv = x_natural.detach() + 0.001 * torch.randn_like(x_natural).detach()
    for _ in range(perturb_steps):
        x_adv.requires_grad_(True)
        with torch.enable_grad():
            loss_kl = criterion_kl(F.log_softmax(model(x_adv), dim=1),
                                   F.softmax(model(x_natural), dim=1))
        grad = torch.autograd.grad(loss_kl, [x_adv])[0]
        x_adv = x_adv.detach() + step_size * torch.sign(grad.detach())     # l_inf sign step
        x_adv = torch.min(torch.max(x_adv, x_natural - epsilon),
                          x_natural + epsilon)                              # project into eps-box
        x_adv = torch.clamp(x_adv, 0.0, 1.0)                               # project into [0,1]

    model.train()
    x_adv = torch.clamp(x_adv, 0.0, 1.0)
    optimizer.zero_grad()

    # outer minimization: natural CE + beta * boundary KL.
    logits = model(x_natural)
    loss_natural = F.cross_entropy(logits, y)
    loss_robust = (1.0 / batch_size) * criterion_kl(
        F.log_softmax(model(x_adv), dim=1),
        F.softmax(model(x_natural), dim=1))                                # KL(p_nat || p_adv)
    loss = loss_natural + beta * loss_robust
    return loss
```
