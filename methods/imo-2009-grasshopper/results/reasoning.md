Let me translate the jumps into partial sums. Once I choose an order, the landing points are increasing partial sums, and the last one is forced to be the total $S$. Since $S$ is not mined, the only possible trouble is before the last jump.

My first instinct is to count the bad orderings and hope they don't cover everything. There are $N!$ orderings; a mine at value $\mu$ kills only those whose partial sums include $\mu$, which needs some subset of lengths to sum to $\mu$ and then any arrangement of that subset and of the rest — at most $j!\,(N-j)!$ orderings for a $j$-element subset. I want the total killed to fall short of $N!$. But a single mine can be a subset-sum in many ways, and $j!(N-j)!$ is enormous when $j$ is near $0$ or $N$, so $N-1$ mines can easily account for more than $N!$ orderings by this crude bound. The union bound has no margin. Counting the bad orderings is hopeless; I'll have to construct a good one.

Let me feel out the small cases. With $N=2$ and lengths $a_1<a_2$, the single intermediate landing is either $a_1$ or $a_2$, and one lone mine can't equal both, so one of the two orders is clean — two choices, one mine, a choice survives. With $N=3$, the first landing has three options and the last intermediate landing $S-a_i$ also has three options, but the middle gets tangled by coincidences like $a_i+a_j=a_k+a_l$; the endpoints of the path are where I have clean freedom and the interior is the swamp. That "more landing options than mines at the controllable ends" is the smell I'll chase.

To get a recursion I'd love to peel off one jump and shrink the problem, but the naive peel stalls: removing a jump does nothing to the mine count unless that jump leaps a mine, and if every mine sits early I still face $N-1$ mines with only $N-1$ jumps. So each peel must launder at least one mine. And there's a second leak to plug first. If the smallest length in the whole set is $a_{\min}$, then every non-final landing point is at most $S-a_{\min}$. Mines past that point are spectators. So the induction should count only the mines in the active window, not every positive mine on the line.

I will sort the current lengths as
$$0<a_1<a_2<\cdots<a_N,$$
and prove the stronger statement with total $S=a_1+\cdots+a_N$: if $S$ is not mined and at most $N-1$ mines lie in the active window $(0,S-a_1]$, then the lengths can be ordered so that no landing point is mined. This contains the original problem, because there are only $N-1$ mines altogether, and a landing before the end cannot get past $S-a_1$.

For $N=1$ the active window is $(0,0]$, so there is nothing to avoid before the only landing at $S$. Now I assume the stronger statement for smaller sets. The largest jump is tempting to save for the end, because if I can stand safely at
$$x=S-a_N,$$
then the jump by $a_N$ lands at the safe endpoint $S$ and crosses every mine between $x$ and $S$ without landing there. I only have to check whether the smaller problem that reaches $x$ has the right mine budget in its own active window.

Suppose first that $x$ is not mined and there is an active mine strictly to the right of $x$. Since there are at most $N-1$ active mines in total, at most $N-2$ active mines remain at or below $x$, and therefore at most $N-2$ lie in the smaller active window $(0,x-a_1]$ for the lengths $a_1,\ldots,a_{N-1}$. The endpoint $x$ is safe, so induction orders those $N-1$ lengths and reaches $x$ without landing on a mine. The final jump $a_N$ lands at $S$ and crosses the active mines to the right of $x$. This case balances exactly because at least one active mine has been kept out of the recursive budget.

Now suppose that $x$ is not mined and there is no active mine strictly to the right of $x$. If there are no active mines, any order is safe. Otherwise I cannot blindly recurse, because all $N-1$ active mines may sit on the smaller side while the recursive problem allows only $N-2$. I remove one mine and plan to repair the damage if it is hit. Let $m$ be the largest active mine. After temporarily ignoring $m$, at most $N-2$ active mines remain anywhere, hence at most $N-2$ lie in $(0,x-a_1]$, and induction gives a path to $x$ using $a_1,\ldots,a_{N-1}$ that avoids every active mine except possibly $m$. If this path misses $m$, I append $a_N$.

If the path hits $m$, it can do so only once. Let the offending hop have length $a_k$, and let the position just before it be $p$, so $p+a_k=m$. I put the larger jump $a_N$ in that place, keep the following jumps in their old order, and save $a_k$ for the end. The bad landing becomes
$$p+a_N=m+(a_N-a_k)>m.$$
Every following pre-final landing is shifted right by the same amount $a_N-a_k$, and just before the final saved jump I am at $S-a_k$, still no farther than $S-a_1$. Since $m$ is the largest active mine, those shifted pre-final landings lie beyond every active mine but still within the active range, and $S$ itself is safe. The one mine I removed from the recursive budget has been pushed behind the path.

If $x$ is mined and there is no active mine strictly to the right of $x$, I can use the same swap with the offending landing forced to be the endpoint $x$. Removing $x$ leaves at most $N-2$ active mines for the recursive call on $a_1,\ldots,a_{N-1}$, and the call reaches $x$ while avoiding all the other active mines. Let the last hop into $x$ have length $a_k$, from $p=x-a_k$. Replacing that last hop by $a_N$ lands at
$$p+a_N=x+(a_N-a_k)=S-a_k,$$
which is strictly to the right of $x$ and still at most $S-a_1$; there are no active mines there. Then the saved jump $a_k$ lands at $S$. So the mined point $x$ is crossed without being a landing point.

The remaining case is that $x$ is mined and there is also an active mine $z$ to the right of $x$. A single final largest jump asks me to stand on the mined point $x$, so I need the ending to have two jumps. For each $i<N$, the ending I would like is
$$y_i=S-a_N-a_i=x-a_i,\qquad p_i=S-a_i=y_i+a_N,$$
followed by
$$y_i\longrightarrow p_i\longrightarrow S.$$
The points $y_i$ all lie below $x$ and are distinct; the points $p_i$ all lie above $x$ and are distinct; no $y_i$ can equal a $p_j$ because $y_i<x<p_j$. The mined point $x$ is not one of them. Thus any active mine other than $x$ blocks at most one candidate pair, by being either its $y_i$ or its $p_i$. There are $N-1$ candidate pairs and at most $N-2$ blocking mines other than $x$, so the count is blockers plus one: at least one pair has both $y_i$ and $p_i$ unmined.

Fix such an $i$. The remaining lengths, all except $a_i$ and $a_N$, sum to $y_i$. The two active mines $x$ and $z$ both lie to the right of $y_i$, so among the active mines at most $(N-1)-2=N-3$ can lie at or below $y_i$, and therefore at most $N-3$ lie in the active window for this smaller set of $N-2$ lengths. Since $y_i$ itself is safe, induction reaches $y_i$ safely with those lengths. Then I jump by $a_N$ to the safe point $p_i$, and by $a_i$ to $S$. The first of these final jumps crosses $x$ because $y_i<x<p_i$. The mine $z$ is not $p_i$, so if $z<p_i$ the first final jump crosses it, and if $z>p_i$ the last jump crosses it. The two-jump ending consumes exactly the two active mines that made the one-jump ending fail.

Those four cases cover whether $x$ is mined and whether an active mine lies to its right. Each recursive call sees the smaller active-window budget it needs, and each repair or double jump accounts for the mines removed from that budget. The strengthened statement follows by induction, and the original set of jumps therefore has a mine-avoiding order.
