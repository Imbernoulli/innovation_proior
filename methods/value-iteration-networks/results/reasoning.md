OK, let me think this through from scratch. I have a grid-world: an agent sees a map — obstacles scattered around, a goal somewhere — and has to move to the goal without hitting obstacles. I have lots of these maps, each with a different obstacle/goal arrangement, and for each I have optimal shortest-path demonstrations. I train a convolutional net to map (map image, my current position) to the next action, the way everyone trains a deep policy now: conv stack for perception, fully connected layers, softmax over the eight moves, cross-entropy against the expert's action. On the maps it trained on, it's fine. On a *new* map drawn from the same distribution, it falls apart. And I want to understand exactly *why* it falls apart, because the failure mode is going to tell me what to build.

Here's the thing that nags at me. When I look at the held-out per-step loss, it's not bad — it's roughly in the same ballpark as a real planner's. But when I actually roll the policy out and ask "did the trajectory reach the goal," the success rate is far lower than the planner, and — this is the part that really bothers me — the gap gets *worse* as the map gets bigger. An $8\times8$ map, the reactive net does okay; $16\times16$, it sags; $28\times28$, it's hopeless, while a planner barely notices the size change. If this were ordinary overfitting, the per-step loss would be the tell. It isn't. The per-step loss says "I'm predicting most steps about as well as a planner." The success rate says "and yet I don't actually get there." So the errors aren't random — the net is nailing the locally easy steps (when there's a clear shot toward the goal, just go that way) and blowing the steps that require actually reasoning about the whole layout (when you have to go *away* from the goal to round a wall). On a small map there aren't many of those decisive steps and you can sometimes stumble through; on a big map, one such miss kills the whole trajectory, and there are many of them.

So what is the reactive net actually computing? It's a fixed feedforward function from the current observation to an action. There is no point in that computation where it looks ahead — where it asks "if I went here, then there, what happens three moves down the line." It can only succeed by having *memorized*, across training maps, a rich enough lookup of "this local pattern of obstacles-and-goal-relative-position ⟹ this action." And the number of distinct global layouts is brutal: $K$ obstacles on an $n\times n$ grid is something like $\binom{n^2}{K}$ arrangements, each potentially demanding a different optimal action field. You cannot memorize your way out of that. The thing that *would* transfer across all those layouts is the planning computation itself: the procedure that, given obstacles and a goal, figures out where to go. That procedure is layout-agnostic — it's the same algorithm on every map. The reactive net never learned the algorithm; it learned a brittle approximation to the algorithm's *outputs* on the training maps.

That reframes the whole problem. I don't want a better-tuned reactive net. I want the policy to *contain a planner*. If the policy literally runs a planning computation as part of its forward pass, then generalizing to a new map is just running the same computation on new inputs — which is the whole reason a planner generalizes in the first place.

Now, the obvious way to put a planner in a policy is model-based RL: identify the dynamics from data, then solve the resulting MDP for a policy. And in principle that generalizes perfectly. But I know how that goes in practice — system identification is the hard part, and it's worse than just "hard": the map from model to optimal plan is wildly sensitive, so a small modelling error can flip the optimal plan to something completely different. For contact-rich control nobody trusts the identified model, which is the whole reason model-free methods took over. So I don't want to commit to "learn the true model, then solve it." I want something model-free in spirit — trained by the same RL/IL machinery as a reactive policy — that nonetheless does lookahead.

Let me also rule out the other classical "planning-flavored" approach, inverse RL / max-margin planning. Those learn a *cost* from demonstrations and then run a planner — but they assume the transition model is *known*, and the planner is a separate black box outside the network. I want to learn transitions too, and I want everything to be one differentiable net trained end-to-end, so any of these gaps disqualifies it.

So here's the shape of what I need. Suppose there's some MDP — call it $\bar M$ — that I get to *invent*, whose optimal plan carries useful information about what to do in the real task. I don't know $\bar M$, and crucially I'm not going to claim it's the *true* MDP of the world. I just hypothesize that *some* such $\bar M$ exists and is useful, and I'll let the policy learn it. Concretely, let $\bar M$'s reward and transitions be functions of the observation that I'll learn: $\bar R = f_R(\phi(s))$ and $\bar P = f_P(\phi(s))$. Then: plan in $\bar M$ — compute its optimal value $\bar V^*$ — and feed the result of that plan into a reactive policy. If I can train all of $f_R$, $f_P$, and the reactive readout jointly, maybe the network will discover a $\bar M$ that's a *useful caricature* of the task: not the true reward and dynamics, but a reward-shaping and a connectivity that, when planned over, point the way around obstacles to the goal. For the grid-world that's intuitively plausible — a reward that's high at the goal and very negative on obstacles, with transitions that just encode "you can step to neighbors," should give an optimal plan that flows around walls to the goal, whether or not those numbers are the "true" reward. I'll want to actually watch that happen on a concrete map before I trust it, but as a hypothesis it's clean.

Why hand $\bar V^*$ to the reactive policy rather than something else? Because the value function is a *sufficient statistic* of the plan: $\bar V^*$ over all states encodes everything about the optimal behavior in $\bar M$. Once you know $\bar V^*$, the optimal action anywhere is just $\arg\max_{\bar a} \bar R(\bar s,\bar a)+\gamma\sum_{\bar s'}\bar P(\bar s'|\bar s,\bar a)\bar V^*(\bar s')$. So attaching $\bar V^*$ as features to the policy gives it the entire plan to read from.

And now stare at that argmax for a second, because it tells me I don't even need to feed in the *whole* value map at every state. The optimal action at $\bar s$ only depends on the values $\bar V^*(\bar s')$ at states $\bar s'$ reachable from $\bar s$ — the states with $\bar P(\bar s'|\bar s,\bar a)>0$. In a grid-world, transitions are *local*: from a cell you can only step to its few neighbors. So the optimal action at a cell depends only on the value of that cell's small neighborhood. That means when the reactive policy is sitting at the current state, it only needs to look at a tiny local slice of $\bar V^*$ (or, even more directly, the local $\bar Q$ values at that cell). This is the same soft-attention idea from image captioning (Xu et al. 2015, "Show, Attend and Tell"): for any given prediction, only a subset of the inputs is relevant, so route only that subset to the predictor. Attention there improved learning by cutting the effective number of parameters the predictor has to wrangle — the same benefit should apply here. So I'll insert an attention module that selects the local value slice $\psi(s)$ at the current state, and feed only $\psi(s)$ to the reactive readout $\pi_{\text{re}}(a\mid\phi(s),\psi(s))$.

Good — conceptually I have a policy: observe, learn $f_R,f_P$, plan to get $\bar V^*$, attend to the local slice, react. And $\psi(s)$ is ultimately a function of $\phi(s)$, so this is still just $\pi_\theta(a\mid\phi(s))$, a perfectly ordinary parametrized policy. If I can backpropagate through it, I train it with the same RL/IL algorithm as any other policy. Designing differentiable $f_R$ and $f_P$ is no problem — they're just little conv nets. The attention is differentiable.

And then I hit the wall. The planner. "Compute $\bar V^*$" is value iteration, and value iteration is an iterative algorithm with a $\max$ and a sum inside, run as a black-box solver to convergence. How do I backpropagate through *that*? If the planner is opaque, I can't get gradients into $f_R$ and $f_P$, and the whole "learn $\bar M$ end-to-end" plan is dead. I need value iteration *itself* to be a differentiable layer in the network. Not "call a solver and stop-gradient," but an actual stack of operations the autodiff can flow through.

Let me write VI out in full and just *look* at it, on the grid.
$$Q_n(\bar s,\bar a)=\bar R(\bar s,\bar a)+\gamma\sum_{\bar s'}\bar P(\bar s'\mid \bar s,\bar a)\,V_n(\bar s'),\qquad V_{n+1}(\bar s)=\max_{\bar a}Q_n(\bar s,\bar a).$$
The state $\bar s$ is a cell $(i,j)$ on the grid. $V_n$ is therefore a 2-D array of numbers, one per cell — a single-channel *image*. Now look at the sum $\sum_{\bar s'}\bar P(\bar s'\mid \bar s,\bar a)V_n(\bar s')$. Because transitions are local, $\bar P(\bar s'\mid \bar s,\bar a)$ is zero unless $\bar s'$ is a neighbor of $\bar s$. So for a fixed action $\bar a$, this sum is: at each cell $(i,j)$, take a weighted combination of $V_n$ at the cells around $(i,j)$, with weights $\bar P(\cdot\mid(i,j),\bar a)$. Take a weighted combination of a value at a pixel and its neighbors, the same weight pattern at every pixel... that looks like a *convolution*. If it really is, then the sum $\sum_{\bar s'}\bar P(\bar s'\mid\bar s,\bar a)V_n(\bar s')$ is $V_n$ convolved with a kernel whose entries are the transition probabilities for action $\bar a$ — discounted by $\gamma$ if I fold the $\gamma$ into the kernel. Add the reward image $\bar R(\cdot,\bar a)$ and I'd have the $Q$-image for action $\bar a$.

I don't want to take that on the strength of "it looks like a convolution," because the indexing is exactly where these things go wrong (off-by-one, a transpose, a kernel flip). Let me actually run it. Take a $5\times5$ grid, $\gamma=0.9$. Put a goal at the center cell $(2,2)$ with reward $+1$, an obstacle just above it at $(1,2)$ with reward $-1$, and a small $-0.05$ everywhere else — exactly the "useful caricature" reward I'm hoping $f_R$ will learn. Give the planner two latent actions with local $3\times3$ transition kernels: action 0 spreads value to the four von-Neumann neighbors ($0.25$ each), action 1 mostly self with a little leak to the right. Fold $\gamma$ in so each kernel is $\gamma\bar P(\cdot\mid\cdot,\bar a)$.

Now compute four VI sweeps two ways. Method A: the literal textbook recurrence — at each cell, sum $\bar P$-weighted neighbor values explicitly with a quadruple loop over $(i,j)$ and the neighbor offsets, add the reward, take $\max$ over the two actions. Method B: stack the two kernels into a conv weight of shape (2 out-channels, 1 in-channel, $3\times3$), convolve the value image with padding 1, add the reward broadcast over the action channels, then take the max over the channel axis. If "Bellman sum = convolution" is real, the two value images should agree.

They agree to floating point. After four sweeps both methods give

```
[[-0.114 -0.141 -0.158 -0.126 -0.087]
 [-0.093 -0.121 -0.715 -0.144 -0.087]
 [ 0.330  1.061  1.863  0.289 -0.039]
 [-0.045  0.121  0.323  0.002 -0.085]
 [-0.098 -0.076 -0.051 -0.086 -0.087]]
```

with a max absolute difference of about $7\times10^{-8}$ — pure float32 rounding. So the convolution formulation *is* value iteration, not an analogy to it. Two things this check settled that I'd otherwise have gotten wrong. First, the indexing: PyTorch's `conv2d` is cross-correlation (no kernel flip), and the Bellman sum, written as $\sum_{\delta}\bar P(\delta)V(i+\delta)$, is *also* cross-correlation — so they line up with no flip, which I'd have second-guessed for an hour. Second, look at the value field itself: the $+1$ goal at $(2,2)$ has flooded outward into a basin (the $0.33$, $1.06$, $0.32$ ring around it), while the obstacle at $(1,2)$ stayed a hole ($-0.715$) and the value *routed around* it — the cells flanking the goal, $(2,1)$ and $(3,2)$, are strongly positive but the cell directly above through the obstacle is not. That is exactly the "flow around the wall to the goal" behavior I was betting the caricature reward would produce, and now I've watched it produce it on real numbers.

So: stack one convolution per action — a convolution *layer* whose output has one channel per action $\bar a$, each channel being $Q_n(\cdot,\bar a)$, the kernel for channel $\bar a$ being the (discounted) transition kernel for that action. The entire $Q$-image — all actions, all cells — is one convolution layer applied to the value image plus reward. Then $V_{n+1}(\bar s)=\max_{\bar a}Q_n(\bar s,\bar a)$ is a max over the action channels at each cell.

I have to be careful about *which* max this is. Standard CNN max-pooling pools over a spatial window and downsamples the image — and that is exactly what I must *not* do here, because I need to keep the full per-cell value field at full resolution. The max I want is over actions at a fixed cell: a channel-wise max that collapses the action channels down to a single-channel value image $V_{n+1}$ with the spatial dimensions untouched. (That's the `torch.max(q, dim=1)` in Method B above, and it's why the output stayed $5\times5$.) So one VI sweep = (convolution producing per-action $Q$ channels) → (max over the action channel) producing the next value image.

Now I just *recur*. Take $V_{n+1}$, stack it back with the reward image, feed it through the same convolution-and-channel-max again to get $V_{n+2}$, and so on. Run it $K$ times and I've performed $K$ sweeps of value iteration — and every operation in the chain is a convolution or a max, both differentiable. The planner becomes a recurrent convolutional module, and I can backpropagate the policy's loss right through $K$ value-iteration sweeps into $\bar R = f_R(\phi(s))$ and into the transition kernels. The wall I hit three paragraphs ago is gone — and not by hand-waving, since I just ran the differentiable version and got value iteration on the nose.

Let me make sure the recurrence is wired correctly and I'm not fooling myself. The first sweep: I don't have a value image yet, so the very first $Q$ is computed from the reward alone — convolve $\bar R$ with the reward-side weights to get the initial $\bar Q$, then channel-max to get the first $\bar V$. After that, each sweep convolves the *stack* of [reward image, current value image] with a kernel that has two parts: weights from the reward into $Q$, and "feedback" weights from the value image into $Q$. The feedback weights are the ones playing the role of $\gamma\bar P$ — they're how last sweep's values propagate into this sweep's $Q$. Stack reward and value as two input channels, give the conv a kernel over both, channel-max the output: that's one Bellman backup, written as a conv over a 2-channel image. Repeat $K$ times. (In my $5\times5$ check the reward was action-independent so I just added it back each sweep; folding it into the conv as a second input channel is the same arithmetic, organized so autodiff sees it as one layer.)

There's a subtlety I want to nail down: should the convolution kernels be the *same* across the $K$ sweeps, or different per sweep? Mathematically, value iteration applies the *identical* Bellman operator at every sweep — same reward, same transitions, over and over. (That's literally what I did in the check: one fixed kernel reused four times.) So if I'm faithful to VI, the kernels must be **tied** across all $K$ recurrences. And tying isn't just faithful, it pays. Tying the weights means the module has effective computational depth $K$ but only one sweep's worth of parameters. An untied stack of $K$ convolution layers has the same representational capacity — in principle a plain depth-$K$ CNN could compute whatever the tied module computes — but it has $K$ times the parameters, so it needs far more data to pin them down, and nothing forces those layers to implement *the same* operator, which is the structural prior that makes this a planner rather than a generic deep net. So weight tying is a strong, correct inductive bias (every layer is the same Bellman backup) that buys depth for free in parameter count. I'd expect that untying the weights drops performance, and drops it hardest when training data is scarce — exactly where the parameter savings matter most. That's a concrete prediction I can test later by literally untying them and watching it degrade; I won't claim the size of the effect until I've run it.

How many sweeps $K$? This is forced by how information moves, and it's worth pinning the number exactly rather than eyeballing it. Each VI sweep is one convolution, so each sweep should propagate value information outward by one grid step. Let me check the claim is literally one step per sweep and not, say, two, on a clean $7\times7$ grid with a single goal at the center, reward $0$ elsewhere, value initialized to $0$, the von-Neumann kernel. Tracking the set of cells with value $>0$ after each sweep:

```
k=1:  reaches Manhattan radius 0  (only the goal cell)
k=2:  radius 1   (5 cells)
k=3:  radius 2   (13 cells)
k=4:  radius 3   (25 cells)
k=5:  radius 4   (37 cells)
```

So a state at Manhattan distance $L$ from the goal first sees nonzero goal value at sweep $k=L+1$ — one cell per sweep, with a one-sweep offset because the first sweep only writes the goal cell itself. With fewer than $L+1$ sweeps, that state's $Q$ values are *blind to the goal*, so any action it picks there is guesswork. So $K$ has to be at least on the order of the map's diameter — enough that goal information can reach every cell. Concretely that scales with map size: something like $K=10$ for $8\times8$, $K=20$ for $16\times16$, $K=36$ for $28\times28$ — keep it as small as I can while still letting the goal talk to every state. Too small and distant states are goal-blind; too large and I'm paying compute and making a very deep recurrent net that's harder to train. The guideline is just "smallest $K$ that lets goal info flow everywhere," and the radius-per-sweep count above is what makes that guideline a number.

A couple of architectural choices fall out now. The transition kernels are $3\times3$: in a grid you can only step to immediate neighbors, so a $3\times3$ kernel exactly captures local transitions, and locality is precisely what keeps the parameter count tiny. (This is the structural difference from the alternative I'd otherwise reach for — a fully-convolutional net with one filter spanning the *whole* image to get the goal's information everywhere in a single layer: that buys global reach but with a gigantic kernel and no iteration, so it has to learn the long-range coupling as a one-shot pattern instead of *propagating* it. My propagation check is exactly why I expect that to generalize about as poorly as the plain CNN: it never builds the value field by flooding, it has to memorize the finished field.) And the number of action channels in the $Q$ layer: I could use exactly the number of true actions, but nothing forces the *learned* planner's action set to match the real one — $\bar M$ is a caricature, not the truth, as the $5\times5$ check made concrete (two latent actions there, with no claim they were "real" moves). Giving the $Q$ layer a few extra channels (say 10 when the task has 8 moves) lets the learned planner use more "latent actions," more freedom to shape a useful plan; I'd expect a slightly-larger-than-true action count to help rather than hurt, though that's a guess to confirm, not something the construction guarantees.

Now the reward front-end $f_R$. It should map the observation to a reward image over the grid. The observation is a 2-channel image — one channel marking obstacles, one marking the goal. So $f_R$ is a small conv net: a $3\times3$ convolution with a bunch of hidden channels (150 is plenty), then collapse to a single channel — the reward map $\bar R$. The reward I hand-built for the check (very negative at the obstacle, very positive at the goal, small negative elsewhere) is exactly the shape I'm hoping $f_R$ discovers on its own, since I watched that shape produce a value field that routes around the wall. Whether gradient descent actually lands there is an empirical question — I'll want to visualize the learned $\bar R$ on a held-out map and see if it looks like that reward-shaping, a useful fiction rather than the literal task reward. The transitions $\bar P$ don't depend on the observation here — they're just fixed-shape $3\times3$ kernels, learned, encoding "grid moves." The striking thing, if this trains, is that I never tell the network the true transition model; by shaping the reward end-to-end it would learn to plan successful trajectories anyway.

The attention and readout. By the locality argument above, at the current cell the reactive policy only needs the local $\bar Q$ values there. In the grid-world the agent's real position maps one-to-one to a cell in $\bar M$, so the attention is trivial: just index the $\bar Q$ tensor at the current cell $(i_s,j_s)$, pulling out the vector of $\bar Q$ values across the action channels at that cell. Feed that vector to a small reactive policy — here just a linear map to the action logits followed by softmax. (For a continuous-control variant where the agent's position is continuous, attention instead grabs a small patch of the value map around the discretized position and a few FC layers output continuous controls — but the grid case is the clean one.)

Let me trace the end-to-end picture against the failure I started from, on a *new* map. $f_R$ turns the observation into a reward image — negative walls, positive goal, the shape I verified produces a value basin. The VI module convolves-and-max-pools $K$ times; from the propagation check I know that floods value out from the goal one ring per sweep and, from the routing check, around the negative walls, producing a value field whose gradient points toward the goal around obstacles. The attention reads the local $Q$ at my cell; the readout picks the move that climbs that field. Because every one of those steps is a *computation run on the new input*, not a lookup of a memorized output, a brand-new obstacle layout is handled by the same arithmetic that handled the training maps — which is the generalization the reactive net structurally couldn't have. And the propagation count is exactly why it should hold up as the map grows: as long as $K$ scales with the diameter, goal info still reaches every cell, so nothing about the mechanism degrades with size. (That's a mechanism-level argument, not a measured success rate — I'd still confirm the held-out success rate on the actual $8/16/28$ benchmarks before believing the size-robustness in practice.)

One more reflection on why this is more than "a deep enough CNN." Someone will object that a depth-$K$ CNN has, in principle, the capacity to do the same computation. True — capacity isn't the issue; my own remark that an untied stack could represent it concedes that. The issue is the *inductive bias and the parameters*. The VI module bakes in: locality (small kernels), the repeated identical Bellman operator (tied weights), and the channel-max action selection. That structure is what should make "learn to plan" learnable from a realistic amount of data; strip the structure and you're back to needing to memorize. The whole move is the same spirit as convolution itself: convolution is to images what this iterated-conv-with-channel-max is to planning — exploit the structure of the problem (locality + a repeated operator) instead of throwing a generic deep net at it. The $5\times5$ check is the small-scale demonstration that the "iterated-conv-with-channel-max" really *is* the planning operator and not just shaped like it.

Let me also note where this generalizes beyond the 2-D grid, because it clarifies what's essential. Nothing forced the state space to be a 2-D image; that just made "transitions are local ⟹ convolution" literal. For a graph (web pages and links), the same construction works with graph-structured transitions instead of image convolutions, a learned reward per node from the query, and an attention that reads values over relevant nodes — the essential content is "a differentiable, weight-tied, iterated Bellman backup," and the grid is the cleanest instance of it.

Now let me write the module. The recurrence is the core; everything else is a thin wrapper. I'll process the observation into a reward map, run the iterated convolution-and-channel-max $K$ times sharing the transition kernels, index the resulting $Q$ at the current cell, and softmax to actions. I'll keep the conv padded so the value image stays full resolution (the padding-1 that kept my check at $5\times5$), and I'll separate the reward-into-$Q$ weights from the value-feedback weights so the two roles ($\bar R$ and $\gamma\bar P$) are explicit.

```python
import torch
import torch.nn as nn
import torch.nn.functional as F
from torch.nn.parameter import Parameter


class VIN(nn.Module):
    """A policy that plans: it learns a latent reward map, runs K differentiable
    value-iteration sweeps over it as a recurrent conv module, then reads off the
    local Q-values at the current state and reacts."""

    def __init__(self, in_channels=2, l_h=150, l_q=10, num_actions=8):
        super().__init__()
        # f_R: observation image (obstacles, goal) -> reward map.
        # 3x3 conv to a hidden representation, then collapse to a single reward channel.
        self.h = nn.Conv2d(in_channels, l_h, kernel_size=3, stride=1, padding=1, bias=True)
        self.r = nn.Conv2d(l_h, 1, kernel_size=1, stride=1, padding=0, bias=False)

        # Reward -> Q weights: one 3x3 kernel per (latent) action channel.
        # This plays the role of R(.,a) in the Bellman backup.
        self.q = nn.Conv2d(1, l_q, kernel_size=3, stride=1, padding=1, bias=False)
        # Value-feedback -> Q weights: gamma * P(.|.,a), how last sweep's value
        # propagates into this sweep's Q. Tied across all K sweeps (same Bellman operator).
        self.w = Parameter(torch.zeros(l_q, 1, 3, 3))

        # Reactive readout: local Q-vector at the current cell -> action logits.
        self.fc = nn.Linear(l_q, num_actions, bias=False)
        self.sm = nn.Softmax(dim=1)

    def forward(self, obs_image, state_x, state_y, k):
        # f_R: perceive the map, produce the latent reward image.
        h = self.h(obs_image)
        r = self.r(h)                                  # reward map (1 channel)

        # First Bellman backup from the reward alone, then channel-max -> value image.
        q = self.q(r)                                  # Q per action channel
        v, _ = torch.max(q, dim=1, keepdim=True)       # V = max_a Q  (max over actions/channels)

        def bellman(r, v):
            # One VI sweep written as a convolution over the [reward, value] stack:
            # reward-side kernels (R) concatenated with value-feedback kernels (gamma*P).
            return F.conv2d(torch.cat([r, v], dim=1),
                            torch.cat([self.q.weight, self.w], dim=1),
                            stride=1, padding=1)

        # Recur the same operator K-1 more times: each sweep floods value one cell further.
        for _ in range(k - 1):
            q = bellman(r, v)
            v, _ = torch.max(q, dim=1, keepdim=True)
        q = bellman(r, v)                              # final sweep's Q

        # Attention: by locality, the optimal action needs only the local Q at the
        # current state -> index the Q tensor at the agent's cell.
        batch_sz, l_q = q.size(0), q.size(1)
        q_local = q[torch.arange(batch_sz), :, state_x.long(), state_y.long()].view(batch_sz, l_q)

        # Reactive policy on the attended Q-vector.
        logits = self.fc(q_local)
        return logits, self.sm(logits)
```

Training is exactly what it is for any reactive policy: cross-entropy against the expert action (imitation), or any RL loss with the same network. RMSProp on a few thousand random maps, with $K$ scaled to the map size so goal information can flow to every cell.

The causal chain, end to end: reactive policies fail to generalize across layouts because they have no lookahead and would have to memorize an astronomical number of layouts — and the diagnostic (low per-step loss, low success rate, gap growing with size) shows they learn the easy steps and miss the planning-critical ones. The fix is to put a planner *inside* the policy: invent a latent MDP whose reward and transitions are learned from the observation, plan in it to get the value field, and react to the local slice of that field. The one obstacle — backpropagating through value iteration — dissolves once I see, and then check on a $5\times5$ grid to float-point agreement, that a Bellman backup on a grid is a convolution (transitions = local kernel) followed by a channel-wise max (the $\max_a$), so $K$ sweeps of VI are a recurrent conv module with tied weights, fully differentiable. Locality gives small $3\times3$ kernels; the repeated-operator structure gives tied weights and effective depth $K$ for one sweep's parameters; the one-ring-per-sweep propagation count sets $K$; attention reads only the local $Q$. The result is a policy that *runs* the planning computation on every new map, which is why I expect it to generalize the way a planner does — a claim I've grounded at the mechanism level here and would close out by measuring held-out success rate on the benchmarks.
