I keep coming back to one stubborn pair of facts. An electron hops between a small +2 and a small +3 cation in water and the exchange is slow; the same electron hops between two big complex ions — ferro/ferricyanide, permanganate/manganate — and it is fast. Nothing is broken, nothing is made; in the self-exchange case the products are literally the reactants over again, so the one thing that usually governs a rate, the relative stability of products and reactants, is gone. This is about as naked as a chemical reaction gets, and yet I cannot say why one is slow and the other fast. Worse, the measured entropies of activation for the clean cases are large and *negative*, and they happen between ions of like sign, where the crude argument said the term should be positive. So I do not even have the sign right. If I want to predict these rates from things I already know — charges, radii, the solvent's dielectric constants, the thermodynamics — I first have to answer a more basic question: when no bonds move, what *is* the barrier?

Take the standard machinery and see where it breaks. Absolute-rate theory says k = (kT/h) exp(−ΔF\*/kT), with ΔF\* the free energy of forming an activated complex sitting at the top of the pass on a potential-energy surface. Fine — but that surface is the electronic energy plotted against the positions of the atoms, and the reaction coordinate is some bond being stretched. Here there is no bond to stretch. The "coordinate" along which the system climbs is invisible. The theory gives me the *form* of a rate law and no way at all to compute ΔF\* for this class of reaction. It is a recipe with the key ingredient missing.

So maybe the barrier is electrostatic — the cost of solvating the activated complex. Reach for Born and Coulomb: an ion of charge e, radius a, gets −(e²/2a)(1 − 1/Dₛ) of solvation free energy; two ions q₁, q₂ a distance R apart interact as q₁q₂/(Dₛ R). Treat the complex as a sphere of the summed charge, compute its solvation, done. But every one of these formulas is built on a single assumption: the polarization of the solvent at each point is in equilibrium with the field of the instantaneous ionic charges, so I can read the polarization straight off the charges through the dielectric constant. And that is *exactly* the assumption that has to break the instant an electron jumps. The heavy solvent cannot have followed the charge yet. If I use equilibrium electrostatics I am describing a state the reaction never visits, and — this is the crux — there is no quantity anywhere in Born's expression that could even register "the solvent's slow polarization is sitting at the wrong value." The framework has no degree of freedom for being out of equilibrium. Dead end, but an instructive one: the missing physics is a polarization that is *not* the equilibrium one.

Now the idea that actually feels right. Libby took the Franck–Condon principle — an electronic transition is fast, the nuclei frozen during it — and said: when the electron jumps, the two product ions are born in the solvent arrangement that belonged to the *reactants*, because the solvent dipoles had no time to move. A small cation orients the water around it very differently in its +2 and +3 states, so the freshly made ion finds itself in a strongly foreign, high-energy environment — a big barrier, slow reaction. A big complex ion barely changes the field at the solvent on transfer, so the environment is hardly foreign — fast. And the discussion even patched the one apparent exception: a complex ion whose metal–ligand bonds change length a lot on reduction is slow despite its size, because now it is the *inner* nuclear coordinates that are caught in a foreign configuration. The size trend, the bond-length case — it all clicks. Something about the Franck–Condon principle is unquestionably involved.

And yet the number Libby gets for the barrier does not hold up, and when I stare harder at the picture something is wrong at a deeper level than arithmetic. Take it literally: the system sits at the reactant's equilibrium nuclear configuration, the electron jumps with the nuclei frozen, and we land on the product electronic state *at those same nuclear positions*. But the product electronic state at the reactant's nuclear configuration is higher in energy than where it started — that is the whole point, that is the "foreign environment." So the system has gone up in energy with nothing supplying that energy. A jump like that, straight up at fixed nuclei, is a *vertical* transition: it is what a photon does. It is fine for absorption spectroscopy. It is not fine for a reaction happening **in the dark**, where there is no photon to pay the bill. Energy is not conserved. Libby has the right principle and an event that cannot actually occur thermally.

So: how can the system obey the Franck–Condon principle — electron jumps with the nuclei frozen — *and* conserve energy, with no light? The electron jump has to happen between two configurations of the *same* total energy. At the reactant minimum the reactant electronic state is lower than the product one; at the product minimum it is the reverse; somewhere in between, the two must be *equal*. If, before the electron jumps, the heavy nuclei — the solvent orientations, and any inner-shell coordinates — fluctuate away from the reactant equilibrium toward a configuration where the energy of the system-as-reactants equals the energy of the system-as-products, then at *that* configuration the electron can jump with the nuclei frozen and the energy books balance. That configuration, reached by a thermal fluctuation, is the transition state. The barrier is not the cost of tunnelling and it is not the vertical Franck–Condon gap; it is the free-energy cost of fluctuating the slow nuclear polarization to the place where reactant and product energies cross.

That reframes everything. The reaction coordinate that absolute-rate theory could not find is *the nuclear polarization of the solvent itself*. The activated complex is a state in which the slow polarization has a value appropriate to neither the reactants nor the products, but to the energy-matching configuration in between. And now I can see exactly why equilibrium electrostatics was useless and exactly what I must build: I need a free energy for a state whose slow polarization is deliberately out of equilibrium with the charges, and then I need to find, among all such states, the most probable one consistent with the equal-energy condition.

Let me make the weak-overlap assumption precise, because it is what licenses talking about "the reactant electronic state" and "the product electronic state" as two separate things with a definite energy each. Write the Schrödinger equation for the electrons of the reacting pair in the field of all the solvent, Hφ = Eφ. If the donor and acceptor orbitals did not overlap at all, two exact solutions would be φ_X\*, the configuration with the electrons distributed as in the *reactants*, and φ_X, the configuration as in the *products*, each for a *given* fixed arrangement of all the nuclei. With a slight overlap the right wavefunction for the activated complex is a linear combination φ_X + c φ_X\*. Plug it in: H(φ_X + c φ_X\*) = E_X φ_X + c E_X\* φ_X\*, and this equals E_X(φ_X + c φ_X\*) only if E_X = E_X\*. So the combination is a legitimate state, with a single well-defined total energy, *exactly when the reactant-configuration energy equals the product-configuration energy*. There it is again, falling straight out of the math: the equal-energy condition is not an extra assumption I am imposing, it is the condition for the activated complex to *be* a quantum state at all. Call the two thermodynamic states with this common energy X\* (electrons as reactants) and X (electrons as products); they share the same average nuclear configuration and differ only in where the electron sits.

The energy condition puts a severe restraint on the solvent. How tightly the solvent orients around an ion depends strongly on its charge, so the equilibrium solvent configuration is wildly different for the reactant charges and the product charges. But X\* and X must have the *same* average nuclear configuration (Franck–Condon) *and* the same total energy. So the solvent configuration in the activated complex cannot be the equilibrium one for either set of charges. It is forced to be something in between — a nonequilibrium polarization. This is the same statement as Libby's "wrong environment," but now anchored to energy conservation: the wrongness is exactly enough to make the two electronic states degenerate.

I should reassure myself that "small overlap" is self-consistent, because the whole equal-energy story leans on it. The uncertainty principle broadens the energy of a state of lifetime τ by ΔE ≈ h/4πτ. If the overlap is small the electronic interaction is weak, the electron does not flip configurations often, lifetimes are long, the broadening is tiny, and "equal energy" is a sharp condition. If τ is about 10⁻¹³ s then ΔE is about 0.075 kcal/mol, so the restraint would be fuzzy by about 2ΔE ≈ 0.15 kcal/mol — negligible against a barrier of many kcal/mol. Even at τ ≈ 10⁻¹⁴ s it is only ~1.5 kcal/mol. Contrast a *large*-overlap complex: electronic frequencies are ~10¹⁵ s⁻¹, τ ≈ 10⁻¹⁵ s, ΔE ≈ 15 kcal/mol — the equal-energy restriction would be meaningless and this whole treatment would not apply. Good: small overlap is exactly the regime where my central condition has teeth, and the estimates of the elementary-step rates put many real electron-transfer reactions there.

Before the electrostatics, let me get the kinetics of the elementary steps straight, because the earlier tunnelling treatment went wrong precisely here by forgetting that the energy-matched intermediate can relax *back*. Write the sequence: reactants A + B form the intermediate X\* (rate k₁, reverse k₋₁); X\* interconverts with X by the electron jump (k₂, k₋₂); X goes on to products (k₃). The state X\* — the energy-matched, nonequilibrium-polarization complex with electrons still as reactants — has two fates: relax the solvent back and fall apart to reactants (k₋₁), or let the electron jump to give X (k₂). Steady-state on X\* and X, then form the overall forward rate. When the forward electron jump is at least about as probable as the reverse dissociation of X\* — k₂ ≳ k₋₁ — the bottleneck is simply *making* X\*, and the overall bimolecular constant collapses to k_bi ≈ k₁ = Z·exp(−ΔF\*/kT), where ΔF\* is the free energy of forming the energy-matched intermediate and Z is the collision number in solution, not the kT/h of a single equilibrium complex. (If k₂ were much smaller than k₋₁ I would keep the full steady-state expression, but the estimates below say I usually need not.) So the entire problem reduces to one number: ΔF\*, the free energy of forming X\*. Everything now is electrostatics of a nonequilibrium-polarization state.

I have no free energy for such a state. Equilibrium electrostatics writes the work to assemble a charge distribution as if P ∝ E everywhere; that is invalid the moment the polarization is decoupled from the field. I have to build the free energy from a reversible path that actually reaches the nonequilibrium state. So first, split the polarization by timescale, which is the lesson from the optical-spectra work on dissolved ions: the electronic polarization follows any change essentially instantly (~10⁻¹⁵ s), while the atomic and orientation polarization lag (~10⁻¹³, ~10⁻¹¹ s). In the activated complex the *slow* part is the one stuck out of equilibrium. Call the fast, always-equilibrated part the E-type polarization P_e(r) — it tracks the instantaneous field, P_e = α_e E — and the slow, decoupled part the U-type polarization P_u(r), which is an independent vector point function not tied to the local field. Total P = P_e + P_u. I will also need the field the charges would produce *in a vacuum*, call it E_c(r) = −∇∫[ρ/|r−r′| + …]; it is computable straight from the known charges, which is the whole reason for introducing it.

Now a reversible two-stage charging to reach a state with a *prescribed* U-type polarization P_u(r) and the *actual* final charges. Stage I: with no real charges yet, use some fictitious charge distribution ρ⁰, σ⁰ to reversibly build up exactly the target P_u(r); during this the E-type part takes whatever value, P_e⁰, equilibrates to that fictitious field; this stage is an ordinary equilibrium problem, so its work is the usual ½∫ψ⁰ρ⁰. Stage II: now hold P_u fixed and reversibly turn the charges from the fictitious ρ⁰ to the real ρ; only P_e moves, re-equilibrating to the changing field. Carry the integral over the charging parameter, using that the potential depends linearly on the charging fraction (which follows from the uniqueness theorem applied to the medium of polarizability α_e in Stage II), and add the two works. After the dust settles the electrostatic free energy of the state is

F = ½ ∫ { E_c·E_c/(4π) − P·E_c + P_u·(P_u/α_u − E) } dV,

with E the actual field, P the total polarization, P_u the slow part, α_u its polarizability. (I can also reach this by a more physical route — summing the self-energies P_u·P_u/2α_u and P_e·P_e/2α_e of each volume element and the interaction energies of each polarization type with the fields E_c, E_u, E_e, dividing the double-counted interaction terms by two — and the two derivations agree, which is reassuring.) The crucial check: let P_u relax to its equilibrium value, P_u = α_u E. Then δF/δP_u = 0 identically and F reduces to the ordinary equilibrium electrostatic free energy. So this functional *contains* Born and Coulomb as the equilibrium special case, and generalizes them to exactly the nonequilibrium states I need. The dead end from before is now a corner of the new theory.

The polarizabilities convert to dielectric constants by 4π(α_e + α_u) = Dₛ − 1 at low frequency and 4π α_e = D_op − 1 at optical frequency (where only the electrons follow). I will need the combination 1/D_op − 1/Dₛ — and I can already feel why: the fast part D_op re-equilibrates for free, so only the slow part can cost reorganization energy, and the slow part lives in the *difference* between the full static response and the optical response. Subtract 1/Dₛ from 1/D_op and that difference is exactly what is left.

Now the variational problem. There is not one nonequilibrium intermediate but infinitely many — every admissible P_u(r) gives a state X\*, X satisfying the energy restriction. The transition state is the *most probable* one, i.e. the pair (X\*, X) of *minimum* free energy of formation, subject to the constraint that X\* and X have equal total energy. So I minimize F\* (the free energy of forming X\* from the reactants) over P_u(r), under the equal-energy restraint. Compute δF\* at fixed charges. From the functional, holding E_c\* fixed (δE_c\* = 0) and using δP\* = δP_u + α_e δE\* (the E-type part follows the field), and the relation between the integrals of δP_u and δE\* that the electrostatics forces,

δF\* = ∫ ( P_u/α_u − E\* )·δP_u dV.

If P_u were unconstrained, setting this to zero would give P_u = α_u E\* everywhere — the equilibrium relation again, i.e. no barrier. But P_u is *not* free: it must keep X\* and X at equal energy. Take the variation of the energy-equality restraint; it reads δF\* − δF = 0, and δF = ∫(P_u/α_u − E)·δP_u with no asterisks (X has the same P_u but the product charges, hence a different field E). So I am minimizing one linear functional of δP_u subject to another linear functional of δP_u vanishing. Lagrange multiplier m: add m·(δF\* − δF) to δF\* and demand the sum vanish for *all* δP_u. The bracket must be identically zero in every volume element:

P_u = α_u { E\* + m (E\* − E) }.

So the slow polarization in the activated complex is the equilibrium polarization for the reactant field E\*, *plus* a correction proportional to the difference between the reactant and product fields, with strength set by the multiplier m. The multiplier is the price of the energy constraint — and I will see in a moment that it is secretly the driving force.

To make this computable I must get E\* and E out of the way in favor of the vacuum fields E_c\* and E_c, which I can write down from the charges. This is the appendix-level grind, but it is what turns the variational statement into a formula. From the potential equation for a nonequilibrium state and the established relation E†(r) = E_c†(r)/(1 + 4πα) between the actual and vacuum fields of a charge set in a medium of polarizability α, I work the substitutions through. Writing ψ − ψ\* as the potential of an equilibrium medium of polarizability α_e driven by charges with vacuum field E_c − E_c\*, then applying the (1 + 4πα_e) = D_op relation, I get the first reduction:

E − E\* = (E_c − E_c\*)/D_op.

Then, feeding the constrained P_u back in and reusing the same machinery with polarizability (α_e + α_u),

E\* = { E_c\* − 4π α_u m (E_c\* − E_c)/D_op } / (1 + 4π(α_e + α_u)),

and since 1 + 4π(α_e + α_u) = Dₛ and α_u can be written through 1/D_op − 1/Dₛ, this closes E\* (and E) entirely in terms of E_c\*, E_c, D_op, Dₛ, and m. Both intermediate-state fields are now explicit functions of vacuum quantities I can integrate.

Substitute these into F\*. The vacuum fields of two pseudo-point ions of charges e₁\*, e₂\* (reactant charges) at separation R, with saturated spheres of radii a₁, a₂, give standard integrals: ∫E_c\*·E_c\* over the dielectric outside the spheres yields the self-terms e₁\*²·(stuff) + e₂\*²·(stuff) and the cross-term through 1/R; the relevant combination that survives, after subtracting the work W_iso\* to charge the *isolated* reactants (so that what is left is the free energy of *formation* of X\* from the separated reactants), is the geometric factor

g ≡ (1/(2a₁) + 1/(2a₂) − 1/R).

Grinding the substitution through, the electrostatic free energy of forming the intermediate comes out as

ΔF\* = e₁\* e₂\*/(R Dₛ) + m² (Δe)² g (1/D_op − 1/Dₛ),

where Δe is the charge transferred (e₁\* − e₁ = −(e₂\* − e₂) by conservation of charge). The first term is just the ordinary Coulomb interaction of the reactant ions — it would be there even with no reorganization. The second term is new: it is the reorganization cost, and it carries the Pekar factor 1/D_op − 1/Dₛ that I anticipated (only the slow polarization reorganizes), the geometric factor g, the square of the transferred charge, and — the only unknown — m².

So I need m. It is fixed by the energy restraint itself, Eq. for which I get by writing the standard free energy of reaction ΔF° as the difference (F\* − W_iso\*) − TΔS_e − (F − W_iso) between the formation free energies of X\* and of X, since X\* and X differ in free energy only by the electronic-degeneracy entropy term −TΔS_e (their atomic configurations and hence vibrational entropies are identical). Carrying the same field substitutions into F\* − F and into the restraint gives a linear equation for m:

−(2m + 1) (Δe)² g (1/D_op − 1/Dₛ) = ΔF° + TΔS_e + W_iso\* − W_iso.

Let me lighten the notation. Define the **reorganization energy**

λ ≡ (Δe)² g (1/D_op − 1/Dₛ) = (Δe)² (1/(2a₁) + 1/(2a₂) − 1/R)(1/D_op − 1/Dₛ),

and bundle the right-hand side — the driving-force quantity, ΔF° plus the degeneracy and isolated-ion work corrections — into a single ΔG° (for a self-exchange it is zero, and in general it is the standard free energy of reaction at the reacting separation). Then the multiplier equation is simply

−(2m + 1) λ = ΔG°,  i.e.  m = −½ − ΔG°/(2λ).

Two sanity checks before I substitute. First, the symmetric self-exchange: ΔG° = 0, the electronic degeneracies match, and by symmetry W_iso\* = W_iso and the E_c\*² and E_c² integrals are equal, so the restraint forces m = −½ exactly. Second, that the inverted-sign structure is right: as the reaction is made more exergonic (ΔG° more negative), m moves up from −½.

Now substitute m into the reorganization term, ΔF\*_reorg = m² λ:

ΔF\*_reorg = ( −½ − ΔG°/(2λ) )² λ = ( ½ + ΔG°/(2λ) )² λ = λ · (λ + ΔG°)²/(4λ²) = (λ + ΔG°)²/(4λ).

There it is. The free energy of activation — the part beyond the bare Coulomb work of bringing the ions together — is

ΔF\* = (λ + ΔG°)² / (4λ).

I did not assume two parabolas; I minimized a nonequilibrium-polarization free energy under an energy constraint and the parabola fell out. But now I see *why* it had to: the reorganization free energy is quadratic in m, and m is linear in the driving force, so ΔF\* is the square of (λ + ΔG°) — and that is precisely the geometry of two identical parabolas, one displaced vertically by ΔG° and horizontally so that the curvatures match, crossing at a height (λ + ΔG°)²/4λ above the reactant minimum. The "reaction coordinate" is the slow polarization; the two parabolas are the free energy of the system-as-reactants and the system-as-products as that coordinate varies; λ is the vertical distance from the bottom of one parabola up to the other parabola directly above it — the energy it would cost to wrench the solvent into the product's equilibrium arrangement *without* letting the electron transfer. Everything in λ is something I already know: charges, radii, and the two dielectric constants. No arbitrary parameters.

Let me read the consequences off the formula, because this is where it earns its keep. Set ΔG° = 0: ΔF\* = λ/4. A self-exchange has a barrier of one quarter of the reorganization energy, and λ shrinks as the ions get bigger (the 1/(2a) terms) — so large complex ions have a small λ and a small barrier and react fast, small cations have a large λ and react slow. That is Libby's trend, but now as a number, and now energy-conserving. The negative entropy of activation also has a home: forming the energy-matched, polarization-frozen intermediate orders the solvent, and between like-signed ions the dominant reorganization term is large, so the ordering is large — a big negative entropy of activation, between ions of like sign, exactly as observed.

Now vary ΔG° at fixed λ and watch ΔF\* = (λ + ΔG°)²/4λ. Start at ΔG° = 0, barrier λ/4. Make the reaction exergonic, ΔG° < 0: the barrier *drops*, the reaction speeds up — the ordinary expectation, the "normal" region, the analog of a Brønsted or Tafel slope, and indeed near ΔG° = 0 the barrier falls with slope ½ in −ΔG°. Keep going. At ΔG° = −λ the barrier hits *zero* — an activationless reaction, the fastest possible. Push further, ΔG° < −λ: now (λ + ΔG°)² grows again, the barrier comes *back up*, and the reaction gets *slower the more exergonic it is*. This is the surprise — the **inverted region**. It is not an artifact; it is forced by the squared dependence. Picture the two parabolas: lowering the product curve vertically (more negative ΔG°) first slides their crossing point down toward the reactant minimum (barrier falling), until at ΔG° = −λ the product curve passes through the reactant minimum (barrier zero); lower it still further and the crossing climbs back up the *far* wall of the reactant parabola (barrier rising again). The most exergonic reactions are slow because the system has to fluctuate *past* its own equilibrium to reach the energy-matched configuration.

A few more things drop out that I want to record. The reorganization energy is additive over the two reactants — λ depends, through the 1/(2a₁) and 1/(2a₂) terms (and an analogous statement for any inner-shell contribution), on each reagent separately — so the λ of a cross reaction between couples 1 and 2 is approximately the mean of the two self-exchange λ's, λ₁₂ ≈ (λ₁₁ + λ₂₂)/2. Feed that into ΔF\* = (λ + ΔG°)²/4λ and the rate of a cross reaction is tied to the two self-exchange rates and the equilibrium constant: k₁₂ ≈ (k₁₁ k₂₂ K₁₂ f)^{1/2}, with f a near-unity correction that is itself fixed by the same algebra, ln f = (ln K₁₂)²/[4 ln(k₁₁ k₂₂/Z²)]. That is a parameter-free prediction relating *different* experiments. And there is an optical counterpart: shine light on a mixed-valence pair and the electron is driven *vertically*, nuclei frozen at the reactant minimum — the Franck–Condon transition Libby was really describing — whose energy is the product parabola read at the reactant minimum, hν_max = λ. The same λ governs the thermal barrier *and* the charge-transfer absorption band. The inner-shell coordinates I set aside enter the same way: a bond-length change Δq_j with reduced force constant k_j = 2 f_j^r f_j^p/(f_j^r + f_j^p) adds an inner reorganization λ_i = ½ Σ_j k_j (Δq_j)², so λ = λ_i + λ_o, and the slow Co(NH₃)₆³⁺/²⁺ exchange with its big bond-length change is the large-λ_i case.

Let me close by checking the small-overlap kinetics one more time so the rate prefactor is honest. The energy-matched intermediate X\* can fall apart by escaping the solvent cage (~10¹³ s⁻¹ times an escape probability < 1) or, more tellingly, by the slow polarization relaxing away from its frozen value — the atomic and orientation relaxation times are ~10⁻¹³ and ~10⁻¹¹ s, so once the nonequilibrium polarization is built it lives ~10⁻¹³ s before reverting, giving k₋₁ ≈ 10¹³ s⁻¹. The electron jump k₂ is the strike frequency of the valence electron (~10¹⁵ s⁻¹) times the small per-strike transfer probability; with the tunnelling-type estimates this comes out comparable to k₋₁. So k₂ ≳ k₋₁ holds and k_bi ≈ k₁ = Z·exp(−ΔF\*/kT) is the right reduction — and notice this also shows the earlier tunnelling treatment's two errors plainly: it forgot k₋₁ (the reorganized state relaxing back) and it forgot the strike frequency in k₂.

The causal chain, start to finish: the barrier of a bond-free electron transfer cannot be a bond-stretch energy and cannot be the equilibrium solvation of a summed-charge sphere, because the slow solvent polarization is out of equilibrium at the moment of transfer; demanding that the electron jump conserve energy in the dark forces the reactant and product electronic states to be degenerate, which forces the solvent into a specific nonequilibrium polarization reached by a thermal fluctuation; to price that I build a free energy for nonequilibrium-polarization states by reversible two-stage charging, splitting the polarization into a fast (always-equilibrated) and a slow (frozen) part; minimizing this free energy over the slow polarization, subject to the equal-energy restraint via a Lagrange multiplier m, yields a reorganization cost m²λ with m = −½ − ΔG°/(2λ); substituting gives ΔF\* = (λ + ΔG°)²/4λ with λ = (Δe)²(1/(2a₁)+1/(2a₂)−1/R)(1/D_op − 1/Dₛ); and the rate is k = Z·exp(−ΔF\*/kT). Out of it fall the size trend, the negative activation entropy, the normal-to-inverted turnover at −ΔG° = λ, the cross-relation, and hν_max = λ — all from charges, radii, and dielectric constants, with no fitted parameter.

```python
import numpy as np

# constants and water at 25 C
kT   = 0.593      # kcal/mol
D_s  = 78.5       # static dielectric constant
D_op = 1.8        # optical dielectric constant
E2   = 332.06     # e^2 in kcal*Angstrom/mol

def lambda_o(dq, a1, a2, R):
    """Outer-sphere reorganization energy: lambda = (dq)^2 (1/2a1 + 1/2a2 - 1/R)(1/Dop - 1/Ds).
       This is the energy to wrench the slow solvent polarization from the reactant arrangement
       to the product arrangement with no electron transfer -- the parabola offset."""
    g = (1.0/(2*a1) + 1.0/(2*a2) - 1.0/R)          # geometric factor (Angstrom^-1)
    return E2 * dq**2 * g * (1.0/D_op - 1.0/D_s)    # kcal/mol

def lambda_inner(force_const, dq_bond):
    """Inner-shell reorganization: 1/2 sum_j k_j (dq_j)^2, k_j reduced force constants."""
    return 0.5 * np.sum(np.asarray(force_const) * np.asarray(dq_bond)**2)

def dF_star(lam, dG0, coulomb=0.0):
    """Free energy of activation: Coulomb work + (lambda + dG0)^2 / (4 lambda).
       m = -1/2 - dG0/(2 lambda) was the Lagrange multiplier; m^2 * lambda = (lambda+dG0)^2/4lambda."""
    return coulomb + (lam + dG0)**2 / (4.0 * lam)

def rate(lam, dG0, Z=1e11, coulomb=0.0):
    """Transition-state rate: k = Z exp(-dF*/kT)."""
    return Z * np.exp(-dF_star(lam, dG0, coulomb) / kT)

# --- worked example: a self-exchange and the inverted region ---
# Fe(2+)/Fe(3+)-like aqueous self-exchange: small ions, contact, unit charge transferred.
a1 = a2 = 3.0          # saturated-sphere radius ~ crystallographic + one water (Angstrom)
R   = a1 + a2          # closest approach
lam = lambda_o(dq=1.0, a1=a1, a2=a2, R=R)
print(f"lambda_o = {lam:.1f} kcal/mol;  self-exchange barrier lambda/4 = {lam/4:.1f} kcal/mol")

# scan the driving force: normal region, activationless point, inverted region
for dG0 in [0.0, -lam/2, -lam, -1.5*lam, -2.0*lam]:
    print(f"dG0 = {dG0:7.1f}  ->  dF* = {dF_star(lam, dG0):6.2f} kcal/mol")
# barrier vanishes at dG0 = -lambda (activationless); rises again for dG0 < -lambda (inverted).
```
