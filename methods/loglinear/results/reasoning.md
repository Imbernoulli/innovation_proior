Let me start from what actually breaks when I try to generate images with scores. I have a network `s_theta(x)` trained to match `grad_x log p_data(x)`, and Langevin dynamics that turns a score into a sampler: `x_t = x_{t-1} + alpha * s_theta(x_{t-1}) + sqrt(2 alpha) z_t`. On a clean single distribution this should walk noise into a sample, and on toy data it does. On real images it does not, and I know the two reasons because I can see them. First, the manifold thing: image data lies on a thin low-dimensional sheet inside pixel space, and a gradient of `log p_data` taken in the full ambient space is simply not defined off that sheet — score matching is only a consistent estimator when the data has full-dimensional support, and indeed if I watch the sliced-score-matching loss on raw CIFAR it never settles, it just rattles, whereas if I add a whisper of Gaussian noise, `N(0, 1e-4)`, imperceptible to the eye but enough to spread the support over all of `R^D`, the loss drops and converges. Second, the low-density thing: score matching weights its error by `p_data(x)`, so in regions where there's almost no data it has almost no signal and the learned score there is garbage — and those empty regions are exactly where a sampler started from random noise spends its first many steps. Worse, even with a perfect score, Langevin between two modes separated by a low-probability gap can't recover the relative mass of the modes, because inside each basin the score is purely local and doesn't know the mixing weight; I've seen plain Langevin on a two-Gaussian mixture with ground-truth scores come back with the wrong ratio between the modes.

So a single noise level puts me in a bind. Crank `sigma` up: the blur fills the empty regions, the score becomes estimable everywhere, the modes fatten and connect so Langevin mixes — but now I'm sampling the *blurred* distribution, and blurred images are not images. Turn `sigma` down: I keep the data sharp but I'm back to undefined-off-manifold and dead-score-in-the-voids. There's no good single choice; the only way out is to use many noise levels at once. Take a descending ladder `sigma_1 > sigma_2 > ... > sigma_L`, perturb the data at each, train one noise-conditional network `s_theta(x, sigma)` to match `grad log p_{sigma_i}` at all of them, and then sample by annealing: run Langevin at the biggest noise `sigma_1` where everything is easy, take that output and use it to initialize Langevin at `sigma_2`, and so on down to `sigma_L` where the perturbed distribution is essentially the clean data. This is just simulated annealing / annealed importance sampling applied to the noise level — a ladder of intermediate distributions, easy at the top, the target at the bottom, carrying samples down. The top `sigma_1` I'll set large so the sampler can explore and so distant modes are reachable; the bottom `sigma_L` I'll set near zero so the last distribution is the data. Fine. That framework I have.

What I don't have is the rungs. Between a fixed large `sigma_1` and a fixed small `sigma_L`, what are the levels in the middle, and how many? I could copy the old ten-level ladder, `sigma_1 = 1`, `sigma_L = 0.01`, because it is cheap and familiar. But as soon as the dimension, endpoints, or data distances change, that ladder gives me no diagnostic for what to change: nobody has told me what quantity the spacing is supposed to control, how many rungs I need, or even how big `sigma_1` really has to be. I want to stop guessing. The spacing has to come out of the actual geometry of these perturbed distributions, not out of what happened to work once.

Let me get precise about what the spacing even *does*. The whole annealing trick rests on one move: I take the samples I produced at level `i-1` and use them as the starting point for Langevin at level `i`. The score at level `i` is trained from samples of `p_{sigma_i}`, so it is most trustworthy where `p_{sigma_i}` has mass. The incoming samples, though, are typical of `p_{sigma_{i-1}}`. For the handoff to be worth anything, the high-density region of `p_{sigma_{i-1}}` has to be covered by the training distribution for level `i`. If consecutive levels are too far apart, level `i` has little mass where the level-`(i-1)` samples arrive, and I'm right back in the failure I built the ladder to avoid: bad score, slow mixing. If consecutive levels are too close, every rung barely changes anything and I'm paying for a Langevin run per level that I didn't need — sampling is `L` sequential runs, so wasted rungs are expensive. So spacing is controlling a single quantity: the *overlap* between adjacent perturbed distributions — specifically, how much mass level `i` puts in the typical region of level `i-1`. I want that overlap to be enough, and I want to not waste rungs, and I want it to be the *same* at every rung, because if some neighbor pairs overlap a lot and others barely overlap, the barely-overlapping pairs are weak links that break the whole chain no matter how good the rest is. A chain is as good as its worst handoff. So my real design goal is: make the step-to-step overlap a constant across the entire ladder.

Now I need to actually compute that overlap, which means I need the shape of `p_{sigma_i}`. Real data is hopeless to analyze, so let me strip it to the bone: one data point, at the origin. Then `p_{sigma_i}(x) = N(0, sigma_i^2 I)`, an isotropic Gaussian in `R^D`. Two isotropic Gaussians centered at the same point, different widths — how much do they overlap? My low-dimensional intuition says "a lot, they're both peaked at zero," and that intuition is a trap in high dimension, so let me not trust it and instead compute where the mass actually is. Decompose `x` into radius and direction, `x = r * phi` with `r = ||x||`, `phi` on the unit sphere. Because the Gaussian is isotropic, the angular distribution `p(phi)` is uniform on the sphere and — this is the key simplification — it's *identical* for every `sigma_i`; the noise level only rescales the radius, not the direction. So all the angular overlap is automatic and free; the entire overlap question collapses to one dimension, the radial one. I only need to know how `r` is distributed and whether two levels' radial distributions overlap.

For `x ~ N(0, sigma^2 I)`, the squared norm normalized, `s = ||x||^2 / sigma^2 = sum_{k=1}^D (x_k/sigma)^2`, is a sum of `D` independent squared standard normals — chi-squared with `D` degrees of freedom, `p_s(s) = s^{D/2 - 1} e^{-s/2} / (2^{D/2} Gamma(D/2))`. I want the density of `r = ||x|| = sigma sqrt(s)`, so change variables: `s = r^2/sigma^2`, `ds/dr = 2r/sigma^2`, giving

```
p(r) = (2r/sigma^2) p_s(r^2/sigma^2)
     = r^{D-1} / (2^{D/2 - 1} Gamma(D/2) sigma^D) * exp(-r^2/(2 sigma^2)).
```

That's exact, but I want its shape for large `D`, because that's the regime I'm in — thousands to millions of pixels. Each coordinate `x_k ~ N(0, sigma^2)` has `E[x_k^2] = sigma^2` and `Var[x_k^2] = 2 sigma^4` (the variance of a scaled chi-squared-1). By the law of large numbers, `(x_1^2 + ... + x_D^2)/D -> sigma^2`, i.e. `r^2/D -> sigma^2`, so `r ≈ sqrt(D) sigma`. By the central limit theorem, `sqrt(D)( r^2/D - sigma^2 ) -> N(0, 2 sigma^4)`. To get `r` itself rather than `r^2`, apply the delta method with `g(u) = sqrt(u)`, `g'(sigma^2) = 1/(2 sigma)`: the variance transforms by `(g')^2`, so `2 sigma^4 * (1/(2 sigma))^2 = sigma^2/2`, giving

```
sqrt(D)( r/sqrt(D) - sigma ) -> N(0, sigma^2/2),   i.e.   r - sqrt(D) sigma -> N(0, sigma^2/2).
```

So in high dimension the radius is sharply concentrated: `r ≈ N(mean = sqrt(D) sigma, variance = sigma^2/2)`. The mass of a high-dimensional Gaussian is not near the center at all — it sits on a thin spherical shell of radius `sqrt(D) sigma` and width about `sigma/sqrt(2)`. The Gaussian soap bubble. And this is exactly where my low-dimensional intuition was lying to me: two same-center Gaussians with different widths don't "overlap a lot near zero" — near zero they both have almost no mass. They live on two *different shells*, radius `sqrt(D) sigma_{i-1}` and radius `sqrt(D) sigma_i`, and whether they overlap is the question of whether those two thin shells are close enough on the radial line to intersect. The overlap problem just became a one-dimensional question about two nearly-Gaussian bumps at `sqrt(D) sigma_{i-1}` and `sqrt(D) sigma_i`. Good — that I can do.

So model each level's radial distribution as `p_{sigma_i}(r) = N(m_i, s_i^2)` with `m_i = sqrt(D) sigma_i` and `s_i^2 = sigma_i^2/2`, and ask my overlap question concretely. The incoming samples for level `i` are typical of level `i-1`, so their radial mass sits in the high-density band of `p_{sigma_{i-1}}`. By the three-sigma rule that band is `I_{i-1} = [m_{i-1} - 3 s_{i-1}, m_{i-1} + 3 s_{i-1}]`. Level `i` is the distribution that supplies the score-training signal for this next Langevin run, so the handoff is healthy when `p_{sigma_i}` puts a fixed amount of mass in that band. The quantity I want constant across the ladder is

```
C_i = P_{r ~ N(m_i, s_i^2)}( r in I_{i-1} ).
```

Let me compute it by standardizing `r` to `(r - m_i)/s_i ~ N(0,1)`. The lower endpoint `m_{i-1} - 3 s_{i-1}` becomes `(m_{i-1} - 3 s_{i-1} - m_i)/s_i`, the upper becomes `(m_{i-1} + 3 s_{i-1} - m_i)/s_i`. Now substitute the high-dimensional values. Let me write the one ratio everything depends on: `gamma_i := sigma_{i-1}/sigma_i`. Then `m_{i-1} = sqrt(D) sigma_{i-1} = sqrt(D) gamma_i sigma_i = gamma_i m_i`, and `s_{i-1} = sigma_{i-1}/sqrt(2) = gamma_i sigma_i/sqrt(2) = gamma_i s_i`. The standardized upper endpoint is

```
(m_{i-1} + 3 s_{i-1} - m_i)/s_i = (gamma_i m_i + 3 gamma_i s_i - m_i)/s_i
                                = (m_i/s_i)(gamma_i - 1) + 3 gamma_i.
```

And `m_i/s_i = sqrt(D) sigma_i / (sigma_i/sqrt(2)) = sqrt(D) sqrt(2) = sqrt(2D)`. The `sigma_i` cancels completely — that's the whole payoff of the standardization. So the upper endpoint is `sqrt(2D)(gamma_i - 1) + 3 gamma_i`, the lower endpoint is `sqrt(2D)(gamma_i - 1) - 3 gamma_i`, and with `Phi` the standard normal CDF,

```
C_i = Phi( sqrt(2D)(gamma_i - 1) + 3 gamma_i ) - Phi( sqrt(2D)(gamma_i - 1) - 3 gamma_i ).
```

Now stare at this, because it's telling me the answer. `C_i` depends on `i` through *one* thing only: `gamma_i = sigma_{i-1}/sigma_i`. Not through `sigma_i`, not through the absolute scale, not through `D` except as a fixed constant for my dataset — purely through the *ratio* of consecutive levels. And what was my design goal? Make the step-to-step overlap the same at every rung, `C_2 = C_3 = ... = C_L = C`. Once I choose the same moderate-overlap branch of this one-variable relation at every rung, demanding a common `C` forces a common `gamma`: `gamma_2 = gamma_3 = ... = gamma_L =: gamma`. Constant ratio. Which means `sigma_i = sigma_1 * gamma^{-(i-1)}` — a geometric progression. The rungs are equally spaced in the multiplicative sense.

And there's the cleanest way to say what that is: take logs. `log sigma_i = log sigma_1 - (i-1) log gamma`, which is *linear in `i`*. Constant overlap between neighbors is the same statement as equal spacing in `log sigma`. The schedule is log-linear. The geometric sequence the incumbent recipe pulled out of a hat isn't an arbitrary convenient choice — it's the unique spacing that equalizes the one quantity that decides whether annealed sampling works, the adjacent-level overlap, and it's forced by the fact that in high dimension the overlap depends only on the ratio of consecutive widths. That's why linear-in-`sigma` spacing was wrong: a constant *absolute* gap `sigma_i - sigma_{i+1}` is a tiny ratio near the large end (`gamma ≈ 1`, neighbors almost identical, wasted rungs) and a huge ratio near the small end (`gamma` large, neighbors far apart, the handoff fails) — uneven `gamma` means uneven `C`, weak links exactly where `sigma` is smallest. Constant `gamma` fixes that by construction.

Let me make sure I'm reading the overlap formula's behavior right, because it also tells me how to *pick* the ratio and the count, not just that the spacing is geometric. Look at `C` as a function of `gamma >= 1`. At `gamma = 1` the levels coincide: the argument `sqrt(2D)(gamma-1)` is zero, the band is `[-3, +3]` in standard units, `C = Phi(3) - Phi(-3) ≈ 0.997` — almost total overlap, as it must be when there's no gap. As `gamma` grows past 1, the term `sqrt(2D)(gamma - 1)` shifts the next shell away by something proportional to `sqrt(D)`, and since `D` is huge even a small `gamma - 1` moves the shells many standard deviations apart and `C` collapses toward zero. So `C` is a steep decreasing function on the relevant high-dimensional branch: a ratio that looked innocent in low `D` can put the two shells completely out of contact in high `D`. Now I can look back at the old ten-level recipe and see what it was doing. Between `sigma_1 = 1` and `sigma_L = 0.01`, a constant-ratio ten-level ladder has `gamma = (sigma_1/sigma_L)^{1/(L-1)} = 100^{1/9} ≈ 1.67`, and on a `32x32x3` image `D ≈ 3072`, so `sqrt(2D)(gamma - 1) ≈ sqrt(6144) * 0.67 ≈ 52` — fifty-two standard deviations of separation. `C` is zero to machine precision. The shells don't touch in the radial approximation; the Langevin runs at each level have to drag the sample across the gap themselves, which is precisely the slow-mixing regime I was trying to escape. So the heuristic was under-covering badly, and silently.

That hands me the recipe for the ratio. I want `C` not vanishing but I also can't afford `C ≈ 1`, because pushing `C` to 1 means `gamma` barely above 1, which means a *lot* of rungs to cover the fixed span from `sigma_1` to `sigma_L`, and each rung is a full Langevin run — sampling cost is linear in `L`. So there's a direct tradeoff: bigger `C` (safer handoffs) costs more `L` (slower sampling). The sweet spot is a moderate overlap. Pick a target like `C ≈ 0.5` — adjacent shells share about half the mass, enough for a reliable warm start without insisting on near-total redundancy — and solve

```
Phi( sqrt(2D)(gamma - 1) + 3 gamma ) - Phi( sqrt(2D)(gamma - 1) - 3 gamma ) = C ≈ 0.5
```

for `gamma`, given my dimension `D`. Then the number of levels is not a free knob anymore; it's pinned by the geometry, because `gamma` and the fixed endpoints determine it: from `sigma_L = sigma_1 gamma^{-(L-1)}`,

```
L = 1 + (log sigma_1 - log sigma_L) / log gamma.
```

Larger images mean larger `D`, which makes `C` steeper, which forces `gamma` closer to 1 to keep `C ≈ 0.5`, which means *more* levels. A fixed short ladder is not scale-free; once `D` grows, preserving the same adjacent overlap can require many more rungs. The thing that looked like a mysterious per-dataset hyperparameter is actually determined.

I still owe myself the two endpoints, since the whole derivation took `sigma_1` and `sigma_L` as given. `sigma_L` is the easy one: it's the smallest noise, I want the bottom of the ladder to be essentially clean data, so `sigma_L` small, of order `0.01`, small enough that `p_{sigma_L} ≈ p_data` (and if I want to be tidy I can append a single denoising step at the end, `x + sigma_L^2 s_theta(x, sigma_L)`, which is just Tweedie's formula removing the residual `N(0, sigma_L^2)` blur). `sigma_1` is more interesting and the same one-point analysis won't give it, because diversity is a *multi*-point property — it's about Langevin being able to travel from one data mode to another. Let me redo the toy with two data points `x^(i)`, `x^(j)`, so `p_{sigma_1}` is a two-component Gaussian mixture, and ask: starting near component `i`, does the score even *see* component `j`? The mixture score is a responsibility-weighted average, `grad log p = sum_k r^(k)(x) grad log p^(k)(x)` with `r^(k)(x) = p^(k)(x) / sum_l p^(l)(x)`, so what I need is for `E_{p^(i)}[ r^(j) ]` — the average pull toward `j` felt while sitting at `i` — to not be negligible. I can bound it: by dropping all the other components from the denominator and using the geometric-mean/harmonic-mean inequality on the two-term sum,

```
E_{p^(i)(x)}[ r^(j)(x) ] = ∫ p^(i) p^(j) / (sum_k p^(k)) dx
                         <= ∫ p^(i) p^(j) / (p^(i) + p^(j)) dx
                         <= (1/2) ∫ sqrt( p^(i) p^(j) ) dx,
```

and the last integral is a Gaussian overlap I can do by completing the square — `sqrt(p^(i) p^(j))` is, up to the normalizer, `exp(-(||x - x^(i)||^2 + ||x - x^(j)||^2)/(4 sigma_1^2))`, and writing the second norm as `||x - x^(i) + (x^(i) - x^(j))||^2` and grouping, the quadratic in `x` integrates to the normalizer and leaves the cross term

```
E_{p^(i)}[ r^(j) ] <= (1/2) exp( - ||x^(i) - x^(j)||^2 / (8 sigma_1^2) ).
```

So the pull toward another mode decays like a Gaussian in the *inter-mode distance over `sigma_1`*. If `sigma_1` is small compared to how far apart the data points are, this is exponentially tiny — Langevin sitting at one mode literally cannot feel another, and the sampler will never produce the diversity of the data, it'll collapse around wherever it started. To get diverse samples I need `sigma_1` *comparable to the largest pairwise distances in the data*. Concretely, for CIFAR the typical pairwise image distance is around 18, so `sigma_1 = 1` gives `0.5 exp(-18^2/8) < 10^{-17}`, no mode-to-mode pull at all; I'd have to take `sigma_1` up around the data's own diameter, tens not ones, for the modes to communicate. So the rule for the top of the ladder: `sigma_1 ≈ max pairwise distance between training points`. That's not a cosmetic endpoint; it is the difference between a top distribution whose modes can communicate and one whose modes are effectively isolated.

There's one more gift from the geometric choice that I didn't go looking for, and it's worth pinning down because it confirms I'm on the right object. Within a level, annealed Langevin runs `T` steps at level `i`, initialized from the previous level's output, and I'd like the per-level Langevin to actually equilibrate — to land at variance `sigma_i^2`, the variance it's supposed to sample. On the one-point toy I can track this exactly. Langevin at level `i` is `x_{t+1} = x_t + alpha grad log p_{sigma_i}(x_t) + sqrt(2 alpha) z_t = x_t - (alpha/sigma_i^2) x_t + sqrt(2 alpha) z_t`, a linear Gaussian recursion, so the variance obeys `Var[x_{t+1}] = (1 - alpha/sigma_i^2)^2 Var[x_t] + 2 alpha`. This is an affine fixed-point iteration; subtract the fixed point `v = 2 alpha / (1 - (1 - alpha/sigma_i^2)^2)` and it telescopes geometrically, `Var[x_t] - v = (1 - alpha/sigma_i^2)^{2t} (Var[x_0] - v)`. The warm start sets `Var[x_0] = sigma_{i-1}^2` (last level's variance), so after `T` steps

```
s_T^2 = (1 - alpha/sigma_i^2)^{2T} ( sigma_{i-1}^2 - v ) + v.
```

Now use the standard step-size choice `alpha = epsilon sigma_i^2 / sigma_L^2` — the one that fixes the Langevin signal-to-noise ratio across levels, since `||s_theta(x,sigma)|| ≈ sqrt(D)/sigma` makes `alpha ||s||^2` scale-independent when `alpha ∝ sigma^2`. Substitute and divide through by `sigma_i^2`:

```
s_T^2 / sigma_i^2 = (1 - epsilon/sigma_L^2)^{2T} ( gamma^2 - 2 epsilon/(sigma_L^2 - sigma_L^2 (1 - epsilon/sigma_L^2)^2) )
                  + 2 epsilon/(sigma_L^2 - sigma_L^2 (1 - epsilon/sigma_L^2)^2),
```

where `gamma = sigma_{i-1}/sigma_i`. And here's the thing: with a *geometric* ladder `gamma` is the same at every level, so this ratio `s_T^2/sigma_i^2` is *identical across all levels* — every rung equilibrates to the same relative degree — and it has no explicit `D` dependence either. The geometric spacing makes the whole annealing chain self-similar: tune `T` and `epsilon` once (pick `T` by compute budget, then grid-search `epsilon` to push this ratio toward 1) and the tuning holds at every level and every dimension. With any non-geometric spacing this ratio would drift level to level and I'd be re-tuning per rung. So constant ratio isn't only the overlap-equalizing choice; it's the choice that makes the per-level sampler dynamics uniform. Same insight from a second direction.

Let me also double-check I'm not fooling myself that geometric is just one nice smooth warp among many. The argument was tight: `C_i` depends on `i` only through `gamma_i`, so equal `C` across rungs is equal `gamma` on the common branch. The only way to get a different spacing is to want unequal overlap along the ladder — and I don't, because the chain breaks at its weakest handoff. Geometric it is: the equal-overlap, equal-dynamics choice, and the right default to land on.

So let me write the schedule as code I'd actually drop into the sampler. The job is: given the number of steps `n` and the endpoints — largest noise `t_max` (this is `sigma_1`) and smallest `t_min` (this is `sigma_L`) — return the ladder of `n + 1` levels, strictly decreasing, log-linearly spaced. Log-linear means: take the endpoints into the log domain, space evenly there, exponentiate back. Two guards I owe the implementation. One, `log(0)` is `-inf` and the smallest level can be set to zero or extremely small, so floor it before taking the log — `max(t_min, 1e-10)`. Two, the round trip `exp(log(t_min))` can miss `t_min` by a few ULPs, and the sampler contract wants the final level to be *exactly* the clean-data endpoint, so pin the last entry to `t_min` after the fact. This is exactly the geometric branch of the standard noise-schedule routine — even spacing in `log(sigma)` — written in the sampler's `sigma`-domain:

```python
import math
import torch


def get_sigmas(n, t_min, t_max, device="cpu"):
    """Log-linear (geometric) noise ladder for annealed score-based sampling.

    The noise levels are spaced evenly in log(sigma) from t_max down to t_min, i.e.
    sigma_{i-1}/sigma_i is a constant ratio gamma (a geometric progression). This is the
    spacing that equalizes the overlap between adjacent perturbed distributions, which in
    high dimension depends only on that ratio: in R^D the mass of N(0, sigma^2 I) sits on a
    thin shell of radius ~ sqrt(D)*sigma, and the chance a level-i draw lands in level-(i-1)'s
    high-density band is a function of gamma = sigma_{i-1}/sigma_i alone, so equal overlap
    across the ladder forces equal gamma -> geometric -> linear in log(sigma).
    """
    log_max = math.log(t_max)
    log_min = math.log(max(t_min, 1e-10))               # floor before log: log(0) = -inf
    sigmas = torch.exp(torch.linspace(log_max, log_min, n + 1))  # even spacing in log(sigma)
    sigmas[-1] = t_min                                   # pin the clean-data endpoint exactly
    return sigmas.to(device)
```

Let me trace the causal chain back to the top. A single learned score plus Langevin can't generate images: off the data manifold the score is undefined and score matching is inconsistent, and in the empty regions where a noise-initialized sampler lives the score is unestimated and Langevin can't even mix between modes — and no single noise level escapes the bind, because the `sigma` that fixes all that is the `sigma` that destroys the data. The way out is a descending ladder of noise levels with one noise-conditional score net, sampled by annealed Langevin that warm-starts each level from the level above. That warm start is the entire mechanism, and it works only to the extent that adjacent levels overlap — and because the chain is only as strong as its weakest handoff, I want that overlap equal at every rung. Reducing the perturbed distributions to one-point isotropic Gaussians and computing where their mass actually is in high dimension showed the mass lives on thin shells of radius `sqrt(D) sigma`, so overlap is a one-dimensional shell-distance question, and the probability a level-`i` draw lands in level-`(i-1)`'s high-density band came out depending on the levels only through their ratio `gamma = sigma_{i-1}/sigma_i`. Equal overlap therefore *forces* equal ratio — a geometric progression, equivalently a schedule linear in `log sigma`. The same overlap formula says a short ladder can have effectively zero overlap once `D` is large (its shells are tens of standard deviations apart), showing why the ratio should be chosen by solving the overlap to a moderate constant like `0.5` and why the level count is then pinned by the ratio and the endpoints. The top endpoint has to sit near the data's pairwise diameter so distant modes can communicate, and the bottom stays near zero so the final distribution is the data. The geometric spacing came with a bonus — it makes the per-level Langevin equilibration ratio identical across levels and `D`-independent, so the within-level sampler tunes once and holds everywhere. The landing is a log-linear schedule on the noise level: log the endpoints, space evenly, exponentiate, floor the log and pin the terminal — equal steps in `log sigma`, which is constant ratio, which is equal overlap, which is the chain that doesn't break.
