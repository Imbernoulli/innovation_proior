A single learned score plus Langevin dynamics cannot generate images, and the reasons are concrete. I train a network $s_\theta(x)$ to match $\nabla_x \log p_{\text{data}}(x)$ and turn it into a sampler through $x_t = x_{t-1} + \alpha\, s_\theta(x_{t-1}) + \sqrt{2\alpha}\, z_t$. On toy data this walks noise into a sample; on real images it fails twice over. First, image data lives on a thin low-dimensional manifold inside pixel space, where the ambient gradient of $\log p_{\text{data}}$ is simply not defined off the sheet, and score matching is a consistent estimator only under full-dimensional support — watch the sliced-score-matching loss on raw CIFAR and it rattles instead of settling, whereas a whisper of Gaussian noise $\mathcal N(0,10^{-4})$, imperceptible but enough to spread the support over all of $\mathbb R^D$, makes the loss converge. Second, score matching weights its error by $p_{\text{data}}(x)$, so in the empty regions where a noise-initialized sampler spends its first many steps the learned score is unestimated garbage, and even with an exact score, local Langevin cannot recover the relative weights of well-separated modes because inside each basin the score knows nothing of the mixing weight. No single noise level escapes the bind: crank $\sigma$ up and the support fills, the score becomes estimable everywhere, and the modes fatten and connect so Langevin mixes — but now I am sampling the blurred distribution, and blurred images are not images; turn $\sigma$ down and I keep the data sharp but I am back off-manifold with dead score in the voids.

The accepted way out is a descending ladder of Gaussian-perturbed distributions $p_{\sigma_1}, \dots, p_{\sigma_L}$ with $\sigma_1 > \cdots > \sigma_L$, one noise-conditional network $s_\theta(x,\sigma)$ trained at every level, and annealed Langevin that runs at the largest noise where everything is easy, then warm-starts each lower level from the output of the level above, down to $\sigma_L$ where the perturbed distribution is essentially clean data. That framework is settled; what is not settled is where to place the intermediate rungs and how many to use. Copying an old ten-level ladder is cheap and familiar, but it gives no diagnostic for what to change when dimension, endpoints, or data distances move, no quantity the spacing is supposed to control. Uniform spacing in $\sigma$, $\texttt{linspace}(\sigma_1,\sigma_L,L)$, respects the endpoints but applies the same absolute decrement at both ends of the ladder where it means very different things. I want the spacing to come out of the actual geometry of these distributions, not out of what happened to work once.

I propose the log-linear (geometric) noise schedule: place the levels at equal spacing in $\log\sigma$, equivalently hold the adjacent ratio $\gamma = \sigma_{i-1}/\sigma_i$ constant down the whole ladder. What forces this choice is the role the spacing actually plays. The entire annealing mechanism is the warm start — samples produced at level $i-1$ become the starting point for Langevin at level $i$, whose score is trustworthy only where $p_{\sigma_i}$ has mass. If consecutive levels are too far apart, level $i$ has little mass where the level-$(i-1)$ samples arrive and I am back in the bad-score, slow-mixing failure I built the ladder to avoid; if too close, every rung barely changes anything and I pay for a wasted Langevin run, since sampling is $L$ sequential runs. So the spacing controls one quantity, the overlap between adjacent perturbed distributions, and because a chain is only as strong as its weakest handoff, I want that overlap equal at every rung — a single bad handoff breaks the whole chain no matter how good the rest is.

To compute the overlap I strip the data to one point at the origin, so $p_{\sigma_i}(x) = \mathcal N(0,\sigma_i^2 I)$ in $\mathbb R^D$. Two same-center isotropic Gaussians of different widths share the same uniform angular distribution — the noise level rescales only the radius, not the direction — so the angular overlap is automatic and the whole question collapses to the radial coordinate $r = \|x\|$. For $x \sim \mathcal N(0,\sigma^2 I)$, $\|x\|^2/\sigma^2 \sim \chi^2_D$, and the exact radial density is $p(r) = r^{D-1} \exp(-r^2/2\sigma^2) / (2^{D/2-1}\Gamma(D/2)\sigma^D)$. In high dimension each coordinate has $\mathbb E[x_k^2]=\sigma^2$ and $\mathrm{Var}[x_k^2]=2\sigma^4$, so the law of large numbers gives $r^2/D \to \sigma^2$ and the central limit theorem gives $\sqrt D(r^2/D - \sigma^2)\to\mathcal N(0,2\sigma^4)$; applying the delta method with $g(u)=\sqrt u$, $g'(\sigma^2)=1/(2\sigma)$, the variance transforms by $(g')^2$ to $\sigma^2/2$, so
$$ r - \sqrt D\,\sigma \;\to\; \mathcal N\!\left(0,\ \tfrac{\sigma^2}{2}\right). $$
The mass of a high-dimensional Gaussian sits not at the center but on a thin spherical shell of radius $\sqrt D\,\sigma$ and width about $\sigma/\sqrt2$ — the Gaussian soap bubble. This is exactly where low-dimensional intuition lies: two same-center Gaussians of different widths do not overlap "a lot near zero," they live on two different shells of radius $\sqrt D\,\sigma_{i-1}$ and $\sqrt D\,\sigma_i$, and whether they overlap is whether those shells are radially close enough to intersect.

Model each level's radial law as $\mathcal N(m_i, s_i^2)$ with $m_i = \sqrt D\,\sigma_i$ and $s_i^2 = \sigma_i^2/2$. The incoming samples for level $i$ are typical of level $i-1$, so by the three-sigma rule their radial mass falls in the band $I_{i-1} = [\,m_{i-1}-3s_{i-1},\ m_{i-1}+3s_{i-1}\,]$, and a healthy handoff puts a fixed amount of level-$i$ mass there. Writing $\gamma_i = \sigma_{i-1}/\sigma_i$ gives $m_{i-1}=\gamma_i m_i$ and $s_{i-1}=\gamma_i s_i$; standardizing $r$ under level $i$, the upper endpoint becomes $(m_i/s_i)(\gamma_i-1)+3\gamma_i$, and since $m_i/s_i = \sqrt D\,\sigma_i/(\sigma_i/\sqrt2) = \sqrt{2D}$ the absolute scale $\sigma_i$ cancels completely, leaving
$$ C_i \;=\; \Phi\!\big(\sqrt{2D}\,(\gamma_i-1)+3\gamma_i\big)\;-\;\Phi\!\big(\sqrt{2D}\,(\gamma_i-1)-3\gamma_i\big). $$
The overlap depends on the level only through the ratio $\gamma_i$ — not through $\sigma_i$, not through the absolute scale, and through $D$ only as a fixed dataset constant. My design goal was equal overlap at every rung, $C_2 = \cdots = C_L = C$, and on the common moderate-overlap branch a common $C$ forces a common $\gamma$, hence $\sigma_i = \sigma_1\,\gamma^{-(i-1)}$, a geometric progression. Take logs and $\log\sigma_i = \log\sigma_1 - (i-1)\log\gamma$ is linear in $i$: constant adjacent overlap is identically equal spacing in $\log\sigma$. This is why linear-in-$\sigma$ spacing was wrong — a constant absolute gap is a tiny ratio near the large end (neighbors almost identical, rungs wasted) and a huge ratio near the small end (neighbors far apart, the handoff fails), so the weak links land exactly where $\sigma$ is smallest; constant $\gamma$ removes them by construction.

The same formula also chooses the ratio and the count. As a function of $\gamma\ge1$, $C$ equals $\Phi(3)-\Phi(-3)\approx0.997$ at $\gamma=1$ and collapses toward zero as $\gamma$ grows, because the shift $\sqrt{2D}(\gamma-1)$ scales with $\sqrt D$ and even a small $\gamma-1$ pushes the shells many standard deviations apart in high dimension. So a ratio that looks innocent at low $D$ can leave the shells out of contact entirely: the old recipe with $\sigma_1=1$, $\sigma_L=0.01$, ten levels has $\gamma=100^{1/9}\approx1.67$, and on a $32\times32\times3$ image $D\approx3072$ gives $\sqrt{2D}(\gamma-1)\approx52$ standard deviations of separation, $C$ zero to machine precision — the shells never touch and each Langevin run has to drag the sample across the gap, the very slow-mixing regime I was escaping. Because pushing $C$ toward $1$ means $\gamma$ barely above $1$ and a great many rungs (each a full Langevin run, cost linear in $L$), the sweet spot is a moderate overlap: pick a target like $C\approx0.5$ and solve
$$ \Phi\!\big(\sqrt{2D}\,(\gamma-1)+3\gamma\big)-\Phi\!\big(\sqrt{2D}\,(\gamma-1)-3\gamma\big)=C\approx0.5 $$
for $\gamma$ at my dimension $D$. The level count is then no longer a free knob; it is pinned by the geometry through $L = 1 + (\log\sigma_1 - \log\sigma_L)/\log\gamma$. Larger images mean larger $D$, a steeper $C$, a $\gamma$ closer to $1$, and therefore more levels — the count was never scale-free.

The endpoints close the design. The bottom $\sigma_L$ is the easy one: keep it small, of order $0.01$, so $p_{\sigma_L}\approx p_{\text{data}}$, with an optional final Tweedie denoising step $x + \sigma_L^2\, s_\theta(x,\sigma_L)$ to remove the residual $\mathcal N(0,\sigma_L^2)$ blur. The top $\sigma_1$ controls diversity, which is a multi-point property, so I redo the toy with two data points and a two-component mixture and ask whether the score sitting near mode $i$ feels mode $j$. The mixture score is a responsibility-weighted average, and bounding the average pull $\mathbb E_{p^{(i)}}[r^{(j)}]$ by dropping the other components and using the geometric-mean inequality, then doing the Gaussian overlap integral by completing the square, gives
$$ \mathbb E_{p^{(i)}}\!\big[r^{(j)}(x)\big] \;\le\; \tfrac12\,\exp\!\left(-\,\frac{\|x^{(i)}-x^{(j)}\|^2}{8\sigma_1^2}\right). $$
The pull toward another mode decays like a Gaussian in the inter-mode distance over $\sigma_1$, so if $\sigma_1$ is small relative to the data's pairwise distances the modes are exponentially invisible and the sampler collapses around wherever it started. For CIFAR, whose typical pairwise image distance is around $18$, $\sigma_1=1$ gives $0.5\exp(-18^2/8)<10^{-17}$, no mode-to-mode pull at all; the rule is therefore to set $\sigma_1$ on the order of the maximum pairwise distance between training points so distant modes can communicate.

One more property confirms the object is right. Within a level, Langevin at level $i$ on the one-point toy is the linear Gaussian recursion $x_{t+1} = (1 - \alpha/\sigma_i^2)x_t + \sqrt{2\alpha}\,z_t$, whose variance telescopes geometrically to the fixed point and, started warm at $\mathrm{Var}[x_0]=\sigma_{i-1}^2$, reaches $s_T^2 = (1-\alpha/\sigma_i^2)^{2T}(\sigma_{i-1}^2 - v) + v$ with $v = 2\alpha/(1-(1-\alpha/\sigma_i^2)^2)$ after $T$ steps. Using the standard step size $\alpha = \epsilon\,\sigma_i^2/\sigma_L^2$ — the one that fixes the Langevin signal-to-noise ratio across levels because $\|s_\theta(x,\sigma)\|\approx\sqrt D/\sigma$ makes $\alpha\|s\|^2$ scale-independent when $\alpha\propto\sigma^2$ — and dividing through by $\sigma_i^2$, the relative equilibration ratio $s_T^2/\sigma_i^2$ comes out depending on the level only through $\gamma$ and with no explicit $D$. With a geometric ladder $\gamma$ is shared by every adjacent pair, so this ratio is identical at every level and dimension: the annealing chain is self-similar, $T$ and $\epsilon$ tune once and the tuning holds everywhere. Geometric spacing is thus not one smooth warp among many — it is the unique equal-overlap choice, and independently the equal-dynamics choice. The implementation logs the endpoints, spaces evenly, and exponentiates, with two guards I owe it: floor the smallest level before the log, since $\log0=-\infty$ and $\sigma_L$ may be set to zero, and pin the terminal entry to $t_{\min}$ exactly, since the round trip $\exp(\log t_{\min})$ can miss by a few ULPs while the sampler contract wants the final level to be precisely the clean-data endpoint.

```python
import numpy as np
import torch


def get_sigmas(config):
    if config.model.sigma_dist == "geometric":
        sigmas = torch.tensor(
            np.exp(np.linspace(np.log(config.model.sigma_begin),
                               np.log(config.model.sigma_end),
                               config.model.num_classes))
        ).float().to(config.device)
    elif config.model.sigma_dist == "uniform":
        sigmas = torch.tensor(
            np.linspace(config.model.sigma_begin,
                       config.model.sigma_end,
                       config.model.num_classes)
        ).float().to(config.device)
    else:
        raise NotImplementedError("sigma distribution not supported")
    return sigmas
```

```python
import math
import torch


def get_sigmas(n, t_min, t_max, device="cpu"):
    log_max = math.log(t_max)
    log_min = math.log(max(t_min, 1e-10))
    sigmas = torch.exp(torch.linspace(log_max, log_min, n + 1))
    sigmas[-1] = t_min
    return sigmas.to(device)
```
