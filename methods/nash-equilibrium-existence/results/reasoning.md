Let me get the irritation straight first, because it's the irritation that's driving me. Von Neumann and Morgenstern have this gorgeous theorem for two-person zero-sum games — the minimax theorem. Over mixed strategies, `max_x min_y x^T A y = min_y max_x x^T A y`; the game has a value `v`, there's a saddle point, and "playing well" means guaranteeing yourself `v`. It's clean and it's complete. And it is locked inside the smallest possible cage. Two players. Strictly opposed payoffs. The moment I have three players, or two players whose interests aren't perfectly antagonistic, the theory has nothing for me — except the cooperative theory, which solves a *different* problem: it lets the players talk, collude, form binding coalitions, and then asks how the spoils get divided. That's not the situation I care about. I care about people who choose simultaneously, independently, no communication, no contracts, no side-payments, and whose payoffs can be anything at all — not summing to a constant, not opposed.

So what would even count as a "solution" here? That's the thing I have to nail before any theorem. And the first move has to be to figure out *why* minimax needs the cage, so I know what to drop. Why does zero-sum, two-player give you a single number `v`? Because under strict opposition, "good for me" is identically "bad for you" — there's one quantity, and one player pushes it up while the other pushes it down, so a single scalar genuinely summarizes the whole game, and "I can guarantee myself `v`" is a meaningful, self-contained claim. Now break the cage. Three players: whose loss is my gain? Nobody's in particular. Non-zero-sum: we might *both* do better or *both* do worse depending on what we pick. There is no single number everyone is simultaneously straining for or against. So the entire apparatus of "the value of the game," "good strategies guarantee the value" — it's gone. It was never the general notion. It was an artifact of antagonism.

That's freeing, actually, because it tells me what a general solution concept must *not* depend on. It must not mention a value. It must not assume opposition. So what's left? Strip away the antagonism and ask the barest possible question about a profile of choices: is it *stable*? Picture all `n` players having committed to their (possibly mixed) strategies, and I'm one of them, and I get to peek at everyone else's commitment and reconsider only my own. Would I want to change? If at some profile *every* player, looking at the others as fixed, finds they have no incentive to move alone — then the profile holds together. Nobody, acting unilaterally, can do better. That's a rest point. And — this is the key thing — it needs no antagonism whatsoever to be defined. It's a pure no-unilateral-improvement condition. It exists for any payoffs, any number of players.

Let me write it down properly so I can work with it. Let `s = (s_1, ..., s_n)` be a profile of mixed strategies, `s_i` a point in player `i`'s simplex, and let `p_i(s)` be player `i`'s expected payoff — the multilinear extension, linear in each `s_i` separately. Write `(s; r_i)` for the profile with player `i`'s strategy swapped to `r_i`, everyone else unchanged. Then I'll say `s` is an equilibrium point iff for every `i`,

    p_i(s) = max_{r_i} p_i(s; r_i).

Each player's strategy maximizes their own payoff given the others held fixed. Each player is already best-responding to everyone else, simultaneously.

Before I trust this, I owe myself the sanity check: does it reduce to von Neumann in the zero-sum two-player case? Take a two-person zero-sum game. At a saddle point `(x*, y*)`, the row player can't gain by moving off `x*` against `y*` (that's `max_x x^T A y* = x*^T A y*`), and the column player, whose payoff is `-x^T A y`, can't gain by moving off `y*` against `x*` (that's `min_y x*^T A y = x*^T A y*`). So a saddle point is exactly a profile where neither can improve unilaterally — exactly my equilibrium condition. And conversely any equilibrium of a zero-sum two-player game is a saddle pair. So the set of equilibrium points of a two-person zero-sum game is precisely the set of pairs of opposing optimal strategies. Good — it's a genuine generalization, it contains the old theory as the special case, it doesn't contradict it. The definition passes the first test. (And it's not a new idea out of nowhere — Cournot, a century ago, had exactly this shape for two firms picking outputs: the rest point where neither wants to change unilaterally. I'm taking that rest-point notion and making it the *general* solution concept, for any finite game.)

Now the real worry: does such a point always *exist*? It's easy to write a stability condition that's frequently empty. So I need a proof, and ideally one that works for every finite game with no exceptions.

Let me massage the definition into something I can attack. The condition `max_{r_i} p_i(s; r_i)` ranges over all mixed `r_i`, which is an infinite set — annoying. But `p_i(s; r_i)` is *linear* in `r_i` (multilinearity), and `r_i` ranges over a simplex, so the maximum of a linear function over a simplex is attained at a vertex — a *pure* strategy. So

    max_{r_i} p_i(s; r_i) = max_alpha p_i(s; pi_{i alpha}),

the max over `i`'s finitely many pure strategies `pi_{i alpha}`. Let me abbreviate `p_{i alpha}(s) = p_i(s; pi_{i alpha})`, the payoff `i` gets by switching to pure `alpha` while the rest of `s` stands. Then `s` is an equilibrium iff for every `i`,

    p_i(s) = max_alpha p_{i alpha}(s).

And there's a tidier way to see what this means. Player `i`'s actual payoff is `p_i(s) = sum_alpha c_{i alpha} p_{i alpha}(s)`, where `c_{i alpha}` are the weights of `s_i` — it's a convex combination of the pure-deviation payoffs. A convex combination equals the maximum of the things being combined only if every term that gets positive weight already *is* the maximum. So the condition is equivalent to: every pure strategy `i` actually plays with positive probability is a best response. Formally,

    if pi_{i alpha} is used in s (c_{i alpha} > 0), then p_{i alpha}(s) = max_beta p_{i beta}(s).

Equilibrium means every strategy in the support is a best response. That's a much more concrete handle.

Now, where does existence come from? Stare at the structure. The profiles live in the product of the players' simplices — a compact, convex polytope, a "cell." And an equilibrium is a *rest point* of the best-response operation on that cell: I assign to each profile `s` the profiles in which everyone is best-responding to `s`, and I want a profile that's already one of its own targets. Compact, convex, a self-mapping, looking for a point that maps to itself — that's the smell of a fixed-point theorem. That's the whole game now: turn "best response" into a self-map of the cell whose fixed points are exactly the equilibria, then let topology hand me a fixed point.

The honest first attempt: just use best-response directly. Define, for each profile `s`, the set of profiles `t` that "counter" `s` — meaning each `t_i` is a best response to `s`'s other components. Call `t` a countering of `s`. A profile that counters itself — every `t_i` best-responds to the rest of `t` — is exactly an equilibrium. So I want a fixed point of the countering correspondence `s |-> {t : t counters s}`.

But notice it's a *correspondence*, not a function: at a given `s`, player `i` might have *ties* — two pure strategies both achieving the best payoff — and then any mixture of them is an equally good response. So the image of `s` isn't a single point, it's a whole set. Brouwer is for single-valued maps; it won't bite here directly. But Kakutani built exactly the generalization I need: for a non-empty compact convex `S` and a set-valued `phi: S -> 2^S` with non-empty convex values and a closed graph, there's a point with `x in phi(x)`. Let me check my correspondence meets the three conditions.

Non-empty values: against fixed opponents, `p_i` is continuous on the compact simplex, so a best response exists — the argmax is non-empty. Good. Convex values: the set of best responses for player `i` against fixed others is the argmax of a *linear* function over the simplex, which is a face of the simplex — convex. The set of countering profiles is the product of these over `i` — convex. Good. Closed graph: suppose `s^{(k)} -> s`, `t^{(k)} -> t`, and each `t^{(k)}` counters `s^{(k)}`. For player `i`, countering means `p_i(s^{(k)}_{-i}, t_i^{(k)}) >= p_i(s^{(k)}_{-i}, pi_{i alpha})` for every pure `alpha`; that is only finitely many continuous inequalities. Letting `k` go to infinity gives `p_i(s_{-i}, t_i) >= p_i(s_{-i}, pi_{i alpha})` for every `alpha`, so `t_i` is still a best response to `s`'s other components. Closed graph. Good. All three hold — Kakutani gives a fixed point, a self-countering profile, an equilibrium. Done, in the sense that existence is proved.

So I have a proof. And yet it leans on Kakutani, which is itself a non-trivial theorem layered on top of Brouwer. It would be more satisfying — and more elementary — to get this straight from Brouwer, the classical single-valued statement, without going through the set-valued machinery. The obstruction is precisely those ties: I need to collapse the multi-valued countering into an honest continuous *function* of `s` without losing the property that its fixed points are the equilibria.

I try not to jump all the way to a best response; I just *nudge* the profile *toward* better responses, by an amount proportional to how much better they are. Measure, for each player `i` and pure strategy `alpha`, the best payoff `i` could get, `q_i(s) = max_alpha p_{i alpha}(s)`, and look at the signed gap `p_{i alpha}(s) - q_i(s)`, which is `<= 0`, zero exactly for the currently-optimal pure strategies. I want to *add* weight to the good pure strategies and starve the bad ones, then renormalize back onto the simplex, and call that my map. The fixed points should be the profiles where the flow has nothing left to do — where the weight already sits only on best responses — which is the equilibrium condition.

But let me actually try to build the flow and watch it break, because I think it does. The naive thing: put weight on `alpha` proportional to `max(0, p_{i alpha}(s) - q_i(s))`. But `p_{i alpha}(s) - q_i(s) <= 0` always, so `max(0, ...)` is zero except where `p_{i alpha} = q_i`, and there it's also zero. Everything's zero; I can't normalize — dividing by a zero sum. So that exact gain is useless as a numerator. The signed gap is the wrong sign to drive a flow.

Let me flip it: drive the flow by `q_i(s) - p_{i alpha}(s) >= 0`? No — that *rewards* the worst strategies, exactly backwards. I want to reward proximity to the best. So take `phi_{i alpha}(s) = p_{i alpha}(s) - q_i(s) + 1/lambda` for a positive integer `lambda`, and then `phi^+_{i alpha} = max(0, phi_{i alpha})`. Now the best pure strategies (shortfall 0) get value `1/lambda > 0`, strategies within `1/lambda` of the best get a positive value shrinking to 0 at the margin, and strategies more than `1/lambda` worse get clipped to 0. The sum over `alpha` is at least the single best term, `>= 1/lambda > 0`, so I can normalize: set

    c'_{i alpha}(s, lambda) = phi^+_{i alpha}(s, lambda) / sum_beta phi^+_{i beta}(s, lambda),

and `s'_i = sum_alpha c'_{i alpha} pi_{i alpha}`, giving a map `s -> s'(s, lambda)`. Every operation — the payoffs, the max defining `q_i`, the `max(0, .)`, the division by a strictly positive denominator — is continuous, so `s -> s'(s, lambda)` is a continuous self-map of the cell. Brouwer hands me a fixed point `s_lambda` for each `lambda`.

But a fixed point of *this* map isn't an equilibrium — it's an approximate one, smeared by the `1/lambda`. At `s_lambda`, every pure strategy within roughly `1/lambda` of optimal can still carry weight. So I have to take `lambda -> infinity` and chase the limit. The cell is compact, so the sequence `s_lambda` has a convergent subsequence `s_lambda -> s*`. Is `s*` an equilibrium? Suppose not: then some player `i` uses, at `s*`, a pure strategy `pi_{i alpha}` that is genuinely non-optimal, `p_{i alpha}(s*) - q_i(s*) < -eps` for some `eps > 0`. By continuity, for large `lambda`, `p_{i alpha}(s_lambda) - q_i(s_lambda) + 1/lambda < 0` too, so `phi^+_{i alpha}(s_lambda) = 0`, so `c'_{i alpha}(s_lambda) = 0`, so `pi_{i alpha}` carries *no* weight in the fixed point `s_lambda`; passing to the limit, `pi_{i alpha}` carries no weight in `s*` — contradicting that `s*` uses it. So `s*` is an equilibrium.

It works, but I'm not happy. Look at what I had to do: introduce an artificial parameter `1/lambda`, build a whole *sequence* of maps, and take a limit through a subsequence. And the reason I can't just set `lambda = infinity` from the start is not a technical nuisance. If I literally drop the `1/lambda` and keep only `max(0, p_{i alpha} - q_i)`, every numerator is zero and the normalization collapses. If instead I take the pointwise limit of the normalized maps, I get the rule "put equal mass on the currently best pure strategies," which jumps when a nearly-best strategy becomes tied for best. At a tie boundary the selected face changes abruptly, so Brouwer has no continuous map to apply to. The `1/lambda` is a crutch holding the denominator off zero and smoothing the tie boundary, and the limit kicks the crutch away. The proof is correct but it's scaffolding, not architecture.

So let me sit with *why* the natural map is degenerate and see if there's a different, honest gain that doesn't need propping up. The trouble was that I measured each pure strategy's quality relative to the *best attainable*, `q_i`, which makes every gain `<= 0` and the optimal ones exactly `0` — a numerator that's flush against zero precisely where I need it positive. What if I measure relative not to the unreachable best but to where the player *currently is*, `p_i(s)` — the payoff of the mix they're actually playing? Define the gain of deviating to pure `alpha` as

    phi_{i alpha}(s) = max(0, p_{i alpha}(s) - p_i(s)).

This is how much player `i` would *gain* by jumping to pure strategy `alpha` instead of their current mixture. It's `>= 0`. It's strictly positive exactly when `alpha` beats the current mix, and zero when `alpha` is no better. And here's the thing that makes it work where the other failed: this is *not* zero everywhere off equilibrium. Whenever the player isn't already best-responding, some pure strategy beats their current average, so some `phi_{i alpha} > 0`. The gain only vanishes *across the board* for a player when no pure deviation beats their current mix — which is the best-response condition itself.

Now I don't add weight relative to a fragile maximum; I add the gain itself as a bonus and renormalize. For each component `s_i`, push extra mass onto each pure strategy in proportion to its gain, then divide by the total to stay on the simplex:

    s'_i = ( s_i + sum_alpha phi_{i alpha}(s) pi_{i alpha} ) / ( 1 + sum_alpha phi_{i alpha}(s) ).

The numerator is the old mix `s_i` plus a non-negative bonus vector `sum_alpha phi_{i alpha} pi_{i alpha}`; the `s_i` part has total mass 1 and the bonus has total mass `sum_alpha phi_{i alpha}`, so the whole numerator has total mass `1 + sum_alpha phi_{i alpha}` — which is exactly the denominator. So `s'_i` is automatically a probability vector, lands back on the simplex by construction, no clipping needed. And the denominator is `1 + sum phi >= 1 > 0` *structurally* — there's nothing to perturb, the `1` does for free what the `1/lambda` was straining to do. Call the whole thing `T: s -> s'`. Every piece — `p_{i alpha}`, `p_i`, `max(0, .)`, a denominator bounded below by 1 — is continuous, so `T` is a continuous self-map of the cell.

The real test is whether the fixed points of `T` are *exactly* the equilibria — both directions, no limit. Let me do it carefully.

Take any profile `s` and fix a player `i`. Among the pure strategies `i` actually uses (positive weight), consider the least profitable one — the used `pi_{i alpha}` with the smallest `p_{i alpha}(s)`. Since `p_i(s)` is the weighted average of the `p_{i alpha}(s)` over the used strategies, the smallest used one is `<= p_i(s)`: that is, `p_{i alpha}(s) <= p_i(s)`. Hence its gain `phi_{i alpha}(s) = max(0, p_{i alpha}(s) - p_i(s)) = 0`. So at *every* profile, each player has at least one used strategy with zero gain.

Now suppose `s` is fixed under `T`, `s' = s`. Look at that least-profitable used strategy `pi_{i alpha}`, the one with `phi_{i alpha}(s) = 0`. In the numerator for `s'_i`, its mass is `c_{i alpha} + phi_{i alpha}(s) = c_{i alpha} + 0 = c_{i alpha}` — the bonus adds nothing to it. After dividing by `1 + sum_beta phi_{i beta}(s)`, its weight in `s'_i` is `c_{i alpha} / (1 + sum_beta phi_{i beta}(s))`. For `s` to be fixed, this must equal `c_{i alpha}`, and `c_{i alpha} > 0` (it's a used strategy). The only way `c_{i alpha} / (1 + sum_beta phi_{i beta}(s)) = c_{i alpha}` with `c_{i alpha} > 0` is for the denominator to be 1, i.e. `sum_beta phi_{i beta}(s) = 0`. Since every `phi_{i beta} >= 0`, that forces `phi_{i beta}(s) = 0` for *all* `beta` — no pure strategy gives player `i` any gain over their current mix. So every `p_{i beta}(s) <= p_i(s)`, while `p_i(s)` is the average of the `p_{i beta}(s)` under `s_i`; therefore `p_i(s)` equals the maximum pure-deviation payoff. By the reduction I did earlier, the maximum over all mixed deviations is the same pure maximum, so `i` cannot improve by any deviation: `i` is best-responding. This holds for every player, so `s` is an equilibrium.

The converse is immediate now. If `s` is an equilibrium, then for every `i` and every `alpha`, `p_{i alpha}(s) <= p_i(s)` (no profitable pure deviation), so every gain `phi_{i alpha}(s) = 0`, so every denominator is 1, the bonus vanishes, and `s'_i = s_i / 1 = s_i`. So `s` is fixed under `T`.

The fixed points of `T` are exactly the equilibrium points — an iff, no `1/lambda`, no sequence, no limit. And the profile space is a cell: compact and convex, a continuous map of it into itself. Brouwer's fixed point theorem says `T` has at least one fixed point. That fixed point is an equilibrium. So:

*Every finite game has an equilibrium point.*

That's the theorem, and the map `T` is the whole proof. The gain function `phi_{i alpha} = max(0, p_{i alpha} - p_i)` is the right object precisely because it's the slack in the best-response condition itself — it's zero, simultaneously for all `alpha`, exactly when the player is best-responding, so its vanishing *is* equilibrium, and feeding it through `renormalize(s_i + bonus)` converts "no gain anywhere" directly into "fixed under `T`." No antagonism anywhere in the argument; it never mattered how many players there were or whether payoffs summed to anything.

Let me push on the structure a little, because the map I built turns out to give more for free. Suppose the game has an automorphism — a relabeling of pure strategies that permutes players and preserves all payoffs; it induces a permutation `chi` of profiles. A profile is symmetric if `s^chi = s` for every such `chi`. Do I lose existence if I insist on respecting all these symmetries? The map `T` was defined purely from the payoffs `p_{i alpha}`, `p_i` — nothing in it refers to a labeling. So `T` is intrinsic: it commutes with every automorphism, `(Ts)^chi = T(s^chi)`. Now the set of profiles fixed by every automorphism is non-empty — the barycentric profile `s_{i0} = (sum_alpha pi_{i alpha}) / (number of pure strategies)` is fixed by every `chi`. It's convex: if `s` and `t` are both symmetric, then applying any `chi` to their midpoint gives `((s+t)/2)^chi = (s^chi + t^chi)/2 = (s+t)/2`. And it's closed. So the symmetric profiles form a compact convex subcell. Because `T` commutes with `chi`, it maps a symmetric profile to a symmetric profile: if `s` is symmetric, `(Ts)^chi = T(s^chi) = Ts`. So `T` maps this subcell into itself, and Brouwer on the subcell gives a fixed point there — an equilibrium invariant under every automorphism of the game.

Next, the shape of the equilibrium set when a game is well-behaved. Say a game is solvable if its set of equilibria `S` is interchangeable: whenever `(t; r_i)` and `s` are both equilibria, so is `(s; r_i)` — you can mix and match equilibrium components freely. (In a two-person zero-sum game this always holds, and `S` is just the product of the two players' sets of optimal strategies — von Neumann's "good strategies"; that's the special case again.) In a solvable game, define player `i`'s set of equilibrium strategies `S_i` = the `s_i` that appear as `i`'s component in some equilibrium. What does `S_j` look like? Fix any equilibrium `t`. Then `(t; s_j)` is an equilibrium iff `s_j in S_j`, and the no-gain inequalities at `(t; s_j)` say: for all `i` and `alpha`, `p_i(t; s_j) - p_{i alpha}(t; s_j) >= 0`, where `p_{i alpha}(t; s_j)` means player `i`'s payoff after switching to pure `alpha` while the rest of `(t; s_j)` is held fixed. But `p_i` is multilinear, so each of these is a *linear* inequality in `s_j` — `F_{i alpha}(s_j) >= 0`. Each linear inequality either holds on all of the simplex or cuts it with a hyperplane, so the solution set is the simplex intersected with finitely many half-spaces: a convex polytope. So `S_j` is a polyhedral convex subset of player `j`'s strategy simplex — equivalently, the convex hull of its finitely many vertices. *The equilibrium-strategy sets of a solvable game are polyhedral convex.* And the underlying fact powering this — that averaging two equilibrium strategies of a player gives another — comes straight from multilinearity: if `(t; s_i)` and `(t; s'_i)` are equilibria, add their defining inequalities and divide by 2; linearity in `s_i` turns the average of the inequalities into the inequality for `(s_i + s'_i)/2`, so the midpoint is an equilibrium strategy too. Convexity of `S_i` falls out of the same linearity that made best responses convex in the first place.

One more tool the structure suggests, for actually *finding* equilibria in a concrete game. Call `s'_i` a dominator of `s_i` if `p_i(t; s'_i) > p_i(t; s_i)` for *every* profile `t` of the others — `s'_i` is strictly better no matter what the opponents do. By multilinearity it's enough to check this against the opponents' *pure* profiles (a linear function exceeds another over the whole product of simplices iff it does at the vertices), so dominance is checkable on finitely many cases. No equilibrium can have a dominated mixed strategy as one of its components: if `i` were using `s_i` while `s'_i` dominates it, `i` could switch and do strictly better against the actual others, so `i` isn't best-responding. So I can delete dominated components from consideration — and deleting one player's strategies can expose new dominations for another player — shrinking the game before hunting for the rest point. Paired with this, a contradiction method: hypothesize an equilibrium lies in some region of the strategy spaces, push the equilibrium conditions through to forced equations, and if they're inconsistent, rule the region out. Between dominance-elimination and contradiction, a concrete game's equilibria can sometimes be pinned down by hand — for instance a stripped-down three-player poker, parameterized by the probabilities of each betting action, where dominance and contradiction leave a small algebraic system. The point isn't the poker; it's that the same conditions that prove a rest point exists also give finite inequalities and eliminations for locating it.

I can now see the clean chain. Minimax was caged by antagonism because it needed a single value, and a value needs strict opposition. Drop the value; replace it with the only thing that survives without antagonism — stability against unilateral deviation, a profile where every player is already best-responding. That definition reduces to the saddle point in the zero-sum two-player case, so it generalizes rather than replaces. Multilinearity collapses the best-response condition to "every used pure strategy is a best response," and puts the whole problem on the product of simplices — a compact convex cell — as the search for a rest point of best-response. Best-response is set-valued because of ties, so Kakutani settles existence at once; wanting it from Brouwer alone, I try to single-value the flow, find that gain-relative-to-the-best only survives with a `1/lambda` crutch and a discontinuous limiting selector; then I measure gain relative to the player's *current* payoff, `phi_{i alpha} = max(0, p_{i alpha} - p_i)`, which is zero across the board for a player exactly when they're best-responding, and renormalizing `s_i` plus the gain-bonus gives a single continuous map `T` whose fixed points are *exactly* the equilibria — no limit. Brouwer on the compact convex cell finishes it: every finite game has an equilibrium. The same intrinsic map, restricted to profiles fixed by all automorphisms, yields a symmetric equilibrium; and multilinearity makes the equilibrium-strategy sets of solvable games polyhedral convex, with dominance and contradiction as levers for computing examples.
