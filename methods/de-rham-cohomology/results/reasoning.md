I want calculus to remember something that a coordinate chart cannot see. In a small patch, everything looks like `R^n`, so the usual local tests keep telling me that compatibility is enough: a curl-free vector field should be a gradient, a divergence-free field should be a curl, and more generally a closed positive-degree form should have a local primitive. That is too local for topology. A loop around a missing point is not visible in any small disk, but the whole loop refuses to shrink. I need a calculus object that behaves locally like a derivative and yet can fail globally for exactly that reason.

Differential forms are already the right language for integration. A `k`-form is meant to be integrated over a `k`-dimensional oriented object, and the exterior derivative raises degree by one. If `alpha = sum_I alpha_I dx_I`, then `d alpha = sum_{I,j} partial_j alpha_I dx_j wedge dx_I`. The alternating sign is not decoration; it is what makes the second derivative disappear. When I apply `d` twice to a function, the `partial_i partial_j f` and `partial_j partial_i f` terms cancel because the coordinate derivatives commute while `dx_i wedge dx_j = - dx_j wedge dx_i`. The product rule then pushes the same cancellation through all forms, so `d^2 = 0`.

So derivatives have a necessary condition: anything of the form `d beta` satisfies `d(d beta)=0`. Let me call `d alpha = 0` "closed" and `alpha = d beta` "exact." Exact implies closed for the purely formal reason `d^2=0`. If I stopped here, I would only have a compatibility test. The real question is whether the reverse implication fails, and where.

On `R^n`, the reverse implication does not fail in positive degree. The Poincare lemma says every closed positive-degree form is exact. I can see why from the homotopy idea: compare `R x X` with `X` by projecting to `X` and by including `X` as the zero section. Write a form on `R x X` as `dt wedge beta(t,x) + gamma(t,x)`, integrate the `beta` part from `0` to `t`, and call that operator `P`. Then the calculation gives `dP + Pd = 1 - pi^* s_0^*`. If the form is closed, it differs from a pulled-back form by an exact form. Iterating this contraction down to a point kills all positive-degree classes on `R^n`. Locally, there is no invariant hiding inside a closed form.

That means the obstruction I want is forced to be global. The punctured plane is the clean test. Consider `omega = (x dy - y dx)/(x^2 + y^2)` on `R^2 - {(0,0)}`. In polar coordinates it is `d theta` on any branch where an angle coordinate is chosen. Locally it has a primitive. Globally, there is no single-valued angle function on the punctured plane. If I restrict to the unit circle `x=cos t`, `y=sin t`, then `x dy - y dx` pulls back to `dt`, and `x^2+y^2` pulls back to `1`, so the integral around the circle is `int_0^{2*pi} dt = 2*pi`. If `omega` were globally exact, say `omega = df`, the integral around a closed loop would be zero. It is not zero. So the missing point has become a failure of "closed" to imply "exact."

Now Stokes tells me exactly what to quotient out. If `alpha = d beta` and `c` is a closed `k`-cycle, then `int_c alpha = int_c d beta = int_boundary c beta = 0`. Exact forms are invisible to closed cycles. Keeping two closed forms separate when their difference is exact would preserve information no cycle integral can detect. So I should identify closed forms that differ by exact forms. The object is `ker(d: Omega^k -> Omega^{k+1}) / im(d: Omega^{k-1} -> Omega^k)`. This quotient is not a recombination of known gadgets; it is the minimal bookkeeping forced by Stokes. Closed forms are the ones whose integrals survive deformation of cycles, and exact forms are the ones whose integrals vanish on cycles.

The same Stokes calculation checks the other side. If `omega` is closed and two cycles differ by a boundary, `c' - c = boundary b`, then `int_{c'} omega - int_c omega = int_boundary b omega = int_b d omega = 0`. A closed form does not care which representative of a homology class I choose. If I change `omega` by an exact form, I also do not change its integral over a cycle. Therefore the formula `[omega]([c]) = int_c omega` is well-defined on closed forms modulo exact forms and on cycles modulo boundaries.

This is the moment when the calculus object has a topological meaning. A class is a way of assigning numbers to cycles by integration. Exact classes assign zero to every cycle. Nonzero classes exist precisely when a closed form has periods that cannot be erased by finding a global primitive. In the punctured plane, the circle around the origin receives `2*pi`; a small local loop bounding a disk receives zero. The number is not recording a local curl. It is recording that the loop encloses something no disk in the manifold can fill.

The quotient also makes the low-degree cases fit. In degree zero, a closed `0`-form is a locally constant function, because `df=0` means all directional derivatives vanish. On a connected manifold this gives one copy of `R`. In top degree on a compact connected oriented `n`-manifold, every `n`-form is closed because there are no `(n+1)`-forms. If an `n`-form is exact, its total integral is zero by Stokes, since the manifold has empty boundary. A bump `n`-form with total integral `1` cannot be exact. Conversely, by cutting the manifold into coordinate patches and comparing bump forms, every top-degree form is cohomologous to a multiple of such a bump form. Integration itself becomes the coordinate on top cohomology.

The global theorem should now be almost unavoidable. Singular homology already says what the holes are by chains, cycles, and boundaries. My form construction gives a linear functional on those cycles by integration. Stokes proves this functional is well-defined on both sides. On a convex open set, both stories reduce to a point: singular cohomology is trivial above degree zero, and the Poincare lemma makes closed positive-degree forms exact. For disjoint unions, both theories split componentwise. For a union `U union V`, the Mayer-Vietoris sequences in the two theories line up, and the integration map commutes with the restriction and connecting maps because pullback, exterior derivative, and Stokes are compatible. If the comparison is an isomorphism on `U`, on `V`, and on `U cap V`, the five lemma forces it to be an isomorphism on `U union V`. Cover the manifold by good local pieces and repeat.

So the final shape is this: differential forms encode global topology because the exterior derivative creates a complex, local exactness removes all local positive-degree obstruction, and Stokes turns closed forms modulo exact forms into functionals on cycles. The failure of a closed form to be exact is exactly the failure of its periods to vanish around holes. De Rham cohomology is the space of those failures, and de Rham's theorem says this smooth calculus measurement recovers singular cohomology with real coefficients.
