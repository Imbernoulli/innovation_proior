I start with the thing the scalar theory silently destroys. A return is not born as a number. It is a random sum, `Z(x,a) = sum_t gamma^t R_t`, with randomness entering through rewards, transitions, and possibly the policy. The number I normally call `Q(x,a)` is only `E Z(x,a)`. That expectation is useful because it satisfies Bellman's equation and gives me a contraction, but it is also a collapse. If one future means survival and another means immediate failure, the mean may describe neither future.

So I want to look at the recursion before the average is taken, not after. The scalar equation `Q = E R + gamma E Q'` is what survives the collapse; the question is what it collapsed from. If I strip the outer expectation off both sides, the natural candidate is a recursion between the random variables themselves:

`Z(x,a) =_D R(x,a) + gamma Z(X',A')`.

The equality has to be equality in distribution, not pathwise. I am not matching one sampled trajectory to another sampled trajectory; I am matching laws. The right side is a compound random variable made from the immediate reward, the random next state-action, and the next return law. That immediately changes the problem. I no longer need a scalar norm on value vectors. I need a distance between distributions, and then a worst-case lift of that distance over all state-action pairs.

My first instinct is to hope that any familiar distribution discrepancy will do, but the discount factor pushes back. Discounting does a geometric operation: it shrinks the support along the return axis. For a contraction I need the distance between two laws to come down by a factor of `gamma` when both are run through `gamma * (...)`. A metric that only cares about overlap or likelihood will not do that. Two distinct atoms have total-variation distance one whether they sit at `0` and `1` or at `0` and `gamma`; multiplying by `gamma` moved them closer on the axis, but TV did not register it. KL and Kolmogorov have the same vertical habit — they read disagreement, not transport. The contraction, if it exists, has to come from a metric that measures horizontal movement of mass.

That requirement is specific enough to name a family. A transport metric — Wasserstein, equivalently the Mallows/`d_p` distance between inverse cdfs under a shared uniform draw — measures exactly the `L_p` cost of dragging one law's mass onto the other along the value axis. The same recursive-distributional fixed-point idea appears in Roesler's Quicksort analysis, where a limiting random variable is the fixed point of a distributional map and contraction is proved in such a metric. For cdfs `F` and `G`, `d_p(F,G) = ||F^{-1}(U_0) - G^{-1}(U_0)||_p` with `U_0 ~ Uniform[0,1]`. I want two properties from it before I trust any proof: that scaling both variables by `gamma` scales `d_p` by `gamma`, and that adding a common reward to both leaves `d_p` unchanged. Both should follow from the inverse-cdf form, since `(gamma F)^{-1} = gamma F^{-1}` and a common shift cancels. But "should follow" is exactly the kind of claim I keep telling myself not to assert, so I check it on concrete laws before leaning on it.

Take `Z_1` with atoms `{0, 1}` at weights `{1/2, 1/2}` and `Z_2` with atoms `{0.5, 2}` at weights `{0.3, 0.7}`, and `gamma = 0.9`. Computing `d_1` as the area between the two cdfs, I get `d_1(Z_1, Z_2) = 1.05`. Now add a common reward `r = 3.7` to both: the area between the shifted cdfs is again `1.05`, unchanged, as the cancellation predicted. Now scale both by `gamma = 0.9`: the area drops to `0.945`, which is exactly `0.9 * 1.05`. So the two properties I needed are not wishful — on this example the shift is invisible to `d_1` and the scaling pulls it down by precisely `gamma`. That is enough to believe the backup will contract, and it tells me why TV could not: TV would have stayed pinned regardless of the scaling.

Now I can write the evaluation operator and see whether the contraction goes through. Define `P^pi Z(x,a) =_D Z(X',A')` and `T^pi Z(x,a) =_D R(x,a) + gamma P^pi Z(x,a)`. For two candidate return laws at a fixed state-action pair, the reward is common additive noise (no increase), the `gamma` is a common scaling (factor `gamma`), and the successor is a mixture over next state-actions that I can couple identically on both sides before applying an optimal conditional coupling. Chaining those three facts,

`d_p(T^pi Z_1, T^pi Z_2) <= gamma d_p(P^pi Z_1, P^pi Z_2) <= gamma sup_{x,a} d_p(Z_1, Z_2)`,

and taking the supremum over state-actions on the left,

`bar d_p(T^pi Z_1, T^pi Z_2) <= gamma bar d_p(Z_1, Z_2)`.

The two numeric facts I just verified are doing the real work in that chain. So for a fixed policy the operator is a `gamma`-contraction in maximal Wasserstein: it has a unique fixed point, iteration converges geometrically, and the fixed point is the actual random return law `Z^pi`. This is more than learning a confidence interval around `Q`. The law itself is the dynamic-programming quantity, and `Q` is only its first moment.

I should be careful before claiming this proof hands me every summary statistic for free. The tempting overstatement is that a Wasserstein contraction on the law gives a clean recursion for, say, the variance. But `d_2` mixes a mean term and a spread term, so variance needs checking on its own. The recursion `Z =_D R + gamma Z'` with `R` independent of `Z'` gives `Var(Z) = Var(R) + gamma^2 Var(Z')`, with `gamma` squared, not `gamma`. Let me confirm the exponent rather than trust the algebra: with `R ~ Normal(2, 1.3)` so `Var(R) = 1.69`, and `Z' ~ Exponential(5)` so `Var(Z') = 25`, the predicted variance is `1.69 + 0.81 * 25 = 21.99`. Sampling four million draws of `R + 0.9 Z'` gives empirical variance `21.98`. They agree, and the `gamma^2` (not `gamma`) is what makes it agree — this matches Sobel's older second-moment result and reinforces that moment recursions are shadows of the full law, propagating at their own rates, not replacements for it.

Then I try to carry the same backup to control, and here the analogy with the scalar case stops cooperating. In the usual Bellman optimality operator, the max returns a number, and if several actions are optimal the scalar value does not record which one I took. A return distribution does record it. To even define a distributional optimality operator I have to pick a greedy policy, and the only sensible criterion is still `E Z`, so the selection rule `pi in G_Z` becomes part of the operator. The mean component is fine: expectation commutes with the backup and reproduces the ordinary scalar optimality operator, which still contracts. The worry is whether the full distribution inherits any contraction, because greedy selection is a discontinuous function of the law — an arbitrarily small change in one action's mean can flip the argmax and swap in a different successor distribution.

I make the worry concrete instead of asserting it. From a state `x2`, action `a0` is terminal with value `0` (a point mass at `0`), and action `a1` pays `eps +/- 1`, i.e. atoms at `eps-1` and `eps+1` with equal weight, mean `eps > 0`. So greedy picks `a1`, and the true optimal law at `x2` is the bimodal `{eps-1, eps+1}`. A predecessor `x1` with `gamma = 1`, `r = 0` just copies the chosen successor law. Now perturb only `a1` downward to `-eps +/- 1`, mean `-eps`. With `eps = 0.05`, the `d_1` distance between the true and perturbed `a1` laws is `0.10`, exactly `2 eps` — a small perturbation. But its mean is now negative, so greedy at `x2` flips from `a1` to `a0`. The backed-up law at `x1` therefore changes from the bimodal `{eps-1, eps+1}` to a point mass at `0`. Computing `d_1` between those two backed-up laws gives `1.0`. So a perturbation of size `0.10` in the input produced an output gap of `1.0`: the operator expanded the distance by a factor of `10`, the opposite of contraction. With tie-breaking it can even cycle; with multiple optimal policies, iterates can approach laws generated by nonstationary sequences of optimal policies rather than a single stationary optimal law.

So the situation splits cleanly. Policy evaluation has a genuine Bellman fixed point in distribution space — the contraction I checked numerically holds. Greedy control does not: action selection is discontinuous in the law, and the two-state example shows a single backup blowing the distance up tenfold. A fixed stationary optimal distribution is recovered only with extra consistent tie-breaking over optimal policies. The negative control result is not a footnote; it is something the scalar theory hides precisely because the scalar max forgets the action.

The practical algorithm raises a separate question. Represent each return law by probabilities on fixed atoms `z_i`. The exact backup sends those atoms to `r + gamma z_i`, so the target support generally no longer coincides with the prediction support. Wasserstein can compare laws on disjoint supports, so the obvious move is to make the training loss the sample Wasserstein distance and descend it by stochastic gradient, one transition at a time. Before committing to that I want to know the sampled gradient actually points the right way, because in RL I never see the true target law, only a sampled successor.

I test the smallest version. Let the target be a Bernoulli law, mass `1/2` at `0` and `1/2` at `1`, and let my model be a single point mass whose location `q` I optimize. The true loss is `d_1(delta_q, target)`; the sampled objective is `E_{Y~target} d_1(delta_q, delta_Y) = (1/2)|q-0| + (1/2)|q-1|`. Tabulating both over `q in [-0.5, 1.5]`, they are identical at every `q`: both are `1.0` at `q = -0.5`, both decrease to `0.5` on the whole interval `[0,1]`, both rise again after `1`. So here the sampled objective is not biased at all — its minimizers are exactly the true minimizers. That is the opposite of the failure I half-expected, and for a moment it looks like the naive sampled-Wasserstein loss is fine.

The point model was the wrong test. A point mass can only translate; the place sampling can mislead me is when the model itself spreads mass and the sampled target collapses that spread. So I rerun it with a model that is a two-atom mixture, atoms fixed at `0` and `1`, parameter `p = P(\text{atom}=1)`, against the same Bernoulli`(1/2)` target. The true `d_1(model_p, target)` is V-shaped: `0.5` at `p=0`, falling linearly to `0` at `p = 1/2` (the correct mixture), rising back to `0.5` at `p=1`. The sampled objective `E_{Y~target} d_1(model_p, delta_Y) = (1/2) d_1(model_p, delta_0) + (1/2) d_1(model_p, delta_1)`, tabulated over `p in [0,1]`, is flat at `0.5` for every single value of `p`. It gives zero gradient toward `p = 1/2`; it cannot tell the correct mixture from a degenerate one. So the sampled Wasserstein loss is biased after all — but only once the model carries spread, which is exactly the regime I need for distributional RL. The earlier point-mass agreement was a coincidence of a model that could not be wrong in the relevant way.

That rules out training the raw Wasserstein objective on single transitions, so I need a different loss on the same fixed grid. The fix that keeps the grid is to project the sampled backup back onto it. Transform each atom to `[r + gamma z_j]` clipped to `[V_min, V_max]`, then split its probability linearly between the two nearest grid atoms. After projection, target and prediction live on the same support `{z_i}`, and I can train with cross-entropy / KL, which does have an unbiased single-sample stochastic gradient. Let me trace the projection on a tiny grid to be sure it does what I think. Take atoms `[0,1,2,3,4]` (`Delta z = 1`), a transition with `r = 0.5`, `gamma = 0.5`, non-terminal, and a next-distribution placing all mass on `z = 2`. The transformed atom is `0.5 + 0.5*2 = 1.5`, which lands halfway between atoms `1` and `2`; the projection splits the mass `0.5/0.5`, giving `[0, 0.5, 0.5, 0, 0]`. Total mass is `1.0` and the mean is `1.5`, exactly the transformed value — the projection preserves both mass and mean. For a terminal transition (`gamma * (1-terminal) = 0`), every atom maps to `r = 0.5`, landing between atoms `0` and `1`, giving `[0.5, 0.5, 0, 0, 0]` with mean `0.5`. Both traces are exactly what the backup should produce.

So the fixed-grid projection is not just a representational convenience; it is what makes the loss sampling-friendly while preserving the moments that matter. The later categorical analysis adds that the projection is a non-expansion in Cramer distance and the projected Bellman operator contracts there with modulus `sqrt(gamma)` — I would want to check that separately, but it is consistent with the fact that the projected algorithm is empirically stable where the raw Wasserstein gradient is not.

The final picture is coherent and, importantly, each load-bearing claim survived a check. The random return law is the right Bellman object. Fixed-policy evaluation contracts in a transport metric, because that metric sees discounting as the `gamma`-shrinkage I verified numerically and TV does not. Greedy control breaks the distributional contraction — the two-state example expands a `0.10` gap into a `1.0` gap — because action selection is discontinuous in the law. And for a learner, the raw sampled Wasserstein loss is biased the moment the model carries spread, so I project the sampled transformed support onto a fixed grid and train the projected target with cross-entropy. The conceptual advance is the Bellman fixed-point theory for the whole return distribution, and the negative control result is part of that advance rather than a defect of it.
