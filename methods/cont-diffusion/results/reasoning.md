Let me start from what actually goes wrong, because the fast version of this head already works and I need to be honest about why it is not enough. I have a parallel-decoding trunk: it ingests vision, language, and proprioception, and at the action slots it hands me a block of final-layer hidden states, one bundle per future timestep in the chunk. The cheapest thing I can do, and the thing I would reach for first, is run each bundle through a small MLP, read off a 7-D continuous action, and train it to match the logged demonstration with a mean absolute error. One trunk pass, a negligible head, full continuous precision, fast convergence. So what is wrong with it? Stare at the objective. Minimizing E|a - a_pred| over the data picks, for each observation, the conditional median of the logged actions; minimizing E(a - a_pred)^2 would pick the conditional mean. Both are a single number per observation.

That is fine if, given what the robot sees, there is essentially one right action. But demonstrations are not always like that. At a fork an expert sometimes goes left and sometimes right; sometimes regrasps now, sometimes later; the logged actions at nearly identical observations can form a multimodal distribution. Let me make the worry concrete rather than wave at it. Take a single action coordinate at one observation where half the demonstrations swerve left (call it +1) and half swerve right (-1), with "go straight" (≈0) being exactly the action that drives into the obstacle. The squared-loss optimum is the mean of {+1 fifty times, -1 fifty times}, which is 0; the absolute-loss optimum is the median, also 0. So both point estimators output 0 — the one value that is *not* in the data and is precisely the collision. If I tilt the data to 55 left / 45 right, the mean moves to 0.1, still essentially straight ahead, while the median snaps to +1. The mean's behavior is the damning one: a smooth interpolation between two valid modes that lands on an invalid action, and there is no learning rate or schedule that fixes it, because it is the exact minimizer of the loss I wrote down. The head outputs one vector; the world wants a distribution. So the real task is not just "predict the action"; it is to represent p(actions | observation) richly enough to be multimodal, and then sample one action from it.

So I need a head that models a whole conditional distribution over the action chunk, not a point. I could put a mixture density network on top and predict Gaussian-mixture weights, means, and variances, but then I have to pick the number of components up front, keep the components alive, and hope a fixed small mixture is enough for the geometry of valid action chunks. I could go energy-based and learn E(o, a), which is expressive and handles multimodality, but then sampling means inner-loop optimization or MCMC, and training needs negative samples. What I want is something that still trains with a regression-flavored loss — no adversary, no partition function, no negative sampler — while producing samples from a multimodal continuous distribution.

One construction that fits this shape is to deliberately corrupt a clean action chunk x_0 with Gaussian noise, then learn to reverse that corruption. The forward step is

  q(x_t | x_{t-1}) = N(x_t; sqrt(1 - beta_t) x_{t-1}, beta_t I).

After enough small steps the data approaches pure noise. If I can learn the reverse direction, I can start from x_T ~ N(0,I) and denoise down to a clean action chunk, and because generation starts from fresh noise, different seeds can flow toward different valid action modes — exactly the multimodality the point head cannot express.

The thing I cannot afford is to iterate the noising chain during training; I need a closed form that jumps to any noise level in one shot. Let me derive it and not just quote it. Write alpha_t = 1 - beta_t and bar_alpha_t = prod_{s=1}^t alpha_s. Each step scales the previous state by sqrt(alpha_t) and adds independent noise with variance beta_t = 1 - alpha_t. Composing two steps, the noise carried from x_{t-2} gets scaled by sqrt(alpha_t) before the new noise is added, so the two independent variances combine as (1 - alpha_t) from the fresh step plus alpha_t(1 - alpha_{t-1}) from the inherited one. I should check that sum collapses the way I expect:

  (1 - alpha_t) + alpha_t(1 - alpha_{t-1}) = 1 - alpha_t + alpha_t - alpha_t alpha_{t-1} = 1 - alpha_t alpha_{t-1}.

With a concrete alpha_t = 0.85, alpha_{t-1} = 0.9 that is 0.15 + 0.85·0.1 = 0.235, and 1 - 0.85·0.9 = 0.235 — they agree. Inducting on this gives the marginal

  x_t = sqrt(bar_alpha_t) x_0 + sqrt(1 - bar_alpha_t) eps,    eps ~ N(0, I).

Before I trust it, let me simulate the actual step-by-step chain and compare. Taking a six-step schedule beta = (.05,.1,.15,.2,.25,.3) and a clean scalar x_0 = 2, running the recursion x <- sqrt(alpha_t) x + sqrt(beta_t) z four million times gives an empirical mean of 1.1055 and variance 0.695 at the last step. The closed form predicts mean sqrt(bar_alpha_T)·2 = 1.105 and variance 1 - bar_alpha_T = 0.6948. Mean and variance match to three digits, so the one-shot marginal is the chain, and I can noise to any level with a single draw.

Now I need to decide what the reverse model predicts. The true posterior q(x_{t-1} | x_t, x_0) is tractable because the process is linear-Gaussian, and its mean is a combination of x_t and x_0. But x_0 is unavailable at sampling time. Rearranging the marginal,

  x_0 = (x_t - sqrt(1 - bar_alpha_t) eps) / sqrt(bar_alpha_t),

so the only missing quantity is the noise eps that was added — knowing eps is equivalent to knowing x_0. So I can have the network predict eps with eps_theta(x_t, t) and substitute, which gives the reverse mean

  mu_theta(x_t,t) = (1/sqrt(alpha_t)) (x_t - ((1 - alpha_t)/sqrt(1 - bar_alpha_t)) eps_theta(x_t,t)).

The same reparameterization turns the variational training bound, after dropping its per-timestep weighting, into plain noise-prediction MSE:

  L = E || eps - eps_theta(sqrt(bar_alpha_t) x_0 + sqrt(1 - bar_alpha_t) eps, t) ||^2.

This is the loss shape I wanted: sample a clean chunk, sample noise, sample a timestep, form x_t in one shot, predict the noise, take MSE. There is a real choice here — I could instead have the network predict the posterior mean directly. The reported evidence is that predicting the mean is competitive only with the weighted variational objective, while epsilon prediction is what makes the simplified unweighted objective work well; since the simple MSE is exactly the regression-flavored loss I am after, epsilon prediction is the parameterization that earns it. So I predict eps.

Sampling can start from the ancestral DDPM reverse step,

  x_{t-1} = (1/sqrt(alpha_t)) (x_t - ((1 - alpha_t)/sqrt(1 - bar_alpha_t)) eps_theta(x_t,t)) + sigma_t z,

but in its plain form this costs one model pass per training timestep. For a 7B trunk, that is a serious latency cost — every reverse step is a full multimodal forward pass, and the demonstrations use a long forward chain. I want to denoise on a sparse subsequence of timesteps without retraining. The lever is that the training objective only fixes the marginals q(x_t | x_0), not the exact Markovian joint, so a non-Markovian process with the same marginals is trained by the same loss and can take larger jumps. The DDIM step first re-estimates the clean chunk from the current state and the predicted noise,

  xhat_0 = (x_t - sqrt(1 - bar_alpha_t) eps_theta(x_t,t)) / sqrt(bar_alpha_t),

then for the next selected (lower) timestep s re-noises that estimate to level s,

  x_s = sqrt(bar_alpha_s) xhat_0 + sqrt(1 - bar_alpha_s - sigma_t^2) eps_theta(x_t,t) + sigma_t z.

I should check this actually does the bookkeeping I think it does, because it is easy to write a step that quietly drifts off the marginal. Take bar_alpha_t = 0.3 and a target bar_alpha_s = 0.6 (so s < t, less noise), x_0 = 1.3, eps = 0.7, and feed the model its own exact noise so eps_theta = eps. Then x_t = sqrt(0.3)·1.3 + sqrt(0.7)·0.7. Inverting, xhat_0 = (x_t - sqrt(0.7)·0.7)/sqrt(0.3) = 1.3 exactly — the clean estimate is recovered. With sigma_t = 0 the step gives x_s = sqrt(0.6)·1.3 + sqrt(0.4)·0.7 = 1.449695, and the genuine forward marginal at level s, sqrt(0.6)·1.3 + sqrt(0.4)·0.7, is also 1.449695. They coincide, so the deterministic step lands a perfect denoiser exactly back on the forward marginal: skipping timesteps is consistent, not approximate, when the noise prediction is right. With sigma_t = 0 the trajectory is also deterministic once the starting noise is fixed, which I want for reproducible control. In this scaffold I still use the fixed 50-step inference setting, but keeping the DDIM form is what makes the latency-quality tradeoff a free knob rather than a retraining cost.

The noise schedule also matters, and it is a smaller decision but a real one. A linear beta ramp drives bar_alpha_t close to zero too early, leaving the late steps with almost no signal left to denoise. Defining bar_alpha_t directly through a cosine,

  bar_alpha_t = f(t)/f(0),   f(t) = cos^2(((t/T + s)/(1 + s)) * pi/2),

keeps it changing slowly near the endpoints and more steadily in the middle, so every step carries some signal. The improved-DDPM line motivates this, and the control-data denoising work reports the square-cosine variant as best for action chunks, which is what the Diffusers squaredcos_cap_v2 schedule implements; that is the one I take.

Now the harder part: attaching this denoiser to the VLA scaffold. A from-scratch diffusion policy keeps a separate noise-prediction network and conditions it on the observation with FiLM or cross-attention. Here the observation is already fused inside the 7B trunk, and the only handle I have is the action-slot hidden states — there is no side network and no separate vision stack to condition. So the trunk itself has to be the conditioning network: I feed the current noisy action chunk and the diffusion timestep into the trunk, let bidirectional attention mix them with vision, language, and proprioception, and read the noise prediction off the resulting action-slot hidden states. The trunk re-reads the noisy actions against the fused context at every diffusion step.

How do I inject the noisy chunk? It has shape (B, K, 7), and the action layout already gives me one token slot per action dimension — K·7 slots total. The natural move is to flatten the chunk to K·7 scalars, give each a singleton feature dimension, and project each scalar from R^1 to the LLM hidden size, so the noisy-action projector is per scalar, not per 7-D action; that is what preserves the one-slot-per-action-dimension layout the trunk already expects. The timestep becomes one sinusoidal embedding token so the trunk knows the current noise level.

There is one layout claim I want to be sure of before I commit the head, because it is exactly the kind of thing that silently transposes. After the trunk, I get (B, K·7, d) action-slot states (train: pulled out by the current/next-action masks; eval: the contiguous K·7 span after the prompt). I plan to reshape to (B, K, 7d) and run a small MLP that maps each timestep's 7d to its 7-D noise. For that to be right, row k of the reshaped tensor must hold exactly the seven hidden states of timestep k, in dimension order 0..6 — not a mix across timesteps. Let me trace it on a tiny case, K=3, D=7, d=2, tagging each of the K·7 states with its (timestep, dim) before the reshape. Reshaping (1, 21, 2) to (1, 3, 14) and unpacking each row back to (7, 2): row 0 contains dims [0..6] all tagged timestep 0, row 1 contains dims [0..6] all tagged timestep 1, row 2 likewise for timestep 2. The grouping is exactly per-timestep, so a pre-LN MLP-residual block over the 7d group predicts that one timestep's 7-D noise, and the loss is MSE against the actual eps.

One training forward then falls out: sample t and eps, form x_t with the one-shot marginal, project the K·7 noisy scalars into action-token embeddings, encode t as one token, call the trunk with both, extract the action slots, group them per timestep, predict the noise, and take MSE. At inference I draw a normalized chunk from N(0,I), set the DDIM timesteps, and loop: encode the current timestep, project the current noisy chunk, run the trunk, extract and group the action states, predict the noise, and call the scheduler step to get the next, less-noisy chunk. The cost is one trunk pass per selected diffusion step — that is the price for representing a distribution instead of a single action vector, and the DDIM step count is the dial that sets it.

So the head that comes out of this is a per-scalar noisy-action projector, a sinusoidal timestep token, a DDIM scheduler on the squared-cosine schedule, and an MLP-residual epsilon predictor over the per-timestep-grouped action-slot hidden states, trained by one-shot forward noising and MSE on epsilon, and run as a reverse DDIM rollout at prediction time. The code goes in the scaffold slot; this reasoning is the record of why each piece is forced by the multimodality of the demonstrations and by the trunk's action-token layout.
