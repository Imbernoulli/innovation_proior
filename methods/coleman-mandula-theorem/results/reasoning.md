Let me start from what is actually bothering people, which is the proton and the neutron. They sit in an isospin doublet, partners under an internal $SU(2)$ that rotates one into the other. And because that internal symmetry commutes with the four-momentum operator — $[P_\mu, I] = 0$ — the two of them are forced to satisfy $P_\mu P^\mu|p\rangle = m^2|p\rangle$ and $P_\mu P^\mu|n\rangle = m^2|n\rangle$ with the *same* $m$. The symmetry that relates them also pins them to the same mass. But they don't have the same mass. So somebody looks at that and thinks: what if the internal generator did *not* commute with $P_\mu$? Suppose instead $[P_\mu, I_+] = c\, I_+$. Then act $P^2$ on the states and push the commutator through, and you get something like $m_p^2 = m_n^2 - c^2$. The mass splitting, which the commuting symmetry *forbids*, would instead be *explained* — you'd read $c$ off the data. That is genuinely seductive. A symmetry whose generators mix with the Poincaré generators would turn the multiplet structure into a predictor of mass differences instead of a straitjacket forcing degeneracy.

And it's not just isospin. $SU(3)$-flavour organizes the hadrons beautifully, octets and decuplets, and then Gürsey and Radicati, and Sakita, glue the spin $SU(2)$ onto the flavour $SU(3)$ and get $SU(6)$, with the **6** being three flavours times two spins. Suddenly the pseudoscalar and vector mesons are one **35**, and the spin-$\tfrac12$ octet and the spin-$\tfrac32$ decuplet are one **56**. That's spectacular, and notice what it does: it puts particles of *different spin* in one multiplet. Spin is a spacetime quantum number — it's about how a state transforms under rotations. So $SU(6)$ is already mixing an internal symmetry with a spacetime one. The catch is that the rotations it uses are rest-frame, non-relativistic rotations, $SU(2)$, not the full Lorentz group. So the obvious dream is: make it relativistic. Build a group containing the whole Lorentz group, or the whole Poincaré group, with the flavour and spin fused, so that the spin-flavour multiplets are *relativistic* multiplets. People try this many times. It keeps failing. The relativistic $SU(6)$ schemes give absurd consequences — the spectra and the scattering they predict are untenable. So the failures are piling up and I want to know whether they're accidents of clumsy construction or whether there's a wall.

Now there are already theorems claiming there's a wall, and I should look at them honestly, because if they were airtight I wouldn't need to do anything. McGlinn's is the cleanest. Take the Poincaré algebra $L$, split it into the homogeneous Lorentz part $M$ and the translation part $P$. Take a semisimple internal algebra $I$. Assume the combined algebra $T$ is spanned by the basis of $L$ together with the basis of $I$ — nothing else — and assume the internal part is Lorentz-invariant, $[I,M]=0$. Then he shows $[I,P]=0$, so $T = L\times I$, a trivial direct product. Fine. But look at the assumptions. He assumed from the start that every generator is either a Poincaré generator or an internal generator — that's what "the basis of $T$ is the basis of $L$ union the basis of $I$" *means*. He has forbidden, by hypothesis, exactly the interesting objects: generators that are neither, that genuinely interpolate between spacetime and internal. He also put in $[I,M]=0$ by hand. So McGlinn proves you can't mix them if you assume up front that there's nothing to mix. That's not a wall, that's a definition.

O'Raifeartaigh does much better. He throws out McGlinn's basis assumption and asks: embed the Poincaré algebra into *any* finite-order Lie algebra $T$, and let the structure decide. He uses the Levi decomposition — every finite-dimensional Lie algebra is a semidirect sum of its radical $S$ (the maximal solvable ideal) and a semisimple Levi factor $G$. Since the translations $P$ are abelian, hence solvable, and the Lorentz part $M$ is semisimple, the way $P$ can sit relative to the radical splits into a small number of cases. He works through them. In every case $M\cap S = 0$, and the only physically reasonable case forces $T = L\times I$ again. The sharp output: *all particles in a multiplet of the combined group have the same mass*. So the mass-splitting dream is dead — within his framework.

But within his framework. Here's where I get uneasy. He *needs* finite order, because the Levi decomposition needs it. So if somebody writes down an infinite-parameter symmetry group — and people have, Fronsdal and others with their infinite supermultiplets spreading particles all along the mass spectrum — O'Raifeartaigh's theorem simply doesn't apply. The question reopens. And worse, his whole argument lives on the one-particle spectrum: it's about how the algebra represents on single-particle states. It never asks what happens when two particles actually collide. Michel says something in the same spirit that sticks with me — that the Poincaré group is really the quotient $E/S$ of the full invariance group by the internal group, and that all this group-theoretic bookkeeping "does not give any help to the very worthwhile purpose of understanding the nature of broken symmetry." These are math-framework theorems. They make assumptions about whether translations are a normal subgroup, about finite-dimensionality, about hermiticity of solvable representations — assumptions with no physical content — and they pay for those assumptions by being escapable.

So I'm stuck if I keep playing this game. Every time I prove an impossibility inside abstract group theory, someone builds a bigger or weirder group that dodges the hypotheses. I need a different lever.

Here's the thing I keep coming back to. The groups that *escaped* O'Raifeartaigh — the ones that survive on the one-particle level — what do they actually predict for scattering? Jordan looked, and the answer is that they give trivial scattering: amplitudes that vanish except in the forward and backward directions. The would-be symmetries that survive group theory die on contact with real scattering. That's the clue. The thing the math-framework theorems can't see is precisely the thing that kills the loopholes. So instead of asking "what abstract group can contain the Poincaré group," I should ask: **what symmetry can commute with an honest, non-trivial scattering matrix?** Make the S-matrix itself the framework. Then I don't have to assume anything about the group's normality or its dimension — I let scattering be the judge. And it automatically uses multiparticle information, which is exactly what was missing.

What does a symmetry of the S-matrix even mean? A unitary $U$ that turns one-particle states into one-particle states, that acts on many-particle states as if they were tensor products — so its infinitesimal generators are *additive*, the generator on a two-particle state is (generator on particle 1) plus (generator on particle 2) — and that commutes with $S$. An internal symmetry is one whose generators also commute with the whole Poincaré algebra: it touches only the particle-type and spin labels, never the momentum.

Let me get a feel for why scattering should be so constraining before I do anything careful. Suppose, on top of energy-momentum, there's some other conserved charge that carries Lorentz indices — say a symmetric traceless two-index tensor $Q_{\mu\nu}$, conserved, and genuinely a new thing, not just $J_{\mu\nu}$ or $\eta_{\mu\nu}$ times something. By Lorentz covariance, its expectation value on a one-particle state of momentum $p$ has to be built from $p$ alone, and being symmetric and traceless it must be
$$\langle p | Q_{\mu\nu} | p\rangle \propto p_\mu p_\nu - \tfrac14 \eta_{\mu\nu} p^2 .$$
It's additive, so on a two-particle state $|p_1, p_2\rangle$ it gives $\langle Q_{\mu\nu}\rangle = (p_1)_\mu(p_1)_\nu + (p_2)_\mu(p_2)_\nu$ (dropping the trace pieces, which are fixed by the masses). Now do elastic scattering $p_1, p_2 \to q_1, q_2$. Conservation of $Q$ says
$$(p_1)_\mu(p_1)_\nu + (p_2)_\mu(p_2)_\nu = (q_1)_\mu(q_1)_\nu + (q_2)_\mu(q_2)_\nu,$$
and I already have, from ordinary energy-momentum conservation, $p_1 + p_2 = q_1 + q_2$. Use the four-momentum law to write $q_1 = p_1 + a$, $q_2 = p_2 - a$ for some shift $a_\mu$ — that's the most general solution of the linear conservation law. Plug into the $Q$ law:
$$(p_1+a)_\mu(p_1+a)_\nu + (p_2-a)_\mu(p_2-a)_\nu = (p_1)_\mu(p_1)_\nu + (p_2)_\mu(p_2)_\nu.$$
Expand. The quadratic-in-$p$ pieces cancel. What's left is
$$a_\mu (p_1 - p_2)_\nu + a_\nu (p_1 - p_2)_\mu + 2 a_\mu a_\nu = 0 .$$
For this to hold at generic incoming momenta $p_1, p_2$, the only solution is $a_\mu = 0$. That means $q_1 = p_1$, $q_2 = p_2$: nothing scattered, the particles passed straight through. A non-trivial conserved tensor charge would allow *only* forward scattering. The same goes, even more rigidly, for any higher-rank conserved tensor. So if there's genuine scattering — if amplitudes are nonzero at generic angles — there can be no such extra Lorentz-tensor charge.

That's the whole physics in a paragraph, and I can already see why analyticity is going to matter. The extra charge doesn't strictly say "no scattering"; it says scattering is allowed only at the discrete momentum configurations that solve the over-determined conservation laws — isolated angles. But a scattering amplitude that is an analytic function of the scattering angle and vanishes except at isolated points must vanish identically. So "extra conserved tensor charge" plus "analyticity" equals "no scattering," which contradicts the premise that scattering occurs. That's the lever. It needs three ingredients I should name as assumptions: the symmetry generators are additive on multiparticle states (built into being a symmetry of the S-matrix); the amplitudes are analytic in $s$ and $t$ near the physical region (weak elastic analyticity — the thing that turns "isolated angles" into "nowhere"); and scattering actually occurs at almost all energies (non-triviality — without it, with $S=1$, *every* unitary operator is a symmetry and the theorem is vacuous). And it tells me what I expect the answer to be: the only conserved generators carrying Lorentz indices are $P_\mu$ and $J_{\mu\nu}$ themselves; everything else commutes with the Lorentz generators, i.e. is a Lorentz scalar — an internal symmetry. The algebra splits as Poincaré $\oplus$ internal.

Now I have to make that rigorous, and the cartoon hides a lot. I assumed $\langle p|Q|p\rangle \propto p_\mu p_\nu$; in general a generator can do much more than multiply by a function of $p$ — it could shuffle the spin and particle-type indices, it could be an integral operator connecting different momenta, it could be a high-order differential operator in $p$. I need to handle all of that. Let me set up the machinery properly.

States: $\mathcal H = \bigoplus_n \mathcal H^{(n)}$ with $\mathcal H^{(n)} \cong (\mathcal H^{(1)})^{\otimes n}$. One-particle states $|\alpha,\lambda,p\rangle$ on mass hyperboloids. The S-matrix $S = 1 - i(2\pi)^4\delta^4(P_\mu - P'_\mu) T$. A symmetry generator $A$ is, on one-particle states, an integral operator with a kernel,
$$A|\alpha,\lambda,p\rangle = \sum_{\alpha'\lambda'}\int d^4p'\; A_{\alpha'\lambda',\alpha\lambda}(p',p)\,|\alpha',\lambda',p'\rangle,$$
the kernel being a distribution in momentum — and I'll have to lean on it being a distribution, that's the one ugly technical assumption I can't yet avoid, because to manipulate the infinitesimal generators of a possibly infinite-parameter group I need them to be honest operators with well-defined kernels. I don't like it; I believe it's removable with more careful analysis; but I'll keep it. I'll also need particle-finiteness: below any mass there are only finitely many particle types. That's doing real work in two ways — it makes the one-particle representation matrices finite-dimensional Hermitian matrices, and it confines the momentum supports to a countable set of mass hyperboloids. And it's what kills the infinite-supermultiplet escape route from O'Raifeartaigh in one stroke.

Let me organize the proof. There are two kinds of generators: those that commute with $P_\mu$ — call that set $\mathfrak B$ — and those that don't — call that $\mathfrak A$. The internal symmetries live in $\mathfrak B$ (they commute with all of Poincaré). My job is to show $\mathfrak B$ consists of internal generators plus nothing dangerous, and that $\mathfrak A$ consists of nothing but Lorentz transformations plus internal pieces. If I can do both, then every symmetry generator is a Poincaré generator plus an internal one, and the algebra is $\mathcal P \oplus \mathfrak B_{\rm int}$.

Start with $\mathfrak B$. A generator $B_\alpha$ commuting with $P_\mu$ can't move momentum, so on a one-particle state it just acts as a matrix on the discrete indices, possibly depending on $p$:
$$B_\alpha|p,m\rangle = (b_\alpha(p))_{m'm}|p,m'\rangle,$$
where $m$ runs over spin and particle type. By additivity, on a two-particle state,
$$B_\alpha|p,m;q,n\rangle = (b_\alpha(p))_{m'm}\delta_{n'n}\,|p,m';q,n'\rangle + \delta_{m'm}(b_\alpha(q))_{n'n}\,|p,m';q,n'\rangle,$$
i.e. $b_\alpha(p,q) = b_\alpha(p)\otimes 1 + 1\otimes b_\alpha(q)$. The $B_\alpha$ close into a Lie algebra, $[B_\alpha,B_\beta] = iC^\gamma_{\alpha\beta}B_\gamma$, and so do the matrices: $[b_\alpha(p), b_\beta(p)] = iC^\gamma_{\alpha\beta} b_\gamma(p)$. Now, the matrices $b_\alpha(p)$ are finite (particle-finiteness) and Hermitian (because the symmetry is unitary, its generators are Hermitian). And there's a theorem about exactly that situation: any Lie algebra of finite Hermitian matrices is a direct sum of a compact semisimple Lie algebra and some number of $u(1)$'s. That's enormously useful — it would tell me the internal symmetry is *compact*, which is what I want (compactness is what gives charge quantization and finite multiplets).

But wait — that theorem applies to the matrices $b_\alpha(p)$, not to the abstract algebra $\mathfrak B$ of the $B_\alpha$. The map $B_\alpha \mapsto b_\alpha(p)$ is a *homomorphism* — they share the same structure constants — but to push the structure of $b$ back to $\mathfrak B$ I need it to be an *isomorphism*, i.e. injective. The danger is degeneracy: maybe some combination $c^\alpha B_\alpha$ is nonzero as an abstract generator but $c^\alpha b_\alpha(p) = 0$ at the particular momentum $p$. I need: if $c^\alpha b_\alpha(p)=0$ for some $p$, then $c^\alpha b_\alpha(k) = 0$ for *all* on-shell $k$, hence $c^\alpha B_\alpha = 0$. So the kernel of the map must be trivial. This is where scattering enters.

Take a two-particle elastic process $p,q\to p',q'$, all on shell, $p+q = p'+q'$. The generator commutes with $S$, so sandwiching $[B_\alpha,S]=0$ between $\langle p',m';q',n'|$ and $|p,m;q,n\rangle$ and grinding through the kinematics gives
$$b_\alpha(p',q')\, M(p',q';p,q) = M(p',q';p,q)\, b_\alpha(p,q),$$
where $M$ is the (matrix in the discrete indices) scattering amplitude. Now non-triviality: scattering occurs at almost all energies, so $M$ is non-singular at almost all momenta (assumptions 3 and 4 together — analyticity says $M$ is well behaved and non-zero off isolated points). Therefore $M$ is invertible and
$$b_\alpha(p',q') = M\, b_\alpha(p,q)\, M^{-1}.$$
The two-particle representations at different (total-momentum-conserving) momenta are related by a *similarity transformation*. That's a beautiful fact: similarity transformations preserve everything algebraic. In particular, if $c^\alpha b_\alpha(p,q) = 0$ then $c^\alpha b_\alpha(p',q') = M\,(c^\alpha b_\alpha(p,q))\,M^{-1} = 0$. Linear dependence at one pair of momenta propagates to every pair connected by scattering.

That's almost what I want, but not quite, because I need it on *one-particle* reps $b_\alpha(p)$, not on the two-particle $b_\alpha(p,q)$. From $c^\alpha b_\alpha(p',q') = 0$ and $b_\alpha(p',q') = b_\alpha(p')\otimes 1 + 1\otimes b_\alpha(q')$, the structure forces
$$c^\alpha b_\alpha(p')\otimes 1 = -\,1\otimes c^\alpha b_\alpha(q'),$$
and an operator of the form (matrix on slot 1)$\otimes 1$ equal to $-1\otimes$(matrix on slot 2) can only happen if each side is a multiple of the identity — $c^\alpha b_\alpha(p')$ and $c^\alpha b_\alpha(q')$ are both proportional to the identity matrix, with opposite constants. Hmm. Proportional to the identity is not zero. So I haven't shown injectivity yet — there's a residual piece proportional to the identity that the argument can't kill. I've hit a wall.

What's the identity piece? It's the trace. Let me compute it. Take the trace of $b_\alpha(p,q)$ over the two-particle discrete indices:
$$\mathrm{Tr}\, b_\alpha(p,q) = \mathrm{Tr}\big(b_\alpha(p)\otimes 1\big) + \mathrm{Tr}\big(1\otimes b_\alpha(q)\big) = N(m_q)\,\mathrm{tr}\,b_\alpha(p) + N(m_p)\,\mathrm{tr}\,b_\alpha(q),$$
where $N(m)$ is the multiplicity of states (spins times types) at mass $m$ — finite, by particle-finiteness. And trace is invariant under the similarity transformation, $\mathrm{Tr}\,b_\alpha(p',q') = \mathrm{Tr}\,b_\alpha(p,q)$. So for any on-shell $p,q,p',q'$ with $p+q=p'+q'$,
$$\frac{\mathrm{tr}\,b_\alpha(p)}{N(m_p)} + \frac{\mathrm{tr}\,b_\alpha(q)}{N(m_q)} = \frac{\mathrm{tr}\,b_\alpha(p')}{N(m_{p'})} + \frac{\mathrm{tr}\,b_\alpha(q')}{N(m_{q'})} .$$
A quantity, additive over the two particles, conserved in every scattering — that means $\mathrm{tr}\,b_\alpha(p)/N(m_p)$ is a conserved additive charge, and the only such things linear and Lorentz-covariant are linear in the momentum:
$$\frac{\mathrm{tr}\,b_\alpha(p)}{N(m_p)} = a_\alpha^\mu\, p_\mu .$$
(A constant term is ruled out by considering processes with different particle numbers on the two sides — the constant wouldn't balance.) So the offending identity piece, the trace, is just a four-momentum charge in disguise: it's $a_\alpha^\mu P_\mu$, a translation, not an internal thing at all.

So I subtract it off. Define
$$B^\#_\alpha \equiv B_\alpha - a_\alpha^\mu P_\mu .$$
This still commutes with $P_\mu$ (since $P_\mu$ commutes with itself), so it's still in $\mathfrak B$, and now its one-particle representation $b^\#_\alpha(p) = b_\alpha(p) - a_\alpha^\mu p_\mu$ is **traceless**. Notice this is exactly the cartoon from before showing up rigorously: the part of an extra "internal-looking" charge that's linear in $p$ is a translation; only the traceless remainder can be genuinely internal. Subtracting $a^\mu_\alpha P_\mu$ is also the right thing for excluding $P_\mu$ itself from the would-be internal algebra — $P_\mu$ has trace $\propto p_\mu$, so it gets mapped to zero by this subtraction and is correctly pushed out of $\mathfrak B^\#$.

There's a consistency check I should do. The trace of a commutator of finite matrices is zero. So $0 = \mathrm{tr}[b^\#_\alpha, b^\#_\beta] = iC^\gamma_{\alpha\beta}\,\mathrm{tr}\,b^\#_\gamma + \,(\text{the }a\text{-pieces})$. Working it out, this forces $C^\gamma_{\alpha\beta}\,a^\mu_\gamma = 0$, which is just the statement that the $a^\mu$ piece is central — consistent with it being the translation $P_\mu$, which commutes with everything in $\mathfrak B$. Good. And the $B^\#_\alpha$ are still symmetry generators, so the whole similarity-transform machinery runs again, now for traceless reps: $c^\alpha b^\#_\alpha(p,q)=0 \Rightarrow c^\alpha b^\#_\alpha(p',q')=0 \Rightarrow c^\alpha b^\#_\alpha(p') \propto 1$ and traceless $\Rightarrow c^\alpha b^\#_\alpha(p') = 0$. Now the residual identity piece *is* killed, because a traceless multiple of the identity is zero. So linear dependence at one $(p,q)$ forces it at $p'$ and $q'$ separately.

But that's still only for the $p',q'$ reachable by a single elastic collision from $p,q$ — same mass shells, momentum-conserving. I need it for *all* on-shell momenta $k$, otherwise the map could still be degenerate somewhere I haven't reached. Here's the trick that does it, and it's the cleverest kinematic step in the whole argument. Suppose $c^\alpha b^\#_\alpha(p,q) = 0$. I've shown then $c^\alpha b^\#_\alpha(p) = c^\alpha b^\#_\alpha(q) = c^\alpha b^\#_\alpha(p') = c^\alpha b^\#_\alpha(q') = 0$ for the scattering $p,q\to p',q'$. In particular it vanishes at $p$ and at $q'$. So now consider a *different* scattering, $p, q' \to k, (p + q' - k)$, with total momentum $p + q'$ — a different total momentum than $p+q$! Since $c^\alpha b^\#_\alpha(p) = c^\alpha b^\#_\alpha(q') = 0$, I have $c^\alpha b^\#_\alpha(p,q') = 0$, and by the similarity transform along *this* collision, $c^\alpha b^\#_\alpha(k) = 0$ for the outgoing momentum $k$. I've moved from total momentum $p+q$ to total momentum $p+q'$, and the outgoing $k$ is free. Let me count the freedom. Starting from $p,q$ on their mass shells, I pick $q'$; the elastic conditions $m_p^2 = (p+q-q')^2$ and $m_q^2 = q'^2$ remove two of its degrees of freedom. Then in $p,q'\to k,(p+q'-k)$ I need $m_p^2 = k^2$, removing one degree of freedom from $k$, and $m_q^2 = (p+q'-k)^2$ — but I still have leftover freedom in $q'$ to satisfy that for any $k$ I like. So the three-vector $\mathbf k$ is completely free: I've gotten rid of momentum conservation and shown $c^\alpha b^\#_\alpha(k) = 0$ for almost all on-shell $k$ on the mass shell of $p$.

"Almost all" because the S-matrix is invertible only off isolated values. And only on the same mass shell as $p$, since I've been doing elastic scattering. Let me close both gaps. Different mass shell: scatter $p$ against something to produce a particle of any other mass in the spectrum; particle-finiteness guarantees there are finitely many shells and they're all reachable, and the analyticity-plus-scattering argument propagates the vanishing across. The isolated bad momenta: suppose at some special $k_*$ we had $c^\alpha b^\#_\alpha(k_*)\neq 0$. Then the scattering $k_*, k \to k', k''$ would be *forbidden* by the symmetry generated by $c^\alpha B^\#_\alpha$ for almost all $k, k', k''$ — because almost all $b^\#_\alpha(k',k'')$ are similarity-related to $b^\#_\alpha(p,q)$ where the combination already vanishes, so the symmetry would force the amplitude to zero. But amplitudes are analytic; an amplitude can't drop to zero on a whole region while being nonzero near $k_*$ in any analytic way. Contradiction with "scattering occurs." So $c^\alpha b^\#_\alpha(k_*) = 0$ too. Hence $c^\alpha b^\#_\alpha(k) = 0$ for *all* $k$, which means $c^\alpha B^\#_\alpha = 0$: the map $B^\#_\alpha \mapsto b^\#_\alpha$ is injective. The homomorphism is an isomorphism.

That isomorphism pays off immediately. First, since the independent $b^\#_\alpha(p,q)$ are matrices on a space of dimension $N(m_p)N(m_q)$, there are only finitely many of them — so $\mathfrak B^\#$ is **finite-dimensional**, which I never had to assume; the S-matrix forced it, killing the infinite-parameter loophole for the part of the algebra commuting with $P$. Second, the finite-Hermitian-matrix theorem now applies to $\mathfrak B^\#$ itself: it decomposes as a compact semisimple Lie algebra direct-sum some $u(1)$'s.

Now I have to show every piece of $\mathfrak B^\#$ commutes not just with $P_\mu$ (by construction) but with the Lorentz generators $J_{\mu\nu}$ too — then it commutes with all of Poincaré and is honestly internal. Do the $u(1)$'s first. Take a $u(1)$ generator $B^\#_i$ and a Lorentz generator $J$. From $[P_\mu, B^\#_i]=0$ and the Poincaré relation $[J,P_\mu] \sim P$, the Jacobi identity gives
$$[P_\mu,[J,B^\#_i]] + [J,[B^\#_i,P_\mu]] + [B^\#_i,[P_\mu,J]] = 0,$$
and the middle term vanishes ($B^\#_i$ commutes with $P$) and the last is $[B^\#_i, \text{linear combo of } P] = 0$, so $[P_\mu,[J,B^\#_i]]=0$. Thus $[J,B^\#_i]$ also commutes with $P_\mu$, so it lies in $\mathfrak B^\#$ and is a linear combination of the $B^\#$'s. But $B^\#_i$ is a $u(1)$ piece — it commutes with everything in $\mathfrak B^\#$. So $[B^\#_i,[J,B^\#_i]] = [B^\#_i, \sum_\alpha (\cdots)B^\#_\alpha] = 0$. Now take the expectation of this double commutator in a two-particle state, using that $J$ (say a rotation about the axis along $\mathbf p$) is diagonal with eigenvalue $\sigma(m,n)$:
$$0 = \langle p,m;q,n|[B^\#_i,[J,B^\#_i]]|p,m;q,n\rangle = 2\sum_{m'n'}\big(\sigma(m',n') - \sigma(m,n)\big)\,\big|(b^\#_i(p,q))_{m'n',mn}\big|^2 .$$
Every term is non-negative, so each must vanish: whenever $\sigma(m',n')\neq \sigma(m,n)$, the matrix element $(b^\#_i(p,q))_{m'n',mn} = 0$. That says $b^\#_i$ doesn't connect states of different $J$-eigenvalue — it commutes with $J$. By the isomorphism, $[B^\#_i, J] = 0$. Choosing the rotation axis and then the boosts in turn, $B^\#_i$ commutes with all of the Lorentz group. The $u(1)$'s are internal.

The semisimple part needs a slightly different argument, and it's the one I find most telling. Take $B_a$ in the compact semisimple piece. Conjugate by a Lorentz transformation: $U(\Lambda)B_a U(\Lambda)^{-1}$. This still commutes with $P_\mu$ (because conjugating the relation $[B_a,P]=0$ by $U(\Lambda)$ just rotates $P$, and $B_a$ commutes with all components), so it lies in $\mathfrak B^\#$, and since the $B_a$ span the relevant subalgebra,
$$U(\Lambda)\,B_a\,U(\Lambda)^{-1} = D(\Lambda)_a{}^b\, B_b ,$$
where $D(\Lambda)$ is some finite-dimensional representation of the Lorentz group $SO(1,3)$. But the $B_a$ are Hermitian, so $D(\Lambda)$ must be a *unitary* finite-dimensional representation of the Lorentz group. The Lorentz group is *non-compact*. A non-compact group has no non-trivial finite-dimensional unitary representations — the only one is trivial. So $D(\Lambda) = 1$, meaning $U(\Lambda)B_a U(\Lambda)^{-1} = B_a$: the semisimple generators commute with all Lorentz transformations too. This is, in the end, the crisp reason spacetime cannot rotate internal charges: a Lorentz-covariant family of Hermitian charges would be a unitary finite-dimensional Lorentz multiplet, and there are none but the singlet. So the whole of $\mathfrak B^\#$ — semisimple and abelian — commutes with $P_\mu$ and with $J_{\mu\nu}$, hence with all of Poincaré. Every generator that commutes with $P$ is, after stripping its $P$-piece, an internal symmetry, and the internal algebra is compact.

Now $\mathfrak A$ — the generators that *don't* commute with $P_\mu$. I have to show there's nothing here but the Lorentz generators (plus internal admixtures). A generator $A_\alpha$ in $\mathfrak A$ acts on a one-particle state through its kernel,
$$A_\alpha|p,n\rangle = \sum_{n'}\int d^4p'\;(A_\alpha(p',p))_{n'n}\,|p',n'\rangle,$$
and the kernel must vanish unless both $p$ and $p'$ are on the mass shell — a symmetry can't take a physical state off shell. First I'll show the kernel is supported only on $p' = p$. Suppose not: suppose $A_\alpha(p, p+\Delta) \neq 0$ for some $\Delta \neq 0$, both on shell. Smear $A_\alpha$ with a function: define
$$A_\alpha^f = \int d^4x\; e^{iP\cdot x}\,A_\alpha\,e^{-iP\cdot x}\, f(x),$$
which is still a symmetry generator for any $f$. Acting on a one-particle state, the $e^{\pm iP\cdot x}$ pick up phases and the $x$-integral Fourier-transforms $f$:
$$A_\alpha^f|p,n\rangle = \sum_{n'}\int d^4p'\;\tilde f(p'-p)\,(A_\alpha(p',p))_{n'n}\,|p',n'\rangle .$$
Choose $\tilde f$ supported in a tiny region around $\Delta$. Then $A_\alpha^f$ shifts momentum by (about) $\Delta$. Now take a generic scattering $p_1, q_1 \to p_2, q_2$. For generic $q_1, p_2, q_2$, the shifted momenta $q_1+\Delta$, $p_2+\Delta$, $q_2+\Delta$ all land *off* the mass shell, so $A_\alpha^f$ annihilates the states $|q_1\rangle, |p_2\rangle, |q_2\rangle$ — the kernel vanishes off shell. But $A_\alpha^f|p_1\rangle = \tilde f(\Delta)\,A_\alpha(p_1+\Delta,p_1)|p_1+\Delta\rangle \neq 0$. So $A_\alpha^f$ kills three of the four legs but not the first. A symmetry must commute with $S$; the amplitude $\langle p_2,q_2|[A_\alpha^f, S]|p_1,q_1\rangle = 0$ then forces the scattering $p_1,q_1\to p_2,q_2$ to vanish — because $A^f$ acting on the out-state gives zero while on the in-state it gives a state that can't scatter into the (annihilated) out-configuration. This forbids generic scattering, contradicting "scattering occurs." So the assumption fails: the kernel can't connect different on-shell momenta. $A_\alpha(p',p)$ is supported at $p' = p$, proportional to $\delta^4(p'-p)$ and its derivatives.

This is the S-matrix generalization of O'Raifeartaigh's old result that the symmetry can't connect different mass hyperboloids — but now it's sharper, $p'=p$ exactly, and it came from scattering, not from group theory. A distribution supported at a single point is a finite sum of derivatives of the delta function. So
$$(A_\alpha(p',p))_{n'n} = \sum_{i=0}^{D_\alpha} \big(a^{(i)}_\alpha(p)\big)_{n'n}^{\mu_1\cdots\mu_i}\,\frac{\partial^i}{\partial p'^{\mu_1}\cdots\partial p'^{\mu_i}}\,\delta^4(p'-p),$$
with $D_\alpha$ finite — and *this* is where the ugly distribution assumption earns its keep: it's what guarantees finitely many derivatives. So $A_\alpha$ is at most a $D_\alpha$-th order differential operator in $p$, multiplying the discrete indices.

Now relate this $\mathfrak A$ to the $\mathfrak B$ I already understand. Commuting $A_\alpha$ with momenta knocks its derivative order down: commuting with $P_\mu$ once lowers the derivative degree by one and, more importantly, each commutator with $P$ kills one factor. Define
$$B_\alpha^{\mu_1\cdots\mu_{D_\alpha}} \equiv [P^{\mu_1},[P^{\mu_2},[\cdots[P^{\mu_{D_\alpha}}, A_\alpha]\cdots]]].$$
Take $D_\alpha$ commutators. I claim this lands in $\mathfrak B$ — it commutes with $P$. Check the matrix element of $[B_\alpha^{\mu_1\cdots\mu_{D}}, P^\nu]$ between momentum eigenstates: every commutator with $P$ pulls out a factor of $(p'-p)$, so the matrix element is proportional to $(p'-p)^{\mu}(p'-p)^{\mu_1}\cdots(p'-p)^{\mu_D}$ — that's $D+1$ factors of $(p'-p)$ — times derivatives of $\delta^4(p'-p)$. The delta function sets $p'=p$, and with $D+1$ prefactors of $(p'-p)$ but at most $D$ derivatives available to "use up" those factors, the whole thing vanishes when the delta sets $p'=p$. So $[B_\alpha^{\mu_1\cdots\mu_D}, P^\nu] = 0$: the $D$-fold commutator is in $\mathfrak B$, and I know everything about $\mathfrak B$. By the structure of $\mathfrak B$, its one-particle representation is an internal (traceless, momentum-independent) matrix plus a linear-in-$p$ translation piece:
$$b_\alpha^{\mu_1\cdots\mu_D}(p) = \big(b^\#_\alpha\big)^{\mu_1\cdots\mu_D} + a_\alpha^{\mu\,\mu_1\cdots\mu_D}\,p_\mu .$$

Two facts constrain the coefficients. First, $A_\alpha$ can't take states off the mass shell, so $[P_\mu P^\mu, A_\alpha] = 0$. For $D\geq 1$, commute this with the $P$'s: $[P^{\mu_1}P_{\mu_1}, [P^{\mu_2}\cdots]] = 2P_{\mu_1} B_\alpha^{\mu_1\cdots\mu_D} = 0$. So $p_{\mu_1}\big((b^\#_\alpha)^{\mu_1\cdots\mu_D} + a_\alpha^{\mu\mu_1\cdots\mu_D}p_\mu\big) = 0$ for every timelike on-shell $p$. Matching powers of $p$: the order-$p$ part gives $(b^\#_\alpha)^{\mu_1\cdots\mu_D} = 0$ — the internal piece vanishes for $D\geq 1$ — and the order-$p^2$ part gives
$$a_\alpha^{\mu\,\mu_1\mu_2\cdots\mu_D} = -\,a_\alpha^{\mu_1\,\mu\,\mu_2\cdots\mu_D},$$
antisymmetry in the *first two* indices. Second, by construction $B_\alpha^{\mu_1\cdots\mu_D}$ is the iterated commutator with $P$'s, so it is *symmetric* in all the upper indices $\mu_1,\ldots,\mu_D$ (the $P$'s commute among themselves), and hence $a_\alpha^{\mu\,\mu_1\cdots\mu_D}$ is symmetric in $\mu_1,\ldots,\mu_D$.

Now I have a tensor antisymmetric in its first two indices and symmetric in its last $D$. Go case by case on $D$.

If $D = 0$: then $A_\alpha = B_\alpha$, the kernel is just a delta function, so $A_\alpha$ commutes with $P_\mu$ — but that's $\mathfrak B$, not $\mathfrak A$. So nothing new.

If $D \geq 2$: I have antisymmetry in $(\mu,\mu_1)$ and symmetry in $(\mu_1,\ldots,\mu_D)$ with at least two indices in that symmetric block. Chase it around:
$$a^{\mu\mu_1\mu_2\cdots} = -a^{\mu_1\mu\mu_2\cdots} = -a^{\mu_1\mu_2\mu\cdots} = +a^{\mu_2\mu_1\mu\cdots} = +a^{\mu_2\mu\mu_1\cdots} = -a^{\mu\mu_2\mu_1\cdots} = -a^{\mu\mu_1\mu_2\cdots},$$
using antisymmetry of the first pair and symmetry of the symmetric block alternately. So $a = -a = 0$. The tensor vanishes, hence $B_\alpha^{\mu_1\cdots\mu_D} = 0$ for $D\geq 2$. There are no generators of derivative order two or higher. Good — that's the rigorous version of "no higher-rank tensor charges," matching the cartoon where the symmetric two-index $Q_{\mu\nu}$ was forced to be trivial.

If $D = 1$: this is the interesting survivor. Antisymmetry $a_\alpha^{\mu\mu_1} = -a_\alpha^{\mu_1\mu}$ — just an antisymmetric two-index constant. And $(b^\#_\alpha)^{\mu_1} = 0$, so the $D$-fold (here single) commutator is
$$[P^\mu, A_\alpha] = a_\alpha^{\mu\nu}P_\nu, \qquad a_\alpha^{\mu\nu} = -a_\alpha^{\nu\mu}.$$
Stare at the right-hand side. An operator whose commutator with $P^\mu$ gives an antisymmetric-tensor-times-$P$ — that is *exactly* the Poincaré commutator of a Lorentz generator. Recall $[P^\mu, J^{\rho\sigma}] = i(\eta^{\mu\sigma}P^\rho - \eta^{\mu\rho}P^\sigma)$. So $[P^\mu, -\tfrac{i}{2}a_\alpha^{\rho\sigma}J_{\rho\sigma}] = a_\alpha^{\mu\nu}P_\nu$ as well. Therefore $A_\alpha + \tfrac{i}{2}a_\alpha^{\rho\sigma}J_{\rho\sigma}$ commutes with $P^\mu$ — it's back in $\mathfrak B$, i.e. it's an internal generator $B_\alpha$. So
$$A_\alpha = -\tfrac{i}{2}\,a_\alpha^{\mu\nu}J_{\mu\nu} + B_\alpha .$$
Every generator that fails to commute with $P_\mu$ is a Lorentz transformation plus an internal symmetry. There is nothing in $\mathfrak A$ but the Lorentz generators (dressed, harmlessly, with internal pieces).

Putting the two halves together: every symmetry generator is a translation, a Lorentz transformation, or an internal symmetry — and the internal generators commute with all of $P_\mu$ and $J_{\mu\nu}$. The full symmetry algebra is the **direct sum**
$$\mathfrak g = \mathcal P \oplus \mathfrak B_{\rm int},$$
the Poincaré algebra and a compact internal algebra, with no cross terms. Exponentiating, the connected symmetry group is locally isomorphic to the direct product of the Poincaré group and an internal symmetry group. The mass-splitting dream and the relativistic-$SU(6)$ dream both die for the same reason: any generator that could relate different masses or different spins non-trivially would carry uncontracted Lorentz indices, would over-constrain elastic scattering, and analyticity would then force the scattering to vanish — contradicting the bare fact that particles scatter. Spacetime and internal symmetries can only combine trivially.

Let me note where the proof leans, because the leaning points are exactly the loopholes. It used massive particles crucially — the mass-shell relation $[P^2, A]=0$ and the matching of powers of $p$ that gave the antisymmetry for $D\geq 2$. For purely *massless* particles, $p^2 = 0$, and that matching collapses; the $D\geq 2$ generators are no longer forced to vanish. So a massless theory can carry extra generators that mix with Poincaré — the dilatation and the special conformal generators of the conformal group, which indeed do not commute with $P_\mu$. The proof correctly does *not* forbid conformal symmetry of massless theories. It used genuine angular scattering — in $1+1$ dimensions there are only forward and backward directions, so the scattering angle isn't an analytic variable and assumption 3 is empty; integrable models in two dimensions can and do have infinitely many higher conserved charges. And it used the S-matrix as the arena — symmetries that don't act on the S-matrix at all, like spontaneously broken symmetries or discrete symmetries, are simply outside its scope. Above all, every single step manipulated *commutators* and used that the charges are ordinary, bosonic, Hermitian operators forming a Lie algebra. The entire scaffolding — the additive action, the finite-Hermitian-matrix decomposition, the Lorentz-rep argument — is built on the Lie bracket. If one allowed charges that close under *anticommutators* instead, spinorial generators that are not part of a Lie algebra but of a graded algebra, none of these steps would even be statements. That is the one door the theorem leaves open.

Here is the causal chain, end to end. The mass-degeneracy of multiplets and the failures of relativistic spin-flavour unification posed the question: can spacetime and internal symmetries mix? The earlier group-theoretic no-go theorems answered "no" but only under artificial finite-parameter, one-particle hypotheses that loophole groups escaped — and those loophole groups gave no real scattering. So I moved the question into S-matrix theory, demanding the symmetry commute with a non-trivial, analytic scattering matrix. Additivity of the generators makes any extra Lorentz-tensor charge over-determine elastic kinematics; analyticity converts "scattering only at isolated angles" into "no scattering," contradicting the premise. Made rigorous: generators commuting with $P_\mu$ are, after subtracting their trace (a translation), traceless reps related across momenta by similarity transformations; a kinematic chaining of collisions makes the homomorphism to the one-particle matrices an isomorphism, forcing the internal algebra to be finite-dimensional, compact, and — because the Lorentz group has no non-trivial finite unitary representations — commuting with the Lorentz generators. Generators not commuting with $P_\mu$ have point-supported kernels, hence are finite-order differential operators in $p$; commuting them down with $P$'s, the mass-shell condition forces all derivative orders above one to vanish and the order-one piece to be antisymmetric, which is exactly a Lorentz generator. So the algebra is Poincaré $\oplus$ internal, a direct sum — and the only escape is to give up the Lie bracket itself.

$$\boxed{\;\mathfrak g \;=\; \mathcal P \,\oplus\, \mathfrak g_{\rm internal}, \qquad [\,\mathcal P,\ \mathfrak g_{\rm internal}\,] = 0,\quad \mathfrak g_{\rm internal}\ \text{compact}.\;}$$
