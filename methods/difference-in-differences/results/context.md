## Research Question

A policy or institutional change reaches one population at a known time while a related population remains untreated. The outcome is observed before and after the change, but the treated population's untreated post-period outcome is never observed. The problem is to recover that missing path well enough to learn whether the intervention changed the outcome.

## Observed Structure

The minimal evidence has two groups and two periods. One group is exposed only after the first observation. The other group is observed over the same calendar interval without exposure. The data therefore contain both cross-sectional variation across groups and longitudinal variation within groups.

## Available Comparisons

Two simple contrasts can be formed from this structure. One compares the treated and untreated groups in the post-period. The other compares the treated group before and after exposure. Each contrast combines the intervention with a nuisance component: the cross-sectional contrast also carries stable differences between the groups, and the before-after contrast also carries movement in the outcome over time.

## Historical Signals

Natural experiments often arise when administrative, geographic, or institutional boundaries change exposure for some units but not others. Snow's water-company evidence and later labor-market policy shocks both show the attraction of such settings: the researcher can observe units near the intervention boundary, verify exposure, and follow outcomes across the moment when only some units receive the change.

## Credibility Requirements

Whether a comparison group informs the treated group's counterfactual depends on substantive evidence about why treatment timing is plausibly unrelated to other outcome-changing forces. Multiple pre-periods, exposure checks, nearby comparison units, and alternative comparison groups help assess that claim. The treated group's post-period untreated outcome remains unobserved.
