## Research Question

A policy or institutional change reaches one population at a known time while a related population remains untreated. The outcome is observed before and after the change, but the treated population's untreated post-period outcome is missing. The problem is to recover that missing path well enough to learn whether the intervention changed the outcome.

## Observed Structure

The minimal evidence has two groups and two periods. One group is exposed only after the first observation. The other group is observed over the same calendar interval without exposure. The data therefore contain both cross-sectional variation across groups and longitudinal variation within groups.

## Failed Comparisons

A post-period treated-versus-untreated comparison is too crude because the groups may differ in stable ways unrelated to the intervention. A treated-group before-versus-after comparison is also too crude because the outcome may be moving over time for reasons unrelated to the intervention. Each simple comparison removes one nuisance component while leaving another one in place.

## Historical Signals

Natural experiments often arise when administrative, geographic, or institutional boundaries change exposure for some units but not others. Snow's water-company evidence and later labor-market policy shocks both show the attraction of such settings: the researcher can observe units near the intervention boundary, verify exposure, and follow outcomes across the moment when only some units receive the change.

## Credibility Requirements

The comparison group must reveal the treated group's untreated movement, not necessarily its level. This requires substantive evidence about why treatment timing is plausibly unrelated to other outcome-changing forces. Multiple pre-periods, exposure checks, nearby comparison units, and alternative comparison groups help assess that claim, but the key counterfactual trend remains unobserved.
