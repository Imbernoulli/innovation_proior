I start with the fact that a test is not a verdict about truth in one observation. It is a rule I will use repeatedly. If I draw a region `w` in the sample space and reject `H0` when the observed point falls in `w`, then `P0(w)` is the long-run frequency with which I reject `H0` when `H0` is true. I can make this number small by shrinking `w`, but that alone tells me almost nothing. There are many small regions, and they do not all behave the same way when the alternative `H1` is true.

My first tempting thought is to choose points that look rare under `H0`. That is too crude. A point can be rare under `H0` and also rare under `H1`; spending error probability there buys little ability to detect the alternative. Another point can be less spectacular under `H0` but much more characteristic of `H1`; spending the same first-error probability there may buy more power. So I should stop asking only whether a point is surprising under `H0`. I should ask what I get under `H1` for the amount of `H0` probability I spend.

Now the problem becomes a budget problem. The size `alpha` is the amount of `P0` mass I am allowed to put into the rejection region. The power is the amount of `P1` mass that region captures. If I add a tiny neighborhood around `x`, I pay approximately `p0(x)` units of size and receive approximately `p1(x)` units of power. The value of that purchase is therefore not the difference `p1(x)-p0(x)`, because differences do not account for the scarce budget. The value is power per unit of size, `p1(x)/p0(x)`. If `p0(x)=0` while `p1(x)>0`, the point is free power and should be included; if `p1(x)=0` while `p0(x)>0`, it is pure cost and should be left out.

I can see the optimal ordering by trying to improve a bad region. Suppose my current rejection region includes a small piece around `a` and excludes a small piece around `b`, and suppose `p1(b)/p0(b)` is larger than `p1(a)/p0(a)`. I can remove enough of the piece near `a` and add enough of the piece near `b` so that the `P0` mass stays the same. The first-error probability does not change. But the gained `P1` mass is larger than the lost `P1` mass, because the new piece has higher alternative probability per unit null probability. So the old region cannot be best.

This exchange argument is the whole pressure of the result. A best region cannot spend size on a lower ratio point while leaving out a higher ratio point. Once all such inversions are removed, the region must consist of the points with the largest values of `p1/p0`, up to a boundary. If the boundary has no mass, the threshold gives the exact size. If the boundary has mass, I randomize there, because inside the boundary all points have the same exchange rate and no deterministic choice within that boundary is forced by power.

The calculus-of-variations form says the same thing. I want to maximize `P1(w)` subject to `P0(w)=alpha`. Introducing a multiplier gives a boundary where the marginal gain and marginal cost balance: `p1(x)-c p0(x)=0`, or `p1(x)=c p0(x)`. On one side of that boundary the marginal gain exceeds the cost, so the point belongs in the rejection region. On the other side it does not. Neyman and Pearson write the reciprocal version as `p0 <= k p1`; in modern notation I read the same region as an upper level set of `p1/p0`.

What changes is the meaning of an efficient test. It is not merely a test based on an elegant statistic, and it is not merely a test with a small first-error probability. It is a rule that uses the allowed first-error probability where it has the greatest chance of catching the specified alternative. That is why the likelihood ratio appears as the ordering quantity. It is not a decorative measure of evidence; it is the exchange rate imposed by the size constraint.
