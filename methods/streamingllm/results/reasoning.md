Let me start from what actually breaks when I try to keep a chat model running forever. I have a decoder-only Transformer, trained with some fixed attention window — say 4K tokens for Llama-2 — and I want it to stay up over a day-long conversation, decoding token after token, in principle without end. The instant I write that down I hit two walls. The first is mechanical: autoregressive decoding caches the Key and Value of every past token in every layer, the KV cache, so that each new query attends to all of history without recomputing it. That cache grows linearly with the number of tokens, and the per-token attention cost grows with it, so on a stream of millions of tokens I run out of memory and the latency climbs until it's unusable. The second is deeper: even if I had infinite memory, the model's quality falls apart once the input is longer than its pretraining window. The position encodings and the attention patterns were simply never exercised at those distances. So I cannot just "let it run." I need to decode from a fixed-size slice of the cache — constant memory, constant per-token latency — and somehow keep quality, on sequences far longer than anything the model saw in training, on an already-trained model, no finetuning. That last constraint matters: I want this to work on Llama-2 as it ships, on standard dense-attention kernels, not as a retrained sparse-attention architecture.

What's the most obvious thing to try? Keep only the most recent tokens. A sliding window: cache the last L tokens' KV, evict the oldest as new ones arrive. Memory and latency go flat the moment the window fills, it scales linearly with sequence length, and intuitively recent context is what matters most for predicting the next token. This is the natural approach and it ought to just work. The known perplexity curve for this setup gives the first clue. For the first L tokens the cache holds everything, so this is exactly dense attention — perplexity is flat and healthy. Then token number L+1 arrives, the cache is full, and to make room I evict the oldest token, which is token number one, the very first token of the text. And right there — exactly at the step where the first token leaves the cache — the perplexity spikes. Not a gentle slope of forgetting old context; a cliff. The model that was generating fluent text starts producing nonsense the moment the initial tokens are gone. That's bizarre. The tokens I evicted are thousands of positions away from what I'm predicting; by any reasonable account of language they should be irrelevant. Recent context is intact, the window is full of perfectly good recent tokens, and yet dropping the ancient first token detonates the model.

So this isn't a "we lost some useful long-range information" failure. The collapse is tied specifically and abruptly to evicting the *initial* tokens, not to the size of the recent window. Something about those first positions is load-bearing in a way that has nothing to do with their being recent or relevant. Before I invent anything, I should look at what the model is actually doing with its attention, because the diagnosis has to come before the fix.

The attention maps give the next clue — across all layers and heads, on a few different models, Llama-2, MPT, Falcon, Pythia. And the pattern is striking and consistent: beyond the bottom couple of layers, nearly every head is dumping a huge fraction of its attention mass onto the first few token positions. Not because those tokens are relevant — they're whatever happened to start the text. On long sequences, length 4096, averaged over hundreds of samples, the attention from the last token back to the very first token often exceeds *half* of the total mass, in most layers. Half. The model is spending more attention on token zero than on everything else combined, in head after head, regardless of distance.

Now the window-attention cliff makes sense as a symptom, but I want to know the cause, because the cause tells me what to keep. Is the model attending to those initial tokens because their *content* matters, or because of their *position*? These have completely different fixes. The clean discriminating observation is to replace the first four tokens of a real text with a meaningless linebreak token. If content drove the attention, garbage tokens shouldn't attract it. But they do — the model still pours attention onto those first four slots even though they're now just newlines, and reintroducing them after a window eviction restores perplexity just as well as the original tokens would. So it is not the semantics. It is the absolute position. The model has learned to use whatever sits in the first few slots as a fixed destination for attention.

Why would it do that? Let me stare at the softmax. Within a head the attention weights are SoftMax(x)_i = e^{x_i} / sum_j e^{x_j}, and the defining property is that they sum to one over all attended tokens. There is no "none of the above," no abstain. So picture a query whose information is essentially self-contained — it has no strong match anywhere in its context, every logit x_j is mediocre. The model still has to produce a distribution that sums to one. It can't output all-zeros and say "I have nothing to add here." It must place that leftover probability mass *somewhere*. And if it's going to dump unwanted mass, the cheapest place to dump it is on some token whose value vector it can afford to mix in harmlessly — a token it can reliably find, that's always there, that doesn't corrupt the residual stream much. So the model learns to manufacture a few such dumping grounds. I'll call them attention sinks: tokens that collect large attention scores precisely *because* they're not semantically important, just to soak up the surplus that softmax forces it to allocate.

This rhymes with something from the quantization world — people had seen persistent outlier activations in these models and traced them to exactly this forced-allocation behavior of softmax, which is why someone proposed a SoftMax-off-by-one, SoftMax_1(x)_i = e^{x_i} / (1 + sum_j e^{x_j}), where the extra +1 in the denominator lets the weights sum to *less* than one — an escape hatch so the head can abstain. That's encouraging: an independent line of work hit the same sum-to-one constraint from the quantization side and reached for the same lever. It doesn't *prove* my sink story — outlier activations and attention sinks are different observables — but it's the same root cause showing up twice, which makes me more willing to lean on the mechanism. I'll treat it as a corroborating hint, not a confirmation.

But why the *initial* tokens as the sinks, rather than tokens in the middle? Here the autoregressive structure decides it. The model is causal: token at position t can only be attended to by queries at positions >= t. Token zero is visible to *every* subsequent query in the sequence. Token in the middle is visible only to the queries after it. If the model wants a *common* dumping ground that every position can reach, the only positions that qualify are the earliest ones — they're the universal back-reference. During training, gradient descent finds this and drives the first few tokens into the sink role, because they're the only slots that work as a shared sink for all queries. So initial-token sinks aren't an accident; they're forced by causal visibility plus the softmax constraint.

Let me check whether evicting the sinks could really produce a *cliff* rather than a gentle shift, because the window kept all the recent tokens — if the recent tokens still carry most of the useful signal, why would dropping a few ancient ones break anything? Write the attended positions as a sink set S plus the ordinary remaining positions R. Before eviction, a non-sink token j in R has weight a_j = e^{x_j} / (sum_{s in S} e^{x_s} + sum_{r in R} e^{x_r}). After evicting S, the same logit x_j becomes a'_j = e^{x_j} / sum_{r in R} e^{x_r}. So a'_j = a_j * (sum_{s in S} e^{x_s} + sum_{r in R} e^{x_r}) / sum_{r in R} e^{x_r} = a_j * (Z_full / Z_rest): every remaining weight is multiplied by the same factor, the ratio of the full denominator to the sink-free one, even though none of the remaining logits changed at all.

Is that factor actually large? The attention maps said the sinks hold over half the mass, so let me put numbers to it and see. Take four sinks with logits around 4.5–6 and eight ordinary recent tokens with mediocre logits around 0.5–1.2 (the "no strong match" regime where the sink story applies). Exponentiating, the four sinks contribute e^6 + e^5.5 + e^5 + e^4.5 ≈ 886 to the denominator and the eight ordinary tokens only ≈ 20, so Z_full ≈ 907, Z_rest ≈ 20 — the sinks are 97.8% of the denominator. A single ordinary token (logit 1.0) had weight e^1/907 ≈ 0.0033 with the sinks present. Evict the sinks and the *same* logit gives e^1/20 ≈ 0.135 — a 45x jump, and 45 is exactly Z_full/Z_rest as the algebra predicted. Summed over the eight ordinary tokens, their total attention weight goes from 0.022 to 1.0: before eviction they collectively absorbed about 2% of the mass and the sinks held the rest; after eviction softmax *forces* them to absorb 100%, redistributing a 50-fold larger share onto value vectors that were never meant to carry it. The attention output also loses the sink-value contribution entirely. So the head's output vector lurches to a place the downstream layers never see in normal dense inference — not a small drift, a 50x reweighting in one step. That is the cliff, and the window brought it on itself: it kept perfectly good recent tokens but threw away the denominator anchor.

The sinks are not valuable for their content; the linebreak test separates that away. They are valuable for the attention mass they absorb and the denominator they hold. Window attention's failure is that, in evicting the oldest tokens, it eventually evicts the sinks. So: don't. Keep the sink tokens' KV permanently, pinned, never evicted, and slide the window over everything else. The cache becomes two pieces: a small fixed set of the very first tokens, plus a rolling window of the most recent tokens, with the middle dropped. The sinks anchor the attention computation, the denominator stays close to its normal shape, and the rolling window carries the actually-useful recent context for language modeling. No finetuning, no architecture change, just a cache-management rule layered on top of standard attention.

How many initial tokens do I pin? Let me reason about it before reaching for a number. If the model had been trained with a single fixed token always at position zero, one sink slot should suffice — the model would have learned to route surplus attention to that one consistent token. But standard pretrained models don't have that. Llama-2 prepends a "<s>" token, but it's applied before the text is chunked into training sequences, so the token actually sitting at position zero of a given training chunk is mostly random. With no consistent starting token, the model can't have learned to concentrate the surplus on one fixed slot; whatever routing it learned has to work for *whichever* token happens to start a chunk, which pushes it to spread the sink role across the first *few* positions. That predicts a specific shape for the perplexity-vs-sink-count curve: pinning one or two tokens should leave a big chunk of the sink mass stranded outside the cache and not recover perplexity, the recovery should be roughly complete once I've captured the handful of positions the role is spread over, and pinning still more should buy almost nothing because there's no sink mass left out there to recover. I don't have the perplexity sweep in front of me, so this is a prediction rather than a measured fact — but if it holds, the saturation point is the right n_sink, and the spread-over-a-few argument makes me expect that point to be small, around four rather than one or twenty. I'll default to n_sink = 4 as the working value and treat the saturation-curve check as the thing that would confirm or move it; the recent window is whatever the budget leaves after the sinks.

Now there's a subtlety that the naive "keep first four plus last L" rule will get wrong, and it's about position encoding. Most of these models use relative position encodings — RoPE in Llama-2, Falcon, Pythia; ALiBi in MPT. Let me think about what happens to positions when I keep tokens [0,1,2,3] and then a window of recent tokens that are at original text positions, say, [6,7,8] while I'm about to decode position 9, with positions 4 and 5 evicted. The intuitive thing is to keep each kept token labeled with its original text position: [0,1,2,3, 6,7,8]. But is that right? With RoPE, the query-key inner product depends only on the *relative* position. Let me write it out to be sure. RoPE rotates query and key by an angle proportional to absolute position: q_m = R_{Θ,m} W_q x_m and k_n = R_{Θ,n} W_k x_n, where R_{Θ,m} is a block-diagonal rotation matrix built from frequencies θ_i = 10000^{-2(i-1)/d}. The rotation matrix is orthogonal, R_{Θ,m}^T = R_{Θ,-m}, and additive in the index, R_{Θ,m} R_{Θ,m'} = R_{Θ,m+m'}. So the inner product is q_m^T k_n = (R_{Θ,m} W_q x_m)^T (R_{Θ,n} W_k x_n) = x_m^T W_q^T R_{Θ,m}^T R_{Θ,n} W_k x_n = x_m^T W_q^T R_{Θ,n-m} W_k x_n. It depends only on n - m, the relative distance.

So what matters is the *gaps* between the kept tokens, as the model sees them through these rotations. If I keep original positions [0,1,2,3,6,7,8] and decode at position 9, the relative distances from my current query include a jump: there's a hole where 4 and 5 used to be. The query at 9 sees the recent window at distances 9-6=3, 9-7=2, 9-8=1, and the sinks at distances 9, 8, 7, 6 — and crucially, as this keeps rolling forward over a long stream, the recent window's *original* positions keep climbing, so the relative distance between the pinned sinks and the rolling window grows without bound. Those huge relative distances are exactly the regime where RoPE was never trained and where it degrades. I'd be reintroducing the very length-extrapolation failure I'm trying to dodge, through the back door.

So the fix has to be to assign positions by index *within the cache*, not by original text position. If the cache contains [0,1,2,3,6,7,8] and I'm decoding the next token, I treat them as occupying contiguous positions [0,1,2,3,4,5,6] and the new query as position 7 — relabel everything to be densely packed. Then all the relative distances the model ever sees are small and contiguous, inside the trained range, no matter how far the stream has progressed. The sinks sit at the start, the recent window right behind the current token, and the gap that physically exists in the original text is simply erased from the position bookkeeping. Getting this wrong — keeping the original indices — would silently reintroduce the extrapolation failure I'm trying to dodge, so the relabeling is doing real work, not bookkeeping cosmetics.

Concretely, how do I re-position a cached key for RoPE? I have to be careful about *when* the rotation gets applied. If I cache keys *after* they've been rotated to their original positions, a key for original position p is the vector R_{Θ,p} (W_k x_p), and now I want it to behave as if it sat at a new cache position p'. I need to turn R_{Θ,p}(W_k x) into R_{Θ,p'}(W_k x). Since the rotations compose, R_{Θ,p'} = R_{Θ,p'-p} R_{Θ,p}, so I just left-multiply the cached, already-rotated key by R_{Θ,p'-p}: rotate it by the *difference* between its new and old position. So I never need the un-rotated key; I can re-rotate the stored key in place by delta = p' - p. If instead I store keys before rotation, I can apply the rotary transform fresh at the cache positions each step; either way the principle is "rotate to the position-in-cache."

Before I trust this, I want to actually watch the re-rotation produce the right vector on a concrete case, because it's the kind of thing where an off-by-a-sign would still typecheck and just quietly corrupt every key. Take a single frequency pair, θ = 0.7, so R_{Θ,m} is an ordinary 2x2 rotation by angle m·0.7. Let the raw key W_k x = [0.4, -1.3], with original text position p = 7 and desired cache position p' = 2, so delta = p' - p = -5. The key as stored is R_{Θ,7}[0.4,-1.3] = [-1.20258, -0.63545]. What I *want* is the key as if it sat at position 2: R_{Θ,2}[0.4,-1.3] = [1.34907, 0.17322]. Now apply my recipe — left-multiply the stored key by R_{Θ,-5}: R_{Θ,-5}[-1.20258,-0.63545] = [1.34907, 0.17322]. It lands exactly on the target (agreement to ~1e-16). So the composition R_{Θ,p'} = R_{Θ,p'-p} R_{Θ,p} does what I claimed, and the delta is negative — I'm rotating *backward* by five steps to pull the key down to the contiguous cache index, which is the right direction. I never needed the un-rotated key; re-rotating the stored one by delta suffices.

Now the efficient RoPE form, because that's what I'll actually code, and I should confirm it agrees with the matrix version rather than assume it. The numerically cheap realization of R_{Θ,m} acting on a vector x is R_{Θ,m} x = x ⊙ cos(m θ) + rotate_half(x) ⊙ sin(m θ), where θ is the vector of per-pair frequencies, the elementwise cos/sin are formed by pairing dimensions, and rotate_half swaps the two halves of the feature vector with a sign flip: rotate_half(x) = [-x_2; x_1] for the two halves x_1, x_2. Taking a 4-D key with two frequency pairs θ = [0.7, 0.13], storing it at p = 7 with this formula and then re-applying the same formula with delta = -5, I get the same vector as applying it once directly at p' = 2 — agreement again to ~1e-16. So re-rotating with cos(delta θ), sin(delta θ) is correct in the form I'll write. For each kept token I need its delta = new_cache_position - original_position; after I've selected which tokens to keep and sorted their original indices, the new positions are just 0, 1, 2, ..., n_kept - 1 in cache order, and delta for the j-th kept token is j minus its original index — generally negative, as the worked example confirmed, and a negative delta is just a rotation by the corresponding negative angle, which R^T = R_{-m} guarantees is consistent.

For ALiBi the same principle is even simpler: ALiBi adds a bias proportional to relative distance directly to the logits, so I just apply a *contiguous* linear bias over the cache positions instead of a "jumping" bias that reflects the original-text gap. No re-rotation needed, just the right distance bookkeeping. But RoPE is the case that needs the explicit re-rotation, so that's what I'll implement carefully.

Now let me reconsider whether I even need a clever per-token *scoring* rule, because there's a whole family of methods that score tokens by importance — keep the heavy hitters by accumulated attention, that sort of thing. Tempting, because "keep the important tokens" sounds smarter than "keep the first four and the last L." But the linebreak test already told me the answer: the initial tokens are not important by content, they're important by position. A scoring rule that ranks tokens by their realized attention would *also* keep the sinks, sure — but it would do so by reading the full attention matrix at decision time, adding dynamic, attention-dependent machinery to the decode loop, and it was built to compress *within* a window, not to extrapolate to unbounded length. I don't need any of that. My retention rule is *positional and static*: keep the first n_sink tokens, keep the last (budget - n_sink), drop the middle, regardless of attention values. That's the whole scoring rule, and it falls directly out of the diagnosis. Simpler, no per-step attention read, and it's the thing the mechanism actually demands.

So let me write the scoring as the harness wants it: a score per cached token, where the harness keeps the top-scoring n_kept. I want to keep the sinks and the recent window and drop the middle, so I give a score of 1 to everything and 0 to the middle block that should be pruned. If the cache has k_len tokens and the compression ratio is ratio, the retained count is n_kept = int(k_len * (1 - ratio)), so the number to prune is n_pruned = k_len - n_kept = k_len - int(k_len * (1 - ratio)). The zero slice starts immediately after the sinks: [n_sink : n_sink + n_pruned].

Let me trace this on a concrete cache to be sure the slice picks the right tokens and not, say, an off-by-one window. Take k_len = 12, ratio = 0.5, n_sink = 4. Then n_kept = int(12·0.5) = 6 and n_pruned = 12 - 6 = 6, so I zero positions [4 : 10] and leave ones on [0,1,2,3] and [10,11]. Top-k by score keeps exactly {0,1,2,3,10,11}: the four sinks plus the two most recent tokens, six total, with the middle eight... no — with positions 4 through 9, the middle six, pruned. That's right: sinks at the front, recent window at the tail, hole in the middle. One more, with a harsher budget — k_len = 20, ratio = 0.7: n_kept = int(20·0.3) = 6, n_pruned = 14, zeros on [4 : 18], kept = {0,1,2,3,18,19}, again four sinks plus the two latest. So the static mask leaves exactly n_kept positions, n_sink of them sinks and the rest the most recent, for any budget. Then top-k by score keeps that set, and I sort the kept indices so the cache stays in chronological order, which I need anyway for the contiguous re-positioning to make sense.

Then select_cache gathers the kept keys and values. The values are just gathered at the kept indices — values carry no position, nothing to re-rotate. The keys are gathered *and* re-rotated by their deltas as I derived. Let me assemble it as the policy the harness exposes — retention_plan to hand over the sink count and budget, score_tokens for the static positional mask, and select_cache to do the top-k, the re-rotation of keys, and the gather of values:

```python
import torch


class SelectionPolicy:
    """StreamingLLM: keep attention sinks (the first few tokens) and the most
    recent tokens; drop the middle. Re-rotate kept keys to their positions
    within the cache so RoPE relative distances stay inside the trained range."""

    method_name = "streamingllm"
    rerotate_selected_keys = True

    def retention_plan(self, layer_id, request_meta, cache_meta):
        # n_sink=4 holds the spread-out sink mass for off-the-shelf models;
        # the recent window is whatever the budget leaves after the sinks.
        return {
            "method": self.method_name,
            "sink_tokens": 4,
            "compression_ratio": cache_meta["compression_ratio"],
        }

    def score_tokens(self, module, hidden_states, keys, values, kwargs, plan):
        # Static positional mask: 1 everywhere, 0 on the middle block to prune.
        # No attention read -- sinks matter by position, not by content.
        k_len = int(keys.shape[2])
        n_sink = int(plan.get("sink_tokens", 4))
        ratio = float(plan["compression_ratio"])
        assert k_len > n_sink, f"Input should contain more tokens than sink_tokens={n_sink}"
        n_pruned = k_len - int(k_len * (1.0 - ratio))   # tokens to drop from the middle
        scores = torch.ones_like(keys[..., 0])
        scores[:, :, n_sink : n_sink + n_pruned] = 0     # zero out the middle block
        return scores

    def rotate_half(self, x):
        # RoPE's pairing: swap the two halves with a sign flip -> [-x2, x1].
        x1 = x[..., : x.shape[-1] // 2]
        x2 = x[..., x.shape[-1] // 2 :]
        return torch.cat((-x2, x1), dim=-1)

    def rerotate_cache_keys(self, module, indices, keys):
        # Re-rotate each kept key by delta = (new cache position) - (original position),
        # so R_{p}(W_k x) becomes R_{p'}(W_k x) = R_{p'-p} R_{p} (W_k x).
        bsz, num_key_value_heads, n_kept = indices.shape
        device = indices.device
        device_type = keys.device.type
        dtype = keys.dtype
        inv_freq = module.rotary_emb.inv_freq[None, None, :, None].float().expand(
            bsz, num_key_value_heads, -1, 1
        )
        # new contiguous positions 0..n_kept-1 in cache order
        new_positions = torch.arange(0, n_kept, device=device).unsqueeze(0)[:, None, :].float()
        new_positions = new_positions.expand(bsz, num_key_value_heads, n_kept)
        delta_pos = (new_positions - indices.float()).unsqueeze(2)   # delta = new - old (<= 0)
        device_type = device_type if isinstance(device_type, str) and device_type != "mps" else "cpu"
        with torch.autocast(device_type=device_type, enabled=False):
            freqs = (delta_pos.float() * inv_freq.float()).transpose(2, 3)   # angles delta*theta
            emb = torch.cat((freqs, freqs), dim=-1)
            cos = emb.cos().contiguous()
            sin = emb.sin().contiguous()
        cos = cos.to(dtype=dtype)
        sin = sin.to(dtype=dtype)
        gather_idx = indices.unsqueeze(-1).expand(-1, -1, -1, module.head_dim)
        gathered = keys.gather(2, gather_idx).contiguous()
        # R_{delta} x = x*cos(delta*theta) + rotate_half(x)*sin(delta*theta)
        return (gathered * cos) + (self.rotate_half(gathered) * sin)

    def select_cache(self, module, keys, values, scores, n_kept):
        indices = scores.topk(n_kept, dim=-1).indices    # keep sinks + recent window
        indices = torch.sort(indices, dim=2).values      # chronological order for re-positioning
        selected_keys = self.rerotate_cache_keys(module, indices, keys)
        gather_idx = indices.unsqueeze(-1).expand(-1, -1, -1, module.head_dim)
        selected_values = values.gather(2, gather_idx).contiguous()   # values: just gather
        return selected_keys, selected_values
```

There's one more thing the diagnosis suggests, and it's about prevention rather than rescue. I had to pin *four* tokens only because the model never had a dedicated sink and spread the role across a few initial positions. What if I gave it one on purpose? If at pretraining I prepend a single learnable token to the start of every training sample, the model has a consistent, always-present slot to offload its surplus attention onto — a designated sink. Then at streaming time I'd need to pin only that one token, not four scavenged content tokens. And it shouldn't cost anything on normal tasks: the sink token just absorbs the leftover mass that softmax forces, which the model was already wasting on initial tokens anyway. The validation criterion is clear: the ordinary model should need several initial positions to stay stable, while the model trained with the dedicated sink should stay stable on that one pinned slot. There's also the softer variant straight from the quantization fix: replace softmax with SoftMax_1(x)_i = e^{x_i} / (1 + sum_j e^{x_j}). I claimed earlier the sinks matter for the denominator mass they hold, so let me check that this +1 really *is* a sink and not just a vaguely similar trick. A token whose key is all zeros has logit q·0 = 0, so it contributes e^0 = 1 to the denominator and, with an all-zero value, nothing to the output. Prepend such a "zero sink" and run ordinary softmax: with real logits, say, [0.0, 1.94] for two tokens, ordinary-softmax-with-a-zero-slot gives the two real tokens weights [0.024, 0.866] and parks the leftover 0.110 on the zero slot — and SoftMax_1 over just the two real tokens gives [0.024, 0.866], the identical numbers. So the +1 is *exactly* a prepended all-zero token: same denominator, same output, the surplus absorbed by a slot that contributes nothing downstream. It should help, since it gives the abstain option the sink story says the model is missing, but it is weaker than a real learnable sink: a zero-valued slot only soaks up mass into the denominator, so the model can still lean partly on other initial tokens for whatever it actually wants from a sink, whereas a learnable sink token gives it a full, content-bearing destination it can route to cleanly. So the recommendation for any model trained from scratch is the dedicated learnable sink token; the four-sink pinning is the no-retraining rescue for models that already exist.

Let me trace the whole chain once more and check it's causal, not just a pile of tricks that happen to work. I needed constant-memory, constant-latency decoding on unbounded streams of an already-trained model. The obvious sliding window gave constant memory but collapsed — sharply, at a cliff — the moment the first token was evicted, even though the evicted tokens were ancient and irrelevant. Looking at the attention maps showed the model concentrates enormous attention mass on the first few positions regardless of content; the linebreak test pinned it to absolute position, not semantics; the softmax sum-to-one constraint plus causal visibility explained *why* the model manufactures these initial-token sinks to absorb surplus attention; and putting numbers to it showed that evicting the sinks multiplies every surviving weight by the full-over-residual denominator ratio — a ~45x reweighting in the example I worked, forcing 100% of the mass onto value vectors that previously held 2% — which is the cliff, made of arithmetic rather than lost information. So the rescue is forced: pin the first few tokens' KV (four, as my saturation-curve argument predicts for off-the-shelf models that spread the sink role) and slide the window over the rest, dropping the middle. To keep this working past the pretraining length, positions must be assigned by index within the cache, not original text index, so all relative distances stay contiguous and inside the trained range; for RoPE that means re-rotating each kept key by the difference between its new and old position, which I derived from the additivity and orthogonality of the rotation matrices and then checked landed exactly on the directly-rotated key. The retention rule is therefore a static positional mask — score 1 on sinks and recent, 0 on the middle, top-k, sort, gather, re-rotate keys — with no per-step attention scoring needed, because the thing to keep is determined by position. And to avoid the rescue entirely in future models, train with a single dedicated learnable sink token so one pinned slot suffices.
