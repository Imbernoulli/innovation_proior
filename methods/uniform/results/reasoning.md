Let me start from what actually hurts. I have a trained denoiser `eps_theta(x_t, t)` and I want a sample out of it, and the only thing that costs me anything is how many times I call that network. Each call is a full forward pass through a big U-Net, and the calls are sequential — I can't parallelize across them because each one needs the state the previous one produced. So my entire wall-clock budget is the count of denoiser evaluations, NFE. And right now the recipe I inherited spends roughly a thousand of them per image, because the way the model was built, generation is a Markov chain with one learned reverse step per forward time index, and there are about a thousand indices. A single 32×32 image is fine; fifty thousand of them for an FID evaluation is most of a day on one GPU. I want to cut that by one or two orders of magnitude. So the question I'm really chasing is: can I get a good sample out of this same frozen network with only a handful of calls — say five — and if so, *where on the time axis do I spend those five calls*?

Let me first be honest about why the inherited chain is locked at a thousand. The forward process turns data into noise; in the Gaussian case the marginal at any time has a clean closed form, `q(x_t | x_0) = N(sqrt(alpha_t) x_0, (1 - alpha_t) I)`, so a noisy state is `x_t = sqrt(alpha_t) x_0 + sqrt(1 - alpha_t) eps`. The generative side, the way Ho, Jain and Abbeel set it up, is a reverse Markov chain `p_theta(x_{t-1} | x_t)`, one Gaussian transition per index, and you sample by walking it all the way down: for `t = T, ..., 1`, `x_{t-1} = (1/sqrt(alpha_t))(x_t - ((1 - alpha_t)/sqrt(1 - alpha_bar_t)) eps_theta(x_t, t)) + sigma_t z`. Here is the trap. Each transition `p_theta(x_{t-1} | x_t)` was trained to invert *exactly one* forward step from `t` to `t-1`. The whole derivation that makes the reverse step Gaussian leans on the forward step being a small amount of added noise; the KL terms in the variational bound pair up `q(x_{t-1} | x_t, x_0)` with `p_theta(x_{t-1} | x_t)` index by adjacent index. If I try to skip from, say, index 1000 straight to index 800, there is no trained transition for that jump — the network only knows how to undo one increment at a time. So the chain length is welded to the training length. To take fewer steps I'd have to retrain with fewer indices, and then I'd lose the high quality that the long fine-grained chain bought me. That's the wall: the sampler's step count is not a free knob, it's baked into the training.

So I need to break the welding. Let me stare at the training loss, because that's the thing that actually constrains what the network learned. The loss is a sum over times of `E‖eps - eps_theta(sqrt(alpha_t) x_0 + sqrt(1 - alpha_t) eps, t)‖^2`. Read what's inside: the only place `t` enters is through `alpha_t`, which defines the *marginal* `q(x_t | x_0)`. The loss never references the joint distribution of two consecutive states; it never asks "given `x_t`, what is `x_{t-1}`." It only ever asks the network to denoise a sample drawn from a single marginal at a single time. That's a much weaker thing than I'd been assuming. The Markov chain — the welding — is an artifact of how the *sampler* was defined, not of what the *network* knows. The network is a function of `t` over a whole continuous interval, trained to denoise the marginal at each `t`. It has no commitment to any particular chain length at all.

That cracks it open. If the loss only sees marginals, then any generative process that (a) reuses this same denoiser and (b) is consistent with these same marginals is a legitimate sampler for this trained model, no retraining needed. And crucially, "consistent with the marginals" does not force me to visit every index. I can pick an increasing subset of times — a sub-sequence `tau` of the full index range — define a process that hops from one chosen time to the next chosen time, arrange its coefficients so the marginal at each `tau_i` still equals `N(sqrt(alpha_{tau_i}) x_0, (1 - alpha_{tau_i}) I)`, and the same trained `eps_theta` slots straight in. There's a deterministic member of this family too — the one where I push the injected noise to zero — and that one is the cleanest to think about because it's a function: given the latent, the sample is determined, and each step is a plain extrapolation, no randomness to average over. The number of denoiser calls is now the number of chosen transitions; in the continuous bridge harness I will represent `n` calls as `n` intervals with `n + 1` fencepost times. The welding is gone. I'm free to take five steps.

But "free to take five steps" immediately hands me a new problem that the chain never had: *which* five? When I was forced to visit all thousand indices the placement was trivial — I visited all of them, in order, and there was nothing to decide. Now I have a budget of `n` denoiser calls and an interval of times from the noisy endpoint `t_max` down to the data endpoint `t_min`, and I have to lay down an ordered list `t_max = t_0 > t_1 > ... > t_n = t_min` of where to stop. The model is frozen, the per-step update rule is frozen; the *only* thing I'm choosing is this list of times. And with `n = 5` it has to matter a lot, because each gap between consecutive stops is now huge — about a fifth of the whole interval — instead of one index wide.

Let me get more precise about why the gap size suddenly matters, because that's the thing that will tell me how to place the stops. The deterministic sampler is, structurally, a numerical integrator marching along the time axis. There's a clean way to see this: rewrite the deterministic update and it turns into an Euler step. Reparameterize `xbar = x / sqrt(alpha)` and `sigma = sqrt((1 - alpha)/alpha)`, and the deterministic step from one stop to the next reads `xbar(t - Δt) = xbar(t) + (sigma(t - Δt) - sigma(t)) eps_theta(...)`. Divide by `-Δt` and let `Δt → 0` and that is exactly `d xbar / dt = (d sigma / dt) eps_theta(...)` — an ordinary differential equation. The continuous-time picture agrees: there's a probability-flow ODE, `dx = [f(x, t) - (1/2) g(t)^2 ∇_x log p_t(x)] dt`, whose trajectories carry the model's marginals, and the learned denoiser supplies the score, so sampling *is* integrating this ODE backward from `t_max` to `t_min`. My list of stops is the integrator's node placement, and the number of denoiser calls is the number of intervals between consecutive stops. Sampling quality is integration accuracy, and integration accuracy with a fixed low number of intervals is governed by where I put the stops.

So now I can actually quantify the cost of a bad placement. On each interval `[t_{i+1}, t_i]` of length `Δt_i = t_i - t_{i+1}`, a first-order (Euler-type) step incurs a local truncation error. Expand the true trajectory `x(t)` about the start of the step: `x(t - Δt) = x(t) - Δt · x'(t) + (1/2)(Δt)^2 x''(xi)` for some `xi` in the interval. The Euler step keeps the linear term and throws away the rest, so the local error it makes is the leftover, `(1/2)(Δt_i)^2 x''(xi_i)` — proportional to `(Δt_i)^2` times the local curvature `|x''|` of the trajectory there. (If the frozen sampler happens to be second-order, like a Heun corrector, the leftover is the next term, order `(Δt_i)^3`, but the structure is the same: a power of `Δt_i` times a local derivative.) The total error I accumulate over the whole sample is the sum of these per-interval errors, roughly `Σ_i (Δt_i)^2 C_i` where `C_i = (1/2)|x''|` on interval `i`. With a thousand tiny intervals every `Δt_i` is minuscule and this sum is nothing; with five calls every `Δt_i` is large and the sum is first-order sensitive to two things — how I size the intervals `Δt_i`, and where the curvature `C_i` is concentrated along the trajectory.

That makes the placement problem a question of *what I know about where the trajectory curves*. Suppose I knew `C_i` — knew exactly which stretch of the interval the denoiser's trajectory bends through hardest. Then I'd want to minimize `Σ_i (Δt_i)^2 C_i` subject to `Σ_i Δt_i = t_max - t_min` (the intervals have to tile the whole range). That's a constrained optimization; set up the Lagrangian `Σ_i (Δt_i)^2 C_i - lambda (Σ_i Δt_i - L)`, take the derivative in each positive-curvature interval: `2 C_i Δt_i = lambda`, so `Δt_i ∝ 1 / C_i`. If an interval has essentially zero curvature, this same condition says I can afford to stretch it. The optimal grid makes each step shorter exactly where the curvature is larger — pack nodes where the trajectory is hard, stretch them where it's smooth. That's a real, principled answer, and it's exactly the intuition behind the warped schedules people reach for: if you believe the trajectory does all its bending up at the high-noise end, you space your stops evenly in some power `t^{1/rho}` so they cluster there; if you believe the action is near the data end, you space them evenly in `log t` so they cluster there; if you believe it's in the middle, you use a cosine warp. Each of those is just an even grid pushed through a fixed monotone map, and each map is a *bet* about the shape of `C_i`.

Now here is the wall I have to be honest about. I do *not* know `C_i`. The curvature of the sampling trajectory depends on the trained denoiser, on the data distribution, on the particular bridge — and I'm being asked for a schedule that generalizes across workloads, edges→handbags and depth maps and ImageNet-scale pairs, with no constant secretly fit to any one of them. If I commit to "curvature lives at the high-noise end" and bake in `rho = 7`, I win on the workloads where that's true and I *lose* on the ones where it isn't — I'll have spent four of my five precious nodes crammed into a region that was actually smooth, and starved the region that actually needed resolution. A warp is only as good as its assumption, and I have no grounds for the assumption. Worse, each warp drags in a shape hyperparameter — the exponent, the log base, the cosine phase — and tuning that parameter is exactly the dataset-specific overfitting I'm forbidden from doing. So the warped schedules aren't a safe *baseline*; they're informed guesses, and I have no information to inform the guess.

So back up and ask the right question for a baseline: what do I place when I genuinely know nothing about where the curvature sits? This is now a minimax problem, not the optimization I did above. I don't get to use the true `C_i`; I have to choose the grid before I see it, and I'll be judged against an adversary who could put a fixed curvature budget anywhere. Concretely: minimize over grids the *worst case* over nonnegative profiles with `Σ_i C_i = B` of `Σ_i (Δt_i)^2 C_i`. Think about what an adversary does to any non-uniform grid. If my grid has one interval `Δt_j` that's noticeably larger than the others — which is what every warp does, it deliberately makes some intervals long where it bets the trajectory is smooth — the adversary dumps the whole budget into that long interval. The error there is `(Δt_j)^2 B`, and because `Δt_j` is large and entered squared, that single interval dominates my worst case. The only way to deny the adversary a fat target is to make no interval larger than necessary — to flatten the `Δt_i` so they're all equal. If every `Δt_i = L/n` with `L = t_max - t_min`, then wherever the adversary concentrates the budget, the worst interval contributes `(L/n)^2 B`, and there's no oversized interval to exploit. Equalizing the squared step sizes minimizes the worst case. That's the uniform grid, and it drops out as the *answer to the no-information problem*, not as a lazy default.

Let me sanity-check that this is really the minimax solution and I'm not fooling myself. Take any non-uniform grid and let `Δt_max` be its largest interval; then `Δt_max > L/n` strictly (the intervals sum to `L`, so if one exceeds the average another falls below it). An adversary placing all its curvature budget on that interval forces error at least proportional to `(Δt_max)^2 > (L/n)^2`. The uniform grid achieves exactly `(L/n)^2` on every interval, so its worst case is `(L/n)^2` times the curvature budget — strictly below any non-uniform grid's worst case. Uniform is minimax-optimal under no curvature information. Good — it's not just "even feels neutral," it's provably the right hedge.

There's another argument that I shouldn't dismiss: the inherited full-length chain already used a uniform grid — it stopped at every consecutive integer index, which is even spacing with `Δt = 1`. So uniform-in-`t` is the honest inheritance of "do exactly what the dense sampler did, just with fewer of the same evenly-spaced stops." It changes one number, the count `n`, and introduces *no* shape parameter — no `rho`, no base, no phase. Nothing to tune, nothing to overfit. For a method that must generalize across workloads with no dataset-specific constants, "carries zero shape parameters" is not a minor convenience; it's the property that makes the generalization automatic.

One subtlety I want to nail before I write it down: in *which* coordinate do I space evenly? I've been saying "uniform in `t`," but the warped schedules are *also* uniform — Karras is uniform in `t^{1/rho}`, log-linear is uniform in `log t`. Spacing evenly in a transformed coordinate is the same as warping the grid in `t`. So "uniform" only means something once I fix the coordinate, and I should pick the coordinate that presupposes the least. The frozen sampler's update rule is written in terms of `t` directly — it indexes its step coefficients by `t`, it asks the denoiser for `eps_theta(x_t, t)` at a time `t`. So `t` is the sampler's own native variable; spacing evenly in `t` means "constant step in the variable the integrator actually marches." Any other coordinate — `t^{1/rho}`, `log t` — presupposes that the trajectory is smoothest in that warped variable, which is a curvature assumption in disguise, the very thing I just argued I have no basis for. Plain uniform-in-`t` presupposes nothing about the warp. So the coordinate is `t`, constant `Δt`.

Now the endpoint discipline, because the contract is strict and it's easy to get wrong with a warp but free with a straight grid. The list must (1) have exactly `n + 1` entries, (2) strictly decrease, (3) start at `t_max`, and (4) end *exactly* at `t_min`. The `n + 1` is the fence-post count: `n` Euler intervals need `n + 1` nodes, `t_0, ..., t_n`, and NFE equals the number of intervals stepped, which is `n`. The terminal-equals-`t_min` requirement is not cosmetic — the last step has to finish on the data endpoint. If the final node lands a little short of `t_min`, the sample still carries residual noise; if the grid goes past `t_min`, the sampler leaves the time range it was trained to use. The uniform-in-`t` grid hits both endpoints *exactly* by construction — it's the linear interpolation `t_i = t_max + (i/n)(t_min - t_max)`, which gives `t_0 = t_max` and `t_n = t_min` with no rounding and no patch. This is one more quiet advantage over warped closed forms that otherwise need an explicit `last = t_min` correction. The straight grid never needs it.

Let me write the formula and check every requirement against it. The grid is
`t_i = t_max + (i / n)(t_min - t_max)` for `i = 0, 1, ..., n`. Check: at `i = 0`, `t_0 = t_max` — start correct. At `i = n`, `t_n = t_max + (t_min - t_max) = t_min` — terminal exact. Strictly decreasing: consecutive difference is `t_{i+1} - t_i = (1/n)(t_min - t_max) = -(t_max - t_min)/n < 0` since `t_max > t_min` — every step goes down by the same constant amount, so strictly decreasing and uniform. Count: `i` runs over `0..n`, that's `n + 1` entries. All four satisfied, and the constant gap `(t_max - t_min)/n` is the equalized `Δt` the minimax argument asked for. This is `linspace(t_max, t_min, n + 1)` — that's the one-line library call that produces exactly this.

I should also pin the discrete face of the same idea, because in the index-based setting (where the denoiser is indexed by integer training steps rather than a continuous `t`) "uniform" is a stride. The mathematical linear subsequence is `tau_i = floor(c · i)`, with `c` chosen so the last selected training index is close to the end of the chain; the code face used by constant-stride timestep selectors is the same rule with `c_int = T // S` and `np.asarray(list(range(0, T, c_int)))`, then a `+1` shift when the alpha arrays are indexed from one. The curvature-warped sibling in index space is `tau_i = floor(c · i^2)`, the quadratic stride — visibly the same kind of fixed warp I rejected, here `i^2` packing stops toward one end. Linear stride `floor(c·i)` and continuous `linspace` are the same constant-step placement; the continuous one is just the limit where the step doesn't have to be an integer. I'll write the continuous form as the primary artifact since the bridge sampler marches a continuous `t`, and note the integer-stride form as its discrete twin.

Let me also be clear-eyed that this is deliberately a *baseline*, and I want to state precisely what it does and doesn't claim, because that's what makes it useful rather than a strawman. It does *not* claim to be the best schedule for any particular workload — if I actually knew the curvature lived at the high-noise end of edges→handbags, a warp tuned to that would beat it there. What it claims is: with no information about the trajectory's curvature, even spacing is the minimax-optimal hedge, it carries zero shape hyperparameters, it generalizes across workloads by construction, and it satisfies the endpoint contract exactly with no correction. That's exactly the role of a baseline — the disciplined "no bet" against which every informed bet must justify itself. Anything that wants to beat it has to earn its assumption.

So let me write the schedule into the one open slot in the harness, the function that hands the frozen sampler its list of evaluation times. The whole method is one line of placement plus the device move the contract requires:

```python
import torch


def get_sampling_times(n, t_min, t_max, device="cpu"):
    """Uniform (linear) time schedule: place the n + 1 evaluation times at constant
    spacing in t, from t_max down to t_min. The 'no-information' baseline placement —
    minimax-optimal when the trajectory's curvature could sit anywhere, and carrying no
    shape hyperparameter, so it transfers across workloads unchanged.

    Contract: length n + 1; strictly decreasing; t[0] = t_max, t[n] = t_min exactly;
    on `device`. For this task n = 5 (NFE = 5)."""
    # t_i = t_max + (i/n)(t_min - t_max), i = 0..n  -> constant step (t_max - t_min)/n.
    # linspace hits both endpoints exactly (no terminal correction needed) and is
    # strictly monotone since t_max > t_min.
    return torch.linspace(t_max, t_min, n + 1).to(device)
```

And the discrete index-space twin, the constant-stride implementation style used by integer
timestep samplers to select a short subsequence from the `T` trained time indices:

```python
import numpy as np


def make_uniform_integer_timesteps(num_sampling_timesteps, num_training_timesteps):
    """Uniform (linear) stride over integer training indices.

    Mathematically this is tau_i = floor(c * i); the quadratic sibling is
    tau_i = floor(c * i**2). The usual implementation realizes the linear
    rule with range(0, T, c), then shifts by +1 for alpha indexing.
    """
    c = num_training_timesteps // num_sampling_timesteps
    selected_timesteps = np.asarray(list(range(0, num_training_timesteps, c)))
    return selected_timesteps + 1
```

The path is now clear. I was stuck because the inherited sampler was a reverse Markov chain welded to its thousand-index training length — each reverse transition only knew how to undo one forward step, so I couldn't take fewer steps without retraining and losing quality. Staring at the training loss broke the weld: it depends only on the per-time *marginals*, never on the joint of consecutive states, so the trained denoiser is a function of `t` over a whole interval, free to be queried at any chosen subset of times — and the deterministic member of that freed-up family turns out to be an Euler integrator of the model's probability-flow ODE. That reframed sampling as numerical integration and made the new question concrete: with only `n` integration intervals, where on `[t_min, t_max]` do I put their `n + 1` endpoints? The per-step Euler error `(Δt_i)^2 C_i` told me the ideal grid would shorten steps where the curvature `C_i` is large — but I don't know `C_i`, can't know it across heterogeneous workloads, and can't fit it without the dataset-specific tuning I'm forbidden from. Treating it as a minimax problem against an adversary who could place curvature anywhere, the grid that minimizes the worst case is the one with no oversized interval to exploit — equal `Δt_i`, the uniform grid — and it is also the honest inheritance of the dense sampler's even spacing. Spacing evenly in the sampler's native variable `t` (not a warped coordinate, which would smuggle in a curvature assumption) gives `t_i = t_max + (i/n)(t_min - t_max)`: exactly `n + 1` nodes, constant step, both endpoints hit exactly so the terminal lands on `t_min` with no correction, no shape hyperparameter to overfit. That's `torch.linspace(t_max, t_min, n + 1)`, with the integer-stride `floor(c·i)` as its discrete twin — the disciplined "no bet" baseline that any informed schedule has to beat.
