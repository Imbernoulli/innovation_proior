OK, let me think this through from scratch. The thing I actually have is a model that's too expensive to deploy — either an ensemble of nets whose predictions I average, or one giant net regularized to the teeth with dropout. It's accurate. But at deployment I want something small and fast. So the question is: how do I move what the big thing learned into a small net?

My first instinct is the obvious thing people already do: take the big model, run it on a pile of data, and train the small model to copy its answers. Caruana and his collaborators did exactly this — compress an ensemble into one small net by labeling a transfer set with the ensemble and fitting the small net to those labels. So the recipe exists. The question that's nagging me is *what* the small net should copy, and why copying works at all.

I keep hitting a conceptual block. I'm tempted to think the "knowledge" of a trained net *is* its weights — the specific parameter values it settled into. But if that's the knowledge, then changing the architecture throws it all away, and the whole enterprise is hopeless. So that framing is wrong, or at least useless. Let me reframe: the knowledge is the *mapping* the model has learned, from an input to a distribution over outputs. That's architecture-free. A small net of a totally different shape could in principle implement the same mapping. Now compression isn't "copy the weights," it's "reproduce the function."

So what does the function look like, concretely? The big model, given an image, doesn't just say "BMW." It puts out a full distribution: BMW gets almost all the mass, but garbage-truck gets a tiny sliver, and carrot gets an even tinier one. And — this is the part that matters — garbage-truck is *many times* more probable than carrot. That ratio isn't noise; it's the model telling me it thinks a BMW looks a lot more like a garbage truck than like a carrot. That's a rich similarity structure over the classes, and it's a *side effect* of training to maximize the correct-class log-prob. The hard label "BMW" throws all of that away. If I train the small net only on hard labels, it never sees that a BMW is truck-ish and not carrot-ish; it has to rediscover all of that from limited capacity and limited data. But the big model already worked it out. So the soft outputs — the full distributions — are the thing to transfer. They carry far more information per training case than a one-hot label, and the gradient varies less from case to case, so the small net should be trainable on much less data and with a higher learning rate.

Good, so: train the small net to match the big net's output *distribution* on a transfer set — the cross-entropy between the small net's softmax and the big net's softmax.

Put numbers on this. Take MNIST. The big net is essentially always right with enormous confidence. Say for a given "2" its logits are roughly $0$ for "2", and far below for the rest — enough that the softened information is "this 2 looks a bit like a 3, not at all like a 7." Let me pick three logits $v=(0,\,-13.8,\,-20.7)$ for classes "2","3","7" and actually compute the softmax. I get $p \approx (1.00,\;1.02\times 10^{-6},\;1.02\times 10^{-9})$. So the interesting "3-ness" lives at $p_3 \approx 10^{-6}$ and the "7-ness" at $10^{-9}$. Now look at the cross-entropy $-\sum_i p_i \log q_i$: the term for "3" is weighted by $p_3 \approx 10^{-6}$, and the correct class outweighs it by a factor of $p_2/p_3 \approx 9.85\times 10^{5}$. So in the loss, and in the gradient, the "3" term is a millionth the size of the "is it a 2" term. The very information I said was valuable contributes essentially nothing. The near-1 mass on the correct class drowns everything out. So naive probability-matching mostly transfers "it's a 2," which I already knew from the hard label. The dark knowledge is sitting right there in the targets but it is invisible to the objective.

This is exactly why Caruana et al. matched *logits* instead of probabilities, with a squared error between the student's logits and the teacher's logits. Logits are unbounded; a logit that produces a $10^{-6}$ probability is a perfectly ordinary, finite number, sitting maybe 14 below the top logit, and squared error treats it on equal footing with the big ones. So logit matching surfaces the small-probability structure. It works. But it bugs me as a target. It's pulled out of nowhere relative to the probabilistic story — why squared error on logits? And it forces the student to match *every* logit equally, including logits that are very negative. Those very negative logits are almost completely unconstrained by the teacher's own training (the teacher's loss barely cares whether a logit is $-20$ or $-30$, both give negligible probability), so they could be mostly noise. Maybe matching them is harmful; maybe it's helpful because even noise-ish logits encode something. I'd like a method with a knob that lets me dial how much attention to pay to those.

Let me go back to the softmax and ask: the reason the small probabilities are invisible is that the softmax has *squashed* them down near zero. What if I un-squash them before computing the target? Introduce a temperature into the softmax:

  q_i = exp(z_i / T) / Σ_j exp(z_j / T).

At T=1 this is the ordinary softmax. As I raise T, the logits get divided down, the exponentials get pulled toward each other, and the relative mass on the small-logit classes should go up. Let me check that it actually does, on the same "2" example — does temperature lift the $10^{-6}$ off the floor by enough to matter? Recompute $p_3/p_2$ (the "3"-to-correct ratio) at a few temperatures: at $T=1$ it is $1.0\times 10^{-6}$ (as above); at $T=5$ it is $6.3\times 10^{-2}$; at $T=20$ it is $5.0\times 10^{-1}$. So at $T=20$ the "3" class carries half the mass of the correct class instead of a millionth — that is a difference the cross-entropy gradient can see. Good: a high temperature does pull the dark knowledge into probabilities big enough to matter. So the move is to produce the soft targets by running the teacher's softmax at a high temperature, and train the student's softmax at the *same* high temperature to match them. After training, the student goes back to T=1 for deployment.

So the simplest form of distillation: soft target $p_i = \mathrm{softmax}(v/T)_i$ from teacher logits $v$, student trains to minimize cross-entropy $-\sum_i p_i \log q_i$ with $q_i = \mathrm{softmax}(z/T)_i$, same $T$ on both sides.

Now I want to also use the true hard labels when I have them — the student can't perfectly match the soft targets, and nudging it toward the actually-correct answer should help. One option is to fold the correct label into the soft targets, by bumping the true class. But that mixes two different signals into one target distribution. Cleaner: keep the large model's softened distribution intact, add an ordinary hard-label objective beside it, and let a coefficient decide how much the hard label is allowed to pull. So

  L = w_soft · CE_soft(T) + w_hard · CE_hard(T=1),

The temperature scales the two terms' gradients differently, so look at the gradients before fixing the weights.

Take one transfer case. The student logit is $z_i$, the teacher logit is $v_i$, soft target $p_i = \mathrm{softmax}(v/T)_i$, student soft output $q_i = \mathrm{softmax}(z/T)_i$. The soft cross-entropy is $C = -\sum_i p_i \log q_i$. I need $\partial C / \partial z_i$. The standard softmax-cross-entropy gradient with respect to the *logit-before-temperature-scaling* picks up a $1/T$ from the chain rule, because $q$ depends on $z_i/T$. Working it out, the gradient of cross-entropy with respect to the input of a softmax is (output − target), and here the softmax input is $z_i/T$, so

  ∂C/∂z_i = (1/T) (q_i − p_i) = (1/T) ( exp(z_i/T)/Σ_j exp(z_j/T) − exp(v_i/T)/Σ_j exp(v_j/T) ).

Now take the high-temperature limit. If $T$ is large compared to the magnitudes of the logits, then $z_i/T$ is small, and $\exp(z_i/T) \approx 1 + z_i/T$. Substitute into both fractions:

  ∂C/∂z_i ≈ (1/T) ( (1 + z_i/T)/(N + Σ_j z_j/T) − (1 + v_i/T)/(N + Σ_j v_j/T) ),

where $N$ is the number of classes (the sum of the $1$'s in each denominator). This is messy because of the denominators. But suppose I've zero-meaned the logits separately for each transfer case — i.e. $\sum_j z_j = 0$ and $\sum_j v_j = 0$. Subtracting a constant from all logits doesn't change the softmax, so this is a free normalization. Then both denominators collapse to $N$, and

  ∂C/∂z_i ≈ (1/T) ( (1 + z_i/T)/N − (1 + v_i/T)/N ) = (1/(NT)) ( z_i/T − v_i/T ) = (1/(N T²)) ( z_i − v_i ).

The $1$'s cancel. So in the high-$T$ limit, the gradient of the soft cross-entropy looks proportional to $(z_i − v_i)$ — which would make minimizing the soft cross-entropy equivalent, up to a positive scalar, to minimizing $\tfrac{1}{2}\sum_i (z_i − v_i)^2$, Caruana's logit-matching squared error.

Two approximations went into this — $\exp(x/T)\approx 1+x/T$, and the zero-mean trick that kills the denominators — worth checking numerically. Take $N=4$ classes; pick teacher logits $v=(4,0,-1,-3)$ and student logits $z=(3,0.5,-0.5,-2)$, then subtract each vector's mean so the zero-mean condition $\sum_j v_j = \sum_j z_j = 0$ holds. Now compute the *exact* gradient $(1/T)(q_i-p_i)$ with the true temperature-softmaxes, and compare it to the approximation $(z_i-v_i)/(NT^2)$, componentwise, sweeping $T$:

| $T$ | exact $(1/T)(q-p)$, class 0 | approx $(z-v)/NT^2$, class 0 | ratio (per-class, all four) |
|----|----|----|----|
| 1    | $-0.081$ | $-0.312$ | $0.26,\ 0.89,\ 0.33,\ 0.03$ |
| 5    | $-0.016$ | $-0.013$ | $1.28,\ 2.22,\ 1.82,\ 0.78$ |
| 20   | $-1.0\times 10^{-3}$ | $-1.0\times 10^{-3}$ | $1.10,\ 1.33,\ 1.26,\ 0.97$ |
| 100  | $-3.19\times 10^{-5}$ | $-3.13\times 10^{-5}$ | $1.02,\ 1.07,\ 1.05,\ 0.99$ |
| 1000 | $-3.13\times 10^{-7}$ | $-3.13\times 10^{-7}$ | $1.00,\ 1.01,\ 1.01,\ 1.00$ |

At $T=1$ the two are nowhere near each other (ratios from $0.03$ to $0.89$) — the approximation is meaningless at low temperature, as expected. But as $T$ climbs, every component's ratio marches to $1$: by $T=1000$ all four are within half a percent. So the exact soft-cross-entropy gradient really does converge to $(z_i-v_i)/(NT^2)$, componentwise, not just in magnitude. The squared-error logit-matching target that looked pulled out of nowhere is what temperature-$T$ distillation becomes as $T\to\infty$. That is reassuring — the probabilistic story and Caruana's heuristic are the same thing seen at two temperatures.

And the knob I wanted may be right here. At *intermediate* temperature I'm not in that limit, and the approximation $\exp(z/T)\approx 1+z/T$ breaks for logits that are large and negative — a logit far below the average makes $\exp(z_i/T)$ much smaller than $1+z_i/T$ would suggest, so the soft target $p_i$ for that class should be even closer to zero, and the gradient should pay *much less* attention to matching very negative logits. Put one class far below the others — teacher logits $(5,1,0,-15)$, zero-meaned — and read off the soft target on the bottom class: at $T=1$ it is $2.0\times 10^{-9}$ (utterly ignored); at $T=4$ it is $4.1\times 10^{-3}$; at $T=25$ it is $1.4\times 10^{-1}$. So at $T=1$ the loss essentially does not care what the student does with that class, and as $T$ rises the class progressively *enters* the objective. That is the dial: lowering $T$ from infinity *down-weights* the very negative, possibly-noisy logits, while high $T$ pulls them all in. Whether ignoring the big negative logits helps or hurts is genuinely an empirical question — they're nearly unconstrained by the teacher's training so they could be noise, but they could also encode real structure. The point is distillation gives me the dial; logit matching is stuck at the high-$T$ end of it.

Now the weighting of hard vs soft. The soft-target gradient I just derived scales as $1/T^2$ (look at the $1/(NT^2)$). Check that this isn't an artifact of one component — take the same $v,z$ and check whether $\|(1/T)(q-p)\|\cdot T^2$ settles to a constant as $T$ grows. It gives $0.405$ at $T=20$, $0.390$ at $T=40$, $0.383$ at $T=80$, $0.379$ at $T=160$ — flattening toward a constant, so yes, the whole soft gradient shrinks like $1/T^2$. The hard-target term, by contrast, runs at $T=1$, so its gradient does *not* shrink with $T$. If I just add the two with fixed weights and then change $T$ while tuning, the soft term's contribution silently collapses relative to the hard term as $T$ grows — at $T=160$ versus $T=20$ it would be down by a factor of $(160/20)^2 = 64$ — and my soft/hard balance drifts out from under me. I need the coefficient to mean the same thing as I sweep temperature, so I multiply the soft-target gradients, equivalently the soft loss, by $T^2$. The same numbers say this works: $\|(1/T)(q-p)\|\cdot T^2$ is flat to within a few percent across an eightfold range of $T$, so after the $T^2$ multiply the soft contribution is roughly scale-invariant in $T$ and the relative weight of hard and soft stays put while I sweep temperature. Concretely I'll compute the KL divergence, or equivalently the cross-entropy up to the teacher's fixed entropy, at temperature $T$, multiply it by $T^2$, and add an optional small-weighted hard loss at $T=1$.

The numbers above only check the gradient algebra, not whether the transferred targets actually teach a student anything useful. MNIST is the place to look: the large regularized net learns invariances from dropout, weight constraints, and jittered inputs, while the transfer set for the small net can be the original unjittered cases. If matching softened outputs really transfers the input-to-distribution mapping, some of that invariance should ride along in the soft targets even though the student never sees a transformed image directly. A sharper version of the same test: drop a whole digit class from the transfer set. The student then has no hard examples of that class, but every other digit's softened target still assigns it some probability in proportion to visual similarity, and temperature makes those small probabilities non-negligible — so the missing class should be at least partly recoverable through cross-class similarity alone, rather than a total blind spot. Either result, if it held up under training, would separate the dark-knowledge story from a null world where the soft targets carry no real transferable information.

One more practical point for very small students. If the student is far too small to absorb everything, very high $T$ (matching all the negative logits) may be worse than an intermediate $T$ — limited capacity is better spent on the salient structure than on chasing nearly-unconstrained negative logits. So I expect a sweet spot in $T$ that gets lower as the student shrinks.

At implementation time the small model forward gives logits; I compute $KL(p_T\|q_T)$ between the large model's temperature-softmax targets and the small model's temperature-softmax outputs, scale it by $T^2$, optionally add ordinary cross-entropy against hard labels at $T=1$, and optimize with the large model frozen and the transfer set fed through both nets each step — that's the whole loop, and at deployment the small model just reverts to an ordinary $T=1$ softmax.
