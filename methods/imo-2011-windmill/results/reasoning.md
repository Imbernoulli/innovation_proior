Let me get a feel for what this process even does before I try to control it. I have a finite set $S$ of points, no three on a line, and a line that pivots: it rotates clockwise about a point of $S$ until it bumps into another point, then that new point becomes the pivot and the rotation continues. I want to start it somewhere so that, forever after, every single point keeps getting used as a pivot.

The first thing that worries me is that the process might fall into a small loop. Maybe the line just spins between two or three nearby points off in a corner of $S$ and never reaches the rest. So the real content of the problem is: there is *some* starting configuration from which the windmill is "global" — it sweeps the whole set. I should hunt for that starting configuration, and to do that I need to understand what stays the same as the line turns.

Let me try the smallest cases by hand. Two points $A, B$: the line through $A$ rotates clockwise, hits $B$, pivots at $B$, rotates clockwise, hits $A$ again, and so on. It alternates $A, B, A, B, \dots$ forever. Both points are used infinitely often, trivially. Three points $A, B, C$ forming a triangle: start the line through $A$. As it turns clockwise it will hit whichever of $B, C$ it reaches first; then pivot there; and intuitively the line "walks around" the triangle. It does seem to visit all three repeatedly. So for tiny sets it just works no matter where I start. The difficulty must be that for larger sets a *bad* start can trap the line, and I need to identify a *good* start.

What does the line actually do to the rest of the points as it turns? At any instant the line splits the plane into two half-planes (plus the line itself, which holds the pivot). So every point of $S$ other than the current pivot is on one side or the other. That partition is the only structure I have. Let me watch how it changes.

While the line sits at one pivot $P$ and rotates clockwise without hitting anyone, no point crosses the line — by definition, the moment a point would touch the line is exactly the moment we stop and swap pivots. So between swaps, the partition into two sides is completely frozen: the same points stay on the same sides. Good, that's stable. The only interesting events are the swaps.

So let me stare hard at a single swap. The line is pivoting clockwise about $P$, and it reaches a point $Q$. Now $Q$ becomes the pivot and the line keeps turning clockwise about $Q$. What happened to the two sides?

Right before the swap, $Q$ was sitting on one of the two sides. At the swap instant the line lies through both $P$ and $Q$. Right after, $P$ is no longer the pivot, so $P$ has to be on one of the two sides — and I need to know which one, because that's what controls whether the partition is preserved.

Let me set up coordinates at the swap instant to keep the sign honest. Put $Q$ at the origin and put $P=(a,0)$ with $a>0$. First take the arrow on the line to point from $Q$ toward $P$, so the contact direction is $(1,0)$ and "left" is measured by a positive signed area $\det(d,x-b)>0$ for a line through $b$ with oriented direction $d$.

Just before contact, the pivot is $P$, and because the line is rotating clockwise into the horizontal position, its direction is slightly counterclockwise from horizontal: $d_-=(\cos\epsilon,\sin\epsilon)$. The point I am about to hit is $Q$, so its signed side is
$$
\det(d_-,Q-P)=\det((\cos\epsilon,\sin\epsilon),(-a,0))=a\sin\epsilon>0.
$$
So $Q$ is on the left just before the swap. Just after contact, the pivot is $Q$, and the line has moved slightly clockwise: $d_+=(\cos\epsilon,-\sin\epsilon)$. Now the old pivot $P$ has signed side
$$
\det(d_+,P-Q)=\det((\cos\epsilon,-\sin\epsilon),(a,0))=a\sin\epsilon>0.
$$
So $P$ is also on the left just after the swap. If the arrow points the other way, both oriented direction vectors are negated, both signed areas change sign, and the word "left" becomes "right" in both computations. Either way, the old pivot lands on exactly the side that the new pivot occupied just before the hit.

Now the bookkeeping is clean. If $Q$ was on the left before the swap, then the left side loses $Q$ when $Q$ climbs onto the line as the new pivot, and gains $P$ when the old pivot drops off the line on that same left side. If $Q$ was on the right, the same one-for-one replacement happens on the right. The number of points of $S$ strictly on each side of the oriented line is invariant throughout the windmill, except at the swap instants themselves, when the line briefly contains two points.

This count gives me the control I was missing. Now I get to *choose* the starting line, so I should choose it to make the conserved count do the work for me.

What do I want the count to force? I want every point of $S$ to be a pivot. Away from a swap, the windmill line contains exactly one point of $S$, namely its current pivot; if I can force a non-swap moment when the line contains a target point $T$, then $T$ must be that pivot. So I need to convert "the side-count is fixed" into "the line is forced to be the particular line through $T$."

The count only helps if it pins the line's position. Suppose the windmill always keeps exactly $k$ points on the left. Now fix any target point $T \in S$ and an oriented direction. The windmill line always contains its current pivot, so I only need uniqueness among parallel lines that contain at least one point of $S$. Let $u$ be the left normal to this oriented direction, and write the parallel line as $u \cdot x=t$; its left side is $u \cdot x>t$, so its left-count is $\#\{X \in S: u \cdot X>t\}$. As $t$ increases through the finitely many values $u \cdot X$, that count strictly drops, by the number of points sitting at the crossed value. Thus along the point-containing parallel positions the left-count is strictly monotone, even if one such position contains two points. For a given oriented direction, at most one parallel position that contains point(s) of $S$ can have left-count $k$. If I can arrange that this left-count-$k$ position in $T$'s balanced direction is the line through $T$, then whenever the windmill's line points in that direction, the windmill line, which also contains a point of $S$ and also has left-count $k$, must coincide with it, hence pass through $T$.

So I want: for every point $T$, some oriented direction in which the unique left-count-$k$ line passes through $T$. And then I want the windmill, as it turns, to actually realize every direction again and again. I should be precise about that. After the first hit, every leg starts on a line through two points of $S$ and ends on another such line. The clockwise angle of each leg is positive, and the possible angles form a finite set, so there is a smallest positive leg angle. Infinitely many legs therefore force the accumulated clockwise rotation to go to infinity. Since the line moves continuously during each leg, every completed half-turn of accumulated motion contains every undirected direction, and every completed full turn contains every oriented direction.

Now I need to pick $k$. Let me first handle the clean case where $|S|$ is odd, say $|S| = 2n+1$. Then through any point $T$ I'd love a line with the remaining $2n$ points split evenly, $n$ on each side. Does such a line exist through every $T$? Take any oriented line through $T$ hitting no other point of $S$; say it has $n+r$ on the left (so $n-r$ on the right, since $T$ uses up the line and $2n$ remain). Rotate this line a full $180^\circ$ about $T$, and watch the open intervals between the finitely many moments when the line also passes through another point of $S$. On each such interval the line contains only $T$, and each time I cross one of those moments the left-count changes by exactly $+1$ or $-1$, because no three points are collinear. After a $180^\circ$ turn the oriented line has reversed direction, so left and right have traded places: the left-count is now $n-r$. The interval counts start at $n+r$, end at $n-r$, and move in unit steps, so one interval has left-count $n$. Since that interval is open, I can choose the direction inside it while avoiding the finitely many directions parallel to lines through two points of $S$. The chosen line contains only $T$, has $n$ points on the left and $n$ on the right, and its direction will not be one of the exceptional swap directions. So through every point $T$ there is an oriented line with $n$ points on each side. Set $k = n$.

Now start the windmill: pick $P$ arbitrarily, take a line through $P$ with $n$ on each side (exists, by what I just showed), give it an orientation, and let it spin. The conserved left-count is $n$ forever. Take any target $T$; choose a generic oriented direction $d_T$ in which the line through $T$ has $n$ on each side. In direction $d_T$, the line with $n$ on the left and containing a point of $S$ is unique, and it passes through $T$. The windmill always has $n$ on the left away from swap instants, and because $d_T$ is not a two-point direction, the moment when the windmill's oriented line points in direction $d_T$ is not a swap instant. So at that moment the windmill line must *be* the unique line through $T$, hence $T$ is the pivot. Since the windmill turns through every direction again and again, every point $T$ gets visited again and again.

Let me tighten the "every direction" part for the odd case. The windmill's oriented line, as it rotates clockwise, passes through all $180^\circ$ of undirected directions in each half-turn. Does it realize direction $d_T$ *with the right orientation*? The balanced split $n/n$ is symmetric: if a line has $n$ on each side, so does the same line with the reversed arrow — flipping the arrow swaps left and right but $n = n$. So I don't need to match orientation; I just need the windmill line to become *parallel* to $\ell_T := $ the balanced line through $T$, which it does in every completed half-turn since all undirected directions occur. The generic choice of $\ell_T$ keeps both orientations away from swap directions, so the invariant count applies at that moment. When the windmill line is parallel to $\ell_T$, it has $n$ on the left; the unique parallel line with $n$ on the left is $\ell_T$ itself (translation unbalances), so the windmill line *is* $\ell_T$ and passes through $T$. So each completed half-turn forces every point of $S$ to be a pivot.

Now the even case, $|S| = 2n$. The symmetric split is impossible: $T$ uses the line, leaving $2n-1$ points, an odd number, which can't split evenly. The best I can do is $n-1$ on one side and $n$ on the other. Let me check this is achievable through every $T$ with a chosen orientation. Use the same open-interval rotation argument: take an oriented line through $T$ avoiding other points, with $a$ on the left; the remaining count on the right is $2n-1-a$. Rotating $180^\circ$ flips the arrow, sending the left-count to $2n-1-a$, and the interval counts change by $+1$ or $-1$ at each event. The two endpoint counts have average $n-\frac12$, so one is at most $n-1$ and the other is at least $n$. The interval-count walk must pass through $n-1$. Choose a line from such an open interval, again avoiding the finitely many directions parallel to lines through two points of $S$. It has exactly $n-1$ on the left and, because no other point lies on the line, exactly $n$ on the right. So through every $T$ there is an oriented line with exactly $n-1$ on its left and $n$ on its right. Set $k = n-1$.

Start the windmill from such a line through an arbitrary $P$: conserved left-count $= n - 1$ forever (right-count $n$). Now the asymmetry bites in a useful way I have to respect: the split $(n-1, n)$ is *not* symmetric under flipping the arrow — reversing the orientation turns it into $(n, n-1)$, a *different* split. So unlike the odd case, I can't ignore orientation. For a target $T$, the construction gives an *oriented* line $\ell_T$ through $T$ with $n-1$ on the left (in $\ell_T$'s own orientation), and I choose it away from the swap directions. I need the windmill's oriented line to match $\ell_T$ *as an oriented line* — same direction and same arrow — because only then does "left-count $n-1$" pin it to $\ell_T$. As an undirected direction, $\ell_T$ occurs twice per full turn ($0^\circ$ and $180^\circ$ apart), but with opposite arrows. The windmill, turning clockwise, realizes each *oriented* direction once per full $360^\circ$. So I need a full $360^\circ$ to be sure the windmill's line points the right way with the right arrow. In every completed full turn, the windmill line attains the exact oriented direction of $\ell_T$; this is not a swap instant, so it has $n-1$ on its left, and the uniqueness from the parallel-line parameter $u \cdot x=t$ says it must be $\ell_T$. Hence the windmill line equals $\ell_T$ and passes through $T$. Because this chosen direction is not parallel to any two-point line, $\ell_T$ contains no point of $S$ except $T$; at that non-swap moment the current pivot is therefore $T$. So each completed full turn forces every point of $S$ to be a pivot.

Let me re-examine the one thing I leaned on hardest. For fixed orientation, the parameter $t$ in $u \cdot x=t$ orders all parallel lines, and the count $\#\{X \in S: u \cdot X>t\}$ strictly decreases as $t$ passes the point-values. So no two distinct parallel positions that contain point(s) of $S$ share the same left-count: the position is uniquely determined by the count $k$ together with the orientation, once I know the line contains a point of $S$. The no-three-collinear hypothesis still does real work in the rotation-about-$T$ argument, because a line through $T$ never meets two other points of $S$ at the same time, and the side-count changes there one point at a time. The target directions are chosen away from all two-point directions for a different reason: the forcing moment should not be a swap instant, and the line through $T$ should contain no other point of $S$.

So I have both parities from the same engine. I orient the line and track the number of points of $S$ on one chosen side. That number is conserved because at each pivot swap the old pivot drops onto exactly the side the new pivot just left, giving a loss of one point and a gain of one point on that side. I choose the start to balance the sides as evenly as the parity allows: $n$ on each side when $|S| = 2n+1$, or $(n-1, n)$ when $|S| = 2n$. For any target point $T$, the rotation-about-$T$ argument supplies an oriented line through $T$ carrying that same balanced count, and for a fixed orientation that balanced line is the unique one through a point of $S$. Whenever the endlessly turning windmill line reaches the needed orientation, it must pass through $T$. The accumulated rotation keeps increasing and runs through the same directions again and again, so every point is forced to be a pivot again and again. I can start from any balanced line through any point.
