I keep seeing the same repeated-decision problem appear in different notation. In experts, I choose a distribution before the cost vector arrives and I compare myself with the best fixed expert. In a repeated game, I choose a mixed strategy before the opponent's realized action fixes my payoff vector and I want no external regret. In online prediction, I update a weight vector after seeing the current loss. These are not really separate phenomena. I am always choosing a point in some convex set, then an adversarially revealed loss evaluates that point, and the right comparison is the best fixed point in hindsight.

I first need to avoid asking for an impossible objective. If I try to minimize the current loss on each round, I am asking to know `c^t` before it is revealed. That cannot be the target. The meaningful target is regret: after `T` rounds I compare `sum_t c^t(x^t)` with `min_{x in F} sum_t c^t(x)`, where the comparator is allowed to see all losses but must use one fixed feasible point. This is exactly the no-regret yardstick from games and experts, and it remains meaningful even when the sequence of losses is adversarial.

Now I look for the one piece of structure that survives this much adversarial freedom. Convexity gives it to me. If `x^t` is the point I play and `g^t = grad c^t(x^t)`, then for any comparator `x*`, the tangent inequality says `c^t(x*) >= c^t(x^t) + g^t dot (x* - x^t)`. Rearranging gives `c^t(x^t) - c^t(x*) <= g^t dot (x^t - x*)`. This is the first decisive simplification: to control regret for arbitrary convex losses, it is enough to control the cumulative linearized losses defined by the gradients at the points I actually play. The curved shape away from my point does not have to enter the proof.

Once the losses are effectively linear, the simplest movement to try is the one that reacts to the only local signal I have. After I see `c^t`, I move opposite `g^t`; if the move leaves the feasible set, I project back. So I consider `y^{t+1} = x^t - eta_t g^t` and then `x^{t+1} = P(y^{t+1})`. This is not ordinary offline convergence to a fixed optimum, because there is no fixed objective. It is a repeated-decision update whose purpose is to keep regret small against a fixed hindsight comparator.

To prove anything, I need a potential that connects this movement to the comparator. The squared distance `||x^t - x*||^2` is the obvious candidate. Before projection, expanding the square gives `||y^{t+1} - x*||^2 = ||x^t - x*||^2 - 2 eta_t g^t dot (x^t - x*) + eta_t^2 ||g^t||^2`. The regret term I need to sum appears as the middle term in this identity. Projection onto a closed convex set then helps rather than hurts: since `x*` is feasible, `||P(y^{t+1}) - x*|| <= ||y^{t+1} - x*||`. Thus the actual next point satisfies the same inequality with the distance only smaller.

Rearranging the one-step inequality gives `g^t dot (x^t - x*) <= (||x^t - x*||^2 - ||x^{t+1} - x*||^2)/(2 eta_t) + (eta_t/2)||g^t||^2`. This is the whole proof in one line. The first term is a potential drop, and the second term is the price of reacting after the loss is revealed. If every gradient norm is at most `G`, summing over time leaves a telescoping distance term plus `(G^2/2) sum_t eta_t`.

The telescoping needs a decreasing step schedule. With nonincreasing `eta_t`, Abel summation bounds the distance part by `D^2/(2 eta_T)`, where `D` is the diameter of the feasible set. Therefore the regret is at most `D^2/(2 eta_T) + (G^2/2) sum_{t=1}^T eta_t`. The two terms now tell me how to choose the learning rate. If I shrink too slowly, the accumulated response error is too large. If I shrink too quickly, the final inverse step size makes the initial geometry term too large. The balance is `eta_t` proportional to `1/sqrt(t)`, because then both `1/eta_T` and `sum_t eta_t` grow like `sqrt(T)`.

With the unscaled schedule `eta_t = t^{-1/2}`, this yields `R(T) <= (D^2/2)sqrt(T) + (sqrt(T) - 1/2)G^2`. If I scale the anytime schedule as `eta_t = D/(G sqrt(t))` when `D` and `G` are positive, the same inequality gives `R(T) <= (3/2)DG sqrt(T)`. If the horizon is known, the constant choice `eta = D/(G sqrt(T))` gives the cleaner `R(T) <= DG sqrt(T)`. If the feasible set has zero diameter or all gradients vanish, the problem is degenerate and the regret is already zero. In every nondegenerate case the average regret vanishes. The result depends on diameter and gradient bound, not on a stochastic model and not on the number of rounds through anything worse than `sqrt(T)`.

This also explains why the method is the right generalization of the older pieces. On the simplex with linear losses, the setting is exactly expert advice, but the proof uses Euclidean distance instead of an entropic weight potential. In a repeated game, the loss is negative utility and the feasible set is a mixed-strategy simplex, so the same update becomes projected gradient ascent on revealed utilities. That removes the two-action restriction from infinitesimal gradient ascent: the proof no longer studies a special `2 x 2` dynamical system, but a convex potential inequality that works in any dimension.

I also see why the `sqrt(T)` rate is not just a weakness of this proof. In a hypercube, let each loss be a random sign linear function. My online point is chosen before the sign vector is drawn, so its expected loss is zero, while the best fixed corner in hindsight gains on the order of `n sqrt(T)`. Since the hypercube has `D = 2 sqrt(n)` and the gradient bound is `G = sqrt(n)`, every algorithm has expected regret of order `DG sqrt(T)` on this instance. The projected-gradient regret order is therefore the right worst-case scale.

The final picture is that optimization is being made online by changing the question, not by pretending a moving sequence of losses has a single minimizer. I treat the learner as repeatedly committing feasible points against adversarially revealed convex losses, measure regret to the best fixed feasible point, linearize each loss at the played point, and use projection plus squared distance to telescope. The projected gradient update is the mechanism, but the distinctive insight is the regret-based adversarial formulation that makes that mechanism provable and unifies experts, online prediction, and gradient dynamics in games.
