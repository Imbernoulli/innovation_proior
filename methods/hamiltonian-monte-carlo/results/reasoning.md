I start with the same problem that ordinary Metropolis solves: I know a density only up to a constant, and I need a Markov transition that leaves it invariant. The ratio trick is already enough to remove the normalizing constant, so the real problem is not correctness in principle. The real problem is movement. If I keep proposing small symmetric displacements, the acceptance rule is simple, but the chain only diffuses. In a high-dimensional target, diffusion is fatal because the probability mass is not sitting at the mode. It is in a thin typical region where density and volume balance each other.

I try to make the random-walk proposal larger, but in high dimension almost every isotropic direction points out of that typical region. The candidate lands in the tails, the density ratio is tiny, and the Metropolis filter rejects it. I can shrink the proposal, and then I get acceptance, but now each accepted movement is only local. The displacement after many accepted moves grows like a square root. That is the first wall: the accept/reject correction is not the bottleneck; the uninformed proposal geometry is.

The natural next thought is to use the gradient. The gradient tells me where the density increases, so perhaps I can use it to steer proposals. But I have to be careful. The target mass is not the same as the target density. The gradient of the log density points toward the mode, while the typical region lies away from the mode in high dimension. If I simply climb the gradient, I do not move along the important region; I fall inward. So the gradient is useful information, but it is pointing in the wrong direction for exploration.

I need a way to rotate that gradient information into motion that stays near the typical region. Mechanics gives exactly such a device. A particle in a potential does not only have a position; it has momentum. Gravity alone pulls inward, but momentum carries the particle across the force field, and the exchange between potential and kinetic energy can produce long orbits instead of crashes. I can translate this into probability by introducing an auxiliary momentum variable `p` for every position coordinate `q`.

The lift must not change the target distribution. I define the potential energy as `U(q) = -log pi(q)` up to an additive constant, then introduce a kinetic energy, usually `K(p) = 1/2 p^T M^{-1} p`. The joint energy is `H(q,p)=U(q)+K(p)`. The corresponding joint density is proportional to `exp(-H(q,p))`, which factors into `exp(-U(q)) exp(-K(p))`. That factorization is crucial: the marginal distribution of `q` is still the target, and `p` is just a Gaussian auxiliary variable. I have added room to move without changing what I ultimately sample.

Now I look at the dynamics generated by this energy. Hamilton's equations give `dq/dt = partial H / partial p = M^{-1}p` and `dp/dt = -partial H / partial q = -grad U(q)`. This is the twist I need. The gradient no longer updates position directly. It updates momentum, and momentum updates position. The motion can therefore travel across the target geometry instead of marching straight toward the mode.

The exact dynamics has three properties that make it almost a perfect proposal. First, it conserves the Hamiltonian, because the rate of change of `H` is the sum of matching products that cancel. If `H` is constant, then `exp(-H)` is constant along the trajectory. A proposal generated by exact dynamics would have acceptance probability one. Second, the dynamics preserves volume in phase space. That means the proposal map does not compress or expand probability volume, so I do not have to pay a Jacobian factor in the Metropolis-Hastings ratio. Third, the dynamics is reversible: if I flip the momentum and run the same flow, I retrace the path.

If exact Hamiltonian flow were available, I would be nearly done. I could resample momentum, follow the flow for a while, flip momentum to make the reverse proposal explicit, and project back to `q`. But exact solutions are rarely available. I have to approximate the trajectory, and the approximation cannot destroy the two properties that the acceptance proof needs. It must remain volume preserving and reversible even at finite step size.

Euler's method fails this test. On the simplest quadratic energy it spirals outward instead of staying on a circle. That tells me it is not respecting the geometry that made the proposal attractive. A modified Euler step is better because it is a shear: update momentum using position, then update position using the new momentum. Shears preserve volume. To recover reversibility as well, I symmetrize the update: half a momentum kick, a full position drift, and another half momentum kick. This is the leapfrog step.

The leapfrog step is not exactly energy conserving. That sounds dangerous at first, because the whole idea depends on conserving `H`. But the error is controlled in the right way. The step is still reversible and volume preserving exactly, and its energy error stays small for stable step sizes. So I can let leapfrog produce the proposal and then use a single Metropolis correction at the end to remove the residual energy error.

The acceptance probability now has a clean form. If the current phase-space state is `(q,p)` and the leapfrog trajectory with final momentum flip proposes `(q*,p*)`, I accept with

```text
min(1, exp(-H(q*,p*) + H(q,p))).
```

Since `H=U+K`, this is the same as

```text
min(1, exp(U(q) - U(q*) + K(p) - K(p*))).
```

This settles one trajectory, but it does not settle the chain. Suppose every iteration were just resample-nothing, run-leapfrog, accept-or-reject, repeated forever. That would be reversible and almost energy conserving, and reversible-and-almost-conserving is itself a trap. Exact dynamics keeps `H` exactly fixed, and leapfrog's error over one trajectory stays small, so `H` barely drifts from whatever value it started with. Since `K(p)` is a non-negative quadratic form, and `U(q)` can always be shifted by its own additive constant so that its minimum is zero without changing any acceptance ratio (only energy differences ever enter the ratio), `U(q)` can then never exceed the initial `H(q,p)`. A chain that starts near the mode, where `U` is small, would stay confined to small `U` forever. A chain that starts far out would never fall back toward the mode. Running leapfrog and accepting or rejecting only samples the conditional distribution of `(q,p)` on whatever energy shell the chain began on; nothing in that step can carry it to a different shell.

So the momentum cannot simply ride along from one trajectory to the next. It has to be thrown away and redrawn. Between trajectories I replace `p` with a fresh draw from its Gaussian marginal, independent of `q`. Because `p` is independent of `q` under `exp(-H(q,p))`, drawing a new `p` from that same marginal while leaving `q` untouched is a Gibbs step for the joint canonical distribution: it trivially leaves `exp(-H(q,p))` invariant, since `q` does not move and `p` is replaced by a sample from its own correct conditional. But it changes `H` by an arbitrary amount, handing the next trajectory a different energy shell to explore. Refresh-the-momentum and run-leapfrog-then-accept are each, on their own, invariant for the joint canonical distribution, so their composition is invariant too, and now nothing confines the chain to a single shell. Marginalizing `p` out of that composed chain at any point recovers `pi(q)`.

One more failure mode is hiding even after the refresh is in place. The leapfrog map has to actually go somewhere on every trajectory, and near-exact solutions of the underlying continuous dynamics can be periodic. For a quadratic energy the true orbit in phase space is a circle traced out with period `2 pi`, and a leapfrog trajectory shadows that circle closely. If `epsilon` and `L` are fixed so that `epsilon L` lands close to `2 pi`, or any multiple of it, the trajectory nearly closes on itself and the proposal lands close to where it started, no matter how many steps are taken - even though every individual step is a valid, reversible, volume-preserving move. The fix costs nothing: jitter `epsilon` or `L` a little on every iteration, so the same unlucky period is never hit twice in a row.

Is any of this elaborate machinery actually worth it over a plain random walk? I check by comparing how the cost of reaching a roughly independent sample grows with dimension `d`. Model the target as `d` replicated, independent coordinates, `U(q) = sum u_i(q_i)`, so a Metropolis-type step touches each coordinate through its own energy term. For any Metropolis update sampling `exp(-E)`, detailed balance forces `E[exp(-Delta)] = 1` where `Delta` is the proposed-minus-current energy; Jensen's inequality then gives `E[Delta] >= 0`, and once the proposal is tuned so `Delta` is typically small, a second-order expansion of `exp(-Delta)` sharpens that to `E[Delta] ~ Var[Delta]/2`. So keeping the acceptance rate away from zero as `d` grows means keeping the total energy change `E[Delta_d]`, summed over all `d` coordinates, pinned near a constant rather than letting it grow with `d`.

For random-walk Metropolis with a fixed per-coordinate proposal standard deviation `sigma`, expanding `U` to second order around the current point gives `E[Delta_1]` proportional to `sigma^2` for a single coordinate, so `E[Delta_d] = d * E[Delta_1]` is proportional to `d sigma^2`. Holding that at a constant forces `sigma` proportional to `d^{-1/2}`, and since the walk explores by accumulated square-root displacement, reaching a nearly independent point costs about `sigma^{-2}`, proportional to `d`, iterations; each iteration touches all `d` coordinates, so the total work grows like `d^2`.

For HMC, the `(q_i, p_i)` pairs evolve independently along a trajectory, so the length of the trajectory in fictitious time never has to grow with `d`. Only the stepsize does. The leapfrog error in `H` over a trajectory of fixed length is order `epsilon^2` per pair, so the per-coordinate energy error `Delta_1` is proportional to `epsilon^2`, and by the `Var ~ 2*mean` relation, `E[Delta_1]` is proportional to `Delta_1^2`, hence to `epsilon^4`. Summed over `d` coordinates, `E[Delta_d]` is proportional to `d epsilon^4`. Holding that constant this time forces `epsilon` proportional to `d^{-1/4}`, not `d^{-1/2}`. Reaching an independent point then costs about `epsilon^{-1}`, proportional to `d^{1/4}`, leapfrog steps, and since a gradient evaluation still costs `O(d)`, the total work grows like `d^{5/4}` - far below the random walk's `d^2`, even though each leapfrog step is individually more expensive than a random-walk proposal. Numerically optimizing the resulting cost curves also pins down the acceptance rate that should be tuned for: about `0.65` for HMC against about `0.23` for random-walk Metropolis. HMC is worth tuning to reject noticeably more often than the classic random-walk folklore suggests, because it is paying for large, structured jumps instead of small aimless ones.

That is the whole payoff for grafting deterministic mechanics onto a Markov chain. Deterministic dynamics alone already knows how to move coherently through an energy landscape, but drifts under discretization error with no way to correct it. A random walk alone already knows how to fix up an approximate proposal into an exact one, but cannot avoid diffusing. Composed together - a Metropolis test absorbing the leapfrog error, and momentum refreshment stopping the chain from being trapped on one energy shell - the result moves through the typical set at a cost that grows only as `d^{5/4}` instead of `d^2`.
