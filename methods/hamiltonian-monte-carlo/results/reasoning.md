I start with the same problem that ordinary Metropolis solves: I know a density only up to a constant, and I need a Markov transition that leaves it invariant. The ratio trick is already enough to remove the normalizing constant, so the real problem is not correctness in principle. The real problem is movement. If I keep proposing small symmetric displacements, the acceptance rule is simple, but the chain only diffuses. In a high-dimensional target, diffusion is fatal because the probability mass is not sitting at the mode. It is in a thin typical region where density and volume balance each other.

I try to make the random-walk proposal larger, but in high dimension almost every isotropic direction points out of that typical region. The candidate lands in the tails, the density ratio is tiny, and the Metropolis filter rejects it. I can shrink the proposal, and then I get acceptance, but now each accepted movement is only local. The displacement after many accepted moves grows like a square root. That is the first wall: the accept/reject correction is not the bottleneck; the uninformed proposal geometry is.

The natural next thought is to use the gradient. The gradient tells me where the density increases, so perhaps I can use it to steer proposals. But I have to be careful. The target mass is not the same as the target density. The gradient of the log density points toward the mode, while the typical region lies away from the mode in high dimension. If I simply climb the gradient, I do not move along the important region; I fall inward. So the gradient is useful information, but it is pointing in the wrong direction for exploration.

I need a way to rotate that gradient information into motion that stays near the typical region. Mechanics gives exactly such a device. A particle in a potential does not only have a position; it has momentum. Gravity alone pulls inward, but momentum carries the particle across the force field, and the exchange between potential and kinetic energy can produce long orbits instead of crashes. I can translate this into probability by introducing an auxiliary momentum variable `p` for every position coordinate `q`.

The lift must not change the target distribution. I define the potential energy as `U(q) = -log pi(q)` up to an additive constant, then introduce a kinetic energy, usually `K(p) = 1/2 p^T M^{-1} p`. The joint energy is `H(q,p)=U(q)+K(p)`. The corresponding joint density is proportional to `exp(-H(q,p))`, which factors into `exp(-U(q)) exp(-K(p))`. That factorization is crucial: the marginal distribution of `q` is still the target, and `p` is just a Gaussian auxiliary variable. I have added room to move without changing what I ultimately sample.

Now I look at the dynamics generated by this energy. Hamilton's equations give `dq/dt = partial H / partial p = M^{-1}p` and `dp/dt = -partial H / partial q = -grad U(q)`. This is the twist I need. The gradient no longer updates position directly. It updates momentum, and momentum updates position. The motion can therefore travel across the target geometry instead of marching straight toward the mode.

The exact dynamics has three properties that make it almost a perfect proposal. First, it conserves the Hamiltonian, because the rate of change of `H` is the sum of matching products that cancel. If `H` is constant, then `exp(-H)` is constant along the trajectory. A proposal generated by exact dynamics would have acceptance probability one. Second, the dynamics preserves volume in phase space. That means the proposal map does not compress or expand probability volume, so I do not have to pay a Jacobian factor in the Metropolis-Hastings ratio. Third, the dynamics is reversible: if I flip the momentum and run the same flow, I retrace the path.

If exact Hamiltonian flow were available, I would be nearly done. I could resample momentum, follow the flow for a while, flip momentum to make the reverse proposal explicit, and project back to `q`. But exact solutions are rarely available. I have to approximate the trajectory, and the approximation cannot destroy the two properties that the acceptance proof needs. It must remain volume preserving and reversible even at finite step size.

Euler's method fails this test. On the simplest quadratic energy it spirals outward instead of staying on a circle. That tells me it is not respecting the geometry that made the proposal attractive. A modified Euler step is better because it is a shear: update momentum using position, then update position using the new momentum. Shears preserve volume. To recover reversibility as well, I symmetrize the update: half a momentum kick, a full position drift, and another half momentum kick. This is the leapfrog step.

The leapfrog step is not exactly energy conserving. That sounds dangerous at first, because the whole idea depends on conserving `H`. But the error is controlled in the right way. The step is still reversible and volume preserving exactly, and its energy error stays small for stable step sizes. So I can let leapfrog produce the proposal and then use a single Metropolis correction at the end to remove the residual energy error.

The acceptance probability now has a clean form. If the current phase-space state is `(q,p)` and the leapfrog trajectory with final momentum flip proposes `(q*,p*)`, I accept with

```text
min(1, exp(-H(q*,p*) + H(q,p))).
```

Since `H=U+K`, this is the same as

```text
min(1, exp(U(q) - U(q*) + K(p) - K(p*))).
```

The proposal density cancels because the deterministic map is reversible, and there is no Jacobian because the map preserves volume. The correction is only paying for the numerical error in energy, not for the long movement itself.

I still need the chain to move between energy levels. A single deterministic trajectory, even an approximate one, stays near one level of `H`. If I never refresh momentum, the chain can remain trapped by its initial energy. So each iteration begins by drawing fresh `p` from its Gaussian distribution. This is just a Gibbs update for the joint canonical density, because `p` is independent of `q` under the additive Hamiltonian. Then the trajectory explores one level set coherently. The refresh changes the level; the trajectory moves across it. The composition leaves the joint density invariant, and the `q` marginal is the target.

The method's tuning now follows from the same reasoning. The step size must be below the stability limit of the narrowest direction. In the one-dimensional quadratic case `U(q)=q^2/(2 sigma^2)` with unit kinetic energy, the leapfrog update is stable when `epsilon/sigma < 2`; above that, the eigenvalues become real and one grows. In more complex targets the same idea remains: high curvature demands smaller steps. If the step is too large, the energy error explodes and the Metropolis correction rejects almost everything.

The trajectory length has the opposite role. It should be long enough that the momentum carries the position across the broad directions of the typical region. If it is too short, I am back to a random walk. If it is too long, the path can turn around and spend computation returning toward places it has already explored. Randomizing the step size or the number of steps helps avoid exact periodicities that would otherwise make some coordinates move poorly.

The mass matrix is a geometric preconditioner. With `K(p)=1/2 p^T M^{-1}p`, the distribution of momenta and the velocity `M^{-1}p` depend on `M`. If `M` is matched to the target's covariance structure, the transformed energy levels are more regular and the same trajectory length can explore more directions evenly. If `M` is badly chosen, some directions are too slow or too unstable.

The payoff is that long moves no longer have to be low-acceptance moves. The proposal can travel far because it follows an approximate constant-energy path, and the Metropolis test only sees the small leftover energy error. For independent replicated dimensions, random-walk Metropolis must shrink its proposal scale like `d^{-1/2}` to keep acceptance reasonable, leading to about `d^2` computation for an independent point. Here the leapfrog energy error leads to a step size like `d^{-1/4}`, so the number of leapfrog steps grows like `d^{1/4}` and the total gradient-cost computation grows like `d^{5/4}`. The mechanism is exactly the one I needed at the start: use Hamiltonian dynamics to propose long-distance Markov-chain moves with high acceptance by conserving energy as closely as possible, then correct the remaining error exactly.
