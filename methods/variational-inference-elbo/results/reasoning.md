I want the posterior, but every direct route keeps circling the same missing denominator. I can write `p(x,z)` cheaply, and I can evaluate it at any hidden configuration, yet `p(z | x)` asks me to divide by `p(x)`. If I try to compute that evidence exactly, I am summing or integrating over the whole hidden space. If I build a junction tree, the graph can hand me a clique so large that exact inference becomes just another name for enumeration. If I sample, I avoid the sum, but now I have convergence questions and a cloud of draws instead of a compact distribution I can reuse.

So I change the object I am solving for. I stop asking for the posterior itself and ask for a tractable distribution `q(z)` that can stand in for it. That is already a different kind of inference problem: the unknown is no longer a normalization constant but a distribution inside a family I choose. The family has to be simple enough that I can compute expectations and entropy under it, but flexible enough that it can land near the posterior. The problem becomes: among all tractable `q`, which one is closest to `p(z | x)`?

The natural closeness measure is `KL(q || p(z | x))`, because its expectation is under `q`. That direction is important. If I used the reverse direction, I would need expectations under the true posterior, which is the thing I cannot compute. But even the forward direction seems to fail at first. Expanding the KL gives `E_q[log q(z)] - E_q[log p(z | x)]`, and the posterior log density contains `log p(x)`. I appear to have hidden the same impossible evidence inside the objective.

The way out is to notice that the impossible term is not a function of `q`. Substituting `log p(z | x) = log p(x,z) - log p(x)` gives `KL(q || p(z | x)) = E_q[log q(z)] - E_q[log p(x,z)] + log p(x)`. The last term is unknown, but it is constant while I search over `q`. Therefore minimizing the KL is exactly the same optimization as maximizing `E_q[log p(x,z)] - E_q[log q(z)]`. The direct posterior problem has turned into a computable optimization problem over a tractable distribution.

This also gives me a bound, not as a separate trick but as the same identity viewed from the other side. Rearranging gives `log p(x) = E_q[log p(x,z)] - E_q[log q(z)] + KL(q || p(z | x))`. Since the KL is nonnegative, the computable term is a lower bound on the log evidence. The slack in the bound is exactly the posterior KL. If I raise the bound, I reduce the gap to the true posterior within my family. This is the crucial conversion: optimizing the bound is posterior approximation, and posterior approximation is optimizing the bound.

Jensen tells the same story in a way that makes the lower-bound direction feel inevitable. I can write `p(x)` as `E_q[p(x,z) / q(z)]`. The log is concave, so moving it inside gives `log p(x) >= E_q[log p(x,z) - log q(z)]`. Convex duality tells the same story for log-sum-exp: the price of introducing a distribution over configurations is an entropy term. These views agree because they are all exposing the same KL slack.

Now I still need a family that makes the optimization real. A completely unrestricted `q` would recover the exact posterior, but that is just the original impossible problem in disguise. The blunt but useful move is to factorize: `q(z) = product_j q_j(z_j)`. This throws away posterior dependence, so I should expect variance underestimation and missed correlations. But the gain is that expectations and entropy split into manageable pieces, and I can update one factor while holding the others fixed.

I look at the objective as a function of one factor `q_j`. Everything that does not depend on `z_j` can be absorbed into a constant. What remains is `E_j[E_-j[log p(x,z)]] - E_j[log q_j(z_j)]`. This has the form of a negative KL from `q_j` to a density proportional to `exp(E_-j[log p(x,z)])`. So the best single-factor update is not a gradient step and not a guess. It is the normalized exponentiated expected log joint.

That update also shows why this connects to Gibbs sampling without being sampling. A Gibbs sampler draws `z_j` from the complete conditional `p(z_j | z_-j, x)`. Here I keep a full factor `q_j`, and I set it from the expected log of that same complete conditional under the other factors. The random local draw becomes a deterministic local distribution update. I am using the same local model structure, but averaging the surrounding variables rather than sampling them.

The method becomes especially clean when the complete conditionals are in an exponential family. Suppose the conditional for `z_j` has natural parameter `eta_j(z_-j, x)`. Taking the expectation over the other factors only changes that natural parameter into `E_-j[eta_j(z_-j, x)]`. The updated `q_j` stays in the same family. So I do not have to solve a functional optimization each time; I update natural parameters by computing expected sufficient statistics.

This is exactly the kind of mechanism the older mean-field physics idea was pointing toward. Peterson and Anderson replace stochastic Boltzmann correlations by deterministic mean-field equations, and the independence approximation turns correlations into products of means. That gives a model-specific fixed-point recipe. The KL and lower-bound derivation turns the recipe into a general posterior-approximation principle: choose the tractable family, optimize the bound, and read off self-consistent coordinate updates.

The EM free-energy view fits into the same picture. If I also have parameters `theta`, the objective `E_q[log p(x,z | theta)] + H(q)` is a lower bound on `log p(x | theta)`. When `q` can be any distribution, the best `q` is the exact posterior, so the E step makes the bound tight and the M step optimizes the expected complete-data log likelihood. When the posterior is intractable, I restrict `q` and do a partial E step inside the tractable family. EM is the exact-posterior special case of the same coordinate-ascent objective.

I have to keep the limitations visible. The bound is usually non-convex in the variational parameters, so coordinate ascent reaches a local optimum. The factorization can prefer one mode and ignore dependence. The direction `KL(q || p)` is chosen because it is computable, but that same direction tends to be mode-seeking. Multiple starts and monitoring the bound are not optional polish; they are part of using the method responsibly.

The final picture is coherent. I replace direct normalization by a search over distributions. The computable target is `E_q[log p(x,z)] + H(q)`, not because I like that formula, but because it is exactly `log p(x)` minus the KL from my approximation to the true posterior. Then I choose a family whose expectations are tractable, use mean-field factorization to split the optimization, and update each factor by the expected log complete conditional. Posterior inference has become optimization over a tractable distribution, with the evidence bound as both the objective and the diagnostic of progress.
