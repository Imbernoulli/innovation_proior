I start from the comparison that bothers me: a modern hierarchical vision Transformer is beating the residual ConvNet that used to be the default backbone, and the field is tempted to explain the whole gap as self-attention. That explanation is too coarse. The two systems differ in training recipe, stage layout, stem, downsampling, activation choice, normalization choice, block shape, and spatial mixing. If I compare them only as finished systems, I cannot know which difference matters.

So I make the comparison surgical. I keep the small model in the ResNet-50 / Swin-T compute regime, around 4.5 GFLOPs, and I change the ConvNet one family of choices at a time. If the ConvNet remains far behind after I import the non-attention choices, then attention is the likely missing primitive. If the ConvNet catches up without attention, then the system gap was carried by the surrounding design bundle. I do not know in advance which of those two outcomes I will get; the point of the procedure is to find out.

The first confound is training. A plain ResNet-50 number around 76.13 top-1 is not a fair opponent for a Transformer-era backbone. I retrain the same architecture with the modern recipe: AdamW, 300 epochs rather than 90, 20 epochs of linear warmup, cosine decay, batch size 4096, weight decay 0.05, Mixup, Cutmix, RandAugment, Random Erasing, label smoothing, and stochastic depth. For the stepwise ablations I keep EMA off, because BatchNorm variants are still in the path and EMA interacts badly with them here. This alone moves the ResNet-50 from 76.13 to 78.82 +- 0.07. Before I touch the architecture, 2.69 points of the gap are already training procedure. That number is large enough that I have to take it seriously: it means any later architectural gain I measure is a gain on top of an already-strong baseline, not on top of the stale 76.13.

Now I can change architecture. I begin at the macro level, because compute placement changes the interpretation of everything downstream. The residual baseline has block counts `(3, 4, 6, 3)`. The hierarchical Transformer I am matching has the stage ratio `1:1:3:1`, so I change the block allocation to `(3, 3, 9, 3)`. That moves the number from 78.82 to 79.36 at 4.53 GFLOPs. This is not a huge gain, but it shows that the old residual allocation is not sacred.

Next is the stem. A standard residual stem uses a 7x7 stride-2 convolution and then a 3x3 max pool, producing the initial 4x downsampling by two overlapping operations. The Transformer-style alternative is a patchify projection: one 4x4 convolution with stride 4. Before I trust that swap, I want to be sure the two stems leave the rest of the network with the same spatial grid, otherwise every later stage's resolution and FLOP count would shift and the comparison would be contaminated. On a 224 input, the residual stem does 224 -> 112 (stride-2 conv) -> 56 (stride-2 pool); the patchify stem does 224 / 4 = 56 directly. Both hand stage 1 a 56x56 grid, so they are genuinely interchangeable as 4x downsamplers and only the *manner* of downsampling differs. I replace the stem with the non-overlapping projection. The number moves from 79.36 to 79.51 and FLOPs stay in the same regime. That tiny gain is still useful because the simpler stem is not worse.

The next question is spatial and channel mixing. A dense 3x3 convolution mixes space and channels together. A depthwise convolution mixes only space, one channel at a time; a 1x1 convolution mixes only channels at a fixed spatial position. That separation looks much closer to the Transformer block, where attention is the token/spatial mixer and the MLP is the channel mixer. I first make the 3x3 convolution depthwise. I want to know roughly how much compute that removes before I run it, so I count multiply-adds for one representative tensor, a 28x28 stage with C = 256 channels. A dense 3x3 with the same in/out width costs `H*W * C * (C * 3 * 3) = 28*28 * 256 * (256*9) = 4.62e8` MACs; the depthwise 3x3 costs `H*W * C * (3*3) = 28*28 * 256 * 9 = 1.81e6` MACs. The ratio is exactly 256 — depthwise is `1/C` of the dense spatial-mixing cost. So depthwise does not just trim a little; it deletes almost the entire convolution's compute. That tells me ahead of time that depthwise alone must shed accuracy with the FLOPs, and that the natural repair is to spend the freed compute back as width. The experiment confirms the first half: depthwise alone drops accuracy to 78.28 and FLOPs to 2.35G, exactly the steep fall the count predicts. ResNeXt already prescribes the second half — after increasing grouping, widen — so I raise the base width from 64 to 96, matching the tiny hierarchical Transformer width. The number jumps to 80.50 at 5.27G. The useful move is not depthwise by itself, which is worse; it is depthwise spatial mixing plus compensating channel width.

I then look at the block shape. A residual bottleneck normally goes wide-to-narrow-to-wide inside the block. A Transformer MLP goes the other direction: hidden dimension is 4 times the input dimension, then it projects back. MobileNetV2 gives the ConvNet analogue with an inverted residual. I use expansion 4, not MobileNetV2's expansion 6, because I am matching the Transformer MLP ratio. The block now has the narrow endpoints connected by the residual branch and a 4x channel expansion inside. The result is 80.64 at 4.64G. The FLOPs fall relative to the previous step because the expensive 1x1s that used to sit on the wide shortcut/downsampling path move out of that shape, and the accuracy slightly improves.

This inverted shape also tells me where the block's cost now lives, which matters for the next decision. At C = 96, the two 1x1 channel-mixing layers cost `C*(4C) + (4C)*C = 8C^2 = 73,728` MACs per spatial position, while a depthwise 7x7 spatial mixer costs only `C*49 = 4,704`. Channel mixing outweighs spatial mixing by about 16x. So the spatial kernel is the cheap part of the block: I can enlarge it substantially and barely move the FLOP budget. That is the headroom I will use next.

But first the kernel has to be in the right place. In a Transformer block, the token mixer comes before the MLP. In an inverted ConvNet block, the endpoints are narrow and the middle is wide. If the spatial operator is the awkward, FLOP-light one, it should act before the channel expansion, while the tensor is still narrow. I move the depthwise convolution to the top of the block. By itself this hurts: 80.64 becomes 79.92 and FLOPs fall to 4.07G. I do not keep that as a final answer; I read it as the enabling move for larger kernels, since the count above says the larger kernel is nearly free here.

Now I sweep the depthwise kernel. With the moved-up depthwise convolution, kernel size 3 gives 79.92. Kernel 5 gives 80.35. Kernel 7 gives 80.57 at 4.15G. Kernel 9 stays at 80.57, and kernel 11 drops slightly to 80.47. The FLOPs confirm the cost prediction: from kernel 3 to kernel 11 the budget only creeps from 4.07G to 4.29G, exactly the near-flat curve the 16x channel-vs-spatial ratio implies. The accuracy sign is clear: bigger than 3 helps, but the benefit saturates at 7 in this small regime. In the larger ResNet-200 / Swin-B regime, the saturation arrives even earlier, around 5 to 7, so I do not have evidence for pushing to 9 or 11. I settle on a 7x7 depthwise convolution because it captures the local-window scale without paying for larger kernels that do not improve the metric.

The remaining changes are micro design. First, I replace ReLU with GELU. That hardly matters by itself: 80.57 becomes 80.62, within the same neighborhood. So the identity of the activation is not the major lever.

The number of activations turns out to matter much more. The old ConvNet habit is to put an activation after nearly every convolution. A Transformer block has only one activation in its MLP, between the two linear layers. I remove all block activations except the one between the two 1x1 channel-mixing layers. This moves 80.62 to 81.27. That is a large gain, and it surprises me enough that I read it the other way to be sure I have the direction right: the block was not under-nonlinear and helped by *adding* nonlinearity; it was over-saturated with activations, and helped by *removing* them. The 0.65-point jump is the largest single micro-design move so far.

I apply the same pressure to normalization. A conventional bottleneck carries multiple BatchNorm layers. I leave one normalization before the first 1x1 layer and remove the others. That moves 81.27 to 81.41. I also check the tempting extra norm at the beginning of the block, but it does not improve the result, so I keep one norm inside the block.

Then I switch the kind of norm. In an unchanged residual ConvNet, replacing BatchNorm with LayerNorm is known to be a bad naive swap. The reason I am willing to revisit it is that the block is no longer that original block: it now has depthwise spatial mixing, sparse activations, and a single normalization. In this remodeled block, LayerNorm trains cleanly and moves 81.41 to 81.47. The old failure was about the old architecture, not a law against LayerNorm in ConvNets.

The last architectural issue is downsampling. The residual baseline downsamples inside the first block of a stage, with stride in the residual branch and in the shortcut. The hierarchical Transformer uses a separate downsampling operation between stages. I try the ConvNet analogue: a standalone 2x2 stride-2 convolution between stages. The first run of this diverges — the loss does not settle and the model does not train. This is a genuine wall, not a small regression, so I have to understand it rather than tune around it. The pattern is that the failure appears precisely where the resolution changes, which are the points where activation statistics shift most abruptly. That points at normalization placement: with the old in-block downsampling, a norm always sat near each resolution change; with a bare standalone conv, those boundaries are now unnormalized. So I add a normalization at every resolution-change boundary: after the stem, before each downsampling convolution, and after the final global average pool. With those boundary LayerNorms, training is stable and the ablation reaches 81.97 +- 0.06 at 4.49G, above the 81.30 Swin-T reference in the same table. So at this point the controlled ConvNet has, without any attention, passed the Swin-T it was being measured against — which answers the original question: the gap was carried by the design bundle, not by self-attention.

The larger regime follows the same direction and helps check that I am not tuning a tiny accident. Starting from the ResNet-200 regime, the enhanced recipe gives 81.14. The stage-ratio/width adjustment gives 81.33, patchify gives 81.59, depthwise alone drops to 80.54, widening recovers to 81.85, inverted dimensions reach 82.64, moving the depthwise layer up drops to 82.04, kernel 5 reaches 82.32, kernel 7 is 82.30, kernel 9 is 82.27, and kernel 11 is 82.18. Then GELU gives 82.19, fewer activations 82.71, fewer norms 83.17, LayerNorm 83.35, and separate downsampling reaches 83.60 against a Swin-B reference of 83.50. The signs are consistent with the small regime: depthwise alone hurts, width is needed, large kernels saturate (here even earlier, peaking by kernel 5-7), sparse activations and norms help, and boundary normalization is required for separate downsampling.

Now I can state the block that all of this points to. It begins with a 7x7 depthwise convolution, because spatial mixing should happen before the expansion and should use the saturated useful kernel size. It permutes to channels-last, applies one LayerNorm over channels, applies a linear 4x expansion, one GELU, and a linear projection back to the original dimension. Then it multiplies the residual branch by a per-channel learnable scale initialized to `1e-6`, applies stochastic depth, and adds the input. The scale is the LayerScale idea: start each residual branch close to zero contribution, so the network begins near an identity stack and learns the branch magnitude per channel.

Before I trust this block I trace it on a concrete tensor, because the channels-last/channels-first permutes and the residual add are exactly the kind of detail that is easy to get subtly wrong. I feed a `(2, 96, 56, 56)` tensor through one block at dim 96. The depthwise conv keeps it `(2, 96, 56, 56)`; the permute to channels-last makes it `(2, 56, 56, 96)`; the first linear expands the last axis to `(2, 56, 56, 384)`; GELU and the second linear bring it back to `(2, 56, 56, 96)`; the permute back gives `(2, 96, 56, 56)`, which matches the input, so the residual add is well-formed and the block is shape-preserving for any stage width. I also check the LayerScale claim numerically: with `gamma = 1e-6` at initialization, the maximum absolute difference between the block's output and its input over the whole tensor is about `1.0e-6`. So at the start of training the block really is an identity to within the scale of gamma, which is the behavior LayerScale is supposed to give, and confirms why initializing the residual branch near zero lets a deep stack train from a near-identity starting point.

The network around the block is equally specific. The stem is a 4x4 stride-4 convolution followed by channels-first LayerNorm. There are four stages. Tiny uses depths `[3, 3, 9, 3]` and dimensions `[96, 192, 384, 768]`; Small uses `[3, 3, 27, 3]` with the same dimensions; Base uses `[128, 256, 512, 1024]`; Large uses `[192, 384, 768, 1536]`; XLarge uses `[256, 512, 1024, 2048]`. Between stages, the downsampling layer is channels-first LayerNorm followed by a 2x2 stride-2 convolution. After the fourth stage, global average pooling over the last two dimensions is followed by an `nn.LayerNorm` and then the linear classifier.

The implementation details matter because they are easy to get subtly wrong. The block's 1x1 convolutions are implemented as `nn.Linear` after permuting to `(N, H, W, C)`, then the tensor is permuted back to `(N, C, H, W)`. The custom LayerNorm must support both channels-last and channels-first layouts. In channels-last it can call `F.layer_norm`; in channels-first it computes the mean and variance over dimension 1, uses `sqrt(s + eps)`, and applies `weight[:, None, None]` plus `bias[:, None, None]`. The epsilon is `1e-6`. The depthwise convolution uses `kernel_size=7`, `padding=3`, and `groups=dim`. Stochastic-depth rates are linearly spaced from 0 to the requested maximum across all blocks. Convolution and linear weights use truncated normal initialization with `std=.02`, biases are zeroed, and the classifier weight and bias are multiplied by `head_init_scale`.

So the reasoning resolves cleanly, and against the original outcomes the result lands on the second one: I do not need to insert attention to close the controlled gap, because the ConvNet caught up without it. The winning object is a pure ConvNet that imports the non-attention parts of the Transformer-era design package: the training recipe, stage allocation, patchify stem, separated spatial and channel mixing, inverted 4x channel expansion, large but not oversized depthwise kernels, sparse activations, sparse normalization, LayerNorm, separate downsampling stabilized by boundary norms, LayerScale, and stochastic depth. I call the resulting family ConvNeXt.
