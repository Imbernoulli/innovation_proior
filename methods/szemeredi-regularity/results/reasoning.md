Let me start where the pain actually is. I want to know when a large dense graph contains a fixed pattern — a triangle, a $K_4$, a copy of some bounded graph $H$. For a random graph this is no trouble at all. In $G(n,p)$, between any two reasonably large sets of vertices $X$ and $Y$ the number of edges is about $p|X||Y|$; every fixed $H$ then appears the expected number of times and I can count it on the back of an envelope. The whole reason random graphs are easy is that single fact — edges spread evenly across all large sets.

An arbitrary graph gives me nothing like that. It can have overall density $1/2$ and yet pile all its edges into one half and leave the other half empty; local densities lurch around. So every counting argument I know how to run on a random graph dies on a general graph, and that is exactly the wall I keep hitting in the progression work. I have a set of integers of positive density, I encode the additive structure as a graph, and then I need to find a configuration in a graph that is just *some* graph — not random, not structured, just arbitrary and dense.

So the question I really want answered is: can I force an arbitrary dense graph to *look* random? Not literally — it isn't random — but can I carve it up so that, piece by piece, it behaves the way a random graph behaves between large sets? If I could split $V$ into a handful of blocks $V_1,\dots,V_k$ and know that between almost every pair $(V_i,V_j)$ the edges are spread evenly, then each pair behaves like a random bipartite graph of its own density, and the counting machinery comes back to life on each block, and I glue.

Two things have to be true for this to be worth anything. First I need the right notion of "this pair of blocks spreads its edges evenly." Second — and this is the one that will be hard — the number of blocks $k$ has to be *bounded*, bounded by something that depends only on how good I want the evenness to be, and not at all on $n$. Because if I'm allowed $k$ to grow with $n$, I can always cut $V$ into singletons: every pair of single vertices is trivially "even," and I've learned nothing. The entire value is in keeping $k$ under one fixed function of the tolerance while $n$ runs off to infinity.

Let me nail the first thing — what "even" should mean for one pair of blocks. The density of a pair is $d(X,Y)=e(X,Y)/(|X||Y|)$, the fraction of possible edges present. A random block of density $p$ has $d(X',Y')\approx p$ for *all* largish subsets $X'\subseteq X$, $Y'\subseteq Y$. So I'll just demand exactly that, with a tolerance. Fix $\varepsilon>0$. Call a pair $(A,B)$ **$\varepsilon$-regular** if for every $X\subseteq A$ and $Y\subseteq B$ with $|X|\ge\varepsilon|A|$ and $|Y|\ge\varepsilon|B|$,
$$|d(X,Y)-d(A,B)|\le\varepsilon.$$
That's it: no large subset, picked however adversarially, sees a density noticeably different from the block's overall density. The $\varepsilon$ on the subset sizes is there because I have no hope of controlling tiny subsets — a single vertex pair has density $0$ or $1$ — so I only insist on uniformity down to the scale $\varepsilon$.

Is this the right condition, or am I asking too little? Let me check it actually buys me the embedding I want. Suppose $(A,B)$ is $\varepsilon$-regular with density $d$, and I take any $Y\subseteq B$ with $|Y|>\varepsilon|B|$. How many vertices of $A$ have *few* neighbours in $Y$ — say fewer than $(d-\varepsilon)|Y|$? Call that bad set $X$. If $|X|>\varepsilon|A|$, then by regularity $d(X,Y)$ is within $\varepsilon$ of $d$, so the edges between $X$ and $Y$ number more than $(d-\varepsilon)|X||Y|$, contradicting that every vertex of $X$ has fewer than $(d-\varepsilon)|Y|$ neighbours in $Y$. So $|X|\le\varepsilon|A|$: all but an $\varepsilon$-fraction of $A$ has roughly the expected degree into any large piece of $B$. That is precisely the lever for greedy embedding — I can place the vertices of a bounded-degree $H$ one at a time, each time choosing from a candidate set that stays large because only an $\varepsilon$-fraction ever gets disqualified. Good. So $\varepsilon$-regular is the right local notion: it is one-block quasirandomness, and it is exactly the strength the counting needs.

Now the hard half. I want a partition of $V$ into a bounded number of (nearly) equal blocks with almost all pairs $\varepsilon$-regular. "Almost all" because I should expect to tolerate a few bad pairs — say at most $\varepsilon k^2$ of the $\binom{k}{2}$ pairs irregular — since insisting on *every* pair being regular feels too rigid and I doubt it's even true. So the target is: $k\le M(\varepsilon,m)$ blocks of equal size, at most $\varepsilon k^2$ irregular pairs, once the graph has at least the requested lower bound $m$ vertices.

How would I even produce such a partition? The honest method is the dumb one: start with some partition, and if it's no good — if it has too many irregular pairs — then *fix it by cutting more finely*. An irregular pair is a pair that has a witness: subsets $X\subseteq A$, $Y\subseteq B$ where the density is off by more than $\varepsilon$. Those witness sets are telling me the block is internally inhomogeneous, so I'll promote $X$ and $Y$ to blocks of their own — refine the partition using the very sets that exposed the irregularity. Then maybe the new partition is regular; if not, repeat.

But this could go forever. Refine, refine, refine, and I might just be approaching the singleton partition with $k\to n$, which is the useless answer. I need a reason the process *stops* after a bounded number of refinements. There has to be some quantity that moves monotonically and is trapped in a bounded range, so it can only move a bounded number of times.

Let me look for that quantity. What changes when I refine? The densities of pairs get re-resolved at a finer scale. I want a number attached to a partition that *goes up* every time I cut, and that I can cap from above. Average density won't do — it's conserved under refinement, it can't detect that I've improved anything. I need something that is *strictly convex* in the densities, so that splitting a block with non-constant internal density strictly raises it.

The simplest convex thing is the square. So let me weigh each pair by the square of its density and by the fraction of the vertex pairs it covers, and sum:
$$q(P)=\sum_{i<j}\frac{|V_i||V_j|}{n^2}\,d(V_i,V_j)^2=\sum_{i<j}\frac{e(V_i,V_j)^2}{|V_i||V_j|\,n^2},$$
where $n=|V|$. Call it the energy, or the index, of the partition. First, is it bounded? Since each $d^2\le 1$,
$$q(P)\le\frac{1}{n^2}\sum_{i<j}|V_i||V_j|\le\frac{1}{n^2}\Big(\sum_i|V_i|\Big)\Big(\sum_j|V_j|\Big)\cdot\frac12\le 1.$$
So $0\le q(P)\le 1$, trapped. That's the cap I needed. Now I have to check the monotonicity and, crucially, that an irregular partition is forced to make $q$ jump by a *fixed* amount — not just rise, but rise by at least some constant depending only on $\varepsilon$. If I get that, the boundedness of $q$ finishes everything: a quantity in $[0,1]$ that climbs by at least a fixed step can only climb finitely often.

Let me first make sure refining never *lowers* $q$ — otherwise the whole monotone story collapses. Take a single pair $(C,D)$ and refine $C$ into $\{C_i\}$ and $D$ into $\{D_j\}$. Writing $e_{ij}=e(C_i,D_j)$, $c_i=|C_i|$, $d_j=|D_j|$, the refined contribution is $\frac{1}{n^2}\sum_{i,j}\frac{e_{ij}^2}{c_i d_j}$. I want this to be at least $\frac{1}{n^2}\frac{e(C,D)^2}{|C||D|}$. This is just Cauchy–Schwarz. In the form I want it: for reals $e_i\ge 0$ and $\mu_i>0$,
$$\sum_i\frac{e_i^2}{\mu_i}\ \ge\ \frac{\big(\sum_i e_i\big)^2}{\sum_i \mu_i},$$
which falls out of $\big(\sum a_i^2\big)\big(\sum b_i^2\big)\ge\big(\sum a_i b_i\big)^2$ with $a_i=\sqrt{\mu_i}$, $b_i=e_i/\sqrt{\mu_i}$. Apply it with the pairs $(i,j)$ as the index, $\mu_{ij}=c_i d_j$:
$$\sum_{i,j}\frac{e_{ij}^2}{c_i d_j}\ \ge\ \frac{\big(\sum_{i,j}e_{ij}\big)^2}{\sum_{i,j}c_i d_j}=\frac{e(C,D)^2}{|C||D|}.$$
So $q(C,D)$ does not decrease under refinement of the pair, and summing over all pairs (the cross terms only add nonnegative contributions, and the within-block terms are new and nonnegative), $q(P')\ge q(P)$ whenever $P'$ refines $P$. The energy never drops when I cut. That makes sense — squaring is convex, refining is conditioning on a finer partition, and a finer average of squares is at least the coarser one. It's Pythagoras: the finer step-function approximation of the edge pattern has at least as much $L^2$ mass.

Now the engine: a pair that is *not* $\varepsilon$-regular must, when I cut it along its witness, raise $q$ by a definite amount. Suppose $(C,D)$ is not $\varepsilon$-regular. Then there are $C_1\subseteq C$, $D_1\subseteq D$ with $|C_1|\ge\varepsilon|C|$, $|D_1|\ge\varepsilon|D|$, and
$$|\eta|>\varepsilon,\qquad \eta:=d(C_1,D_1)-d(C,D).$$
Refine into $\mathcal C=\{C_1,C_2\}$, $\mathcal D=\{D_1,D_2\}$ with $C_2=C\setminus C_1$, $D_2=D\setminus D_1$. I'll compute the gain. Abbreviate $c=|C|$, $d=|D|$, $e=e(C,D)$, and $c_i=|C_i|$, $d_j=|D_j|$, $e_{ij}=e(C_i,D_j)$. Then the refined contribution is
$$n^2\,q(\mathcal C,\mathcal D)=\sum_{i,j}\frac{e_{ij}^2}{c_i d_j}=\frac{e_{11}^2}{c_1 d_1}+\sum_{(i,j)\ne(1,1)}\frac{e_{ij}^2}{c_i d_j}.$$
On the off-$(1,1)$ terms apply the same Cauchy–Schwarz, lumping them into one:
$$\sum_{(i,j)\ne(1,1)}\frac{e_{ij}^2}{c_i d_j}\ \ge\ \frac{(e-e_{11})^2}{cd-c_1 d_1}.$$
So
$$n^2\,q(\mathcal C,\mathcal D)\ \ge\ \frac{e_{11}^2}{c_1 d_1}+\frac{(e-e_{11})^2}{cd-c_1 d_1}.$$
Now substitute the definition of $\eta$. Since $d(C_1,D_1)=e_{11}/(c_1 d_1)$ and $d(C,D)=e/(cd)$,
$$e_{11}=\frac{c_1 d_1}{cd}\,e+\eta\,c_1 d_1.$$
Plug in. The first term:
$$\frac{e_{11}^2}{c_1 d_1}=\frac{1}{c_1 d_1}\Big(\frac{c_1 d_1\,e}{cd}+\eta c_1 d_1\Big)^2=\frac{c_1 d_1\,e^2}{c^2 d^2}+\frac{2e\eta c_1 d_1}{cd}+\eta^2 c_1 d_1.$$
For the second term, $e-e_{11}=e\big(1-\frac{c_1 d_1}{cd}\big)-\eta c_1 d_1=e\frac{cd-c_1 d_1}{cd}-\eta c_1 d_1$, so
$$\frac{(e-e_{11})^2}{cd-c_1 d_1}=\frac{1}{cd-c_1 d_1}\Big(e\frac{cd-c_1 d_1}{cd}-\eta c_1 d_1\Big)^2=\frac{e^2(cd-c_1 d_1)}{c^2 d^2}-\frac{2e\eta c_1 d_1}{cd}+\frac{\eta^2 c_1^2 d_1^2}{cd-c_1 d_1}.$$
Add them. The two cross terms $\pm\frac{2e\eta c_1 d_1}{cd}$ cancel. The two $e^2$ terms combine: $\frac{c_1 d_1\,e^2}{c^2 d^2}+\frac{e^2(cd-c_1 d_1)}{c^2 d^2}=\frac{e^2}{cd}$. What's left over is $\eta^2 c_1 d_1+\frac{\eta^2 c_1^2 d_1^2}{cd-c_1 d_1}\ge\eta^2 c_1 d_1$. So
$$n^2\,q(\mathcal C,\mathcal D)\ \ge\ \frac{e^2}{cd}+\eta^2 c_1 d_1=n^2\,q(C,D)+\eta^2 c_1 d_1.$$
And now the irregularity pays off: $|\eta|>\varepsilon$ gives $\eta^2>\varepsilon^2$, while $c_1\ge\varepsilon c$ and $d_1\ge\varepsilon d$ give $c_1 d_1\ge\varepsilon^2 cd$, so $\eta^2 c_1 d_1>\varepsilon^4 cd$. Therefore
$$q(\mathcal C,\mathcal D)\ \ge\ q(C,D)+\varepsilon^4\,\frac{|C||D|}{n^2}.$$
There it is — cutting one irregular pair along its own witness strictly raises the energy, by an amount I can write down. The leftover $\frac{\eta^2 c_1^2 d_1^2}{cd-c_1 d_1}$ I threw away only helps; I don't need it.

One pair's gain $\varepsilon^4|C||D|/n^2$ is tiny — smaller than any fixed constant, since $|C||D|/n^2$ is small for many blocks. So a single bad pair isn't enough to force termination. But I don't have one bad pair; if the *partition* is not $\varepsilon$-regular, more than $\varepsilon k^2$ pairs are irregular. That's the multiplicity I cash in. Refine *every* irregular pair at once and add up.

To do all the cuts simultaneously and keep the gains, take a partition $P=\{C_0,C_1,\dots,C_k\}$, equal blocks of size $c$ (the set $C_0$ is an exceptional bin I keep to absorb leftover vertices so the real blocks stay exactly equal; treat its vertices as singletons in $q$, so they never lower it). For every irregular pair $(C_i,C_j)$, the pair lemma hands me a two-set cut of $C_i$ with respect to $C_j$ and of $C_j$ with respect to $C_i$. For each $i$, overlay all the cuts that $C_i$ receives from the various $j$ — at most $k-1$ of them, so $C_i$ shatters into at most $2^{k-1}$ cells. Let this common refinement be $\mathcal C$, with $k\le|\mathcal C|\le k\,2^{k-1}$.

Now bound $q(\mathcal C)$ from below. Because $\mathcal C$ refines every individual pair-cut, the refinement-doesn't-decrease lemma lets me replace each $q(C_i,C_j)$ by the refined-pair value $q(\mathcal C_{ij},\mathcal C_{ji})$, which for the irregular pairs is at least $q(C_i,C_j)+\varepsilon^4 c^2/n^2$ by the pair lemma, and for the regular pairs is at least $q(C_i,C_j)$. Summing,
$$q(\mathcal C)\ \ge\ \sum_{i<j}q(C_i,C_j)+(\text{number of irregular pairs})\cdot\varepsilon^4\frac{c^2}{n^2}\ \ge\ q(P)+\varepsilon k^2\cdot\varepsilon^4\frac{c^2}{n^2}=q(P)+\varepsilon^5\Big(\frac{kc}{n}\Big)^2.$$
There is no loss in assuming $0<\varepsilon\le\frac14$; if $\varepsilon$ is larger, a partition built at tolerance $1/4$ is already good enough for it. Now $kc$ is the number of vertices in real blocks, which is $n-|C_0|\ge n-\varepsilon n\ge\frac34 n$. So $(kc/n)^2\ge 9/16>1/2$, and
$$q(\mathcal C)\ \ge\ q(P)+\frac{\varepsilon^5}{2}.$$
A *fixed* increment, depending only on $\varepsilon$. That is the whole point. An irregular partition can always be refined to raise the energy by at least $\varepsilon^5/2$.

I still have to tidy $\mathcal C$ back into equal blocks before iterating. Cut every cell into pieces of one common size $\lfloor c/4^k\rfloor$ and sweep the slivers into the exceptional bin. This re-balancing is a further refinement once those slivers are treated as singletons, so the monotonicity lemma cannot lower $q$; it changes the block count to some $\ell$ with $k\le\ell\le k\,4^k$, and it adds at most $n/2^k$ vertices to the bin in that round.

Now everything closes, but I have to choose the starting number of blocks carefully so the exceptional bin stays small for all possible rounds. The real-valued round bound is
$$s_0=\frac{1}{\varepsilon^5/2}=\frac{2}{\varepsilon^5},$$
and for the block-count bookkeeping I use the integer $s=\lceil s_0\rceil$. Choose an initial block count $k_0\ge m$ with $2^{k_0-1}\ge s/\varepsilon$. If $n$ is small, the singleton partition will already have at most whatever upper bound $M$ I choose. If $n$ is large, start with $k_0$ equal blocks and fewer than $k_0$ leftover vertices in $C_0$, and require $n\ge 2k_0/\varepsilon$ so that this initial exceptional set has size at most $\varepsilon n/2$. During the iteration the number of real blocks never falls below $k_0$, so even if all $s$ possible rounds occur, the added exceptional vertices total at most
$$s\,\frac{n}{2^{k_0}}\le \frac{\varepsilon n}{2}.$$
Thus $|C_0|$ stays at most $\varepsilon n$, so the refinement lemma remains available at every step.

While the partition is not $\varepsilon$-regular, each refinement raises $q$ by at least $\varepsilon^5/2$. But $q$ is trapped in $[0,1]$, so there is no way to keep finding irregular pairs after $s_0=2/\varepsilon^5$ rounds — the energy would exceed $1$, which is impossible. Each round can blow the block count up from $r$ to as much as $r\,4^r$; call this map $f(x)=x\,4^x$. Taking
$$M=\max\{f^s(k_0),\,2k_0/\varepsilon\}$$
handles both cases: if $n\le M$, singletons give a legal partition with $m\le n\le M$; if $n>M$, the iterative construction starts legally and has at most $f^s(k_0)\le M$ real blocks. Iterating $x\mapsto x4^x$ for $\lceil2/\varepsilon^5\rceil$ rounds produces a tower of exponentials of height proportional to $\varepsilon^{-5}$. That is monstrous, but it is a function of $\varepsilon$ and $m$ alone, with no dependence on $n$, and that bounded-in-$n$-ness was the entire requirement. I should not waste effort trying to make it small here: the multiplicity $\varepsilon k^2$ of bad pairs is what forced the fixed $\varepsilon^5$ step, and the square-and-shatter that gives the fixed step is exactly what towers the count.

So the statement I've landed on: for every $\varepsilon>0$ and every $m$ there is an $M=M(\varepsilon,m)$ such that every graph with $n\ge m$ vertices admits a partition $V=V_0\cup V_1\cup\dots\cup V_k$ with $m\le k\le M$, an exceptional set $|V_0|\le\varepsilon n$, equal blocks $|V_1|=\dots=|V_k|$, and all but at most $\varepsilon k^2$ of the pairs $(V_i,V_j)$ being $\varepsilon$-regular. Every dense graph, no matter how tangled, is a bounded union of random-looking blocks plus a negligible remainder.

Let me make sure this is the *useful* object and not just a curiosity, by building the payoff I was after. From the partition form the **reduced graph** $R$: one vertex per block $V_i$, and an edge $V_iV_j$ whenever $(V_i,V_j)$ is $\varepsilon$-regular with density at least some floor $d$. This $R$ has at most $M(\varepsilon,m)$ vertices — bounded — and it carries a weight (the density) on each edge. The claim I want is that $R$ governs the subgraph content of $G$: a small graph living in $R$ lifts to a copy in $G$.

The lift works greedily. Suppose I want to embed a bounded-degree $H$ whose vertices have been assigned to blocks so that every edge of $H$ lands on a regular pair of density $\ge d$. Set $\delta=d-\varepsilon$. Place the vertices of $H$ one at a time. For each not-yet-placed vertex $v_j$ I keep a candidate set $C_j$ inside its block, starting as the whole block of size $m$. When I place $v_i$, I pick it from its current candidate set so that for every unplaced neighbour $v_j$ it has many neighbours in $C_j$ — at least $\delta|C_j|$ of them — and then I shrink each such $C_j$ to those neighbours. By the most-degrees-into-a-large-set fact, the vertices of the current block that *fail* to have $\delta|C_j|$ neighbours in a candidate set of size $>\varepsilon m$ number at most $\varepsilon m$; over the at most $\Delta$ neighbours that's at most $\Delta\varepsilon m$ disqualified, so as long as the candidate set hasn't shrunk below $\varepsilon m$ there's always a legal choice. After placing $v_i$, a candidate set with $d_{ij}$ commitments against it has size $>\delta^{d_{ij}}m\ge\delta^{\Delta}m$, and this is more than enough if
$$\varepsilon\ \le\ \varepsilon_0:=\frac{\delta^{\Delta}}{2+\Delta}.$$
Choose $\varepsilon$ that small and leave the usual room for already-used vertices inside a block; the greedy embedding never gets stuck. In the counting form this gives more than $\big((\delta^{\Delta}-\Delta\varepsilon)m\big)^h$ labelled embeddings under the corresponding blow-up hypothesis. So *the existence of $H$ in the bounded reduced graph $R$ forces a copy — indeed many copies — of $H$ in $G$.* The bounded blueprint controls the arbitrary graph. That is the entire content I was missing for counting in non-random graphs.

And this immediately gives the structural counting facts I wanted. If a graph is missing some pattern, the reduced graph must be missing it too (else it would lift), so I can run a *classical* extremal theorem — Turán, Erdős–Stone, König–Hall — on the bounded graph $R$ and pull the conclusion back to $G$. Turn it around and I get the removal phenomenon: if $G$ has only $o(n^{v(H)})$ copies of $H$, then $H$ must sit only on irregular or low-density pairs of any regular partition; deleting the edges inside blocks, across irregular pairs, and across sparse pairs costs only $o(n^2)$ edges and destroys every copy — so few copies of $H$ means all copies are coverable by a vanishing fraction of edges. This is the graph-theoretic form of the density-to-configuration bridge I needed for arithmetic progressions. The clean general partition is what was hiding inside that tangled bipartite lemma; pulling it out into a statement about *all* graphs is what makes it reusable.

I wanted to count patterns in arbitrary dense graphs, which only works for random-looking graphs, so I asked for the right local notion of random-looking — $\varepsilon$-regularity, density stable on all large subsets — and then for a *bounded* partition into such blocks. To get boundedness I attached to each partition its mean-square density $q(P)=\sum_{i<j}(|V_i||V_j|/n^2)d^2$, which lives in $[0,1]$; refinement never lowers it (Cauchy–Schwarz, i.e. a finer $L^2$ approximation has more mass); and an irregular pair, cut along its own witness, strictly raises it by $\varepsilon^4|C||D|/n^2$ (the $\eta^2 c_1 d_1$ leftover after the cross terms cancel); so an irregular *partition*, with its more-than-$\varepsilon k^2$ bad pairs cut at once, raises $q$ by a fixed $\varepsilon^5/2$; trapped below $1$, this can happen at most $2/\varepsilon^5$ times, forcing $\varepsilon$-regularity after a bounded number of rounds, with the block count an $\varepsilon^{-5}$-height tower; and the resulting bounded reduced graph, via greedy embedding, lifts patterns back to $G$ — so every dense graph is, for counting purposes, a bounded weighted blueprint of random-looking blocks.
