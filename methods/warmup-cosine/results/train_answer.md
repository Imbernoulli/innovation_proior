Everything in this training pipeline is frozen except a single number: the learning rate I feed SGD each epoch. The optimizer is SGD with momentum $0.9$ and weight decay $5\times10^{-4}$, the base rate is $0.1$, initialization is Kaiming, and the budget is $200$ epochs, none of which I may touch. So the entire design space is one function $\eta(t)$ — a curve over the epochs — and I want the one that lands the best test accuracy. A single flat value is structurally wrong, and it fails at both ends for the same underlying reason: the admissible step size is bounded by local curvature. Model the loss near the current weights as a quadratic with Hessian $H$; one gradient step along an eigendirection of curvature $\lambda$ multiplies the distance to the minimum by $(1-\eta\lambda)$, which contracts only while $\eta < 2/\lambda$. Push $\eta$ past $2/\lambda_{\max}(H)$ and the iteration expands along the sharpest direction and diverges. Curvature is largest right after random initialization — freshly initialized nets sit in sharp regions, $\lambda_{\max}$ is big, $2/\lambda_{\max}$ is small, and $0.1$ can easily sit above that ceiling, so the first steps overshoot and the loss spikes instead of falling. That is the early end. The late end is the mirror image: near a minimum the true gradient has shrunk but the *stochastic* gradient has not — minibatch noise never vanishes — so a step of $0.1$ keeps kicking the parameters around at the noise floor instead of letting them settle. The start wants $\eta$ well below $2/\lambda_{\max}(\text{init})$ and the end wants $\eta$ tiny to beat the noise floor; those are different numbers, so the curve must be small at both ends and high in the middle, for two different reasons.

What everyone does today is drop the rate. Step decay holds $\eta = 0.1$, then multiplies by a fixed factor at hand-picked milestones — the wide-residual-net recipe multiplies by $0.2$ at epochs $60$, $120$, $160$ of a $200$-epoch run. It is the workhorse and it does encode "large early, small late," but only as a crude piecewise-constant staircase, and its gaps are exactly what a better curve should fix. The milestones are extra knobs glued to this specific budget and model, with nothing principled saying where they go; each drop is a discontinuity — the rate falls by a factor of $5$ or $10$ in a single epoch, a shock to the dynamics, and with momentum it is worse, because the velocity $v = \mu v - \eta\,\nabla$ has been accumulating rate-scaled gradients at the old $\eta$, so the instant $\eta$ drops tenfold the carried-over $v$ is suddenly tenfold too large and the first post-drop steps are dominated by stale history. And the deepest gap: step decay does nothing about the start — the very first step already uses the full $0.1$, so the early-instability failure is left entirely untreated. The constant-prefix patch (hold a low rate for a few epochs, then resume) does not save it either: after the prefix the net has descended *somewhere*, but with no guarantee that curvature has fallen enough for $0.1$ to be admissible, so the snap from the low prefix to the full rate just postpones the loss spike to the moment of the snap. The snap itself is the disease.

I propose the warmup-cosine schedule: a short linear warmup that ramps the rate from a small value up to $base\_lr$, stitched continuously to a cosine annealing tail that decays smoothly from $base\_lr$ toward $0$ over the rest of training. The two ends of the curve are designed by splitting the problem, because the start-of-training instability and the body-of-training decay are largely independent. For the start, the cure is forced by the diagnosis above. Since a sudden jump to $0.1$ is unsafe while curvature may still be high, I climb gradually instead of snapping: increase the rate by a constant increment every epoch so there is no value discontinuity anywhere. As $\eta$ rises, the weights keep moving into flatter regions and the ceiling $2/\lambda_{\max}$ keeps rising, and a ramp is meant to keep $\eta$ underneath that rising ceiling rather than leaping over it; it is also gentle on the momentum velocity, since $\eta$ changes by only a small amount per epoch and $v$ is never abruptly mismatched to the current rate. So the opening phase is a linear ramp,
$$\eta(\text{epoch}) = base\_lr \cdot \frac{\text{epoch}+1}{warmup}, \qquad \text{epoch} = 0,\dots,warmup-1,$$
which samples the values $base\_lr/5,\, 2\,base\_lr/5,\, \dots,\, base\_lr$ in equal increments of $base\_lr/5$, reaching the full rate exactly at the last warmup epoch. I start the sampled ramp at $base\_lr/warmup$ rather than literally $0$ because a true zero start wastes the first epoch on no progress at all; sampling the zero-start line at $k = 1,\dots,warmup$ gives a clean arithmetic ramp whose first rate is five times smaller than the target, deliberately reducing $\eta$ precisely while the curvature ceiling is tightest. I take $warmup = 5$ — long enough to give the sharp initial region a chance to relax, short enough ($2.5\%$ of $200$ epochs) to be a small fixed prefix rather than a tuned milestone schedule.

For the body — epochs $warmup$ through the end — I want to begin at the full $base\_lr$, continuous with the top of the ramp, and decay smoothly and monotonically to a tiny rate with no cliffs and no milestones. The question is only which decreasing curve. A straight line from $base\_lr$ to $0$ has the right endpoints but exactly the wrong slope profile: I want to *linger* near the top early, where the gradient is large and informative and the model is far from any minimum and fast progress is most valuable, and I want to *linger* near the bottom late, spending many epochs at a small rate to fine-tune below the noise floor and actually resolve the minimum. A line leaves the top immediately and reaches the bottom only at the last instant. So I want a curve whose slope is near zero at both ends and steepest in the middle — which is exactly a half-period of a cosine. Over the interval where its argument runs from $0$ to $\pi$, $\cos$ decreases monotonically from $+1$ to $-1$, and its derivative $-\sin$ is zero at both ends and most negative at the midpoint. I let progress run from $0$ at the start of the body to $1$ at the end and feed $\pi\cdot\text{progress}$ into the cosine, then rescale the $[+1,-1]$ range onto $[base\_lr, 0]$ with the affine map that sends $+1\mapsto base\_lr$ and $-1\mapsto 0$:
$$\eta = base\_lr \cdot \frac{1 + \cos(\pi\,\text{progress})}{2}.$$
Writing it with a general floor, sending $+1\mapsto \eta_{\max}$ and $-1\mapsto \eta_{\min}$ gives $\eta = \eta_{\min} + (\eta_{\max}-\eta_{\min})\,\tfrac{1+\cos(\pi\,\text{progress})}{2}$; for a single anneal with no plan to ever raise the rate again I take $\eta_{\min}=0$ and $\eta_{\max}=base\_lr$, since I want the smallest possible steps at the very end and $0$ is the natural floor. The derivative confirms the choice: $\frac{d}{d\,\text{progress}}\,base\_lr\,\tfrac{1+\cos(\pi\,\text{progress})}{2} = -\tfrac{base\_lr\,\pi}{2}\sin(\pi\,\text{progress})$, which is $0$ at $\text{progress}=0$ and $\text{progress}=1$ and most negative at $\text{progress}=\tfrac12$ — the rate barely changes near the top and near the bottom and falls fastest through the middle, precisely the "linger high, then linger low" behavior I wanted, and the smooth replacement for step decay's cliffs.

The seam between the two phases must be handled carefully. The ramp ends at exactly $base\_lr$ at the last warmup epoch, so the cosine body must *begin* at $base\_lr$ for the sampled curve to be continuous across the seam. That means the cosine clock has to be measured from the end of warmup, spanning the remaining $total\_epochs - warmup$ interval, not from epoch $0$:
$$\text{progress} = \frac{\text{epoch} - warmup}{total\_epochs - warmup}.$$
At $\text{epoch} = warmup$ this gives $\text{progress}=0$, $\cos(0)=1$, $\eta = base\_lr$ — continuous with the top of the ramp; if I had instead measured progress from epoch $0$ over the full budget, the cosine at the seam would already sit below $base\_lr$ and I would have a value drop, and the warmup epochs would eat into the cosine's budget. With the scheduler clock $T_i = total\_epochs - warmup$, the mathematical floor $\text{progress}=1 \Rightarrow \cos(\pi)=-1 \Rightarrow \eta=0$ falls at the epoch boundary *after* the last in-loop epoch, so the last sampled training epoch is just above $0$ — matching the usual closed-form cosine scheduler convention where $T_{cur}/T_i$ reaches $1$ at the end of the interval, not at the last integer sample inside it. The harness calls the schedule once per epoch and holds the returned rate constant over that epoch, so the smooth cosine is sampled at $195$ points across the body; the per-epoch jumps are on the order of $base\_lr/100$, so the staircase is a fine discretization with effectively no shock. The whole schedule is one branch on the epoch index and two short formulas, with a single fixed warmup length, no milestones, and no $config$ conditioning — both mechanisms, surviving the sharp init and then annealing to settle, are architecture-agnostic, so the same curve serves ResNet-20 on CIFAR-10, ResNet-56 on CIFAR-100, and MobileNetV2 on FashionMNIST without per-case retuning.

```python
import math


def get_lr(epoch, total_epochs, base_lr, config):
    """Linear warmup (5 epochs) then cosine annealing toward 0.

    Epochs 0..4:   linear ramp base_lr/5 -> base_lr (each step base_lr/5),
                   reducing early steps while the curvature ceiling is tight,
                   with no discontinuity.
    Epochs 5..end: cosine anneal base_lr toward 0, clock measured from end of warmup
                   so it opens at base_lr (continuous with the ramp); the floor is
                   at the next epoch boundary, with slope ~0 at both cosine ends.
    """
    warmup = 5
    if epoch < warmup:
        return base_lr * (epoch + 1) / warmup
    progress = (epoch - warmup) / (total_epochs - warmup)
    return base_lr * 0.5 * (1 + math.cos(math.pi * progress))
```
