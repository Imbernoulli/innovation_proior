Everything in this pipeline is frozen except one number: the learning rate I feed SGD each epoch. Optimizer is SGD with momentum 0.9 and weight decay 5e-4, base rate 0.1, Kaiming init, 200 epochs, and I cannot touch any of it. So the entire design space I have is a function eta(epoch) — a curve over 200 epochs — and I want the one that lands the best test accuracy. Let me start from why a single flat number is wrong, because that tells me the shape the curve has to have.

Take eta(epoch) = 0.1 for all 200 epochs and ask what goes wrong. Two failures, at opposite ends, and they come from the same place: the relationship between the step size and the local curvature. Model the loss near the current weights as a quadratic with Hessian H. One gradient-descent step along an eigendirection of curvature lambda multiplies the distance-to-minimum in that direction by (1 - eta*lambda). That contracts — makes progress — only while |1 - eta*lambda| < 1, i.e. while eta < 2/lambda. Push eta past 2/lambda_max(H) and the iteration *expands* along the sharpest direction: it diverges. So the admissible learning rate is capped by the curvature: eta < 2/lambda_max. Now, where is curvature largest? Right after random initialization. Freshly initialized nets sit in sharp regions of the loss surface — lambda_max is big there, so 2/lambda_max is small, and 0.1 can easily be above that ceiling. The first few steps then overshoot; the loss spikes up instead of down, and either the run dies or it gets violently knocked into some flatter basin before it can recover. That's the early end. The late end is the mirror image: near a minimum the true gradient has shrunk toward zero but the *stochastic* gradient hasn't — minibatch noise gives every step a random component that does not vanish — so a step of size 0.1 keeps kicking the parameters around the minimum at the noise floor instead of letting them settle in. There I need eta small, not because of curvature divergence but because the step has to be small enough that the noise-driven jitter is small. So one constant rate is structurally impossible: the start wants eta well below 2/lambda_max(initial), and the end wants eta tiny to beat the noise floor, and those are different numbers. The curve has to be high in the middle and small — for different reasons — at both ends. Actually, "high at the start" is too strong, given the curvature argument; let me hold that thought, it's the crux of the early end.

What does everyone actually do today? They drop the rate. Step decay: hold eta = 0.1, then multiply by a fixed factor at hand-picked milestones — divide by 10 partway through and again later, or the wide-residual-net recipe multiplies by 0.2 at 60, 120, 160 of a 200-epoch run. This is the workhorse; it produced the leading residual-net numbers. And it does encode "large early, small late," just in a crude piecewise-constant way: a high plateau, a cliff, a lower plateau, another cliff. Let me be precise about why I'm not satisfied with it, because the gaps are exactly what a better curve should fix. The milestones are extra knobs — 60/120/160 are glued to this particular 200-epoch budget and this model; change the budget and I have to re-pick them, and there's nothing principled telling me where they go. Each drop is a discontinuity: the rate falls by a factor of 5 or 10 in a single epoch. That's a shock to the dynamics at the exact moments it fires, and with momentum it's worse — the velocity v = mu*v - eta*grad has been accumulating rate-scaled gradients at the old eta, and the instant eta drops by 10x the carried-over v is suddenly 10x too large relative to the new rate, so the first post-drop steps are dominated by stale history. I can patch that by rescaling v by the rate ratio at the drop, but the cleaner fix is to not have a sudden drop in the first place. And the deepest gap: step decay does *nothing* about the start. The very first step already uses the full 0.1. The whole early-instability failure is untouched. So step decay handles the late end (eventually it gets eta small) but not the early end, and it does the late end with shocks and milestone hyperparameters I'd rather not have.

So let me split the problem cleanly: there's a start-of-training problem and there's a body-of-training decay problem, and they're somewhat independent. Handle them separately and then stitch.

The start first, because it's the one step decay ignores. The disease is: eta = 0.1 is above the curvature ceiling 2/lambda_max while the net is in its sharp initial region. The obvious move is to *not* use 0.1 at the start — use something small while curvature is high, then raise it. Does the small-then-raise actually buy me anything, or am I just delaying? The reason it works is that the sharp initial region is transient: even a few small, safe steps move the weights into a flatter region where lambda_max has come down and 2/lambda_max has risen, so the full 0.1 becomes admissible. The point of starting small is to *survive long enough for the curvature to relax*, and then ramp up to the rate I actually want for the bulk of training. So at the start the curve should rise, not fall — a brief opening phase where eta climbs from a safe small value to 0.1.

Now, how to climb. Try the laziest version: hold a low constant rate, say 0.1/5 = 0.02, for the first 5 epochs, then snap to 0.1. This is the existing "constant prefix" idea. Let me think about whether the snap is safe. After 5 epochs at 0.02 the net has descended *somewhere* — into a region whose curvature has come down some, but I have no guarantee it has come down enough that 0.1 is now below 2/lambda_max there. If it hasn't, the snap to 0.1 is exactly the loss spike I am trying to avoid, just postponed by 5 epochs: the training error spikes right at the transition and may never recover. And empirically that's what's seen with a constant prefix on aggressive setups — the constant phase looks fine, then the error jumps the moment the rate jumps. So the constant-prefix-then-snap *recreates the discontinuity disease at the end of the prefix.* The snap is the problem.

If a sudden jump from 0.02 to 0.1 is unsafe because the curvature might not yet admit 0.1, then the alternative is to climb *gradually* — increase the rate by a constant amount every step, so that at no point is there a value discontinuity. As eta rises, the weights keep moving into flatter regions and 2/lambda_max keeps rising; a ramp is meant to keep eta below that rising ceiling instead of leaping over it. It's also gentle on the momentum velocity — eta changes by a small increment per step, so v is not suddenly scaled relative to the current rate. So the opening phase is a *linear ramp*. I'll write it as eta(epoch) = base_lr * (epoch + 1) / warmup for epoch = 0..warmup-1, and check what values that actually samples. With base_lr = 0.1 and warmup = 5: epoch 0 -> 0.1*1/5 = 0.02, epoch 1 -> 0.04, epoch 2 -> 0.06, epoch 3 -> 0.08, epoch 4 -> 0.1. The differences are 0.02 each — a constant increment of base_lr/5 — so it is genuinely a linear ramp, and it reaches exactly base_lr at the last warmup epoch, right where I want the cosine branch to take over. Why start the sampled ramp at base_lr/5 rather than at literally 0? Starting at exactly 0 means the first epoch makes no progress at all; sampling the zero-start line at k = 1..5 gives a clean arithmetic ramp, the first epoch already does a little work, and the kth warmup epoch is k*base_lr/warmup. And why 5 epochs? It is long enough to give the initial sharp region a chance to relax but short enough to be a small fixed prefix — 5 out of 200 is 2.5% — rather than a schedule of hand-tuned milestones. I choose 5.

That handles the start. Now the body: epochs 5 through 199, where I want to begin at the full 0.1 (continuous with the top of the ramp) and decay down to a tiny rate by the end. Step decay does this with cliffs; I've already argued against the cliffs (shocks, milestones, momentum mismatch). What I want instead is a *smooth* monotone decay from 0.1 to ~0 with no discontinuities and no milestone hyperparameters. So the question is: which smooth decreasing curve?

The crudest smooth option is a straight line: eta goes linearly from 0.1 to 0 over the body. Let me feel out whether linear is the right shape by thinking about where in training I want to spend my "rate budget." Early in the body the model is still far from any good minimum and the gradient is large and informative — I want to *stay* at a high rate here and make fast progress; dropping the rate quickly early would waste the most productive phase. Late in the body I'm closing in on a minimum and need the rate to come down slowly and spend a lot of time *small*, to fine-tune below the noise floor and actually resolve the minimum. So the ideal curve should be *flat-ish near the top* (linger at high rate), then fall through the middle, then *flat-ish near the bottom* (linger at low rate). A straight line leaves the top at a constant rate and reaches the bottom only at the very last instant — it has the right endpoints but possibly the wrong derivative profile. Before I reach for something fancier I should check that this "lingering" intuition is real and not just a story, because if a line already spends most of its time near the extremes there is no point complicating the curve.

Let me make "linger" concrete. Over the body (epochs 5..199), count how many epochs a line spends with eta in the top region (eta >= 0.075, i.e. above 75% of base_lr) versus the bottom region (eta <= 0.025). For the line eta = base_lr*(1 - progress), eta >= 0.075 means progress <= 0.25, which is the first quarter of the body, and eta <= 0.025 means progress >= 0.75, the last quarter — so a line spends roughly a quarter of the body high and a quarter low, about 49 epochs each out of 195. That is the baseline I want to beat. Now I'm looking for a decreasing curve that, for the same endpoints base_lr -> 0, spends *more* of its epochs parked in the top and bottom regions and rushes through the middle — i.e. a curve whose slope is near zero at both ends and steepest in the middle.

What function has slope zero at both ends of an interval and a single steep middle? A half-period of a cosine. Picture cos over the interval where its argument goes from 0 to pi: cos(0) = 1, cos(pi) = -1, it decreases monotonically the whole way, and its derivative -sin is zero at both ends (at 0 and pi) and most negative at the midpoint pi/2. That's precisely the profile I described — flat at the top, steep in the middle, flat at the bottom. So map the body onto that. Let progress go from 0 at the start of the body to 1 at the end; feed pi*progress into cosine. cos(pi*progress) runs from +1 down to -1. I need to rescale that from the [+1, -1] range to the [base_lr, 0] range. The affine map that sends +1 -> base_lr and -1 -> 0 is base_lr * (1 + cos(pi*progress))/2: at progress 0, (1+1)/2 = 1, so eta = base_lr; at progress 1, (1 + (-1))/2 = 0, so eta = 0. Let me write it with a general floor too, in case I don't want to hit exactly zero — sending +1 -> eta_max and -1 -> eta_min gives eta = eta_min + (eta_max - eta_min)*(1 + cos(pi*progress))/2. With eta_min = 0 and eta_max = base_lr this collapses to base_lr*(1 + cos(pi*progress))/2. For a single decay with no plan to ever raise the rate again, eta_min = 0 is the natural choice: I want the smallest possible steps at the very end to settle into the minimum, and 0 is the floor.

Let me double check the shape claim with the derivative, because the whole reason I'm comparing cosine against a line is the slope profile. d/d(progress) of base_lr*(1 + cos(pi*progress))/2 is -(base_lr*pi/2) * sin(pi*progress). At progress 0 and progress 1, sin(0) = sin(pi) = 0, so the slope is zero at both ends of the cosine body — the rate barely changes near the top and near the bottom. At progress 1/2, sin(pi/2) = 1, so the derivative is -base_lr*pi/2 ~= -0.157, most negative, and the slope magnitude is maximal — the fast decay happens in the middle of the body. That is the right qualitative profile, but I set up the line comparison as a count, so let me settle it with the same count rather than leaving it at "the slope is flatter." Repeating the tally for eta = base_lr*(1+cos(pi*progress))/2 over epochs 5..199: the cosine keeps eta >= 0.075 for 66 epochs versus the line's 49, and keeps eta <= 0.025 for 64 epochs versus the line's 48. So against the very criterion I cared about, the cosine parks about a third more of its body epochs in each tail region and spends correspondingly fewer in the fast-moving middle — it lingers high and lingers low, and the line does not, both confirmed by the counts and not just by the shape of the formula. That decides it in favor of cosine. It also has no milestones (the only inputs are the start of the body and its length), the cosine body is smooth, and the stitch has no learning-rate jump, though it does have a slope change from the linear ramp into the flat-tangent cosine. So there is no cliff to shock the dynamics or suddenly mismatch momentum history.

Now stitch the two phases and be careful about the seam. The ramp occupies epochs 0..4 and ends at exactly base_lr at epoch 4. The cosine body should *begin* at base_lr so the sampled curve is continuous across the seam. So the cosine's "progress" clock must be measured from the *end of the warmup*, spanning the remaining (total_epochs - warmup) epoch interval — not from epoch 0. If I instead measured progress from epoch 0 over the full 200, the cosine at the seam (epoch 5) would already be slightly below base_lr and I'd have a value drop, and worse, the warmup epochs would eat into the cosine's budget. Measuring from the end of warmup is the clean choice: progress = (epoch - warmup) / (total_epochs - warmup). Let me check the seam by evaluating both branches at the boundary. The warmup branch at epoch 4 gives 0.1. The cosine branch at epoch = warmup = 5 has progress = 0, cos(0) = 1, eta = base_lr*0.5*(1+1) = 0.1 — so the two branches meet at 0.1 with no jump across the seam. Now the far end: at the last in-loop epoch, epoch = 199, progress = (199 - 5)/(200 - 5) = 194/195 ~= 0.99487, and eta = 0.1*0.5*(1 + cos(pi*0.99487)). cos(pi*0.99487) ~= cos(3.1255) ~= -0.99987, so eta ~= 0.1*0.5*(1 - 0.99987) ~= 6.5e-6 — just above 0, not exactly 0. The mathematical floor eta = 0 only occurs at progress = 1, i.e. epoch = total_epochs = 200, which is the boundary *after* the last training epoch and is never actually sampled by the loop. So the last epoch trains at ~6.5e-6 rather than at literally 0, which is what I want anyway — a vanishingly small but nonzero final step. That matches the usual scheduler convention where T_cur/T_i reaches 1 at the end of the interval, not at the last integer sample inside it.

So the whole schedule, piecewise: for epoch < 5, the linear ramp eta = base_lr*(epoch+1)/5; for epoch >= 5, the cosine body eta = base_lr*(1 + cos(pi*(epoch - 5)/(total - 5)))/2. One branch on the epoch index, two short formulas, only a small fixed warmup length, and the choice of cosine for the body (which I derived from the slope profile). It conditions on nothing in config — the same curve serves ResNet-20 on CIFAR-10, ResNet-56 on CIFAR-100, and MobileNetV2 on FashionMNIST, because the two arguments (survive the sharp init, then anneal smoothly to settle) are architecture-agnostic; I'd only reach for arch/dataset-specific tweaks if a particular model showed it needed them, and the point of this curve is to not need them.

Let me make sure the per-epoch granularity is fine, because the whole pitch against step decay is "no shocks," and a coarse staircase could smuggle a shock back in. The harness calls get_lr once per epoch and writes the returned float into every parameter group for that whole epoch, so within an epoch the rate is constant and it steps down at epoch boundaries. The cosine is sampled at 195 points across the body (epochs 5..199). The question is how big the *largest* single epoch-to-epoch drop is, since that is the worst staircase step. The drop is biggest where the slope is steepest, at the midpoint of the body (progress ~ 1/2). Analytically the per-epoch change there is about |d eta/d progress| * (1/195) = (base_lr*pi/2)/195 = 0.1*1.5708/195 ~= 8.1e-4. Let me confirm by actually differencing the sampled curve: the midpoint of the body is epoch 102 (eta = 0.0504) and epoch 103, and eta(102) - eta(103) ~= 8.06e-4 — matching the analytic estimate. So the worst single drop the optimizer ever sees in the body is ~0.0008, well under base_lr/100 = 0.001, and every drop away from the midpoint is smaller. Compare that to step decay, whose drops are a factor of 5 or 10 — a single-epoch fall of 0.08 (from 0.1 to 0.02). The cosine's worst step is two orders of magnitude gentler, so there is effectively no shock. A per-batch evaluation would be smoother still but per-epoch is what this pipeline gives me and it's more than enough.

The first warmup value, base_lr/5 = 0.02 in this pipeline, is five times smaller than the target rate. I should be honest that this is not a universal proof of eta < 2/lambda_max for every possible Hessian — I do not know lambda_max of these particular nets at init and cannot compute it here — but it is the part of the schedule that deliberately reduces eta while the local curvature ceiling is expected to be tightest, and a 5x reduction is a meaningful margin against that ceiling. I'd want to verify on a real run that the early training-error spike I'm worried about does not appear with this prefix; that is the one claim in this whole construction I cannot settle on paper.

Let me write it as the function the harness calls, the single editable slot:

```python
import math


def get_lr(epoch, total_epochs, base_lr, config):
    """Learning rate for this epoch: linear warmup, then cosine decay toward 0.

    Epochs 0..warmup-1: ramp linearly from base_lr/warmup up to base_lr,
                        so early steps are reduced while the curvature ceiling
                        2/lambda_max is tight and the sharp initial region relaxes,
                        with no discontinuity (gentle on the momentum velocity).
    Epochs warmup..end: cosine anneal from base_lr toward 0, measured from the
                        end of warmup. The cosine's slope is ~0 at both ends and
                        steepest in the middle, so the rate lingers high early
                        (fast progress while far from a minimum), falls through
                        the middle, and lingers low late (settle below the
                        gradient-noise floor). No milestones, no cliffs.
    """
    warmup = 5
    if epoch < warmup:
        # linear ramp: base_lr/warmup, 2*base_lr/warmup, ..., base_lr
        return base_lr * (epoch + 1) / warmup
    # cosine body, clock starts at the end of warmup so it opens at base_lr
    progress = (epoch - warmup) / (total_epochs - warmup)   # 0 at seam, floor at next boundary
    return base_lr * 0.5 * (1 + math.cos(math.pi * progress))
```

The last epoch trains at ~6.5e-6, a real near-zero tail. What I cannot settle here is the load-bearing physical premise — that base_lr/5 actually sits under 2/lambda_max at init for these specific nets, and that the warmup removes the early spike — because that depends on Hessians I can't evaluate on paper; that is the one thing I'd put on a real run before trusting the schedule.
