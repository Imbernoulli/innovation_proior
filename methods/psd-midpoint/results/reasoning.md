Let me start from what actually costs me at inference. I have a flow or diffusion model, and to draw a single sample I integrate `dot x_t = b_t(x_t)` from a Gaussian at `t=0` to the data distribution at `t=1`. The samples are gorgeous, but every step of the ODE solve is one forward pass of a big network, and an accurate solve is tens to hundreds of passes. I want a sample in one pass, or a small handful. So I don't want the velocity `b_t` — the *instantaneous* rate — I want the thing that integrates it: a map that, given a point `x_s` on a trajectory at time `s`, hands me back the point `x_t` on the *same* trajectory at any later time `t`, for an arbitrary gap `t-s`. Call it `X_{s,t}`. If I had it, then `X_{0,1}(x_0)` with `x_0` Gaussian is a finished sample with no integration at all, and if I want to spend a bit more compute for a bit more fidelity I chain a few jumps over a coarse grid. The whole game becomes: train this two-time map directly, stably, and ideally as cheaply and robustly as I already train a flow-matching velocity — and without first training a separate teacher and distilling it, which is the two-phase pipeline I'm trying to escape.

So what do I actually know about `X_{s,t}` for free? It's defined by `X_{s,t}(x_s) = x_t` along solutions of the probability flow. Two facts fall out instantly. On the diagonal it's the identity: `X_{t,t}(x) = x`, because jumping from `t` to `t` does nothing. And if I take the time derivative of the defining relation at the upper time, `partial_t X_{s,t}(x_s) = dot x_t = b_t(x_t) = b_t(X_{s,t}(x_s))`; now close the gap, `s -> t`, and since `X_{t,t}(x) = x` this collapses to `lim_{s->t} partial_t X_{s,t}(x) = b_t(x)`. The velocity field is *implicit* in the map — it's the map's own time-derivative at coincident times. That's a gift: it means the diagonal of the map is pinned to exactly the velocity I already know how to learn by flow matching. The hard part, the genuinely open part, is everything *off* the diagonal, where the gap is finite and there is no direct regression target sitting in front of me — `dot I_t` gives me the instantaneous velocity, but nothing gives me "the right place a finite jump of size `t-s` should land."

Let me pick a parameterization before I go further, because the raw map has too much freedom and the boundary conditions matter. The cleanest thing that bakes in `X_{t,t}(x) = x` automatically is an Euler-step shape, `X_{s,t}(x) = x + (t-s) v_{s,t}(x)`, where `v` is the network I'll actually train. This isn't a truncated Taylor series — it's an exact rewrite, just a shift and a rescaling of `X`, so I lose no expressivity; `v_{s,t}` is whatever it has to be. And it's lovely because it makes the diagonal relation explicit: from this form, `lim_{s->t} partial_t X_{s,t}(x) = v_{t,t}(x)`, so combined with the tangent fact, `v_{t,t}(x) = b_t(x)`. The *same network* `v`, restricted to the diagonal, *is* the flow-matching velocity. So I can train `v_{t,t}` for free with the standard objective `E |v_{t,t}(I_t) - dot I_t|^2`, and the entire remaining problem is: how do I get a learning signal for `v_{s,t}` when `s != t`? Geometrically `v_{s,t}(x)` is the *slope of the chord* between `x_s` and `x_t` on one trajectory, and the diagonal says the chord between two infinitesimally close points is the velocity. I need to determine the chord for far-apart points.

The naive idea: I know the trajectory, just integrate it and regress the chord. But that's the thing I'm trying to avoid — integrating is the expensive solve, and worse, regressing against the *true* `x_t` means I'd need ground-truth trajectories I don't have. I only have interpolant pairs `(x_0, x_1)` and the diagonal velocity. So I can't get an external target for the off-diagonal. The signal, whatever it is, has to come from the map's *internal consistency*. Wall. Let me think about what internal consistency even means for this object.

The trajectory gives me one exact finite-gap relation. If I go from `s` straight to `t`, I land on `x_t`. If I instead go from `s` to some intermediate `u`, landing on `x_u`, and then from `u` to `t`, I land on `x_t` again — same trajectory, same endpoint. So `X_{u,t}(X_{s,u}(x)) = X_{s,t}(x)` for any intermediate `u` in `[s,t]`. This is just the trajectory being a single curve; it has to hold for the true map. And it's *exactly* a relation among finite jumps — no derivatives, no integration, no external target. The two sides both come from my own network. That's the off-diagonal signal I couldn't find: not "match the truth," but "be self-consistent across composition." Let me turn it into a loss by squaring the residual:

  `L = E | X_{s,t}(I_s) - X_{u,t}( X_{s,u}(I_s) ) |^2`,

averaged over `(s,t)` in the upper triangle and over the intermediate `u`. Start the trajectory at the interpolant point `I_s = alpha_s x_0 + beta_s x_1`, which is a genuine sample from `rho_s`. I should pause and check what is actually proved. If the proposal over `u` has full support on `[s,t]`, then pairing this residual with the diagonal flow-matching term gives a clean correctness theorem. Write `L_sd = L_b + L_psd`, where `L_b` is the diagonal velocity regression. The true map makes `L_psd = 0` exactly and makes `L_b` equal to its global minimum `L_b(b)`, so the true map achieves the lower bound `L_sd >= L_b(b)`. Conversely, suppose some continuous `v*` is a global minimizer hitting that bound. Then it must drive `L_b` to its minimum, so `v*_{t,t} = b` on the diagonal, and it must drive the full-support composition residual to zero, so the composition identity holds for all intermediate choices after using continuity to remove null-set language. A continuous map satisfying the composition identity with the correct diagonal velocity is forced to be the flow map: Taylor-expand the identity with `t` near `u`, `X_{s,t+h}(x) = X_{t,t+h}(X_{s,t}(x)) = X_{s,t}(x) + h v_{t,t}(X_{s,t}(x)) + o(h)`, which says `partial_t X_{s,t}(x) = v_{t,t}(X_{s,t}(x)) = b_t(X_{s,t}(x))`, the Lagrangian ODE whose unique solution (under the standard one-sided Lipschitz condition on `b`) is the flow map. So the full-support version is not a heuristic: it pins down the right map. The midpoint version I actually want is narrower — a deterministic slice of the same exact identity, not the full-support theorem itself — but the true map still zeros that slice exactly.

But before I get excited, the second known pain: bootstrapping. The target side `X_{u,t}(X_{s,u}(I_s))` is the model composed with itself — I'm asking the model to be consistent with its own outputs. Early in training the model is wrong, so I'm regressing the big jump toward a composition of two *wrong* small jumps, and the error of the inner jump `X_{s,u}` gets fed as input to the outer jump `X_{u,t}`, so errors compound and the input distribution to the outer call drifts away from anything the model has seen. This is the documented failure mode of every bootstrap scheme, and it's a real structural difficulty — I can't make it vanish, it's baked into "use yourself as your own teacher." What I *can* do is shape the information flow so it doesn't go unstable. The diagonal is where the external truth lives: `v_{t,t}` is anchored to `dot I_t`. I want that truth to flow *outward* from the diagonal into the off-diagonal, not the off-diagonal to corrupt the diagonal. So the off-diagonal `v_{s,t}` should learn to match the composition, while the composition itself should *not* try to adapt to `v_{s,t}`. Concretely: put a stopgradient on the target side. The big jump `X_{s,t}` is the student, carrying the gradient; the composed two-jump term `X_{u,t}(X_{s,u}(...))` is the teacher, frozen at the current parameters via `stopgrad`. This mimics the distillation setting that was stable — an external teacher the student chases — except the teacher is the model's own current self rather than a separately trained network. So `L_psd^{s,t} = | X_{s,t}(I_s) - sg[ X_{u,t}(X_{s,u}(I_s)) ] |^2`. I could maintain a separate exponential-moving-average teacher, `phi_k = delta phi_{k-1} + (1-delta) theta_k`, but the cleanest version of the stopgradient rule is simpler: differentiate with respect to the student parameters and then evaluate the frozen teacher calls at the current parameters, i.e. `phi = theta` or `delta = 0`.

Now I have a working off-diagonal loss in `X`-space. Let me actually expand it with the parameterization, because I suspect there's structure hiding in the `(t-s)` factors. Write each jump out: `X_{s,u}(x) = x + (u-s) v_{s,u}(x)`, and `X_{u,t}(X_{s,u}(x)) = X_{s,u}(x) + (t-u) v_{u,t}(X_{s,u}(x)) = x + (u-s) v_{s,u}(x) + (t-u) v_{u,t}(X_{s,u}(x))`. Set that equal to `X_{s,t}(x) = x + (t-s) v_{s,t}(x)`. The `x` cancels on both sides, and I'm left with a relation purely among the `v`'s:

  `(t-s) v_{s,t}(x) = (u-s) v_{s,u}(x) + (t-u) v_{u,t}(X_{s,u}(x))`.

Divide by `(t-s)`:

  `v_{s,t}(x) = ((u-s)/(t-s)) v_{s,u}(x) + ((t-u)/(t-s)) v_{u,t}(X_{s,u}(x))`.

The two coefficients are the fractions of the interval that the first and second sub-jumps cover, and they sum to one — it's a convex combination of the two sub-slopes, which makes total geometric sense: the chord slope over `[s,t]` is the length-weighted average of the chord slopes over `[s,u]` and `[u,t]`. Now here's why this `v`-space form is *better* than the `X`-space loss I started with, not just cosmetically equivalent. If I square the residual in `X`-space, the residual carries an overall factor of `(t-s)`, so the loss scales like `(t-s)^2`. That means the effective gradient magnitude — and hence the effective learning rate — depends on the gap size: large gaps get loud gradients, tiny gaps get whisper-quiet ones, and the optimizer sees wildly different step scales across the batch. That's exactly the kind of cross-`(s,t)` variance I want to kill. Working in `v`-space — squaring the residual of `v_{s,t} = (convex combination)` directly — divides that `(t-s)^2` out, preconditioning the loss so every gap contributes on a comparable scale. So I drop the `(t-s)^2` factor and define the off-diagonal loss as the squared residual of the slope identity:

  `L_psd^{s,t} = | v_{s,t}(I_s) - sg[ ((u-s)/(t-s)) v_{s,u}(I_s) + ((t-u)/(t-s)) v_{u,t}(X_{s,u}(I_s)) ] |^2`.

Now, that intermediate `u` — the free knob the correctness proof said I could pick however I like. Let me parameterize it as a convex combination too, `u = gamma s + (1-gamma) t` for `gamma in [0,1]`, because then the coefficients become beautifully simple: `(u-s)/(t-s) = 1-gamma` and `(t-u)/(t-s) = gamma`. (Check: `u-s = (1-gamma)(t-s)` and `t-u = gamma(t-s)`.) So the slope identity is just

  `v_{s,t}(x) = (1-gamma) v_{s,u}(x) + gamma v_{u,t}(X_{s,u}(x))`,

a clean `gamma`-weighted mix of the two sub-slopes with no interval lengths left in it at all. The remaining decision is *how to choose `gamma`*. The obvious thing is to draw it at random each step, `gamma ~ U[0,1]`, sweeping the intermediate node uniformly across the interval. That keeps the full-support theorem intact, and it gives an unbiased look at all decompositions. But let me think about what randomizing `gamma` actually costs. Each off-diagonal sample now has *two* sources of randomness for the same `(s,t)`: the data pair and the split point `gamma`. The teacher target `(1-gamma) v_{s,u} + gamma v_{u,t}(X_{s,u})` is a function of `gamma`, so as `gamma` rattles around, the target the student is chasing rattles too — I'm injecting extra variance into the regression target on top of the bootstrap noise I already can't avoid. If I collapse `gamma` to one deterministic value, I give up the clean full-support uniqueness proof, but I keep an exact residual of the true map and get a much sharper target. That is a practical stability trade.

Which single `gamma` should I fix it at? If I have to pick one decomposition of `[s,t]` into two sub-jumps, the natural choice is the *midpoint*, `gamma = 1/2`, so `u = (s+t)/2`. Why the midpoint specifically and not, say, `gamma = 1/4`? Symmetry and balance. The two sub-jumps `[s,u]` and `[u,t]` then have equal length, so neither leg dominates the composition; the teacher weights become `1/2` and `1/2`, an even average of the two sub-slopes. A lopsided split would make one leg a near-full jump (which is almost as hard to get right as the full jump I'm trying to teach — defeating the point of decomposing) and the other a near-trivial jump (which carries almost no information). The midpoint puts the auxiliary node at the geometric center, the place that maximizes how much *easier* each sub-jump is than the whole, while keeping the two legs balanced. It's also exactly the recursive structure that the dyadic-grid bootstrap schemes use — two equal half-steps composing into one full step — except here it falls out continuously, for any `(s,t)`, rather than being tied to a fixed grid. So midpoint sampling: `gamma = 1/2` deterministically, `u = (s+t)/2`, teacher `= 1/2 (v_{s,u} + v_{u,t}(X_{s,u}))`.

So the off-diagonal loss is now concrete:

  `L_psd^{s,t} = | v_{s,t}(I_s) - sg[ 1/2 ( v_{s,u}(I_s) + v_{u,t}(X_{s,u}(I_s)) ) ] |^2`,   `u = (s+t)/2`.

Three network evaluations per off-diagonal sample: `v_{s,t}(I_s)` for the student, `v_{s,u}(I_s)` for the first half-jump, and `v_{u,t}(X_{s,u}(I_s))` for the second half-jump — and note the second one is evaluated at `X_{s,u}(I_s) = I_s + (u-s) v_{s,u}(I_s)`, the *output* of the first half-jump, which is what makes this a genuine composition and also what feeds the bootstrap. The two teacher legs are inside the stopgradient, so I don't backprop through them; only the student leg `v_{s,t}` carries the gradient.

Let me now handle the loss-scale heterogeneity, because even after the `(t-s)^2` preconditioning, residual magnitudes can differ a lot across `(s,t)` pairs — different regions of the time-square are intrinsically harder and noisier, and if I just average raw squared residuals the loud pairs dominate the gradient. The fix carried over from single-time training is uncertainty weighting: attach a learned per-`(s,t)` weight `w_{s,t}` — a small head that predicts the loss log-variance — and replace the raw loss by `exp(-w_{s,t}) * loss + w_{s,t}`. Why this exact form? Read it as a Gaussian negative-log-likelihood with predicted log-variance `w`: the `exp(-w)` downweights pairs the model expects to be noisy, and the bare `+w` is the normalizer that stops the model from just sending all weights to `-infinity` to trivially shrink the loss. At the optimum `w_{s,t}` tracks the actual log-variance of the residual at `(s,t)`, so every pair contributes on a comparable scale — the gradient stops being hijacked by the few highest-variance pairs, and I can run a larger learning rate without diverging. I generalize the single-time EDM2 weight to take *both* times; in code `calc_weight(s,t)` returns `w_{s,t}`. The diagonal term gets the same treatment with `w_{t,t}`.

Now assemble the full objective. The diagonal carries the only external truth, so I want most of the compute there: split each batch with a fraction `eta` on the diagonal (flow matching) and `1-eta` on the off-diagonal (the consistency term). All three would-be off-diagonal characterizations reduce to flow matching as `s -> t` anyway — I checked the limit: as the gap closes, `v_{s,t} -> v_{t,t}` and the consistency residual degenerates into the diagonal velocity condition — so using `L_b` *on* the diagonal is the natural `s=t` boundary of the same loss, not a separate gadget bolted on. I put `eta = 0.75` of the batch on the diagonal: the off-diagonal term costs three network evaluations versus one for the diagonal, and the diagonal is where the grounding signal is, so I want the majority of the effort learning the flow and proportionally less distilling it into finite jumps. The diagonal term is the weighted velocity regression,

  `L_b^t = exp(-w_{t,t}) | v_{t,t}(I_t) - dot I_t |^2 + w_{t,t}`,   `dot I_t = dot alpha_t x_0 + dot beta_t x_1`,

and the off-diagonal pairs `(s,t)` are drawn uniformly from the upper triangle `s < t` (sample two uniform times and order them), with `u` set to their midpoint.

Let me sanity-check this lands somewhere sensible by seeing what it specializes to. If instead of a continuous `(s,t)` I restricted to a dyadic grid and fixed the intermediate node to the grid midpoint, the off-diagonal term becomes "one full grid step matches two half grid steps composed," recursively over the grid — which is precisely the discrete bootstrap recipe of shortcut models. So that prior recipe is the grid-restricted special case of this midpoint consistency loss, and the present scheme is its continuous-time generalization that works for arbitrary `(s,t)`. And the frozen-teacher progressive-distillation idea — one student step reproducing two teacher steps with a halving schedule — is the same composition relation but with an external pre-trained teacher instead of the model's own current parameters. Seeing both fall out as special cases tells me I've found the general principle rather than a fourth isolated recipe.

One honest caveat I should hold onto rather than paper over: I could *prove* the diagonal-plus-consistency objective has the right minimizer, but I can't get a clean bound saying "small loss implies small Wasserstein error to the data" for this scheme, the way I can when the off-diagonal residual is a derivative-based (Lagrangian or transport) relation. The obstruction is exactly the bootstrap: the composition target is a chain of model jumps, so loss small *at the current iterate* doesn't directly bound how far the generated distribution sits from the truth, because the errors of the inner jumps propagate through the outer jumps and through the shifting input distribution. That's a structural feature of bootstrapping small steps into large ones. The consistency loss buys me a scheme that needs *no spatial Jacobian* (unlike the transport-residual schemes, whose `nabla X` gradient is the thing that goes unstable on big image networks) and *no pre-trained teacher*, but it does not buy me the same loss-to-sample-error guarantee. It's the trade I'm making with eyes open.

Let me write it as the off-diagonal slot of the training harness, in the JAX/Flax style of the actual network API — the map `X.apply(..., return_X_and_phi=True)` returns both the mapped point `X_{s,t}(x)` and its implicit slope `phi = v_{s,t}(x)`, `calc_b` returns the diagonal velocity `v_{t,t}`, and `calc_weight(s,t)` returns the learned `(s,t)` weight:

```python
import jax
import jax.numpy as jnp


def diagonal_term(params, x0, x1, label, t, rng, *, interp, X):
    """Diagonal (s = t) flow-matching term: v_{t,t}(I_t) must match dot I_t."""
    It = interp.calc_It(t, x0, x1)                       # I_t = alpha_t x0 + beta_t x1
    It_dot = interp.calc_It_dot(t, x0, x1)              # dot I_t = dot alpha_t x0 + dot beta_t x1
    bt = X.apply(params, t, It, label, train=True, method="calc_b", rngs=rng)  # v_{t,t}(I_t)
    velocity_loss = jnp.sum((bt - It_dot) ** 2)
    weight_tt = X.apply(params, t, t, method="calc_weight")                    # w_{t,t}
    return jnp.exp(-weight_tt) * velocity_loss + weight_tt   # uncertainty-weighted NLL form


def psd_term(params, teacher_params, x0, x1, label, s, t, u, h, rng,
             *, interp, X, psd_type, stopgrad_type):
    """Off-diagonal progressive self-distillation: the big slope v_{s,t} must equal the
    length-weighted mix of two sub-jump slopes (midpoint makes them equal half-jumps)."""
    Is = interp.calc_It(s, x0, x1)                       # start on a real sample x_s ~ rho_s

    # student: the full jump's implicit slope v_{s,t}(I_s)  (carries the gradient)
    X_st, phi_st = X.apply(
        params, s, t, Is, label, train=False, rngs=rng, return_X_and_phi=True
    )

    # teacher: the two composed sub-jumps, frozen (stopgrad) so the diagonal truth flows
    # outward into the off-diagonal rather than the off-diagonal corrupting it.
    if stopgrad_type == "convex":
        # first sub-jump s -> u: returns X_{s,u}(I_s) and slope v_{s,u}(I_s)
        X_su, phi_su = jax.lax.stop_gradient(
            X.apply(teacher_params, s, u, Is, label,
                    train=False, rngs=rng, return_X_and_phi=True)
        )
        # second sub-jump u -> t, evaluated at the OUTPUT of the first (the composition)
        X_ut, phi_ut = jax.lax.stop_gradient(
            X.apply(teacher_params, u, t, X_su, label,
                    train=False, rngs=rng, return_X_and_phi=True)
        )
    elif stopgrad_type == "none":                        # pure self-consistency, no stopgrad
        X_su, phi_su = X.apply(params, s, u, Is, label,
                               train=False, rngs=rng, return_X_and_phi=True)
        X_ut, phi_ut = X.apply(params, u, t, X_su, label,
                               train=False, rngs=rng, return_X_and_phi=True)
    else:
        raise ValueError(f"Invalid stopgrad_type: {stopgrad_type}")

    if psd_type == "uniform":                            # h is gamma; u = h*s + (1-h)*t
        student = phi_st
        teacher = (1 - h) * phi_su + h * phi_ut
    elif psd_type == "midpoint":                         # h unused; sampler sets u = 0.5*(s+t)
        student = phi_st
        teacher = 0.5 * (phi_su + phi_ut)
    else:
        raise ValueError(f"Invalid psd_type: {psd_type}")

    psd_loss = jnp.sum((student - teacher) ** 2)         # v-space residual: (t-s)^2 already divided out
    weight_st = X.apply(params, s, t, method="calc_weight")                    # w_{s,t}
    return jnp.exp(-weight_st) * psd_loss + weight_st     # same uncertainty-weighted form
```

with the intermediate time supplied as the midpoint `u = (s+t)/2` for the midpoint variant, the off-diagonal `(s,t)` drawn uniformly from the upper triangle, the teacher parameters set to the current parameters (instantaneous, `delta = 0`), and the batch split `eta = 0.75` diagonal / `0.25` off-diagonal.

The causal chain is now clear. I started needing a finite-gap jump map to escape the many-evaluation ODE solve at inference, and I knew its diagonal for free (the implicit velocity, learnable by flow matching) but had no external target off the diagonal. The trajectory's own structure gave me the only target available — a big jump must equal two smaller jumps composed, the semigroup identity — and squaring that residual, paired with the diagonal velocity term, pins down the true map for full-support intermediate sampling. The midpoint variant keeps the exact same identity at one deterministic split, where the true map still has zero residual, but trades the full-support theorem for a lower-variance teacher. To keep the inevitable bootstrap from corrupting the anchored diagonal, I made the composition a stopgradient teacher and the full jump the student. Expanding the composition under the Euler parameterization turned the `X`-space identity into a convex-combination identity among slopes, which both reads geometrically (the chord slope is the length-weighted average of sub-chord slopes) and divides out the `(t-s)^2` factor that would otherwise make the effective learning rate depend on gap size. Choosing the intermediate as a convex combination `u = gamma s + (1-gamma) t` turned the weights into `(1-gamma, gamma)`; fixing `gamma = 1/2` — the symmetric midpoint, equal half-jumps, a lower-variance deterministic node, and exactly the dyadic bootstrap made continuous — collapsed the teacher to the even average `1/2(v_{s,u} + v_{u,t}(X_{s,u}))`, trading the swept node's unbiasedness for a much sharper target. A learned per-`(s,t)` uncertainty weight equalizes the residual scale across the time-square so a few noisy pairs don't hijack the gradient, and a `0.75/0.25` diagonal/off-diagonal split puts the compute where the external truth lives. The result trains a one-/few-step map directly, with no pre-trained teacher and no spatial Jacobian, at the cost of three network evaluations per off-diagonal sample and the bootstrap's structural lack of a clean accuracy bound.
