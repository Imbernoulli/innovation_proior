Let me start from what I actually have in hand, because the shape of the signal is going to dictate everything. I have an SFT model that writes chain-of-thought math solutions, and a rule checker that, after a solution is finished, tells me one thing: did the final answer match the gold answer. That's it. One bit per response, maybe with a tiny format bonus stapled on. I want to do RL with this. And the very first thing I have to be honest about is that this reward is not a per-token reward and never can be — "is the final answer correct" is only defined once the whole solution exists. There is no ground truth that says token 412 was a good token. So whatever I build, the atom of feedback is: one scalar, for one complete trajectory, realized at the moment the answer is emitted, i.e. at the last token. Everything else along the way gets nothing directly.

The default move, the one everybody reaches for, is PPO. And I should walk through exactly why it's the default and then exactly where it hurts me here, because the hurt is the whole motivation. PPO maximizes the clipped surrogate: with the probability ratio r_t = π_θ(o_t|q,o_{<t})/π_θold(o_t|q,o_{<t}), it does E[(1/|o|) Σ_t min(r_t A_t, clip(r_t,1−ε,1+ε) A_t)] with ε around 0.2. I like the clip — it's a pessimistic lower bound, it only lets me ignore a ratio change when that change would *improve* the objective and forces me to feel it when it would worsen things, so one minibatch can't yank the policy off a cliff. That part I want to keep; it's just good hygiene for taking a step from samples. The problem is A_t. PPO gets A_t from Generalized Advantage Estimation, and GAE needs a learned value function V_ψ(state) that predicts the expected return from every state — every prefix (q, o_{<t}). So PPO is actually two networks: the policy and a critic of comparable size. On a memory-constrained setup that alone is painful, I'm paying double to train.

But the memory is the lesser of the two problems. Stare at what the critic is being asked to do *in my setting*. My reward is zero at every token except the last, where a single correctness scalar lands. GAE wants V_ψ to be accurate at each intermediate token so it can form A_t = (reward-to-go) − V at every step. So I'm asking a network to regress, per token, the expected eventual correctness of a half-written math proof — from a training signal that only ever appears, sparse and once, at the end of each trajectory. That's an ill-posed, high-variance regression, and it's leaned on hardest exactly where it's flimsiest. The critic is both expensive and shaky. So the question reframes itself: can I get the variance-reduction benefit a baseline gives me *without* fitting a per-token value function at all?

Let me go back to the bare policy gradient and see what's actually forced and what's a choice. For a trajectory-level return R (here, the correctness scalar), the score-function gradient is E[R · ∇_θ log π_θ(o|q)], and since log π_θ(o|q) = Σ_t log π_θ(o_t|q,o_{<t}), that's E[R · Σ_t ∇_θ log π_θ(o_t|q,o_{<t})]. This is unbiased but its variance is brutal. The standard fix is a baseline: subtract any action-independent b, E[(R − b)·Σ_t ∇ log π], and it stays unbiased because E[b · ∇ log π] = b·∇_θ E_π[1] = b·∇_θ 1 = 0 — the baseline term integrates to zero no matter what b is, as long as b doesn't depend on the action being scored. So I have total freedom in choosing b, and the only thing that matters is getting it close to the expected return so that (R − b) is small and low-variance. PPO's answer to "what's a good b" was "fit V_ψ." But V_ψ is precisely the thing I want to kill. So: is there a baseline I can get for free?

Here's the thing I keep circling back to. To do RL at all, I'm already going to sample multiple responses per prompt — I have to explore. So for a given question q, I draw a group {o_1, ..., o_G} from the current policy and check each one, getting rewards {r_1, ..., r_G}. Now look at the empirical mean of that group, mean(r) = (1/G) Σ_i r_i. What is it? It's a Monte-Carlo estimate of E_{o~π}[reward | q] — the expected return of this prompt under the rollout policy. That is *exactly* what a value function at the root state q would try to predict. And I got it for free; the samples were already drawn for exploration. So instead of training V_ψ to predict the prompt's expected return, I just *measure* it from the group I already have. The baseline for response i is the mean reward of its sibling responses to the same prompt. The critic evaporates.

There's a subtlety I should be honest about: the baseline theorem is exact only when the baseline for a sampled response does not depend on that response's own action. A leave-one-out group mean would have that property. The full-group mean that is simplest to compute includes o_i itself, so as an estimator of the plain uncentered policy gradient it carries a finite-G self-dependence; without the standard-deviation division, it shrinks the expected reward-gradient term by a factor (G−1)/G. That is not a sign error and it is not a new value function — it is the deliberate group-relative estimator used in the surrogate, trading the exact leave-one-out independence for one shared same-prompt baseline with very small self-effect when G is large. So the centered signal for response i is r_i − mean(r). Let me sanity-check the sign and feel of it: a response that's more correct than its group's average gets a positive coefficient (push the policy toward it); one that's worse than average gets a negative coefficient (push away). And critically, this *does* penalize wrong answers — if most siblings were correct, a wrong one sits below the mean and gets pushed down. Compare that to the cheap alternative I could have taken, rejection-sampling fine-tuning: there the coefficient is just I(o) ∈ {0,1}, reinforce correct ones at uniform intensity, ignore wrong ones entirely. The group-mean centering gives me a *signed* signal that grades responses against each other, where RFT gives me a 0/1 gate. That difference — penalizing below-average and reinforcing above-average, by how much — is the difference between reinforcement and filtered imitation. I want the signed one.

Now I have to decide how this single per-trajectory number becomes a per-token learning signal, because the surrogate is a sum over tokens, each term multiplying ∇_θ log π_θ(o_{i,t}|·). I have one advantage per response, A_i = r_i − mean(r), and I have |o_i| tokens. What do I attach to each token? Let me reason from the score-function structure rather than guess. The return for the trajectory is R_i, and there is no per-token value function to differentiate the credit — that's the whole point, I just threw it away. So the scalar trajectory coefficient multiplies every token score in the trajectory: E[(R − b) Σ_t ∇ log π] has the same (R − b) on each term of the sum. So I set Â_{i,t} = A_i for all t ∈ o_i. Broadcast the one response-level advantage uniformly to every token of the response. It feels coarse — surely some tokens mattered more than others — but to say which tokens mattered I'd need exactly the per-token value estimate I refused to fit. Without a critic, uniform broadcast is the honest credit assignment supported by a trajectory-level reward. I'll note "fine-grained credit" as something I'd want to validate later if I ever bring step-level signal back, but right now it would reintroduce the machinery I'm trying to remove.

Let me look at the centered signal r_i − mean(r) across prompts now, because I expect trouble. Easy prompt: nearly all of the G samples are correct, so the rewards are like {1,1,1,...,1}, mean ≈ 1, and r_i − mean(r) ≈ 0 for everyone — tiny signal. Hard prompt at the frontier: a wide mix, {1,0,1,0,0,1,...}, mean ≈ 0.5, and the centered values are around ±0.5 — large signal. Medium prompt: somewhere between. So the *magnitude* of my learning signal is being set by how lucky each prompt's group happened to be, not by how much I want to learn from that prompt. Across a batch, the high-spread prompts dominate the gradient and the near-unanimous prompts contribute almost nothing. That's not a property I chose; it's an artifact of using the raw centered reward. I want each prompt to contribute on a comparable footing.

The fix that suggests itself is to standardize within the group: divide the centered reward by the group's own standard deviation. r̃_i = (r_i − mean(r)) / std(r). Now each prompt's signal is a z-score — unit spread per group — so the high-variance and low-variance prompts contribute comparably, and the gradient stops being hijacked by whichever prompts had the widest reward spread in this particular batch. Let me check the degenerate case, because a std in a denominator always smells like a future divide-by-zero. If a group is exactly unanimous — all correct or all wrong — then std(r) = 0 and r_i − mean(r) = 0 too, so I get 0/0. I'll floor the denominator with a small ε, std(r) + ε, which makes a unanimous group contribute essentially nothing (numerator is zero anyway), which is the right behavior: a prompt where every sample agrees carries no relative signal to learn from. So r̃_i = (r_i − mean(r)) / (std(r) + ε).

Now a question that's easy to get wrong and that I want to settle deliberately: *where* in the pipeline should this normalization live? I could have whitened the rewards earlier — subtract a batch mean, divide by a batch std, clip outliers, whatever — before they ever reached this advantage stage. Let me think about what happens if I do both. Suppose upstream I rescale the raw reward by some per-prompt or per-batch factor, and then downstream I *again* subtract the group mean and divide by the group std. The group z-score is scale-and-shift invariant in a way that partly *undoes* an upstream affine transform — (a·r + b − mean(a·r + b))/std(a·r + b) = (r − mean(r))/std(r) for a>0 — so a clean affine upstream normalization is silently erased by the group standardization, wasted work. And a *non-affine* upstream transform (clipping, a length divide, a nonlinear squash) is worse: it isn't erased, it's compounded — the group statistics are now computed over a distorted reward, so the z-score I get is not the z-score of the actual correctness signal, and the two normalizations interact in a way I can't cleanly reason about. Both outcomes are bad. The conclusion is to keep the reward space *raw* — let the per-response correctness scalar pass through untouched, sitting at its last token — and put *all* the normalization in exactly one place, the group-relative advantage. One normalizer, applied to the honest reward, whose interaction with the gradient I understand completely. The reward-side transformation should therefore be the identity: take the per-response scalar exactly as the checker emitted it and leave it alone. So the first of my two empty slots — turn the raw reward tensor into the per-token reward tensor — collapses to "do nothing, just respect the mask." That feels almost too simple, but it's the *right* simple: any cleverness here would fight the one normalizer downstream.

Now the regularizer, because if I optimize this correctness signal to convergence the policy will over-optimize it — drift off fluent text, exploit the checker's blind spots, mode-collapse onto whatever degenerate string happens to grade as correct. The standard guard is a KL penalty toward the frozen reference (the SFT model) π_ref. RLHF folds this into the reward at each token: r_t = r_φ − β log(π_θ/π_ref), per token. Let me think about whether I want to do it that way here, and I don't think I do, for a reason that's specific to what I just built. If I fold the KL into the reward, then the thing I center and standardize by the group is no longer the pure correctness scalar — it's correctness minus a per-token KL, mixed together. My group mean and std would then be statistics of that *mixture*, and the clean interpretation of r̃_i as "how correct relative to siblings" is gone; the advantage now smears together "is this answer right" and "how far has each token drifted." I worked to make the reward a pure carrier of the correctness bit; folding KL in throws that away. So I'll keep the KL *out* of the reward and *out* of the advantage, and add it directly as a separate term in the loss. The advantage stays a clean normalized reward; the regularization is a parallel pull, not an entangled one.

How do I estimate this KL per token from single samples? I want D_KL[π_θ || π_ref] at each token, and I only have one sampled token there. The naive estimator is log(π_θ/π_ref) — unbiased for the KL in expectation, but on any given sample it can be *negative* (the KL is nonnegative, so a negative per-sample estimate is ugly and noisy as a penalty), and it's high-variance. Let me look for something better. Write r = π_ref/π_θ (the reference-over-policy ratio; I'll keep this convention). Consider the family of estimators −log r + λ(r − 1) for a free constant λ. Take its expectation under π_θ: E_θ[−log r] = E_θ[log(π_θ/π_ref)] = D_KL[π_θ||π_ref], and E_θ[r − 1] = E_θ[π_ref/π_θ] − 1 = Σ π_θ · (π_ref/π_θ) − 1 = Σ π_ref − 1 = 1 − 1 = 0. So for *any* λ, E_θ[−log r + λ(r−1)] = D_KL[π_θ||π_ref]: the whole family is unbiased, because the added λ(r−1) term has mean exactly zero — it's a control variate. Now I get to pick λ to make the estimator nice. With λ = 1 I get k3 = (r − 1) − log r. Why is that the good choice? Two reasons fall out. First, non-negativity: define h(r) = (r−1) − log r; h(1) = 0, h'(r) = 1 − 1/r which is negative for r<1 and positive for r>1, so r=1 is the global minimum and h(r) ≥ 0 everywhere — it's the Bregman divergence of −log at r=1, equivalently the gap in log r ≤ r − 1. So every per-sample estimate is nonnegative, matching the true KL, no more ugly negative penalties. Second, variance: near the small policy steps where this regularizer is meant to operate, write r = 1 + δ. Then −log r = −δ + δ²/2 + O(δ³), while r−1 = δ. Choosing λ=1 cancels the whole linear fluctuation and leaves δ²/2 + O(δ³), so this is the local minimum-variance control-variate choice around r=1; the exact finite-distance optimum for λ can differ, but it gives up the global non-negativity that λ=1 guarantees. So k3 = π_ref/π_θ − log(π_ref/π_θ) − 1 is unbiased, nonnegative, and low-variance in the regime where the KL leash is supposed to stay small — exactly what I want for a regularizer I'm adding straight to the loss.

Let me assemble the objective. Sample {o_1,...,o_G} from π_θold for each q, score them, form r̃_i = (r_i − mean(r))/(std(r)+ε), broadcast Â_{i,t} = r̃_i to every token, keep PPO's clipped surrogate per token, and subtract the per-token k3 KL with coefficient β:

  J(θ) = E[q, {o_i}~π_θold] (1/G) Σ_i (1/|o_i|) Σ_t { min( ρ_{i,t} Â_{i,t}, clip(ρ_{i,t}, 1−ε, 1+ε) Â_{i,t} ) − β [ π_ref(o_{i,t}|·)/π_θ(o_{i,t}|·) − log(π_ref(o_{i,t}|·)/π_θ(o_{i,t}|·)) − 1 ] }

with ρ_{i,t} = π_θ(o_{i,t}|q,o_{i,<t})/π_θold(o_{i,t}|q,o_{i,<t}). The (1/G)Σ_i averages over the group, the (1/|o_i|)Σ_t averages over tokens within a response.

I want to see this as a gradient, both to sanity-check it and to compare it against the cheaper recipes on equal footing. Simplify by assuming a single policy update per exploration batch, so π_θold = π_θ at the moment I differentiate; then ρ_{i,t} = 1, the clip is inactive (the ratio is exactly 1, inside [1−ε,1+ε]), and min(·,·) just returns ρ Â. The surrogate term's gradient is then (1/|o_i|)Σ_t Â_{i,t} ∇_θ log π_θ(o_{i,t}|·) — that's the plain score-function gradient with the group-relative advantage as coefficient, which is what I wanted. The KL term: ∇_θ of −β[π_ref/π_θ − log(π_ref/π_θ) − 1]. Let p = π_θ(o_{i,t}|·). The bracket is π_ref/p − log π_ref + log p − 1; its gradient is −π_ref/p² · ∇p + (1/p)∇p = (1/p)(1 − π_ref/p)∇p = (1 − π_ref/p) ∇ log p = −(π_ref/π_θ − 1) ∇ log π_θ. So −β times that is +β(π_ref/π_θ − 1)∇ log π_θ. Putting both together, the gradient is

  ∇_θ J = E[ (1/G) Σ_i (1/|o_i|) Σ_t ( Â_{i,t} + β(π_ref(o_{i,t}|·)/π_θ(o_{i,t}|·) − 1) ) ∇_θ log π_θ(o_{i,t}|·) ].

So in the unified policy-gradient form ∇J = E[(1/|o|) Σ_t GC · ∇ log π], my gradient coefficient is GC = Â_{i,t} + β(π_ref/π_θ − 1) = r̃_i + β(π_ref/π_θ − 1). Now I can line this up against the alternatives and *see* why mine is doing more. Supervised fine-tuning: GC ≡ 1, every selected token reinforced identically. RFT and online RFT: GC = I(o) ∈ {0,1}, reinforce correct responses uniformly, never penalize wrong ones. Mine: GC = r̃_i + (a small KL pull), where r̃_i is a signed, magnitude-graded number — strongly positive for a response well above its group's mean, strongly negative for one well below, near zero for an average one. That graded, signed coefficient is the substance: it differentially reinforces *and* penalizes by how good each response is relative to its siblings, which the 0/1 gate of RFT simply cannot express. Same score-function skeleton, much richer coefficient — and no critic anywhere in it.

Let me reconsider the broadcast one more time, now that I have the gradient in front of me, because I want to be sure I'm not leaving obvious signal on the table. The coefficient r̃_i is identical across all t in response i. Is there a sharper, still-critic-free choice? The only extra information I have is per-step structure, and to use it I'd need a per-step reward — a process signal that scores each reasoning step, then Â_{i,t} = Σ_{steps j ending at or after t} r̃_i^{(j)}, the sum of normalized future-step rewards. That genuinely gives finer credit. But it requires a per-step reward source — step annotations or a trained process reward model — which is exactly the heavy supervision and extra modeling error I'm avoiding by using a rule that only checks the final answer. From a single final-answer bit, there is nothing legitimate to differentiate the tokens by; the uniform broadcast is what that information content supports. So broadcast it is, and the finer-grained variant is a known door I'm deliberately not walking through here.

Now the constants and the degenerate cases, then I'll write it. ε in the std denominator floors a zero-spread group, and since such a group has zero numerator too, it contributes nothing — correct. There's a second degenerate case I have to handle in code: a group of size one (only one sample for some prompt id in this batch). The canonical fallback is mean = 0 and std = 1, so the lone scalar passes through rather than producing an undefined standard deviation; a real group-relative update should normally have G > 1, but this keeps the tensor path well-defined. The clip ε ≈ 0.2 is inherited straight from PPO and does the same job — bound the per-step policy change — so I keep it. β is the KL coefficient, small, set so the regularizer is a gentle leash, not a straitjacket. And recovering the scalar reward from the tensor: the per-response number lives at the last valid token of a (bs, response_length) tensor, so summing along the token axis (everything else is zero) recovers the per-response scalar cleanly — that's how I'll pull r_i out of the token-level tensor before grouping.

Let me also be precise about the trainer wiring, because this is where the design can be accidentally undone. I argued the KL belongs in the loss, not the reward. So the reward tensor that reaches the advantage estimator must still be the raw terminal correctness tensor, masked to valid response positions, and the k3 KL term must be computed separately from log probabilities. If I subtract the KL before the group statistics, I am back to centering and scaling a mixed quantity. The reward-side normalization I own is the identity; the group-relative standardization downstream is the sole normalizer of the correctness signal itself.

So I fill the two slots. The reward-side transform is the identity-with-mask: leave the per-response correctness scalar exactly where the checker put it. The advantage rule recovers the per-response scalar by summing over tokens, groups responses by their prompt id, subtracts the group mean and divides by (group std + ε), and broadcasts that one number to every token of the response through the mask. Two state-free passes, no value network, memory just the rollouts I already had.

```python
import torch
import numpy as np
from collections import defaultdict
from typing import Optional


@torch.no_grad()
def normalize_rewards(token_level_scores, response_mask, index=None,
                      epsilon=1e-6, config=None, **kwargs):
    """Reward-side slot. The correctness scalar is left raw at its last token;
    all normalization is deferred to the group-relative advantage stage, so the
    one normalizer downstream operates on the honest reward and nothing
    double-normalizes against it. The transform is the identity (respecting the
    response mask)."""
    return token_level_scores * response_mask


@torch.no_grad()
def compute_grpo_outcome_advantage(token_level_rewards: torch.Tensor,
                                   response_mask: torch.Tensor,
                                   index: np.ndarray,
                                   epsilon: float = 1e-6,
                                   norm_adv_by_std_in_grpo: bool = True,
                                   config: Optional[object] = None):
    """Advantage slot. One scalar per trajectory -> group-relative z-score ->
    broadcast uniformly to every token. No value function: the per-prompt group
    mean is the free Monte-Carlo baseline, the per-prompt std puts prompts on a
    comparable footing, and the single advantage is the critic-free
    per-token coefficient supported by a trajectory-level return."""
    # recover the per-response scalar: it sits at the last valid token, rest are 0
    scores = token_level_rewards.sum(dim=-1)               # (bs,)

    id2score = defaultdict(list)
    id2mean, id2std = {}, {}
    bsz = scores.shape[0]
    for i in range(bsz):
        id2score[index[i]].append(scores[i])               # group siblings by prompt id
    for idx in id2score:
        if len(id2score[idx]) == 1:                        # singleton: no siblings to be relative to
            id2mean[idx] = torch.tensor(0.0)
            id2std[idx] = torch.tensor(1.0)
        else:
            g = torch.stack(id2score[idx])
            id2mean[idx] = torch.mean(g)                   # free MC baseline (group mean)
            id2std[idx] = torch.std(g)                     # per-prompt spread
    for i in range(bsz):
        if norm_adv_by_std_in_grpo:
            # r_tilde_i = (r_i - mean(r)) / (std(r) + eps)  -- signed, group-standardized
            scores[i] = (scores[i] - id2mean[index[i]]) / (id2std[index[i]] + epsilon)
        else:
            scores[i] = scores[i] - id2mean[index[i]]      # center only (no std scaling)
    # broadcast the one trajectory advantage to every token, masked to the response
    scores = scores.unsqueeze(-1) * response_mask          # (bs, response_length)
    return scores, scores                                  # advantages == returns
```

And the KL helper I use beside that advantage path is the same single-sample k3 form, with the numerical clamps kept where the trainer keeps them:

```python
def kl_penalty_forward(logprob, ref_logprob, kl_penalty="k3"):
    """k3 / low_var_kl: r = pi_ref / pi_theta = exp(ref_logprob - logprob)."""
    if kl_penalty not in ("low_var_kl", "k3"):
        raise NotImplementedError(f"unsupported KL penalty: {kl_penalty}")
    kl = ref_logprob - logprob
    kl = torch.clamp(kl, min=-20, max=20)
    ratio = torch.exp(kl)
    kld = (ratio - kl - 1).contiguous()                    # (r - 1) - log r
    return torch.clamp(kld, min=-10, max=10)
```

Let me retrace the causal chain so I'm sure it holds together. I started with a reward that is one correctness bit per complete trajectory, realized only at the last token — so per-token ground truth doesn't exist and any per-token value function is an ill-posed regression from a once-per-trajectory signal. PPO's critic is therefore both expensive (a second policy-sized net) and shaky (asked to be per-token-accurate from last-token-only reward), so I dropped it. The baseline theorem tells me why subtracting a prompt-level value is the right variance-reduction shape, and the same-prompt group gives me a measured version of that value: the group mean is a free Monte-Carlo estimate of the prompt's expected return, with the full-group implementation carrying only the finite-G self-dependence I already accounted for. Centering by that group mean gives a signed advantage that both reinforces above-average and penalizes below-average responses, unlike RFT's 0/1 gate. Because the centered signal's magnitude is hijacked by each prompt's reward spread, I standardized within the group, dividing by (std + ε), so prompts contribute comparably; the unanimous-group degeneracy is harmless because its numerator is zero. With no critic to differentiate tokens, the only credit assignment supported by a trajectory-level return is the one group-relative advantage broadcast uniformly to every token. I kept the correctness reward raw and made the group-relative standardization the *sole* normalizer, because any upstream normalization would either be erased by the group z-score (affine) or compounded with it unpredictably (non-affine). I kept the KL regularizer out of the reward and in the loss so the advantage stays a pure correctness signal, and estimated it per token with k3 = (π_ref/π_θ − 1) − log(π_ref/π_θ), which the control-variate argument makes unbiased, the Bregman/log inequality makes nonnegative, and the λ=1 choice cancels the leading small-step variance while preserving that nonnegativity. Reading the assembled objective as a gradient gives the coefficient r̃_i + β(π_ref/π_θ − 1), a graded signed signal that is strictly richer than SFT's constant 1 or RFT's 0/1 — and the whole thing runs as two stateless tensor passes over the rollouts I already had, with no value network at all.
