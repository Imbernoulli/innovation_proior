I start from the uncomfortable part of the problem: the learner receives reinforcement, not a target. If a unit outputs an action and the world returns one scalar `r`, there is no direct error signal saying that this unit should have output a different value. In the older adaptive search element picture, this is handled by trying actions stochastically and by keeping eligibility traces, but that still leaves me with a question I cannot ignore: why should multiplying reinforcement by a local trace be a gradient step rather than just a plausible reinforcement rule?

The first correction is to stop trying to differentiate the realized reward. The environment may be unknown, discontinuous, delayed, or effectively a black box. The differentiable object I can actually write down is the probability law of the stochastic unit. For unit `i`, I have a conditional distribution `g_i(y_i | w_i, x_i)`. The objective is not the reward from this one sample, but the expected reinforcement under the current weights, `J(W) = E[r | W]`. If I can differentiate that expectation with respect to a weight, I have a real hill-climbing target.

I condition on the sampled output of the unit. Holding `W` and the unit input `x_i` fixed, the expected reward can be decomposed by the unit's possible outputs. For a discrete unit, this looks like `E[r | W, x_i] = sum_y g_i(y | w_i, x_i) E[r | W, x_i, Y_i = y]`. The key independence move in Williams's proof is that once the unit's output is fixed, the downstream reward distribution no longer depends directly on the particular weight `w_ij` except through the probability of having selected that output. That lets the derivative pass onto `g_i`.

So the derivative I want has the form `sum_y E[r | Y_i = y] d g_i(y) / d w_ij`. This is close, but it is not yet a sample rule. A sample gives me one realized `Y_i`, not the whole sum over possible outputs. If I try to use `r d g_i(Y_i) / d w_ij` directly, the expectation will include another factor of `g_i(Y_i)` from sampling, and I will be off by that probability. The missing division is the important step. Since `d g_i(y) = g_i(y) d log g_i(y)`, a sample of `r d log g_i(Y_i) / d w_ij` has expectation `sum_y g_i(y) E[r | y] d log g_i(y) / d w_ij`, which simplifies to the derivative term I need.

That is the whole estimator in its most compact form: take the derivative of the log probability of the sampled output and multiply it by the reinforcement. The log-probability derivative is not a cosmetic rewrite; it is the correction that turns a sampled output into an unbiased estimate of a derivative of an expectation. Modern texts call it the score-function or likelihood-ratio estimator. Williams calls the local log-derivative term the characteristic eligibility, and the algorithm family is REINFORCE.

Now I have to check that this local result really survives the network setting. A downstream weight can affect reward through later units and the environment, but the proof isolates one stochastic unit at a time. For `w_ij`, the input `x_i` comes from upstream parts of the feedforward network, so its distribution is independent of this downstream weight. Williams uses this to average the conditional result over `x_i` and obtain `E[Delta w_ij | W] = alpha_ij d E[r | W] / d w_ij` when the learning-rate factor is constant for that weight. Summing over weights then gives the theorem: the expected update has a nonnegative inner product with the true gradient, and with one common positive learning rate it is exactly proportional to the gradient.

The baseline is the next place where the derivation could break. Subtracting a reference level from reward is useful because raw reinforcement may be noisy or shifted, but if the baseline changes the mean of the estimator then the method is no longer the same gradient estimator. The proof works because the expected score is zero. For a discrete distribution, `E[d log g_i(Y_i) / d w_ij] = sum_y g_i(y) d log g_i(y) / d w_ij = sum_y d g_i(y) / d w_ij = d 1 / d w_ij = 0`. So any baseline that is conditionally independent of the unit's current sampled output cancels in expectation. This is stricter than "subtract whatever seems convenient"; the baseline must not smuggle in dependence on the very sample whose score it is multiplying.

With that condition, the update becomes `Delta w_ij = alpha_ij (r - b_ij) e_ij`, where `e_ij = d log g_i(Y_i | w_i, x_i) / d w_ij`. This is the formula that makes the old reward-modulated trace picture precise. It says which trace is needed: not any activity trace, but the score of the sampled output under the unit's own distribution. It also says why centering the reward can reduce variance without biasing the direction.

The Bernoulli case shows why the result looks familiar. If a unit emits `y in {0,1}` with probability `p`, then `d log g(y) / d p = (y - p) / (p(1-p))`. If `p` itself is the parameter and the learning factor absorbs `p(1-p)`, the rule becomes proportional to `r(y-p)`. If `p` is a logistic function of the net input, then the chain rule cancels the `p(1-p)` denominator and the characteristic eligibility for a weight is `(y_i - p_i) x_j`. That is the local form that makes associative reward-inaction and related actor rules appear as special cases rather than separate inventions.

The delayed-reward case initially looks harder because the reinforcement can arrive after many stochastic choices. If I treat the whole episode as one sampled object, however, its probability is a product over the stochastic choices made through time. The log of that product is a sum. Therefore the score for the trajectory is the sum of the per-time scores, and the same reward can multiply the accumulated eligibility: `Delta w_ij = alpha_ij (r - b_ij) sum_t e_ij(t)`. Williams describes this by unfolding the recurrent or time-dependent network. The eligibility trace is not merely a memory trick; it is the online way to accumulate the score of the sampled trajectory before terminal reinforcement is known.

This also explains the policy-gradient form. A stochastic policy samples actions from `pi_theta(a_t | s_t)`. The probability of a trajectory contains the product of the action probabilities controlled by `theta`; environment transitions may be unknown, but if they do not depend on `theta` directly, their derivatives are irrelevant. The score of the trajectory is `sum_t grad_theta log pi_theta(a_t | s_t)`. Multiplying that by return gives an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator. Replacing the full return with reward-to-go or with an advantage is a variance and credit-assignment improvement that preserves the same log-probability score structure when the baseline part has zero expectation.

The Gaussian case keeps me honest because the method is not only for binary action units. For a Gaussian unit with mean `mu` and standard deviation `sigma`, the score with respect to `mu` is `(y - mu) / sigma^2`, and the score with respect to `sigma` is `((y - mu)^2 - sigma^2) / sigma^3`. Williams also points out the exponential-family relationship where the score with respect to the mean can be written as output deviation divided by variance. The same principle is doing the work: use the derivative of the log density of the sampled output.

There is also a boundary with ordinary backpropagation. If the network has deterministic hidden layers feeding stochastic output units, the score at the stochastic outputs can be propagated backward through deterministic functions. The stochastic node contributes the log-probability derivative; ordinary chain rule carries it to earlier deterministic weights. For reparameterizable continuous noise, Williams notes that one can sometimes differentiate through the sampled value itself, such as `y = mu + sigma z` for Gaussian noise. But that is a different pathwise route and is not available for generic discrete units. The score-function route remains valuable because it does not require differentiating the reward through the sample.

The modern implementation artifact is almost terse after all this. In the Spinning Up examples, the policy produces a distribution, the taken action's log probability is read, and the loss is the negative mean of `logp * weights`. When `weights` is the episode return, reward-to-go, or advantage, automatic differentiation of that loss produces the negative of the same estimator. Minimizing the loss is therefore ascending the estimated expected return. The line `loss_pi = -(logp * adv).mean()` is a compact descendant of the Williams theorem.

I also have to keep the limitations in frame. Unbiasedness is a statement about the estimator's mean, not about low variance or guaranteed fast learning. A single scalar reward can be a very noisy multiplier, and poor baselines can make the estimator impractical. Williams explicitly warns that the algorithms can be slow, can settle at poor optima, and do not come with a general convergence proof. Mohamed and coauthors make the same modern point: the score-function estimator is broadly applicable, including to black-box or discrete stochastic systems, but variance reduction is central.

So the reconstructed reasoning is this: stochasticity creates a probability distribution I can differentiate even when the reward mechanism is opaque. The sampled output needs to be weighted by the derivative of its log probability because that exactly cancels the sampling probability and recovers the derivative of the expectation. Multiplying that score by reward gives an unbiased stochastic gradient estimate; subtracting an output-independent baseline leaves the mean unchanged; summing scores through time handles delayed reinforcement; and the same structure becomes the policy-gradient update used in modern code.
