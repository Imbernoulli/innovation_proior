The starting difficulty is that a connectionist controller is asked to learn from a single scalar the world hands back after it acts. That number reports whether the outcome was good or bad, but it never says which unit should have emitted which value, which action would have been better, or how the environment would have answered a nearby alternative. The familiar supervised move — compare an output to a target and descend the error — is simply not available, because there is no target. Earlier adaptive-control work makes progress by building the action selectors out of stochastic units, so that weights bias a random choice and reinforcement later strengthens or weakens traces of recent activity; this is enough to learn things like cart-pole balancing where the dynamics are unknown and the feedback is delayed until failure. Temporal-difference prediction adds a critic that converts delayed outcomes into denser ongoing evaluations, which addresses temporal credit assignment. But neither of these settles the actor-side question I keep returning to: the action selector sampled an output, and no teacher ever supplied the derivative of reward with respect to that sampling probability. Reward-modulated eligibility traces — each weight holds a local trace and later multiplies it by a scalar evaluation — have exactly the right shape, and special cases behave well, but a plausible-looking local rule is not enough. What is missing is a one-sample quantity, computable at the stochastic unit itself, that we can prove actually points uphill on a well-defined objective, that tolerates a reference level subtracted from reward, that accumulates through time when reward is delayed, and that reduces to the known local rules for Bernoulli units while still covering broader stochastic policies.

I propose REINFORCE, the score-function (likelihood-ratio) policy-gradient estimator. The first and decisive move is to stop trying to differentiate the realized reward at all. The environment may be unknown, discontinuous, delayed, or an outright black box, so the reward itself is the wrong thing to differentiate. The object I can actually write down and differentiate is the probability law of the stochastic unit: for unit $i$, a conditional distribution $g_i(y_i \mid w_i, x_i)$ over its sampled output. And the right objective is not this one episode's reward but the expected reinforcement under the current weights, $J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}[r]$. If I can differentiate that expectation with respect to a weight, I have a genuine hill-climbing target instead of a heuristic. Conditioning on the unit's sampled output and decomposing the expected reward over its possible values gives, for a discrete unit, $\mathbb{E}[r \mid W, x_i] = \sum_y g_i(y \mid w_i, x_i)\,\mathbb{E}[r \mid W, x_i, Y_i = y]$. The crucial independence is that once the output is fixed, the downstream reward distribution no longer depends on the weight $w_{ij}$ except through the probability of having selected that output, so the derivative falls entirely onto $g_i$ and the wanted derivative takes the form $\sum_y \mathbb{E}[r \mid Y_i = y]\,\partial g_i(y)/\partial w_{ij}$.

That is correct but not yet a sample rule, because a sample gives me one realized $Y_i$, not the whole sum over outputs. If I naively used $r\,\partial g_i(Y_i)/\partial w_{ij}$, the act of sampling contributes an extra factor of $g_i(Y_i)$ and the estimate is off by exactly that probability. The fix is the load-bearing step, and it is the log-derivative identity
$$\nabla_\theta\, g_\theta(y) = g_\theta(y)\,\nabla_\theta \log g_\theta(y).$$
Because $\partial g_i(y) = g_i(y)\,\partial \log g_i(y)$, a sample of $r\,\partial \log g_i(Y_i)/\partial w_{ij}$ has expectation $\sum_y g_i(y)\,\mathbb{E}[r \mid y]\,\partial \log g_i(y)/\partial w_{ij}$, and the $g_i(y)$ from sampling cancels the $1/g_i(y)$ hidden in the log-derivative, recovering precisely the derivative term I needed. Differentiating the log probability of the sampled output is therefore not a cosmetic rewrite: it is the correction that turns one sampled action into an unbiased estimate of a derivative of an expectation. Williams names the local term $e_{ij} = \partial \log g_i(Y_i \mid w_i, x_i)/\partial w_{ij}$ the characteristic eligibility, and the one-sample estimator in its most compact form is just reward times this score,
$$\hat g(\theta) = (r - b)\,\nabla_\theta \log g_\theta(y),$$
which is unbiased for $\nabla_\theta J(\theta)$.

This survives the network setting because the proof isolates one stochastic unit at a time. A weight $w_{ij}$ can influence reward only through later units and the environment, but the unit's input $x_i$ comes from upstream parts of the feedforward network and so its distribution is independent of this downstream weight; averaging the conditional result over $x_i$ yields $\mathbb{E}[\Delta w_{ij} \mid W] = \alpha_{ij}\,\partial \mathbb{E}[r \mid W]/\partial w_{ij}$ when the learning-rate factor for that weight is constant. Summing over weights gives Williams's theorem: the expected update has nonnegative inner product with the true gradient, and under one common positive learning rate it is exactly proportional to $\nabla_\theta \mathbb{E}[r]$. The per-weight update that results is
$$\Delta w_{ij} = \alpha_{ij}\,(r - b_{ij})\,e_{ij},$$
which makes the old reward-modulated-trace picture precise: not any activity trace, but specifically the score of the sampled output under the unit's own distribution.

The baseline $b$ is where the derivation could quietly break, and the design choice there is sharp. Subtracting a reference level is worthwhile because raw reinforcement can be noisy or shifted, but it must not change the estimator's mean or the method is no longer the same gradient. What saves it is that the expected score is zero: for a discrete distribution, $\mathbb{E}[\nabla_\theta \log g_\theta(Y)] = \sum_y g_i(y)\,\partial \log g_i(y)/\partial w_{ij} = \sum_y \partial g_i(y)/\partial w_{ij} = \partial(1)/\partial w_{ij} = 0$. So any baseline that is conditionally independent of the unit's current sampled output cancels in expectation; this is a real constraint, not licence to subtract whatever is convenient, because a baseline that smuggles in dependence on the very sample whose score it multiplies would bias the direction. Centering the reward can thus cut variance without tilting the gradient — and choosing this score-function route over the obvious alternative of differentiating through the sample is precisely what keeps the method usable when the reward mechanism is opaque or the unit is discrete.

The special cases confirm the rule rather than introduce new ones. For a Bernoulli unit emitting $y \in \{0,1\}$ with probability $p$, the score is $\partial \log g(y)/\partial p = (y - p)/(p(1-p))$; when $p$ is a logistic function of the net input the chain rule cancels the $p(1-p)$ denominator and the characteristic eligibility for a weight becomes $e_{ij} = (y_i - p_i)\,x_j$, giving $\Delta w_{ij} = \alpha\,(r-b)\,(y_i - p_i)\,x_j$ — exactly the local form under which associative reward-inaction and related actor rules appear as special cases. For a Gaussian unit the same principle gives $\partial \log g/\partial \mu = (y-\mu)/\sigma^2$ and $\partial \log g/\partial \sigma = ((y-\mu)^2 - \sigma^2)/\sigma^3$, so the method is not confined to binary actions. Delayed reward, which looks harder because reinforcement can arrive after many stochastic choices, dissolves once a whole episode is treated as one sampled object: its probability is a product over the choices through time, its log is a sum, and so the trajectory score is the sum of per-time scores, $\Delta w_{ij} = \alpha_{ij}\,(r - b_{ij})\sum_t e_{ij}(t)$. The eligibility trace is then not a memory trick but the online way to accumulate the trajectory score before terminal reinforcement is known. This is also the policy-gradient form: a stochastic policy samples $a_t \sim \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t)$, the trajectory probability carries the product of action probabilities controlled by $\theta$, environment transitions that do not depend on $\theta$ contribute nothing to the derivative, and the trajectory score is $\sum_t \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t)$. Multiplying it by the return gives an unbiased stochastic gradient; replacing the full return by reward-to-go or by an advantage $A_t$ is a variance and credit-assignment improvement that preserves the same log-probability score structure as long as the subtracted part has zero expectation.

What I am careful to keep in frame is that unbiasedness is a statement about the estimator's mean, not about variance or speed. A single scalar reward is a noisy multiplier, poor baselines can make the estimator impractical, and there is no general convergence guarantee; the algorithms can be slow and can settle at poor optima. So baselines, critics, reward-to-go, and advantage estimates are not competitors to REINFORCE but practical variance-reduction layers wrapped around its unbiased score-function core. The modern implementation is almost terse after all of this: a policy produces a distribution, the taken action's log probability is read off, and the loss is the negative mean of log-probability times a reward-derived weight, so that automatic differentiation of the loss reproduces the negative of the same estimator and minimizing the loss ascends estimated expected return. When `weights` is the episode return, reward-to-go, or advantage, this single line is a direct descendant of Williams's theorem:

```python
logp = get_policy(obs).log_prob(act)
loss = -(logp * weights).mean()
```

and in full vanilla policy gradient, with `adv` the advantage multiplier,

```python
loss_pi = -(logp * adv).mean()
```
