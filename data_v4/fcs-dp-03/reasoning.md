The fuse rule is the entire problem. I may only combine two shards that are *currently adjacent*, and the fused shard takes the place of the two it replaced, so the left-to-right order never changes. That is exactly what stops this from being "repeatedly merge the two cheapest piles": I can never reach past a neighbor to fuse shard `1` with shard `5` while `2,3,4` sit between them. Before any algorithm I fix the arithmetic scale, because it fixes the data types. With `n <= 2000` and `w[i] <= 10^9` the total byte volume is only up to `2*10^12`, but the *cost* is larger — every fuse re-pays the full size of what it produces, so a byte is re-paid once for each fuse sitting above it, up to about `n` times in a lopsided schedule. That puts the worst-case total around `2*10^12 * 2000 = 4*10^15`: three orders past the 32-bit limit (`~2.1*10^9`), comfortably inside signed 64-bit (`~9.2*10^18`). Every size, prefix, and accumulator is `long long`. An `int` here passes every tiny test and silently loses the large ones, which is the worst kind of wrong.

Spelling out what a schedule is makes the structure obvious. Since fuses only ever join neighbors and preserve order, every complete schedule is a full binary tree whose leaves are the original shards *in order*, and each internal node is one fuse costing the total size of the leaves beneath it. So total cost = sum over internal nodes of subtree weight = sum over leaves of `w[i] * depth(i)`. Minimizing cost is choosing the tree shape that minimizes this depth-weighted leaf sum with the leaves locked into their given order — a contiguous-interval combination, which is a clean interval DP, not a Huffman heap.

That distinction is why the natural greedy — repeatedly fuse the cheapest adjacent pair — has no correctness argument. The last fuse on any block costs `sum(block)` no matter how the block is split, so the only thing a schedule actually controls is how the *interior* splits stack; greedy commits its first fuse on a local minimum and never reasons about that stacking globally, and on asymmetric weight spikes it does strictly worse than optimal. I keep it only as a foil and build the DP, which does carry a proof.

Let `dp[i][j]` be the minimum cost to collapse block `i..j` into one shard. Its last fuse joins a left part `i..k` with the right part `k+1..j` and costs `sum(w[i..j])` regardless of `k`, since by then both parts are single shards whose combined size is the whole block. So `dp[i][j] = min_{i<=k<j}(dp[i][k] + dp[k+1][j]) + sum(w[i..j])`, with `dp[i][i]=0`. Prefix sums give `sum(w[i..j]) = prefix[j+1] - prefix[i]` in O(1), and processing intervals by increasing length keeps every shorter interval ready when I need it. On the sample `w=[4,1,2,3]` the recurrence yields `dp[0][3]=19` with the optimal outermost split peeling the leading `4` (`k=0`), matching the schedule in the statement.

The plain recurrence is `O(n^2)` intervals times an `O(n)` scan over `k`, i.e. `O(n^3)` — about `8*10^9` inner iterations at `n=2000`, well past a 2-second budget. Correct but unshippable; the inner scan has to go.

The escape is that the optimal split is *monotone*. Writing `K(i,j)` for an optimal `k` of `dp[i][j]`, if `K(i,j-1) <= K(i,j) <= K(i+1,j)` then the inner search for `dp[i][j]` only has to sweep from `opt[i][j-1]` to `opt[i+1][j]`, and across one fixed interval length those windows telescope to `O(n)` total work — `O(n^2)` overall. The monotonicity needs the merge cost to be **Monge**. For `cost(i,j) = prefix[j+1] - prefix[i]`, the quadrangle inequality `cost(a,c)+cost(b,d) <= cost(a,d)+cost(b,c)` holds with *equality* — the prefix terms telescope to the same thing on both sides — and the inner-vs-outer monotonicity `cost(b,c) <= cost(a,d)` holds because every weight is nonnegative, so a sub-range sum never exceeds an enclosing one. Both conditions met, Knuth's optimization applies and computes the identical optimum quadratically.

So I carry two tables, `dp[i][j]` and `opt[i][j]` (a recorded optimal split). Base `dp[i][i]=0`, and I anchor `opt[i][i]=i` so the length-2 bounds have something to read. For `len` from `2` to `n` with `j=i+len-1`, I sweep `k` from `opt[i][j-1]` to `opt[i+1][j]`, keep the best `dp[i][k]+dp[k+1][j]`, add `range(i,j)`, and store the achieving `k`. One detail is not optional: a split index lives in `[i, j-1]`, but the anchored base leaks values just outside that at the smallest lengths — `hi=opt[i+1][j]` can equal `j`. If I let `k` reach `j`, the loop reads `dp[i][j]` (the cell I am still filling) and `dp[j+1][j]` (an interval that does not exist), both stale `0`s, so it invents a phantom zero-cost split and writes an illegal `opt[i][j]=j` that then corrupts the Knuth window of every larger interval reading it. Clamping `lo=max(lo,i)`, `hi=min(hi,j-1)` fixes it: at `len=2, i=0, j=1` it forces the single legal `k=0`, and every `dp[i][k]`, `dp[k+1][j]` read stays inside a strictly-shorter, already-computed interval.

I do not want to ship on the monotonicity argument alone, so I diff against an independent `O(n^3)` oracle that scans all `k` with no Knuth bounds, driven by a generator emitting small cases — degenerate `n=0,1,2`, small weights to force many ties in the DP, and occasional `10^9`-scale weights to exercise the 64-bit path. Over hundreds of random cases plus the explicit edges (`n=0`, `n=1`, equal big pair, ascending, descending, all-equal, big-uniform, spiky alternating), the quadratic DP reproduces the oracle's optimum exactly, zero mismatches.

Edge cases: `n=0` and `n=1` short-circuit to `0` before the DP since no fuse exists, and `n=2` is one length-2 interval costing `w0+w1`. The spiky `[1, 10^9, 1, 10^9, 1, 10^9, 1]` gives `9000000011`, with the big values kept shallow where that pays. Memory is the two `n*n` tables, `2000*2000*(8+4) = 48` MB inside the 256 MB limit, and `n=2000` runs in a small fraction of the 2-second budget. Parsing leans on `cin >>` skipping arbitrary whitespace, and output is exactly one integer and a newline.

So the shipped program is the `O(n^2)` Knuth-bounded interval DP in `long long`, printing `dp[0][n-1]`; the full module is in the answer.
