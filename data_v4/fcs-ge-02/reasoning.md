I have `m` half-planes, each the closed set `a*x + b*y <= c` with integer coefficients, and I must decide whether some real point `(x, y)` lies in all of them at once — a single `YES`/`NO`. Two numbers shape everything. `m` goes up to `2*10^5`, which kills anything quadratic in the number of half-planes: pairwise vertex enumeration is `O(m^3)` to test and hopeless at this scale, so I need `O(m log m)`. And `|a|, |b|, |c| <= 10^6`, which is quieter but more dangerous — products of two coefficients reach `10^12`, of three `10^18`, right at the edge where `long long` starts to strain. The precision story, not the algorithm skeleton, is where this problem is actually won or lost.

Pairwise vertex enumeration I keep only as a brute-force oracle. The feasible region, if non-empty, is a convex polygon (possibly unbounded) whose corners are intersections of pairs of boundary lines; testing each of the `O(m^2)` corners against all `m` constraints is trivially correct and makes a perfect check on small inputs, but it does not fit the limit. The route that does is incremental half-plane intersection: sort the half-planes by the angle of their boundary direction and sweep them into a double-ended queue that maintains the running convex boundary. Because the boundary of a convex intersection visits its edges in angular order, each half-plane is pushed once and popped at most once, so after the `O(m log m)` sort the sweep is linear. Right asymptotics; the whole difficulty is that the deque-pop invariant and the emptiness/unboundedness detection are notoriously fiddly to get exactly right, so I will derive every branch explicitly.

The generic move here is a two-variable LP/simplex feasibility routine, but a floating-point simplex on adversarial near-degenerate inputs gives wrong `YES`/`NO` answers right at the feasibility boundary, which is exactly where an exact judge probes. The structure I actually have is far more special than general LP — all constraints are two-variable, the intersection is a convex polygon, and "non-empty" is a purely combinatorial question about whether the directed boundaries wrap all the way around. That reframing is what lets me drop floating point entirely and decide with integers.

For the representation, I attach to each half-plane its line `(a, b, c)` and a direction along the boundary, and I want the interior (`a*x + b*y <= c`) to lie to the left of that direction — the standard convention for a counterclockwise hull of half-planes. The outward normal, pointing where `a*x + b*y` increases, is `(a, b)`. Rotating a direction `d` by +90 degrees gives `(-d.y, d.x)`; I want that rotation to point outward, i.e. to equal `(a, b)`, which solves to `d = (-b, a)`. With this convention, "point `r` is strictly outside the half-plane" is "r is strictly to the right of the directed boundary", `cross(d, r - p) < 0`, and that reduces to `a*r.x + b*r.y - c > 0` — exactly the algebraic statement that `r` violates the inequality.

The angular sort is where floating point usually creeps in via `atan2`, so I do it with integer comparisons only. Split directions into two halves: `half = 0` for angles in `[0, pi)` (`dy > 0`, or `dy == 0 && dx > 0`) and `half = 1` for `[pi, 2pi)`. Within a half, order is decided by the sign of the cross product of the two directions, `dx_i*dy_j - dy_i*dx_j`, whose magnitude is at most `2*(10^6)^2 = 2*10^12` and fits `long long`; I carry it in `__int128` anyway.

The corner where lines `i` and `j` meet is the solution of the 2x2 system, by Cramer:

```
D  = a_i*b_j - a_j*b_i        (this is exactly cross(d_i, d_j))
Nx = c_i*b_j - c_j*b_i
Ny = a_i*c_j - a_j*c_i
```

so the corner is `(Nx/D, Ny/D)` with `D != 0` for non-parallel lines. "Is that corner strictly outside half-plane `k`?" means `a_k*(Nx/D) + b_k*(Ny/D) - c_k > 0`, but I do not want to divide. Define `S = a_k*Nx + b_k*Ny - c_k*D`; then `a_k*x + b_k*y - c_k = S/D`, so the corner is strictly outside `k` iff `sgn(S)*sgn(D) > 0` — a pure integer sign test, no division, no epsilon. This is where the coefficient bound bites: `Nx, Ny, D` are around `2*10^12`, multiplying by another coefficient (`10^6`) gives `~2*10^18`, and once a box-sized corner near `4*10^12` enters, the worst term is coefficient times that, `~4*10^24`. That is thirteen orders below the `__int128` ceiling of `~1.7*10^38`, so `__int128` makes every predicate exact and safe while `long long` would silently overflow.

The sweep, sorted by direction angle, maintains the boundary as a deque `dq[lo..hi]`: for each new half-plane `cur`, pop the back while the back corner `inter(dq[hi], dq[hi-1])` is strictly outside `cur`, pop the front likewise, then push. After all half-planes a wrap-around cleanup removes members the first/last made redundant.

The first real subtlety is unboundedness. If the true intersection is unbounded (a single half-plane, or a wedge open to infinity), the deque never closes into a polygon and "is it empty?" becomes ambiguous. The fix is to intersect everything with a large axis-aligned bounding box first, so every non-empty region becomes a bounded polygon. The box must never cut off a genuinely feasible region: every corner of the arrangement is `(Nx/D, Ny/D)` with `|D| >= 1` and `|Nx|, |Ny| <= 2*10^12`, so any non-empty region contains a point with `|x|, |y| <= 2*10^12` (or, in the whole-line / whole-plane degeneracies, a point near the origin). A box at `B = 4*10^12` is comfortably larger than that and keeps box-involved products within the `__int128` budget above, so I add `x <= B`, `x >= -B`, `y <= B`, `y >= -B`.

Parallel half-planes need care. Two with the same boundary direction (`crossDir == 0`, directions agreeing so `dot > 0`) are redundant except for the tighter one, and after the angular sort they are adjacent, so I keep the more restrictive by comparing offsets `c/|normal|` on a shared nonzero axis component. The dangerous case is anti-parallel (`crossDir == 0`, `dot < 0`): two opposite half-planes whose feasible bands may fail to overlap, like `x >= 2` and `x <= 1`. That is an emptiness witness, and whether my first draft handles it is exactly what I need to test.

So I wrote the `O(m^2)` vertex-enumeration brute (same bounding box, exact `Fraction` arithmetic) with a random small-case generator and ran it against my solution. My first cut collapsed same-direction parallels before the deque pops, handled only the `dot > 0` case, and decided feasibility by `len >= 3` alone — a deque of at least three half-planes was taken to be a polygon. On paper that looked complete; over 600 cases it produced two mismatches, `sol = YES` where `brute = NO`.

Shrinking one failing seed to a four-constraint core:

```
-1 -3 0     (x + 3y >= 0)
 0 -5 0     (y >= 0)
 2  6 -1    (x + 3y <= -0.5)    [= 2x + 6y <= -1]
-2 -3 0     (2x + 3y >= 0)
```

Constraints 1 and 3 say `x + 3y >= 0` and `x + 3y <= -0.5` — a flat contradiction, so the region is empty and the brute correctly says `NO`, but my solution said `YES`. Dumping the final deque showed three survivors with directions at angles roughly `0`, `-0.59`, `-0.32`, all clustered within well under half a turn: not a closed polygon but an open wedge. My `len >= 3` test saw three half-planes and declared victory, but three half-planes whose directions all fit inside a half-plane enclose no bounded region. The two contradictory half-planes are anti-parallel (`(2,6) = -2*(-1,-3)`), so they sort a full `pi` apart, were never adjacent, and my same-direction-only collapse never compared them.

The diagnosis hands me the criterion. A bounded convex polygon — which, thanks to the box, is what every non-empty region becomes — has edge directions that span the entire circle: walking the boundary counterclockwise, the directions sweep a full `2pi`, equivalently every cyclic gap between consecutive sorted directions is less than `pi`. A gap of `pi` or more means all survivors fit in one half-plane, an open wedge, which (the box having otherwise closed any real region) signals emptiness. And the gap test is exact: the directed gap from `u` to the next direction `v` is less than `pi` iff `crossDir(u, v) > 0`. So after the sweep I require `crossDir > 0` for every consecutive pair including the wrap from `dq[hi]` back to `dq[lo]`; on the wedge above the gap `(5,0) -> (3,-2)` has `crossDir = -10 <= 0`, correctly rejected.

With the closure test the 600 cases passed, but scaling to 3000 surfaced two more `sol = YES / brute = NO` mismatches, each irreducible to a six-constraint core. Tracing one, the deque ended with three survivors whose directions did span the circle (closure passed) yet the region was empty. Checking those three survivors by themselves with the brute, they were feasible — so the deque had dropped a constraint that was actually binding, one that cut the survivors' region to nothing. The bug was the sweep order, not the emptiness test: running the same-direction collapse before the pops meant an anti-parallel contradiction that surfaced adjacent in the deque was never detected. Two corrections follow — the back/front pops must run first, then the parallel handling against the new deque back; and when `cur` is anti-parallel to the deque back (`crossDir == 0`, `dot < 0`), the intersection is empty and I stop immediately. With pops-first ordering, anti-parallel contradictions are driven to adjacency exactly when they conflict, and the `dot < 0` check catches them. Re-tracing both six-constraint cores, both now return `NO`.

The differential test then passed cleanly, but an oracle that shares my blind spots proves little, so I built a second one on a different principle: exact rational Fourier-Motzkin elimination with no bounding box at all — eliminate `x` by pairing every positive-`a` constraint with every negative-`a` one, then eliminate `y` to a 1-D interval and check it is non-empty. Vertex enumeration and variable elimination agreeing, one of them box-free, is much stronger than either alone. A three-way run (solution vs. vertex-enum vs. Fourier-Motzkin) over thousands of random cases plus adversarial modes — anti-parallel pairs, lines through the origin, tight single-point boxes, same-direction duplicates — gives zero mismatches.

The corner cases the two oracles cover, and where the box/closure logic is load-bearing: `m = 0`, the box alone survives as a square, closure passes, `YES` (whole plane feasible). Single half-plane: clipped by the box to a bounded polygon spanning the circle, `YES`. Anti-parallel disjoint bands (`x <= 1`, `x >= 2`): the `dot < 0` flag fires, `NO`. Degenerate-but-feasible regions — a single line (`x <= 0` and `x >= 0`), a single point (`x = 0` and `y = 0`) — the box fills the perpendicular directions so the survivors span the circle and closure passes, `YES`, matched by both oracles. A feasible region far from the origin whose corner sits near `2*10^12` is still inside the `4*10^12` box, `YES`. Every predicate is `__int128`, worst magnitude `~4*10^24`, thirteen orders under the ceiling. And `m = 2*10^5` runs in about 60 ms, far under the 2-second limit.

The final program is one self-contained C++ file: exact-integer half-plane intersection in `O(m log m)` — angular sort, deque sweep with pops-before-parallel ordering and the anti-parallel emptiness flag, a `4*10^12` bounding box, and the max-angular-gap closure test, every predicate an `__int128` sign test.
