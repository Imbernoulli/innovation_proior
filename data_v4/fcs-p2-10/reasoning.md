A rod of integer length `n`, a price `p[k]` for a piece of length exactly `k`, cuts free — I choose a composition of `n` (a multiset of piece lengths summing to `n`) that maximizes the summed prices, and print that maximum. Two facts about the numbers settle the shape of the solution before I pick an algorithm. First, the scale: `n <= 5000` and `p[k] <= 10^9`, so the worst-case revenue is `n` pieces at the top price, `5000 * 10^9 = 5 * 10^12`. That blows past the 32-bit signed range of `~2.1 * 10^9`, so the answer and every accumulator must be `long long` — an `int` here is a silent wrong-answer on the large tests, even though the index `n` itself fits an `int` fine. Second, the objective invites a greedy that I need to rule out first.

The seductive move is greedy by density: repeatedly cut off the length `k` with the best price-per-unit `p[k]/k` that still fits, then recurse on the remainder. It is short and resource-rational, but the constraint that the lengths sum to `n` is *global* while `p[k]/k` is a purely *local* figure of merit, so I try to break it before committing. Take `n = 4`, `p = [1, 5, 8, 9]`: densities are `1.0, 2.5, 2.667, 2.25`. Greedy grabs the densest length-3 piece for `8`, stranding a length-1 remnant worth `1`, total `9`. But two length-2 pieces tile the whole budget at density `2.5` with no scrap left over: `5 + 5 = 10 > 9`. The locally densest first choice spent its budget on a piece that stranded a low-value remnant. Greedy is out.

So I take the DP I can defend. Let `dp[L]` be the maximum revenue from a rod of length `L`. Whatever the optimal cutting of a length-`L` rod is, its leftmost piece has some length `k` in `1..L` earning `p[k]`, and the length-`(L-k)` remnant is itself cut optimally — else I could improve the whole by improving the remnant. I don't know which first length the optimum uses, so I try them all:

`dp[L] = max over k in 1..L of ( p[k] + dp[L-k] )`,  with base case `dp[0] = 0`.

Peeling the leftmost piece and recursing covers every composition of `L` exactly once, and the `max` keeps the best — no greedy choice, every first length considered. Since `dp[L]` depends only on strictly shorter `dp[L-k]`, building the table in increasing `L` means every value I read is already finished. That is `O(n^2)` time, `O(n)` space.

On the instance that broke greedy: `dp[1] = 1`; `dp[2] = max(1+1, 5+0) = 5`; `dp[3] = max(1+5, 5+1, 8+0) = 8`; `dp[4] = max(1+8, 5+5, 8+1, 9+0) = 10`. It delivers `10`, matching the optimum and beating greedy's `9` on the very instance that broke the ratio idea. At `n = 5000` the double loop is `n(n+1)/2 ≈ 1.25 * 10^7` add-and-compare operations, a few milliseconds against the 1-second limit, so there is no reason to reach for anything sub-quadratic.

One transcription point on the inner maximum: I seed it with `LLONG_MIN`, not `0`. Because the loop `k in 1..L` always runs at least once for `L >= 1`, the first real candidate overwrites the sentinel immediately, and the stored `dp[L]` is a maximum over genuine first-piece choices only. Seeding with `0` happens to be correct on these inputs — prices are non-negative, so every candidate `p[k] + dp[L-k] >= 0` and the `0` could never win — but it quietly folds a phantom "cut nothing" option into a non-empty rod, which becomes a real error the moment a price could go negative. `LLONG_MIN` keeps the recurrence meaning what it says regardless.

The corners fall out of the recurrence. `n = 0`: the `dp` loop never runs, output `dp[0] = 0`, the empty rod. `n = 1`: `dp[1] = p[1] + dp[0] = p[1]`, the rod sold whole. Monotone-increasing prices: the `k = L` candidate is `p[L] + dp[0] = p[L]`, so "don't cut" is always among the choices and wins when it should — exactly the case greedy-by-ratio would wrongly cut. Monotone-decreasing prices: the `k = 1` candidate chains `p[1] + dp[L-1]`, building the all-singletons composition. Empty stdin: `if(!(cin>>n)) return 0;` exits cleanly with no output.

To trust the rest, I differential-tested against an independent oracle: for small `n` an exhaustive enumeration of every composition (the literal problem definition, zero cleverness), for larger `n` a memoized top-down recursion structured oppositely to the bottom-up table so a transcription slip in one would not silently match the other. Across 613 instances — the edge corners plus generated monotone, spiky, and generic random price lists — zero mismatches. Running the discarded greedy against the DP on the same battery, it disagreed on roughly `1.5%` of instances — the ratio idea is not just theoretically beatable but empirically wrong often enough to matter.
