A rooted tree of `n` junctions: water enters at the root, and each outlet — a childless junction — must receive one unit that pays `cost[i]` once for every pipe on its root-to-outlet path. I want the total pumping work over all outlets. Before I pick a traversal, the constraints already force a decision: `n` up to `2*10^5` and `c_i` up to `10^6` mean the moment I multiply those out the answer stops fitting in 32 bits, and getting that wrong here is a silent wrong answer, not a crash. So I fix the arithmetic width first.

The worst shape for magnitude is a broom: the root, one stem junction `c` beneath it, and roughly `2*10^5` outlets hanging directly off `c`, every pipe at `10^6`. The stem pipe carries one unit per outlet, so it alone contributes `10^6 * ~2*10^5 ≈ 2*10^11`; the outlets' own pipes add another `~2*10^11`; the total lands near `4*10^11`. Two distinct things overflow, not one: the running total blows past `2^31 - 1 ≈ 2.1*10^9` by two orders of magnitude, and a *single* product `cost[v] * leaves[v] ≈ 2*10^11` overflows `int` before it is ever added in. So both the multiplicands and the accumulator must be 64-bit — widening only the accumulator still overflows in the multiply. `long long` tops out near `9.2*10^18`, leaving seven orders of magnitude of headroom, so it is safe and `int` is not.

Once the tree is parsed every method is some depth-first aggregation; the choice is what to aggregate. The literal definition — for each outlet walk parent pointers up to the root, summing pipe costs — is obviously correct and makes a perfect independent reference, but on the broom (a stem of length `L` feeding a bush of `M` leaves) every leaf walks the whole stem, `O(L*M) = Θ(n^2)`, which is `~10^10` operations at `n = 2*10^5` and far over the 1-second limit. That is the brute, not the submission.

The efficient version turns on one structural fact: the pipe `(v -> par[v])` is traversed once for each outlet in `v`'s subtree, because every such outlet's water descends through it. So if `leaves[v]` counts the outlets under `v`, that pipe contributes exactly `cost[v] * leaves[v]`, and the answer is the sum of that over all non-root `v`. Every `leaves[v]` falls out of one DFS — a childless node is `1`, an internal node is the sum of its children's counts — giving `O(n)` time and space.

The whole method rests on that equivalence, so I prove it rather than lean on it. Fix an outlet `L`; its contribution to the per-outlet definition is the sum of `cost[v]` over the pipes on its root path. Sum over all outlets and swap the order: for each pipe `(v -> par[v])`, count the outlets whose path uses it. An outlet's path uses that pipe exactly when the outlet lies in `v`'s subtree, and there are `leaves[v]` such outlets. So the double sum equals `sum over non-root v of cost[v] * leaves[v]` — an exact reindexing of a finite sum, nothing lost. On the sample (root `1`; `2`,`3` under it at cost `5`,`3`; `4`,`5` under `2` at `7`,`2`; `6` under `3` at `4`) the per-outlet view gives `12 + 7 + 7 = 26` and the per-edge view gives `5*2 + 3*1 + 7 + 2 + 4 = 26`; the two sides agree, so the brute and the submission should match to the last digit.

Transcribing the DFS, my reflex is recursion: find the root, `dfs(root)` returns a subtree's outlet count while accumulating `cost[w] * leaves[w]` into a total. That is correct as arithmetic, but it walks straight into a second scale trap independent of the overflow: on a `2*10^5`-node chain the recursion goes `2*10^5` frames deep, and against a typical ~8 MB stack that overflows — I built a straight chain and ran the recursive version, and it segfaulted. On judges with tighter stacks it is even more certain. So the DFS has to run on an explicit stack.

The clean iterative post-order is: push the root, then in an explicit-stack loop pop a node, emit it into an `order` list, and push its children. That produces a pre-order in which every node precedes all its descendants. Then walk `order` in reverse: a child always comes after its parent in pre-order, hence before it in the reverse pass, so when I reach `u` every child is already finalized — `leaves[u]` is `1` if `u` is childless, otherwise the sum of its children's `leaves`. This depends only on parent-before-child, which pre-order guarantees, so it holds for every tree shape and every sibling order. A final flat loop then adds `cost[v] * leaves[v]` for each non-root `v`, no recursion anywhere.

I confirm the overflow empirically rather than trust the estimate: I built the broom (`n = 200000`, stem cost `10^6`, `199998` leaves at `10^6`) and ran an all-`int` build against a `long long` build. The `int` build printed `564041472`; the `long long` build printed `399996000000`, exactly `2 * 199998 * 10^6`. The `int` output is not slightly off — it is garbage from two independent wraparounds, which is why widening the accumulator alone would not have saved it.

A few corners decide whether this survives. `n = 1`: the lone junction is the root and is itself an outlet, but it has no pipe above it — the reverse pass sets `leaves[root] = 1`, the final loop skips the root, total `0`, which is right since there are no pipes and no work. The root id is not guaranteed to be `1`, so I locate it by `p_i == -1` (accepting `0` as a root marker as well) rather than assuming node `1`. A parent may be listed after its child, so I read every line into `par/cost/children` first and only then traverse from the located root — the adjacency is fully built before any DFS. Zero-cost pipes contribute `0` and are harmless.

For evidence beyond the hand traces I ran the independent per-outlet brute against the solution on 750 random small trees with zero mismatches. The brute is genuinely independent: it sums root paths per outlet (the definition) while the solution sums `cost * leaves` per edge (the derived identity), so agreement is real corroboration, not a tautology. Alongside it, the sample prints `26`, the broom `399996000000`, and the `2*10^5`-deep chain prints `199999` (the single outlet's path cost) without crashing.

Both traps the problem is built around are closed — the broom's `int` overflow by 64-bit arithmetic on the products and the total, the deep chain's stack overflow by the explicit-stack DFS. The full iterative program is in the answer.
