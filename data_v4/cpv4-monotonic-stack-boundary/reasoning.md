The sum runs over every contiguous subarray of `a[0..n-1]`, reported mod `1000000007`. Two numbers off the constraints settle the whole approach before I pick an algorithm. First, the count of subarrays is `n*(n+1)/2`, about `2*10^10` at `n = 2*10^5`, so anything that visits subarrays one at a time is dead; I need an `O(n)` per-element accounting that never materializes a subarray. Second, the arithmetic is wider than it looks: the number of subarrays owned by a single index can be `~(10^5)^2 = 10^10`, already past 32-bit, and with `|a[i]| <= 10^9` a single term `c[i]*a[i]` reaches `~10^19`, past even `long long`'s `~9.2*10^18`. So every count lives in `long long`, and every product is folded mod `MOD` *before* it can grow — an `int` anywhere in the counting, or a multiply before the fold, is a silent wrong answer on exactly the large tests.

**Reframing from subarrays to elements.** The minimum of a subarray is one specific element, so I flip the question from "what is the minimum of each subarray" to "for each index `i`, how many subarrays have `a[i]` as their minimum?" Call that `c[i]`. Every subarray is counted under exactly one minimum-owner, so `S = sum_i c[i] * a[i]`. That is the only move that turns the `O(n^2)` enumeration into per-element work, and the whole problem collapses to computing `c[i]` correctly.

**The reach formula.** `a[i]` is the minimum of `a[l..r]` iff `l <= i <= r` and everything in `[l, r]` is `>= a[i]`. So the subarray can extend left until just before an element smaller than `a[i]`, and right until just before an element smaller than `a[i]`, and the two choices are independent: `c[i] = L * R`. If `prev` is the nearest left boundary and `nxt` the nearest right boundary, the legal left endpoints are `(prev, i]` and the legal right endpoints are `[i, nxt)`. Both ranges are *inclusive of `i`*, which is where the one off-by-one this problem invites lives: the count is `i - prev`, not the histogram-rectangle habit `i - prev - 1`. In the largest-rectangle setting the current index is an excluded wall; here it is its own legal endpoint, because `[i,i]` is a real subarray. The exclusive form silently drops every single-cell subarray — a one-element input `[5]` would print `0` instead of `5`. So `left[i] = i - prev`, `right[i] = nxt - i`, inclusive on both sides.

**Ties, and why the two sides need different strictness.** Take `a = [2, 2, 2]`. The subarray `[0..2]` has minimum `2`, attained at all three indices, and each of the six subarrays must be credited to exactly one owner. If I extend the reach over equal elements on *both* sides — pop condition `>=` left and right — then each member of the equal block reaches over the others in both directions, and `[0..2]` gets owned by several indices at once. Concretely that gives reaches `left = [1,2,3]`, `right = [3,2,1]`, so `c = [3,4,3]`, a total count of `10` against only `6` subarrays, and `S = 20` instead of `12`. The fix is asymmetric comparisons: strict `>` on the left pop (an equal element to the left is a wall, so the reach stops at it) and non-strict `>=` on the right pop (the reach extends over equal elements to the right). This makes the *leftmost* element of any equal block its sole owner. Re-running `[2,2,2]`: left `>` gives `[1,1,1]`, right `>=` gives `[3,2,1]`, so `c = [3,2,1]`, total `6`, `S = 12`. The count `6` matching `n*(n+1)/2` is the structural invariant I lean on — with the asymmetric convention `sum_i c[i]` must equal `n(n+1)/2`, and any tie-handling bug shows up as that sum being off. (The mirror convention, non-strict left and strict right, works equally; what is fatal is the *same* strictness on both sides.)

Both boundaries are nearest-smaller scans, so two monotonic-stack passes deliver them in `O(n)`: left-to-right for `prev`, right-to-left for `nxt`.

**The modular fold.** Because `c[i]` reaches `~10^10` and `a[i]` up to `10^9`, I never form `c[i]*a[i]` in integers directly. I reduce first: `cnt = (left[i] % MOD) * (right[i] % MOD) % MOD` is `< MOD < 10^9`, and `val = ((a[i] % MOD) + MOD) % MOD` maps possibly-negative values into `[0, MOD)`. Then `cnt * val < 10^18` is safe in `long long`, and I accumulate mod `MOD`. The `+ MOD` on `val` is what keeps the printed answer non-negative when `a[i] < 0`, which the contract explicitly demands.

**Corners.** `n = 0`: the `if (!(cin >> n)) return 0` guard handles empty input, and for a given `0` token the loops simply run zero times and the answer stays `0` — no subarrays, correct. `n = 1` with `a = [-7]`: `c = [1]`, `val = MOD - 7`, printed `1000000000`, which is `-7 mod MOD` and non-negative. Monotone inputs fall out of the reach formula (increasing arrays give `left[i] = 1` for `i > 0`, decreasing arrays give the full left reach), so they need no special casing. Cross-checking against a brute-force sum on small random inputs — short arrays over a tight value range so ties are frequent, with occasional negatives to exercise the fold — turns up no mismatch, so I ship the two-pass stack with the strict-left/non-strict-right convention and the pre-multiply fold; the full program is in the answer.
