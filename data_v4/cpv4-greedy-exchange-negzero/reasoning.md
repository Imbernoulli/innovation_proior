I have `n` gigs, each with a deadline `d[i]` (finish it on some day in `1..d[i]`) and a payout `v[i]`; at most one gig per day, I may decline whatever I want, and I want to maximize the total payout, which floors at `0` because declining everything is always available. This is job sequencing with deadlines, but two details in the constraints are where the textbook version gets ambushed: payouts run `-10^9..10^9`, so a gig can *cost* me, and deadlines run `0..10^9`, so a gig can carry deadline `0` — and no day `>= 1` is `<= 0`, meaning that gig can never be placed at all. Those two corners are the whole difficulty; the scheduling mechanics underneath are standard.

Scale first, because it fixes the types. With `n` up to `2*10^5` and `|v[i]|` up to `10^9`, the accumulated total can reach `2*10^14`, well past 32-bit range, so the running sum must be `long long` — an `int` there is a silent wrong answer on the large tests. Deadlines up to `10^9` cannot be used as array indices, but I only ever fill at most `n` distinct days, so a deadline beyond `n` is worth exactly a deadline of `n`; capping `d[i]` to `min(d[i], n)` only discards day indices `> n` that no feasible schedule could use, and keeps every day-indexed structure `O(n)`.

The scheduling core is: process gigs in decreasing payout, assign each to the latest still-free day at or before its deadline, drop it if none is free. The non-obvious part is the direction — *latest*, not earliest. Take `A=(d=2, v=10)`, `B=(d=1, v=9)`. Placing the bigger gig `A` as early as possible puts it on day 1; then `B`, whose only legal day is 1, finds it taken and is dropped — total `10`. Placing `A` as late as legal, on day 2, leaves day 1 free for `B` — total `19`. Parking each gig as late as its deadline allows never blocks a placement that a different choice would have kept open, and it maximally protects the early days for the deadline-tight gigs that have nowhere else to go. That is the exchange direction, and it is what makes the greedy optimal among any fixed set of accepted gigs.

But which gigs do I accept? Here the negative payouts set the trap. Suppose an optimal plan accepts a gig with `v[i] <= 0` on some day `t`. Delete it, leaving day `t` empty: every other gig keeps its day, so the schedule stays feasible, and the total changes by `-v[i] >= 0` — it does not decrease. So there is always an optimal plan that accepts only strictly positive gigs, and I filter to `v[i] > 0` before anything else. That filter is load-bearing: a greedy that accepts any gig it can physically fit will happily take a `-5` and report a worse total. Concretely, on `n=1, d=[3], v=[-7]`, a fit-anything greedy finds day 1 free and prints `-7` — impossible, since I could simply decline. The filter also collapses several corners into one path: if no gig is positive (all-negative, all-zero, or the empty instance), the accepted set is empty and the answer is `0`, exactly the floor.

For the repeated "latest free day at or before `x`" query, with a "mark day used" between calls, a disjoint-set forest is the right structure: `par[x]` points toward the latest free day `<= x`, `findFree(x)` walks parents to a root, and accepting day `s` sets `par[s] = s-1` so later queries skip past it. Day `0` is the sentinel meaning "no free day," and this is where the deadline-`0` corner has to be handled with care. A gig with deadline `0` caps to `0`, and I must never treat slot `0` as a real placement. The accept test has to be strict `slot > 0`, not `slot >= 0`: with `>=`, a deadline-`0` gig would be "accepted" on day `0`, adding its payout *and* setting `par[0] = -1`, which both counts an unplaceable gig and writes a negative index that corrupts every later `findFree`. I guard it two ways — skip immediately when the capped deadline is `<= 0`, and require `slot > 0` before accepting. On `d=[0,1], v=[100,50]` the `100` gig caps to day `0` and is dropped while the `50` gig takes day 1, giving `50`, not `150`.

`n=0` needs no special case: the stream is just `0`, `order` is empty, and the answer is `0` (a stream that ends right after `n` is caught by the input-read guard). Ties — several equal payouts fighting for the same early day — resolve harmlessly, since the tied payouts are equal and which one wins a given day never changes the total. On performance, the DSU with path compression is near-linear, so even the adversarial all-deadline-`1` case, where every positive gig collapses onto the day-`0` sentinel, stays well under the 1-second limit at `n=2*10^5`.

I checked the greedy against an independent oracle: an exhaustive `O(2^n)` brute that tests each subset with the Hall condition (sorted accepted deadlines satisfy `d_j >= j`), run over many random small instances seeded to hit `n=0`, all-negative, all-zero, mixed signs, deadline-`0`, and deadlines exceeding `n`. They agree everywhere. The final program is the `O(n log n)` sort plus the disjoint-set placement, filtered to positive payouts and guarded so invalid days never get accepted; the full module is in the answer.
