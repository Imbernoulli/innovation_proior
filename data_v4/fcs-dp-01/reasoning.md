I must choose exactly `k` pairwise-disjoint non-empty contiguous segments of `a[0..n-1]` (values possibly negative) so the total of all covered cells is maximized; input is `n k` then the `n` values, output one integer. Two numbers fix the terms before I pick an algorithm. Both `n` and `k` run up to `2*10^5`, so anything indexed by (position, segment-count) is `nk = 4*10^10` cells â€” dead on a 1-second limit. And `|a[i]| <= 10^9` over up to `n` cells means a single segment's value reaches `2*10^14`, so every sum is 64-bit from the outset. I keep that flag up; the accumulator I eventually carry is where the width question gets sharp.

The "exactly `k`" is the whole personality of the problem. Under "at most `k`" I could greedily grab the profitable segments and stop. "Exactly `k`" can force me into bad segments: with `k` large and the array mostly negative I must open `k` segments and eat the least-bad cells, and with `k` small and the array mostly positive I must merge regions I would rather keep apart. The count is a hard global constraint, not a budget I can underspend.

The textbook exact DP makes that explicit. Let `closed[i][j]` be the best value over `a[0..i-1]` with exactly `j` segments and cell `i-1` not inside an open segment, and `open[i][j]` the same but with the `j`-th segment open through `a[i-1]`. Then `closed[i+1][j] = max(closed[i][j], open[i][j])` (stay out, or close the open segment at the boundary) and `open[i+1][j] = max(open[i][j], closed[i][j-1]) + a[i]` (extend, or start the `j`-th segment here from a closed state, the required gap automatic). The answer is `max(closed[n][k], open[n][k])`. Correct, every transition defensible â€” but `O(nk)`, `4*10^10` at the limits. Rolling the `i` index down to two layers fixes memory but not time. I need the same value in something like `O(n log(range))`.

A tiny instance shows why the count can't just be capped. Take `a = [5,-100,5,-100,5]`. With `k=2` the choices are two lone `5`s (value `10`) or a bridged pair like `[5,-100,5]` plus `[5]` (value `-85`); the optimum `10` refuses to bridge the negative run. With `k=3` I am forced to take all three `5`s as singletons (`15`), and `k=1` gives `5`. So `f(1)=5, f(2)=10, f(3)=15` â€” the marginal gains `5, 5` are flat.

That flatness is the clue. Let `f(j)` be the optimum for exactly `j` segments and `s_j = f(j) - f(j-1)` the marginal value of the `j`-th. If `s_j` is non-increasing in `j` â€” `f` concave â€” the whole Lagrangian route opens up. The intuition is that one more segment either splits a chosen region or adds a fresh disjoint one, and the most valuable such moves get used first, so each additional opportunity is worth no more than the last. Concavity carries the entire method, so before building on it I compute the slope sequence directly from the brute `O(nk)` DP over many random arrays; it comes out non-increasing every time.

Concavity means the points `(j, f(j))` sit on a concave curve, which lets me drop the `j` dimension entirely. Charge a penalty `lambda >= 0` per opened segment and define `g(lambda) = max_j ( f(j) - lambda*j )`, the value of the unconstrained problem "take any number of segments, each costing `lambda`." Because the `s_j` are non-increasing, the maximizing `j` is where the slope crosses `lambda`: the optimal count is `{ j : s_j >= lambda }`, a plateau when some `s_j` equal `lambda`, and it decreases monotonically as `lambda` rises. So I solve the penalized problem fast, read off how many segments it used, and binary-search `lambda` until that count is `k`, then recover `f(k) = g(lambda) + lambda*k`. The penalized solve has no `j` dimension â€” just "inside a segment or not" â€” so it's an `O(n)` scan, and the search adds `O(log(range))`, roughly `2*10^5 * 50 = 10^7` operations.

For a fixed `lambda` I carry two states, each a `(penalized value, segment count)` pair: `out`, best with no segment currently open, and `in`, best with a segment open through the current cell. Processing `a[i] = x`: to be inside after `i`, either open a new segment from an `out` state â€” value `out.value + x - lambda`, count `out.count + 1` â€” or extend the open one â€” value `in.value + x`, count unchanged â€” and keep the better; to be outside, either carry `out` or close `in` right before `i`, whichever is better. Initialize `out = (0,0)` and `in = (-inf, 0)` (can't be inside before any element), and read `g(lambda)` off the better of `out`/`in` at the end.

The tie-break is load-bearing. On a plateau where several counts are all optimal at this `lambda`, the DP must report a predictable count or the search loses monotonicity. I break ties toward fewer segments, so `solve(lambda).count = cntMin(lambda) = #{ j : s_j > lambda }`, non-increasing in `lambda`. The target is then clean: the smallest integer `lambda` with `cntMin(lambda) <= k`. At that `lambda`, `k` lands inside the optimal plateau `[cntMin, cntMax]`, every optimal `j` satisfies `f(j) = g(lambda) + lambda*j`, and so `f(k) = g(lambda) + lambda*k`. The one discipline is to hold the pairing consistent: a fewer-segments tie-break demands the smallest-`lambda` search, and mixing the two directions breaks the monotonicity the search relies on.

The search bounds need care. The slopes `s_j` are differences of segment-sum values, and forcing an extra segment can require bridging a whole negative run, so a marginal `s_j` can plunge to roughly `-S` where `S = sum|a[i]|` â€” not merely `-10^9`, and empirically the slopes do run far below `min(a)`. So I search `lambda` in `[-S-1, S+1]`; at `hi = S+1` the penalty exceeds every slope and the optimal count is `0 <= k`, which is the invariant the "smallest with count `<= k`" search needs.

One implementation hazard is the in-place update: `newOut = better(out, in)` must read the pre-update pair, so I compute both new states from the old `out`/`in` and commit only after both are formed â€” no read-after-write corruption.

The subtler hazard is the width of the penalized accumulator, and with `long long` for the value it produced a real failure. The small differential cases all passed, then I ran the boundary `n=100000, k=50000` (its ceiling `ceil(n/2)`), every `a[i]=10^9`. The forced layout covers `n-(k-1)=50001` cells, so the true answer is `50001*10^9 â‰ˆ 5.0001*10^13`, cleanly positive â€” yet the solver printed a large negative. A negative where a large positive is forced is the signature of int64 overflow, so I sized the intermediates. Pushing the optimal count up to `k=50000` drives `lambda` down toward `-S â‰ˆ -10^14`; opening a segment then adds `x - lambda â‰ˆ 10^14`, so over `5*10^4` opens the accumulator `g(lambda)` reaches about `5*10^18`, and the recovery term `lambda*k â‰ˆ 10^14 * 5*10^4` is another `5*10^18` â€” both within a factor of two of the `int64` ceiling `9.2*10^18`, and in the genuine worst case (`S` up to `2*10^14`, `k` up to `10^5`) `lambda*k â‰ˆ 2*10^19` overflows outright. The final answer `f(k)` is itself small (a real segment sum, `<= 2*10^14`), but it is the difference of two enormous opposite-signed terms, so those terms have to be carried wider. I move the state value, the sentinel, and the `lambda*k` recovery to `__int128` (headroom `~1.7*10^38`); `lambda` stays 64-bit since `|lambda| <= S+1 <= 2*10^14`. The sentinel becomes `(__int128)(-1) << 100`, unreachably negative and safe to leave un-added-into once the extend transition is guarded so `x` never lands on it; and since `cout` won't print `__int128`, I emit the final value digit by digit. Recompiled, the boundary case prints `50001000000000`, and the differential suite â€” all-positive, all-negative, mixed-sign with long negative runs, and an all-`Â±10^9` overflow mode â€” matches the brute with no mismatches.

The corner cases behave as the mechanism predicts. `k=1` collapses to Kadane, the single best subarray (`[-2,1,-3,4,-1,2,1,-5]` gives `6`). All-negative with forced `k` returns the `k` least-bad single cells (`[-3,-1,-4,-1,-5]`, `k=2` gives `-2`). `k=ceil(n/2)` forces a near-fixed every-other-cell layout (`[2,2,2,2,2,2]`, `k=3` gives `8`, four of six cells). `n=1, k=1` takes the lone cell even at `-10^9`.

That is the whole program: the penalized `solve(lambda)` returning `(g, cntMin)` with the fewer-segments tie-break, the binary search for the smallest `lambda` with `cntMin <= k` over `[-S-1, S+1]`, the `__int128` accumulator and recovery, and a manual `__int128` printer for the final line â€” assembled in the answer.
